## ABSTRACT
Soft-tissue sarcoma (STS) poses unique challenges to treatment due to its rarity and heterogeneity. Comprised of multiple histologies which, among other characteristics, differ in their genetic makeup, tumor microenvironment, recurrence patterns and therefore response to treatment, STS is treated primarily with surgical resection, with systemic therapy used in high-risk or metastatic cases. In the era of immunotherapy, STS has been characterized as predominantly immune-cold with few histologies demonstrating consistent robust responses to checkpoint inhibition in the metastatic setting. Strategies to improve checkpoint inhibition responsiveness, such as combination therapies and treatment in the neoadjuvant setting, have shown promise in certain sarcoma subtypes. Other immunotherapeutic approaches, such as adoptive cellular therapies, as well as development of reliable biomarkers for response to immune checkpoint inhibitors, are the subject of ongoing research in this field.

## Introduction
Sarcoma is a group of rare, diverse malignancies comprising more than 100 histologic subtypes which remains challenging to treat. Surgical resection is the preferred modality of treatment for localized soft tissue sarcoma (STS), with chemotherapy mainly used in advanced or metastatic cases and radiation used for local control. The various histologic subtypes of sarcoma differ in their local and distant recurrence patterns, which drive clinical decision-making regarding the prescription of local or systemic treatment. The lack of effective systemic treatments available for treating soft-tissue sarcoma is underscored by the 10-y disease-specific death approaching 40% among patients with high-grade resectable disease. In the metastatic setting, the survival rate declines even further, reaching 30% at 5 y. One of the current front line chemotherapy regimens for unresectable or metastatic disease, comprising of doxorubicin and ifosfamide, demonstrates a response rate of roughly 20% with median survival of just over 1 y. This regimen has been in place since the 1980s. A second combination in use for STS, gemcitabine and docetaxel, has a response rate of 16%. Apart from chemotherapy, the tyrosine kinase inhibitor pazopanib and antineoplastic drug trabectedin have also been approved for use in STS, both with response rates less than 10%. The need for more effective therapies is evident, with the field of immunotherapy representing a key area of investigation for decades. This state-of-the-art review will summarize key findings in the field as well as works-in-progress and future directions.

## Methods
Databases such as Pubmed and the Cochrane library were searched for immunotherapy in sarcoma. Results were filtered for clinical trials in English, with emphasis given to studies in adult patients with soft-tissue sarcoma. References from relevant reviews and publications were also explored.

## Results

## Immune checkpoint inhibitors demonstrate activity in certain sarcoma histologies
Antibodies to immune checkpoints, such programmed cell death 1 (PD-1), its ligand (PD-L1), or cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated antigen 4 (CTLA4), have been approved for use in many different malignancies, with some of the greatest successes seen in malignancies such as melanoma, urologic malignancies, lymphoma, and lung cancer. Multiple studies have explored their use in sarcoma, with promising histology-specific results.
The SARC028 trial, published in 2017, investigated the use of anti-PD-1 antibody pembrolizumab in bone sarcoma and four common histologies of STS: undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma (UPS), dedifferentiated liposarcoma (DDLPS), synovial sarcoma (SS) and leiomyosarcoma (LMS). The most notable responses were in the UPS cohort, which saw a progression-free survival rate of 70% at 12 weeks, with one patient out of 10 treated achieving a complete response by RECIST 1.1 criteria lasting over a year. In the UPS and DDLPS cohorts, there were a few meaningful and durable responses to pembrolizumab monotherapy. However, the objective response rate (ORR) in the entire STS cohort was 18%, which meant the study failed to reach its primary endpoint. The trial subsequently enrolled an additional 30 patients with UPS and DDLPS each; the ORR was 23% in the UPS cohort and 10% in the DDLPS cohort, with the UPS cohort reaching its primary endpoint. The translational studies of SARC028 analyzed PD-L1 expression in the tumors via IHC; the two tumors which had PD-L1 positivity were both UPS, but other histologies which demonstrated response to pembrolizumab did not have PD-L1 positivity on IHC, so no clear biomarkers of response were identified. 
The activity of two different immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), nivolumab (anti-PD1) and ipilimumab (anti-CTLA4), was studied in patients with advanced or metastatic STS in the Alliance A091401 trial. The trial randomized patients with diverse histologies into either a nivolumab monotherapy arm or a combination arm with both nivolumab and ipilimumab. The ORR for the nivolumab monotherapy arm was 5%, with responses occurring in alveolar soft part sarcoma (ASPS), LMS, and sarcoma not otherwise specified (NOS). The ORR for the combination therapy arm was 16%, with responses seen in LMS, UPS, myxofibrosarcoma (MFS), and angiosarcoma. While the nivolumab monotherapy arm did not reach its primary endpoint, the results of the combination arm were promising. Given the results of the SARC028 study, the Alliance trial subsequently enrolled an expansion cohort of patients with gastrointestinal stromal tumor (GIST), DDLPS, and UPS. The combination arm of nivolumab and ipilimumab demonstrated a higher response rate than the nivolumab monotherapy arm of 16.6% vs 8.3% for both DDLPS and UPS. None of the patients with GIST responded to either treatment.
Correlative analyses for the Alliance A091401 trial demonstrated low utility of traditional biomarkers for ICIs. There was low prevalence of PD-L1 expression across all patients, and PD-L1 expression was not found to correlate with clinical response. Aside from CD4+ cells in DDLPS, no other immune markers or cell types studied were associated with survival outcomes. The median tumor mutational burden (TMB) was low, and no tumors were microsatellite unstable. The metrics that were associated with improved progression-free survival were the presence of gene fusions and subclonal mutations.
These trials and others continue to demonstrate that certain sarcoma sub-types have higher response rates to ICIs. The DART trial studied the combination of ipilimumab and nivolumab in rare cancers. This phase II trial enrolled and treated 16 patients with angiosarcoma, nine of whom had cutaneous tumors. The ORR rate was 25%, with four out of the 16 patients responding. Three out of the four responders had cutaneous angiosarcoma of the scalp or face. Interestingly, a separate study has shown that angiosarcoma of the head, neck, face and scalp has higher TMB out of all angiosarcoma cases studies, and analysis across tumor types has demonstrated improved survival in those with higher TMB who have received ICI therapy. , 
One histologic subtype of sarcoma in which ICIs have shown clear benefit is alveolar soft part sarcoma (ASPS). ASPS is an ultra-rare sarcoma with overall survival of 20–46% at 5 y in the metastatic setting. , It is genetically characterized by a translocation resulting in a gene fusion, ASPSCR1-TFE3. A phase II trial of atezolizumab, a PD-L1 inhibitor, in advanced ASPS demonstrated promising efficacy. Investigators found an objective response rate of 37%, with one patient out of 52 evaluable patients having a complete response, and 18 patients having a partial response. The median duration of response was 2 y. These results led to FDA approval for atezolizumab for metastatic or advanced ASPS.
The trial’s correlative studies demonstrated PD-L1 expression and a favorable regulatory T cell to CD8+ cytotoxic T cell ratio in six out of six pre-treatment biopsies from tumors that went on to have a partial response. Five out of six tumors had CD3+ lymphocytes with PD-1 expression pre-treatment, and the remaining tumor converted to having PD-1 expressing lymphocytes during treatment with atezolizumab. In those with disease progression, three out of four patients had tumors with either lack of both PD-L1 and PD-1 expression, lack of PD-1 expression, or with an unfavorable T reg:CD8+ ratio. While the study was not powered to compare the expression of these markers across response groups, both the clinical and biochemical results of this trial demonstrate the responsiveness of ASPS to immunotherapy, a conclusion which may be counterintuitive. Translocation-associated sarcomas such as ASPS have few mutations apart from the translocation itself. ASPS has been shown to have low tumor mutational burden using data from the American Association for Cancer’s GENIE database. The seven ASPS samples in the MSK sarcoma IMPACT database published in 2022 all have a TMB less than 2 mutations per Mb. Therefore, simple karyotype sarcomas with low TMB, such as ASPS, would be hypothesized to generate fewer neoantigens as compared to sarcomas with complex karyotypes, such as UPS, yet ASPS appears to be the most sensitive of any of the sarcoma subtypes to checkpoint inhibition.
These studies of immune checkpoint inhibitors in sarcoma demonstrate the importance of histology-specific studies, as the response to immunotherapy varies widely among different histologic subtypes. They also highlight our incomplete understanding of the mechanisms behind the efficacy of or resistance to ICIs in specific subtypes of sarcoma, and the challenges in finding reliable biomarkers for immunotherapy.

## Immune-cold sarcomas yield few potential biomarkers for efficacy of ICIs
Sarcoma in general is thought to be an immune-cold disease which does not activate the immune system as much as other disease processes such as melanoma. Even within this broad categorization of sarcoma as a disease with low immunogenicity, there is substantial variation in the immune tumor microenvironment based on histologic subtypes, as evidenced by studies of clinical response to immunotherapy and their correlative analyses. Chen et al. studied the tumor microenvironment (TME) of UPS, a genetically complex sarcoma subtype which has demonstrated response to ICIs, and rhabdomyosarcoma, which can be translocation associated and is less responsive to ICIs. , RNA gene expression analysis demonstrated high levels of M0 and M2 macrophages in both histologies, with a predominance of M2 macrophages in UPS compared to rhabdomyosarcoma. M2 macrophages have been shown to have a pro-tumor, immunosuppressive role across different malignancies. , UPS samples demonstrated diffuse presence of CD8+ T cells in the tumor, and rhabdomyosarcoma samples demonstrated tertiary lymphoid structures (TLS), with CD8+ T cells aggregating with CD20+ B cells near tumor vasculature. PD-L1 expression also differed across the samples; UPS samples had expression of PD-L1 on tumor cells and tumor associated macrophages (TAMs), while rhabdomyosarcoma samples had PD-L1 expression on mainly on TLS immune cells and lesser PD-L1 expression on tumor cells.
Even with advances in the understanding of the TME in subtypes sarcoma, identification of a practical and reliable biomarker for ICI treatment remains elusive. The utility of PD-L1 as a prognostic indicator or as a biomarker in ICI therapy in sarcoma is unclear. Correlative studies from the aforementioned trial demonstrated that PD-L1 expression was not necessary for response to ICI. , One study aiming to characterize the prognostic value of PD-L1 expression found a correlation between PD-L1 expression across sarcoma subtypes and overall survival, whereas another study, which included different histologies, did not. , 
Tumor mutational burden is another biomarker for ICI use that is well established across varying cancers, however sarcoma subtypes have low TMB, making its use as a biomarker challenging. The phase II KEYNOTE-158 trial studied the use of pembrolizumab in non-colorectal solid tumors; the exploratory analysis for the trial found that TMB-high (defined as ≥10 mutations/Mb) cancers were associated with a higher response rate. This finding led the FDA to approve pembrolizumab for any patient with metastatic or unresectable TMB-high solid tumors, making TMB the first tumor-agnostic biomarker to be approved and used for ICI treatment in solid tumors. Sarcomas, however, have been shown to have low TMB, which greatly decreases the relevance of TMB as a biomarker in this field. Analysis of The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) sarcoma dataset demonstrated that across the six subtypes of sarcoma included in the study, the average TMB was 1.06 mut/Mb. Nacev et al. analyzed 2,138 sarcoma samples across 45 pathological subtypes and found that the median TMB range among all the subtypes was 0.9–3.0 mut/Mb. Angiosarcoma and UPS were notable subtypes which had a portion of tumors skewed toward higher TMB; 7.6% of angiosarcomas had TMB ≥ 10 mut/Mb, and 6.7% of UPS had TMB ≥ 10 mut/Mb. Immune checkpoint inhibitors have shown some promising efficacy in these very subtypes, however more work remains to be done in identifying which patients with sarcoma would benefit from treatment with immune checkpoint inhibitors.
Efforts to categorize the genetic and immune landscape of sarcomas have led to classifications that may be predictive of ICI efficacy. Rosenbaum et al. analyzed biopsy and blood samples from 185 patients treated with ICIs across 5 clinical trials, and they found that an immune-enriched, non-fibrotic TME as well as lower amounts of peripheral CD8+PD-1-T cells which were TIM-3+ were independently associated with longer PFS and overall survival. Another system of categorizing the varying immune phenotypes within STS was devised by Petitperez et al., who analyzed gene expression across 608 tumors consisting of LMS, DDLPS, and UPS subtypes. Their analysis highlighted five distinct TMEs, or sarcoma immune classes (SIC A-E), which were found to be independently associated with patient survival. After analyzing the characteristics of the five SICs in depth, the B cell populations and presence of TLS within SIC E emerged as significantly associated with improved overall survival. In addition, the tumor mutation burden (TMB) was low throughout the samples, and there was no difference in TMB across the SICs. The investigators then applied the concept of SICs to pre-treatment samples of patients enrolled in SARC028 and found that SIC E was associated with and therefore predictive of response to pembrolizumab.

## Strategies to overcome immune-cold TME of sarcomas
The overall immunosuppressed tumor microenvironment across sarcoma subtypes have prompted investigation into novel combinations of treatments which could theoretically enable the evolution of immune-cold sarcomas into immune-hot tumors and thereby increase the efficacy of immunotherapy agents such as ICIs.

## Combinations of chemotherapy and immunotherapy
PEMBROSARC, a phase II single-arm trial investigating the combination of cyclophosphamide and pembrolizumab, was published in 2018. Patients with advanced subtypes of STS were enrolled in four cohorts: LMS, UPS, other sarcoma and GIST. All patients with LMS and UPS had progression of disease while on trial, with one partial response noted in a patient with solitary fibrous tumor. The patient with partial response was the only patient with high PD-L1 expression on immune cells. Tumor samples across patients revealed predominant infiltration of inhibitory M2 macrophages as well as high percentage of macrophages expressing the inhibitory enzyme indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase 1 (IDO1). The exceptionally low response rate in this trial as compared to other trials investigating ICIs in STS may have been due to the advanced nature of disease in these patients, whose tumors displayed immunosuppressed TMEs across histologies. A follow-up trial which selected patients based on the presence of intratumoral tertiary lymphoid structures (TLS) demonstrated an ORR of 30% as compared to 2.4% in all patients. 
Other combinations of ICI and chemotherapy have also been studied, such as in the use of doxorubicin and pembrolizumab in phase II trials which enrolled patients with advanced or metastatic STS who were naïve to anthracycline treatment. Pollack et al. found an ORR of 19% across their phase I/II trial, with durable responses seen in patients with UPS and DDLPS. Livingston et al. found an ORR of 37% in a similar study of advanced STS, with one complete response seen in a patient with liposarcoma, and partial responses seen in LMS, UPS, and epithelioid angiosarcoma. The two trials also differed in the reported OS and PFS, with Pollack et al. reporting an OS of 27 months and PFS of 8.1 months, and Livingston et al. reporting OS of 17 months and PFS of 5.7 months, likely due to differences in the patient populations enrolled in the studies in addition to differences in treatment regimens. The SAINT trial was a phase I/II trial studying the combination of ipilimumab, nivolumab and trabectedin as a first-line treatment for advanced STS, with a response rate of 25% seen in LMS, DDLPS, pleomorphic sarcoma, synovial sarcoma, MFS, endometrial stromal sarcoma, and clear cell sarcoma. This study suggested that administering immunotherapy in combination with chemotherapy early in the treatment paradigm might be beneficial for advanced STS.
Additional combinations of ICI and chemotherapy under scrutiny include combining retifanlimab with gemcitabine and docetaxel. Interim results of this phase II trial (NCT04577014) have been published as an abstract; investigators found a response rate of 22%, with responses seen in UPS/MFS, LMS, angiosarcoma, and follicular dendritic cell sarcoma. 
Bempegaldesleukin is an interleukin-2 pathway agonist which was combined with nivolumab (anti-PD-1) in a pilot study. Efficacy varied by histology, with clinical benefit rate, or percent of patients achieving complete response, partial response, or stable disease for 6 months, reaching 63% in angiosarcoma. Median overall survival was not reached in the ASPS and angiosarcoma cohorts. Overall, the addition of bempegaldesleukin did not seem to improve the efficacy of ICI treatment except for in angiosarcoma. RNA expression was used to identify classes of tumors as immune-hot, intermediate tumors, and immune-low tumors. Immune-hot tumors with higher levels of CD8+ T cells and PD-1 expression were found to be associated with better ORR and longer PFS.

## Tyrosine kinase inhibitors and immunotherapy
Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) have also been studied in combination with ICIs for STS. A phase II trial of axitinib (a TKI targeting VEGF or vascular endothelial growth factor) with pembrolizumab was shown to have a progression-free survival of 66% at 3 months in ASPS patients, which is better than expected based on historical control. In other histologies, the median PFS was similar to what would be expected with axitinib monotherapy. A subsequent histology-specific trial of the TKI lenvatinib and pembrolizumab enrolled patients with LMS, UPS, vascular sarcomas, synovial sarcoma, MPNST, and bones sarcomas. The best overall response rate was 25% in the UPS cohort and 30% in the synovial sarcoma and MPNST cohort, with median PFS of 35 weeks for synovial sarcoma and median duration of response of 11 months. The IMMUNOSARC trial studied another combination of TKI/ICI, sunitinib and nivolumab, in certain sarcoma sub-types. One complete response was noted in a patient with angiosarcoma, and partial responses were observed in ASPS, angiosarcoma, extraskeletal myxoid chondrosarcoma, and synovial sarcoma. The 6-month progression-free survival was 48%, and the median overall survival of 2 y was very encouraging. One challenge with interpreting the results of some single-arm trials studying combination treatments is discerning the impact of the combination treatment as compared to the impact of the individual agents themselves on the efficacy of the overall treatment. Nevertheless, promising results provide the basis for ongoing clinical research on these combination treatments.
Several trials have also explored the TKI and ICI combination in patients who would otherwise be predicted to not respond to immunotherapy as well as in patients who would stand to benefit from this combination treatment. A phase II trial, REGOMUNE, studied the combination of an anti-VEGF TKI regorafenib combined with avelumab (anti-PD-L1 antibody) in advanced sarcoma which was immune-cold and lacked TLS, with the hope that the combination treatment would make the tumor susceptible to ICI. The combination treatment in this population of immune-cold tumors did not demonstrate synergy or potentiation of ICI; the ORR was 11%, with partial responses seen in angiosarcoma, UPS, malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumor (MPNST), and synovial sarcoma. Two ongoing trials are studying a novel TKI and ICI combination involving the TKI cabozantinib. The Alliance A091902 trial has enrolled patients with angiosarcoma previously treated with taxanes onto treatment with cabozantinib and nivolumab; preliminary results demonstrate ORR of 62%, with final results pending publication. Another ongoing trial has enrolled patients with advanced STS onto either cabozantinib monotherapy or cabozantinib with nivolumab and ipilimumab combination therapy with crossover permitted; preliminary results published in abstract form have demonstrated response in angiosarcoma, LMS, epithelioid sarcoma, and MFS. Interestingly, two patients with LMS, a histology which is not particularly immune-sensitive, progressed on cabozantinib monotherapy and subsequently had partial responses in the combination arm after crossover.

## Novel agents with immunotherapy
Talimogene laherparepvec (T-VEC) is an oncolytic herpes simplex virus which has been approved for use in melanoma and is thought to be immunogenic by causing release of tumor antigens. , A phase II clinical trial evaluated the efficacy of combining T-VEC with pembrolizumab in patients with advanced or metastatic sarcoma. The ORR was 35%, with median duration of response of 56 weeks. Histologies that demonstrated response to the combination treatment were UPS, MFS, epithelioid sarcoma, cutaneous angiosarcoma, and unclassified sarcoma. Two patients who had progressed on ICI therapy prior to the trial then went on to respond to the combination treatment. These results highlight the efficacy and possibly synergy of a combination of T-VEC with ICI.
Another strategy to improve the efficacy of ICIs in advanced sarcoma was employed by a phase Ib trial which administered botensilimab, an engineered antibody against CTLA-4, in combination with balstilimab, an anti-PD-1 antibody. Botensilimab is an Fc-enhanced anti-CTLA-4 antibody which acts by enhancing antigen presentation and activation of T cells as well as downregulating immunosuppressive regulatory T cells. Its use has been studied in treatment-refractory cancers, such as ovarian and colorectal cancer. , In the sarcoma trial, durable responses to the combination ICIs were seen in angiosarcoma, DDLPS, LMS, and UPS, with an ORR of 19% and median duration of response of 22 months. An exploratory analysis of efficacy revealed an ORR of 27.8% in angiosarcoma patients and 33.3% specifically in visceral angiosarcoma patients. As seen in previous studies, PD-L1 expression did not correlate with outcomes. Most patients in the study had TMB < 10 mut/Mb, demonstrating that the Fc-enhanced anti-CTLA-4 antibody along with PD-1 inhibition has promising, durable activity in sarcomas with low TMB.

## Combining ICI and radiation in the neoadjuvant setting shows promise
Much of the research into immunotherapy in sarcoma has been conducted for metastatic or advanced tumors, but perhaps the most promising development of checkpoint inhibitors in sarcoma has been in the neoadjuvant setting, specifically in combination with radiation. Radiation therapy (RT) is theorized to have possible synergy with immune checkpoint inhibitors in pre-clinical studies, however the clinical benefit of combining RT and immunotherapy in advanced cancers has been mixed. , In sarcoma, recent trials investigating the use of ICI and RT in the neoadjuvant setting have become practice changing.
The following trials demonstrate the efficacy of neoadjuvant ICIs in histologies which are known to be more sensitive to immunotherapy in the advanced setting. A single-institution, noncomparative phase II trial randomized patients in the neoadjuvant setting to nivolumab + RT or ipilimumab/nivolumab + RT if they had UPS, and to either nivolumab or ipilimumab/nivolumab if they had DDLPS. Recurrence-free survival (RFS) at 24 months was 78% in UPS; 60% in the nivo + RT arm and 100% in the combination ICI + RT arm. In DDLPS, RFS at 24 months was 38%, 25% in the nivolumab monotherapy arm and 50% in the ipi/nivo arm. Correlative studies demonstrated that the presence of B cells in the tumor after surgery was associated with improved survival outcomes. This exploratory trial demonstrated promising efficacy of ICI therapy with radiation in the neoadjuvant setting specifically for UPS and DDLPS. Subsequently, SARC032 enrolled patients with high-grade UPS and DDLPS over 5 cm in the extremity to receive either neoadjuvant RT followed by surgery with or without perioperative pembrolizumab starting prior to the first dose of radiation and continuing for a year of total therapy in the adjuvant setting. The 2-y disease-free survival in the combination arm of 67% was longer than the 52% seen in the neoadjuvant RT alone arm. As this trial did not have an arm with neoadjuvant pembrolizumab alone prior to surgery followed by adjuvant RT and pembrolizumab, it is difficult to surmise the benefit of concurrent neoadjuvant RT and pembrolizumab on survival outcomes and determine whether neoadjuvant RT acts synergistically to increase the efficacy of pembrolizumab. Certainly, the benefit of radiation in these regimens is to provide local control while neoadjuvant immunotherapy is administered, lessening the risk of disease progression during the delay in definitive surgical resection.
These studies demonstrate the efforts taking place to sensitize sarcomas to immune checkpoint inhibitors by combining ICI with treatments that may act in synergy or allow for the neoadjuvant administration of ICIs. Research to date has demonstrated some promising signals in certain histologies, such as UPS and DDLPS, however the elucidation of an effective combination remains to be seen. The challenge lies in not only identifying effective treatments or treatment combinations with checkpoint inhibitors but also selecting appropriate patients who would benefit from these therapies. Patient selection for ICI based on biomarkers in sarcoma is difficult because sarcomas are generally immune-cold, and biomarkers such as TMB or PD-L1 expression that are used with other malignancies are not reliable in sarcoma. More complex characterization of tumors as immunogenic based on RNA or cell populations in the peripheral blood may have some utility in identifying patients who would benefit from ICI treatment. Histologies in which ICIs show some efficacy are also those which are more genomically complex or immune-hot, such as UPS, DDLPS and angiosarcoma. These tumors tend to have higher TMB than other subtypes of sarcoma, possibly producing more neoantigens which can activate the immune system and serve as targets. Perhaps surprisingly, ASPS, which is a translocation associated sarcoma and therefore has fewer mutations which can theoretically lead to neoantigen production, exhibits a clear response to ICI. Translocation associated sarcomas, which make up approximately 20% of all STS, can produce specific antigens such as fusion proteins which in theory could be targeted by therapies, however the success of ICI, whose efficacy has been shown to be closely tied to the mutational burden and genomic complexity of tumors, in a simple karyotype subtype such as ASPS shows us how little we understand about the biology of the tumors and the mechanisms behind the efficacy of ICI in sarcoma subtypes. 
Efforts are currently underway to investigate other novel combinations of therapies with ICI ( ). Ongoing trials include combining isolated limb infusion with chemotherapy agents and pembrolizumab (NCT04332874), palbociclib with ICI (NCT06113809), and trabectedin with ICI (NCT03138161). The diversity within sarcoma histologies and their immune microenvironments necessitates the investigation of a broad range of therapies and their combinations. Identifying the patient population which would benefit the most from checkpoint inhibitor therapy is key, however, there remains a significant portion of the patient population which is immune-cold, and therefore resistant to ICIs by mechanisms which remain unknown. Elucidating these mechanisms of resistance, which may be diverse among sarcoma subtypes, remains a significant challenge for the future and would greatly assist in the development of effective future therapies. 

## Ongoing work in the field of adoptive cellular therapies
With the limitations and challenges surrounding the use of immune checkpoint inhibition, other modalities such as adoptive cellular therapies in the treatment of sarcoma are also being explored. Adoptive cellular therapies (ACT) such tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TIL) and chimeric antigen receptor T-cell (CAR T cells) consist of the administration of engineered or cultured tumor reactive T-cells. ACT have been successful in other malignancies such as melanoma and lymphoma, and current work is ongoing to apply these treatments to sarcoma.
Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TIL) have been shown to not only exist in sarcoma subtypes but also correlate with survival outcomes in studies characterizing the immune microenvironment in sarcoma. , Mullinax et al. demonstrated that sarcoma tissue can yield TIL cells reactive to tumors that can be cultured and grown. A pilot study, ATLAS-IT-04, subsequently treated patients with unresectable or metastatic disease with intratumoral LTX-315 prior to TIL therapy. LTX-315 is a non-viral oncolytic peptide which acts by membranolysis and results in T cell infiltration. TIL were able to be expanded from 4 out of 6 patients with desmoplastic small round cell tumor (DSRCT), LMS, or solitary fibrous tumor (SFT), with 3 patients experiencing stable disease as best overall response. PFS ranged from 72 to 208 d, and all patients eventually had progression of disease. Correlative analyses found expansion of de-novo T cell clones in the blood after LTX-315 treatment in two out of two patients analyzed. While no patient had partial or complete response after therapy, TIL therapy still remains under active investigation with various trials being conducted investigating TIL in solid tumors (NCT03935893, NCT06566092) or combining ACT with IL-2 in STS (NCT04052334, NCT06474676).
TIL therapy has theoretical advantages in targeting numerous, tumor-specific antigens, however, patient selection and its resource-intensive nature remain challenges to its widespread adoption. Another type of adoptive cellular therapy which is under investigation is the use of CAR T cells, which requires the identification of a target antigen. As soft tissue sarcoma has been described to have low tumor mutational burden and an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, treatments employing CAR T cells are challenging. Trials have been conducted using T cells engineered to target antigens such as GD2 (a glycolipid antigen) and HER2 (human epidermal growth factor 2) in solid tumors. These two trials included one patient each with STS who had stable disease after treatment, demonstrating feasibility of this approach in STS. , 
Adoptive cellular therapy targeting intracellular proteins, as opposed to CAR-T cells which target surface antigens, has been most thoroughly explored in synovial sarcoma (SS) and myxoid/round cell liposarcoma with exciting results. Synovial sarcoma is characterized by a gene fusion caused by a translocation between SYT on the X chromosome and SSX1, SSX2, or SSX4 on chromosome 18, and myxoid/round cell liposarcoma is characterized by a fusion between FUS and DDIT3. , Both SS and myxoid/round cell LPS have been shown to have high expression of the immunogenic cancer/testis antigens such as NY-ESO-1 in 70–80% of SS patients and 90% of myxoid/round cell LPS patients, and MAGE-A4 in 80% of SS patients. Afamitresgene autoleucel (afami-cel) is an autologous engineered T-cell therapy in which T cells with a receptor for MAGE-A4 peptides are transduced with a self-inactivating lentiviral vector. SPEARHEAD-1, a phase II trial investigating the use of afami-cel, saw an ORR of 37% (39% in synovial sarcoma and 25% in myxoid/round cell LPS) in HLA-A*02-positive patients who had progressed on multiple prior lines of therapy. These results led to FDA approval of afami-cel in patients with treatment refractory synovial sarcoma. ACT targeting NY-ESO-1 have also shown promising results. Robbins et al. studied the administration of autologous T cells transduced to express a T cell receptor reactive to NY-ESO-1 along with treatment with IL-2 in patients with metastatic synovial sarcoma. The ORR was 61% with durable responses in two patients; one partial response sustained for almost 4 y, and one complete response lasting for more than 1 y after treatment. D’Angelo et al. subsequently investigated the use of engineered autologous T cells expressing a TCR reactive to NY-ESO-1 (SPEAR T cells) in the setting of lymphodepletion. The ORR was 50% with 1 complete response and 5 partial responses; the median duration of response was 31 weeks. Correlative analysis demonstrated expansion and persistence of infused T cells for 200 d after therapy. A similar study was conducted with this therapy targeting NY-ESO-1 in myxoid/round cell LPS, with response rates of 20% in a reduced-dose lymphodepletion regimen and 40% in the standard-dose lymphodepletion cohort. Targeting known antigens in both synovial sarcoma and myxoid/round cell LPS using engineered T-cell therapies has shown promise even in patients who have been exposed to and progressed on multiple lines of therapy.

## Vaccine therapies against known tumor-associated antigens have mixed results
The use of a vaccine to induce an immune response to NY-ESO-1 in synovial sarcoma was trialed in a study by Somaiah et al. They utilized a prime-boost vaccine regimen called CMB305, with priming by a lentiviral vector encoding the NY-ESO-1 protein which targets dendritic cells (antigen-presenting cells), and boosting by NY-ESO-1 protein along with toll-like receptor 4 agonist. The trial had a 61.9% disease control rate and overall survival of 26.2 months. Overall, therapies targeting NY-ESO-1 in synovial sarcoma appear to have efficacy and promising results. However, not all targeted vaccine therapies have had success in sarcoma. A randomized phase II trial studied an adjuvant trivalent vaccine targeting commonly expressed gangliosides GM2, GD2 and GD3 in patients with sarcoma who had undergone metastasectomy and were disease-free at the time of enrollment. The vaccine was paired with an immunological adjuvant, OPT-821, which was also the control arm of the study. Investigators did not find significant difference in the recurrence-free survival or overall survival between the two arms.
Targeting specific tumor-associated antigens by using adoptive cellular therapies or other mechanisms such as vaccines is a promising endeavor in hard-to-treat sarcoma. This approach is particularly appealing in the translocation associated sarcoma subtypes which are more often genetically cold but are characterized by consistent changes, including fusion proteins, which may be targetable immunologically. Identification of such targetable antigens would be key to developing effective therapies in the future.

## Conclusions
Immunotherapy in soft tissue sarcoma is a promising field with unique challenges. With some exceptions, many subtypes of STS are immune quiescent and have immunosuppressive tumor microenvironments. Even within this broad categorization, variability in the tumor microenvironment can add inconsistency to results of studies which include many sarcoma histologies. UPS, DDLPS, ASPS, angiosarcoma and synovial sarcoma are a few that stand out as immunogenic or targetable histologies, with promising results leading to FDA approval of immunotherapies. On the other hand, there is evidence to suggest that immunotherapy does not work as well in some common histologies such as LMS, GIST, or rhabdomyosarcoma. In more uncommon histologies, there may not be any clear evidence of benefit of immunotherapy, however there is also insufficient evidence given the rarity of the diseases.
While many of the historical immunotherapy trials have focused on advanced, metastatic sarcoma that has progressed through standard of care therapies, there is emerging evidence of benefit of immunotherapy in the neoadjuvant setting in less advanced, resectable cases. There may also be benefit to administering immunotherapy early in the treatment course in advanced STS, as evidenced by the SAINT trial studying immunotherapy as a first-line treatment in those cases. Certainly, there may be limited advantage in initiating immunotherapy as a last resort – even though utilization of immunotherapy at end-of-life is increasing in certain malignancies, the benefits of its use have to be weighed in the context of its potential toxicities and the overall prognosis. 
In addition to immune checkpoint inhibitors, antibody-based therapies as well as adoptive cellular therapies are currently being investigated, with emphasis given to combination therapies that can act in synergy to enhance the immunogenicity and therefore susceptibility of tumors to immunotherapies. Injectable agents such as TVEC which are immune-modulating are also being studied; while none are approved as monotherapy for sarcoma, investigation of these agents in conjunction with other immunotherapy agents such as ICIs is ongoing.
Biomarkers for prognosis and treatment efficacy are also under investigation, as standard biomarkers used in other malignancies are not reliably predictive of efficacy or prognostic. Overall, the field of immunotherapy has yielded some successes and holds much promise in the treatment of this rare and hard-to-treat disease.
