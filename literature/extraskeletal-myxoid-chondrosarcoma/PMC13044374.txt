## Background
The isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH) family comprises three important metabolic enzymes that convert isocitrate to alpha-ketoglutarate (α-KG) via oxidative decarboxylation . The IDH enzymes play important roles in epigenetic regulation, DNA repair, and cellular metabolism and biosynthesis, including regulating cellular redox status, glucose sensing, lipogenesis, glutamine metabolism, and mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation , .
The three IDH enzymes differ in terms of their structure and location in the cell. The IDH2 and IDH3 enzymes are located in the mitochondria, whereas the IDH1 enzyme is located in the cytosol and peroxisomes . The IDH1 and IDH2 enzymes are obligate homodimers, which convert the oxidised form of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADP+) to the reduced form of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) . The IDH3 enzyme is a heterotetramer composed of two alpha, one beta and one gamma subunit, which converts the oxidised form of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+) to the reduced form of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NADH) . Synthesizing NADPH, required for lipid synthesis and reduction reactions, is the main function of IDH1 . IDH2 and IDH3 are involved in the tricarboxylic acid cycle, in which a sequence of important metabolic processes produce energy in the form of adenosine triphosphate (ATP) via the oxidation of acetyl coenzyme A from carbohydrates, fatty acids and proteins . Inhibition of IDH1 and IDH2 leads to reductions in NADPH, α-KG, glutathione, and the synthesis of deoxynucleotide triphosphate, and increases in reactive oxygen species .
Mutant IDH (mIDH) genes are associated with a variety of cancers, including but not limited to, cholangiocarcinoma (CCA), acute myeloid leukaemia (AML), glioma, and chondrosarcoma, and the incidences of mIDH in these cancers is approximately 13%, 33%, 73%, and 56%, respectively ( ) . However, different studies have reported wide variations in these incidences. In addition, there is a wide variation in the reported involvement of different mIDH single amino acid mutations, which are almost always heterozygous, in mIDH cancers ( ) , , , , . There is no evidence that the mIDH inhibitors developed to date inhibit any of these mutations preferentially. 
This article will discuss mIDH genes and their importance in each of these cancers. IDH3 is not frequently mutated in human cancers , and will not be discussed further.

## The role of mIDH in tumour initiation and progression
In mIDH cells, α-KG is converted into the functional oncometabolite 2-hydroxyglutarate (2-HG) in a process that consumes NADPH, instead of producing it, and generates NADP+ . 2-HG and α-KG are structurally similar except that the oxygen atom linked to the second carbon atom in α-KG is replaced by a hydroxyl group in 2-HG .
In wild type IDH cells, the endogenous 2-HG dehydrogenase enzyme catalyses the conversion of 2-HG to α-KG, preventing high levels of 2-HG from accumulating . However, 2-HG production by mIDH enzymes overwhelms the normal clearance mechanism leading to consistently increased 2-HG concentrations, which has been shown in serum samples from patients with mIDH glioma and AML , . 2-HG competitively inhibits α-KG from binding to the active site of histone and DNA demethylases . This leads to hypermethylation, which inhibits cellular differentiation and induces tumour cell proliferation, and contributes to tumorigenesis . In addition, lack of NADPH availability increases oxidative stress and 2-HG reduces sensitivity to alkylating agents, which are used to treat gliomas . The biological differences between the functions of IDH1 and IDH2 mean that their inhibition leads to some differences in outcomes, with inhibition of IDH1 leading to reduced lipid biosynthesis and inhibition of IDH2 leading to reduced ATP production .
Similarly, despite the presence of mIDH in many different tumours, the biological effects of the mutations are distinct in different cancers . One study found that the methylation and transcription patterns seen in mIDH gliomas were completely different to those in mIDH AML, CCA, and melanoma . This may explain why glioma is the only one of these cancers in which mIDH is associated with improved prognosis . The increased level of 2-HG also has other tumorigenic effects on cellular biology, including altered metabolism and chromatin restructuring, blocks to normal differentiation pattern , dysregulation of gene expression, alterations in the DNA damage repair pathway, inflammation, and cell death ( ) , . In addition, there is evidence that mIDH may collaborate with other mutations to promote oncogenesis in several tumour types . For example, mutations in IDH and Kirsten rat sarcoma viral oncogene homolog (KRAS) co-exist in cases of intrahepatic CCA (iCCA), and work together to promote liver progenitor cell proliferation, metastasis, and premalignant biliary lesion development . 
The reason that mIDH promotes tumour development in some tissues and not others is not clear, however mIDH subtypes have different 2-HG producing abilities . For example, mIDH1 R132C produces higher concentrations of 2-HG than mIDH1 R132H , and mIDH2 R172 produces higher concentrations of 2-HG than mIDH2 R140 . The concentration of 2-HG required to have an effect on tissue-specific enzymes and processes that promote tumour development differs with tumour type . To put this into context, 2-HG inhibits distinct α-KG-dependent dioxygenases differently, with higher concentrations of 2-HG needed to inhibit enzymes such as ten-eleven translocation 2 (TET2) .
Although the impact of IDH mutations is fundamentally related to 2-HG accumulation, the role of mIDH in tumour initiation is determined by the primary tumour location and its genetic and molecular profile.

## Importance of mIDH in CCA
CCA is a rare, aggressive disease that arises in the biliary tract , with an incidence of 1.6, 2.17, and <6 cases per 100,000 people in the USA, the UK, and worldwide, respectively , . Treatment options for patients with CCA are limited, and include surgery, chemotherapy, immunotherapy, and liver-limited local therapy depending on the stage of disease . CCA is the second most common primary liver tumour and represents 3% of gastrointestinal malignancies , with almost 75% of cases being non-resectable or metastatic upon presentation . The frequencies of CCA subtypes varies between publications, with one publication reporting that iCCA accounts for 10–20% of cases, while perihilar CCA (pCCA) and ductal CCA (dCCA) account for 50–60% and 20–30% of cases, respectively . Another publication also reported that iCCA accounted for 10–20% of CCA cases, however the proportion of cases attributed to pCCA (50%) and dCCA (30–40%) were slightly different .
A recent retrospective analysis of over 6000 patients with iCCA found that mIDH1 and mIDH2 occurred in 14.3% and 4% of cases, respectively . However, another recent study of 255 patients with iCCA reported a slightly higher frequency, with mIDH identified in approximately 16% of cases . mIDH1 and mIDH2 were found to be mutually exclusive in a recent comprehensive genomic profiling study of over 3000 patients with advanced iCCA .
A systematic literature review of patients with CCA found that the mean age of mIDH1 CCA diagnosis in 57 patients with data available was 59 years, and in 130 patients with gender data, more females than males were affected by mIDH1 CCA (66.2% versus 33.8%) . The prevalence of mIDH1 iCCA is significantly higher in non-Asian countries than in Asian countries, with a weighted mean of 16.5% compared to 8.8% . A more recent study of 125 patients with mIDH1 CCA reported similar demographics, as the median age at diagnosis was 59 years and more females were included than males . However, there is a lack of published information on the demographic characteristics of patients with mIDH2 CCA.
The most frequent co-mutations occurring in patients with mIDH1 and mIDH2 iCCA are AT-rich interaction domain 1A (ARID1A) (22.0% and 16.2%, respectively), BRCA1 associated protein 1 (BAP1) (15.5% and 29.7%, respectively), and polybromo 1 (PBRM1) (13.3% and 17.1%, respectively) , . In addition, the recent comprehensive genomic profiling study found that mutations in tumour protein P53 (TP53), cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor 2A (CDKN2A), and cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor 2B (CDKN2B) co-occurred in 12%, 20%, and 16% of mIDH1 iCCA cases, respectively . TP53, CDKN2A, and CDKN2B co-occur in 9%, 12%, and 9% of mIDH2 iCCA cases, respectively, and this is a lower frequency than occurs in wild type IDH CCA . Furthermore, microsatellite instability-high, tumour mutational burden, and programmed cell death ligand 1 (PD-L1) positivity are lower in patients with mIDH1/2 compared to patients with wild type IDH iCCA, indicating potential therapeutic targets for patients with mIDH iCCA .
The systematic literature review found that the mIDH1 gene was not associated with progression free survival or overall survival (OS) in CCA patients and another study found that the median OS in patients with iCCA was not significantly different between mIDH1/2 and wild type IDH patients . However, a recent real-world study reported the first evidence of mIDH1 being a negative prognostic marker in patients with advanced iCCA who progressed on first-line therapies . More data are needed to assess the impact of mIDH1/2 and frequently occurring co-mutations on prognosis.
mIDH1/2 represent targetable alterations in patients with CCA. Our knowledge of CCA is rapidly evolving as several targetable alterations have been identified , which has provided a better understanding of their real prevalence.

## Importance of early molecular testing in CCA
Almost 50% of CCA patients present with at least one targetable genetic alteration, such as mIDH1, fusion events involving fibroblast growth factor receptor 2 (FGFR2), and mutations in phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase catalytic subunit alpha (PIK3CA) . Therefore, it is important to carry out molecular testing using next generation sequencing (NGS) analysis to determine the treatment that should be administered . Due to the time needed to analyse NGS results and the potential need to repeat the biopsy, NGS should be carried out at diagnosis or at the earliest possible stage . Whilst waiting for the results of NGS, patients should continue with first-line chemotherapy and immunotherapy treatment . Molecular testing generates a mutation profile of the tumour, allowing clinicians to identify targetable alterations and implement a personalised treatment plan for the patient , for example, ivosidenib for mIDH1 CCA . One study reported that molecular profiling for CCA diagnosis was mainly used in clinical trials rather than in clinical practice, but that it should, and likely will, become routine practice in diagnosing biliary tract cancers .

## Importance of mIDH in AML
AML is a haematologic malignancy derived from haematopoietic stem cells , in which these cells proliferate and differentiate abnormally in the bone marrow . AML has an incidence of 4.0 per 100,000 people in the USA, which increases to 4.9 and 18.8 per 100,000 people aged 50–64 and >65 years, respectively, when incidence is age-adjusted .
Frequency of mIDH1 and mIDH2 AML varies in the literature, from approximately 6–16% and 8–19%, respectively , and these mutations are thought to be mutually exclusive . The prevalence of mIDH in AML tends to increase with age , with a median age of disease onset of 67 years in patients with mIDH AML . mIDH1 and mIDH2 are frequently found in AML patients presenting with diploid or other intermediate-risk cytogenetics, like trisomy 8, and a maintained platelet count . A recent study of 2668 adult AML patients found that the frequency of mIDH1 was higher at relapse than at diagnosis or when disease was refractory, and a similar trend was observed for mIDH2
 . A study that included 89 mIDH AML patients reported that R132C and R132H were the most frequent substitutions in mIDH1 AML, together accounting for 84.2% of mIDH1 cases . In addition, the study reported that the R140 codon was most frequently mutated in mIDH2 AML, accounting for 58.8% of mIDH2 cases, with R140Q reported as the most frequent R140 substitution .
The effect of mIDH on prognosis in AML patients is unclear, however, in general, mIDH1 is associated with poorer outcomes than mIDH2 in patients receiving standard intensive chemotherapy . However, in a real-world study, mIDH2 conferred more favourable clinical outcomes in AML patients treated with non-intensive chemotherapy compared to both mIDH1 and wild type IDH1/2
 .
Compared to patients with AML with any mIDH, paediatric and intermediate-age patients who have mIDH and a nucleophosmin 1 (NPM1) mutation have superior survival outcomes, whereas patients with mIDH without an NPM1 mutation have inferior survival outcomes .
Molecular testing plays a critical part in deciding subclassification of disease, risk stratification, and treatment decision, and the identification of mIDH impacts diagnosis and prognosis in mIDH AML patients . A recent survey of 72 AML care providers in community oncology clinics reported that 62% of care providers used molecular testing to identify mIDH1/2 in AML patients . Although there are several methods of molecular testing available, karyotyping and fluorescence in situ hybridization are recommended in AML .
mIDH has been shown to induce B-cell lymphoma 2 (BCL-2) dependence in AML . The BCL-2 protein reacts to oxidative stress and DNA damage by inhibiting programmed cellular apoptosis by making the mitochondrial membrane stable and inhibiting cytochrome c release and activation of caspase . In mIDH cells, increased 2-HG inhibits cytochrome c oxidase in the mitochondrial electron transport chain, leading to a lower threshold for the initiation of apoptosis when BCL-2 is inhibited . Therefore BCL-2 inhibition is a treatment option in mIDH AML .

## Importance of mIDH in glioma
Gliomas are the most frequent tumours of the central nervous system in adults and are categorised according to the cell type from which they arise into astrocytomas, oligodendrogliomas, or ependymomas, or according to the severity of the tumour into Central Nervous System World Health Organization (WHO) grades 1–4 gliomas . In 2018, the incidence of mIDH glioma in the USA was 0.7 per 100,000 people in the general population .
mIDH occurs in >68% of lower grade astrocytomas and oligodendrogliomas, with mIDH1 and mIDH2 accounting for 95% and 5% of these cases, respectively, and IDH1 R132H being the most frequently occurring mutation . mIDH is rarely found in lower grade ependymomas , . Most patients with mIDH glioma are young , with a median age at diagnosis of 36 and 38 years for WHO grades 2/3 and 4, respectively . The incidence of mIDH glioma decreases with increasing age and is higher in males than females, at a ratio of 1.3:1 . Astrocytoma is a subtype of mIDH glioma, in which the α-thalassemia retardation X-linked (ATRX) protein that is used to molecularly classify gliomas is absent , . The median age of diagnosis of mIDH1/mIDH2 astrocytoma and oligodendroglioma is 37 and 45 years, respectively . This contrasts with the median age of diagnosis of wild type IDH glioblastoma, which is 65 years, demonstrating the association between mIDH glioma and younger age .
mIDH is present early in glioma tumorigenesis, and is an important driver in low grade gliomas . However, in vivo models of glioma have found that IDH mutations alone are insufficient to promote tumour development . This means that mIDH needs to cooperate with other mutations, such as those in the telomerase reverse transcriptase (TERT) promoter, to promote oncogenesis .
Diffuse gliomas with mIDH1 or mIDH2 are classified as distinct disease entities in the 2021 WHO classification and are used as disease defining markers . In addition, mIDH1 and mIDH2 are actionable mutations and therefore are therapeutic targets. Taken together, assessment of mIDH in glioma is important for accurate diagnosis and evaluation of treatment options .
Most mIDH gliomas initially present as non-contrast-enhancing tumours , and these tumours are thought to have a better prognosis and survival rates compared to enhancing gliomas . However, these gliomas eventually progress, develop contrast enhancement, and transform to a more aggressive form with neovascularization and poor outcomes , .

## Importance of mIDH in chondrosarcoma
Chondrosarcoma constitutes a group of diverse primary malignant bone tumours that develop in cartilage cells , accounting for approximately 20–30% of all bone sarcomas . There are five main types of chondrosarcomas; conventional, dedifferentiated, mesenchymal, clear cell, and extraskeletal myxoid .
Only conventional and dedifferentiated chondrosarcomas can carry mIDH
 , which occur in approximately 40–65% and 40–80% of patients with these chondrosarcomas, respectively , . There are limited treatment options for chondrosarcomas and the different types can be difficult to distinguish with radiological assessment, therefore expert histological opinion from a sarcoma pathologist should be sought when diagnosing a patient with chondrosarcoma .
A recent meta-analysis of 14 studies including 488 patients with chondrosarcoma reported that mIDH1 and mIDH2 were found in 38.7% and 12.1% of patients, respectively . Of those with mIDH1/2 chondrosarcoma, more males (62.7%) are affected than females (37.3%), and the mean age at diagnosis is 57.2 years . This meta-analysis also found that mIDH1/2 were associated with a significantly higher risk of death than wild type IDH in chondrosarcoma, as patient deaths were reported in 47.8% of mIDH1/2 patients compared to 33.3% of wild type IDH patients . However, our understanding of mIDH chondrosarcoma is not as advanced as that of other mIDH tumours, and the role of mIDH in later stages of this disease is not clear .
Surgery is the only curative treatment for mIDH chondrosarcoma, as the tumours are inherently resistant to chemotherapy and radiotherapy . However, not all patients are eligible for surgery, therefore effective treatments for mIDH tumours are needed . Effective mutation-targeted treatments for these mIDH chondrosarcomas are emerging, including ivosidenib and enasidenib , , , and if approved, could potentially provide patients with mIDH chondrosarcoma with more effective treatment options.
6.1

## Conventional chondrosarcoma
Conventional chondrosarcoma accounts for 85–90% of all chondrosarcoma cases , and in approximately 90% of conventional chondrosarcoma cases, the tumour is of a low or intermediate grade (grade 1 or 2) . Atypical cartilaginous tumours are the benign counterpart of conventional chondrosarcoma that have the potential to become a more aggressive grade 1 chondrosarcoma .
Conventional chondrosarcoma occurs more frequently in males than females and patients are usually between 40 and 60 years old at diagnosis . The 10-year OS of patients is 88.6% in low grade and 67.7% in non-low-grade conventional chondrosarcoma , therefore OS is shorter in cases of more severe disease. However, in contrast to the meta-analysis in chondrosarcoma patients mentioned previously, another study reported that IDH mutation status had no statistically significant impact on prognosis in conventional chondrosarcoma patients, therefore the effect of mIDH on prognosis in this subtype remains unclear . This study also found that IDH1 R132C was the most frequent mutation in the mIDH patients (63%) followed by IDH1 R132G and IDH2 R172S, both occurring in 17% of mIDH patients .

6.2

## Dedifferentiated chondrosarcoma
Dedifferentiated chondrosarcoma is rarer than conventional chondrosarcoma, accounting for <5% of chondrosarcoma cases , . Dedifferentiated chondrosarcoma tumours consist of two histopathological parts: a low-grade chondrosarcoma tumour and a high-grade non-cartilaginous sarcoma tumour . This chondrosarcoma can occur spontaneously, or the high-grade part of the tumour can develop from a pre-existing low-grade chondrosarcoma, in which some of the cells have dedifferentiated . mIDH occurs in most dedifferentiated chondrosarcoma cases , .
A study that included a patient with mIDH2 dedifferentiated chondrosarcoma reported a reduction in 2-HG levels in the urine and plasma after surgical resection, demonstrating the efficacy of surgery in this tumour type, however some mIDH dedifferentiated chondrosarcoma patients are not suitable for surgical resection .

## Conclusion
mIDH1/2 are metabolic oncogenes that may cooperate with other mutations to initiate and drive oncogenesis in multiple haematologic and solid tumours. mIDH plays a crucial role in reprogramming cellular metabolism, inhibiting cellular differentiation, and contributing to tumorigenesis in several cancers, most frequently in those discussed here. mIDH acts as an important targetable alteration for clinicians to consider when deciding on treatment plans for patients with mIDH cancers. Although our understanding of mIDH cancers has developed in recent years, a better understanding of tissue-specific mechanisms of tumorigenesis and the effect of mIDH on prognosis is required. More mIDH targeted therapies are needed to improve the outcomes of patients with mIDH cancers.

2-hydroxyglutaratea functional oncometabolite produced by the reduction of alpha-ketoglutarate that contributes to oncogenesis, if accumulated.
Alpha-ketoglutaratean intermediary metabolite in the tricarboxylic cycle that functions as an energy donor, a precursor in the amino acid biosynthesis, a signalling molecule, and a regulator of epigenetic processes and cellular signalling via protein binding.
ARID1Aa gene that encodes a protein that is involved in regulating gene expression by chromatin remodelling.
ATRXa gene that regulates the DNA damage response and prevents chromosomal instability.
BAP1a gene that encodes a nuclear-localized deubiquitinating protein that regulates cellular proliferation.
BCL-2a gene that encodes a protein that inhibits apoptosis.
CDKN2Aa gene that encodes a protein that regulates cell growth, division, and apoptosis.
CDKN2Ba gene that encodes a protein that regulates cell growth by inhibiting G1 progression in the cell cycle.
FGFR2a gene that produces a protein involved in several processes including cell division and maturation.
Heterotetramera protein composed of four subunits where one or more subunits differ.
Homodimera protein composed of two polypeptide chains that are identical in the order, number, and kind of their amino acid residues.
Isocitrate dehydrogenasea family of enzymes that catalyse oxidative decarboxylation of isocitrate into α-ketoglutarate by using nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD) or NAD phosphate (NADP) as an electron acceptor to yield NADH or NADPH, respectively. These enzymes play important roles in cellular metabolism and cancer.
KRASan oncogene that encodes the KRAS protein, which is involved in the regulation of cell division. KRAS gene mutations impair the ability of the KRAS protein to switch between active and inactive states, leading to cell transformation and increased resistance to chemotherapy.
Microsatellite instability-highdescribes cancer cells that have a defect in the ability to correct errors that occur when DNA is copied in the cell.
Next generation sequencinga technology for determining the sequence of DNA or RNA to study genetic variation associated with diseases.
NPM1a gene that codes for a nucleus-cytoplasmic shuttling protein that carries out multiple functions, including regulation of centrosome duplication, ribosome biogenesis and export, histone assembly, maintenance of genomic stability and response to nucleolar stress.
PBRM1a gene that encodes a protein that is involved in transcriptional activation and repression of select genes by chromatin remodelling. The protein acts as a negative regulator of cell proliferation.
PD-L1a protein that suppresses the adaptive immune response. It is considered a pro-tumorigenic factor in cancer cells via binding to its receptors and activating proliferative and survival signalling pathways.
PIK3CAa gene that produces a protein that is a component of the PI3K enzyme, which is involved in cell growth, proliferation, and differentiation.
Reactive oxygen speciesfree radicals containing oxygen that are highly unstable and reactive. A build-up of reactive oxygen species in cells can lead to DNA, RNA, and protein damage, and possibly cell death.
TERTa gene that encodes the telomerase protein that maintains telomeres, which are segments of DNA at the end of chromosomes.
TET2a gene that encodes a protein that alters the epigenetic status of DNA by oxidising 5-methycytosine to 5-hydroxymethylcytosine.
TP53a gene that encodes a protein that regulates cell division and apoptosis.
