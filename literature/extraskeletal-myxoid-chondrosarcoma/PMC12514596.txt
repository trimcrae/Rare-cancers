## Introduction
Malignant melanoma (MM) is a malignant tumor that arises from melanocytes and represents the most aggressive cutaneous malignancy with the poorest prognosis among all skin cancers ( ). Although MM ranks as the third most common type of skin cancer after basal cell carcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma, it has the worst prognosis among all skin cancers ( ). Typical macroscopic features of MM include brown-to-black elevated tumors on the skin with in situ lesions in the surrounding area ( ). From a histological and developmental perspective, MM includes several subtypes, such as Spitz and desmoplastic ( , ). However, MM with myxoid changes is not currently classified as a distinct subtype. This condition is rare and its diagnosis is extremely challenging ( , ). While MM is generally considered relatively straightforward to diagnose, MM with myxoid changes presents significant diagnostic challenges ( ). Additionally, magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) features such as T1-weighted imaging (T1WI) hyperintensity reflect the presence of melanin within the tissue and bone invasion is considered rare ( , ). We report a case of MM with myxoid changes, which presented as a clinical manifestation of a soft tissue tumor without any visible black masses on the skin. This case presented diagnostic challenges based on imaging findings, which raised the suspicion of sarcoma with lymph node metastasis.

## Case presentation
A 72-year-old woman presented with swelling of the right great toe and nail loss for 4 months, along with pain starting one month prior to presentation. No apparent tumor lesions were observed on the skin of the right great toe. However, circumferential swelling, particularly pronounced on the lateral side, redness, and pain were observed. Tinel’s sign was negative, suggesting a low possibility of nerve tumors. Moreover, flexion and extension of the toes were nearly impossible. Relatively rapid progression and range of motion limitations due to pain may suggest malignancy rather than benignity ( ). Her medical history included hypertension, diabetes mellitus, and hypothyroidism, for which she was on medication. Blood tests revealed normal C-reactive protein (0.03 mg/dL, normal range, 0.02–0.5 mg/dL) and alkaline phosphatase (77 U/L, normal range, 38–113 U/L) levels, and borderline elevated white blood cell level of 9,500/µL (normal range, 4,000–8,000/µL). Radiography ( ) demonstrated scalloping in the distal phalanx but no calcification of the soft tissues or periosteal reactions. Furthermore, MRI revealed heterogeneous iso- to low signal intensity on T1WI ( ) and fat-suppressed T1WI ( ) compared to the dermis, without evidence of fatty components. A heterogeneous high to isointense signal was observed on T2-weighted imaging (T2WI) ( ) and fat-suppressed T2WI ( ). Contrast-enhanced fat-suppressed T1WI ( ) revealed uneven enhancement effects with findings suggesting degeneration, necrosis, and hemorrhage in areas lacking enhancement. Based on these MRI findings, a tumor characterized by a heterogeneous composition of myxoid and fibrous components was suspected, leading to the initial diagnosis of soft tissue sarcoma. Fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG)-positron emission tomography/computed tomography (PET/CT) revealed bone destruction in the distal phalanx of the toe due to infiltration ( ), and the tumor showed heterogeneous FDG accumulation, with a maximum standardized uptake value (SUVmax) of 5.27 ( ). Additionally, FDG accumulation with an SUVmax of 7.75 was observed in the intermuscular region of the right thigh, suggesting lymph node or soft tissue metastasis originating from the toe tumor ( ). Based on these findings, a primary sarcoma of the right great toe and a metastatic tumor in the right thigh were suspected, considering diagnoses such as malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumor (MPNST), myxoid fibrosarcoma, extraskeletal chondrosarcoma, or clear cell sarcoma (CCS), depending on the site of origin. A biopsy was performed for a definitive diagnosis. The histopathological findings from the biopsy specimen indicated schwannoma, myxoid schwannoma, atypical fibroxanthoma, MPNST, CCS, and melanoma. However, none were typical, and a definitive diagnosis could not be made. Considering the imaging and clinical findings, sarcoma was suspected, and a wide excision was planned. Because the tumor extended near the proximal phalanx of the second toe, amputation of the great toe and second toe at the metatarsal level was performed, with a 1 cm margin ensured. Histopathological findings by resected specimens showed that polygonal to spindle-shaped atypical cells had proliferated in the dermis, with some showing mitotic figures ( ). The tumor stroma contained abundant alcian blue-positive myxoid components and partial invasion of the bone was observed ( ). Immunohistochemical findings were as follows: positive for S-100, MITF (focally), PRAME (focally), and HMB45 (a few), and negative for Melan A (mostly) ( ). Moreover, the surgical margins were negative. No split EWSR1 signal was observed via fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) ( ). Based on these findings, melanoma was diagnosed. After the definitive diagnosis, the thigh lesion was resected. Interestingly, many CD68-positive macrophages engulfing melanin were identified in the medial thigh; however, the tumor cells were negative. Consequently, the tumor stages were T4a, N0, M0, and Stage IIb. The BRAF V600E mutation was negative, and postoperative adjuvant therapy with anti-PD-1 antibodies is currently underway. Six months after surgery, no recurrence or metastasis was observed. ## Discussion
MM arises from melanocytes, which are found not only in the skin but also in various other locations, such as the choroid and mucous membranes. However, most cases originate from the skin, with the lower extremities being the most common sites of occurrence ( , ). The etiology of melanoma is complex and involves both genetic and environmental factors. While UV exposure is a well-known significant risk factor, other risk factors include male sex, high nevus count, and atypical nevi ( , ). Notably, melanoma is the third most common skin tumor after basal cell carcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma and accounts for approximately 1% of all skin tumors. However, it has the highest mortality rate among skin tumors. Therefore, early detection through skin examination is critical, and algorithms such as ABCD and CASH have been proposed to differentiate melanoma from benign conditions ( ). Here, although the local findings included redness and swelling, no elevated tumors or nevi suggestive of melanoma were observed. Instead, the condition was initially suspected to be soft tissue sarcoma.
Melanoma is classified into 9–10 subtypes based on the clinical features, sites of occurrence, and pathological characteristics ( , ). Although not included in this classification, myxoid changes in MM have also been proposed, although rare, and are reported to be variants of amelanotic, spindle cell, and desmoplastic types; however, a consensus has yet to be established ( ). While the diagnosis is considered relatively straightforward in many cases of MM, MM with myxoid changes is considered difficult to diagnose. Myxoid changes were also observed in the melanocytic nevi. Furthermore, benign and malignant soft tissue tumors such as neural tumors (schwannoma, MPNST, and neurofibroma), myxoid liposarcoma, chondrosarcoma, and myxofibrosarcoma also exhibit myxoid areas ( ). In the present case, the differential diagnosis during biopsy included a wide range of conditions from benign to malignant: schwannoma, myxoid neurothekeoma, atypical fibroxanthoma, MPNST, CCS, and melanoma. When combining radiological findings for differential diagnosis, malignancy, particularly CCS and MPNST, is considered crucial ( ). Differentiating from CCS based on pathological and immunohistochemistry findings is challenging. CCS is characterized by fusion genes such as EWSR1-ATF1 and EWSR1-CREB1, and analysis using FISH or polymerase chain reaction is highly effective ( ). Differentiation from MPNST can also be challenging because the positivity rates for SOX10 and S100 are 84% and 52%, respectively, overlapping with those for melanoma, necessitating caution during diagnosis ( ). Furthermore, the loss of H3K27me2, a characteristic feature of MPNST, can also be observed in melanoma, thus requiring careful consideration ( ).
The characteristic MRI findings of melanoma include T1 hyperintensity and T2 hypointensity due to melanin. When melanin is absent, T1 shows isointense to low signal intensity, and T2 exhibits high signal intensity, with no definitive characteristic findings reported ( ). It has been reported that T1 hyperintensity is observed in approximately 50% of the cases ( ). Other MRI features of melanoma include edema in the surrounding fat, unclear deep margins, and heterogeneously high T2 signal intensity within the tumor, with positivity rates of 75%, 69%, and 63%, respectively. However, because these findings are also observed in other diseases, careful interpretation is required ( ). Bone invasion is extremely rare (approximately 6%) ( ). In this case, MRI findings suggested the possibility of a myxoid tumor. The differential diagnoses were broad and encompassed benign and malignant neurogenic tumors, acral fibromyxoma, myxoid fibrosarcoma, and chondrosarcoma. Ultimately, myxoid changes were observed in the pathological findings, which were consistent with the imaging results. Moreover, bone destruction and PET/CT findings provide a rational basis for considering sarcoma and its metastasis.
The principal treatment of MM is surgery, with wide excision being the standard approach, as in the case of soft tissue sarcomas. A safety margin of 1–2 cm was proposed ( ). Sentinel lymph node biopsy is also performed for staging, particularly for T2 or higher stages and even for high-risk T1 patients ( ). Additionally, PET/CT is useful for staging and evaluating treatment effectiveness. However, false positives and negatives have been reported, and caution is required ( ). In this case, although FDG accumulation was observed on PET/CT, no lymph node metastases were identified histologically. Recent reports from Japan have indicated favorable outcomes for Stage I MM cases, with a 5-year disease-specific survival (DSS) rate of >96.4% and a 10-year DSS survival rate of >93.9%. In contrast, Stage IV cases show extremely low survival rates, with 5-year survival rate of 26.2% and 10-year survival rate of 14% ( ). In recent years, the emergence of immune checkpoint inhibitors and BRAF/MEK inhibitors has led to comparatively better results, even in Stage IV cases ( ).

## Conclusions
In conclusion, we report a challenging case of melanoma with myxoid changes based on the clinical, imaging, and pathological findings. Melanoma and sarcoma can be challenging to diagnose, and melanomas with myxoid changes are particularly difficult to identify. Accurate diagnosis based on clinical and imaging findings is not possible, and the utility of pathological findings is unquestionable. However, it is essential to consider the presence of such variants carefully and proceed with a cautious diagnosis.
