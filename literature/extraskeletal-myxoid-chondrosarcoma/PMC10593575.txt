## Highlights
•We identified 64 unique fusion partners. Rare fusion partners were identified more often in adults than children and included additional members of the ETS and CREB TF families. The remaining RFPs were classified as other transcription factors and non-transcription factors.
•Five genes were identified to be altered at a higher prevalence in rare fusion partner tumors including CCNE1, KRAS, MYC, PIK3CA, and RAD21. Pathway enrichment associated alterations in cell cycle control and DNA damage response genes as driving the differences between fusion partners.
•Potentially clinically actionable genomic variants were more prevalent in tumors harboring rare fusion partners than common fusions. Commonly altered genes associated with targetability included PIK4CA, CHEK2, ATM, EZH2, and KRAS.

EWSR1 fusions are highly promiscuous and are associated with unique malignancies, clinical phenotypes, and molecular subtypes. However, rare fusion partners (RFP) of EWSR1 has not been well described. Here, we conducted a cross-sectional, retrospective study of 1,140 unique tumors harboring EWSR1 fusions. We identified 64 unique fusion partners. RFPs were identified more often in adults than children. Alterations in cell cycle control and DNA damage response genes as driving the differences between fusion partners. Potentially clinically actionable genomic variants were more prevalent in tumors harboring RFP than common fusions. While the data presented here is limited, tumors harboring RFP of EWSR1 may represent molecularly distinct entities and may benefit from further molecular testing to identify targeted therapeutic options.

## Introduction
Gene fusions have been identified across numerous malignancies and contribute to oncogenesis through a variety of molecular mechanisms [ , ]. Fusions involving proto-oncogenes may amplify the molecular signaling of the genes involved, while fusions involving tumor suppressor genes commonly act by down regulating the activity of the genes involved . A third category of oncogenic fusions includes fusions involving transcription factor genes, representing a complex oncogenic mechanism that is yet to be fully understood . Fusions involving the transcription factor EWS RNA Binding Protein 1 (EWSR1) are drivers of a diverse set of malignancies including Ewing sarcoma (EwS) as well as various other tumors , , . The EWS protein maintains a variety of roles in normal cells including epigenetic regulation of gene transcription as well as RNA processing . Therefore, fusions involving the EWSR1 gene can lead to complex transcriptional and phenotypic changes when dysregulated in cancers.
EWSR1 fusions are highly promiscuous leading to a large spectrum of fusion partners . The most common and well-studied fusion partners for EWSR1 belong to the ETS family of transcription factors. In contrast, rare fusion partners (RFP) are less understood and characterize uncommon malignancies - each associated with unique phenotypes . We have previously described that common EWSR1 fusion partners demonstrate specific predilections for secondary genomic variants unique to each specific fusion partner . To better understand rare EWRS1 fusion partners, we present the largest genomic exploration of rare EWSR1 fusion partner genes to date. We assess the prevalence of RFP as well as their molecular correlates.

## Materials and methods

## Clinical and genomic profiling data
Foundation Medicine Incorporated (FMI) provided summary genomic data for this study. This study is reported in accordance with the Strengthening the Reporting of Genetic Association Studies (STREGA) reporting guideline . Genomic profiling data from EWSR1 fusion positive tumors was collected as assayed during clinical care using FMI's hybrid-capture-based next-generation sequencing (NGS) platform as previously described , , . Approval for the retrospective collection of genomic data from FMI, including a waiver of informed consent and a HIPAA waiver of authorization, was obtained from the Western Institutional Review Board (protocol: 20,152,817). Additional local Institutional Review Board approval was obtained at the University of Florida for analysis of the included data (protocol: 202,101,122). EWSR1 fusions and individual partner genes were identified by RNA sequencing. From this database, microsatellite instability (MSI), tumor mutation burden (TMB), and pathogenicity of genomic variants were determined utilizing FMI's analysis pipeline. Only known or likely pathogenic genomic variants (pathogenic variants) were included in this analysis. Fusions expected to be non-functional were not included in the dataset. Pathogenic genomic variants in EWSR1 fusion positive tumors, secondary to the EWSR1 fusion, were referred to as fusion-associated secondary genomic variants (FASGV). De-identified participant characteristics and genomic data were extracted for this study. Cohort size was based on available data and sample size calculations were not performed.

## Statistical analysis
Data was analyzed in Rv.4.1.1 or Graphpad Prismv.9.2.0. We defined rare EWSR1 fusion partners as prevalent in <1 % of the dataset based on prior established cutoffs of rare fusion prevelance . Fusion partners were further classified by transcription factor (TF) status and class as determined using TF Class . Targetable genomic variants were determined using Memorial Sloan-Kettering (MSK) OncoKB Level 1 evidence . To compare categorical variables, Fisher's exact tests were used to generate p-values. Principle component analysis (PCA) was conducted using the FactoMineRv2.3 package in R. Two-tailed p-values ≤ 0.05 were considered statistically significant. P-value correction for multiplicity was conducted using the Dunn's multiple comparison test.

## Results

## Clinical and pathologic characteristics of cohort
A total of 1140 unique tumors harboring an EWSR1 fusion were identified. Males compromised a majority of the population (n = 679, 59.6 %). Reported histologies included primarily sarcomas, both soft tissue – not otherwise specified (n = 471, 41.3 %) and Ewing (n = 452, 39.6 %). Additional histologic designations were significantly less common, with the next-most prevalent histology being cancer of unknown primary (n = 39, 3.4 %). Extended histologic and pathology data is available in Table S1. The median age at sequencing was 31 years (range, 0–89). Patients were delineated into three age categories for this study including pediatric (age 0–15 years, n = 198, 17.4 %), adolescent and young adult (AYA, age 15–39 years, n = 505, 44.3 %), and older adults (age 40–89 years, n = 430, 37.7 %). TMB was low as anticipated, with an interquartile range of 0.8–2.5 mut/Mb. No tumors exhibited MSI. Complete demographic data is available in . 

## Identification of rare EWSR1 fusions
Among the 1140 tumors in this study, we identified 74 unique fusion partners to EWSR1 ( A). These included well-characterized fusion partners such as members of the ETS family (FLI1/ERG; n = 496, 43.5 %) [ , ], members of the CREB family (ATF1/CREB1/CREB3L1/CREM; n = 249, 21.8 %) , WT1 (n = 151, 13.2 %) , NR4A3 (n = 88, 7.7 %) , PATZ1 (n = 15, 1.3 %) , , , and NFATC2 (n = 14, 1.2 %) [ , , ] genes. Many of these well-characterized fusion partners are generally considered pathognomonic for specific malignancies such EWSR1-WT1 and desmoplastic small round cell tumor (DSRCT) . As our focus was to identify the rare EWSR1 fusion partners, we chose a cutoff for rare fusion partners that correlated with the long tail of counts of fusion partners ( B). Using this inflection point, we defined RFP as those comprising less than 1 % of the fusions present in this dataset. This cutoff distinguished gene fusions previously molecularly characterized (as named above) from RFP which have not yet been characterized. Using this cutoff, we reduced the number of RFPs to 64. While some RFP have been previously reported in the literature, many RFP had not been previously reported (Table S2). The RFPs identified included additional members of the ETS (ETS1, ETV1, ETV4 [ , ], FEV
 , , ) and CREB (CREB3L2 [ , ], CREB3L3 [ , ], CREB3L4) TF families ( ). The remaining RFPs were classified as other transcription factors (Other (TF) – Rare; n = 53) and non-transcription factors (Other (non-TF) – Rare; n = 49). Demographic features were similar between RFPs and common fusion partners of similar gene families (ETS, CREB, Other; Figure S1). Together, this data represents the largest assessment of EWSR1 fusions, including many RFP, which are yet to be reported in the literature. 

## Fusion-Associated secondary genomic variants (FASGV) in EWSR1 RFP tumors
In order to assess the genomic landscape of the tumors selected for this study, we focused our attention on genomic variants identified in addition to the driving EWSR1 fusion present in each tumor (hereafter, Fusion-Associated Secondary Genomic Variants (FASGV)). We had previously described that FASGV were often associated with different EWSR1 fusions . In order to uncover whether different FASGV were associated with RFPs compared to more common fusion partners, we incorporated genomic data from each tumor into our analysis. From the 1140 unique tumors harboring a EWSR1 fusion, we identified 1717 total FASGVs. We limited the genomic variants to only include well-described oncogenic variants . In 357 (31.3 %) tumors, the EWSR1 fusion was the only genomic variant identified (0 FASGV). The total number of FASGV in each tumor by fusion partner varied (p<0.0001, Fig. S2). To highlight the FASGV present in RFPs, we selected genes highly altered for each common fusion partner as well as across all RFP ( A). Five genes were identified to be altered at a higher prevalence in RFP including CCNE1, KRAS, MYC, PIK3CA, and RAD21 ( B). These data suggest that the genomes of RFP may differ from those of more common EWSR1 fusions. 

## Genomic landscape of EWSR1 fusion partner tumors
To define the genomic landscapes of EWSR1 fusion positive tumors, we first determined the frequency of FASGV by fusion partner prevalence and transcription factor family for further analysis. To determine whether there were natural clusters of RFPs using similarities among their FASGV, we conducted principal component analysis (PCA) on the FASGV prevalence in each EWSR1 fusion partner ( A). Visually, RFP were demonstrated to be distinct from common fusion partners. To further determine the differences between genomic landscapes, we condensed the prevalence of non-fusion, pathogenic genomic variants (FASGV) by transcription factor family (ETS, CREB, Other). Using a similar approach as described above, we conducted PCA on the secondary genomic variants across the dataset, this time delineated by transcription factor family membership (ETS, CREB, Other) and partner prevalence (common/rare) ( B). Along the first PCA dimension, Other (non-TF) - Rare fusion partners were highly distinct from other fusion partners. The second PCA dimension identified WT1, NFATC2, PATZ1, CREB-rare, and ETS-rare fusion partners as highly distinct. To better assess which genes alterations were responsible for the variable genomic landscapes between EWSR1 fusion partners, we first surveyed the top genes associated with the first three dimensions of the PCA (Fig. S3). Contribution to PCA dimension 1 was driven by genomic variants in KRAS, TET2, CCND2, FGF2, and FLT3; dimension 2 by NOTCH2, KMT2D, SPEN, ZRSR2, and BCL2; and dimension 3 by RICTOR, NF1, TOP1, AKT3, VHL, and MTOR. 

## Prevalence of potentially targetable genomic variants in tumors harboring rare fusion partners
To determine the potential clinical importance of the genomic differentiation present in tumors harboring RFP, we used MSK OncoKB to assign clinical actionability to each p FASGV present in the cohort. Given the rarity of the cancers included in our dataset, we selected the highest level of evidence for each gene-drug pair available in OncoKB regardless of tumor histology. Targetable FASGV were more prevalent in tumors harboring RFPs than common fusion partners ( A) for any MSK OncoKB evidence level (Common 28.5 %, Rare 46.5 %, p = 6.1 × 10−05). We noticed a similar trend using MSK OncoKB Level 1 evidence (Common 11.0 %, Rare 29.1 %, p = 3.8 × 10−07). Noteworthy, these Level 1 targetable FASGV were most common in Other (non-TF) fusion partners (46.9 %) but were prevalent across fusion partners ( B). Commonly altered genes associated with Level 1 targetability included PIK4CA, CHEK2, ATM, EZH2, and KRAS ( C). Notably, eleven BRCA1/2 genomic variants were identified, five of which were in tumors harboring EWSR1::FLI1 fusions, and four were in tumors harboring EWSR1::WT1 fusions. It is important to note that tumors harboring RFPs exhibited a higher TMB compared to those harboring common fusions (Common 1.8 mut/Mb, Rare 2.8mut/Mb, p = 0.0006). While the difference in the number of mutations is modest, it is possible that tumors harboring RFPs are more genomically unstable or are not solely driven by the identified fusion. Either option may explain the increased prevalence of targetable mutations in tumors harboring RFPs and warrant further investigation. This data may suggest that molecular analysis of RFP tumors can provide clinical actionability to guide therapeutic decision-making for patients without access to good standard of care therapies. 

## Discussion
Common EWSR1 fusions are pathognomonic for specific diseases. Established disease defining fusions such as Ewing sarcoma (FLI1/ERG/FEV), desmoplastic round cell tumors (WT1), extraskeletal myxoid chondrosarcomas (NR4A3), clear cell sarcomas (ATF1/CRE1/CREM), and low-grade fibromyxoid sarcoma/sclerosing epithelioid fibrosarcoma (CREB3L1/CREB3L2) are well-described . Furthermore, recent studies have increased our knowledge of more rare EWSR1 fusions, such as EWSR1::PATZ1
 and EWSR1::NFATc2
 , as potentially novel malignancies. There remains a need to explore the clinical impact, which can be made by furthering our knowledge of RFP.
While different partners to EWSR1 clearly result in molecularly unique malignancies, RFP are typically grouped with common EWSR1 fusions based on tumor histology or the molecular classification of the fusion partner gene [ , ]. The data presented here suggests that RFP may have additional subgroupings that are distinct from more common EWSR1 fusions. The data presented here is limited in the histology and pathology available; therefore, interpretation of these results as diagnostic refinement should be measured. While we do not argue that the data here proves diagnostic distinction, we do believe that the unique genomic landscapes identified here suggest that RFP may represent unique molecular entities. It is also important to note that this data is not able to determine the driving event for any of the tumors presented here. In the cases where the fusion is the sole identified genomic event, we may presume that it could be the primary driver; however, if multiple genomic events are identified we are not able to determine driver versus passenger status. This limitation may be a vital covariate in the unique molecular landscapes identified in RFP if some fusions identified are in fact passenger fusions not responsible for driving pathogenesis. It is unclear if the unique RFP represent a clinically distinct phenotype; however, the molecular distinction is striking. With more data, prospective sequencing of EWSR1 fusions may lead to further refinement of disease diagnosis and therapy selection .
We also identified a significant number of potentially therapeutically targetable FASGV in tumors harboring RFP. The targetable genomic variants in tumors harboring RFP are clinically exciting as few therapeutic agents have activity in EWSR1 fusion driven tumors. The potential to identify individualized therapy for patients with these cancer could represent significant clinical actionability. At this point it is unclear what molecular mechanism is responsible for this increased prevalence of targetable FASGV. Given the prevalence of potentially targetable FASGV in tumors harboring RFP, it may be beneficial to molecularly characterize EWSR1 fusion harboring tumors at an early stage in clinical care. While the high prevalence of potentially actionable variants may ultimately be used to select therapies for patients, targeted trials to prospectively test their clinical actionability will be needed to develop new treatments.
To our knowledge, this is the largest genomic assessment of EWSR1 fusion positive tumors to date; however, limitations inherent to retrospective genomic studies apply here. As commercial tumor sequencing is biased toward late stage disease, our data available may represent more aggressive diseases as these are more commonly sequenced during clinical care. Additionally, our data is skewed towards AYA and older adult patients, despite the high frequency of EWSR1 fusion positive tumors in pediatric patients. This may reflect the lower use of genomic sequencing in pediatric populations compared to older patients. This dataset also lacks the clinical and pathologic variables necessary to fully incorporate molecular pathology and prognosis into our analysis; therefore, interpretation of our data for the use of diagnostic refinement or clinical management of RFP tumors is not appropriate. Our data was collected with a focus on EWSR1 fusions in mind. Because of this, our analysis is unable to compare the genomes of the RFP tumors presented here with other potentially relevant fusions. Gene fusions which used to be considered in similar molecular families as traditional EWSR1 fusions, such as CIC::DUX4 and BCOR::CCNB3, or alternative fusion partners such as FUS which is a common alternative partner in place of EWSR1. Comparison of the genomes of these fusion driven cancers is warranted. Finally, while our data suggests that there may be clinical actionability to be had in genomic sequencing these tumors, validation including extensive correlation with histologic and pathologic data is necessary. Prior studies have demonstrated the potential in aiding clinical decision making in sarcomas, either through identifying targeted treatment strategies as well as the potential for diagnosis refinement [ , , ]. The findings in this study should be validated prospectively to determine their clinical import. Efforts to pool clinical and molecular data through consortia, similar to the GRACefUl study (Global collaboRAtive study of CIC-rearranged, BCOR::CCNB3-rearranged and other ultra-rare unclassified undifferentiated small round cell sarcomas), should be encouraged to extend our knowledge regarding rare EWSR1 fusions.

## Conclusion
Tumors harboring rare EWSR1 fusions may represent a molecularly distinct groups of diseases. The clinical application of this molecular data, when combined with histology and pathology findings, necessitates further inquiry. These rare EWSR1 fusions are also enriched with secondary alterations that may be clinically useful to select targeted therapeutic agents for treatment. Further research is needed to assess whether this targetability translates into clinical benefit.
