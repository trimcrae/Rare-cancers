## Background
The hypoxic status of cancer tumour is disparate within tumour microenvironment and is considered as the main cause of treatment resistance. Chondrosarcoma, a type of bone cancer, combines heterogonous tumour microenvironment and resistance to therapies. We aimed to image tumour microenvironment response to hypoxia activated prodrug therapy with Evofosfamide (TH-302) on a rat chondrosarcoma model thanks to an original multimodal imaging strategy. Bimodal imaging strategy consisted of nuclear imaging and MRI on TH-302 treated and control animals. To monitor TH-302 effects on tumour growth and structure, we measured T2 and ADC by MRI and we used 18F-FDG to explore tumour metabolism. 18F-FMISO and APT to Guanidyl ratio CEST MRI evaluated hypoxic status. Proteoglycans in the extracellular matrix were explored in vivo by a radiotracer targeting proteoglycans (99mTc-NTP 15 − 5) and GAG CEST MRI. Immunohistochemistry, biochemical assays and RNA sequencing were performed to confirm in vivo observations.

## Results
TH-302 demonstrated a strong efficacy on tumour structure with a reduction of its volume of 75% at the end of the protocol, a modification of the tumour microstructures and a decreased of both cellularity and cell proliferation. The drug also modified the tumour metabolisms as shown by 18F-FDG PET and GLUT-1 staining for the energetic one, CEST-MRI and pimonidazole for the hypoxic status and 99mTc-NTP 15 − 5 SPECT and sGAG assay for proteoglycans. Genomic analysis shown changes in gene expression related to therapy.

## Conclusions
TH-302 shows an in vivo anti-tumour activity in chondrosarcoma. Our multimodal imaging approach allows monitoring complex exchanges between tumour cells and their neighboring under therapy.

## Introduction
The concept of tumor microenvironment (TME) has raised considerable interest in translational research and emerged as one of the compulsory bases for a personalized medicine in oncology [ ]. TME includes tumor cells, stroma with fibroblasts, endothelial and immune cells, as well as non-cellular component such as extracellular matrix (ECM) with collagen and proteoglycans (PGs). Hypoxia, characterized by low oxygen/low pH, has emerged as one of the most important properties of TME, responsible of tumor drug resistance [ ]. Spatial heterogeneity of the TME in solid tumors leads to non-uniform spatial distribution of nutrients, growth factors and oxygen generating local niches of cells resistant to therapies [ , ].
To enhance therapeutic efficiency, several strategies of selective drug delivery targeting TME biomarkers emerged [ , ]. The high prevalence of hypoxic regions in the resistance of tumors to therapies led to the emergence of hypoxia-activated prodrugs (HAPs) [ , ]. Many preclinical studies identified TH-302 (Evofosfamide) as one of the most promising HAP. This HAP was used either in monotherapy or in combination with conventional chemo- and radiotherapy against head and neck squamous cancer, sarcoma and colorectal cancer [ – ] and the success of these preclinical studies led to clinical studies ongoing in phase III on glioma [ ]. Imaging methods sensitive to tumor hypoxia were able to predict the efficacy of TH-302 therapy. Hypoxia positon emission tomography (PET) tracers, including 18F-FAZA [ ], 18F-HX4 [ ], and 18F-FMISO [ , ] were predictive of a tumor’s response to TH-302. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) contrasts like dynamic contrast enhanced (DCE) [ ], intravoxel incoherent motion diffusion-weighted imaging (IVIM-DWI) [ ] or blood-oxygen-level dependent (BOLD) [ ] were also used to monitor the TME anatomical-only changes associated to this therapy.
Chondrosarcomas constitute a highly heterogeneous group accounting for about 20% of bone sarcomas. Current treatments present a limited efficacy [ ] leading to surgery as main solution. However, unresectable locations as well as recurrence after curettage are still an issue [ – ]. Chondrosarcoma resistance to chemotherapy was largely attributed to its TME characterized by dense ECM, low pH, increased interstitial pressure and poor vascularity associated to hypoxia [ , ]. To reach an efficient treatment, the use of a HAP prodrug might be an interesting solution. Our group highlighted the relevance of TH-302 in vitro in multicellular tumor spheroid of human extraskeletal myxoid chondrosarcoma suggesting its relevance to treat this pathology [ ]. While TH-302 has not yet been evaluated in vivo in chondrosarcoma models, it has shown efficacy in other types of sarcomas and bone tumors [ , ] Given the critical role of the TME in driving therapeutic resistance in chondrosarcoma, there is a strong rationale for visualizing TME remodeling during TH-302 treatment by an in vivo multimodal approach to optimize therapeutic strategies.
In this paper, we evaluated in vivo in a preclinical model of chondrosarcoma the antitumor activity of TH-302 thanks to multimodal imaging on a Swarm rat chondrosarcoma (SRC) model [ , ]. We monitored phenotypic and functional remodeling of the TME by combining nuclear imaging and MRI. Nuclear medicine was focused on tumor glucose metabolism (18F-FDG), hypoxia (18F-FMISO) and ECM PGs by exploiting the PGs (99mTc-NTP 15 − 5) [ ]. Multiparametric MRI (T2, apparent diffusion coefficient - ADC) and chemical exchange saturation transfer (CEST) MRI (glycosaminoglycans - GAG and amide proton transfer -APT/Guanidyl CEST contrasts) allowed longitudinal monitoring of tumor size, molecular interactions, PG quantity and extracellular pH, respectively. At the end of the protocol, histological and immunohistochemistry/immunofluorescence analyses of tumor tissues as well as biochemical assays of sulfated glycosaminoglycans and RNA sequencing on tumor extracts were performed.

## Methods

## Swarm rat chondrosarcoma model
For reactivation, frozen SRC fragments were defrosted in DMEM medium (Gibco Laboratories), manually calibrated to 3 mm3 and implanted on the right posterior tibia of male Sprague Dawley OFA rats (n = 10) (Charles River Laboratories France, Saint Germain Nuelles, FR) as previously described [ ]. Isoflurane (inhalation, 2.5%, 1 L/min, air/O2, 70/30, v/v) and carprofen (sc injection, 4 mg/kg; Rimadyl®, Pfizer) were used for anesthesia and pain management protocols, respectively. 31 days post-surgery, whole tumors were resected and immediately placed in cold DMEM. Reactivated SRC tumors were transplanted, in the same way, on the right posterior tibia of male Sprague Dawley OFA rats (n = 35). This last step corresponded to the day 0 of the longitudinal study.

## TH-302 treatment and tolerance
TH-302 was bought from MedChemTronica and dissoved in NaCl/EtOH, 95/5, v/v. Animals were randomly divided into control (vehicle; n = 21) and TH-302 (50 mg/kg; n = 14) treated groups. Treatment was administered by ip injection 5 days a week from d11 to d22 days post-transplant. Rats were daily weighted. ## In vivo imaging

## Nuclear imaging

## 99mTc-NTP 15 − 5 planar imaging
99mTc-NTP 15 − 5 radiosynthesis was performed as previously described [ ]. Planar scintigraphic images were acquired using a camera (NanoScan®,Mediso Ltd) equipped with one low energy ultra-high resolution (LEUHR) collimator. Planar acquisitions were performed with a 20% window centered on the 140 kev photopeak of 99mTc, 30 min after 99mTc-NTP 15 − 5 iv injection (30 Mbq/animal; n = 8) by using Nucline software (v3.00.018, Mediso Ltd, Budapest, HU). The acquisition lasted 20 min. Each animal following this protocol was imaged 4 times at d5, d12, d26 and d33. Analyses were performed with InterviewTMFUSION (v3.03.074, Mediso Ltd) software using an 2D elliptical region of interest (ROI) delineated over tumor uptake and a manually drawn ROI over muscle of the contralateral paw. ROI sum total activities were obtained in counts/s and normalized to both the ROI area and the injected activity. Then, tumor to muscle ratios were calculated.

## 18F-FDG and 18F-FMISO PET imaging
PET images were acquired using a small animal device (eXplore Vista®, GE Healthcare). 30 MBq of 18F-FDG (GlucoTEP, Curium) was iv injected (n = 6) or 50 MBq of 18F-FMISO, radiolabeled as previously described [ ] was iv injected (n = 6). The PET imaging was performed 1 h (animals kept asleep) or 4 h (animals awake) after the 18F-FDG and 18F-FMISO, respectively by using MMWKS® Image software (GE Healthcare). Imaging parameters for both radiotracers were a 250 to 700 keV energy window and 6 ns coincidence time window (20 min duration, 2 bed positions). Each animal was imaged at 4 different time points: d4, d14, d28 and d32 for 18F-FDG and d6, d13, d27 and d37 for 18F-FMISO. After 2D OSEM reconstruction, analyses were performed with VivoQuant™ software (v4.0 patch1, inviCRO) using a volume of interest (VOI) manually drawn for tumor and muscle. VOI sum total activities were obtained in counts/s then were normalized to both the VOI area and the injected activity. Results were expressed as tumor to muscle ratios (T/M).

## MRI
MR images of rats were acquired at 11.7T (BioSpec 117/16, Bruker BioSpin). Each animal had 5 imaging sessions at d6, d13, d20, d27 and d34 for T2 and ADC or 4 imaging session at d4, d11, d18, d25 and d32 for CEST protocols, respectively. A 72 mm circular polarizer volume coil was used for RF transmission and a surface array coil (2 × 2) received the NMR signal (Bruker BioSpin). A T2-weighted image was used to localize the tumor and to measure the tumor volume. For this, tumor ROIs were hand-drawn using Bspline tool on MIPAV software (v11.0.3; National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, MD, US).

## Diffusion weighted and T2 imaging
Tumor ADC maps were performed with a diffusion-weighted (DW) sequence (b = 0, 500 and 1000 s.mm2, direction [0,0,1]) with an echo time (TE) of 25 ms and a repetition time (TR) of 2000 ms. The field of view (FOV) was 50 mm with an axial slice thickness of 1 mm leading to a nominal resolution of 0.33 × 0.33 × 1 mm3. The number of slices was adjusted to image the whole tumor. T2 mapping was performed on a representative slice in the center of the tumor by a spin-echo RARE sequence with effective TEs of 6.5; 19.5; 32.5; 45.5; 58.5 ms, TR 5500 ms and a RARE acceleration factor of 2. The FOV was 50 mm with an axial slice thickness of 1 mm leading to a nominal resolution of 0.78 × 0.78 × 1 mm3. ADC and T2 values were calculated by the image display and processing tool of Paravision 6.0.1 with a hand-drawn ROI on the tumor.

## CEST imaging
CEST acquisitions were based on a RARE sequence with an effective TE and TR of 4.756 and 4500 ms, respectively and a RARE acceleration factor of 16. The slice was positioned in the center of the tumor (slice thickness: 1 mm, FOV: 50 mm, in-plane voxel size: 0.78 × 0.78 mm²). A first z-spectrum was recorded to correct for B0 inhomogeneities by using the WASSR approach [ ] (saturation pulses B1 : 0.3 µT, 54.8 ms gaussian pulses repeated 18 times; interpulse delay 0.010 ms; bandwidth : 50 Hz; saturation train : 1 s; saturation offsets : ± 1000 Hz with Δω 50 Hz) followed by the CEST z-spectrum (30 min acquisition duration with saturation pulses: B1 1.5 µT, 54.8 ms gaussian pulses repeated 72 times; bandwidth : 50 Hz; saturation train : 4 s; continuous wave CEST saturation of 0.6 µT; saturation offsets : ± 2500 Hz with Δω 50 Hz).
CEST MRI data were analyzed with an in-house program written in MATLAB® R2017a (MathWorks). In brief, a ROI was manually drawn on the T2-weighted image to delineate the tumor and muscle on contralateral paw. Then, B0 shift was corrected voxel-by-voxel with the WASSR approach. Finally, the CEST methodology applied to analyze the data depended on the saturation offset. For GAG CEST, the z-spectrum was fitted with two Lorentzian shapes per voxel: the first one to estimate the signal attenuation due to the water direct saturation (centered at 0 ppm) and the second one for the hydroxyl moieties. The GAG CEST signal intensity corresponded to the area of this latter Lorentzian shape. Regarding guanidyl and APT CEST, we applied the three-offset measurement approach proposed by Jin et al. [ ] to avoid nuclear overhauser enhancement (NOE) interferences. Two limits were chosen (1.60 to 2.40 and 3.20 to 4.00 ppm for the guanidyl and amide moieties, respectively) and a baseline was drawn between these frequencies. The CEST effect for APT and guanidyl were quantified as the difference between the baseline and the z-spectrum intensity. The ratio of APT/guanidyl was then calculated voxel-by-voxel in tumor ROI and a mean value was determined. To evaluate tumor heterogeneity, the voxels were merged in function of their CEST intensities and the number of voxels contained in each bin was represented (as percentage of the total voxel number). To set the intensity intervals, we used the Freedman-Diaconis rule and the widest bin was chosen between the two groups (treated vs. control) for a same day. Voxel number was normalized to the tumor ROI area and weighted sum of voxel was calculated for each group and each time before being transformed in percentage.

## Histology
At the end of imaging session (d35) rats were sacrificed by progressive CO2 inhalation. Histology was performed at the histopathology facility ANIPATH (Clermont-Ferrand, FR). Ex vivo experiments size was detailed in supplementary Fig.  . Slides were recorded using a slide scanner (X20, Zeiss AxioScan Z1, ZEN software). Tumor slices were analyzed on QuPath software (v0.3.0) with positive cell detection protocol. For each slice, three rectangular ROI of 3.25 mm2 were placed at the periphery of different tumor lobules as illustrated in supplementary Fig.  . Detailed analysis per ROI is available in supplementary Table .

## Alcian blue and hematoxylin Eosin staining
Slices were stained by Alcian blue (pH 1, 1% m/V, #A5268; Sigma-Aldrich) to visualize PGs, counterstained by nuclear red or by hematoxylin-eosin.

## Gamma-H2AX and GLUT-1 immunohistochemistry
5 μm thick sections of embedded tumors were cut and adhered to poly-L-Lysine-coated microscope slides. After dewaxing and rehydration of tumor slices, antigens were retrieved (boiling citrate buffer solution: 10 mM, pH 6). Endogenous peroxidases were quenched (H2O2: 0.3%, 30 min) and saturated (BSA: 1%, 1 h). Slides were then incubated overnight at 4 °C with primary antibodies at 1/500 (Merck-Millipore, anti-phospho-histone H2A.X antibody #05-636-I) and anti-GLUT-1 antibody at 1/100 (Abcam Cat# ab652, RRID: AB_305540). Biotinylated secondary antibody at 1/500 in BSA 0.1%, 1 h at RT (Jackson ImmunoResearch Laboratories) was used to reveal the first antibody. Biotin was then complexed with streptavidin-coupled horseradish peroxidase (HRP) (Vector Laboratories Cat# PK-4000, RRID: AB_2336818). Peroxidase was detected using diaminobenzidin (DAB) as per manufacturer’s instructions (Vector Laboratories Cat# SK-4105, RRID: AB_2336520). Nuclei were counterstained with hematoxylin.

## Ki-67 and Pimonidazole Immunofluorescence
For pimonidazole staining, an intraperitoneal injection of pimonidazole hydrochloride (60 mg/kg, Hypoxyprobe™, Burlington, MA, US) was performed one hour before sacrificing the animals. 5 μm thick sections of embedded tumors were cut and adhered to poly-L-Lysine-coated microscope slides. After dewaxing and rehydration of tumor slices, antigens were retrieved (boiling citrate buffer solution: 10 mM, pH 6) and slides were placed into the InsituPro VSi robot (Intavis bioanalytical instruments). Endogenous peroxidases were quenched (H2O2: 0.3%, 30 min) and saturated (BSA: 1%, 1 h). Slides were then incubated 1 h at room temperature with primary antibodies against Ki-67 at 1/2000 (Abcam Cat# ab15580, RRID: AB_443209) and against pimonidazole at 1/300 (Hypoxyprobe Cat# HP MAb-1, RRID: AB_2801307). Biotinylated secondary antibody (1/500, BSA 0.1%, 1 h; Vector Laboratories) was used to reveal the first antibody. Biotin was then complexed with streptavidin-coupled horseradish peroxidase (HRP) (1/500, 30 min; Vector Laboratories). Peroxidase was detected using tyramide signal amplification (TSA) as per manufacturer’s instructions (Alexa FluorTM 488; Thermo Fisher Scientific). Nuclei were counterstained with Hoechst 33,342. The fluorescence intensity of the slide was measured at 461 and 519 nm for Hoechst and TSA, respectively.

## Biochemical assay
Tumor and muscle samples were weighed (20–50 mg) and incubated overnight at 65 °C in 1 mL of a papain extraction buffer and then centrifuged (1000 g, 10 min). Sulfated GAG content was determined using the dimethyl methylene blue method (Blyscan® Sulfated Glycosaminoglycan assay kit; Biocolor Ltd) as per manufacturer’s instructions. Absorbance was measured at 656 nm using Thermo Scientific Multiskan GO device (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Experiments were performed in duplicates.

## RNA sequencing
RNA sequencing was performed as previously described [ ]. In short, total RNA was isolated from frozen tumours, using RNeasy micro Kit (Qiagen, France), with DNAse I treatment. The quality of total RNAs were determined using 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies) (RIN ≥ 7.6) and the concentration was evaluated using QuBit assay (Invitrogen). cDNA libraries were prepared from 50 ng of total RNA, using KAPA RNA HyperPrep Kit with RiboErase (Roche Diagnostics). Sequencing of indexed cDNA libraries was performed with 75 bp-long-single-end reads according to the standard Illumina protocol using NextSeq 550 High Output v2 kit (Illumina). An average of 30.6 ± 3.8 million reads were generated per sample.
Demultipexed reads were aligned to the rat reference genome (rn7) using Star software (v2.7.10a) with default parameters. HTSeq (v1.99.2) was used to quantify raw gene expression. For global analysis DESeq2 software v1.34.0 was used to carry out data normalization for sequencing depth differences and differential expression analyses. Differentially expressed genes were identified based on a significant p-value (p < 0.05). For specific genes analysis, after HTSeq, differential expression was identified by Mann-Whitney test (p < 0.05).

## Statistical analyses
Statistical analyses were performed on Prism software (v8.0, GraphPad Software). Data normal distributions were checked with Shapiro-Wilk tests before performing parametric tests. For tumor volume assessment, in vivo nuclear imaging and MRI quantifications, multiple unpaired parametric t-tests were applied. One-way ANOVA with multiple comparison was used to evaluate dynamic change of 18F-FDG T/M ratio. To evaluate CEST voxel distribution, Wilcoxon-Mann-Whitney tests were used. For histological analyses, unpaired parametric t-tests or Mann-Whitney test were performed, depending on data distribution. Multiple unpaired nonparametric t-tests were applied for biochemical assay. Results were considered significant at p values < 0.05 (*p < 0.05, **p < 0.01, ***p < 0.001, ****p < 0.0001). Data are presented as mean value ± SD.

## Results

## TH-302 has an antitumor activity in SRC model
Figure  a illustrates the tumor growth on one representative animal for both control and treated groups. Both anatomical MRI and digital caliper measurements showed a significant inhibition of tumor growth from d26 (p < 0.0001) and d19 (p < 0.01), respectively (Fig.  b, c). The inhibition of tumor growth at the end of the longitudinal study was comparable between both methods: −75.7% and − 74.3% as measured by caliper and MRI, respectively. BWCs were − 23.1% ± 7.4% and + 16.0% ± 2.1% for treated and control groups, respectively (Fig.  d). A weight loss was observed in the treated group, then animals regain weight after the end of the treatment (supplementary Fig.  ).

 

Antitumor efficacy was confirmed by histologic examination. HE staining (Fig.  a) evidenced a decreased cellularity with an average of 568 ± 126 positive nuclei per mm² in TH-302 group compared to 806 ± 141 for the control group (p < 0.01, Fig.  b). Ki-67 staining (red), a marker of cell proliferation, was significantly decreased in treated tumors with a value of 8 ± 4 positive cells per mm2 in regards of 53 ± 29 for control animals (p < 0.05, Fig.  d). Moreover, Ki-67 positive cells in control group were mainly located in the outer periphery of tumor lobules compared to treated tumors (Fig.  c). TH-302 efficacy was revealed by the significant increase of γ-H2AX expression (p < 0.01) in treated tumors when compared to control ones (Fig.  b). Lower γ-H2AX staining (green)was observed in the inner periphery of the controls tumors compared to TH-302 treated tumors. γ-H2AX positive cells were preferentially localized within hypoxic regions as identified by comparing with the pimonidazole staining (green) images (Fig.  a, c). Hypoxic cells were preferentially located at the inner periphery, below Ki-67 staining, in tumor lobules of control animals (Fig.  c). In treated tumor, spotted staining with a weak fluorescence was observed. Fluorescence quantification (Fig.  d) showed significantly less positive cells per mm2 in treated than in control groups (65 ± 24 vs. 430 ± 122; p < 0.01).

 

 

## TH-302 induces changes of water mobility
During the longitudinal study, the water T2 relaxation time was constant for the treated group while it increased for the control one leading to a significant difference between both groups from d19 (p < 0.01) (Fig.  a). Relative ADC evolution of water present inside the tumor tissue was observed to be significantly decreased (p < 0.05) in treated group compared to the control one from d13 (Fig.  b).

 

## TH-302 reduces tumor glucidic metabolism
Tumor accumulation of 18F-FDG appeared homogeneously distributed within tumor tissue at early stages and for both TH-302 and control groups (Fig.  a). For later stages of the protocol (from d28), 18F-FDG accumulated in control animals at the periphery of the tumor. Interestingly, this was not observed for TH-302 treated animals. The T/M ratio for treated and control groups for this latter point were 1.94 ± 0.04 and 2.51 ± 0.03, respectively leading to a significant difference (p = 0.0005). Moreover, the T/M ratio shows a significant decrease of 18F-FDG accumulation in the TH-302 group between d15 and d28 (3.16 ± 0.08 vs. 3.16 ± 0.43; p < 0.05) and between d15 and d32 (3.16 ± 0.08 vs. 1.91 ± 0.04; p < 0.05) Fig.  b. This was confirmed also by GLUT-1 immunohistochemistry (Fig.  c). GLUT-1 positive cells were significantly decreased in treated tumors (633 ± 136 vs. 1151 ± 292 positive cells per mm2 for treated and control groups, respectively; p < 0.001) (Fig.  d).

 

## TH-302 reduces hypoxic fraction
Figure  a shows for representative animals, the accumulation of the 18F-FMISO tracer. For later stages of the pathology (from d27), the tumor was readily observed by 18F-FMISO fixation in the control animal whereas this was not the case for the treated animal. Quantitative analysis showed a non-significant increase in T/M ratio at d27 and d34 for control animals (n = 3 per group) (Fig.  b) in line with tumor growth (Fig.  c). At the same time points, no detectable ¹⁸F-FMISO uptake was observed in the sonly TH-302–treated rat of the group.

 

Figure  d illustrates the APT/Guanidyl CEST ratio for representative animals. A strong spatial tumor heterogeneity was observed, especially for controls that evidenced higher voxels with negative ratio respectively to treated animals. However, mean APT/Guanidyl CEST ratio within the tumor ROI did not show any significant difference between both groups (Fig.  e). To consider the tumor spatial heterogeneity, all voxel intensities were grouped by class intervals for each timepoint and then analyzed as a distribution (Fig.  f and supplementary Fig.  ). The distribution of voxel intensities within the tumor was significantly different for treated and control groups for the last timepoint (p < 0.05).

## TH-302 induces PG remodeling
99mTc-NTP 15 − 5 accumulation was observed in joints and in chondrosarcoma as early as d5 (Fig.  a). As pathology evolved, both groups displayed an increase of radiotracer accumulation in tumors. Nevertheless, increase of radiotracer accumulation in tumor was significantly higher in TH-302 animals as compared to control ones from d26 (p < 0.05, Fig.  b).

 

GAG CEST imaging is displayed on Fig.  c for a representative treated and control animal. As pathology evolved, GAG CEST signal increased in both groups, as evidenced by increasing AUC values (Fig.  d) but without any significant difference between both groups. This was also confirmed by voxel intensity distribution analysis (Fig.  e and supplementary Fig.  ).
The blue alcian coloration (Fig.  f) showed that tumor from both groups contained GAGs with a cellular structure which was not comparable. Biochemical GAG assay at the final timepoint showed that treated tumor ECMs contained significantly more sulfated GAGs than control tumor ECMs (92.0 ± 14.6 vs. 68.5 ± 9.5) (p < 0.001) while GAG quantity in muscle for both groups were equivalent (1.8 ± 0.2 vs. 1.9 ± 1.0 for control and treated groups, respectively, Fig.  g).

## TH-302 genes signature in SRC model
Global mRNA expression analysis, without any preconceptions, on tumor extracts identified 19 differentially expressed genes between TH-302 treated tumors and controls (Fig.  a). A first set of mRNA was related to the drug efficacy. Downregulated genes were Rn7sl1 (p < 0.05) associated with inflammation and cancer progression, Acp7 (p < 0.05) associated with generation of reactive oxygen species, immune response and increased bone resorption and Kng2l1 (p < 0.05) involved in blood coagulation and vasodilation. A second set of mRNA was related to ECM-associated genes. Ibsp (p < 0.05), Ostn (p < 0.05), Galnt13 (p < 0.05), Krt5 (p < 0.01) were significantly upregulated in TH-302 treated tumors compared to controls (Fig.  a). Differences in gene expression between control and TH-302 animals were highlighted in the volcano plot (Fig.  b).

 

To go further, a second analysis was performed on selected genes involved in tumor response to therapy or genes belonging to the ECM. Genes coding for PGs such as col6a6 (p < 0.05), col4a5 (p < 0.05) and hyal1 (p < 0.01) were significantly increased whereas the matrix metalloproteinases mmp12 (p < 0.001) and adamts (p < 0.01), were significantly reduced in TH-302 treated tumors compared to controls (Fig.  c). Identified upregulated mRNA in the treated group were coding for cytochrome c oxidase (COX) subunits (Fig.  d), apoptosis related gene Casp16 (p < 0.01) and the hypoxia related gene Hif3a (p < 0.001) (Fig.  e).

## Discussion
Tumor hypoxia as well as a high chondrogenic matrix were identified as the two factors driving resistance of chondrosarcoma to treatments. As some drugs were developed to specifically target one or several of the ECM properties, it appeared interesting to treat animal model of chondrosarcoma with one of these prodrugs. Currently, TH-302 is the most promising HAP already tested in a wide range of hypoxic cancers [ ] making it a natural candidate against chondrosarcoma. In this study, the efficacy of TH-302 therapy was evaluated by an in vivo imaging approach. Immunohistochemistry, GAG biochemistry and gene analysis was used to confirm imaging observations.
The paratibial SRC model used in this study was a grade II chondrosarcoma since its aggressivity and chemo-resistance status mimics its human counterpart [ ]. TH-302 therapy on SRC model resulted in an inhibition of tumor growth that was observed from a week after the beginning of the treatment. Treatment efficacy was confirmed by histology with a decrease by a factor of 1.5 in cell density and by a factor higher than 6 for cell proliferation as determined with Ki-67 positive cell quantification. To date, few studies evaluated the interest of HAPs in chondrosarcoma [ ].
Tumor metabolic activity, as evidenced by 18F-FDG PET imaging, was reduced in treated animals for the two last imaging sessions. Ex vivo imaging showed that GLUT-1 was strongly affected by treatment. Interestingly, GLUT-1 transporters were homogeneously distributed within tumor tissue in treated group whereas in control one, they were preferentially located in the inner periphery of tumor lobules as previously described [ , ]. This observation correlated with the pimonidazole staining images suggesting a reduction of the hypoxia in treated tumors. The upregulation of Hif3a, a dominant negative regulator of the hypoxia inducible factor [ ], and the upregulation of COX subunits genes, involved in the modulation of COX activity [ ], argued in favor of a reduced tumor redox status and potentially reoxygenation of tissue during TH-302 therapy. Such conclusion was recently made in Mia-PACA-2 pancreatic xenograft models [ ]. This hypothesis is supported in vivo by 18F-FMISO imaging that showed a more important hypoxic statue in control animals. Interestingly, DNA damages measured by ɣ-H2AX were not only present in hypoxic regions but also propagated to the whole tumor tissue. All together, these results demonstrated that reduced hypoxia, as evidenced by 18F-FMISO and Hif3a upregulation, may contribute to TH-302-mediated growth suppression in SRC model and were in agreement with a previous study on nasopharyngal carcinoma [ ].
In this SRC model, the inhibition of tumor growth was associated with changes in TME biomarkers, e.g. PG content [ ]. mRNA sequencing highlighted the upregulation of ECM-associated genes such as collagen, hyaluronic acid and metalloproteases in TH-302 treated animals and supported the ECM changes observed by imaging. Regarding TME structure, T2 relaxation time and ADC are markers of the interactions of water molecules with their surrounding environment and they are affected by cellular structures, tissue density, interstitial fluid pressure as well as biomolecule conformations and changes. The increase of both T2 and ADC in the control animals was most likely a sign of necrotic areas in the core of the tumor. This contrasted with the absence of evolution of these parameters for the treated animals. From literature analysis, it appeared difficult to go further as results were described as being strongly dependent on individual tumor types, organs and therapies [ ]. Literature also reported contradictory results on the effect of TH-302 therapy on ADC [ , ].
Both nuclear and MR imaging were applied to study hypoxia and PG evolution during the therapy. The information obtained from one method is complementary to the other one. Concerning the GAG content, the 99mTc-NTP 15 − 5 imaging is sensitive to the negatively charged PGs while the GAG CEST images the whole different PGs [ , ]. This was probably the reason for the discrepancy between both approaches. The ECM remodeling due to the TH-302 treatment would lead to an increase of negative charge accessibility but not to an evolution of GAG concentration. This was also supported ex vivo by GAG biochemical assay showing a significant increase of negatively charged sGAG. This interpretation is further supported by ex vivo biochemical assays, which demonstrated a significant increase in negatively charged sGAG. In addition, global mRNA expression analysis revealed a marked upregulation of ECM- and PG-related genes, together with a downregulation of matrix metalloproteases (mmp12 and adamts), consistent with tumor microenvironment remodeling under TH-302 treatment.
Concerning the tumor hypoxic status, no evolution occurred during this longitudinal study. However, by exploiting the excellent MRI resolution, the heterogeneity of the tumor was characterized. Even though no difference was detected at the whole tumor level by CEST MRI, the voxel intensity repartition was significantly different for the last timepoint. To go further, 3D CEST MRI would be an interesting tool to evaluate the precise impact of TH-302 therapy on the whole tumor volume. Moreover, based on our previous results showing that APT/Guanidyl ratio decrease with a lower pH [ ], we presumed that TH-302 therapy limits the tumor acidification. RNA-seq analysis supported this hypothesis. Indeed, a decrease in HIF3A expression in animals treated with TH-302. HIF3A functions as a negative regulator of HIF1 and HIF2, thereby contributing to the attenuation of extracellular pH reduction as observed by imaging [ ].
One of the limitations of our work was the low number of animals used. However, this study was ambitious and used a significant number of in vivo imaging approaches to obtain complementary information on the different chondrosarcoma properties. During the study, few animals reached an ethical limit regarding either their tumor size or their weight loss and were sacrificed. Our therapeutic planning was guided by previously published data on TH-302 administration in rodents. In mouse xenograft models, the maximum tolerated dose (MTD) has been established at 50 mg/kg (i.p., QDx5, 2 weeks), which induced only a modest body weight loss of approximately 3-5.6% (Sun et al., 2016). To date, no MTD has been reported in rats. However, one study demonstrated that TH-302 was well tolerated following intravenous administration once weekly for three weeks at a dose of 50 mg/kg (Jung et al., 2011). In a subsequent study, a body weight reduction of 10–20% was observed in WAG/Rij rats bearing syngeneic R1 rhabdomyosarcoma tumors treated with TH-302 at 50 mg/kg (i.p., QDx4 over one week) (Peeters et al., 2015). Despite an established MTD, in mice, two additional studies have also reported treatment-related weight loss. In patient-derived xenograft (PDX) models, TH-302 administered at 50 mg/kg (i.p., QD×5 for 3 weeks) induced up to a 20% reduction in body weight (Harms et al.). Similarly, in a xenograft model of nasopharyngeal carcinoma, weight losses of approximately 10% and 20% were observed following treatment with 50 mg/kg and 75 mg/kg, respectively (i.p., QD×4 for 2 weeks) (Huang et al., 2018). Hematology/serum biochemistry could have helped to identify the underlying causes of this weight loss. Despite these limitations, the interest of TH-302 therapy was demonstrated as well as the relevance of bimodal imaging strategy for therapeutic monitoring.

## Conclusion
Our multimodal imaging strategy allowed monitoring the impact of TH-302 on the key features of chondrosarcoma microenvironment such as proteoglycan contain, proliferation and hypoxia in vivo. We demonstrated the cytotoxic activity of this HAP against hypoxic cells in SRC model, highlighting TH-302 monotherapy as an excellent candidate for effective chemotherapy in chondrosarcoma. This multimodal imaging study demonstrated the impact of TH-302 treatment on the remodeling of the TME in CHS. While CEST-MRI provides insights into spatial heterogeneity of PGs and hypoxia, nuclear imaging offers high sensitivity to detect early TME alterations post-therapy highlighting the complementarity of these techniques. TME-targeted multimodal imaging allows for a comprehensive evaluation of tumor microenvironment remodeling under therapy. Overall, this non-invasive in vivo strategy could be a support to the development of personalized therapeutic approaches in chondrosarcoma.
