## ABSTRACT
GenMineTOP, the first dual DNA–RNA comprehensive genomic profiling (CGP) test in Japan, was approved for reimbursement in 2023. To evaluate its clinical utility, we analyzed 1356 cases from the Center for Cancer Genomics and Advanced Therapeutics (C‐CAT) database. Oncogenic genomic alterations were identified in 91.5% of cases. Somatic mutations were the most prevalent, followed by amplifications and fusion/exon skipping events. The DNA panel, covering 737 genes, detected not only alterations relevant to therapeutic decisions but also those providing insights into tumor biology. Among the latter, frequently observed examples included mutations in KMT2C (n = 28) and ARID1B (n = 24), and amplifications in GLI1 (n = 14) and YAP1 (n = 10), which are not included in other CGP tests approved in Japan. The RNA panel identified 105 fusion events, including 11 NTRK fusions (0.8%), of which five were NTRK3 fusions: two with the well‐known ETV6‐NTRK3 fusion and three with non‐ETV6 partners. Forty‐nine of these fusions were diagnostically significant, highlighting the utility of the RNA panel. Amplification‐RNA expression analyses revealed strong correlations for MDM2, CDK4, EGFR, and ERBB2. In contrast, weaker correlations observed for MYC and FGFR1 highlighted the need for careful interpretation of amplification in these genes. Cancer type significantly influenced RNA expression, with KIT and TERT mutations linked to increased expression and significant overexpression observed in ALK, FGFR3, NTRK1, NTRK3, and RET fusions. In summary, this study demonstrated the real‐world clinical utility of the dual DNA–RNA CGP test and provided a valuable resource for interpreting RNA expressions.

This study analyzed 1356 cases using GenMineTOP, Japan's first dual DNA–RNA CGP test, and identified oncogenic alterations in 91.5% of cases. The RNA panel detected 105 fusion events, including 11 NTRK fusions. Furthermore, the analysis clarified the independent effects of genomic abnormalities and cancer type on RNA expression, providing valuable insights for precise and reliable diagnostics.

The GenMineTOP DNA panel reports TMB only when all of the following quality criteria are satisfied: (1) tumor DNA input of at least 100 ng, (2) pre‐capture library yield of at least 187.5 ng, (3) post‐capture library concentration of at least 4.0 nM, and (4) total sequencing reads of at least 40 million. Therefore, the proportion of cases with reported TMB reflects the proportion of samples meeting these criteria. Similarly, the GenMineTOP RNA panel reports RNA expression levels only when all of the following quality criteria are met: (1) tumor RNA input of at least 200 ng, (2) pre‐capture library yield of at least 187.5 ng, (3) post‐capture library concentration of at least 4.0 nM, and (4) total sequencing reads of at least 20 million. Consequently, the proportion of cases with reported RNA expression levels reflects the proportion of samples meeting these quality standards.
Across all cases analyzed, 98.5% of samples met the quality criteria for the DNA panel, and 97.1% met the criteria for the RNA panel, indicating high overall success rates. For both panels, as shown in Table  , the success rate decreased with increasing time from sampling to testing. Fisher's exact test demonstrated statistically significant associations between success rate and time from sampling for both panels (p < 0.001 for the DNA panel, p = 0.005 for the RNA panel).
 Notably, even among samples stored for more than 5 years, over 90% were successfully analyzed in the RNA panel, demonstrating the robustness of the RNA panel even with long‐term sample storage. These findings highlight the reliability of the RNA panel in clinical practice, providing robust data for RNA analysis under real‐world conditions.

3.2

## Landscape of Genomic Alterations
The landscape of genomic alterations revealed a comprehensive overview of oncogenic genomic alterations (somatic mutations, amplifications, fusions/exon skipping events), as well as high tumor mutational burden (TMB‐high) (Figure  ). Among these, oncogenic somatic mutations were the most frequently detected, found in 1140 cases, followed by oncogenic amplifications (342 cases), oncogenic fusions or exon skipping events (117 cases), and TMB‐high (72 cases; Figure  ). Notably, oncogenic fusions or exon skipping events were more prevalent in cases without somatic mutations. Specifically, these events were observed in 68 of 1140 mutation‐positive cases and 49 of 216 mutation‐negative cases (p < 0.001, chi‐squared test). Overall, oncogenic genomic alterations (somatic mutations, amplifications, or fusions/exon skipping events) were detected in 1241 of 1356 cases (91.5%).

3.3

## Genomic Alterations Detected by DNA Panel: Frequently Detected Mutations, Amplifications, and TMB‐High Cases
The distribution of frequently detected oncogenic mutations and amplifications is summarized in Figure  . Oncogenic somatic mutations were detected in 333 genes, with the most frequently mutated genes being TP53 (n = 661), KRAS (n = 299), and APC (n = 153) (Figure  and Table  ). Additionally, KMT2C (n = 28) and ARID1B (n = 24), which are not included in other CGP tests approved in Japan, were identified in a relatively large number of cases. These genes are epigenetic regulatory genes involved in chromatin remodeling and transcriptional regulation, processes critical for cell differentiation and development [ , ].
 Oncogenic amplifications were detected in 64 genes, with the most frequently amplified genes being MDM2 (n = 64), CDK4 (n = 63), and MYC (n = 41) (Figure  and Table  ). Amplifications in GLI1 (n = 14) and YAP1 (n = 10), which are among the frequently detected amplifications (Figure  ), were also observed. These genes are not analyzed by other CGP tests approved in Japan.
As described in the previous section, TMB‐high cases were observed across multiple cancer types, with a total of 72 cases identified (Figure  and Table  ). Among cancer types with 50 or more cases, lung cancer exhibited the highest frequency of TMB‐high cases at 25.4%.

3.4

## Fusions and Exon Skipping Events Detected by RNA Panel
The RNA panel analysis identified a total of 105 fusion events and 13 exon skipping events across various cancer types, as summarized in Table  . Exon skipping events were observed in CTNNB1, EGFR, and ERBB2, all of which have been previously reported in the literature as relevant to tumorigenesis [ , , ]. No splicing junction mutations were detected at the DNA level in any of the exon skipping events. Specifically, CTNNB1 exon 3 skipping was identified in seven cases, including four colon cancers and three liver cancers. EGFR exon 2–7 skipping, known as variant III (vIII), was detected in five cases of brain tumors. Additionally, ERBB2 exon 16 skipping was observed in a single case of head and neck cancer. Among the 105 fusions, 17 were classified as evidence level A, indicating the availability of PMDA‐ or FDA‐approved drugs either for the specific cancer type or as tumor‐agnostic indications. These level A fusions included NTRK fusions (n = 11) and RET fusions (n = 3), for which tumor‐agnostic treatments have been approved [ , ]. The overall prevalence of NTRK fusions was 0.8% (11/1356 cases).
 To estimate the NTRK fusion positive rate in F1CDx, a simulation matched cancer type and age group distributions with GenMineTOP. Of 1356 GenMineTOP cases, 1352 were matched (Table  ). Four cases (two vulva/vagina and two ovary/fallopian tube cancers) lacked sufficient F1CDx counterparts and were excluded; all were negative for NTRK fusions. The final analysis compared 1352 cases from both datasets, yielding an NTRK fusion rate of 0.8% (11/1352) in GenMineTOP. The simulation, repeated 10,000 times, produced a median F1CDx fusion rate of 0.44% (Figure  ). The 95% confidence interval (CI) ranged from 0.147% to 0.739%.
 Furthermore, a high‐frequency fusion event was identified in colorectal cancer involving the PTPRK‐RSPO3 fusion, which is known to be associated with WNT pathway activation [ ]. This fusion was detected in 10 cases, including eight colon cancers, one biliary tract cancer, and one cancer of unknown primary. Additionally, 49 of the 105 fusions were identified as diagnostically relevant [ , , , , , , , , , , , , , , , , , , ], as summarized in Table  . Among them, a case initially diagnosed as cancer of unknown primary was found to harbor the ATP1B1‐PRKACB fusion, which is known to be characteristic of intraductal oncocytic papillary neoplasm of pancreas and bile duct [ ]. This result led to a revised diagnosis of intraductal oncocytic papillary neoplasm of the pancreas.
 These findings emphasize the capability of the RNA panel to detect fusion and exon skipping events with significant diagnostic and therapeutic implications.

3.5

## Relationship Between Gene Expression and Copy Number
Among the 27 genes with available expression data, nine genes (MDM2, CDK4, MYC, EGFR, FGFR1, ERBB2, PDGFRA, KIT, and MET) were identified as having at least 15 cases with amplification. The relationship between gene amplification and RNA expression levels was analyzed for these genes. As shown in Figure  , RNA expression levels were consistently higher in amplification‐positive cases compared to amplification‐negative cases for all nine genes.
Next, we assessed the correlation between copy number and gene expression levels in cases with gene amplification (Figure  ). Strong positive correlations were observed for four genes: MDM2, CDK4, EGFR, and ERBB2. In contrast, for genes such as MYC and FGFR1, no significant correlation between copy number and expression levels was observed. As shown in Figure  , the interquartile ranges of RNA expression levels for MYC and FGFR1 overlapped between amplification‐positive and amplification‐negative cases, indicating that gene amplification has a relatively weak impact on the expression levels of these genes.
 
3.6

## Factors Influencing Gene Expression
Gene expression levels are influenced not only by genomic abnormalities such as gene amplification but also strongly by cancer type. To evaluate the independent effects of cancer type and genomic abnormalities (gene amplification, fusion, and oncogenic somatic mutations), a multivariate analysis was conducted. As shown in Figure  , cancer type was statistically significant for all 27 genes analyzed. The violin plots in Figure  further detail the variation in gene expression by cancer type. For instance, ALK, NTRK2, and NTRK3 were highly expressed in CNS/brain cancers, ROS1 was highly expressed in lung cancers, and ESR1 showed high expression in breast, ovary/fallopian tube, and uterine cancers. In bowel cancer, MYC was highly expressed, with significantly higher expression levels in cases with pathogenic APC mutations compared to APC wild‐type cases (Figure  , p < 0.001, t‐test).
 Among the genomic abnormalities, the effect of amplification on expression levels was particularly prominent for CDK4, EGFR, ERBB2, and MDM2 (Figure  ), aligning with findings from the previous section. For fusion events, while the number of cases was often small, statistically significant overexpression was observed in cases positive for ALK, FGFR3, NTRK1, NTRK3, and RET fusions (Figure  and Figure  ).
Mutations also influenced expression levels in specific genes. For example, KIT mutations and TERT promoter mutations were associated with higher expression levels (Figure  ).
These findings provide crucial data for interpreting gene expression in clinical cases, highlighting the combined roles of cancer type and genomic abnormalities in determining expression levels.

## Discussion
This study comprehensively analyzed 1356 cases and detected oncogenic genomic alterations in 91.5% of cases. Somatic mutations were the most prevalent, followed by amplifications and fusion/exon skipping events. Notably, fusion/exon skipping events were more frequently observed in mutation‐negative cases, underscoring the complementary role of RNA panels in detecting these alterations.
We compared our findings with previous large‐scale studies. A study using C‐CAT data from F1CDx and NCC Oncopanel [ , ] reported TP53, KRAS, and APC as the most frequent mutations, consistent with our results. Fusion detection rates were 2.6% (F1CDx) and 0.8% (NCC Oncopanel). In our study, GenMineTOP detected fusions in 7.7%. In the MSK‐IMPACT cohort [ ], TP53 was the most frequent alteration (41%), with KRAS mutations in 90% of pancreatic and 44% of colorectal cancers, and fusion detections in 15%. Differences in patient backgrounds, target genes, and pipelines may explain the differences in fusion detection.
The GenMineTOP RNA panel demonstrated high reliability, with 97.1% of cases meeting quality criteria. In this study, 78.6% (11/14) of samples stored for more than 5 years met quality criteria for the DNA panel, while 92.9% (13/14) met the criteria for the RNA panel. Although the difference was not statistically significant based on Fisher's exact test, the RNA panel showed a higher success rate, demonstrating the robustness of the RNA panel under real‐world conditions. This observation aligns with our previous findings [ ], which showed that while DNA quality, as assessed by ddCq and Q values, significantly declined over time with differences between the 3–5 years and over 5 years groups, RNA quality degradation plateaued after 3 years. Specifically, 49% of samples older than 5 years achieved a DV200 value of 50 or higher.
This study identified mutations in KMT2C and ARID1B, and amplifications in GLI1 and YAP1, which are not included in other CGP tests approved in Japan. KMT2C and ARID1B are epigenetic regulatory genes pivotal in chromatin remodeling and transcriptional regulation, processes crucial for tumorigenesis [ , ]. Amplifications of GLI1, associated with Sonic Hedgehog pathway activation, have been linked to poor prognosis in mesenchymal tumors [ ], and YAP1 amplifications promote aggressive phenotypes in several cancers, including lung and cervical cancers [ ]. The detection of these mutations and amplifications provides valuable insights into tumor biology, even if their current clinical relevance remains limited.
RNA panels are superior to DNA panels in detecting fusions and exon skipping events due to differences in probe design [ ]. DNA panels require probes targeting intronic regions, which make up about 25% of the human genome and often contain repetitive sequences. In contrast, RNA panels design probes on exonic regions that constitute only 1%–2% of the genome and have fewer repetitive sequences, making design simpler and more efficient. For exon skipping, RNA panels can directly detect missing exons at the transcript level, regardless of the underlying genomic changes.
In lung adenocarcinoma, adding whole transcriptome RNA sequencing to DNA panels increased the overall actionable fusion detection rate from 5.9% to 6.8%, raising the number of detected cases from 329 to 376 out of 5570. The addition of RNA sequencing also increased the number of MET exon 14 skipping‐positive cases from 97 to 115, further highlighting the contribution of RNA‐based approaches to detecting actionable alterations [ ]. We have also demonstrated that MET exon skipping detection rates are high using RNA panels in lung cancer [ ]. In another study, a mathematical model estimating the contribution of RNA panels after DNA panels in non‐small cell lung cancer predicts that approximately 1.3% of cases harbor fusions detectable only through RNA analysis, raising the overall detection rate from 4.2% to 5.5% [ ].
In the present study, the analysis included only 59 lung cancer cases, and we focused on NTRK fusions due to their clinical significance to guide targeted therapies. The prevalence of NTRK fusions in this cohort was 0.8%. Previous reports from Japanese databases have shown prevalence rates of 0.22% using F1CDx and 0.15% using OncoGuide NCC Oncopanel System [ ]. F1CDx detects NTRK1 fusion, NTRK2 fusion, and ETV6‐NTRK3 fusion, whereas both GenMineTOP and OncoGuide NCC Oncopanel System target fusions involving all three NTRK genes (NTRK1‐3). Notably, this study identified five cases of NTRK3 fusions, of which two involved the well‐characterized ETV6‐NTRK3 fusion. The remaining three cases demonstrated non‐ETV6 partners, including two cases with EML4‐NTRK3 and one case with KHDRBS1‐NTRK3. This finding underscores the diversity of NTRK3 fusion partners and highlights the importance of covering such diversity to ensure comprehensive detection. In addition to data from Japanese cohorts, the prevalence of NTRK fusions has been reported in large‐scale pan‐cancer genomic databases from outside Japan. In the Foundation Medicine genomic database, NTRK fusions were identified in 244 out of 459,751 cases (0.05%), while the GENIE database reported 76 out of 97,592 cases (0.08%) [ ]. This study estimated the NTRK fusion positive rate in the F1CDx dataset by aligning patient backgrounds, including cancer types and age distributions, with those in GenMineTOP. The simulation showed a 95% CI for the estimated positive rate ranging from 0.147% to 0.739%, with a median of 0.44%. The observed positive rate in GenMineTOP (0.8%) exceeded the upper limit of the simulated 95% CI, suggesting that the higher detection rate in GenMineTOP cannot be attributed solely to differences in cancer types or age distributions. However, as the comparison is based on simulation, unmeasured confounding factors may exist. While notable, this trend should be interpreted with caution, as direct comparison is not feasible. Additionally, the higher detection rate in GenMineTOP may not be solely due to the use of an RNA panel but may also be at least partly attributed to the inclusion of NTRK3 as a target gene. This underscores the importance of probe design in RNA panels to ensure comprehensive detection of fusion genes. As shown in Table  , this study identified a total of 49 diagnostically relevant fusions, many of which were associated with sarcomas. These findings underscore the diagnostic utility of the RNA panel in real‐world settings.
In this study, exon skipping events were identified without splicing junction mutations at the DNA level, confirming that RNA analysis enables the detection of exon skipping regardless of genomic alterations. Expanding exon skipping analyses beyond the five approved genes may aid in assessing splice site mutation pathogenicity.
The relationship between gene expression and copy number was analyzed for nine genes with frequent amplifications. Among these genes, amplification significantly influenced expression levels, although the magnitude of this effect varied. CDK4, EGFR, ERBB2, and MDM2 showed strong correlations between amplification and expression levels, while FGFR1 and MYC exhibited weaker associations. For these latter genes, the overlap in expression levels between amplification‐positive and amplification‐negative cases suggests that amplification may not correctly reflect increased expression. In colorectal cancer, MYC expression is frequently elevated even in the absence of amplification, as activation of the Wnt/β‐catenin signaling pathway drives MYC transcription [ ]. Consistent with this, our data confirmed that APC mutations correlate with higher MYC expression. These findings suggest that not all amplifications serve as effective biomarkers for targeted therapy, emphasizing the need for careful interpretation of amplification data. RNA analysis could help refine copy number interpretation by identifying functionally relevant amplifications, though further studies are needed to confirm its utility.
Gene expression levels were also strongly influenced by cancer type. Multivariate analyses and violin plots (Figure  ) revealed that cancer type had a significant impact on gene expression. Generally, tumor gene expression is largely influenced by cell origin‐related factors, including epigenetic modifications and lineage‐specific transcriptional programs [ ]. For example, ALK, NTRK2, and NTRK3 were highly expressed in CNS/brain cancers, while ROS1 was highly expressed in lung cancers. Such patterns highlight the importance of considering cancer type when interpreting expression data. While our data provide valuable insights, further data collection is necessary to validate the results.
This study has several limitations. First, the retrospective nature of the analysis may introduce inherent biases and limit the generalizability of the findings. For example, the dataset shows a relative enrichment for cases of soft tissue and CNS/brain origin, which may introduce a bias compared to the general population of patients undergoing CGP testing in Japan. Second, with respect to the discussion on quality, the C‐CAT database includes only cases for which the analysis was completed. Cases excluded due to poor nucleic acid quality or other factors leading to discontinuation of the analysis are not registered in the database. As a result, the true proportion of cases meeting quality standards remains uncertain. Third, although we detected many fusions with potential diagnostic utility, information on how diagnoses were revised based on CGP testing results was largely absent from the C‐CAT database.
In conclusion, this study demonstrates the utility of RNA panels in detecting clinically relevant fusions and exon skipping events while also highlighting the benefits of analyzing a large number of genes in both DNA and RNA panels. While the impact of amplification on expression levels varies among genes, cancer type remains a dominant factor influencing gene expression. The robustness of the RNA panel in terms of quality control was also confirmed in real‐world settings, demonstrating its reliability for clinical use.
