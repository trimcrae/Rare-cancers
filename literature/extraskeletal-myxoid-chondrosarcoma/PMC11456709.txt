## Objective
Multitargeted tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) have been approved as second‐line therapy in refractory sarcoma, prolonging progression‐free survival (PFS) but with short‐lived duration of disease control. Fruquintinib is a TKI that specifically inhibits vascular endothelial growth factor receptor‐1,2,3 with no metabolism by liver enzymes. In this retrospective study, we assessed the efficacy and safety of fruquintinib‐based treatment in patients with refractory sarcoma after developing several lines of TKI resistance.

## Methods
We retrospectively analyzed the clinical data of patients with refractory sarcoma after they had developed several lines of resistance to TKIs and who received fruquintinib‐based treatment from November 2021 to August 2023. The primary endpoint was the progression‐free survival rate at 4 months (4m‐PFSR). Secondary endpoints were the median PFS, overall survival (OS), objective response rate, disease control rate, and adverse effects (AEs). PFS and OS were estimated using the Kaplan–Meier method. A log‐rank test was used to compare survival curves between different clinical and pathological factors. Cox proportional hazards analysis was performed to identify PFS‐related prognostic factors.

## Results
We included 124 patients: 56 (45.2%) with osteosarcoma, 28 (22.6%) with Ewing sarcoma, seven (5.6%) with chondrosarcoma, and 33 (26.6%) with soft tissue sarcomas (STS). Only 18 (14.5%) patients received monotherapy with fruquintinib. With a median follow‐up time of 6.8 (interquartile range [IQR], 4.6–9.4) months, 22 (17.7%) patients had partial response and 78 (62.9%) had stable disease. The 4m‐PFSR was 58.4% (95% confidence interval [CI], 49.6%–67.1%). The median PFS and OS were 4.4 (95% CI, 3.9–5.0) months and 11.4 (95% CI, 10.3–12.5) months. In multivariate analysis, a high hazard ratio for progression was associated with target lesions located outside the lung and bone with 1.79 (95% CI, 1.10–2.93; p = 0.020). Eighty‐eight AEs were recorded in 47 (37.9%) patients; the most common were pneumothorax (18/124, 14.5%), diarrhea (8/124, 6.5%), oral mucositis (7/124, 5.6%), and thrombocytopenia (7/124, 5.6%).

## Conclusions
Fruquintinib may be a potential option for patients with refractory sarcoma after developing several lines of TKI resistance, with a satisfactory efficacy and safety profile in combination therapy. However, the degree of contribution of fruquintinib to results is unclear when combined with other effective substances. Additional prospective trials of fruquintinib should be conducted, especially involving different pathological types and combination regimens.

## Toxicity and Safety
A total of 88 AEs were recorded in 47 (37.9%) patients, among which the most common grade 3–4 AEs were pneumothorax (18/124, 14.5%), diarrhea (7/124, 5.6%), anorexia (6/124, 4.8%), abdominal pain (6/124, 4.8%), oral mucositis (6/124, 4.8%), and thrombocytopenia (6/124, 4.8%) (Table  ). One patient achieved PR but discontinued fruquintinib because of pneumothorax. There were no treatment‐related deaths reported at the two centers.
 

## Discussion

## Overall Efficacy
Our retrospective study revealed that fruquintinib‐based treatment in patients with refractory bone and soft tissue sarcoma after developing several lines of TKI resistance had a curative effect that is comparable to that of other TKIs (Supplementary Table  ). Fruquintinib is a novel TKI with a high selectivity for VEGFR‐1,2,3 with no metabolism by liver enzymes and that has been approved for application in mCRC. However, with low incidence and various subtypes, there is no related research on the use of fruquintinib in sarcoma except one retrospective study conducted by Shanghai Jiao Tong University Affiliated Sixth People's Hospital and the Affiliated Hospital of Jiangxi University of Traditional Chinese Medicine. Unlike this investigation, our patients usually received higher doses of fruquintinib than the recommended phase II dose (RP2D). Although our participants had even progressed after several lines of TKI therapy, the 4m‐PFSR of fruquintinib (58.4%; 95% CI, 49.6%–67.1%) was similar to that of apatinib (56.76%; 95% CI, 39.43%–70.84%) and surpassed that of sorafenib (46%; 95% CI, 28%–63%) in osteosarcoma patients. With 85.5% of patients receiving the drug combination, AEs were acceptable and manageable. Fruquintinib has the potential to be combined in therapy and to reverse several lines of TKI resistance at a higher dose than the standard dose in clinical practice (RP2D for clinical use). The ORR (17.7%; 95% CI, 10.9%–24.6%) is relatively poor, but the DCR (80.6%; 95% CI, 73.6%–87.7%) is satisfactory for heavily treated patients, which contributes to slowing tumor growth. We did not expect tumor shrinkage in patients with a heavy tumor burden and poor general condition. Despite more than 80% of patients receiving combination therapy, a short duration of disease control was still observed. With a low risk of DDI for fruquintinib, further research is needed to explore which combinations could ultimately delay TKI resistance.

## Subgroup Analysis and Prognostic Factors
We categorized patients according to different characteristics and conducted a log‐rank test to verify the effective factors influencing prognostic outcomes. The antitumor activity of fruquintinib varied among different pathological types. The longest median PFS was obtained for STS, whereas the shortest median PFS was obtained for Ewing sarcoma, which was consistent with the average duration of previous TKI treatment (Supplementary Table  ). Patients with Ewing sarcoma had far more previous lines of treatment than patients with STS in our study (p = 0.006), which might indicate that patients with Ewing sarcoma might be in a later stage of disease than patients with other types of sarcomas. This may be one of the reasons why the PFS and DCR of Ewing sarcoma was lower than other groups. According to a study conducted by Fleuren et al., hepatocyte growth factor receptor (MET) and anaplastic lymphoma kinase (ALK) were overexpressed in Ewing sarcoma cell lines (86% and 69%) using in vitro data. However, fruquintinib is a TKI with a high selectivity for VEGFR‐1, 2, 3 without targeting MET and ALK, which might explain why fruquintinib does not appear to be very beneficial in Ewing sarcoma. Due to the activity of anti‐MET and ALK, cabozantinib and crizotinib are potential novel therapeutic targets in the treatment of Ewing sarcoma. Especially, we found that combining fruquintinib with Irinotecan or Topotecan resulted in more promising outcomes for Ewing sarcoma compared with other combination therapies, suggesting that Irinotecan or Topotecan may be preferred for anti‐angiogenic combination therapy in Ewing sarcoma. Moreover, our data revealed that patients with lesions located in bone benefited more than those with lesions in the lung, which was opposite to our usual understanding of those TKIs. Previous studies have reported that the median PFS of musculoskeletal lesions is shorter compared with that of pulmonary lesions. The reason for our results may be attributed to our patients having several lines of TKI resistance and the lung is a drug‐resistant active organ but bones developed relatively slowly. Therefore, the advantages of skeletal lesions appeared in fruquintinib‐based treatment after patients developed several lines of TKI resistance. Furthermore, most of our fruquintinib‐based regimens were in combination with second‐line chemotherapy, which might partly contribute to the local control of these bone lesions. In the multivariate analysis, only sites of the target lesions were a useful index to determine PFS. Patients with target lesions confined to the lung or bone might achieve a more favorable prognosis, which is probably linked to a lower tumor load in these patients. In other words, smaller tumor sizes led to longer PFS in patients who received fruquintinib‐based treatment.
Relevant research has shown that only a small subset of patients with advanced sarcoma derives meaningful clinical benefit from PD1/PD‐L1 inhibitors. On the contrary, our patients who received fruquintinib combined with PD1/PD‐L1 inhibitors obtained more favorable results than those receiving non‐combination and combination chemotherapy. This suggests that PD1/PD‐L1 inhibitors still modulate the immune microenvironment in end‐stage patients with a large tumor burden to delay resistance to TKIs. However, we found an interaction between pathology and combination therapy. In our study, the PD1/PD‐L1 inhibitor combination was more frequently applied in STS but was not administered in Ewing sarcoma; this might partly be because patients with Ewing sarcoma were in an immune‐desert condition according to the perception of our clinicians. Therefore, further trials should be conducted to clarify the effect of PD1/PD‐L1 inhibitors in fruquintinib‐based treatment according to different pathologies. Additionally, our findings showed that the combination of fruquintinib and chemotherapy resulted in the worst outcomes, which indicated that the role of chemotherapy was minimal in patients with poor bone marrow reserve after multi‐line drug resistance. Perhaps, this is because our dose was lower than that used in regular treatment, which was unavoidable due to the large tumor load and because of our patients with end‐stage disease who could not tolerate high‐intensity chemotherapy. Moreover, chemotherapy impaired patients' immunity by inducing a series of negative effects, such as myelosuppression, which contributed to the interruption of fruquintinib‐based treatment. Meanwhile, the comparison of the baseline information indicated that the location of target lesions was different between monotherapy and combination therapy groups (p = 0.029), attributing to extrapulmonary disease usually treated with combination therapies in clinical practice (Supplementary Table  ). The patients who received monotherapy tended to have target lesions limited to the lung and bone (14/17, 82.4%), which may contribute to the superior result of monotherapy. In addition to the combination of immunotherapy and chemotherapy, Grignani et al. proved the synergistic therapeutic effects of TKIs and different pathway inhibitors. Because only one patient was administered fruquintinib and everolimus (an inhibitor of the mammalian target of rapamycin pathway), we did not examine the efficacy of this combination regimen. However, the approach using fruquintinib with diverse targeted drugs may be a potential option. To maximize the benefit for heavily treated patients, clinicians should consider these aspects carefully when choosing combination agents in fruquintinib‐based treatment.

## Toxicity and Safety
In general, fruquintinib at a dose higher than the standard dose for clinical practice with combination therapy was fairly well tolerated and related AEs were acceptable and manageable. Diarrhea, abdominal pain, and oral mucositis were common when using TKIs separately. Anorexia and thrombocytopenia may be attributed to chemotherapy. Pneumothorax is a ubiquitous AE in sarcomas but not in other cancers because sarcomas are prone to pulmonary metastasis. Pneumothorax may be the result of fruquintinib targeting pulmonary lesions. We did not find hypertension among our patients, which is the most common AE with fruquintinib in mCRC ; this might be because our patients were much younger than the patients enrolled in previous studies. Although fruquintinib scarcely interacts with CYP450, it increases the risk of bleeding in combination with warfarin. Thus, unnecessary interactions between warfarin and fruquintinib should be avoided, and the warfarin dosage should be adjusted.

## Strengths and Limitations
There are three strengths in this retrospective study. First, the number of samples (N = 124) is sufficient for reliable results in these malignancies with low incidence. Second, this is a real‐world research to investigate a feasible therapy for heavily treated patients after developing several lines of resistance to TKIs, providing a potential option for clinicians. Meanwhile, there is no prospective research on using fruquintinib in sarcoma before our investigation. The results of this retrospective study probably provide a reference for further clinical trials of fruquintinib in sarcoma.
However, our study has several limitations. First, this is a retrospective study without a control group. Additional prospective trials should be conducted to clarify the efficacy of fruquintinib. Second, with the small sample of STS, we did not distinguish subtypes in the analysis. The efficacy should be evaluated according to the subtype because STS is a set of heterogeneous sarcomas with diverse sensitivity to TKIs. Third, patients treated at our center mainly had osteosarcoma, leading to selection bias. Fourth, the drug combinations were complicated and we did not classify them in detail, which may affect the accuracy of comparison among different combination therapies. Fifth, it is difficult to determine how large a contribution fruquintinib made to the results, as more than 80% of the patients received fruquintinib in combination with other effective substances in this retrospective analysis. Finally, the follow‐up time for partial cases was short. Also, some patients might have been in the end‐stage of disease and some had not yet progressed but died for various reasons.

## Conclusions
Fruquintinib may be a potential option for patients with refractory bone and soft tissue sarcomas after developing several lines of resistance to TKIs, with a satisfactory efficacy and safety profile in combination therapy. Greater effects may be obtained for target lesions confined to the lung or bone due to a lower tumor burden. However, the degree of contribution of fruquintinib to results is unclear when combined with other effective substances. Further prospective trials of fruquintinib should be carried out, especially involving different pathological types and diverse combination regimens.
