1. RARE YET RESILIENT: SYNOVIAL SARCOMA OF THE PAROTID GLAND, AN UNCOMMON PRESENTATION AT A RARE SITE - A CASE REPORT

Alokananda Mandal1, Vandana Raphael1, Yookarin Khonglah1, Biswajit Dey1, R Naveen Kumar1, Chayasmita Mali1, Mainak Bhattacharjee1, Vikas Jagtap2, Donboklang Lynser3; 1Department of Pathology, North Eastern Indira Gandhi Regional Institute of Health and Medical Sciences, Shillong, Meghalaya, India, 2Department of Radiation Oncology, North Eastern Indira Gandhi Regional Institute of Health and Medical Sciences, Shillong, Meghalaya, India, 3Department of Radiology, North Eastern Indira Gandhi Regional Institute of Health and Medical Sciences, Shillong, Meghalaya, India

Introduction: Synovial sarcoma is a rare and aggressive malignant soft tissue tumor, primarily affecting the extremities. Primary synovial sarcoma of the parotid gland is extremely uncommon. Due to its histological resemblance to other neoplasms, accurate diagnosis can be challenging, making up only a small fraction of salivary gland malignancies. Case Description: A 30-year-old female presented with a progressive swelling over the left parotid region for four months, which had increased in size over the past two weeks. On examination, the swelling was tender with associated facial nerve palsy. Imaging revealed a large lesion completely replacing both the superficial and deep lobes of the left parotid gland, extending into adjacent structures. FNAC suggested a Malignant Spindle Cell Lesion. Subsequent core needle biopsy with Immunohistochemistry (IHC) correlation confirmed the diagnosis of Synovial Sarcoma. Discussion: Initially suspected to be a Pleomorphic Adenoma, further investigations confirmed a Malignant Salivary Gland Neoplasm. Differentiation from Myoepithelial Carcinoma and Solitary Fibrous Tumor is crucial for a definitive diagnosis and treatment planning. Conclusion: Parotid Gland Synovial Sarcoma is a rare malignancy with both epithelial and spindle cell differentiation. Although typically found in the extremities, its occurrence in the parotid gland is exceptionally rare, necessitating thorough histopathological and Immunohistochemistry (IHC) analysis for accurate diagnosis.

2. FNAC DIAGNOSIS OF TWO UNUSUAL ADIPOCYTIC LESIONS.

A P Ananya, Norton Stephen, Arun Kumar, Niraimathi, Balamurugan; Department of Pathology, JIPMER, Karikal, Puducherry, India

Introduction: Fine needle aspiration cytology (FNAC) plays a crucial role in diagnosis superficially accessible adipocytic soft tissue lesions. We present two cases unusual adipocytic lesion: 1. Atypical spindle cell/pleomorphic lipomatous tumor (ASPLT), a novel entity recognized in the recent WHO Soft tissue tumors. 2. Hibernoma, tumor of brown fat, in a rare location- the abdomen. Objective: To emphasis the role of FNAC in rare adipocytic soft tissue lesions. Case Report: Case 1: ASCPLT: A 44-year-old male presented with a swelling on dorsum of the hand measuring 6x6 cm, well defined. On FNAC, Aspirate smears showed cellular adipocytic fragments admixed with pleomorphic stromal cells, lipoblasts, multinucleated giant cells and floret-like cells are seen and diagnosis of pleomorphic lipoma/ ASPLT was given. On excision, specimen measuring 8x8x6 cm revealed circumscribed, biphasic tumor comprising adipocytic component and stromal component. There are lobules of adipocytes admixed with multinucleated conventional lipoblasts. The stroma shows sheets of numerous atypical spindle cells with marked pleomorphism. Mitosis is <1/ mm2 and no areas of necrosis/ dedifferentiated areas seen. MDM2 IHC was negative. The final diagnosis of “atypical spindle cell/ pleomorphic lipomatous tumor’ (ASPLT) was given. Case 2: Hibernoma: A 45-year male with 4x4x2 cm lump in anterior abdomen. FNAC revealed Polygonal to oval cells with multivacuolated adipocytes with central round nucleus and granular eosinophilic cytoplasm (brown fat) with mature adipocytes. A diagnosis of Hibernoma is given and confirmed on excision. Conclusion: This paper highlights the subtle cytomorphological clues on aspiration smears that helps Pathologists to pick these rare and unusual adipocytic tumors, which is the crux of the paper.

3. CERVICAL SOFT TISSUE METASTASIS OF CLIVAL CHORDOMA: A CYTOLOGIC DIAGNOSIS

Subarna Sharma Pinky, Abha Thakur, Haimanti Sarin; Department of Pathology, Medanta-The Medicity, Gurugram, Haryana, India

Introduction: Chordoma is a rare malignant neoplasm of notochordal origin, accounting for 1-4% of primary bone tumors. The clivus and sacrococcygeal region are the most frequent sites of occurrence. These tumors, although histologically low-grade, demonstrate locally invasive growth, resistance to conventional therapies, and a high propensity for late recurrence or metastasis. Reported metastatic sites include the lungs, liver, lymph nodes and bone. Involvement of cervical soft tissues is exceptionally uncommon. Cytological diagnosis of metastatic chordoma is particularly challenging due to its rarity and potential overlap with other myxoid and clear cell tumors. Objectives: To present a rare case of metastatic chordoma manifesting as a right cervical nodule two years following definitive treatment of a clival primary, and to emphasize the pivotal role of fine-needle aspiration cytology (FNAC). Case Report: A 39-year-old male, previously managed with surgical excision and adjuvant radiotherapy for clival chordoma, presented two years later with a gradually enlarging right cervical swelling. Clinical examination revealed a discrete, firm, and mobile mass. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the neck demonstrated a well-defined, heterogeneously enhancing mass lesion in the soft tissues involving right sternocleidomastoid muscle. FNAC smears were moderately cellular, composed of cords, clusters, and singly dispersed tumor cells within a myxoid background. The cells exhibited abundant vacuolated cytoplasm and round to oval nuclei, with characteristic physaliphorous cells. Correlation with the clinical history and radiological findings substantiated the diagnosis of metastatic chordoma. The patient was referred for multidisciplinary evaluation and palliative management. Conclusion: Chordomas of clival origin, though indolent, retain the capacity for late metastatic spread to unconventional sites. MRI provides detailed anatomical characterization, while cytological recognition of physaliphorous cells offers a rapid and minimally invasive means of confirmation. Sustained long-term surveillance remains essential, as delayed extra-axial metastases pose significant diagnostic and therapeutic challenges.

4. UNMASKING PLEOMORPHIC SARCOMA: A DIAGNOSTIC ODYSSEY IN THE UPPER LIMB

M Bala Abirami, R L Geetha, J Shalini, T R Supriya, B R Vani; Department of Pathology, ESIC Medical College and PGIMSR, Bangalore, Karnataka, India

Introduction: Pleomorphic sarcoma is a highly aggressive and rare soft tissue tumor known for its rapid progression and diagnostic complexity. Its heterogeneous presentation poses significant challenges in clinical evaluation and management, often requiring a combination of advanced imaging, cytopathology, and immunohistochemistry for accurate diagnosis. Objectives: To highlight the diagnostic challenges and clinical significance of pleomorphic sarcoma in the upper limb, emphasizing the importance of a multidisciplinary approach in timely recognition, diagnosis, and formulation of an effective treatment strategy. Case Report: A 42-year-old male presented with a sudden onset, rapidly enlarging swelling in the lateral aspect of his left arm, persisting over three months. Clinical examination revealed a firm, non-tender, immobile mass measuring 7 × 5 cm. MRI of the left arm identified a well-defined, large lobulated lesion (11.5 × 8 × 11 cm) occupying the postero-lateral intramuscular plane, raising strong suspicion for a malignant neoplasm. FNAC demonstrated a moderately cellular smear featuring highly atypical spindle-shaped cells, increased nuclear-cytoplasmic ratio, prominent nucleoli, and occasional bizarre multinucleate forms, with background RBCs. Histopathology confirmed a high-grade malignant spindle cell neoplasm. Immunohistochemistry was crucial in diagnosis, showing tumor cells positive for SMA, CD34, vimentin, and desmin, but negative for S100, supporting the diagnosis of pleomorphic sarcoma and ruling out neural differentiation. In view of the high grade and extensive local involvement, the patient was urgently referred for oncological evaluation, resulting in initiation of radiotherapy. The patient continues to be on close follow-up, with ongoing monitoring for recurrence and metastatic spread. Conclusion: This case underscores the necessity of timely, coordinated multidisciplinary intervention in pleomorphic sarcoma, highlighting the significance of integrating imaging, cytology, and immunohistochemistry for precise diagnosis and management. Early detection and aggressive treatment remain pivotal in improving patient outcomes in these challenging malignancies.

5. GOUTY TOPHUS ON PINNA

Katyayni Singh, Priyanka Munjal, Anand Kumar Verma; Department of Pathology, ESIC PGIMSR BASAIDARAPUR, New Delhi, India

Introduction: Gouty tophi arise from monosodium urate (MSU) crystal deposition due to hyperuricemia, commonly affecting joints like the metatarsophalangeal but rarely the pinna or auricle. Risk groups include middle-aged men with hypertension, obesity, or purine-rich diets. These lesions on the helical rim or antihelix are typically painless, well-circumscribed nodules that may mimic neoplasms, chondrodermatitis nodularis helicis, or elastotic nodules. Hyperuricemia is a key risk factor, though some cases present with normal serum uric acid (SUA). Diagnosis involves demonstrating negatively birefringent needle-shaped MSU crystals via FNAC and polarized microscopy, distinguishing from pseudogout (positively birefringent) or tumoral calcinosis (non-birefringent). Objective: This presentation is to report a rare case of gouty tophus on the pinna in a patient with hyperuricemia, diagnosed on FNAC using polarizing microscopy, and review diagnostic challenges and management. Case Report: A 41-year-old male presented with painless swelling over the left pinna and left elbow for the last 5-6 months with gradual increase in size. Patient had a history of multiple joint pains for 7-8 years. On examination the swelling on the pinna was 0.5 × 0.5 cm in size with yellow crusting. The swelling on the elbow was 1 × 1cm. Both the swellings were soft, nontender and mobile. Patient had a history of hyperuricemia (SUA 8.2mg/dl). USG elbow revealed ill-defined heteroechoic areas with no internal vascularity. Possibility of olecranon bursitis/tophus/RA nodule was given. FNAC yielded chalky white material. Smears examined showed amorphous granular debris with stacks of needle-shaped crystals. Polarizing microscopy confirmed negatively birefringent MSU crystals, so a diagnosis of gouty tophus was given. Conclusion: Gouty tophi on the pinna, though uncommon, should be considered in hyperuricemic patients with auricular nodules. FNAC with polarized microscopy provides rapid, minimally invasive confirmation of MSU crystals, avoiding misdiagnosis as neoplasm. Early urate-lowering therapy prevents complications; clinicians must recognize atypical sites for timely intervention.

6. OSTEOSARCOMA AND ITS NODAL INVOLVEMENT: CHALLENGING THE PARADIGM OF HEMATOGENOUS SPREAD

Apurva Arora1, Deepak Vedant1, Vikarn Vishwajeet1, Rashim Sharma1, Sangeeta Pradhan1, Purnima Shree1, Poonam Abhay Elhence1, Binit Sureka2; 1Department of Pathology and Lab Medicine, AIIMS, Jodhpur, Rajasthan, India, 2Department of Diagnostic and Interventional Radiology, AIIMS, Jodhpur, Rajasthan, India

Introduction: Osteosarcoma is the most commonly diagnosed primary malignancy of bone, particularly among children and adolescents. It is more common in males (1.5: 1–2: 1) than in females. The tumor demonstrates a predilection for metaphyseal regions of long bones, most commonly involving the distal femur, proximal tibia, and proximal humerus. Osteosarcomas commonly metastasize hematogenously to the lungs and bones. In contrast, lymphatic spread and nodal involvement are exceedingly uncommon. Case Report: An 11-year-old male presented with distal femoral osteosarcoma and multiple pulmonary metastases. He received six cycles of chemotherapy followed by above-knee amputation, with histopathology confirming conventional osteosarcoma. One month post-surgery, imaging revealed persistent lung metastases along with mediastinal, inguinal, and external iliac lymphadenopathy. FNAC confirmed metastatic osteosarcoma in the inguinal node, highlighting the rare occurrence of lymphatic spread in osteosarcoma. Conclusion: This case underscores the critical role of fine needle aspiration cytology (FNAC) in the diagnostic evaluation of atypical metastatic patterns in osteosarcoma. While lymphatic spread is rare in primary bone sarcomas, timely identification through FNAC can confirm nodal involvement, guide staging, and influence treatment decisions. In complex presentations with unusual metastatic sites, FNAC remains a minimally invasive, rapid, and reliable tool for confirming metastatic disease, ultimately aiding in comprehensive disease assessment and personalized patient management.

7. INTRA-OPERATIVE SQUASH CYTOLOGY OF SACRAL CHORDOMA - A CASE REPORT

Deepak Vedant, Sachin Kulkarni, Saloni Middha, Monira Hassan, Apurva Arora, Arun, Kshitiz Mahajan, Poonam Abhay Elhence; Department of Pathology, Diagnostic and Interventional Radiology, Neurosurgery, AIIMS Jodhpur, Rajasthan, India

Introduction: Squash cytology is a rapid, cost-effective technique and plays a vital role in the intraoperative diagnosis of central nervous system tumors. Chordoma is a rare primary central nervous system tumor of notochordal origin. An accurate preoperative diagnosis is essential for surgical management. In cases where preoperative diagnosis could not be established, the intraoperative squash cytology provides a quick diagnosis. Case Report: A 53-year-old male presented with right gluteal pain and constipation for 4 months. The NCCT lumbosacral spine revealed a well-defined, lobulated, expansile soft tissue mass lesion involving the sacrum, predominantly arising from the midline and centered at S3–S4. The sacral vertebral body and posterior elements were destroyed with an associated cortical breach. The mass was extending into the presacral space involving mesorectal fat. The patient underwent laminectomy, and intraoperative tissue was sent for evaluation. The squash smear showed the presence of epithelioid cells with vacuolated/ bubbly cytoplasm (physaliphorous cells) in a myxoid matrix. The possibility of chordoma was suggested. Conclusion: Squash smear cytology is a reliable and rapid intraoperative diagnostic modality for the diagnosis of sacral chordomas. The characteristic cytological features can be easily examined in well-spread smears.

8. UTILITY OF FINE-NEEDLE ASPIRATION CYTOLOGY OF ARTICULAR AND PERIARTICULAR LESIONS AT A TERTIARY CARE HOSPITAL

Sai Jyoti, Prajwala Gupta, Shraddha Singh; Department of Pathology, ABVIMS and Dr RML Hospital, New Delhi, India

Introduction: Articular and periarticular swellings pose a diagnostic challenge due to overlapping clinical and radiologic features. Fine-needle aspiration cytology (FNAC) is a rapid, minimally invasive technique that aids early categorization of these lesions for clinical decision-making. Objectives: To assess the diagnostic efficacy of FNAC and characterize the spectrum of cytological findings in articular and periarticular swellings. Case Report: This retrospective study analyzed 222 FNAC samples from palpable articular and periarticular swellings over six years (2019–2024). Standard cytological stains including Giemsa, Papanicolaou, and Ziehl-Neelsen were used. Out of 222 cases, 192 yielded diagnostic aspirates. Non-neoplastic lesions predominated (61.4%), with ganglion cysts (28.6%) and epidermal inclusion cysts (17.7%) being most frequent. Benign neoplasms accounted for 36.9%, predominantly lipomas and mesenchymal lesions. Malignancies were rare (1.6%). Most lesions occurred in the upper limb, particularly the wrist (28.4%). Cytological findings showed 85.4% concordance with clinico-radiological or histopathological diagnoses. Conclusion: FNAC proves to be a highly effective frontline diagnostic tool for evaluating articular and periarticular swellings. Its accuracy in identifying benign versus malignant pathology enables timely management and reduces the need for surgical intervention.

9. EWING’S SARCOMA: CYTOLOGY OF THREE RARE PRESENTATIONS

Prachi Prajapati1, Meeta Singh1, Nimisha Dhankar2, Lovenish Bain3; 1Department of Pathology, Maulana Azad Medical College, New Delhi, India, 2Department of Pathology, GB Pant, New Delhi, India, 3Department of Surgery, Maulana Azad Medical College, New Delhi, India.

Introduction: Ewing’s sarcoma (ES) is an aggressive malignant tumour of bone and soft tissues, primarily affecting adolescents and young adults. Although Primary site is bone but extraskeletal Ewing’s Sarcoma (EES) constitutes 20%–30% of cases and may arise in atypical anatomical locations,complicating diagnosis and management. Due to rare occurrence and histological overlap with other small round cell tumours, each case required thorough evaluation to improve diagnostic accuracy and more effective treatment strategies. Objective: To present three rare cases of Ewing’s sarcoma occurring at unusual sites and to highlight the diagnostic importance of cytology, histopathology and immunohistochemistry for accurate classification and appropriate treatment. Case Report: Case 1: A 15-year-old girl presented with a lobulated swelling on the left face. Fine-needle aspiration cytology (FNAC) showed monomorphic small round blue cells with PAS positivity and strong CD99 expression. Histopathology confirmed Ewing’s sarcoma. Case 2: A 3-year-old girl had a rapidly enlarging mass over the left chest wall and axillary fold. FNAC revealed dual-population small round cells with necrosis; immunocytochemistry was positive for CD99, vimentin and NSE. A Trucut biopsy confirmed Ewing’s sarcoma. Case 3: A 60-year-old woman presented with bilateral breast swellings with nipple ulceration. FNAC showed atypical round cells with marked pleomorphism. IHC demonstrated vimentin andCD56 positivity but lacked epithelial markers. The findings were consistent with a high-grade malignant neoplasm with features overlapping Ewing’s sarcoma. Conclusion: These cases emphasize the clinical and histological diversity of Ewing’s sarcoma, especially in extra-skeletal locations. Awareness of its variable presentation and use of a comprehensive immunohistochemical panel (CD99, vimentin, NSE) are vital for distinguishing it from differentials such as neuroblastoma, rhabdomyosarcoma, lymphoma and neuroendocrine carcinoma, thereby preventing misdiagnosis and overtreatment.

10. NOT WHERE YOU EXPECT THEM: EXPLORING THE SPECTRUM OF METASTATIC SARCOMAS

Janani Parthasarathy, Neelaiah Siddaraju, Debasis Gochhait; Department of Pathology, Jawaharlal Institute of Postgraduate Medical Education and Research, JIPMER, Puducherry, India

Introduction: Metastatic sarcomas present significant diagnostic and therapeutic challenges due to their diverse histologic subtypes and variable metastatic patterns. Fine needle aspiration cytology (FNAC) is a minimally invasive, rapid diagnostic tool that aids in the early detection and staging of metastatic lesions, thereby guiding timely management. Despite its increasing use, the diagnostic accuracy and challenges of FNAC in metastatic sarcoma require further elucidation, particularly in the context of evolving sarcoma subtypes and immunocytochemical techniques. Objectives: To analyze the cytologic features and diagnostic accuracy of FNAC in 22 cases of metastatic sarcoma diagnosed over the last five years. To correlate FNAC findings with primary sarcoma subtypes and metastatic sites. Case Series: This retrospective study includes 22 patients (14 males, 8 females; age range 6–69 years) presenting with metastatic lesions confirmed by FNAC. Primary sarcoma diagnoses encompassed a broad spectrum: leiomyosarcoma, synovial sarcoma, epithelioid sarcoma, malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumor (MPNST), pleomorphic liposarcoma, alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma, embryonal rhabdomyosarcoma, osteosarcoma, Ewing’s sarcoma, and undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma. The lymph nodes (13/22), including inguinal, axillary, supraclavicular, submandibular, and cervical regions, were the most common metastatic sites, followed by the scalp (4/22), liver (3/22), pleura (1/22), and subcutaneous nodules (1/22). FNAC smears were hypercellular exhibiting spindle, epithelioid, or round cell morphology depending on subtype. Common cytologic features included nuclear pleomorphism, mitotic activity, necrosis, and cell discohesion. Immunocytochemistry was utilized as needed to confirm the diagnosis. Conclusion: FNAC is a valuable and reliable diagnostic tool for detecting metastatic sarcoma, demonstrating high sensitivity for detection of malignancy and potential for subtype identification when combined with immunocytochemistry and clinical correlation. This case series highlights the diverse presentations of metastatic sarcoma and reinforces the role of FNAC in early diagnosis and treatment planning. Continued refinement of cytologic criteria and integration of ancillary studies will further improve diagnostic accuracy.

11. ADENOID CYSTIC CARCINOMA OF THE MALE BREAST: A DIAGNOSTIC PITFALL ON FINE NEEDLE ASPIRATION CYTOLOGY

K Archana, Bitan Nayak, Mahima Yadav; Department of Pathology, Institute of Medical Sciences, BHU, Varanasi, Uttar Pradesh, India

Introduction: ACC (adenoid cystic carcinoma) is commonly seen in the major and minor salivary glands. Breast ACC is a very rare primary breast cancer, making up less than 0.1% of all breast malignancies. Of these, male patients are even rarer. Cytological diagnosis can be challenging due to its rarity and overlapping features with other benign and malignant breast lesions. Case Report: We report a 63-year-old male presenting with a palpable lump in the left breast of 2 years duration. Ultrasound revealed nodular left breast gynecomastia. FNA smears are cellular and show many cohesive clusters and fragments of epithelial cells admixed with myoepithelial cells on a mildly hemorrhagic background. Many bare nuclei, occasional stromal fragments and few eosinophilic globules are also noted embedded within the clusters. Based on the presence of myoepithelial cells, a diagnosis of benign breast disease was rendered. Histopathology Findings: Subsequent wide local excision revealed an infiltrating tumor disposed in tubular, cribriform and solid nodules. The tumor cells show uniform basaloid cells with minimal atypia. Few tubules and cribriform spaces show myxoid globules. No evidence of malignancy or high grade transformation is noted. Immunohistochemistry findings were consistent with the diagnosis of ACC. Conclusion: This case highlights the diagnostic difficulty of ACC on FNAC, where uniform and cohesive epithelial cells and admixed myoepithelial cells may lead to under-diagnosis. Awareness of this pitfall is important to avoid false-negative reports. Correlation with histopathology remains crucial for definitive diagnosis.

12. ADENOID CYSTIC CARCINOMA OF THE BREAST

Sushma, Th Deeparani, Kh Sucheta Devi, Sharmila Laishrum, O Okendrajit Singh, Mutum Reeta Devi, Sushma Khuraijam; Department of Pathology, Regional Institute of Medical Science, Imphal, Manipur, India

Introduction: Adenoid cystic carcinoma (ACC) of the breast is a rare malignancy, accounting for less than 0.1% of all breast cancers. Objectives: To highlight the cytological features of adenoid cystic carcinoma of the breast on fine-needle aspiration cytology (FNAC) and emphasize the importance of careful evaluation for early suspicion, while reaffirming that histopathology remains essential for definitive diagnosis. Case Report: A 61-year-old female presented with complaints of a left breast lump for 1½ months. Local examination revealed a single, firm, non-tender, subareolar lump in the left breast measuring 1.5 × 1 cm and brownish discoloration over the nipple–areolar region. Examination of the contralateral breast showed no palpable lump, and bilateral axillae revealed no palpable swelling. Fine-needle aspiration was done and yielded blood-mixed material. Microscopic examination revealed highly cellular smear and showed numerous hyaline strands and spherical globules of varying sizes with adherent basaloid cells showing mild to moderate pleomorphism, high N: C ratio, coarse chromatin and scant amount of cytoplasm. A few cells exhibited nuclear moulding. Many scattered naked nuclei along with few cup-shaped epithelial fragments were also noted. Background showed blood mixed with eosinophilic proteinaceous material. The overall cytomorphological features were suggestive of Adenoid Cystic Carcinoma of the breast, and histopathological examination was advised for definitive confirmation. Conclusion: Adenoid cystic carcinoma of the breast is an uncommon entity that can be suspected on FNAC through its distinctive cytological features. Careful interpretation is essential to differentiate it from other malignant breast tumors, and confirmation with histopathology remains essential for accurate diagnosis and management.

13. DIFFUSE LARGE B-CELL LYMPHOMA INVOLVING THE CERVIX: A RARE DIAGNOSIS VIA LIQUID-BASED CYTOLOGY

Sofia Dondapati1, Navatha Vangala1, Tara Roshni Paul1, G Sadashivudu1, Radhika Gollapudi2; 1Department of Pathology, Medical Oncology, Nizam’s Institute of Medical Sciences, Hyderabad, Telangana, India, 2Department of Gynaecology, Nizam’s Institute of Medical Sciences, Hyderabad, Telangana, India

Introduction: Extranodal involvement in Diffuse Large B-Cell Lymphoma (DLBCL) can rarely affect the female genital tract. Cervical infiltration, especially diagnosed via cytology, is extremely uncommon and often mimics primary gynaecological malignancies. Objectives: To highlight the rare involvement of the uterine cervix by DLBCL and demonstrate the diagnostic utility of liquid-based cytology (LBC). Case report: An 81-year-old immunocompetent female with no known comorbidities presented with swelling of the right chest wall and right axilla, along with intermittent fever for one month. PET-CT revealed mild FDG uptake in the skin over right breast with ill-defined retro areolar thickening (15 × 16 mm) and widespread lymphadenopathy including axillary, supraclavicular, internal mammary, para-aortic, intra-abdominal, bilateral external iliac, and inguinal nodes. Axillary lymph node biopsy confirmed Non-Hodgkin’s Lymphoma—Diffuse Large B-Cell Lymphoma (DLBCL), Non-GCB type. The patient underwent six cycles of chemotherapy. A post-treatment PET-CT showed a bulky uterus with intense FDG uptake involving the myometrium, cervix, vagina, and bilateral adnexa. Per speculum examination revealed a hypertrophied and congested cervix with atrophic vaginal mucosa. LBC of cervical brushings showed numerous medium-to-large atypical lymphoid cells with round to oval nuclei, open chromatin, scant cytoplasm, and admixed squamous epithelial cells. A cell block prepared from the residual LBC sample showed diffuse CD20 positivity and a high Ki-67 index on immunohistochemistry, confirming cervical involvement in a known case of DLBCL. Conclusion: Non-Hodgkin lymphoma of the uterine cervix is rare, accounting for 0.5%–1% of primary and up to 40% of secondary cases in disseminated disease. While more common in immunocompromised individuals, the exact cause remains unclear. It can mimic chronic cervicitis or undifferentiated carcinoma. This case illustrates the rare extranodal involvement of the cervix by DLBCL, diagnosed through LBC, and highlights the importance of cytology in detecting lymphoma relapse or progression at uncommon sites.

14. ISOLATED EARLY 5TH RIB BONY METASTASIS OF LOW-GRADE ENDOMETRIAL CARCINOMA – USG GUIDED FNAC DIAGNOSIS

Kalpana Kumari, Kaninika Sanyal, Spandan Roy, Chandan Pal1, Preeti Diwaker; Departments of Pathology and 1Radiology, UCMS and GTB Hospital, Delhi, India

Introduction: The occurrence of bone metastases in endometrial carcinoma (EC) is rare, prevalence around 4-7%. Osseous metastasis is confined to the pelvic and vertebral axial skeleton. Metastasis to rib cage (exception to axial skeleton), appendicular skeleton, and extremities is rare. The rare occurrence however impacts prognosis and alters treatment strategies. Identifying targeted therapy option based on molecular profiling would enhance treatment strategies for patients with EC and bone metastases. Objectives: Clinico-pathological and molecular profile of EC presenting with bone metastases. Case History: A 50-year-old female underwent TAH and BSO following biopsy diagnosis of EC. The gross examination showed 8 cm × 3 cm × 3 cm endometrial mass showing more than 50% myometrial invasion. Cut section revealed exophytic grey, white friable growth. Microscopy showed histomorphological features of EC, endometrioid type, FIGO grade 2. The tumor displayed dispersed focus of squamous morules with mild nuclear atypia. A total of 19 bilateral pelvic lymph nodes were sent for histopathological examination, free of tumor. Serosal, cervical, vaginal, or ovarian involvement was not detected. Six months post-surgery, PET scan identified a lytic lesion in the left fifth rib, with no abnormal uptake in the brain, lung, breast, or other skeletal systems. Due to the lesion’s proximity to the heart, biopsy was deemed unfeasible. USG guided FNAC revealed PDC with focal squamous differentiation. Discussion: The case emphasizes importance of incorporating additional prognostic factors, such as LVI, in clinical decision making rather than solely relying on EC grading for initiating post-surgical adjuvant therapy to enhance patient outcomes. Conclusion: USG guided FNAC can be a useful tool for patient management in advance EC. Bone metastases in LG EC need to be defined prognostically and molecularly for better survival outcomes.

15. VULVAR MALIGNANT MELANOMA: CYTOLOGICAL PITFALLS IN AN UNCOMMON TUMOR

Introduction: Vulvar melanoma represents the second most common malignancy of the vulva after squamous cell carcinoma. Although rare, with an incidence of approximately 0.2 per 100,000 women and accounting for only 1-2% of all melanomas, the vulva remains the most frequent site of melanoma within the female genital tract. It typically affects women of Caucasian ethnicity, most commonly between the ages of 60 and 63 years, though pediatric cases have also been reported. Overall, vulvar melanoma carries an unfavorable prognosis. Objectives: To describe the cytological features of vulvar malignant melanoma, discuss the potential diagnostic pitfalls in cytology, and highlight the pivotal role of histopathology in confirming the final diagnosis. Case Report: We report a case of a 62 yr old female who presented with a lesion on the vulva since 1 year, associated with itching and foul smelling urethral discharge from one month. On examination a hyperpigmented plaque with rough surface of size 4 × 5 cm over left labia majora was noted extending to involve right labia majora, labia minora and vaginal wall. On pap smear, crowded sheets and singly scattered highly atypical cells with high N: C ratio, irregular nuclear membrane, inconspicuous nucleoli and dense cytoplasm were seen, some of which showed intracytoplasmic brown black pigment. Further incisional biopsy confirmed the lesion to be Malignant Melanoma with tumor cells showing positivity for HMB 45. Conclusion: This case underscores the diagnostic challenges of vulvar malignant melanoma on cytology, where features may overlap with squamous intraepithelial lesions, and highlights the role of histopathology for definitive diagnosis, reinforcing the need for careful cytomorphological evaluation of subtle features in such rare presentations.“

16. UNVEILING OVARIAN ADENOSARCOMA VIA CYTOLOGY: DIAGNOSTIC CHALLENGES AND INSIGHTS

Purvasha Kardam, Tanvi Paul, Nidhi Verma, Varuna Mallya, Y M Mala; Maulana Azad Medical College, New Delhi, India

Introduction: Müllerian adenosarcoma is a rare mixed neoplasm characterized by a benign glandular (epithelial) component and a malignant stromal (mesenchymal) component.
