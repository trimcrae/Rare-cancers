## Abstract

## Introduction
Extra-axial chordomas are extremely rare tumours that are characterised by slow growth and local aggressiveness and can potentially metastasise during follow-up.

## Materials and methods
This is the case of a 37-year-old woman with a bone lesion in the distal third of her fibula, with mild symptoms at that site consisting of a mass and mild pain. After local and distant staging, a percutaneous core needle biopsy was performed and a diagnosis of malignant bone tumour with cartilage differentiation was made, although the diagnosis and lesion grade could not be specified.

## Results
After evaluation by the sarcoma committee, and given the biopsy diagnosis and negative distant extension, wide resection and reconstruction of the tibio-fibular reconstruction complex were performed. The definitive diagnosis was a well-differentiated extra-axial chondroid bone chordoma in the cortex of the fibula. Four years after the procedure, the patient is disease free, with good ankle function and independent in her activities of daily living. An AOFAS score of 62 points, an MSTS-ISOLS score of 18 points (60%) and SF-12 scores of 35.56 on the PCS and 24.58 on the MCS were recorded.

## Conclusion
Bone and soft tissue neoplasms require local and distant staging prior to biopsy. For a suspected diagnosis, clinical, anatomical and radiological correlation must be maintained. Treatment can proceed after the case has been reviewed by a multidisciplinary sarcoma committee. In chordoma cases, extra-axial locations are extremely rare. Reconstruction of the ankle presents major anatomical difficulties, with multiple techniques having been described. In such cases, a careful pre-surgical anatomical study must be performed to obtain wide margins and plan the reconstruction based on the type of patient, anatomical structures after resection and the multiple techniques described for the lateral ankle region, in order to maintain maximum function without limiting the patient’s quality of life.

## Introduction
 Chordomas are primary malignant bone neoplasms with a notochordal cellular immunophenotype. These tumours are usually located in bones of the axial skeleton, almost exclusively affecting the base of the skull and the spine, including the coccyx; they account for 1 to 4% of malignant bone tumours [ , ]. From the perspective of anatomical pathology, three types have been defined. The most common is conventional chordoma, which presents as a shiny greyish tumour consisting of vacuolated cells in a myxoid stroma, which may mimic the cartilaginous matrix. Immunohistochemistry tests are positive for Brachyury, S100, EMA and cytokeratin AE1/AE3. Next, dedifferentiated chordoma is defined as a biphasic neoplasm consisting of conventional chordoma and high-grade fusiform cell–pleomorphic sarcoma and even heterologous differentiation (osteo-rhabdomyosarcoma). Finally, poorly differentiated chordoma is a poorly differentiated neoplasm with notochordal (non-morphological) differentiation.
Chordomas are characterised by slow growth, local aggressiveness and frequent local recurrence due to their locations and, therefore, the difficulty of achieving adequate margins. Histological grade is not a key factor in the ability of these tumours to metastasise. However, the prognosis is worse in dedifferentiated and poorly differentiated chordoma. Extra-axial location of these lesions is extremely uncommon, but extra-axial lesions share the same histological features as axial lesions. In this respect, the transcription factor Brachyury is key for diagnosis, as it demonstrates notochordal differentiation.
Surgical resection with wide margins is the only potentially curative treatment. However, due to the nature of chordomas and considering that the vast majority of cases involve an extracompartmental location, even if an R0 resection margin is achieved, the potential for local recurrence is relatively frequent [ ]. Furthermore, chordomas respond poorly to conventional chemotherapy, which is not a treatment option except in cases with major local progression. High-dose radiotherapy has been shown to be effective for unresectable lesions, and radiation therapy (electron, proton and heavy particle radiation therapy) combined with surgery also plays a role in the treatment of chordomas, improving the rate of local disease control.
Primary bone tumours of the distal fibula are exceptionally uncommon, representing only a small fraction (around 3–6%) of all primary bone sarcomas, with the distal segment involved far less frequently than the proximal fibula. Among malignant lesions in this region, Ewing sarcoma, osteosarcoma and fibrosarcoma predominate, and tumours arising in the distal third tend to show a comparatively better prognosis than those affecting more proximal locations [ , ].
Resection of the distal fibula is uncommon, and there are various reconstructive techniques available for this region. However, some such techniques may predispose the patient to valgus deformities, requiring a careful approach to preserve ankle alignment and function [ , ].
Here we present the case of a female patient with a chordoma located in the bone of the distal fibula, emphasising its rarity and highlighting the importance of the staging and proper diagnosis of bone and soft tissue lesions. Multiple reconstruction methods have been described after oncological resection in this particular anatomical region. Regardless of the method, the aim must be to optimise the anatomical reconstruction to ensure lateral stability of the ankle in order to maintain proper joint function, while maintaining the patient’s quality of life as far as possible.

## Materials and methods
A 37-year-old woman presented with a mass on the lateral surface of her ankle, with a suspected bone lesion in the distal third of the right fibula. Local and distant staging (by X-ray, MRI and CT scan of the tibia and fibula and bone scintigraphy with Tc-99) was performed to assess the lesion, and a complete body scan and a CT scan of the chest, abdomen and pelvis were performed to rule out secondary deposits. The patient has no functional impairment of her ankle and is living a normal life.

## Results
Conventional radiology showed a geographic lesion on the lateral surface of the fibula, with a periosteal reaction with cortical expansion and small internal lytic foci (Fig.  ). A CT scan was performed (Figs.  A and B), showing a lesion in the lateral cortex of the distal third of the fibula, with a solid and continuous periosteal “buttress” reaction, measuring over 4 cm, as well as a central lytic lesion measuring 7 mm. By MRI (Figs.  C, D and E), the lesion had similar intensity to cartilage, taking up intravenous contrast, with slight oedema in the endosteum despite not penetrating the cortex. The findings were radiologically compatible with periosteal chondroma as a first option, although a biopsy was recommended to confirm this, given the atypical presentation and the inability to differentiate between malignancy and benignity in this bone tumour. For this reason, an extension study was carried out by thoracic-abdominal-pelvic CT scan and bone scintigraphy, finding no evidence of distant disease.
 The patient was then assessed by the sarcoma committee, and a CT-guided core needle biopsy (CNB) was indicated (Fig.  F). Following the anatomical pathology diagnosis of fragments of a cartilaginous neoplasm with atypia, suggestive of chondrosarcoma, and given the discordant clinical and radiological findings and re-evaluation by the committee, a second CNB of the lesion was performed, again guided by CT. At the subsequent committee meeting, after a combined evaluation including all results, anatomical pathology and the morphology of the lesion in imaging studies with no distant involvement, as this was found to be a bone tumour with intermediate aggressiveness and chondral differentiation, a decision was made to provide oncological treatment through wide resection and reconstruction of this anatomical region, preserving the stability and functionality of the ankle, without adjuvant treatment. ## Surgical technique
Using a direct lateral approach, identifying and preserving the superficial fibular nerve, a 5.5 cm intercalary resection of the distal fibula was performed with wide margins, including the skin island from the path of the biopsy, preserving the distal epiphysis of the fibula, and margins were assessed according to the Enneking classification. Preservation of the distal epiphysis of the fibula allowed the anterior and posterior tibiofibular syndesmoses to be spared, with stability being verified by stress tests under dynamic intraoperative radiological control. A cannulated, partially threaded screw with a diameter of 4.5 mm was placed from the distal fibular malleolus remnant to the tibia for stabilisation. An iliac crest graft measuring 6 cm long and 1 cm wide was harvested. This graft was then synthesised using an anatomical distal fibula plate with five distal screws, two on the graft and three proximal, under intraoperative radiological control. Immobilisation with a long-leg splint was used for two weeks, followed by immobilisation with a rigid ankle brace up to the knee. The patient continued offloading until week 6. Progressive loading was authorised from week 6 onwards.
The anatomical pathology diagnosis of the resection specimen was extra-axial bone chordoma, located in the cortex of the fibula, measuring 1.1 cm (Fig.  A and B) and showing cortical thinning and focal infiltration of the medulla (noted as mild oedema in the pre-surgical MRI). No necrosis was identified, and the surgical margins were free of tumour infiltration, with the closest being at 5 mm. The biopsy path, which was included in the specimen, showed scar-like fibrosis and nests of tumour infiltration, with free margins as they were included in continuity with the surgical specimen. In immunohistochemistry tests, the neoplastic cellularity was positive for Brachyury (diffuse) (Fig.  C), S100 (focal), EMA (patchy), SATB2 (focal and faint); it was negative for smooth muscle actin and ERG.
 In follow-up, proper function had been achieved at 7 months, with the patient resuming her work activities and walking without external aids. No ankle instability was noted. Four years after the procedure, complete consolidation of the graft was noted at both ends (Fig.  ), where the synthetic material remains in place. An AOFAS (American Orthopedic Foot and Ankle Society scale) score of 62 out of 100 points, an MSTS (modified functional scale for musculoskeletal tumours) score of 18 out of 30 points (60%) and SF-12 (Short Form 12) scores of 35.56 for the physical component and 24.58 for the mental component were recorded.
 

## Discussion
Chordomas are uncommon bone tumours arising from remnants of the primitive notochord. They are usually located at the base of the skull (32%), spine (32.8%) and sacrum (29.2%). Overall, they have a median survival of 6.29 years [ , ].
These tumours are often indolent and slow-growing until the later stages of the disease. Symptoms vary depending on their size and location, ranging from endocrine disturbances in those located in the saddle area to radicular disturbances in those located in the spine or sphincter control disturbances in those located in the lower sacral region [ ].
Radiologically, they generally appear as destructive lesions, with a bony epicentre surrounded by a soft tissue mass. They typically invade the intervertebral space, extending into the upper or lower vertebrae, unlike osteosarcomas or chondrosarcomas (although these also do so in advanced stages). Calcifications and bone expansions typically appear isointense or hypointense on T1 MRI sequences and hyperintense on T2 sequences [ ]. This type of tumour has normal or reduced uptake of the technetium-99 m isotope at the tumour site in scintigraphy [ ].
Only 5% of chordomas have metastatic presentations, although they can generate secondary deposits in later stages. They usually do this in the lungs, bones, skin and central nervous system. The most important factor in terms of mortality is usually local recurrence, as well as extension prior to the initial resection, both of which are very important factors in predicting the success of treatment; local control of the lesion through surgery, sometimes combined with any kind of radiotherapy, is essential.
Extra-axial chordomas are extremely rare and originate in bone or soft tissue. They were first described by Laskowsky in 1951 as “peripheral chordoma”, with a presumed origin in synovial cells. Dabska used the term “parachordoma”, describing its similarities to chordoma. According to the World Health Organisation (WHO), the tumour is classified as a mixed soft tissue or bone tumour [ ]. Histologically, they are similar to axial chordomas. The main difference is the location, with lesions near bones or in periarticular regions, which makes them more surgically accessible; negative margins can be achieved more easily and adjuvant radiotherapy is generally not required [ ]. Differential diagnoses include conventional and extraskeletal chondrosarcoma. It is difficult to establish a definitive diagnosis by needle biopsy, due to the complex architecture and cellular pleomorphism. For this reason, more tissue is sometimes needed to characterise the lesion, requiring an incisional biopsy to confirm the suspected diagnosis [ ].
The treatment of choice for extra-axial chordomas is surgery with wide margins. In this case, the aim is to achieve negative resection margins and adequate reconstruction of the defect, if necessary. Survival, local recurrence and metastasis rates are not well known, given the unusual nature of this tumour. A recent cohort study after reviewing 86 cases (43 in soft tissue, 25 with bone invasion and 17 exclusively intraosseous) concluded that axial cases and cases without wide and complete resection have higher rates of recurrence [ ]. In our case, which involved wide resection and an extra-axial chordoma, adequate local and systemic control of the disease could be expected, although the patient required long-term follow-up.
Tumours in the distal fibular region are uncommon, including metastases, and there is therefore little evidence in the literature on the ideal type of reconstruction to perform. Techniques must be individualised for each patient and tumour. Some of these techniques spare the metaphyseal-epiphyseal fibular complex and others perform total resection of the fibular malleolus [ , ]. In cases where the distal fibula is fully spared, it is possible to perform either no reconstruction or tibiofibular arthrodesis with screws. For diaphyseal-metaphyseal resections that affect the distal fibula, use of a cortical graft between the lateral malleolus and the proximal fibula may be the recommended technique, as shown in the case described in this article [ , , ].
For total resections of the fibular malleolus, one option is not to perform reconstruction, which could compromise ankle function, leading to a loss of strength and valgus deviation of the hindfoot. Another option is soft tissue reconstruction. One of the most common techniques is transfer of the posterior tibial tendon to the insertion site of the fibularis brevis [ ]. This technique, according to Vaseenon et al., avoids the use of allografts or autografts at the reconstruction site, where tissue consolidation could be compromised by adjuvant radiotherapy or chemotherapy [ ]. The resulting post-immobilisation fibrosis would help to achieve proper function, despite a reduced range of motion relative to the contralateral ankle. Other soft tissue techniques described involve the use of meshes or autografts for reconstruction of the external fibulotalar ligament complex. These could lead to greater instability in the tibiotalar region or progression to progressive valgus deformities of the hindfoot.
Another interesting option is reconstruction of the distal tibia using bone; homolateral free flap reconstruction of the proximal fibula is likely to be performed. Kiyokawa et al. recommend rapid recovery of blood flow to the bone, as well as sparing the soft tissue around the bone [ ]. Donor bone morbidity in the knee area would consist of a lesion in the external popliteal sciatic nerve, as well as a lesion in the collateral ligament of the knee [ ]. Other techniques involve customised autografts, allowing adequate congruence with the talus associated with soft tissue reconstruction, and report good results without significant comorbidities [ ]. The use of allografts avoids donor site morbidity, but there is a probability of graft incorporation as well as a risk of infection. Other options involve tibiotalar joint arthrodesis, which results in a loss of mobility but an acceptable functional outcome [ ].
The final outcome will thus depend on multiple factors such as the type of surgery, the initial tumour, the use of adjuvant radiation therapy or chemotherapy, the rate of local recurrence or post-surgical complications [ , , , ]. As such and as far as possible, we believe that sparing and fixation of the distal fibular remnant is essential to maintaining lateral ankle stability, enabling joint function in the frontal plane, as well as proper soft tissue coverage, while avoiding complications such as valgus deformity and instability, ankle instability and subsequent progression to osteoarthritis [ ]. This article describes the defect reconstruction technique using a non-vascularised iliac crest autograft to fill the 6 cm bone defect, with subsequent fixation using a plate and screws, as well as stabilisation of the distal tibiofibular syndesmosis with a trans-syndesmotic screw, which resulted in adequate clinical, functional and aesthetic outcomes with the patient resuming her work and activities of daily living.
To the best of our knowledge, this is the first reported case of an extra-axial chordoma arising in the distal fibula. The uniqueness of this case lies in the fact that chordomas are classically restricted to the axial skeleton, and reports of lesions originating in long bones are exceptionally scarce. The cortical, extra-axial location described here broadens the current understanding of the potential sites of origin for chordomas and highlights the importance of maintaining a high index of suspicion when evaluating atypical cartilaginous or chondroid lesions outside the axial skeleton. This case therefore contributes novel pathological and clinical evidence that may aid in the differential diagnosis and surgical management of rare bone tumours with notochordal differentiation.
However, this report is limited by the scarce and mostly case-series–based literature on distal fibular reconstruction, which makes decision-making in this region particularly challenging. Moreover, the patient’s four-year follow-up offers only a moderate mid-term assessment of both oncologic evolution and reconstructive durability.
By reviewing this case, we aim to emphasise the vital importance of adequate management of bone neoplasms (as well as soft tissue neoplasms) by means of proper local and distant staging, which allows us to provide the best information with a good correlation with other additional studies, aiming for a correct diagnosis. Even so, some conditions are very difficult to diagnose, requiring the expertise of a multidisciplinary musculoskeletal tumour committee to minimise inappropriate decisions in both diagnosis and treatment. In our case, given the suspicion of a small primary malignant bone tumour and given the clinical, morphological imaging and pathological biopsy information, wide resection was performed in order to provide proper oncological treatment. Subsequently, with the definitive diagnosis having been made based on the surgical specimen, it was possible to assess the need for adjuvant treatment. Reconstruction of bone lesions in the distal fibula will depend on various factors inherent to the lesion, local involvement, adjuvant treatments and the type of patient in terms of work and activities of daily living, with the main aim being stability in the ankle reconstruction.

## Conclusion
Extra-axial chordoma can be challenging to diagnose, less due to its histology than because it is not recognised in atypical locations. Differential diagnoses should be correlated with other studies, overseen by the musculoskeletal tumour committee.
In cases with oncological reconstruction following resection of the distal fibula, we consider it important to preserve the external malleolar remnant, if the oncological resection allows this, to maintain the stability of the tibiofibulotalar complex. Based on the above, as this tumour is uncommon in this anatomical location, we recommend assessment and treatment at expert centres by multidisciplinary teams with the aim of proper diagnosis and patient management.
