## Objective
To evaluate the clinical efficacy of ultrafast dynamic contrast-enhanced (DCE)-MRI using a compressed sensing (CS) technique for differentiating benign and malignant soft-tissue tumors (STTs) and to evaluate the factors related to the grading of malignant STTs.

## Materials and Methods
A total of 165 patients (96 male; mean age, 61 years), comprising 111 with malignant STTs and 54 with benign STTs according to the 2020 WHO classification, underwent DCE-MRI with CS between June 2018 and June 2023. The clinical, qualitative, and quantitative parameters associated with conventional MRI were also obtained. During post-processing of the early arterial phase of DCE-MRI, the time-to-enhance (TTE), time-to-peak (TTP), initial area under the curve at 60 s (iAUC60), and maximum slope were calculated. Furthermore, the delayed arterial phase parameters of DCE-MRI, including Ktrans, Kep, Ve, and iAUC values and time-concentration curve (TCC) types, were determined. Clinical and MRI parameters were statistically analyzed to differentiate between benign and malignant tumors and their correlation with tumor grading.

## Results
According to logistic regression analysis, the TTE value (P < 0.001) of the early arterial phase and Ve (P = 0.039) and iAUC (P = 0.006) values of the delayed arterial phase, as well as age, location, peritumoral edema, and contrast heterogeneity on conventional MRI, were significant (P = 0.001–0.015) in differentiating benign and malignant tumors. Among all the quantitative parameters, the TTE value had the highest accuracy, with an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve of 0.902. The grading of malignant tumors was significantly correlated with peritumoral edema; CE heterogeneity; visual diffusion restriction; minimum and mean ADC; TTP, Kep, and Ve values; and the TCC graph (all P < 0.05).

## Conclusion
Among the quantitative parameters obtained using ultrafast DCE-MRI, early arterial phase TTE was the most accurate for distinguishing between benign and malignant tumors.

## INTRODUCTION
Soft-tissue tumors (STTs) represent a markedly heterogeneous group of benign and malignant tumors, and overlapping radiological findings make accurate differential diagnosis challenging [ ]. There are limitations in evaluating STTs using only conventional MRI, including static-enhanced images. Images need to be acquired at multiple time points following the injection of the contrast agent because tumors have different contrast-enhancement patterns compared to those of normal tissues. Therefore, rapid imaging is necessary to capture temporal changes after the injection of the contrast agent [ ].
Dynamic contrast-enhanced (DCE)-MRI is an important method because it provides both morphological information on STTs and enables the monitoring of dynamic changes in enhanced characteristics. It involves rapid sequential imaging through an area of interest using intravenous gadolinium [ ]. However, conventional data acquisition methods have limitations in simultaneously enhancing both temporal and spatial resolutions [ ]. Therefore, ultrafast DCE-MRI using faster sequences attempts to extract both morphological and kinetic information during the early post-contrast period [ ].
Compressed sensing (CS) is another accelerated method used to reconstruct a signal from a small number of measurements [ ]. CS can enforce continuity in a time series, enabling very high temporal resolution, while preserving high spatial resolution without sharing data with successive time points [ ]. The addition of the CS technique is expected to reduce the time required to obtain DCE images and analyze the early arterial phase.
This study aimed to investigate the clinical efficacy of ultrafast DCE-MRI using a CS technique that can obtain parameters of the early arterial phase, as well as conventional DCE parameters, in differentiating between benign and malignant STTs and evaluating factors related to the grading of malignant STTs.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS
This retrospective study was approved by the Institutional Review Board of Pusan National University Hospital (IRB No. 1905-009-079). The requirement for informed consent was waived.

## Study Patients
Between June 2018 and June 2023, 634 patients, aged 18 years or older, clinically suspected of having STTs were examined using DCE-MRI with the CS technique.
Among the 634 patients, cases of prior surgery at the same site (n = 369), lack of surgical confirmation (n = 7), adipocytic tumors without apparent vascularity (n = 19), cystic masses inadequate for DCE evaluation (n = 4), lymph node or soft-tissue metastasis (n = 7), intermediate grade tumors (n = 15), and superficial malignancies such as melanoma or squamous cell carcinoma (n = 48) were excluded. Finally, 165 patients (69 female and 96 male; mean age, 61 years; age range, 22–87 years) with an initial single STT were included. According to the World Health Organization (WHO) pathological classification of STTs (2020), all STTs in this study were divided into benign and malignant groups. In total, 111 and 54 patients had malignant and benign STTs, respectively.
The medical records of all patients were reviewed to collect data on age, sex, and pathological results, including final diagnosis and Fédération Nationale des Centres de Lutte Contre Le Cancer (FNCLCC) grading for malignant STTs.

## MRI Protocols
MRI examinations were performed using a 3T scanner (MAGNETOM Skyra, Siemens Healthcare, Erlangen, Germany) with variable coils, depending on the lesion size and location.
Conventional MR series, including T1-weighted (repetition time [TR]/echo time [TE], 331–825 ms/9–20.8 ms; echo train length [ETL], 3–5) and T2-weighted (TR/TE 2220–5883 ms/60–124 ms, ETL 7–23) fast spin-echo sequences with or without fat suppression, primarily in transverse planes and alternatively in sagittal or coronal planes, were initially conducted to locate lesions and display their morphological characteristics. The field of view (FOV) and matrix size were variably determined based on lesion size.
Diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) was also included in the routine protocol. DWIs were acquired in the axial plane using the single-shot echo-planar imaging technique for typical mass lesions or the zoomed echo-planar imaging technique (Zoom-it) for superficial and small-sized lesions. The parameters of the single-shot echo-planar sequence were as follows: b = 0, 500, and 1000 s/mm2; TR/TE of 4100–6800 ms/53–86 ms; FOV of 100–400 mm; matrix size of 68–142 × 42–140; 1–9 (median 4) number of excitations (NEX); slice thickness of 2–5 mm with a variable slice gap; and acquisition time of approximately 4–5 minutes. The parameters of the zoom-it sequence were as follows: b of 0, 500 and 1000 s/mm2; TR/TE, 3900–7700 ms/59–91 ms; phase FOV, 35–100 mm; readout FOV, 70–140 mm; matrix size, 70–100 × 36–72; 6–8 NEX; slice thickness of 2–3 mm with a variable slice gap; and acquisition time of 4–6 min. Apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) maps were generated automatically.
Subsequently, DCE-MR scans were acquired. A gadolinium-based contrast agent (Dotarem, Guerbet, Princeton, NJ, USA) was intravenously infused at a dose of 0.1 mmol/kg and rate of 1.3 mL/s using a power injector, followed by a 20 mL saline flush at the same rate. In the pre-contrast phase, two pre-contrast T1W 3D gradient-echo volumetric interpolated breath-hold examinations (3D VIBE, TR/TE of 5.04–5.69 ms/1.55–2.15 ms, FOV 120–380 mm, matrix 192–224 × 154–157, 1 NEX, 2 mm slice thickness with different flip angles [2°, 8°, and 15°]) were conducted to determine the tissue T1 relaxation times before the injection of the contrast agent. Ultrafast DCE-MRI was performed using a 3D VIBE sequence with CS reconstruction. The sequence parameters are listed in .
A delayed CE image was scanned in all three directions using fat-suppressed spin-echo T1WI (TR/TE, 420–825 ms/7–15 ms; FOV 150–380 mm; matrix size of 256–448 × 192–448; slice thickness of 2–5 mm; ETL, 3–5; and acquisition time of 2–4 minutes).

## Post-Processing of DCE Images
DCE-MRI data were transferred to a post-processing workstation. Quantitative parameters of the early arterial phase were obtained using noncommercial prototype software (DCE toolbox, Siemens Healthineers, Erlangen, Germany). Furthermore, conventional DCE parameters were determined using Tissue 4D software during the delayed arterial phase.

## Image Analysis

## Conventional MR Images, Including DWIs
All images were reviewed by two musculoskeletal radiologists (with 23 and 13 years of experience) who were blinded to the clinical and histopathological findings of the patients. All qualitative analyses and size measurements were conducted in consensus between the two radiologists, without independent reading.
The following lesion characteristics were evaluated: tumor size, location, depth, presence of peritumoral edema, and tumor heterogeneity.
The tumor size was measured in all imaging planes, and the maximum diameter was used. Tumor location was classified as central or peripheral after investigating its anatomical location. Tumors located on the hand or foot, including the wrist and ankle, were classified as peripheral tumors. In contrast, masses located in the upper or lower extremities, including the large joints and trunk, were classified as central. Tumor depth was classified as superficial, deep, or mixed. Tumors located in the dermal, epidermal, or subcutaneous fat layer were defined as superficial, while masses located deeper than the superficial layer of the fascia were considered deep.
The presence or absence of peritumoral edema was assessed using T2WI. Edema was confirmed when a high signal intensity (SI) in an infiltrative or linear pattern was observed around the tumor border. Tumor heterogeneity was evaluated using both T2WI (T2 heterogeneity) and CE imaging (CE heterogeneity). In each T2WI and CE image, if the tumor showed homogeneous SI, it was defined as having no heterogeneity. If high SI or enhancement within the tumor involved more than 50%, it was classified as mild heterogeneity; and if less than 50%, it was classified as moderate heterogeneity.
To evaluate the presence or absence of diffusion restriction by visual analysis, we investigated whether signal drop portions were present on ADC maps in regions corresponding to high SI areas in masses on high-b-value DWIs. To determine ADC values, a region of interest (ROI) was placed to encompass as much of the lesion as possible in the region thought to have the lowest ADC value, that is, the area showing a signal drop on the ADC map. If no signal drop was observed on the ADC map, we drew an ROI of the maximum size within the mass, avoiding mass margins and gross artifacts. Care was taken to include the solid portions of the tumors. In each case, the minimum and mean ADC values were obtained. Two radiologists independently measured the ADC values, and the average values were used.

## DCE-MR Images
Post-processing and analysis of DCE-MRI data were performed by one vendor scientist and two radiologists.

## Analysis of Early Arterial Phase
Ultrafast DCE images were uploaded to the software and motion correction was performed. For the pharmacokinetic model of quantitative data, it is necessary to determine the arterial input function (AIF). AIF was measured from an artery within the FOV adjacent to the tumor in each patient. Next, we set the threshold at which the volume of interest (VOI) was analyzed, and care was taken not to omit the tumor. The breast model in the software was used to quantify the curve characteristics of the early arterial phase [8]. To select the ROI for analysis, we identified the minimum value on the time-to-enhancement (TTE, seconds) map and the maximum value on the maximum slope (MS, %/s) map by referring to conventional images and DWIs. TTE, time-to-peak (TTP) after enhancement, in seconds, the initial area under the curve 60 s after enhancement (iAUC60), and MS were measured in the selected ROI.

## Analysis of Delayed Arterial Phase
We selected an optimal slice by referring to conventional images and DWIs and then applied a VOI to contain the mass. The VOI was drawn almost in line with the outer boundary of the mass according to a previous report [ ]. Based on the color pattern within each given VOI, several ROIs were applied to the regions of greatest presumed hyperperfusion (red). The total pixel areas of the ROIs and VOIs were dependent on the tumor size and the size of the regions with the greatest presumed hyperperfusion.
After applying VOI or ROI, voxel-wise perfusion maps, including volume transfer constant between blood plasma and extracellular/extravascular space (EES) (Ktrans, min−1), rate constant between EES and the blood plasma (Kep, Ktrans/Ve, min−1), volume of EES per unit volume of tissue (Ve), and initial area under the concentration curve (iAUC) were obtained using a dedicated software (Tissue 4D; Siemens Healthcare) by applying Tofts model [ ]. For each patient, the maximum DCE parameter values were used for analysis.
Additionally, time-concentration curves (TCCs) were obtained using Tissue 4D. The TCC plots were classified into four types: 1) Type I, an increase in concentration with time regardless of the shape of the early arterial phase, 2) Type II, an initial increase in concentration followed by a plateau, 3) Type III, an initial increase in concentration followed by a slight decrease or slow washout, and 4) Type IV, an initial increase in concentration followed by rapid washout.

## Statistical Analysis
Continuous variables such as age, size, ADC values, and all DCE parameters are represented as mean values and standard deviations. Multivariable logistic regression analysis was conducted to identify factors useful for differentiating between benign and malignant tumors. The factors included all clinical data, conventional MRI data (including ADC values), and DCE-MRI variables, from both the early and delayed arterial phases. A forward selection method based on the likelihood ratio test was employed. Spearman’s rank correlation analysis and Fisher’s exact test were used to investigate factors correlated with FNCLCC grade in malignant STTs. Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analysis was used to discriminate between malignant and benign STTs. The analysis was performed using IBM SPSS (version 21; IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA) and MedCalc software, version 14.12.0 (Microsoft Partner, Ostend, Belgium). Statistical significance was set at P < 0.05.

## RESULTS
The pathologically confirmed tumor types in the patients included in this study are summarized according to the WHO 2020 classification ( ).
The clinical characteristics and conventional MRI results, including DWI and DCE-MRI, are summarized in .
Patients with malignant STTs were significantly older (P = 0.015) and had larger masses (P = 0.802) than those with benign tumors. If the tumor was located peripherally, the probability of it being benign was high (P = 0.002). In CE images, greater heterogeneity was associated with a higher likelihood of a malignant tumor (P = 0.007). Peritumoral edema also occurred with significantly higher frequency in patients with malignant tumors (P = 0.001).
In contrast, there was a high tendency for diffusion restriction and low ADC values in malignant tumors ( ); however, this was not statistically significant (P = 0.280 and 0.366, respectively) ( ). The TTE value (P < 0.001) among the early arterial phase factors ( , , ), and the Ve (P = 0.039) and iAUC (P = 0.006) values among the delayed arterial phase parameters were significant in differentiating benign and malignant tumors. All four TCC graph types showed no statistically significant difference in distinguishing between benign and malignant tumors (P = 0.831) ( , ).
Factors that significantly correlated with the FNCLCC grade in malignant tumors included peritumoral edema (P < 0.001), depth (P = 0.020), visual diffusion restriction (P = 0.001), minimum and mean ADC values (P < 0.001), TTP (P < 0.001), Kep (P < 0.001), Ve (P = 0.001), and TCC graph (P = 0.009). As the FNCLCC grade increased, the frequency of peritumoral edema, deep location, and diffusion restriction also increased, whereas the minimum and mean ADC and TTP values decreased significantly. Moreover, the higher the FNCLCC grade, the higher the Kep and Ve values and the higher the frequency of types 3 and 4 on the TCC graph ( , ).
The results of the ROC curve analysis for age, size, ADC values, and early and delayed arterial phase factors are presented in . Specifically, when comparing the ROC curves for age, TTE, Ve, and iAUC, which are factors that can significantly distinguish between benign and malignant STTs, the area under the ROC curve for the TTE value was the highest.

## DISCUSSION
Ultrafast DCE-MRI enabled the analysis of the early and conventional arterial phases. In particular, the TTE value in the early arterial phase is an efficient kinetic parameter that can be used to discriminate between benign and malignant STTs. Additionally, several kinetic parameters of the early and delayed arterial phases were correlated with the grading of malignant STTs. Therefore, according to our results, the likelihood of a malignant tumor increases with older patient age, more central tumor location, the presence of peritumoral edema, greater CE heterogeneity; lower TTE values in the early arterial phase; and higher Ve and iAUC values in the delayed arterial phase of DCE-MRI.
Angiogenesis is an essential step in STT growth [ ]. DCE-MRI is an imaging tool for evaluating the microvascular environment of a tumor and measuring the contribution of tissue perfusion and capillary permeability to signal changes caused by the contrast agent [ ]. High temporal and spatial resolutions are key factors in DCE-MRI [ ]. Typically, 3D T1-weighted gradient-echo sequences are used to obtain DCE-MRIs. However, it may not be possible to differentiate arteries from veins using conventional DCE-MRI because of the long scanning time required to achieve an acceptable spatial resolution. Therefore, faster sequences are required to obtain clinically useful DCE-MR images.
CS is a new strategy for accelerating data acquisition using dynamic MRI. Using CS, it is possible to accurately reconstruct an MR image from less Fourier data than when using traditional acceleration methods, resulting in a further reduction in the data-collection burden [ ]. Sparsity, pseudo-random under-sampling, and nonlinear iterative reconstruction are the three essential components of CS [ ]. Because most dynamic images are approximately constant over time, the most significant redundancy often manifests in the temporal direction [ ]. With the application of CS technology to MRI, temporal resolution has improved considerably [ ]. In the present study, ultrafast DCE-MRI using CS enabled high-speed image acquisition, requiring only 4.35–5.26 seconds for scanning the target site. This is about two times the speed of the previous DCE-MRI. Unlike conventional DCE-MRI, parameters of the early arterial phase can be obtained, allowing for additional information when evaluating STTs.
The biological behaviors and microcirculation characteristics of benign and malignant STTs vary, resulting in differences in the pharmacokinetisc distribution of contrast agents [ ]. Conventional DCE-MRI is analyzed in three different ways: time-contrast enhancement curve patterns, semi-quantitative, and quantitative [ ]. The calculated values included the Ktrans (volume transfer constant between EES and blood plasma), Kep (rate constant from EES to blood plasma), and Ve (EES volume per unit tissue volume) [ ]. Tumor vessels have a high level of permeability, which results in a high level of perfusion and permeability in the microcirculation, ultimately resulting in higher Ktrans and Kep values for malignant lesions [ ]. These parameters are obtained using the Toft model. Our previous study [ ] had shown that all DCE-MRI parameters were helpful in differentiating between malignant and benign STTs, with Kep being the most significant factor. However, in ultrafast DCE-MRI, these parameters were obtained in the delayed arterial phase, and Ve and iAUC were statistically significant in distinguishing benign from malignant tumors.
To realize the clinical use of ultrafast DCE-MRI, new kinetic parameters are being sought as alternatives to the classic curve types. MS is a parameter for evaluating the upslope of the time-intensity curve, whereas TTE represents how quickly a lesion starts to enhance [ ]. The MS was determined as the slope of the tangent (percentage relative enhancement/second [%/s]) along the steepest part of the wash-in curve [ ]. The TTE was determined as the time interval between the two time points; “the time point where the lesion starts to enhance” minus “the time point where the aorta starts to enhance” [ ]. To date, only a few studies have been conducted on ultrafast DCE-MRI of STTs, and consistent results have not been obtained. Similar to our findings, Mus et al. [ ] identified another useful parameter called “TTE.” However, Mann et al. [ ] found that the “MS” of the contrast-enhancement versus time curve provided high accuracy in differentiating between malignant and benign lesions. Therefore, larger studies involving more STTs are required to further validate ultrafast DCE-MRI.
The present study has some limitations. First, the number of malignant tumors in the study was approximately twice that of the benign tumors. Most benign masses are often excised without detailed MRI examinations in other hospitals. Only cases where biopsy results indicated malignancy or where the tumor was large with poorly defined margins were referred to a tertiary hospital. Therefore, DCE-MRI is more commonly performed for malignant tumors. Second, selection bias occurred because patients with superficial cancers such as melanoma or squamous cell cancer as well as those with intermediate STTs were excluded. Notably, intermediate STTs are classified as a subcategory in the WHO classification and are also encountered in clinical practice. However, intermediate STTs form an independent group that cannot be classified as either benign or malignant. Therefore, in this study, intermediate STTs were excluded to focus on distinguishing between benign and malignant groups. Third, we could not perform an inter-reader agreement analysis of the DCE-MRI parameters. Special software is required to process raw DCE-MRI data, and the assistance of a professional technician is necessary to use this program effectively. Consequently, the post-processing and analysis of the DCE-MRI data were performed by one vendor scientist and two radiologists.
In conclusion, the kinetic characterization of STTs through early and delayed arterial analysis can be achieved in a significantly shorter time using ultrafast DCE-MRI. The early arterial phase TTE value, along with age, conventional MRI findings (peritumoral edema, location, and CE heterogeneity), and delayed arterial phase DCE-MRI parameters (Ve and iAUC) may enable highly accurate differentiation between benign and malignant STTs.
