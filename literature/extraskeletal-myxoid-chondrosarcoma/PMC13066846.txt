## Introduction
Myoepithelial tumors are neoplasms that differentiate into myoepithelial cells and are classified as myoepitheliomas, myoepithelial carcinomas, or mixed tumors. When ductal structures composed of epithelial cells are intermixed with myoepithelial cells, the lesion is termed a mixed tumor ( ). Although these tumors most commonly arise in the salivary glands (ref. pleomorphic adenomas), their occurrence in soft tissues is rare, and cases occurring in the hands are particularly uncommon ( , ). Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is useful for diagnosing soft tissue tumors; however, because few cases of soft-tissue myoepithelioma and mixed tumors have been reported, their imaging characteristics remain unclear. Histologically, these lesions exhibit a morphologic spectrum of structural and cytologic heterogeneity and may show chondroid differentiation, ossification, or adipocytic metaplasia ( , ); therefore, various MRI appearances can be expected. Here, we report a rare case of a mixed tumor with adipocytic metaplasia arising in the finger that was difficult to diagnose preoperatively because the MRI findings were suggestive of a lipomatous tumor.

## Case presentation
A 65-year-old woman presented to our hospital with a slowly enlarging mass on the right index finger that had been present for 15 years. A soft, elastic mass was palpable distal to the distal interphalangeal (DIP) joint of the right index finger, without swelling, erythema, or tenderness. The range of motion of the finger joints was normal, and Tinel’s sign was negative. Her medical history was unremarkable, and all the blood test results were within normal limits.
Plain radiographs ( ) revealed a radiolucent area without evidence of calcifications or bony abnormalities. MRI ( ) showed a subcutaneous mass in direct contact with the dermis. On T1-weighted imaging ( ), the lesion was predominantly hypointense with focal hyperintense areas that were suppressed on fat-suppressed T1-weighted images ( ), indicating the presence of coarse macroscopic adipocytic components. T2-weighted imaging ( ) demonstrated areas of high signal intensity, and fat-suppressed T2-weighted imaging ( ) revealed a mixture of high- and low-signal intensities, with persistent areas of high signal intensity that were not fully suppressed, suggesting the presence of non-adipocytic components, such as myxoid elements. Contrast-enhanced fat-suppressed T1-weighted imaging ( ) demonstrated mildly heterogeneous enhancement, predominantly in the non-adipocytic portions of the lesion. Based on these findings, a subcutaneous mass in contact with the dermis containing a mixture of myxoid, adipocytic, and fibrous components was suspected. Differential diagnoses included squamous cell carcinoma (SCC), myxoma, spindle cell lipoma (SPL), atypical spindle cell/pleomorphic lipomatous tumor (ASPLT), and liposarcoma. Because of the lesion’s small size, needle biopsy was deemed difficult, and an excisional biopsy was performed. Macroscopically, the tumor was a grayish-white lobulated mass measuring 20 mm × 14 mm × 8 mm ( ). Histologically, it was surrounded by a thin fibrous capsule ( ) and showed gland-like structures formed by the epithelial cells ( ), along with nests and solid proliferation of myoepithelial cells ( ). The myoepithelial components were associated with hyalinized stroma ( ), myxoid matrix ( ), cartilaginous matrix ( ), squamous metaplasia ( ), and adipocytic metaplasia ( ). Immunohistochemical analysis revealed that the epithelial component was positive for EMA ( ), S-100 was positive throughout, including in the cartilaginous areas ( ), and the SMA was weakly positive in the myogenic component ( ). The Ki-67 (MIB-1) labeling index was approximately 3% ( ). Based on these findings, the lesion was diagnosed as a mixed tumor with adipocyte metaplasia. At the 4-year postoperative follow-up, no evidence of disease recurrence was observed. ## Discussion
Myoepitheliomas, myoepithelial carcinomas, and mixed tumors are classified into a single group of myoepithelial tumors that share similar morphological, immunophenotypic, and genetic features ( ). Myoepitheliomas and mixed tumors in soft-tissue are rare. One of the most comprehensive reports to date was published by Kilpatrick et al. in 1997, who described 19 cases and detailed their clinicopathological characteristics ( ). Osseous and cartilaginous differentiation in myoepitheliomas and mixed tumors is uncommon, while squamous and adipocytic metaplasias are exceedingly rare ( ). In Hornick’s series of 101 myoepithelial tumors, only two cases exhibited squamous metaplasia, and one case showed adipocytic metaplasia ( ). To our knowledge, only one case of myoepithelioma with adipocytic metaplasia has been reported ( ), and there are no prior reports of adipocytic metaplasia in mixed tumors have been identified. Clinically, myoepithelial tumors are typically painless, slow-growing, and tend to arise in the extremities or their proximal regions, with a slightly higher frequency in the subcutaneous tissues than in the deep soft tissues ( ). Mixed tumors of the hand are extremely rare; in the literature reviewed, only three cases were identified: two in the palm and one in the subungual region ( - ). Myoepithelial tumors generally show a bimodal age distribution, with incidence peaks in the 30s and 50s, and disease durations ranging from 2 to 20 years (mean: 4 years) ( , ). In reported cases of mixed tumors of the hand, the average age was 66 years (range, 55–79 years), and the average disease duration was 6.6 years (range, 3–10 years) ( - ). Our case, involving a 65-year-old patient with a 15-year history of the disease, is consistent with these previous reports. Although a long disease duration and slow growth are suggestive of a benign tumor, it is important to note that some sarcomas may also follow an indolent clinical course ( ), warranting caution during the diagnostic process.
Regarding the imaging findings, a key challenge in diagnosing the present case was the presence of adipocytic components on the MRI. In addition to fat, MRI revealed myxoid and fibrous components, suggesting a SPL or an ASPLT rather than a conventional lipoma. Previous reports have shown that both SPL and ASPLT exhibit variable amounts of fat along with myxoid and fibrous elements, which closely resemble the imaging features observed in this case, making differentiation difficult ( , ). Imaging was available for two of the three reported cases of mixed tumors arising in the hand. In both cases, no fat components were observed on T1-weighted images. One case (subungual) showed a homogeneous high signal intensity on T2-weighted imaging ( ), whereas the other (palmar) showed a slightly heterogeneous high signal intensity ( ), indicating variability in signal characteristics. Notably, the subungual case exhibited imaging features suggestive of a glomus tumor ( ). Contrast-enhanced imaging was performed, and contrast enhancement was observed. To date, no large-scale studies have analyzed the imaging characteristics of myoepithelial tumors in soft tissues, including mixed tumors, underscoring the need for further investigations using larger case series. Additionally, based on the MRI findings in the present case, cutaneous tumors should be considered in the differential diagnosis of this condition. In the present case, although no significant skin findings were observed, SCC was included in the differential diagnosis because of its frequency. Given the diverse histological features of myoepithelial tumors, such as fat appearing as high signal intensity on T1-weighted imaging, myxoid stroma appearing as high signal intensity on T2-weighted imaging with enhancement, and hyalinized areas appearing as high signal intensity on T2-weighted imaging without enhancement, as observed in our case, it is challenging to make a definitive diagnosis based solely on imaging. Therefore, histopathological evaluation is essential in such cases.
The histopathological features of myoepithelial tumors are highly diverse. Tumor cells, namely core myoepithelial-like cells, exhibit a wide range of morphological phenotypes, including epithelioid, clear cell, plasmacytoid, and spindle cell types, with epithelioid and spindle cell morphologies being the most commonly observed morphologies. The tumor stroma is also variable, ranging from myxoid to hyalinized or collagenous in appearance ( , ). Osseous and cartilaginous differentiation occurs in approximately 10–15% of cases, whereas adipocytic differentiation and squamous metaplasia, as observed in our case, are considered even rarer ( , ). Distinguishing between benign and malignant lesions is a particularly important diagnostic challenge. Although myoepitheliomas exhibit moderate nuclear atypia, they are characterized by marked nuclear atypia, high mitotic activity, and necrosis ( , ). Mixed malignant tumors arising from the fingers have been reported, underscoring the need for a careful diagnostic evaluation ( ). Immunohistochemically, myoepithelial tumors typically express epithelial markers, such as EMA, and myoepithelial markers, including S-100, calponin, and SMA, with reported positivity rates of approximately 70%, 85%, 90%, and 60%, respectively ( , ). In recent years, molecular cytogenetic analyses have revealed rearrangements involving EWSR1, FUS, and PLAG1, which, although not performed in our case, may aid in diagnosis ( ). The pathological differential diagnoses of myoepithelial tumors are broad and include extraskeletal myxoid chondrosarcoma, synovial sarcoma, ossifying fibromyxoid tumors, and epithelioid schwannoma ( ). In contrast, SPL and ASPLT, which were considered in the differential diagnosis of the present case, do not exhibit the myoepithelial component that typically predominates in myoepithelioma, nor the epithelial marker EMA or myoepithelial markers such as SMA ( , ). Furthermore, key pathological features distinguishing SPL from ASPLT include CD34 positivity, RB1 deletion, and the presence of atypical spindle cells ( , ).
Myoepithelial tumors, including benign and malignant tumors, have a reported recurrence rate of approximately 18%. Even among benign tumors arising in the hand, one case (6.7%) has been reported to recur; however, no instances of metastasis have been documented ( , ). In contrast, malignant myoepithelial tumors exhibit significantly higher recurrence and metastasis rates, with reported recurrence rates of 37% and metastasis rates of 40–50% ( ). Surgical excision remains the primary treatment; however, the clinical and pathological factors influencing recurrence and metastasis have not yet been clearly identified ( ). The relationship between adipocytic metaplasia and recurrence is also unclear; further case accumulation and analysis are required to clarify it.

## Conclusions
We report a rare case of a mixed tumor with adipocytic metaplasia that was difficult to diagnose using imaging alone. The wide spectrum of histological features of myoepithelial tumors, including mixed tumors, makes differentiation based solely on imaging findings challenging. An accurate diagnosis requires a comprehensive assessment that integrates clinical presentation, imaging characteristics, and histopathological evaluation.
