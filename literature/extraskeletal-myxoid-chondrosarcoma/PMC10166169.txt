Department of Internal Medicine, Division of Medical Oncology, The Ohio State University Comprehensive Cancer Center, Division of Medical Oncology, Columbus, OH, USA Over 100 distinct STS histological and molecular subtypes exist, each with a unique clinical, therapeutic, and prognostic feature. The most common STS subtypes in adults are liposarcoma (LPS), leiomyosarcoma (LMS), undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma (UPS), and gastrointestinal stromal tumors (GIST). Most patients present with a painless, gradually enlarging mass, usually without constitutional symptoms such as unintentional weight loss or fever. Forty-six percent of the sarcomas occur in the thigh, buttock, and groin regions, but sarcomas can develop at any anatomical site. Histologic grade and degree of cellular differentiation determine biologic aggressiveness and potential to metastasize. Most metastases disseminate via the hematogenous route, predominantly to the lungs, and are thus difficult to cure. The goals of primary therapy for localized STS are to prevent local recurrence or metastatic disease development and maximize functional status. , Patients who develop metastatic disease have limited therapeutic options and a median overall survival (OS) of about 18 months. , Systemic chemotherapy with anthracyclines, ifosfamide, gemcitabine, taxanes, trabectedin, eribulin, pazopanib, or a combination of these is the standard of care. However, the response rate is low, and toxicities often limit the treatment dosing and length of therapy. , GIST are typically treated with KIT or PDGFR inhibitors, but also demonstrate resistance patterns. Research to develop new therapies for sarcoma treatment is critical, especially techniques that could enhance our understanding of how the immune system could modulate sarcomas. This review will discuss the underlying biology of the STS tumor immune microenvironment, immunomodulatory strategies that augment pre-existing immune responses, and novel approaches to develop ­sarcoma-specific antigen-based therapies.

## Tumor Microenvironment

## Tumor-Infiltrating Lymphocytes
Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) are immune cells that have migrated and invaded tumor tissue. TILs are made up of CD3+ proteins: they can exert specific cytotoxic antitumor activity (CD8+), and promote (CD4+/Th1) or limit (CD4+/Th2, CD4+/FOXP3+Treg) antitumor response. They have been identified in a diverse number of solid tumors and, when infiltrating the tumor, correlated to an improved response to cancer treatments. , Compared to immunoreactive carcinomas, STS have lower numbers of TILs per gram of tissue and lower CD4/CD8 ratios of TIL infiltration. , In GIST and Ewing sarcoma, the infiltration of sarcoma tissue with CD3+ and CD8+ lymphocytes correlates with improved progression-free survival (PFS). , However, in a series of 249 STS, mainly LPS and UPS, the prognostic significance of lymphocyte infiltration for improved disease-free survival correlates with the presence of CD20+ B cells. 
In addition, high levels of CD3+ and CD4+ regulatory T cells were associated with poorer survival. The immune-based classification of cellular components in the sarcoma microenvironment showed that B cells are the most substantial for prognosis compared to T cells and follicular dendritic cells. Patients treated with pembrolizumab had a higher response rate and improved survival if the B-cell population was prominent. Hence, subtyping STS for potential immunotherapy response could impact treatment decision-making in future trials.
The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) utilized an RNASeq-based immune infiltration score to determine TIL gene signatures across 100 000 unique human cancer genomes. Studies evaluating TCGA have focused on 6 sarcoma subtypes: 80 LMS, 50 dedifferentiated liposarcomas (DDLPS), 44 UPS, 17 myxofibrosarcomas (MFS), 10 synovial sarcomas (SS), and 5 malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumors (MPNST). CD8+ immune cell scores correlated with improved survival in gynecological LMS, and dendritic cell scores were associated with better survival in UPS and MFS. These observations further support that the tumor microenvironment and the presence of TILs in the tumor microenvironment influence STS prognosis.
Therapeutic approaches using TILs in patients with advanced, unresectable STS have been tested previously. Adoptive transfer of TIL involves the isolation of TIL from resected surgical specimens. TILs are extracted from the TME, isolated, cultured, and induced to rapidly divide ex vivo to numbers greater than 1010 through cytokine stimulation. Once expanded to sufficient numbers, the patient is treated with a lymphodepleting regimen—usually a combination of cyclophosphamide and fludarabine—providing a receptive terrain for the adoptively transferred TIL. After host conditioning, TILs are administered back to the patient along with IL-2.
TILs have several advantages over other forms of immunotherapy. Because there can be multiple TIL clones, they can recognize a broader array of tumor antigens. Since TILs are expanded ex vivo, they can be activated without the suppressive tumor microenvironment of regulatory T cells, myeloid-derived suppressor cells, and inhibitory macrophages. Finally, the patient can be conditioned before administering the TILs to provide an ideal environment for continued expansion and engraftment. However, the lack of persistence of transferred autologous mature lymphocytes in humans has been a major limitation of this therapy.
The safety and efficacy of TILs for the treatment of advanced cancers were initially reported by the Surgery Branch of the National Cancer Institute and have since been validated by multiple centers. This approach has been licensed and is currently accruing patients with advanced melanoma and non-small cell lung cancer for multiple phase III trials. For STS, the densely fibrotic stroma makes this approach more difficult. Recently, novel approaches to TIL isolation and culture (for example, tumor fragments, fresh tumor digest, or the expansion of a primary TIL culture) may overcome previous challenges. A phase I trial investigating the safety of lymphodepletion plus the adoptive cell therapy of TIL with high-dose IL-2 is currently enrolling patients with advanced STS. Another current trial is the phase II clinical trial evaluating LN-145 (autologous TILs) in relapsed/refractory ovarian cancer, triple-negative breast cancer, anaplastic thyroid cancer, osteosarcoma, and other bone and soft tissue sarcomas (NCT03449108). There is also a phase II trial evaluating MDA-TILs in patients with ovarian cancer, colorectal cancer, or pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma. The ovarian cancer cohort allows for ovarian carcinosarcomas, which are considered STS (NCT03610490).

## Checkpoint Inhibitor Therapy in Soft Tissue Sarcomas

## PD-1/PD-L1 Axis
One of the most studied immunotherapeutic targets is the programmed cell death 1 (PD-1) pathway. PD-1 is expressed on the surface of various immune cells, including natural killer cells, dendritic cells, activated T cells, B cells, and monocytes. Some tumor cells express programmed cell death ligand 1 (PD-L1) on their cell surface, which binds to PD-1 to inhibit the activity of the T cells and evade immune recognition. Programmed cell death ligand 2 (PD-L2) can also be expressed and is blocked by anti-PD-1 antibodies. In a retrospective cohort of 82 STS patients with 5 different subtypes, PD-L1 was expressed in 43% of cases, with the highest expression seen in epithelioid sarcomas (7/7 cases, 100%). In another large STS cohort, PD-L1 expression on tumor and immune cells was observed in 23% of cases (47/203 STS specimens). These observations corroborate the therapeutic potential of targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 axis for STS. The SARC028 multicenter phase II trial (NCT02301039) evaluated the anti-PD-1 monoclonal antibody pembrolizumab in patients with advanced soft-tissue and bone sarcomas. Of 40 patients, 7 (18%) had an objective response (1 complete response and 6 partial responses) using the RECIST criteria as the primary endpoint, with the best results observed for UPS and DDLPS. While this STS cohort did not meet the primary endpoint, the 12-week 55% progression-free survival rate meets the expected criteria for therapeutic activity. In the follow-up expansion study of SARC028, the UPS cohort met its primary endpoint with a 23% response rate. A retrospective examination of the tolerability and efficacy of various immunotherapies in 40 patients with unresectable or metastatic advanced STS showed that combinations of checkpoint inhibitors induced the most robust clinical responses. The most significant responses were in patients with alveolar soft part sarcomas (ASPS) who underwent anti-PD-L1-based therapy, with 2 of 4 patients achieving a partial response and the remaining 2 having stable disease. The phase II clinical trial (NCT02636725) testing pembrolizumab in combination with the VEGF receptor tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI), axitinib, showed activity in 30 patients with advanced sarcomas, especially in those with progressive ASPS who had not received prior TKI. Of 11 patients with ASPS, 6 attained a partial response and 2, stable disease, with a 3-month progression-free survival of 72.7%. In addition, single-agent atezolizumab was shown to have an objective response rate of 42% in 8 of 19 patients with advanced ASPS who were naive to PD-1/PD-L1 treatment. Another therapy that has shown some benefit in case series is the TKI, anlotinib, in combination with PD-1 inhibitors in ASPS and epithelioid sarcoma. Two patients with ASPS achieved stable disease for 12 months and complete response (CR) in 3 months that maintained for 19 months; the patient with epithelioid sarcoma achieved a CR in 1 month that maintained for 23 months. Most recently, durvalumab/tremelimumab combination therapy displayed activity in advanced or metastatic sarcoma, including STS and osteosarcoma and chordoma, with 49% reaching a 12-week PFS (median follow-up of 37.2 months). Of significance, the ASPS subtype had the highest response, with a PFS of 80% at 12 weeks. In those patients that responded, there was an increase in PD-L1 expression after treatment along with an increase in CD3+, CD8+, and PD-1+ T-cells. 
Histology may indicate response to PD-1/PD-L1 blockade, as patients with advanced uterine leiomyosarcoma do not respond to nivolumab, with a median PFS of only 1.8 months. It is hypothesized that the tumor microenvironment contributes a significant role in this response. Despite pembrolizumab’s efficacy in trials, a phase II trial demonstrated minimal efficacy of pembrolizumab with metronomic cyclophosphamide in patients with advanced STS: LMS, UPS, and GIST. There was an increased presence of M2 macrophages that expressed the inhibitory enzyme indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase 1 (IDO1), which can play a role in resistance to immunotherapy. , 

## CTLA-4 Inhibitors
Cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 (CTLA-4) is another immune checkpoint that downregulates immune responses by binding 2 distinct ligands, CD80 and CD85. As a single agent, CTLA-4 inhibitors have shown prolonged survival in melanoma patients. Ipilimumab, the only FDA-approved monoclonal antibody targeting CTLA-4, was tested in combination with the PD-1 inhibitor nivolumab in patients with unresectable or metastatic STS in 2 randomized phase II trials (Alliance A091401—NCT02500797 and NCT01643278 ). The Alliance A091401 trial evaluated the role of nivolumab and ipilimumab combined and single-agent nivolumab in a non-comparative fashion. In the single-agent nivolumab group, the confirmed responses were 2 of 38 patients (5%), with a median progression-free survival (mPFS) of 1.7 months. This group did not achieve the primary endpoint of at least 13% responses, and it was considered inferior to currently available chemotherapy-based options. The ipilimumab plus nivolumab combination therapy group demonstrated efficacy in multiple sarcoma subtypes, including UPS, LMS, angiosarcoma, and MFS, and met its primary study endpoint of confirmed objective response rate. In 38 patients, refractory to chemotherapy that received this combination immunotherapy, the response rate was 16%, and the mPFS was 4.1 months. Safety and tolerability of the combination group (3-4 adverse events, 14%) were consistent with the expectations for this treatment class based on prior studies. More recently, the results of the A091401 expansion cohorts for LPS, UPS, and GIST were presented as a 2020 ASCO abstract. The primary endpoint was a 6-month confirmed response rate. The ipilimumab plus nivolumab combination led to responses in the LPS and UPS groups (6-month response rate of 14% for both groups). No confirmed responses in GIST were seen with the monotherapy or the combination. Reported adverse events (AEs) were higher in the GIST combination group, demonstrating the need to determine genomic and clinical biomarkers of response, resistance, and toxicity. 
Another study investigated ipilimumab in combination with the TKI dasatinib in patients with refractory and/or unresectable GIST (20 patients) or other STS (8 patients), including LMS and epithelioid sarcoma. The results showed no complete or partial responses per RECIST criteria, but partial responses were seen in 7 of 13 patients with GIST by Choi criteria. The study concluded that dasatinib did not favorably alter the microenvironment to enhance the immune response to ipilimumab based on correlative immunologic blood analysis. 
Given variable results with immunotherapy, the degree of TIL infiltration needs further study to determine if it can help predict response to immunotherapy in sarcoma. The phase II trial of tremelimumab (CTLA-4) and durvalumab (PD-L1) , enrolled 57 patients with advanced sarcoma (liposarcoma, leiomyosarcoma, angiosarcoma, undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma, synovial sarcoma, osteosarcoma, ASPS, and chordoma). The median OS for all 57 patients was 20.8 months with a median PFS of 4.5 months. About 25% of patients experienced grade ≥3 related adverse events. Thirty-six tumors had an inflamed phenotype, defined by an increased median density of 3 major TILs (CD3+, CD3+CD8+, CD3+CD8+GZB+) and had an improved clinical benefit compared to those that were not considered inflamed. 

## Combination of Immunotherapy with Other Classes of Agents
Several clinical trials have recently assessed the safety and efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors in combination with non-immune systemic therapies for STS treatment. A randomized phase I/II trial (NCT02888665) testing the combination of doxorubicin and pembrolizumab in advanced anthracycline-naive sarcoma in 37 patients yielded a 78% complete response rate with a median OS that has not been reached. In combination with nivolumab, sunitinib, a multi-targeted TKI, was found to improve PFS in patients with advanced STS (UPS, synovial sarcoma, clear cell sarcoma, angiosarcoma, epithelioid hemangioendothelioma, solitary fibrous tumor, epithelioid sarcoma, extraskeletal myxoid chondrosarcoma, or ASPS). Median OSs were not reached (3 and 6 months were 86% and 77%, respectively), and PFS at 3 and 6 months was 69% and 50%, respectively. The SAINT phase I/II trial of safety/efficacy with nivolumab, ipilimumab, and trabectedin (an alkylating agent) as first-line treatment in patients with advanced STS (synovial sarcoma, liposarcoma, or leiomyosarcoma) reported interim results, with the phase II trial still ongoing. Median OS and PFS rates have not been reached after 12 months. In grades 3 and 4 side effects were minimal. The DART trial (SWOG S1609, cohort 51) is evaluating the combination of nivolumab and ipilumumab in metastatic or unresectable angiosarcoma. The overall response rate was 25% in 16 patients with a 6-month PFS of 38%. Another ongoing trial is the phase II study of the oncolytic herpes virus, talimogene laherparepvec (T-vec), in combination with pembrolizumab for patients with metastatic and/or locally advanced sarcoma. The overall response rate is 68% with a majority of enrolled patients achieving stable disease (47%). Those who achieved a partial response have continued responses. and present a list of ongoing trials further assessing the effect of immunotherapies in combination with other treatments such as radiotherapy, TKIs, and conventional chemotherapy. presents an overview of the ORR and mPFS with the different immunotherapies.
 

## Sarcoma Antigen-Based and Cell Therapy
Potential targets for immunotherapy have been categorized as mutated, shared tumor-specific, differentiation antigens, overexpressed antigens, or viral antigens. , Mutated antigens are generated by somatic mutations in the coding DNA of neoplastic cells, not present in normal tissues, thus making the target inherently cancer-specific. Fusion genes, such as the SYT-SSX fusion protein in synovial sarcoma and constitute potential targets. , Shared tumor-specific antigens are frequently expressed by several malignancies but rarely by normal tissue. This category includes the cancer-­testis antigens (described below). They are frequently seen in the developing embryo but not in significant quantities in adults except in the testis and occasionally the placenta. Differentiation antigens are involved in the normal differentiation of a specific tissue type (for example, MART-1 has been targeted in melanoma ). Overexpressed targets are expressed in normal tissue but greatly overexpressed in tumors (for example, HER2). Viral antigens from viruses such as EBV have presented immunogenic epitopes in EBV-related cancers. , 

## Dendritic Cell-Based Immunotherapy
Dendritic cells (DCs), the pivotal antigen-presenting cells of the human immune system, have long been studied as potential cancer immunotherapies due to their ability to induce antitumor T cells. , DC-based immunotherapies have experienced some success in treating prostate, renal, and melanoma skin cancers, but few clinical studies have been done for sarcomas. In a pediatric study, patients with newly diagnosed metastatic rhabdomyosarcoma were treated with monocyte-derived tumor lysate (TL)-pulsed DCs after surgery. The 5-year overall survival was 76.9%, significantly higher than any previously reported treatment in the same patient population. Another ongoing phase I/II clinical trial with a monocyte-derived dendritic cell-based vaccine is being studied in 9 patients with advanced/metastatic high-risk sarcoma. Preliminary data reveals a complete response, partial response, or stable disease in 5 of the 9 patients. In another phase I/II clinical trial using autologous TL-pulsed DCs to treat 35 adult patients with refractory STS, 6 patients had stable disease, and 1 had a partial response. However, most (28/35, 80%) had progression. No treatment-related adverse events were observed in the study patients. Therefore, despite the good safety profile of DC-based regimens, further investigation is necessary to better understand whether this therapy could yield improved activity. Active clinical trials assessing DC vaccines in patients with STS include NCT01803152 and NCT01883518.

## Personalized Peptide Vaccines
Personalized peptide vaccines (PPVs) utilize multiple individualized peptides chosen from a list of appropriate candidates (that is, DNA expression libraries) to induce more rapid and more substantial antitumor immunity. Therapeutic trials involving PPVs in the past several years have displayed interesting results in various types of malignancies, including prostate, pancreatic, and colorectal cancers. A phase II study examining the feasibility of PPVs in treating STS was conducted in 20 patients with refractory bone and soft tissue sarcomas that included 9 different subtypes. The median OS in patients receiving PPV therapy was 9.6 months, which is slightly higher than the OS for second-line palliative therapy (8 months) and for supportive care (5.3 months). The authors acknowledge the limitation of the small sample size. Therefore, PPVs need further testing in refractory patients with sarcoma and could be applicable to many different STS subtypes.

## T-Cell Targeting Vaccine
The New York esophageal squamous cell carcinoma 1 (NY-ESO-1) protein is highly expressed by specific sarcoma subtypes, namely synovial sarcoma (about 80%) , and myxoid/round-cell liposarcoma (about 95%). , Immunohistochemical antigen expression of NY-ESO-1 is observed in 58.8% of synovial sarcomas and 55.6% of myxoid liposarcomas. In a recent phase I/II clinical trial, 12 adult patients with recurrent and/or metastatic synovial sarcoma received transduced T cells engineered to recognize and target NY-ESO-1. The results suggested that these adoptively transferred T cells could self-regenerate and produce a continuous supply of effector cells to successfully mediate tumor regression, with an overall response rate of 50% (1 complete response, 5 partial responses). In another first-in-human phase I study, a lentiviral vector that induces T-cells to recognize NY-ESO-1 via dendritic cell presentation was utilized as a cancer vaccine in 30 patients with sarcoma ; 62.5% of all sarcoma patients maintained or achieved stable disease. One patient with synovial sarcoma was able to achieve a near-complete response. The above results indicate that NY-ESO-1 may be a potential receptor of interest for targeted immune therapies, such as TCR cell and bispecific antibody therapies. Of note, ipilimumab was also studied in patients with advanced synovial sarcoma to determine if it can augment the immune system’s response against NY-ESO-1, as ipilimumab was shown to increase T-cell response to cancer-testis antigens in melanoma studies. Unfortunately, all patients had disease progression after 3 cycles, and there was no increased serologic expression of NY-ESO-1 after treatment with ipilimumab. The study was halted due to lack of efficacy. 
A clinical trial was performed in patients with metastatic melanoma or metastatic synovial cell sarcoma refractory to all standard treatments. Patients with NY-ESO-1-positive tumors were treated with autologous TCR-transduced T cells and IL-2 stimulation. Objective clinical responses were observed in 4 of 6 patients with synovial cell sarcoma and 5 of 11 patients with melanoma-bearing tumors expressing NY-ESO-1. Two of 11 patients with melanoma demonstrated complete regressions that persisted after 1 year. A partial response lasting 18 months was observed in 1 patient with synovial cell sarcoma. 
An ongoing phase 1 trial is evaluating MAGE-A4 in patients with multiple tumor types, including synovial sarcoma and myxoid round cell liposarcoma (NCT03132922). Another potential target is the epitope mutation, NY-SAR-4/FH, associated with the fumarate hydratase or FH gene. Data from complementary DNA (cDNA) expression libraries of human tumor samples (SEREX) identified NY-SAR-4/FH as a potential neoantigen as it was only expressed in patients with cancer; 5 of 39 (13%) patients with sarcoma had serum antibodies to NY-SAR-4/FH (osteosarcoma, malignant fibrous histiocytoma (MFH), fibrosarcoma (FS), and Ewing sarcoma).

## CAR-T
CAR-T therapy has been tested in solid tumors after successfully treating hematologic malignancies. So far, little benefit was seen in solid tumors and STS with limited tumor-specific surface antigen targets and complex tumor microenvironments that offer a barrier to intratumoral treatment penetration. There is emerging data on CAR-T and sarcoma and potential antigen targets (HER2, IFGR1, CD166, EphA2, and Gd2+) , across different histologies (eg, osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma, synovial sarcoma, and chondrosarcoma). These targets are not specific to any particular sarcoma or tumor cells in general, such as CD166, which are also seen in fibroblasts and neurons. This is problematic as demonstrated when EphA2 was targeted in osteosarcoma cell lines; there was downregulation of EphA2 and an increase in EphA2-negative tumor cells, suspected through cancer immunoediting. Although Ewing cell lines demonstrated improved survival with CAR-T targeted toward antigens like IGF1R and ROR1, there were still concerns regarding low expression and off-target toxicity. Further research is required to optimize the clinical applicability of CAR-T in sarcoma. There is a current phase 1 trial studying HER2 CAR-T therapy in combination with PD-1 inhibitor in patients with advanced sarcoma that have HER-2 overexpression (NCT04995003).

## Future Management/Directions

## Adding Immunogenicity Profile to the Molecular/Biological Profile in Sarcomas
Advanced immunohistochemistry and molecular diagnostics have revolutionized our ability to classify STS in order to make more rational therapeutic decisions. Along with new molecular and biological information on sarcoma subtypes, the immunogenicity of sarcoma subtypes is of particular interest. The cancer immunome, an “extensive repertoire of tumor antigens recognized by the immune system,” is being categorized via multiple methods: T-cell epitope cloning, major histocompatibility complex (MHC) peptide elution, serological expression cloning (SEREX), and other techniques. The goal is to identify novel specific tumor antigens targeted particularly to each sarcoma, such as NY-ESO-1 for synovial sarcomas, MAGE-A3 for UPS and MFS, MAGE-A4 for synovial sarcoma, and myxoid round cell liposarcoma (NCT03132922), and potentially for fusion proteins specific to some sarcomas. The determination of immunogenic cancer antigens by serological analysis of recombinant tumor cDNA expression libraries, SEREX, is a technique to identify tumor antigens eliciting high IgG responses in humans. SEREX showed fewer overlapping immune responses among sarcoma patients than other solid tumors. This suggested that sarcoma histological subtypes have distinct immunomes and that immune response could be highly variable or act via other biological pathways. High PD-1 and PD-L1 expression in sarcoma does not predict benefits from checkpoint inhibition. Thus, further research is necessary to better understand the immunologic profiles of sarcomas with the hope of tailoring immunotherapy for better therapeutic outcomes.

## Biomarkers for Immune Treatment Selection
Oncogenic driver mutations can be a biomarker of immunotherapy response. In 75 GIST specimens that carried mutational drivers, PDGFRA was associated with more immune infiltrating cells than the KIT mutation. Higher levels of expression of CD96, a marker for NK cells; CD4; CCR4; CCR5 (indicative of regulatory T cells); and immunomodulators BTLA, CD48, TNFRSF9, and TIGIT were observed in these PDGFRA-mutated specimens. These findings suggest that PDGFRA-mutant GIST could be more sensitive to some forms of immunotherapy.

## Limitations
One of the main limitations of studying immunotherapies for STS treatment is the small patient sample size and low enrollment in clinical trials. Most STS clinical trials have fewer than 100 patients enrolled. As a result, inconsistent results among different studies and clinically significant correlations will be more challenging to identify. The paucity of phase III studies in STS clinical trials is also limiting. Many treatments, including immunotherapy trials, as described demonstrate hopeful results in phase II trials, and need to be validated in subsequent phase III studies. In addition, the remarkable heterogeneity of STS subtypes makes it challenging to implement a one-size-fits-all approach to treatment. The data in the literature lacks histology-specific biomarkers to predict clinical response to immunotherapy in STS.

## Conclusion
Sarcomas represent a broad range of histological and molecular subtypes that comprise various entities with distinct immune sensitivities. The immunogenic status of STS and how it affects the response rate to immunotherapy is not well understood. Whereas there is data demonstrating a correlation between PD-L1 expression and response in specific subtypes, like ASPS, this is not consistent in others like LMS. A breakthrough in the field is the discovery of specific cancer testis antigens that has led to the development of TCRs that have demonstrated efficacy in initial trials, specifically in synovial sarcoma and myxoid round cell liposarcoma. However much more needs to be discovered to better personalize therapies. By continuing to search for antigenicity and evaluate antitumor immunity in different sarcoma subtypes, the sarcoma treatment landscape will broaden with ­histology-specific immune profiles, and novel insights will clarify the role of immunotherapy and immunomodulatory therapies.
