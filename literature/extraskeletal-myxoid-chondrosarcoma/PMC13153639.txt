## Abstract

## Purpose
Patients with soft tissue sarcomas (STS) of the extremity require ongoing follow-up to detect treatment failures. There is a significant variation in follow-up schemes among sarcoma specialists due to a lack of evidence.

## Methods
A retrospective single-center review was performed on all patients with a minimum 1-year follow-up after resection of a localized STS, utilizing a prospectively collected database. Kaplan-Meier curves were used to calculate the incidence of local recurrence and metastases on an annual basis over 10 years.

## Results
There were 407 patients who met the inclusion criteria. Considering the annual incidence of local recurrence and distant metastasis per STS size and grade, patients with small, low-grade STS should be followed yearly for 10 years. Patients with small, intermediate/high-grade STS and those with large, low-grade STS should be followed every 6 months for the first 2 years and then yearly until 10 years postoperatively. Patients with large, intermediate/high-grade STS should be followed every 3 months for the first 2 years and then every 6 months thereafter until 10 years postoperatively.

## Conclusion
Compared to National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) and European Society for Medical Oncology (ESMO) guidelines, our proposed scheme has the potential to reduce hospital visits, mainly for patients with small intermediate and high-grade tumors. This protocol validates the previously published scheme, despite being relatively more conservative.

## INTRODUCTION
Soft tissue sarcomas (STS) are a diverse group of more than 70 histological entities of malignant tumors that share common characteristics of biological behavior [ , ]. They are grouped because of their common mesenchymal origin and represent together less than 1% of all human malignancies [ , ]. Despite optimal treatment, patients with STS are at a significant risk for local recurrence and distant metastasis [ , ]. It was shown that nearly every third patient with primarily local STS will develop distant metastasis during follow-up [ , ]. Local recurrence was less frequently observed, occurring in 11 to 25% of cases according to series [ – ]. Rates of disease progression vary depending on the tumor’s size, depth, histological type, grade, margin status and patient’s age [ , – ].
High rates of disease progression make appropriate surveillance critical [ ]. Even though several schemes have been proposed, there is no clear consensus as to when and how often follow-up investigations should be performed for STS patients. Scientific societies such as National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) and European Society for Medical Oncology (ESMO) acknowledge the lack of high-level evidence to suggest follow-up for patients, and stratify recommended protocols based on tumor’s grade, whereas a heuristic approach is pursued in many centers [ , ]. This has led to significant discrepancies in the practice of sarcoma specialists around the world [ , ].
Strict surveillance to detect STS treatment failures improves overall survival [ , , – ]. However, a “one-follow-up strategy fits all” approach will lead to excessive surveillance in some cases, increasing mental stress for the patients and their families, radiation exposure, the health care related costs and the workload in the clinics and radiology departments [ , – ].
A rational follow-up schedule, based on annual rates of metastasis in the 10 years following treatment for primary STS was proposed in 2020 [ ]. Different follow-up schedules were proposed for patients with small grade I tumors; those with small grade II/III and large grade I STS and those with large grade II/III STS ( ). Follow-ups were closer and longer as the grade and the size of the STS increased ( ). This scheme came from the experience of one large referral center for STS care, however, it was not validated through data coming from other centers with different practices.
 This study analyses a large prospectively followed cohort of patients with STS and assesses annual rates of local recurrence and distant metastasis in these patients to propose a follow-up schedule for patients with STS and compare it to the already published scheme for sake of validation.

## METHODS
This is a retrospective review on a prospective registry in a tertiary multidisciplinary referral sarcoma center, performed after Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval. Patients who underwent resection of localized primary extremity STS between 2004 and 2020 with a minimum 1 year of follow-up were included. Patients with well differentiated liposarcomas (atypical lipomatous tumor), those who presented with a metastatic disease or after a local recurrence and those with incomplete charts were excluded from this study. Patients who had concomitant other primary neoplasms and those who were younger than 15 years at diagnosis were not included either. Patients who were referred after an unplanned resection performed elsewhere were, however, included.
Treatment plans for every patient were made after a multidisciplinary cancer specialists’ discussion. In our center, when radiation is felt necessary, the neoadjuvant radiation regimen is generally preferred. In cases with significant sarcopenia, with severe symptoms, such as pain or neurological deficit, when tumor is fungating, or when patients are judged at high risk of complications following neoadjuvant radiotherapy, surgery is proposed first. Chemotherapy is not given for most primary localized STS except for some entities, such as rhabdomyosarcoma or extra skeletal Ewing sarcoma, where its benefits are demonstrated.
Our pre-operative staging includes magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the primary tumor site, a low-dose chest computed tomography (CT)-scan and a positron emission tomography (PET)-scan for grade 2–3 histology. Patients with myxoid\round cell liposarcoma undergo a whole-body MRI (WBMRI) pre-operatively due to the unusual pattern of metastasis associated with this entity [ , ].
The standard follow-up protocol in our center includes a physical examination and a low-dose contrast-free chest CT scan every 3 months for the first 2 years postoperatively, followed by a physical examination and a chest X-ray every 6 months for the next 3 years, then annually until 10 years postoperatively. Any suspicious new finding on the chest X-ray warrants a subsequent low-dose chest CT scan to rule out a metastasis. WBMRI is performed every 6 months for the first 2 years postoperatively in patients with myxoid\round cell liposarcomas and then yearly up to year 5. No standard imaging is regularly performed for the surgical bed, but MRI is sometimes performed when suspecting local recurrence or in cases where post-radiation therapy changes hinder accurate physical examination. PET scans are not routinely used for follow-up except for diseases known at risk of multicentricity or lymph nodes involvement such as epithelioid sarcoma, clear cell sarcoma, angiosarcoma and hemangioendothelioma.
Based on a thorough review of literature, we identified variables that would influence the risk of local recurrence including tumor size, depth, grade, histological type, and margins status [ , , ]. Association between these variables and the risk of local recurrence was looked for in our series through a univariate analysis. Variables that would influence the risk of distant metastasis were also searched in the available literature and tumor size, grade, depth, histological type, local recurrence were selected for univariate analysis looking for an association between these variables and the risk of distant metastasis in our patients [ , , ].
Cox regression analysis was then performed to look for significant risk factors for local recurrence and distant metastasis. Variables were selected in each Cox regression models based on the results of the univariate analysis and on the literature review.
For local recurrence, three clinical cohorts were identified for the risk of local recurrence centered on histologic grade (low, intermediate, high). Kaplan-Meier curves were generated for each subgroup and the event rates for local recurrence were calculated on an annual basis for 10 years. Patients with positive margins were excluded from this analysis solely because the postoperative follow-up of these patients will be decided by the multidisciplinary team on a case-by-case basis.
For distant metastasis, six clinical cohorts were identified for the risk of distant metastasis based on histologic grade (low, intermediate, high) and tumor size (<5 cm, ≥5 cm). Kaplan-Meier curves were generated for each subgroup and the event rates for metastases were calculated on an annual basis for 10 years. The six clinical cohorts were then merged into three cohorts based on similar behavior.
Annual incidence of distant metastasis and of local recurrences were used to determine the follow-up regimen. The closest follow-up interval suitable for a timely detection of distant metastasis or local recurrence for every clinical cohort was selected. A metastatic or local recurrence incidence rate greater than 10% per year was used to define patient subgroups who would benefit from close follow-up on a 3-monthly basis. An event rate of 2.5% to 10% would suggest a recommended follow-up on a 6-monthly basis, an event rate of 1% to 2.5% would suggest annual follow-up. An event rate of less than 1% per annum would suggest no further follow-up is required. These cutoffs were previously reported and adopted based on expert consensus and this consensus was endorsed by our multidisciplinary team for this work [ , ].
All statistical analyses were performed using R software version 4.0.2 (R Foundation for Statistical Computing).

## RESULTS
From the 568 patients in the database, 407 patients met the inclusion criteria and were followed for 67.7±39.3 months. There were 236 males (58.0%) and 171 females (42.0%) with a mean age of 55.8±17.2 years. Liposarcomas, myxofibrosarcomas and leiomyosarcomas were the most frequent histological types ( ). Fifty-eight percent of patients had large tumors (>5 cm) and 37% had grade 3 tumors ( ). Positive margins were reported in 12% of cases. Patients with positive margins were included in the overall cohort for all the performed analysis (descriptive and analytic) except for the statistical analysis pertaining to the risk factors for local recurrence.
 Half of the patients had neoadjuvant radiation-therapy followed by surgical resection whereas 13% of patients had adjuvant radiation, 14% had a combination of neo-adjuvant and adjuvant radiation and 22% had none.
At the end of follow-up, 325 patients (79.9%) were alive with no evidence of disease, and 23 patients (5.7%) were alive with evidence of disease. Forty-two (10.3%) deaths of the disease were recorded, while 17 patients (4.2%) died of causes unrelated to their STS.
Eighty-two patients (20.0%) in this cohort developed a metastasis during their follow-up. Seventy-five percent of these metastases occurred in the first 3 years postoperatively. Forty-seven patients (11.5%) experienced local recurrence with 85% occurring within the first 36 months.

## Risk factors for distant metastasis
Univariate analysis showed a significant association between occurrence of distant metastasis and tumor’s size, depth, grade and local recurrence ( ). Multivariate analysis showed that tumor’s size and grade were the only factors associated with the development of distant metastasis (P<0.01) ( ). Whereas tumor histological subtype, depth and occurrence of local recurrence were not significantly associated with distant metastasis. Given these results it was decided to use only tumor’s size and grade to develop our follow-up clinical cohorts for distant metastasis. Annual incidence of metastasis was calculated in the six cohorts of patients created according to the STS size and grade ( ). For the sake of simplifying the proposed scheme of follow-up and based on similar annual rates of metastasis, the six clinical cohorts were merged into three cohorts. Small low and intermediate grade tumors behaved similarly up to 10 years of follow-up with consistently low annual incidence of metastasis (<2.5%); thus these two categories were merged together. Also, large intermediate and high-grade tumors behaved similarly with high annual incidence of metastasis up to 10 years of follow-up; therefore, these two categories were merged together. Finally, the remaining small high-grade and large low-grade tumors behaved more aggressively than small low and intermediate grade tumors but less aggressively than large intermediate and high-grade tumors; and therefore, small high-grade and large low-grade tumors were merged into one category. The corresponding annual rates of metastasis of the merged categories were then calculated accordingly ( ).
 

## Risk factors for local recurrence
Univariate analysis showed a significant association between local recurrence and the grade of STS. Multivariate analysis showed that STS grade and resection margins were significantly associated with risk of local recurrence. Authors decided not to assess annual rates of local recurrence depending on margins status because patients with positive margins had a special follow-up schedule based on a case-by-case decision by the multidisciplinary tumor board. Therefore, excluding patients with positive margins, three clinical cohorts according to tumor grade were created and annual rates of local recurrence per cohort were assessed ( ). Risk of local recurrence increased with the tumor grade from 1% in the first year of follow-up in low grade tumors to 4.50% in high-grade tumors. The risk of local recurrence is high in the first 2 years of follow-up (2.17% in low-grade tumor; 3.61% in intermediate grade tumor and 4.50% in high-grade tumor) and decreases with follow-up to 0.60% annually in low-grade tumors and 0.51% annually in high-grade tumors between 5 and 10 years of follow-up.
 

## Proposed follow-up scheme
Considering annual incidence of distant metastasis per STS size and grade categories and annual incidence of local recurrence per STS grade, we decided to choose the closest follow-up interval per event, per risk factor for every clinical cohort. Therefore, patients with small low-grade STS should be followed yearly for 10 years. Patients with small intermediate/high grade STS and those with large low-grade STS should be followed every 6 months for the first 2 years and then yearly thereafter until 10 years postoperatively. Finally, patients with large intermediate/high grade STS should be followed every 3 months for the first 2 years and then every 6 months thereafter till 10 years postoperatively ( ). When annual rate of metastasis and annual rate of local recurrence dictated different follow-up intervals for the same tumor category; the closest follow-up interval was chosen. Therefore, for small grade I tumors, follow-up interval was dictated local recurrence and annual risk of metastasis for the first 5 years; and then solely by annual risk of metastasis between 5 years and 10 years of follow-up. For small intermediate grade tumors, follow-up scheme in the first 2 years was dictated by local recurrence rate and thereafter by local recurrence and annual metastasis rate. For small high-grade tumors follow-up was based on annual local recurrence and distant metastasis rates. The follow-up scheme in large low-grade tumors and large intermediate and high-grade tumors was dictated mainly by the annual rates of distant metastasis that needed closer follow-up intervals.
 

## DISCUSSION
Most postoperative follow-up protocols after STS surgery in referral centers worldwide do not define clear schemes according to risk factors. They keep a wide range of subjectivity for the personal clinical judgment of the treating team based on the interpretation of available clinical data [ , , ]. This work aims to design an easy-to-follow schedule that spaces follow-up intervals personalized to each tumor risks. Tumors with similar prognosis, either high or intermediate or low risk will be offered the same follow-up schedule. Initiatives such as Persarc and Sarculator applications did not yet translate into clear follow-up regimen for groups of patients with similar risks [ , ].
Annual incidence of events was previously used to determine a follow-up scheme in sarcomas. Only one study has used annual incidence of distant metastasis to determine a follow-up plan for STS leading to three follow-up protocols according to STS’s grade and size [ ]. In that study, annual incidence of local recurrence was not taken into consideration because of the low overall expected rate of local recurrences compared to distant metastasis and because STS grade, size and histological profile did not influence the risk of local recurrence in their patients [ ]. Nevertheless, in our series, local recurrence event rate reached more than 50% of distant metastasis event rate and the risk of local recurrence was significantly associated with STS grade and margins status in multivariate analysis, accounting for confounding factors. Therefore, local recurrence was taken into consideration to decide for the follow-up scheme. Annual incidence of local recurrence was calculated based on the STS’s grade for patients with negative margins.
Distant metastasis was found to be significantly associated with tumor’s size and grade, and annual incidence of distant metastasis was calculated according to these two variables. Both annual rates of distant metastasis and local recurrence were taken into consideration when recommending a scheme for follow-up. The same previously used cutoffs for event rates were used to decide for follow-up intervals.
The proposed three risk groups for follow-up schedule appears logical and easy to implement. Most of it is concordant with the one proposed previously, and seemingly validates it. Two differences were noted, however. The first one is a longer follow-up term for patients with small low-grade tumors as >1% annual metastasis rate after 5 years of follow-up was recorded in our series. The second difference is the necessity to continue with a twice-yearly follow-up for patients with grade 2 and 3 large tumors between 5 and 10 years postoperatively since a significant 3.3% annual metastatic incidence was observed in our cohort in this timeframe. Seven patients (10.6%) out of 66 patients with grade 2 and 3 large tumors developed metastasis between 5 and 10 years of follow-up. Only one patient developed metastasis after a disease-free interval of more than 10 years making visit after 10 years of low yield.
This study has several limitations. Our recommendations are applicable to our setting. They represent the experience from our institutional sarcoma team with its own management policies which may slightly differ from another sarcoma center. However, there are no substantial differences in practice that preclude generalization of our findings to other settings. More, despite the prospective data collection our study remains retrospective with risks of selection bias and incomplete data. The most important limitation appears to be the relatively small number of patients accounting for the many tumor characteristics tested. Albeit it did not influence the strength of the multivariate statistics models and did not jeopardize the statistically significant associations it could lead to an under- or an over-estimation of the events annual rates mainly between 5 to 10 years of follow-up and might account for the differences observed with the previously reported schedule in this follow-up time frame. Another limitation is the lack of routine imaging of the surgical bed in to assess for local recurrence in our practice routine. While there is no recommendation to perform such imaging routinely, some sarcoma centers around the world perform these tests; and this might limit the generalizability of the recommendations related to local recurrence incidence rate.
Compared to ESMO and NCCN guidelines, this scheme has the potential to significantly reduce hospital visits, mainly for patients with small intermediate and high-grade tumors. This protocol validates the previously published scheme despite being more conservative for small low-grade tumors and large intermediate and high-grade tumors. It provides a good rationale for implementing a risk-based follow-up schedule instead of a generic one as currently proposed. A multicenter study including a larger number of patients and clinical settings is needed to draw stronger conclusions regarding the 5 to 10 years follow-up period; till then, the conservative approach is recommended.
