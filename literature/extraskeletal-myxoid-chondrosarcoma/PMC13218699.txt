## Abstract

## Background
Mesothelial proliferations range from reactive lesions to benign and malignant tumors, bearing witness to the plasticity of these cells. Their diagnosis often requires combination of morphological assessment, immunohistochemistry, and molecular testing.

## Summary
Considerable progress has been made in recent years in all the above aspects. The separation of reactive from neoplastic mesothelial proliferations has become more robust. Mesothelioma in situ is now recognized as precursor of invasive mesothelioma and better tools exist for its differentiation from benign mimics. Differentiating mesothelial tumors from other malignancies, including metastatic carcinomas, sarcomas, and other tumors, is now more easily achieved. Genetic conditions which predispose to development of mesothelioma at a young age have been identified and characterized. These issues are discussed in this review.

## Key messages
Mesothelial pathology is an evolving field and more knowledge of these processes is likely to be gained in coming years. The diagnosis of these tumors requires experts with subspecialty in cytology, thoracic, soft tissue/bone, and gynecologic pathology, cooperating with surgeons, radiologists, and oncologists at specialized centers.

## 1. The Presence or Absence of Invasion
True stromal invasion is the most reliable feature of malignancy. The invading mesothelial cells are identified in the adjacent fat, skeletal muscle, or the lung parenchyma [ , ]. Unquestionable invasion through the elastic layer of the pleura and into the adjacent fat strongly indicates malignancy. Invasive mesothelium is usually more extensive than the sequestered one and is multifocal or present as “raining down” pattern. Similarly, the finding of papillary proliferation of mesothelial cells deep within the tissue is an abnormal finding and further supports invasion [ ]. In a biopsy that only sampled fat or muscle, the presence of mesothelial cells should be considered malignant. Keratin and mesothelial markers can be valuable in identifying the mesothelial cells and their distribution.

## Pitfalls

Sequestered Mesothelium. Invasive mesothelial cells may be accompanied by desmoplastic stroma and are usually more extensive, while sequestered mesothelium always appear to be in a state of “peaceful coexistence” with surrounding connective tissue in which the mesothelial cell aggregates have a cobblestone-like pattern embedded into the collagenous stroma [ ].

Pseudoinvasion. Pseudoinvasion results from artificial displacement of the mesothelial cells or cross-sectioning of mesothelial layer along folded serosal membrane.

Entrapment. A very common benign process in which individual cells or simple glands in the setting of fibrous pleurisy/organizing pleuritis are entrapped in areas of active fibrin deposition and is accompanied by inflammatory cell reaction without deep penetration [ ]. Entrapment is the result of rapid layering and subsequent organization of an effusion on top of relatively intact mesothelial surface. When the inflammatory reaction subsides, the mesothelium usually appears as linear arrays of individual cells and small glands.
Usually, the entrapped mesothelial cells are few in number and confined to the superficial portion of a fibrotic pleura. In contrast, carcinoma and mesothelioma do not form linear arrays within tissue [ ]. The presence of active fibrin deposition and inflammation favors a benign process while the presence of branching glands or papillae should be suspected for malignancy [ , ]. In some cases, such as when adhesions of the visceral and parietal pleura occur, the reactive mesothelial cells line the elongated clefts and may resemble glands [ ].
En face sections can be extremely difficult to separate from true invasion and may present as small tumor nests separated by relatively large amounts of stoma. In such instances, identification of fat or muscle invasion is helpful [ ].

Adenomatoid Tumor. Adenomatoid tumor (AT) is more commonly encountered in the peritoneum and rarely in the pleura where it poses difficulty to distinguish it from localized mesothelioma and microcystic variant of diffuse mesothelioma. In the pleura, the radiological or gross finding of a distinct nodule separates it from reactive or sequestered mesothelium while its localized nature and lack of invasion separates it from mesothelioma. Both AT and microcystic mesothelioma form variable-sized cysts. In AT, the cysts lie within a collagenous stroma, the cells are bland and do not express any of the molecular abnormalities described for mesothelioma, instead AT expresses LICAM as a TRAF7 mutation surrogate marker [ , ] ( ).
 

## 2. Extent and Architectural Appearance of the Mesothelial Proliferation

Architecture. Surface mesothelial proliferation on the pleura should not be interpreted as malignant (except in cases of mesothelioma in situ [MIS], discussed later). Such proliferation may present as a single layer or papillae-like proliferations with no true connective tissue cores, and occasionally, gland-like formation or intracytoplasmic lumina may be present. Inflammation may or may not be present, and the mesothelial cells may show marked nuclear atypia [ ].
Proliferation in mesothelioma is usually more extensive and the presence of the following features further support malignancy:a.Presence of papillae, covered by mesothelial cells and with definite connective tissue cores [ ]. Papillary excrescences or tufts of cells without fibrovascular cores are usually benign [ ].
b.Presence of solid exfoliated malignant mesothelial cells in the form of sheets of cytologically malignant cells adjacent to the biopsy [ ].
c.A biopsy that comprises a solid tumor from one end to the other is safe to consider as malignant [ ].

Polarization. In mesothelioma, the proliferating mesothelium extends through the whole depth of the thickened pleura, while in a reactive process, the organizing mesothelial proliferation typically shows evidence of zonation with greater cellularity toward the free pleural surface/effusion and the deeper tissue is more fibrotic ( e,f). Organizing effusions typically show markedly reactive endothelial cells, and the capillaries run parallel to each other and perpendicular to the pleural surface [ , ].

## Other Soft Features

Cytological Atypia. Cells are usually larger, though this feature is difficult to recognize on biopsy sections. While mesothelioma is renowned for its bland features or subtle atypia, some cases may present with obvious atypia (hyperchromasia, coarse chromatin, etc.). However, the most striking feature is the prominent nucleoli in most cases regardless of other features of atypia [ ]. While the absence of prominent nucleoli argues against mesothelioma, it should be cautiously interpreted since this feature may be difficult to discern in some biopsies [ ]. Some mesotheliomas are heterogeneous, with focal areas of high pleomorphism. In general, the presence of pleomorphism throughout the mesothelial proliferation should raise the differential of carcinoma [ ].

Pitfall: Reactive mesothelium may show considerable nuclear atypia in some settings such as recurrent pneumothorax. Talc pleurodesis can also induce significant cytologic atypia in the reactive mesothelium, and subsequently, the pathologist should be very cautious in rendering a diagnosis of mesothelioma following talc injection [ ].

Inflammatory Reaction. While inflammation usually suggests a benign diagnosis, about 5% of mesotheliomas are associated with prominent inflammatory reaction, particularly neutrophilic exudate [ ], and the lymphohistiocytoid variant of mesothelioma is characterized by intense lymphocytic background that can mask the malignant nature of the histiocyte-like mesothelial cells [ ]. Great caution should be exercised when evaluating a highly inflamed biopsy, and malignancy should only be rendered if a bulk of tumor is evident. In cases with intense lymphocytic background, lymphoma should be ruled out.

Necrosis. While generally a sign of malignancy, it is only detected in 20% of mesotheliomas [ ]. Necrosis can occasionally be seen in bacterial empyema, tuberculous and fungal infections. In the latter situations, the necrotic tissue comprises inflammatory cells and relatively few mesothelial cells [ ].

Desmoplasia. Desmoplasia is not a helpful feature by itself. It is difficult to define and may be extensive in organizing effusions [ ].

Mitosis. Mitosis is not a helpful feature unless bizarre and is common in reactive conditions [ ].

Nodular Histiocytic Histiocytic/Mesothelial Hyperplasia. This is usually an incidental finding discovered adjacent to other disease-causing irritation to the serosal surface. It presents as discrete nodules of variable numbers of mesothelial cells admixed with histocytes and should not be confused with the lymphohistiocytoid variant of mesothelioma [ ]. The latter is characterized by histiocyte-like malignant mesothelial cells in a background of intense lymphocytic reaction. As with other mesotheliomas, it presents as a diffuse thickening/nodularity of the serosal surface, and its immunoprofile is similar to that of mesothelioma [ ].

(2) Serosal Fluids and FNA

FNA of mesothelioma, epithelioid or sarcomatoid, has increased recently especially with the advancement of transbronchial FNA either for sampling a metastatic lesion and/or more commonly for staging the disease. In that setting, the neoplastic nature of the mesothelial cells is usually not a challenge in an FNA retrieved from a pleural nodule or a lymph node. Application of immunocytochemistry to establish mesothelial cell origin is all that is needed to render a diagnosis of mesothelioma. Occasionally benign mesothelium may be retrieved in the path of the needle and may result in erroneous diagnosis of malignancy. The benign cells are usually few in number and present as small monolayer sheets rather than clusters in the background of the lesional cells. Closer examination reveals abundant cytoplasm, low nuclear-to-cytoplasmic ratio, and rounded, usually central, nuclei. Reactive mesothelial cells adjacent to other lesions may exhibit more conspicuous nucleoli and multinucleated cells [ – ].

## Effusions
The possibility of metastatic carcinoma should be ruled out in any malignant effusion and mesothelial origin should be established by immunocytochemistry before a mesothelioma diagnosis is rendered. Several publications have recently elaborated on the differential of benign versus malignant mesothelium and set the guidelines for the cytologic diagnosis of mesothelioma [ – ]. With the introduction of molecular surrogate markers (refer to section on Ancillary Testing), this differential can be established with certainty in the majority of cases. Yet, since these markers do not have 100% sensitivity, morphology will remain the cornerstone of the diagnosis. In general, the presence of inflammatory exudate, whether acute or chronic, is not helpful in the differential diagnosis and both can have extensive inflammation, especially after chest tube insertion. Similarly, mitotic activity can be seen in reactive effusions and only abnormal mitosis can raise suspicion for malignancy.

## Bird’s Eye View of the Specimen
Recognition of an abnormal proliferation of mesothelium starts at low-power examination. While smears of either benign or malignant mesothelium can be high in cellularity, they are still morphologically distinct. Epithelioid mesothelioma that sheds properly, presents with super cellularity that is unmatched by any reactive process. The examiner will notice a monotonous population of cells and clusters that are larger and vary widely in size and shape [ ]. Reactive mesothelium may manifest some clusters, but never as numerous ( ).
 

## Hallmark Features Diagnostic of Mesothelioma

1.
Hypercellularity: mesothelioma most commonly presents as numerous clusters in a background of single cells. Some present exclusively as clusters and rare cases as single cells only. Regardless of the pattern, hypercellularity remains the striking feature of these effusions. Even at high magnification, the clusters are innumerable in contrast with reactive effusions where the cellularity is generally moderate to high at best, and the number of clusters does not exceed 2–3 per high-power view. In fact, mesothelioma should be suspected in overwhelmingly cellular effusions regardless of cellular atypia. Also, the presence of only cellular clusters in a low cellular sample should raise the suspicion for mesothelioma and trigger further evaluation. It should be noted that because cell density of the specimen is controlled in liquid-based preparations, the hypercellularity is unfortunately not as striking as in direct smears. However, specimens are still relatively more cellular than reactive fluids.
2.
The presence of complex papillae or tissue fragments: in mesothelioma, the clusters are not only variable in size and shape and form either morules with scalloped borders or, more frequently, three-dimensional (3D) spheres, but can also show complexity in architecture. They tend to cluster together and/or show branching. True papillae can sometimes be detected with elongated/oval shapes and/or collagen cores. Sometimes, the clusters appear as hollow rings (best seen in cell blocks) lined by cuboidal cells with central nuclei, and these should not be mistaken for carcinoma. The presence of large tissue fragments is an abnormal finding and diagnostic of malignancy. However, it should be remembered that relatively large aggregates are more frequently encountered in pericardial effusions, and in peritoneal fluids associated with cirrhosis, dialysis, or hernia sac, and therefore should be judiciously interpreted. In reactive effusions, the cellularity and number of clusters are less striking. The clusters are more commonly simple, two-dimensional (2D) with no papillary or complex architecture.

3.
Cytologic atypia: this can be deceptive and not as useful in the differential of benign versus malignant mesothelium. While the presence of overt nuclear atypia diagnostic of malignancy favors mesothelioma, many mesotheliomas exhibit either subtle or no atypia, while in some reactive effusions, particularly those encountered with liver cirrhosis, peritoneal dialysis, pulmonary emboli, or pneumonia, mesothelial cells can exhibit considerable nuclear atypia. It is important therefore to recognize that the diagnosis of mesothelioma does not rest on cytologic atypia alone, but also on architectural atypia.

## Soft Criteria That Favor Mesothelioma

1.Variation in size and shape of the cells and clusters. Cell sizes range from that of benign mesothelium to large and sometimes gigantic cells.
2.Large cells regardless of number of nuclei that may approach the size of adjacent small morules.
3.Cells with abundant cytoplasm and normal nuclear-to-cytoplasmic ratio.
4.Multinucleated cells that may also exhibit atypia.
5.Prominent nucleoli and frequently macronucleoli. This feature is very common in mesothelioma and sometimes the only feature of atypia detected.
6.Increased number of “pseudokeratotic cells” that represent small, degenerated cells with intense eosinophilia.
7.Thick mucin-like material that reflects increased hyaluronan secreted in the setting of mesothelioma. It may also cause large intracytoplasmic vacuoles and imparts the gross impression of very thick tar-like consistency. It appears as small pink blebs around the cells or as granular background debris in May-Grünwald Giemsa (MGG) stain. It stains dark blue with Alcian blue and is sensitive to digestion.

## Mesothelioma In Situ
The World Health Organization of Thoracic tumors in 2021 added a new category of preinvasive malignant mesothelium coined “mesothelioma in situ” [ ]. By definition, MIS presents as a single layer of bland-appearing cuboidal-to-columnar cells covering the pleural surfaces with no evidence of invasion in a patient with unexplained recurrent effusions and negative radiological findings. The diagnosis can only be established in mesothelial cells that show loss of molecular surrogate markers such as BAP1, methylthioadenosine phosphorylase (MTAP), and NF2 [ – ]. To date, a total of 43 cases were reported in the literature. Of those, 30 cases arose from the pleura, 11 from the peritoneum, and 1 in the testis [ ].
It is important to realize that the finding of MIS in a biopsy does not rule out invasive disease elsewhere and thorough sampling of the pleura or the peritoneum is necessary before planning for a definitive therapy such as extrapleural pneumonectomy or heated intrathoracic chemotherapy.
Reports of effusions from patients with MIS illustrated that the cytological findings may initially be subtle and overlap with benign/reactive changes, but the features progress with recurrences and eventually become indistinguishable from those of mesothelioma [ ] ( a-d). Studies have shown that molecular surrogates are supportive of neoplasia even in the initial benign-appearing fluids [ , ]. Since invasion cannot be assessed by cytology, the diagnosis of “in situ” in obviously malignant effusions can be suggested but not definitively rendered. Instead, if neoplasia is confirmed by surrogate markers, the report should be issued as “malignant mesothelial cells/mesothelioma” with a comment stating that in the context of negative imaging, this may represent at least MIS and further evaluation is recommended. This will initiate the necessary investigations and early intervention. If surrogate markers are not supportive despite the malignant morphology in radiologically negative cases, those effusions should be diagnosed as suspicious for mesothelioma [ , ].
 

## Pitfalls

Reactive Surface Mesothelium. Early effusions in some cases of MIS may be cytologically subtle and mimic reactive changes. We recommend performing molecular surrogate markers in cases with recurrent unexplained effusions especially in high-risk patients [ ].

MIS Mimicking Well-Differentiated Papillary Mesothelial Tumor. Cases of MIS were reported to present as papillae with myxoid or fibrous cores lined by a single layer of mesothelium, morphologically indistinguishable from well-differentiated papillary mesothelial tumor (WDPMT) but demonstrate the loss of BAP1 or MTAP. It is therefore recommended that surrogate markers be applied in such papillary lesions to rule out MIS [ , ] ( e, f).

## Spindle Cell Proliferations

(1) Biopsy Specimens

When the bulk of the biopsy is made of a dense fibrous/collagenous tissue, the differential diagnosis includes DM versus fibrous pleurisy. Either one may have areas with higher cellularity.

## Features Favoring Fibrous Pleurisy
The following are the features favoring fibrous pleurisy [ , ] ( ):(1)Typically has uniform appearance and thickness with no nodular proliferation.
(2)Spindled cells may be crowded and atypical toward the effusion side and beneath the fibrinous exudate, i.e., zonation.
(3)Presence of numerous elongated capillaries that seem to almost completely traverse the thickened pleura in a perpendicular direction to the pleural surface. These capillaries are the result of organization of the fibrinous exudate and are part of the resulting granulation tissue.
(4)Collagen bundles do not extend into the fat. In rare occasions, when collagen bundles extend into the fat, it is either acellular or contains few spindled cells that are negative to broad spectrum cytokeratin.

 

## Features Favoring DM or SM

1.
Invasion: identified in two-thirds of biopsies and may be the sole indication of malignancy in addition to low cellularity (DM) in 12% of cases [ ]. Invasion is seen as trickling of spindled cells amidst the adipocytes of the chest wall or along the secondary lobular septa or into adjacent alveoli and are typically identified by broad spectrum cytokeratin stains. The finding of fibrous extension through the interlobular septa is suggestive of DM since very few processes in the lungs cause such fibrosis [ ]. Extension of the fibrous process into the alveolar spaces may appear as polypoid projections of paucicellular collagen with few spindled atypical cells and superficially resemble bronchiolitis obliterans organizing pneumonia. While DM forms expansile stromal nodules, this is not seen in organizing pneumonia [ ]. Care should be exercised not to misinterpret puckering and indentation in the lungs of fibrotic pleura in the setting of rounded atelectasis.
2.
Bland necrosis: a peculiar type of necrosis identified in one-third of biopsies. It manifests as a subtle change in the hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) stain in well-circumscribed areas, in which eosin appears lighter and may contain nuclear debris or be totally devoid of nuclei. The underlying tissue pattern remains intact ( ).
3.
Frankly sarcomatoid areas: the finding of a biopsy with increased spindle cells through the full thickness of the biopsy and obvious atypia with no evidence of zonation is indicative of SM. The spindle cells typically show a short storiform “patternless pattern” or “herringbone pattern” of interlacing fascicles. In desmoplastic biopsies, the finding of areas of sarcomatoid features usually with the storiform pattern is supportive of DM and can be detected in 80% of biopsies and may be the sole finding in 20% of DM. Typically, there is abrupt transition from collagenous areas to more cellular areas with spindled cells that may also form a vaguely defined nodular expansion. While cytologic atypia is supportive of malignancy in SM, it can be very difficult to discern nuclear atypia in the elongated spindled cells squeezed between the collagen bundles of DM.
4.
Distant metastasis: the presence of metastasis is supportive of malignancy, and cases of DM were reported to initially present as osseous metastasis. The diagnosis of DM should be entertained if a bone biopsy contains dense collagen with few scattered spindle cells, and concurrent evaluation of the chest X-ray findings is recommended. These metastases were reported to consist of hyalinized center of the deposits and more cellular areas at the periphery.

 DM typically produces a markedly thickened pleura. However, in some instances, it may present as a thin pleura superficially resembling organizing pneumonia. The findings in DM can be very heterogeneous, making identification of invasion crucial for accurate diagnosis. It is recommended therefore that larger specimens be totally submitted in suspicious cases [ ].

## Differential Diagnosis and Pitfalls

Pleomorphic Carcinoma. A meta-analysis showed considerable overlap in immunophenotype and emphasized the need to correlate with clinical and imaging information [ ].

Solitary Fibrous Tumor. Presents as a bulky well-defined mass arising from the submesothelial mesenchymal tissue with a wide age range (5–78 years) [ ]. About 32% of cases present in the abdomen/pelvis and 28% in the pleura. This is a fibroblastic tumor with bland-appearing spindled cells growing in a “patternless” pattern, alternating hypercellular and hypocellular areas, areas of collagenous stroma, and hemangiopericitic-like vessels. The tumor cells express STAT6, CD34, CD99, and BCL-2 and are negative for cytokeratins and desmin [ – ].

Heterologous Mesothelioma. SM may contain heterologous elements, most commonly chondrosarcoma and osteosarcoma. Identifying an area of typical mesothelioma, especially if it expresses keratin and mesothelial markers, is instrumental in this diagnosis. However, the findings in biopsies, even generous ones, depend on the area sampled and, in some cases, may comprise heterologous elements only. Klebe et al. [ ] recommend that in pleural biopsies regardless of keratin expression, in patients with diffuse pleural thickening/nodularity rather than a discrete large nodule, the diagnosis of heterologous mesothelioma should be favored over primary osteosarcoma or chondrosarcoma, in a way similar to guidelines for conventional non-heterologous mesothelioma [ , ].

Synovial Sarcoma. Depending on the sampled area, synovial sarcoma (SS) may present as sarcomatoid or biphasic tumor that may arise from the parietal or visceral pleura. The median age at presentation is 52 years, range (19–81 years) [ ]. It has been reported to at least focally react with calretinin, D2-40, and CK5/6, but not WT-1, thus mimicking mesothelioma. However, SS presents as a localized mass rather than the diffuse thickening and nodularity of mesothelioma. The malignant cells are immunoreactive to epithelial membrane antigen (EMA), keratins, BerEp4, CD99, CD56, Bcl-2, and TLE-1 which are negative in mesothelioma [ ]. SS is characterized by SYT-SSX1 fusion gene that can be identified by RT-PCR or surrogate immunohistochemistry (IHC) markers [ – ], as well as next generation sequencing (NGS) and fluorescent in situ hybridization (FISH).

Other Sarcomas. The initial panel of IHC should include markers to rule out other sarcomas such as desmin, ERG, etc. along with mesothelial markers. Keratin and calretinin have the most value in the differential of sarcoma versus SM. However, a study by Lucas et al. [ ] showed that many SM show either decreased or completely negative expression for cytokeratins, while other sarcomas showed variable expression of all other markers with the exception of SMA, rendering the differential more arbitrary and depending mostly on the clinical and radiological findings. Staining for keratin by itself is not always helpful since it is poorly expressed in SM and may be expressed by some sarcomas.

## Effusions and FNA
The cytologic diagnosis of mesothelioma in effusions is limited to epithelioid or epithelioid component of the biphasic type. SM presents as hemorrhagic recurrent effusions but does not shed cells in the fluid, and if they do, only rare highly atypical cells can be detected. In these seldom cases, if the number of cells allow further testing and are proven to be malignant mesothelial cells, a diagnosis of “suspicious for mesothelioma” should be rendered to trigger further clinical evaluation.
SM can be diagnosed by FNA, based on the morphology and confirmation of mesothelial origin. The differential diagnoses are similar to those described for biopsy.

## The Peritoneum
Many of the criteria and differential diagnoses in the discussion above pertain also to the peritoneum, and the guidelines dealing with the diagnosis of cytological specimens are essentially identical. There are nevertheless differences with respect to the mesothelial entities and the main differential diagnoses which are relevant in this context. These can be summarized as follows:1.The vast majority of peritoneal mesotheliomas are epithelioid. Biphasic tumors are less common than their pleural counterparts; sarcomatoid tumors are exceedingly rare; and DM, though mentioned in a few case reports, is essentially nonexistent. In the large series by Baker et al. [ ], 71/75 tumors were epithelioid, with only 4 biphasic and 1 sarcomatoid tumor. In the more recent series by Malpica et al. [ ], 132/164 tumors were epithelioid, 32 were biphasic, and none was sarcomatoid.
2.Two entities, adenomatoid tumor and multicystic mesothelial proliferations (a.k.a. multicystic mesothelioma) are far more common in the peritoneal cavity. Of note, the latter is not listed as entity in the 2020 edition of the WHO classification of female genital tumors [ ].
3.The most important and most challenging differential diagnosis of mesothelioma within the peritoneal cavity is from serous carcinoma of the female genital tract, particularly tubo-ovarian high-grade serous carcinoma [ ]. These tumors bear much closer resemblance to mesothelioma than lung or breast carcinomas do. Although they readily metastasize to the pleural cavity, this differential diagnosis is more frequently encountered in the peritoneal cavity. Furthermore, expression of female genital markers such as PAX8 in peritoneal mesothelioma is well recognized, as is the expression of mesothelioma markers in serous carcinomas (see Ancillary Testing section below).

## Adenomatoid Tumor
ATs are uncommon tumors that are most often diagnosed in female genital organs, particularly the uterus and fallopian tube, where they are often found incidentally, as well as in the paratesticular region. They are rare in the peritoneum and extremely rare in the pleura.
Tumors consist of slit-like or tubulocystic structures lined by cuboidal or flat cells with low-grade atypia that proliferate within fibrous tissue or smooth muscle. The majority of tumors are small and pose no diagnostic challenge, but large, multicystic, diffuse, and/or multiple ones may be confused with other entities [ ]. Within the peritoneal cavity, the latter include sequestered mesothelium, multicystic mesothelioma, Müllerian tumors, and microcystic mesothelioma, a rare but recognized entity in the peritoneal cavity [ ].
AT expresses mesothelial markers such as calretinin, WT1, and D2-40, as well as cytokeratins. In contrast to mesothelioma, it is frequently negative for GATA3 [ ]. In contrast to mesotheliomas, AT frequently harbor mutation in TRAF7 and show no BAP1 mutations or loss of MTAP [ ]. In the peritoneum, they are separated from Müllerian tumors by their negative reaction for PAX8 [ ].
The separation of AT from multicystic mesothelial proliferation/multicystic mesothelioma may be challenging, given their overlapping morphology and IHC profile. The latter lesion is not uncommonly seen in the peritoneum, often as incidental finding in gynecologic surgery, and has variable size and extent. It characteristically consists of multiple cystic spaces lined by mesothelial cells with little atypia or mitotic activity ( ). When extensive, these tumors may undergo malignant transformation to mesothelioma and have consequently been treated by surgery, in some cases with heated intraperitoneal chemotherapy (HIPEC) [ ], though laparoscopic surgery may be sufficient when disease is more limited [ ].
 Agaimy et al. [ ] recently published a series of 5 peritoneal mesothelial tumors of uncertain clinical potential which harbored NR4A3 fusions and had pure adenomatoid/microcystic morphology. Tumors had limited expression of calretinin, a feature which may be helpful in the differential diagnosis from AT. No recurrence was observed after a relatively short follow-up period.

## WDPMT
WDPMT grossly presents as single or multiple papillary or nodular lesions, usually less than 2 cm in the largest dimension. The papillae have fibrotic or myxoid core, and their surface is lined by a single layer of typically bland, flattened to cuboidal mesothelial cells. Stromal invasion is absent ( ). Cells stain for mesothelial markers, such as calretinin, CK5/6, D2-40, and WT1 [ ].
 In the WHO classification of female genital tumors, this tumor is specified to be benign. However, as mentioned above, tumors with identical morphology are in fact MIS, which is some cases may be seen in the vicinity of a bona fide invasive mesothelioma and should not be classified as benign [ ]. A growing body of literature has in recent years reported molecular changes in these tumors at both the pleural and peritoneal location, the most common of which is the loss of BAP1 [ , , – ]. Other mutations reported in this tumor or its mimic MIS are in MTAP/CDKN2A [ ], TRAF7 or CDC42 (mutually exclusive) [ ], and TP53 [ ].
Shrestha et al. [ ] reported on the presence of several mutations in WDPMT, including changes in EHD1, ATM, FBXO10, SH2D2A, CDH5, MAGED1, and TP73, all of which are absent in mesothelioma. In another study, 17/19 sequenced tumors carried a TRAF7 alteration within the WD40 domain and 2/19 had a CREBBP alteration. Germline mutation analysis of 17 patients identified inherited mutations in 4, affecting MSH6, RAD51C, RAD51D, and TGFBR1 [ ].

## Solid Papillary Mesothelial Tumor (SPMT)
Solid papillary mesothelial tumor is a newly described tumor diagnosed in peritoneal specimens from women aged 30–72 years in the case series published (n = 9) [ ]. Tumors were composed of sheets or nests of epithelioid cells with bland morphology and little to no mitotic activity, with the absence of necrosis. BAP1 was retained and CDKN2A FISH did not show homozygous deletion, findings which may be of aid in differentiating this tumor from malignant mesothelioma. No recurrences or tumor-related deaths were observed [ ].

## Mesothelioma
The criteria described above for identifying mesothelial cells as malignant are applicable to the peritoneum as well. Examples are shown in and . In addition to IHC (see Ancillary Testing, below), genetic testing may of useful in equivocal cases.
 A review of 13 studies, in which a total of 824 peritoneal mesotheliomas were analyzed, identified 7 genes which were altered in ≥10% tumors, including BAP1 (50%), NF2 (25%), CDKN2A (23%), CDKN2B (17%), PBRM1 (15%), TP53 (14%), and SETD2 (13%). The presence of germline mutations was investigated in 5/13 studies, in which 16/96 (17%) patients were found to harbor such mutations, mainly in BAP1 (7%) [ ]. In contrast, mesotheliomas in the pediatric population or young adults are characterized by ALK gene rearrangements [ – ].

## Ancillary Testing
The main differential diagnostic issues in surgical pathology and cytopathology of the serosal cavities are the differentiation between mesothelioma and nonneoplastic mesothelial proliferations and between mesothelioma and other cancers, most commonly metastatic carcinoma. An in-depth discussion of IHC markers and genetic tests used in each scenario is beyond the scope of this review, though many of the genes involved in mesothelial neoplasia are discussed in previous sections.
Currently, the most widely used test for diagnosing mesothelioma is the loss of BRCA1-associated protein-1 (BAP1), the protein product of the BAP1 gene at chromosome region 3p21.1, and of MTAP, which are seen in >50% of pleural mesotheliomas. The MTAP gene is located at chromosome region 9p21.3, where the CDKN2A gene, encoding the cell cycle regulators p14 and p16, is similarly found, and the homozygous loss of this region, best visualized using FISH, is strongly suggestive of mesothelioma [ ]. It should nonetheless be commented that while the loss of BAP1 is common also in peritoneal mesotheliomas, the loss of MTAP is seen in only a minority of these tumors [ – ], a fact that argues against the use of this marker in differentiating mesothelioma from both reactive mesothelium and metastatic carcinomas. Finally, while not favored at present by some experts, the authors still find staining for EMA and desmin to be useful in this differential diagnosis, with thick brush-like membrane expression of EMA and loss of desmin favoring mesothelioma over reactive proliferation [ ].
In the differential diagnosis between mesothelioma and metastatic carcinoma, different antibody combinations are used at different institutions. Several markers, such as calretinin for mesothelioma and claudin-4 for carcinoma, are widely accepted to be robust irrespective of carcinoma origin ([ ] and elsewhere). For others, caution is advised. One recent example is HEG1, a recently described mesothelial marker, which is highly specific in the differential diagnosis from lung carcinoma [ ] and breast carcinoma [ ] but performs poorly in differentiating mesothelioma from tubo-ovarian carcinoma [ ].
In the case of sarcomatoid tumors, protein expression may be less informative than in epithelioid or biphasic mesotheliomas, requiring the addition of molecular testing in order to arrive at a conclusive diagnosis [ ].

## Concluding Comment
Recent understanding of the morphological, phenotypic, and molecular changes undertaken by mesothelial cells in reaction to injury and in tumorigenesis has resulted in more robust diagnosis of these processes. Some cases nevertheless remain challenging and should be diagnosed at specialized centers which have sufficient experience in this area. The separation between reactive proliferations, tumors of low malignant potential, and bona fide malignant ones should allow for better tailoring of treatment, particularly with respect to avoiding the significant side-effects of extensive surgery and systemic or local chemotherapy. As in other areas of pathology, new tests require validation in large cohorts through multi-institutional collaboration.
