## Abstract

## Background
Approximately 1% of clinically treatable tyrosine kinase fusions, including anaplastic lymphoma kinase, neurotrophic tyrosine receptor kinase, RET proto-oncogene, and ROS proto-oncogene 1, have been identified in soft tissue sarcomas via comprehensive genome profiling based on DNA sequencing. Histologic tumor-specific fusion genes have been reported in approximately 20% of soft tissue sarcomas; however, unlike tyrosine kinase fusion genes, these fusions cannot be directly targeted in therapy. Approximately 80% of tumor-specific fusion-negative sarcomas, including myxofibrosarcoma and leiomyosarcoma, that are defined in complex karyotype sarcomas remain genetically uncharacterized; this mutually exclusive pattern of mutations suggests that other mutually exclusive driver oncogenes are yet to be discovered. Tumor-specific, fusion-negative sarcomas may be associated with unique translocations, and oncogenic fusion genes, including tyrosine kinase fusions, may have been overlooked in these sarcomas.

## Questions/purposes
(1) Can DNA- or RNA-based analysis reveal any characteristic gene alterations in bone and soft tissue sarcomas? (2) Can useful and potential tyrosine kinase fusions in tumors from tumor-specific, fusion-negative sarcomas be detected using an RNA-based screening system? (3) Do the identified potential fusion tumors, especially in neurotrophic tyrosine receptor kinase gene fusions in bone sarcoma, transform cells and respond to targeted drug treatment in in vitro assays? (4) Can the identified tyrosine kinase fusion genes in sarcomas be useful therapeutic targets?

## Methods
Between 2017 and 2020, we treated 100 patients for bone and soft tissue sarcomas at five institutions. Any biopsy or surgery from which a specimen could be obtained was included as potentially eligible. Ninety percent (90 patients) of patients were eligible; a further 8% (8 patients) were excluded because they were either lost to follow-up or their diagnosis was changed, leaving 82% (82 patients) for analysis here. To answer our first and second questions regarding gene alterations and potential tyrosine kinase fusions in eight bone and 74 soft tissue sarcomas, we used the TruSight Tumor 170 assay to detect mutations, copy number variations, and gene fusions in the samples. To answer our third question, we performed functional analyses involving in vitro assays to determine whether the identified tyrosine kinase fusions were associated with oncogenic abilities and drug responses. Finally, to determine usefulness as therapeutic targets, two pediatric patients harboring an NTRK fusion and an ALK fusion were treated with tyrosine kinase inhibitors in clinical trials.

## Results
DNA/RNA-based analysis demonstrated characteristic alterations in bone and soft tissue sarcomas; DNA-based analyses detected TP53 and copy number alterations of MDM2 and CDK4. These single-nucleotide variants and copy number variations were enriched in specific fusion-negative sarcomas. RNA-based screening detected fusion genes in 24% (20 of 82) of patients. Useful potential fusions were detected in 19% (11 of 58) of tumor-specific fusion-negative sarcomas, with nine of these patients harboring tyrosine kinase fusion genes; five of these patients had in-frame tyrosine kinase fusion genes (STRN3-NTRK3, VWC2-EGFR, ICK-KDR, FOXP2-MET, and CEP290-MET) with unknown pathologic significance. The functional analysis revealed that STRN3-NTRK3 rearrangement that was identified in bone had a strong transforming potential in 3T3 cells, and that STRN3-NTRK3–positive cells were sensitive to larotrectinib in vitro. To confirm the usefulness of identified tyrosine kinase fusion genes as therapeutic targets, patients with well-characterized LMNA-NTRK1 and CLTC-ALK fusions were treated with tyrosine kinase inhibitors in clinical trials, and a complete response was achieved.

## Conclusion
We identified useful potential therapeutic targets for tyrosine kinase fusions in bone and soft tissue sarcomas using RNA-based analysis. We successfully identified STRN3-NTRK3 fusion in a patient with leiomyosarcoma of bone and determined the malignant potential of this fusion gene via functional analyses and drug effects. In light of these discoveries, comprehensive genome profiling should be considered even if the sarcoma is a bone sarcoma. There seem to be some limitations regarding current DNA-based comprehensive genome profiling tests, and it is important to use RNA testing for proper diagnosis and accurate identification of fusion genes. Studies on more patients, validation of results, and further functional analysis of unknown tyrosine kinase fusion genes are required to establish future treatments.

## Clinical Relevance
DNA- and RNA-based screening systems may be useful for detecting tyrosine kinase fusion genes in specific fusion-negative sarcomas and identifying key therapeutic targets, leading to possible breakthroughs in the treatment of bone and soft tissue sarcomas. Given that current DNA sequencing misses fusion genes, RNA-based screening systems should be widely considered as a worldwide test for sarcoma. If standard treatments such as chemotherapy are not effective, or even if the sarcoma is of bone, RNA sequencing should be considered to identify as many therapeutic targets as possible.

## Introduction
Soft tissue sarcoma is a rare malignant tumor of various histologic types that arises in soft tissues [ , ]. High-grade soft tissue sarcoma is managed via surgical resection, radiation, and chemotherapy [ , ]. Despite these multidisciplinary therapies, advanced soft tissue sarcomas are still characterized by poor prognoses, and effective systemic therapies have not yet been established [ ]. Notably, the 5-year overall survival rate for advanced soft tissue sarcoma is only 30% [ ]. Although molecule-targeting drugs such as imatinib, pazopanib, and olaratumab have been approved to treat soft tissue sarcomas [ , ], the response rates are not very high [ ]. Therefore, novel therapeutic targets and effective therapies for soft tissue sarcomas should be identified.
The discovery of gene rearrangements involving the receptor tyrosine kinase genes, including anaplastic lymphoma kinase, ROS proto-oncogene 1, and RET proto-oncogene, has revolutionized the management of non–small cell lung cancer subsets harboring these alterations [ , , ]. Approximately 20% of soft tissue sarcomas are induced by pathognomonic chromosomal translocations, which lead to the formation of fusion oncogenes; they exhibit unique genomic characteristics [ ]. The pathogenesis of tumor-specific fusion genes in soft tissue sarcomas, including SS18-SSX1 in synovial sarcoma, are defined by the WHO Classification of Soft Tissue Tumors [ ]. These genes are associated with chromosomal translocations that lead to the formation of oncogenic fusion proteins [ , ], although these fusions are not directly therapeutic, as tyrosine kinase fusion genes are. The remaining 80% of soft tissue sarcomas in tumor-specific fusion-negative sarcomas, including myxofibrosarcoma and leiomyosarcoma that are identified in complex karyotype sarcomas, are associated with genetic abnormalities including losses, gains, amplifications, and mutations, but the genetic characteristics of tumor-specific, fusion-negative sarcomas remain unclear. In our previous study, we observed a mutually exclusive mutation pattern, suggesting that other mutually exclusive driver oncogenes may eventually be discovered [ ]. We theorized that tumor-specific, fusion-negative sarcomas should be considered when referring to any unique translocations. Notably, RNA-based sequencing has revealed novel fusion genes in soft tissue sarcomas, and tyrosine kinase fusion genes are associated with therapeutic agents in tumor-specific fusion-negative sarcomas [ ]. In particular, the recent recognition of neurotrophic tyrosine receptor kinase fusions in a broader spectrum of tumors, including soft tissue sarcomas, has been followed by reports of therapeutic responses to tropomyosin receptor kinase inhibitors in soft tissue sarcomas [ , , ]. Tyrosine kinase inhibitors targeting these oncogenic signaling pathways have elicited dramatic and durable responses in patients [ , ] and are currently available as first-line treatments for fusion-positive tumors. However, tyrosine kinase fusion genes including NTRK, ALK, ROS1, and RET have also been detected in bone and soft tissue sarcomas, where comprehensive genome profiling using DNA sequencing has shown that the frequency of these tyrosine kinase fusion genes is approximately 1% [ , ]. Furthermore, in a particularly rare case, nonfunctional NTRK gene fusions have been reported in osteosarcomas [ , ]. This suggests that further screening studies and functional assays are required, even for NTRK fusions.
Cancer gene panels that include a substantial portion of potentially targetable genomic alterations as next-generation sequencing platforms are garnering increasing attention in precision medicine [ ]. However, recent reports have revealed that despite being well-designed, these cancer gene panels and associated DNA-based next-generation sequencing assays have several limitations when detecting targetable fusion genes [ , ]. For example, gene fusions usually occur in introns, which usually contain many repetitive sequences, making it difficult to detect intron fusion points with high sensitivity using DNA capture probes [ , ]. The RNA capture method, which targets only the junction of exons and is not affected by introns, thus has a marked advantage in detecting multiple types of fusion genes [ ].
In this study, we therefore asked: (1) Can DNA- or RNA-based analysis reveal any characteristic gene alterations in bone and soft tissue sarcomas? (2) Can useful and potential tyrosine kinase fusions in tumors from tumor-specific, fusion-negative sarcomas be detected using an RNA-based screening system? (3) Do the identified potential fusion tumors, especially in NTRK gene fusions in bone sarcomas, transform cells and respond to targeted drug treatment in in vitro assays? (4) Can the identified tyrosine kinase fusion genes in sarcomas be useful therapeutic targets?

## Material and Methods

## Overview of Study Design
To detect new therapeutic targets in bone and soft tissue sarcomas, we used DNA- and RNA-based analyses. We used a DNA workflow to identify single nucleotide variants and small insertions and deletions in 151 genes and copy number variations in 59 genes. A panel of 55 genes for an RNA workflow was also used to identify splice variants and gene fusions. Presumed useful and potential fusion events in positive cases were validated using RNA sequencing and confirmatory reverse transcription–polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR). Functional analyses using in vitro assays were performed to determine whether the identified fusions were associated with oncogenic abilities and drug responses. Based on these genomic profiles, two pediatric patients were treated with tyrosine kinase inhibitors in clinical trials.

## Patients
The study cohort consisted of 82 patients, eight with bone sarcoma and 74 with soft tissue sarcoma, who were treated at the Juntendo University Hospital (74 patients), Saitama Medical University International Medical Center (five patients), Tochigi Cancer Center (one patient), Tohoku University Hospital (one patient), and Kyushu University Hospital (one patient). Between 2017 and 2020, we treated 100 patients for bone and soft tissue sarcomas at these five institutions. Of those, any patient with biopsy or surgery from which a specimen could be obtained was considered potentially eligible. Based on that, 90% (90 patients) were eligible; a further 8% (eight of 100) were excluded because they were either lost to follow-up or their diagnosis was changed, leaving 82% (82 of 100) for analysis here. Tumor tissue specimens were mainly collected via either a surgical specimen or diagnostic biopsy before chemotherapy. EDTA decalcification was performed for bone tumors. Histologic subtypes were diagnosed by two board-certified pathologists (TH and TS) based on the WHO classification of tumors.

## Characteristics of Tumor Samples
Samples were collected from 82 patients, eight with bone sarcoma and 74 with soft tissue sarcoma. The median (range) age of the patients at diagnosis was 48 years (0 to 88 years). Most tumors were present in soft tissue (90% [74 of 82 patients]). Twenty-nine percent (24 of 82 patients) of tumors were diagnosed as bone and soft tissue sarcomas with tumor-specific fusion, whereas 71% (58 of 82 patients) were diagnosed as those without tumor-specific fusion (Table ).
 

## TruSight Tumor 170 Assay
To identify genetic alterations that can act as therapeutic targets for bone and soft tissue sarcomas, a TruSight Tumor 170 assay was performed with 82 samples. The TruSight Tumor 170 panel consists of full coding sequences of 170 genes. This includes a DNA workflow to identify single nucleotide variants and small insertions and deletions in 151 genes, and copy number variations in 59 genes. A panel of 55 genes for an RNA workflow was also used to identify splice variants and gene fusions. DNA and RNA extracted from formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded specimens were isolated using a Maxwell RSC DNA FFPE Kit or a Maxwell RSC RNA FFPE Kit (Promega). For DNA and RNA library preparation and enrichment, a TruSight Tumor 170 Kit (Illumina) was used following the manufacturer's instructions. Post-enriched libraries were quantified, pooled, and sequenced on a NextSeq 550 sequencer (Illumina). The quality of the NextSeq 550 sequencing runs was assessed via the Illumina Sequencing Analysis Viewer (Illumina). Sequencing data were analyzed with the TruSight Tumor 170 Local App 1.0.0 software program (Illumina). Fusions were detected at the RNA level [ ].
Next-generation sequencing data of the patients were analyzed as follows: Raw sequencing data were aligned to the hg19 genome build and variants were called using the TruSight Tumor 170 v1.0 LocalApp software program (Illumina). Mutations were annotated using the VariantStudio version 3.0.12 software program (Illumina).

## OncoPrint Plot
We used a heatmap to visualize multiple genomic alterations in sarcomas and tumor-specific, fusion-negative sarcomas. To that end, an OncoPrint plot was created using the ComplexHeatmap software package [ ] in Bioconductor version 3.11. For variants plotted in OncoPrint, the filters described below were applied.
Small variants (missense, insertion, and deletion) were accepted if they passed all of the following filters: Filter (the status of the variant call quality) is “PASS,” Alt Variant Freq (the frequency of the Alt Allele) ≥ 10%, Allele Freq (the allele frequency from all populations of 1000 genome data) < 1%, and ExAC Freq All (the allele frequency of the variant in the Exome Aggregation Consortium database, all populations) < 0.1%. In addition, variants were accepted if they passed any of the following filters: ClinVar Significance (the clinical significance or classification assigned to the allele as reported in the ClinVar database) is “pathogenic” or “likely pathogenic” or Consequence (a consequence of the variant, described in Sequence Ontology standardized vocabulary) is “frameshift_variant” or “stop_gained.” Furthermore, for missense, insertion, and deletion, variants were accepted if they passed all of the following filters: Exonic (a variant found within a coding region) is “yes” and Amino Acids (amino acid change) contain “/”, meaning that an amino acid was changed. All small variants were manually reviewed using Integrative Genomics Viewer [ ]. Fusions were accepted if KeepFusion (high confidence fusion) was True. Copy number variants (amplification) were accepted if the detected fold change was 2.0 or more. Copy number variants (deletion) were accepted if the detected fold change was no more than 0.5.

## RNA Extraction, cDNA Synthesis, RT-PCR, and Sequencing
To confirm the detection of fusion genes by the TruSight Tumor 170 assay, total RNA was extracted from formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded samples using an RNeasy Plus Mini Kit (Qiagen), and first-strand synthesis was performed using a SuperScript IV First-Strand Synthesis System (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The resulting template cDNA was subjected to RT-PCR using Go Taq G2 Green Master Mix (Promega Corp). Primers were used to amplify the expected fusion genes (Supplemental Table 1; http://links.lww.com/CORR/B252). The amplified products were subjected to Sanger sequencing using a 3130xl Genetic Analyzer (Thermo Fisher Scientific).

## Cell Lines
To identify potential tyrosine kinase fusions associated with oncogenic abilities and drug responses, we performed functional analyses using in vitro assays on the following cell lines. Human embryonic kidney 293T cells and mouse 3T3 fibroblasts were purchased from the American Type Culture Collection and maintained in Dulbecco modified Eagle medium-F12 supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (both from Thermo Fisher Scientific). Ba/F3 cells were cultured in RPMI 1640 medium (Thermo Fisher Scientific) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum and mouse interleukin (IL)-3 (20 U/mL; Sigma-Aldrich).

## Establishment of Retroviral Vector
To identify whether potential tyrosine kinase fusions were associated with oncogenic abilities and drug responses, we performed functional analyses involving in vitro assays using retroviral vectors. Full-length wild-type cDNA of human NTRK3 and EGFR genes were inserted into the pCX6 vector. The pCX6 vector was established by inserting random 10-base pair (bp) DNA barcode sequences upstream of the start codon of the genes of interest into the pCX4 vector [ ]. The EGFR L858R mutation was selected in this study. Seven fusion genes (CD74-ROS1, LMNA-NTRK1, STRN-NTRK3, VWC2-EGFR, ICK-KDR, FOXP2-MET, and CEP290-MET) were also cloned into the pCX6 vector. The plasmids encoding fusion genes were constructed by the Gene Synthesis Service (GENEWIZ) (Supplemental Table 2; http://links.lww.com/CORR/B253).

## Preparation of Retrovirus and Gene Transduction Into Cell Lines
Recombinant plasmids and packaging plasmids (Takara Bio) were introduced into human embryonic kidney 293T cells to retrieve recombinant retroviral particles. Next, 3T3 cells were seeded in six-well plates and infected with ecotropic recombinant retroviruses using 4-μg/mL Polybrene (Sigma-Aldrich) for 24 hours. Ba/F3 cells were seeded in retronectin-coated (Takara Bio) six-well plates and infected with retroviruses in RPMI 1640 medium containing 20 U/mL IL-3.

## Focus Formation Assay
To identify whether potential tyrosine kinase fusions were associated with oncogenic abilities, we performed a focus formation assay using 3T3 cells. For the focus formation assay, 3T3 cells expressing various variants were cultured for up to 2 weeks in Dulbecco modified Eagle medium-F12 supplemented with 5% bovine serum albumin. Cell transformation was quantitatively assessed by staining with Giemsa solution. A focus formation assay was performed using 3T3 cells infected with recombinant retroviruses expressing wild-type cDNA (NTRK3 and EGFR) and mutant cDNA (CD74-ROS1, LMNA-NTRK1, STRN-NTRK3, VWC2-EGFR, ICK-KDR, FOXP2-MET, CEP290-MET, and EGFR L858R); green fluorescent protein and empty cells were used as a negative control.

## PrestoBlue Cell Viability Assay
To evaluate the drug sensitivity of STRN3-NTRK3 and VWC2-EGFR fusions, we treated the mixture of Ba/F3 cells that expressed different types of fusions or mutations with targeted kinase-inhibitor drugs (larotrectinib: inhibitor of tropomyosin kinase receptors TrkA, TrkB, and TrkC; osimertinib: third-generation EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor; afatinib: second-generation epidermal growth factor receptor tyrosine kinase inhibitor that is mainly used to treat non–small cell lung cancers that harbor mutations in the EGFR gene; and gefitinib: a first-generation EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor that interrupts signaling through the EGFR in target cells) at various concentrations. Transformed Ba/F3 cells that expressed each mutant were cultivated in 96-well plates (with 100 μL of culture medium per well) in RPMI1640 medium without IL-3, whereas green fluorescent protein, which was used as a negative control, was cultivated with IL-3. Each drug was added at the following concentrations: larotrectinib (0.1 nM to 10 μM), afatinib (0.01 nM to 1 μM), gefitinib (0.1 nM to 10 μM), and osimertinib (0.1 nM to 10 μM). After 72 hours of exposure to these inhibitors, each drug was added to the plates, after which 10 μL of PrestoBlue (Thermo Fisher Scientific) was added to the plates. Fluorescence was measured after 3 hours of incubation (excitation 530 nm, emission of 590 nm). The fluorescence intensities of wells without cells were used as negative controls, and survival data were graphically analyzed using GraphPad Prism software program version 7.0 for Windows (GraphPad Software Inc). The half-maximal inhibitory concentration was defined as the inflection point on a dose-response curve.

## Immunohistochemistry
To visualize in situ protein expression on biological sections, we used the relationship between antibodies and antigens to perform immunohistochemistry staining as described [ ] for tumors obtained from five patients with in-frame tyrosine kinase fusions identified by the TruSight Tumor 170 assay and validated via RT-PCR using antibodies (Supplemental Table 3; http://links.lww.com/CORR/B254). Of the 82 patients, seven had in-frame tyrosine kinase fusions. Two of these patients had oncogenic known fusions (LMNA-NTRK1 and CTLC-ALK), so we analyzed only five patients.

## Ethical Approval
The ethics committees of Juntendo University (number 2017185) approved the study protocol. Written informed consent was obtained from all patients. All procedures involving human participants were performed in accordance with the ethical standards of the Institutional Research Committee and the 1964 Declaration of Helsinki and its later amendments or comparable ethical standards.

## Results

## DNA- and RNA-based Analysis Reveals Characteristic Gene Alterations
We detected several genetic alterations in bone and soft tissue sarcomas by DNA- and RNA-based analyses. Among the 82 bone and soft tissue sarcoma tumors we analyzed via the TruSight Tumor 170 assay, genes were altered in at least 2% (two of 82) of tumors (Fig. ). Among the common mutations, TP53 mutations were identified in 12% (10 of 82) of patients. Copy number amplifications in MDM2 and CDK4 were detected in 10% (eight of 82) and 9% (seven of 82) of these tumors, respectively. Interestingly, nine of 10 TP53 alterations were detected in the tumor-specific, fusion-negative group, and TP53 alterations were mutually exclusive to MDM2 and CDK4 amplification. Androgen receptor gene deletion was detected in 6% (five of 82), and all patients had tumor-specific, fusion-negative sarcomas. In the tumor-specific, fusion-negative groups, there were similarly trends (Supplemental Fig. 1; http://links.lww.com/CORR/B255). TP53 mutations were identified in 16% (nine of 58), whereas MDM2 and CDK4 amplification was detected in 14% (eight of 58) and 10% (six of 58), respectively. CDK4 amplification was always accompanied by the amplification of MDM2. However, the tumor-specific, fusion-positive group showed a different trend; only one patient each had TP53 mutation and CDK4 amplification (Supplemental Fig. 2; http://links.lww.com/CORR/B256). The TruSight Tumor 170 assay included 55 genes corresponding to fusions and splice variants; the assay detected fusions in 24% (20 of 82) of patients (Supplemental Table 4; http://links.lww.com/CORR/B257). Seven of 20 patients, including those with EWSR1-DDIT3 in myxoid liposarcoma, were in the tumor-specific, fusion-positive group. The remaining 13 patients were in the tumor-specific, fusion-negative group. Initially, 71% (58 of 82) of patients were classified into the tumor-specific, fusion-negative group. In this group, fusion genes were detected by the TruSight Tumor 170 assay in 22% (13 of 58) (Supplemental Table 4; http://links.lww.com/CORR/B257).
 

## Useful Potential Tyrosine Kinase Fusions Detected in Tumors From Tumor-specific, Fusion-negative Sarcomas
We detected several useful potential tyrosine kinase fusions in tumor-specific, fusion-negative sarcomas using an RNA-based screening system (Fig. 2A). The fusions detected in the 13 tumor-specific, fusion-negative sarcomas showing fusions were EWSR1-RASL10A, FLI1-VAV2, KMT2C-EWSR1, NOTCH2-SEC22B, STRN3-NTRK3, BRF2-FGFR1, FOXP2-MET, TACC1-CDK4, COL27A1-ESR1, VWC2-EGFR, ICK-KDR, SCFD2-PDGFRA, EWSR1-BRMS1L, RAB3IP-PDGFRA, NOTCH3-HFM1, HFM1-ETV1, ETS1-DLEU1, ETS1-SLC5A7, CEP290-MET, MET-POU2F, MLLT3/IFT74, UCK2/MET, and LMNA-NTRK1.
The fusions detected via the TruSight Tumor 170 assay were confirmed using independent RT-PCR analysis. In the tumor-specific, fusion-positive group, RT-PCR was performed to confirm the pathologic diagnosis of fusion genes that were not included in the TruSight Tumor 170 panel, such as FUS-DDIT3, COL1A1-PDGFB, and SS18-SSX1, which are fusions in myxoid liposarcomas, dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans, and synovial sarcoma. However, several fusion genes were undetected via RT-PCR (Supplemental Table 4; http://links.lww.com/CORR/B257). For example, HFM1-ETV1 was not confirmed via RT-PCR in either small round cell sarcoma or epithelioid hemangioendothelioma, although EWSR1-ETV1 fusion was detected in a small subset of patients with Ewing sarcomas [ ]. Thus, among the 13 patients in the tumor-specific, fusion-negative group in whom fusion genes were identified by the TruSight Tumor 170 assay, fusion genes were confirmed to be present in 11 via RT-PCR. Seven of these were novel tyrosine kinase fusion genes identified by RT-PCR. Nine of 11 patients carried tyrosine kinase fusion genes (Fig. B), and five of nine patients harbored in-frame tyrosine kinase fusion genes (STRN3-NTRK3, VWC2-EGFR, ICK-KDR, FOXP2-MET, and CEP290-MET) with unknown pathologic significance (Fig. ). The oncogenicity of LMNA-NTRK1 fusion has been shown [ , ].
 

## NTRK Gene Fusions in Bone Sarcoma Transform Cells and Respond to Targeted Drug Treatment in In Vitro Assays
Tumors with the NTRK fusion gene are potential targets for drug treatment. A tumor with STRN3-NTRK3 fusion was found to originate in the tibia of one of the patients who was treated in this study (Fig. A). Histologically, this tumor was diagnosed as leiomyosarcoma of bone, and immunohistochemistry revealed that the tumor cells were diffusely positive for Pan-Trk immunohistochemistry (Fig. B and C). This patient did not receive any chemotherapy after surgery. The patient is alive and remains without any evidence of recurrence or metastasis. In the other four patients with in-frame tyrosine kinase fusion genes with unknown pathologic significance, FOXP2-MET fusion was detected in a patient with high-grade myxofibrosarcoma (Fig. D and E), CEP290-MET was detected in a patient with high-grade sarcoma not otherwise specified (Fig. F and G), VWC2-EGFR was detected in a patient with a malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumor (Fig. H and I), and intestinal cell kinase-kinase insert domain receptor (ICK-KDR) was detected in a patient with undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma (Fig. J and K). Overexpression of C-MET was also observed in both patients with MET fusion but not detected in the other two patients. EGFR expression was detected in all four patients, wherein the staining intensity in the patient with EGFR fusion was not markedly different from that in the other patients. KDR expression was observed only in a patient undergoing KDR fusion, but ICK-KDR-transfected cells did not form accumulated foci. Accumulated foci were observed in CD74-ROS1-, LMNA-NTRK1-, STRN3-NTRK3-, and EGFR-L858R-transfected cells, indicating that a strong transforming potential was associated with these rearrangements compared with the negative control and wild type (Fig. A). The change in VWC2-EGFR-transfected cells was almost the same as that in the wild type, and the changes in ICK-KDR-, FOXP2-MET-, and CEP290-MET-transfected cells were similar to those in negative controls (Supplemental Fig. 3; http://links.lww.com/CORR/B258).
 LMNA-NTRK1 and STRN3-NTRK3 were sensitive to larotrectinib, whereas EGFR-L858R was sensitive to osimertinib, afatinib, and gefitinib (Fig. B). VWC2-EGFR was resistant to these drugs (Supplemental Fig. 3C; http://links.lww.com/CORR/B258). The half-maximal inhibitory concentration values of Ba/F3 cells expressing STRN3-NTRK3 (12 nM) and LMNA-NTRK1 (21 nM) indicated that the STRN3-NTRK3 fusion gene exhibited oncogenic abilities, while larotrectinib exhibited antitumor activity against STRN3-NTRK3 fusion cells.

## Identified NTRK and ALK Fusion Genes in Sarcomas Can Be Useful as Therapeutic Targets
Based on these genomic profiles, clinical trials that involved treating two pediatric patients harboring NTRK fusion and ALK fusion with tyrosine kinase inhibitors were conducted [ , ]. LMNA-NTRK1 fusion was detected in one patient who had undergone resection of a low-grade sarcoma in the right brachialis (Patient 82; Supplemental Table 4; http://links.lww.com/CORR/B257). When local recurrence was confirmed, larotrectinib was initiated, because a reoperation would likely cause disability of the affected arm. A complete response was achieved after 3 months. After dosing for 6 months, we performed local resection, which pathologically confirmed that complete remission had occurred [ ]. The other patient had an inflammatory myofibroblastic tumor in the bile duct and celiac artery (Patient 67; Supplemental Table 4; http://links.lww.com/CORR/B257). CLTC-ALK fusion was performed in this patient, which has been reported to recurrently occur in inflammatory myofibroblastic tumors [ ]. Crizotinib, an ALK inhibitor, was orally administered for 2 years to induce a complete tumor response, and the patient was progression-free for 2 years after treatment was terminated.

## Discussion
Bone and soft tissue sarcomas are still characterized by poor prognosis, and effective systemic therapies have not been established. Therefore, novel therapeutic targets and effective therapies for bone and soft tissue sarcomas must be developed. Approximately 80% of tumor-specific fusion-negative sarcomas remain genetically uncharacterized. This mutually exclusive pattern of mutations suggests that other mutually exclusive driver oncogenes are yet to be discovered. DNA-based next-generation sequencing assays have several limitations when used for detecting targetable fusion genes; however, the RNA capture method has a marked advantage in detecting multiple types of fusion genes. Therefore, in this study, we conducted DNA- and RNA-based screening focusing on useful and novel tyrosine kinase fusion genes, specifically fusion-negative sarcomas. We successfully identified several tyrosine kinase fusion genes, particularly an STRN3-NTRK3 fusion gene in leiomyosarcoma of bone, where STRN3-NTRK3 rearrangement revealed a strong transforming potential and was inhibited by larotrectinib, despite a study reporting nonfunctional NTRK gene fusions in bone sarcomas [ ]. Therefore, comprehensive genome profiling should be considered even if the tumor is a bone tumor, and an RNA-based screening system may be useful for detecting tyrosine kinase fusion genes in specific fusion-negative sarcomas.

## Limitations
RNA sequencing revealed a higher frequency of fusion genes than expected in specific fusion-negative sarcoma groups; however, some of these fusion genes contained out-of-frame fusions. Some of the out-of-frame fusion tumors were because of sequencing errors, while others were confirmed through RT-PCR. Whether the lack of a kinase domain in these fusions affects tumorigenesis or whether they are simply passenger mutations is currently unknown, and further studies are needed to clarify the nature of these fusions.
Tyrosine kinase fusions are considered oncogenic drivers; however, chromosomal instability-associated tumors, including sarcomas, may induce chromothripsis, resulting in random gene fusions. We recommend that clinicians use this approach to specifically examine fusion transcripts for their functional impact before considering molecular-targeted therapy using tyrosine kinase inhibitors.
The TruSight Tumor 170 panel is not specific for sarcomas but targets common therapeutic fusions in all tumor types. Rare fusions in sarcomas, especially those with a large extent, were not covered by this panel. The disclaimer for this program is “for research only.” Our study did not validate the procedure or the bioinformatics pipeline. Copy number variant values were not exactly evaluated in the context of the tumor cell percentage.
The expression of recombinant proteins was not evaluated in vitro. Instead, we analyzed immunohistochemistry results to confirm the expression of proteins in actual tissue samples that were obtained from patients with sarcomas.

## DNA- and RNA-based Analysis Reveals Characteristic Gene Alterations
DNA- and RNA-based analyses detected characteristic alterations, copy number alterations, and fusions in bone and soft tissue sarcomas. RNA-based analyses detected fusion genes in 24% (20 of 82) of patients. The TruSight Tumor 170 assay showed that of these 20 patients, 13 were in the tumor-specific, fusion-negative group; that is, fusion genes were detected in 22% (13 of 58) of patients in this group. RNA sequencing allowed us to identify more fusion genes in the tumor-specific, fusion-negative group than we expected. Additionally, in this study, RNA analysis identified several unknown fusion genes and conventional fusion genes that might be linked to diagnosis and treatment. We believe that adding an RNA analysis to DNA analysis in the comprehensive genome profiling test may inform the development of therapeutics that can improve clinical outcomes for patients who have bone and soft tissue sarcomas.
Consistent with previous reports on soft tissue sarcomas [ , ], our DNA-based analyses detected commonly affected TP53 alterations and copy number alterations of MDM2 and CDK4 [ ]. The androgen receptor gene deletion, which has been reported in liposarcoma, leiomyosarcoma, and osteosarcoma [ ], was detected in 6% (5 of 82) of patients in our study, all of whom had tumor-specific, fusion-negative sarcomas. These single nucleotide variants and copy number variations were enriched in specific fusion-negative sarcomas. DNA- and RNA-based analysis is used to increase the detection rate of fusion genes and accurately diagnose sarcomas [ , ]. The functional significance of this novel fusion gene is not completely understood. In the future, as data from RNA analysis accumulate and each fusion gene is elucidated, there may be further improvements in the treatment of sarcomas.

## Useful Potential Tyrosine Kinase Fusions Detected in Tumors From Tumor-specific, Fusion-negative Sarcomas
We investigated whether using an RNA-based screening system would aid in identifying tyrosine kinase fusions, especially in tumor-specific, fusion-negative sarcomas. We detected fusion genes in 19% of the tumor-specific, fusion-negative group; however, actual in-frame fusion genes were detected in only six patients in this group. All six had tyrosine kinase fusions, including an apparent therapeutic target, LMNA-NTRK1 fusion [ , ]. The remaining five patients had in-frame tyrosine kinase fusion genes (STRN3-NTRK3, VWC2-EGFR, ICK-KDR, FOXP2-MET, and CEP290-MET fusions) of unknown pathologic significance. The STRN3-NTRK3 fusion gene was reported in a patient with fibrosarcoma [ ], in whom the tumor was diagnosed as leiomyosarcoma of bone. We examined the diagnosis and found that the myogenic markers were very well stained and consistent with the diagnosis of leiomyosarcoma of bone. Based on these previous studies and our current findings, we suggest that RNA-based screening can detect tyrosine kinase fusions in tumor-specific, fusion-negative sarcomas [ ]. Only a few tyrosine kinase fusion genes have been reported in soft tissue sarcomas [ ]. DNA-based next-generation sequencing assays with large gene panels have been unable to detect driver gene fusions or oncogenic isoforms perfectly compared with alterations identified via RNA-based approaches. Gene fusions, which usually arise from interchromosomal or intrachromosomal ligation to distinct introns, are difficult to detect via DNA sequence capture and next-generation sequencing, especially when the corresponding introns are large or contain repetitive sequences, as seen in NTRK and ROS1 [ , ]. In addition, various breakpoints and fusion partners may pose unique barriers to DNA-based genotyping. By contrast, RNA sequencing is not influenced by large introns and is superior to DNA sequencing in identifying NTRK fusion genes [ ]. Because of these issues, previous studies have tended to overlook oncogenic fusion genes, including tyrosine kinase fusions, in specific fusion-negative sarcomas [ , ]. Currently, DNA-based analysis is the mainstay of genetic testing; however, as suggested, RNA sequencing should be performed in the future, especially for tumor-specific, fusion-negative sarcomas. As testing for RNA analysis becomes more widespread in patients with bone and soft tissue sarcomas, tyrosine kinase fusion genes that are directly linked to treatment will be detected.

## NTRK Gene Fusions in Bone Sarcoma Transform Cells and Respond to Targeted Drug Treatment in In Vitro Assays
We identified five in-frame tyrosine kinase fusion genes (STRN3-NTRK3, VWC2-EGFR, ICK-KDR, FOXP2-MET, and CEP290-MET) with unknown pathologic significance. Our in vitro analysis showed the STRN3-NTRK3 fusion gene possessed oncogenic abilities, and larotrectinib exhibited antitumor activity in STRN3-NTRK3 fusion cells. NTRK fusions in malignant tumors are promising therapeutic targets for tyrosine kinase inhibitors, including larotrectinib. We identified an STRN3-NTRK3 fusion in a patient with leiomyosarcoma of bone and elucidated the malignant potential of this fusion gene via functional analyses and drug effects. NTRK fusions were recently reported in three patients with osteosarcomas; however, they were all nonfunctional and required a functional analysis along with a sequence confirmation of the fusion transcript [ ]. A functional analysis of STRN3-NTRK3 has not been performed [ ]; hence, a focus formation assay and drug sensitivity test were performed in our study. Previous studies have focused on NTRK3 rearrangements [ , ]. NTRK fusions generally lead to ligand-independent constitutive kinase activation via dimerization. The same phenomenon was believed to be true in patients with STRN3-NTRK3 fusion. STRN3-NTRK3 may lead to ligand-independent activation of NTRK3 kinase through dimerization that is mediated by the coiled-coil domain of STRN3. A focus formation assay was performed in our study, and a transformant focus was observed in STRN3-NTRK3–transfected cells.
The sensitivity of STRN3-NTRK3 fusions to larotrectinib was examined in vitro, and the results revealed that larotrectinib exhibits antitumor activity in cells with STRN3-NTRK3 fusion. This indicated that the STRN3-NTRK3 fusion gene exhibited oncogenic abilities, while larotrectinib exhibited antitumor activity against STRN3-NTRK3 fusion cells. Thus, identifying STRN3-NTRK3 fusion may prompt treatment with a tyrosine kinase inhibitor. Despite a report that described nonfunctional NTRK gene fusions in bone sarcomas [ ], these assays finally revealed via functional analyses and drug effects that STRN3-NTRK3 fusion in patients with leiomyosarcoma of bone has malignant potential. Thus, comprehensive genome profiling should be considered even if the tumor is a bone tumor. Another four tyrosine kinase fusions, including VWC2-EGFR, ICK-KDR, FOXP2-MET, and CEP290-MET, did not show any clear differences between the focus formation assays of wild-type tyrosine kinase–transfected cells and the negative control.
In patients with VWC2-EGFR fusion tumors, the change in VWC2-EGFR–transfected cells was almost the same as that in the wild-type tumor. Immunohistochemistry analysis revealed that an EGFR fusion tumor showed an intense EGFR expression that was similar to that of EGFR wild-type tumors. We considered EGFR to be oncogenic and performed drug experiments; however, EGFR inhibitors were ineffective. Kinase insert domain receptor fusion tumors showed very weak VEGFR2 expression, although VEGFR2 expression has been observed in synovial sarcoma and chordoma in patients with nonendothelial mesenchymal tumors [ ]. Moreover, PTPRZ1-MET (P1, M2, P2, M2, P3, and M2) fusions were detected in tumors of the central nervous system [ ], which covered almost the entire tyrosine kinase region, similar to the four tyrosine kinase fusions, VWC2-EGFR (V3; E2), ICK-KDR (I5; K2), FOXP2-MET (F2, M12), and CEP290-MET (C5, M12). A previous study showed there was ligand-independent phosphorylation of MET [ ]. Overexpression of C-MET was observed in patients with FOXP2-MET and CEP290-MET. In the patient with MET fusion, similar to that of PTPRZ1-MET, a coiled-coil motif added to the SEMA domain of MET is the key structure involved in ligand binding and dimerization [ ]. The fusion sections in FOXP2-MET and CEP290-MET fusions appear to contain a coiled-coil motif on the SEMA domain, leading to MET hyperactivation in a ligand-independent manner. It is unknown whether all tyrosine kinase fusion genes identified might be linked to therapy. Validation experiments are needed to ensure appropriate treatment. We believe that by reporting and analyzing each identified fusion gene with an unknown function, we will be able to use therapeutic agents with confidence.

## Identified NTRK and ALK Fusion Genes in Sarcomas May Be Useful as Therapeutic Targets
In this study, well-characterized LMNA-NTRK1 and CLTC-ALK fusions were identified in two patients with pediatric sarcomas using the TruSight Tumor 170 assay. These two patients with NTRK and ALK fusions were treated with tyrosine kinase inhibitors as part of a clinical trial, which helped to achieve a complete response. Patients who underwent RNA analysis were linked to appropriate treatment. NTRK was confirmed by RT-PCR. Additionally, ALK was confirmed by immunohistochemical examination in one patient; however, its expression was weak. This therapeutic target might not have been identified by general testing. In addition, DNA-based clinical sequencing failed to detect LMNA-NTRK1 fusion (data not shown). A study reported that tyrosine kinase fusion genes are not properly identified by DNA analyses [ ]. Although DNA-based sequencing tends to overlook fusion genes, current comprehensive gene profile tests that are clinically used worldwide in sarcoma mainly use DNA analysis. Bone and soft tissue sarcomas often contain fusion genes; therefore, adding RNA analysis in order to identify these genes is recommended.

## Conclusion
In this study, we conducted DNA- and RNA-based screening to identify useful and potential therapeutic targets including tyrosine kinase fusions in bone and soft tissue sarcomas. Furthermore, we successfully identified STRN3-NTRK3 fusion in a patient with leiomyosarcoma of bone and elucidated the malignant potential of this fusion gene via functional analyses and drug effects. The RNA-based screening system might detect tyrosine fusion genes in specific fusion-negative sarcomas and might identify therapeutic targets, leading to potential breakthroughs in the treatment of bone and soft tissue sarcomas. Thus, comprehensive genome profiling should be considered even if the tumor is a bone tumor. There seems to be some limitations regarding current DNA-based comprehensive genome profiling tests, and it is important to use RNA testing for proper diagnosis and accurate identification of fusion genes. Studies with more patients, validation of results, and further functional analysis of unknown tyrosine kinase fusion genes are required to establish future treatments.
