## Abstract

## Purpose:
Intratumoral injection of Clostridium novyi-nontoxic (C. novyi-NT), lacking alpha-toxin, germinates and subsequently replicates in the tumor hypoxic regions, causing cell lysis and inflammation. This phase Ib study investigated the safety and synergistic effects of pembrolizumab and C. novyi-NT in advanced solid tumors.

## Patients and Methods:
Patients with injectable solid tumors received one intratumoral C. novyi-NT injection (day 8) across four dose cohorts (3 × 104 to 100 × 104 spores) with pembrolizumab (200 mg intravenously every 3 weeks, day 0–24 months). Sixteen patients were enrolled with a median age of 62.5 years, and the most common tumor types were breast cancer and melanoma.

## Results:
The combination therapy demonstrated a manageable safety profile, with one grade 3 dose-limiting toxicity of abscess formation and the remainder of treatment-related adverse events in ≥10% of patients being grade 1 or 2 injection site reaction (25%), pyrexia (19%), pruritus (13%), leukopenia (13%), and anemia (13%). The maximum tolerated dose was determined to be pembrolizumab intravenously at 200 mg every 3 weeks and a single dose of 100 × 104C.novyi-NT spores intratumorally on day 8. The confirmed overall objective response rate of 25% was observed among four patients with nonkeratinizing undifferentiated nasopharyngeal squamous carcinoma, human papilloma virus–positive squamous cell carcinoma of the base of the tongue, vulvar melanoma, and chordoma. Among these patients, we obtained three partial responses and one complete response. The median duration of the response was 10.93 months, and stable disease was observed in 69% of patients.

## Conclusions:
This study highlights the potential of the C. novyi-NT and pembrolizumab combination as a promising therapeutic strategy for treatment-refractory solid tumors.

## Translational Relevance
The translational significance of intratumoral Clostridium novyi-nontoxic (C. novyi-NT) is underscored by its targeted action within hypoxic tumor regions, in which it triggers tumor cell lysis and tumor inflammation. This study evaluated the combination of C. novyi-NT spores and pembrolizumab in patients with advanced solid tumors, focusing on the synergy between oncolytic bacterial therapy and immune checkpoint inhibition. The combination of C. novyi-NT and pembrolizumab demonstrates the potential for sustained T-cell cytokine release and immune memory, with long-lasting antitumor effects, as evidenced by the continued elevation of interferon gamma (IFNγ), tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNFα), and granzyme B levels up to 336 days after therapy in one patient. The combination promoted increased tumor antigen response and enhanced immune infiltration into the tumor microenvironment. Notably, pembrolizumab’s checkpoint inhibition amplified C. novyi-NT–induced tumor lysis, increasing neoantigen presentation and T cell–mediated immune responses. This study demonstrates translational potential in improving immunotherapeutic efficacy in hypoxic, immune-resistant tumors, laying the groundwork for larger trials in immunotherapy-refractory cancers.

## Introduction
One of the biggest challenges in cancer treatment is the effective delivery of therapeutic agents to all areas of the tumor, including poorly perfused regions. Hypoxic regions within tumors have been associated with more aggressive tumor growth, an increased risk of metastasis, and resistance to chemotherapy and radiotherapy ( , ). Hypoxia can alter the expression of genes and proteins involved in tumor progression and response to treatment ( ). To address this challenge, researchers have been exploring various strategies to improve the delivery of anticancer agents to hypoxic regions of tumors. Clostridium novyi is an obligate anaerobe in which spores colonize and replicate exclusively within the hypoxic regions of tumors ( ). One novel approach is intratumoral therapy with oncolytic bacteria using the Clostridium species. As early as 1947, Parker and colleagues ( ) demonstrated the potential of Clostridium histolyticum as an experimental cancer therapy by injecting the bacteria into sarcoma mouse models, leading to tumor regression and extended survival of the animals. Later, Carey and colleagues ( ) investigated the potential of Clostridium butyricum by injecting it into five patients with advanced solid tumors. Upon the intravenous administration of 1010C. butyricum M55 spores, antitumor activity became evident in three patients, primarily within the largest tumors, with no discernible effect on adjacent tissues or smaller metastatic lesions. Notably, in one instance, a transient clinical improvement was attributed to the oncolytic actions induced by Clostridium ( ). In 1978, Heppner and Mosë extended this investigation, employing intracarotid injection of the same spores in patients diagnosed with vascular glioblastomas. Most patients exhibited full oncolysis within a week of therapy, resulting in the transformation of glioblastoma into a cerebral abscess. Subsequently, surgical intervention was performed to avert the risk of hemorrhage and fatality ( ). Although safety and efficacy were demonstrated in some patients, these studies were not well designed or performed and lacked a control group, making it difficult to draw definitive conclusions.
More recently, with the advent of immunotherapy, there is renewed interest in developing oncolytic bacterial therapy. C. novyi-nontoxic (C. novyi-NT) is an obligate anaerobic, spore-forming gram-positive bacterium lacking the alpha-toxin with the ability to induce immune-mediated anticancer responses after either intravenous or intratumoral injection into hypoxic areas of the tumor, leading to immunogenic cell death ( , ). The alpha-toxin is a glycosyltransferase that can inactivate various members of the Rho and Ras subfamily of GTP-binding proteins. It causes morphologic changes to all cell types, especially endothelial cells, by inhibiting signal transduction pathways, resulting in cytoskeletal breakdown, leading to cell attachment and leakage from the capillaries. The removal of the alpha-toxin is vital in improving the safety profile of C. novyi-NT ( ). C. novyi-NT underwent assessment in a first-in-human, phase I trial published by Janku and colleagues ( ). Among the 24 enrolled patients, instillation of a singular intratumoral C. novyi-NT spore injection prompted germination in 42% (n = 10) of the patients, establishing the maximum tolerated dose (MTD) at 1 × 106 spores with manageable adverse events (AE). Encouragingly, initial signs of clinical efficacy emerged with 41% of response-evaluable patients displaying a reduction in injected lesions and 86% of evaluable patients exhibiting stable disease as the systemic response. In this study, C. novyi-NT–induced tumor lysis correlated with escalated infiltration of T cells and other myeloid cell populations, which are important in the augmentation of tumor-specific T-cell responses ( ).
Optimal functioning of checkpoint inhibitors relies upon adequate lymphocytic infiltrate, a condition not universally met within all tumors ( ). The modifications induced within the local tumor microenvironment (TME) subsequent to the introduction of oncolytic microbes offer a strategic opportunity for the synergistic deployment of oncolytic microbes alongside checkpoint inhibitors. Preclinical studies suggest that combining the intratumoral administration of oncolytic viruses or bacteria with checkpoint inhibitors can augment anticancer therapeutic efficacy. For example, the combination of intratumoral administration of paramyxovirus (the virus that causes Newcastle disease) and the systemic anti-cytotoxic T lymphocyte–associated antigen 4 antibody ipilimumab led to profound immune-mediated responses in colon cancer and melanoma in vivo models ( ). Similar observations have been reported from in vivo experiments with intravenous Coxsackievirus A21 and systemic PD-1 antibody in melanoma models ( ). Clinically, a phase I study combining intratumoral talimogene laherparepvec and the systemic anti-cytotoxic T lymphocyte–associated antigen 4 antibody ipilimumab in patients with advanced melanoma demonstrated encouraging activity with an objective response rate (ORR) of 50%, which is much higher than expected with either drug alone ( ). Additionally, in a phase Ib study of intratumoral Coxsackievirus A21 and systemic pembrolizumab in patients with advanced melanoma, the ORR was 47% ( ).
We hypothesized that the intratumoral administration of C. novyi-NT in combination with intravenous pembrolizumab could synergistically enhance anticancer efficacy by potentiating and sustaining tumor-localized cell lysis, coupled with a robust host immune and inflammatory response culminating in immunogenic cell death. In this study, we present the safety and efficacy results of an open-label, single-center phase Ib study combining pembrolizumab with intratumoral C. novyi-NT injection in patients with advanced solid tumors (NCT03435952).

## Patients and Methods

## Patients
Enrolled subjects were nonrandomized, ≥18 years of age, of male and female sex, who had a histologic diagnosis of advanced or metastatic solid tumor and had a tumor amenable to percutaneous C. novyi-NT spore injection, either by palpation or radiographic imaging. Injectable tumors must have had a longitudinal diameter ≥1 cm and ≤12 cm without bone involvement at the injection site and could not be lesions in the thoracic, abdominal, or pelvic cavity. A cap of 12 cm for tumor size was instituted based on clinical data showing that the larger size of the injected tumor was a prominent determinant of C. novyi-NT germination and grade >3 AEs when given intratumorally in the phase I monotherapy study ( ). Key inclusion criteria included an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status ≤1 and sufficient bone marrow, renal, hepatic, and cardiac function. Key exclusion criteria included any condition impeding the clearance of C. novyi-NT infection, such as asplenia, concurrent immunosuppressive therapy, or multiple antibiotic sensitivities/allergies. Notably, the exclusion of patients with antibiotic sensitivities/allergies was required for patient safety, as it was important not to preclude treatment should C. novyi-NT infection occur. An antibiotic sensitivity profile of C. novyi-NT was provided to the clinical site by the sponsor.

## Study design and treatment
Eligible patients were enrolled at the University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center in a sequential manner across four escalating dosing cohorts for C. novyi-NT, employing a 3 + 3 dose-escalation design ( ). Pembrolizumab was administered intravenously at a fixed dose of 200 mg every 3 weeks. The primary objective was to characterize the safety profile, dose-limiting toxicities (DLT), and MTD of combined C. novyi-NT and pembrolizumab therapy. Key secondary endpoints included the evaluation of the preliminary antitumor activity within the injected tumor lesion, along with overall systemic response as evaluated by RECIST v1.1 criteria ( ). All enrolled participants finished the study; that is, there was no attrition.
Toxicities were evaluated based on the NCI Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events version 4.03 ( ). DLTs were defined as any grade 3 AE with a minimum duration of 3 days or any grade 4 AE of any duration, deemed possibly linked to C. novyi-NT and manifesting within 4 weeks of administering C. novyi-NT and pembrolizumab. Additionally, DLTs included any C. novyi-NT infection necessitating invasive intervention, such as percutaneous abscess drainage, or leading to extended hospital stays (>5 days). DLT monitoring spanned the initial 6 weeks of therapy. Cycle 1 pembrolizumab was initiated on day 0, whereas a single dose of C. novyi-NT injection took place on cycle 1, day 8. Subsequent cycles of pembrolizumab followed a 21-day schedule. All patients receiving at least one cycle of pembrolizumab and the C. novyi-NT injection were deemed evaluable for DLTs. Notably, cycle 2 of pembrolizumab could not experience a delay exceeding 6 weeks after the C. novyi-NT injection.
The study protocol and amendments were approved by the Institutional Review Board of the University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center. The study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and Good Clinical Practice and met all federal, state, and local regulatory guidelines. Written informed consent was obtained from all patients prior to their enrollment.
The study was funded by Biomed Valley Discoveries, which also provided the C. novyi-NT spores (Omnia Biologics). The C. novyi-NT spores were reconstituted on the day of administration to the patient. Dilution of the concentrated spore suspension was conducted within a designated biological safety cabinet, utilizing sterile saline (0.9%) vials of suitable capacities to achieve the necessary dose aligned with the respective cohorts. An injection volume of 3 mL was drawn from the saline vials and percutaneously administered, with or without radiographic guidance [computed tomography (CT) or ultrasound], utilizing a 21- to 25-gauge needle. Merck & Co., Inc., provided pembrolizumab for the study, and this was prepared as per institutional guidelines.
Following C. novyi-NT spore injection, patients were admitted to the inpatient service for 8 days to be closely observed for indications of C. novyi-NT spore germination and monitored closely for any AEs. C. novyi-NT spore germination was defined as local tumoral inflammation resulting in tumor lysis, accompanied by a spectrum of clinical manifestations, including fever, injection site discomfort, erythema, swelling, tenderness, and, in certain instances, ulceration, spontaneous drainage, tissue shedding, hemorrhage, and/or malodor. Initiation of antibiotic therapy was guided by the Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score. Clinical management, including the decision to use antibiotics before day 8, was guided by an algorithm that incorporated the SOFA tool, with scores above 8 predicting mortality rates averaging 55% (range, 26%–95%) and scores below this threshold predicting mortality rates averaging 13% (range, 0%–21%; ref. ). On the eighth day, patients were started on oral doxycycline (100 mg twice daily) as a preventive measure against further C. novyi-NT spore germination. However, doxycycline could be stopped after 4 weeks if there were no clinical, laboratory, or radiological signs of infection. After discharge, patients underwent outpatient evaluation twice weekly for 3 weeks. Thereafter, clinic visits were every 3 weeks from week 6 to week 48.

## Anaerobic blood culture
Patient whole blood samples were collected for anaerobic culture just prior to C. novyi-NT treatment, then 12 hours after the dose, and daily from days 9 to 15 and on days 21, 28, and 35 after the dose. The samples were sent to Microbiology Specialists Inc. for analysis using the VersaTREK system.

## Assessment of tumor response
Baseline radiographic imaging was performed using a CT scan or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) within 4 weeks of starting treatment. The same imaging techniques were used for subsequent tumor response assessments. Tumor measurements were taken at baseline before treatment and at the end of every two cycles until confirmed disease progression or patient removal from the study. Additional radiographic imaging was obtained if clinically indicated. The response of measurable target lesions was assessed using RECIST v1.1 ( ).

## Assessment of inflammatory and immunologic characteristics

## Cytokine responses
Venous blood was drawn from the patients at baseline and various time points after C. novyi-NT treatment. Serum protein levels were analyzed using the Ciraplex Assay (Quanterix BioSystems) and the SomaScan Assay (SomaLogic). The Quanterix Ciraplex system, a multiplexed, sandwich-type enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA), was used to quantify the concentration of a panel of 33 cytokines in serum samples collected from the first nine patients in the study. Following a shift in Quanterix’s business strategy, samples from the remaining patients were analyzed using the SomaScan v4.1 multiplex proteomic assay (SomaLogic), a high-throughput platform based on aptamers that enables the simultaneous quantification of multiple protein analytes. To ensure comparability between the datasets generated from the two different systems, all cytokine concentrations were normalized to each patient’s baseline values and expressed as a percentage increase relative to baseline ( , ). The percentage difference between baseline and days 1, 3, 5, and 7 after C. novyi-NT treatment for each protein was calculated. Relative differences of all proteins were compared across patients. Results are presented as bar plots showing group means and SD. A t test was performed to compare the difference between the cytokine increase in the responders and nonresponders.

## Systemic tumor antigen–specific T-cell responses
The protocols used for the analysis of patient T-cell responses to tumor antigens were as reported by us earlier ( ). Tumor biopsy collections were mandatory before the dose, at week 12, and at week 24. A biopsy at week 6 was optional. Venous blood samples were collected from patients at baseline (day 0) and after the C.novyi-NT injection at day 8 at multiple time points. Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC) were isolated by density gradient centrifugation as described previously ( ). Tumor cell lines that matched the cancer of each patient were used to prepare cell extracts, which then served as sources of allogenic tumor antigens. Pre- and posttreatment PBMCs from each patient were plated at 0.5–1 × 106 cells per well and incubated at 37°C in a 5% CO2 atmosphere with cytomegalovirus, Epstein–Barr virus, and influenza virus peptide pools (control peptide pool) and cytotoxic T lymphocytes (positive control) or PBMC lysate at a final concentration of 10 μg/mL (negative control). The concentration of tumor antigen–specific T cells within PBMCs was determined by ImmunoSpot, which is a fluorospot adaptation of the enzyme-linked immunosorbent spot assay. Using this approach, we measured the release of three T-cell cytokines: IFNγ, TNFα, and granzyme B by each cell in the well ( ). The T-cell concentration was indirectly calculated from the enzyme-linked immunosorbent spot signals by assessing the concentration of the three cytokines. A positive response for T-cell activation was defined as a T-cell concentration that was detectable (i.e., >1:100,000) and significantly (P < 0.05, two-tailed t test; Microsoft Excel, RRID: SCR_016137) greater than the mean T-cell concentration of the control no-antigen wells. Differences in the pre- and posttreatment concentration of tumor antigen–specific T cells were analyzed with the paired Student t test (Microsoft Excel, RRID: SCR_016137), comparing values with day 0.

## Local tumor-specific T-cell responses
Core needle biopsy specimens of the injected tumor and one noninjected lesion were collected at baseline (day 0) and multiple time points after C.novyi-NT administration. Formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded biopsy tissue blocks were sectioned, and 5 μm sections were subjected to immunohistochemistry (IHC) staining using antibodies against human cluster of differentiation (CD)3 (Thermo Scientific Pierce, MA1-90582, RRID: AB_1956722), CD8 (Abcam, ab4055, RRID: AB_339774), and forkhead box P3 (Foxp3; Abcam, ab20034, RRID: AB_445284). Images were captured using a Leica inverted microscope. The concentration of stained cells in 200× fields of view was calculated using QuPath.

## Statistical analysis
Baseline patient characteristics, treatment-related AEs (TRAE), and response rates were summarized using descriptive statistics. This was an open-label, unblinded, nonrandomized study. Overall survival (OS) was defined as the time from treatment start to death or the last follow-up. Progression-free survival (PFS) was defined as the time from treatment start to progression or death, whichever occurred first, or the last follow-up. The distributions of OS and PFS were estimated by the Kaplan–Meier method ( ). All analyses were performed in SAS 9.4 and R 4.3.1 (GraphPad Prism, RRID: SCR_002798).

## Results

## Demographics and disease characteristics
Between July 2018 and September 2022, 16 patients with advanced refractory solid tumors were enrolled in four dose levels of a single dose of intratumoral injection of C. novyi-NT spores and fixed dosing of pembrolizumab (Supplementary Table S1). Eligibility criteria are summarized in the Patients and Methods section and detailed in the protocol (Supplementary File S1). The median patient age was 62.5 years (range, 40–71 years), in which predominant tumor types included breast cancer (n = 5; 31%) and melanoma (n = 3; 19%). Eleven patients (69%) were female, and all patients (100%) had an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status of 1. Eight patients (50%) had received prior immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) therapy, and five patients (31%) had >5 lines of prior therapies. Patient baseline demographics and disease characteristics are shown in . Representativeness of study participants is denoted in Supplementary Table S2. Microsatellite instability status, tumor mutational burden, and prior immunotherapy exposure at the individual patient level are provided in Supplementary Table S3.
 The median number of treatment cycles (each lasting 21 days) completed by all patients was 5 (range, 1–34). Fifteen patients (94%) received more than two cycles. For patients showing stable disease or better, the median number of completed cycles was 6 (range, 1–34). At the time of data cut (June 30, 2023), all patients were off study. Twelve patients (75%) experienced progression; one withdrew consent due to financial burden from trial commitments; one completed the study with 2 years of pembrolizumab therapy; one was lost to follow-up; and one came off trial due to toxicity (grade 2 immune-related dermatitis).

## Safety evaluation
All 16 patients enrolled across four dose levels were evaluable for safety and DLTs. Ten patients (63%) experienced at least one grade 1 or 2 TRAE, with only one patient experiencing a DLT of grade 3 abscess formation at the intratumoral injection site ( ). The most prevalent grade 1 and 2 TRAEs were injection site reaction (25%), pyrexia (19%), pruritus (13%), leukopenia (13%), and anemia (13%). AEs specifically related to C. novyi-NT included four patients with injection site reaction from C. novyi-NT germination. Signs and symptoms of C. novyi-NT germination (infection) included fever, injection site pain, erythema, swelling, and, in some cases, ulceration, spontaneous drainage, tissue sloughing, bleeding, and malodor. Serious AEs (SAE) were reported in six patients, with one serious TRAE in dose level 4 linked to C. novyi-NT secondary to grade 3 abscess formation at the injection site, requiring hospitalization and medical intervention, which led to the only DLT observed in the study. Other nontreatment-related SAEs included one patient who developed grade 3 dyspnea secondary to progression, requiring hospitalization; another with grade 3 bleeding from the injected tumor site secondary to progression; and one patient who developed grade 3 aspiration pneumonia and sepsis, requiring hospitalization. One patient developed grade 3 dyspnea secondary to malignant pleural effusion due to progression, leading to hospitalization, and another patient developed grade 3 arthralgia unrelated to therapy, requiring hospitalization. Notably, only one patient discontinued the trial due to a TRAE secondary to grade 2 immune-related dermatitis from pembrolizumab, refractory to UV phototherapy and a topical steroid regimen.
 

## Efficacy
All 16 patients enrolled across four dose levels were evaluable for efficacy (Supplementary Table S4). A waterfall response plot depicting the best overall RECIST v1.1 response and best response in the injected lesion across the four dose levels and tumor histologies is depicted in . Among the 16 patients, there were 3 (19%) partial responses (PR) and 1 (6%) complete response (CR). The confirmed ORR was 25%. The CR was seen in an ICI-refractory patient (MDA017) with vulvar mucosal melanoma with a duration of response of 14.8 months. She ultimately came off the study due to progressive disease. The PRs include an ICI-naïve patient (MDA014) with nasopharyngeal carcinoma who participated for 24 months in the trial with the best response of PR (−87%) in the target lesions and a duration of response of 18.1 months. Another patient (MDA019) with ICI-naïve chordoma experienced confirmed PR in the injected lesion (−40%) and confirmed PR in the target lesions (−37%) with a duration of response of 7.06 months and later discontinued therapy secondary to grade 2 immune-related dermatitis refractory to UV therapy and topical steroid therapy. The third PR was seen in a patient (MDA010) with human papilloma virus (HPV)–positive squamous cell carcinoma of the base of the tongue who had a confirmed PR in the target lesions (−33%) with a duration of response lasting 2.8 months and subsequently came off study after withdrawing consent due to the cumulative financial burden from trial commitment. Among the responders, the median duration of response was 10.93 months (2.8–18.1 months), and one patient (25%) had prior ICI exposure.
 The median injected tumor size for all patients was 2.65 cm (range, 1–11) with the confirmed ORR in injected lesions at 19% with two PRs and one CR. At the time of data cutoff on June 30, 2023, five patients (31%) remained alive, whereas nine patients (56%) had died, and two patients had unknown survival status with no further patients remaining in the trial. Among the responders, two patients had elevated C-reactive protein, whereas one patient had leukocytosis; however, none of the responders had evidence of germination or sepsis. A swimmer plot depicting the duration of response among the 16 participants is shown in .
Notably, 11 patients (69%) had stable disease in the target lesions, whereas nine patients (56%) had stable disease in the injected lesions as their best response. One patient (6%) experienced clinical progression (MDA027) in the target lesions, whereas four patients (25%; MDA009, MDA013, MDA022, MDA027) developed progressive disease (PD) in the injected lesions as their best response.
The median OS was 13.0 months [95% confidence interval (CI), 7.9–not reached]. The median OS rate at 3 months was 93.8%. The median OS rate at 6 months was 87.1%. The median OS rate at 12 months was 53.4%. The median PFS was 3.6 months (95% CI, 2.1–8.2). The median PFS rate at 2 months was 81.2%. The median PFS rate at 3 months was 56.2%. The median PFS rate at 4 months was 43.8%. Kaplan–Meier curves for the median OS and median PFS are shown in .

## Host immune and inflammatory response to C. novyi-NT

## Cytokine responses
The percentage difference of various proteins between baseline and days 1, 3, 5, and 7 after C. novyi-NT treatment was calculated ( ). Due to the small number of responding patients, the analysis did not show any statistical difference between the responders and nonresponders for interleukin (IL)-10, IL-15, IL-18, TNFα, and tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinase at all time points. A similar observation was made for the IFN family members. Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF)-C, which plays a crucial role in tumor lymphangiogenesis and lymph node metastasis, showed a slightly increasing but statistically insignificant trend for days 3, 5, and 7. VEGF-A is an endothelial growth factor that interacts with vascular endothelial growth factor receptor (VEGFR)-1 and VEGFR-2. It is the only protein in this analysis that showed a significant upregulation in the responding patients (P < 0.05) on day 3 after C. novyi-NT treatment.
 Tumor antigen–stimulated release of T-cell IFNγ, TNFα, and granzyme B was simultaneously measured following the pulse treatment of PBMCs with specific tumor antigens as reported before ( ). There were six patients for whom serial PBMC samples were available with two patient responders and four patient nonresponders. T cells from responder patient MDA014 demonstrated tumor-specific T-cell reactivities following administration with concomitant increased secretion of TNFα and granzyme B; the IFNγ responses were less consistent (Supplementary Fig. S1). Patient MDA010 showed transient enhanced T-cell reactivities for the secretion of TNFα, granzyme B, and IFNγ that were mostly attributed to pembrolizumab. T cells from the four nonresponders had inconsistent reactivities across TNFα, granzyme B, and IFNγ.

## Local tumor-specific T-cell responses
To determine local immune responses to treatment, we performed IHC for T-cell and regulatory T-cell markers: CD3 marking all T cells, CD8 marking T cells with potential cytotoxic activity, and FOXP3 marking regulatory T cells. Injected and noninjected tumors were compared on day 0 before treatment and day 84 after pembrolizumab administration. Lymphocyte infiltration of the tumor biopsies was variable and patient-dependent and largely limited to the tumor margin (Supplementary Fig. S2). Increased infiltration of lymphocytes in the noninjected tumor was detected in patients MDA019 and MDA010, who were clinical responders.

## Whole blood anaerobic culture
The main objective of whole blood anaerobic culture was to monitor patients’ systemic exposure to C. novyi-NT. Anaerobic blood cultures were collected on the following schedule: day 1 or 0 (before dose), 12 hours after the dose, and daily from days 9 to 15 and on days 21, 28, and 35 after the dose. A total of 189 cultures were collected from the patients and analyzed. A positive C. novyi-NT culture was detected for two patients on days 14 and 9. All other culture results for C. novyi-NT were negative. The second patient also had a positive culture for days 28 and 35 for Staphylococcus species.

## Discussion
The effective delivery of therapeutic agents to hypoxic regions within tumors remains a challenge in cancer treatment. These regions are associated with aggressive tumor growth, metastasis, and resistance to traditional therapies. To address this, oncolytic bacterial therapy using Clostridium species has emerged as a novel approach due to their ability to induce immune anticancer responses. Our study explored the combination of C. novyi-NT and pembrolizumab in patients with advanced solid tumors to enhance anticancer activity by promoting tumor-confined cell lysis and immune responses.
Our findings underscore the safety of the combination therapy across four dosing cohorts. Notably, the MTD was established as pembrolizumab intravenously at 200 mg every 3 weeks and 100 × 104C. novyi-NT spores intratumorally. This dose of C. novyi-NT spores was also established as the MTD in the previous phase I study (NCT01924689) and was safely combined with pembrolizumab in this study ( ). AEs were predominantly of grade 1 or 2 severity, with manageable TRAEs, and only one patient experienced a DLT of grade 3 abscess formation at the intratumoral injection site. SAEs were observed only in six patients, including one treatment-related SAE linked to C. novyi-NT. Importantly, only one patient discontinued the trial due to an AE secondary to pembrolizumab. This is strikingly contrasted with the phase I monotherapy study in which C. novyi-NT monotherapy was studied, and among 24 patients, three DLTs were noted with grade 4 sepsis in 2 patients and grade 4 gas gangrene in 1 patient, albeit 2 of the DLTs occurred in 2 patients from the 3 × 106C. novyi-NT spores cohort, a dose level not explored in the present study. Additionally, differences in the size of the injected tumor, which tended to be smaller in the present study, and the spectrum of tumor histologies may have contributed to the difference in the germination rate and incidence of higher-grade AEs between the two studies ( ). The dataset was not large enough to support any statistical analysis on the correlation between tumor size versus response and germination.
Sustaining a consistently robust and targeted immune response within the human body has remained a significant challenge, necessitating the use of strategic combinations of molecules and enhanced techniques. In this context, intratumoral therapy has emerged as a promising strategy to reshape the TME and convert immunologically “cold” tumors into “hot” tumors ( ). Direct delivery of therapeutic agents to the tumor site can facilitate immune cell infiltration, improve antigen presentation, counteract immunosuppressive factors, and trigger an inflammatory reaction, thereby amplifying the effectiveness of ICIs ( ). Hence, it becomes crucial to develop synergistic treatment regimens that enhance T cell–mediated antitumor responses. Numerous studies underscore the importance of baseline tumor-specific T cells for favorable responses to ICI, thereby necessitating strategies that harness these immune cells ( – ). Intratumoral immune therapy offers a means to effectively engage the tumor’s innate immune system. By activating dendritic cells at the tumor locale, this approach recruits and primes antitumor T cells, ensuring the optimal presentation of local tumor antigens/neoantigens while minimizing systemic toxicities. Overcoming these challenges can be achieved through the intratumoral administration of agents that stimulate innate immune cells, ultimately fostering the generation or expansion of existing antitumor T cells ( , ). Immune memory following C. novyi-NT treatment was supported by both cytokine modulation and mechanistic evidence of tumor-specific immune activation in this study. Notably, VEGF-A, an endothelial growth factor involved in tumor angiogenesis, was significantly upregulated (P < 0.05) in responding patients on day 3 after treatment, indicating an immune-driven response. Tumor-specific T-cell reactivity was further demonstrated in responder patient MDA014, in which increased secretion of TNFα and granzyme B following antigen stimulation indicated cytotoxic T-cell activation. This suggests that C.novyi-NT treatment primes T cells for the recognition of tumor antigens. In contrast, nonresponders displayed inconsistent T-cell responses, suggesting a lack of effective immune activation. Mechanistically, the response was further supported by local tumor immune responses, with increased infiltration of lymphocytes, particularly in noninjected tumors of patient MDA019, a clinical responder. This infiltration, along with the upregulation of proinflammatory cytokines such as VEGF-A, underscores the role of C.novyi-NT in inducing both systemic and local immune memory, likely enhanced by subsequent checkpoint inhibition with pembrolizumab.
Efficacy evaluation for the combination therapy studied herein revealed a confirmed ORR of 25%, with diverse histologies represented among responders with C. novyi-NT and pembrolizumab. Both complete and partial responses were observed, highlighting the potential translational synergy of the combination therapy. However, except in the patients with vulvar mucosal melanoma (MDA017) and the patient with chordoma (MDA019), there was no concordance in response in the injected versus target lesions. For example, MDA017 had a CR in both the injected and target lesions, and MDA019 had a PR in both the injected and target lesions. The median duration of response among responders was encouraging at 10.93 months despite the challenges of a diverse array of “hot” and “cold” solid tumors, in which regardless of prior ICI exposure, antitumor efficacy was demonstrated. Stable disease was observed in a significant proportion of patients in both injected and target lesions, with only one patient who experienced clinical PD (MDA027) as the best response. This once again presents a stark contrast with the monotherapy study in which no objective responses were noted although 86% of patients experienced stability. These findings align with previous research demonstrating the potential of bacterial therapy in combination with immunotherapy ( , ). Combining oncolytic bacterial therapy with checkpoint inhibitors creates a milieu that can be exploited for enhanced therapeutic efficacy.
The addition of pembrolizumab synergizes with the ability of C. novyi-NT to selectively replicate within hypoxic tumor regions, leading to tumoricidal death and local inflammation. This resulted in an increased neoantigen burden for durable immunologic responses ( ). Pembrolizumab enhances T-cell activity by blocking immune checkpoint pathways, whereas C. novyi-NT–induced tumor lysis releases tumor antigens that could potentiate immune responses. The combination might lead to increased antigen presentation and subsequent T-cell activation. Although the translation assays showed trends in this direction, the low number of samples did not allow for a definitive conclusion.
The immune microenvironment’s influence on the combination therapy’s outcomes underscores the complexity of achieving successful immune activation. Tumors with immunosuppressive microenvironments may hinder effective therapy ( ). However, the combination’s impact on immune cell infiltration, including the augmented presence of T cells, macrophages, and dendritic cells, can potentially overcome the immunosuppressive milieu ( ). This phenomenon suggests that the therapy might remodel the immune landscape, fostering a more favorable antitumor environment. Coupling this with possible predictive biomarkers and a focus on tumor histologies more prone to contain hypoxic regions could allow for patient stratification and enhance the precision and effectiveness of the combination therapy.
Investigating the capacity for sustained T-cell cytokine release and durable immune memory within the context of the C. novyi-NT and pembrolizumab combination therapy reveals a remarkable potential for enduring therapeutic influence. The initiation of a robust immune response has the potential to persist beyond the treatment period, creating a shield against tumor recurrence and metastatic progression ( ). This memory-centric phenomenon not only holds the power to reshape the inherent trajectory of the disease but also positions the combination therapy as a dynamic force in achieving long-lasting antitumor effects. Notably, tumor antigen–stimulated release of T-cell IFNγ, TNFα, and granzyme B cytokines continues to have sustained and incremental levels in the presence of 3 weekly doses of pembrolizumab after one dose of C. novyi-NT in the PBMCs, even at day 336 in the patient with nasopharyngeal cancer (MDA014) who had confirmed an overall response of PR (−87%), which was durable for up to 2 years until the completion of the study ( ).
 There are some limitations to consider from this study. Our study used only a single injection of C. novyi-NT spores into a single tumor, and the translational readouts were very limited in number. Although the effect of a single dose of C. novyi-NT can lead to significant tumoricidal lysis, it is unlikely to sufficiently reverse the course of metastatic disease. The rationale behind the chosen dosing and scheduling strategies for both C. novyi-NT and pembrolizumab underpins the treatment’s efficacy. Considering alternative dosing regimens, specific tumor types, tumor size, and imaging to identify the anaerobic core and metabolic state of the tumor becomes essential in optimizing therapeutic outcomes. Exploring varied dosing patterns might uncover synergistic effects that maximize therapeutic benefits and minimize potential toxicities. This was a study conducted in a tertiary single center where expertise to perform intratumoral therapies and inpatient monitoring was feasible. Assessing the feasibility of integrating this therapy into current protocols becomes pivotal. The potential impact on patient outcomes, disease progression, and survival highlights the significance of conducting larger-scale clinical trials. These trials can validate the findings, ascertain the therapy’s reproducibility, and expand the patient cohort, leading to more robust and comprehensive data. No hypoxia or tumor resistance markers were studied biologically to augment the further understanding of the underlying mechanism and synergy with the tumor hypoxic environments. Additionally, tumor mutational burden was unknown in three out of four responders, limiting our ability to explore its correlation with treatment response. This should be considered when interpreting the efficacy signals observed in this cohort.
The promise of combining C. novyi-NT and pembrolizumab with other therapeutic modalities opens doors to broader treatment strategies. Integrating targeted therapies, other immunotherapies, or radiotherapy could potentially amplify therapeutic outcomes ( ). By targeting various facets of the TME, this combinatorial approach might pave the way for multifaceted and potent antitumor effects.
In conclusion, our study establishes the safety and efficacy profile of combining intratumoral C. novyi-NT and pembrolizumab systemic treatment in patients with advanced solid tumors. We observed manageable AEs, promising response rates, and translational immune responses to support the underlying synergy between C. novyi-NT and pembrolizumab. These results underscore the potential of this combination therapy to address the challenges posed by hypoxic regions within tumors. Further exploration in larger, randomized studies will provide deeper insights into the utility and potential applications of this approach in the evolving landscape of oncolytic bacterial therapies, in which much headway is required.
