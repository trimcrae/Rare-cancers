## Introduction:
Differentiating benign and malignant myxoid soft-tissue tumors preoperatively can be challenging due to shared clinical, imaging, and histologic features. Biopsy specimens are frequently described as “low-grade myxoid neoplasms” without benign or malignant classification (ie, indeterminate). Our purpose is to evaluate the prevalence and prognosis of myxoid tumors with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection.

## Methods:
A retrospective review identified 439 patients with a musculoskeletal myxoid soft-tissue tumor confirmed on final resection pathology. Biopsy result (benign, malignant, indeterminate) for each biopsy type was compared with the final resection diagnosis. Indeterminate diagnosis before resection was defined as when the preoperative diagnosis was uncertain to be benign or malignant based on all biopsy data. For patients with sarcomas on final resection and 2-year follow-up, the prevalence of positive margins and local recurrence were compared between patients with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection and those with a malignant diagnosis known preoperatively.

## Results:
The prevalence of indeterminate diagnosis before resection was 28% (66/235). In these patients with sarcomas on final resection and 2-year follow-up, the positive margin rate at resection surgery was 37% (10/27) compared with 15% (11/74) in patients with a malignant diagnosis known preoperatively (P = 0.049). Their 5-year local recurrence–free survival was 73% (95% confidence interval, 58 to 92) compared with 92% (95% confidence interval, 86 to 98) in patients with a malignant diagnosis known preoperatively (P = 0.022).

## Conclusion:
Indeterminate diagnosis before resection in myxoid tumors is relatively common, compared with prior reports in nonmyxoid neoplasms. Patients with indeterminate diagnosis before resection had two times higher rate of positive margins at resection surgery and markedly shorter local recurrence–free survival at 5 years compared with patients with malignant diagnosis known preoperatively. Diagnostic uncertainty at the time of resection is associated with worse oncologic outcomes in myxoid tumors.

Magnetic resonance imaging classically shows a mass with intermediate to low T1 signal and very high T2 signal, secondary to the high water content of myxoid tumors. Similarity in histologic features is known to contribute to potential tissue sampling error with needle biopsy, particularly with cellular myxomas and low-grade myxoid sarcomas, , as only 50% of cases can GNAS1 differentiate myxoma from myxofibrosarcoma. Figure highlights two case examples illustrating the imaging and histologic overlap that can exist between benign and malignant myxoid tumors. Because of this, some needle biopsy specimens are described as “low-grade myxoid neoplasms” without benign or malignant classification, that is, “indeterminate.” When the malignant potential of a suspected myxoid tumor is indeterminate after biopsy, upfront resection is often recommended when feasible to obtain more tissue to render a diagnosis. In this scenario of uncertainty, the opportunity for neoadjuvant radiation therapy is missed in the case of a malignant tumor, and wide resection may increase morbidity for the patient in the case of a benign tumor.
 There exist limited data on myxoid soft-tissue tumors with an indeterminate diagnosis before surgical resection. In 2019, Rohela et al. reported core needle biopsy (CNB) correctly diagnosed malignancy in 84% (58/69) of myxoid tumors compared with 95% (184/194) of nonmyxoid tumors. Although they reported a rebiopsy rate of 8% (6/69) in myxoid tumors, they did not report any further on patients who may have had an indeterminate diagnosis before surgical resection. In 2021, Klein et al. reported a 27% (5/19) prevalence of indeterminate diagnosis by needle biopsy in myxofibrosarcomas compared with a 13% prevalence (19/196) in a series of other soft-tissue sarcomas, although conclusions are limited with a sample size of less than 20 patients. Thus, the prevalence of myxoid tumors with an indeterminate preoperative diagnosis is unknown, and question remains as to if preoperative diagnostic uncertainty has implications on oncologic outcome.
The purpose of this study is to evaluate the prevalence and prognosis of musculoskeletal myxoid soft-tissue tumors with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection. In this study, we asked the following questions: (1) What is the prevalence of tumors with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection? (2) What is the comparative diagnostic accuracy of fine needle aspiration (FNA), CNB, and incisional biopsy in myxoid soft-tissue tumors? (3) What clinical features might differentiate benign from malignant tumors? (4) What is the prognosis of tumors with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection, as measured by adverse alterations in care, positive margins during resection surgery, and local recurrence?

## Methods

## Diagnostic Accuracy by Biopsy Type
Following approval from our institutional review board, we did a retrospective analysis of patients surgically treated at our institution with a histologically confirmed myxoid soft-tissue tumor on final resection pathology between January 1, 1995, and December 31, 2020. Tumors included in this study were of the extremity, pelvis, trunk, chest wall, and head and neck, whereas intrathoracic or intra-abdominal were excluded. Biopsy type and result were obtained from the electronic medical record. Biopsy types included FNA, CNB, and incisional biopsy (IB). FNA is performed in the office by the pathology team and read by attending cytopathologists. CNB is either performed in the office by the surgical team or in the radiology suite under image guidance by interventional radiology. IB refers to an open biopsy procedure in the operating room. All biopsy results included in this study were based on the pathologist's read at our institution. A biopsy result was considered benign if a specific benign diagnosis was reported, or no sign of malignancy was explicitly stated in the report. A biopsy result was considered malignant if a specific malignant diagnosis was reported or malignancy was stated to be favored. A biopsy result was considered “indeterminate” if no specific benign or malignant diagnosis was reported, or a diagnosis was not rendered because the tissue specimen was inadequate. Common examples of nonspecific diagnoses considered indeterminate were “myxoid neoplasm” or “low grade myxoid neoplasm not otherwise specified.” The diagnosis rendered on the pathology report of the definitive/final tumor resection was the benchmark diagnosis. Biopsy diagnoses were compared with the final resection pathology diagnosis to determine diagnostic accuracy. A biopsy result was considered correct if the diagnosis was concordant (ie, benign-benign) with the diagnosis obtained on final resection pathology. A biopsy result was considered incorrect if the diagnosis was discordant (ie, benign-malignant, or malignant-benign) with the diagnosis obtained on final resection pathology. A biopsy result was considered indeterminate if no benign or malignant diagnosis was rendered.

## Prevalence of Indeterminate Diagnosis Before Resection
The prevalence of indeterminate diagnosis before resection was calculated for patients with a preoperative biopsy obtained before resection and read by the pathologists at our institution. All patients had final resection pathology to compare to as the benchmark diagnosis. For patients with multiple biopsies before final resection, the highest-quality biopsy specimen (IB > CNB > FNA) available was used as the presumptive preoperative diagnosis. Thus, if a patient had an indeterminate FNA but a benign CNB, then their preoperative diagnosis was benign. When the presumptive preoperative diagnosis based on the biopsy was concordant with the final resection diagnosis (ie, benign-benign), this was labeled as correct diagnosis. When the presumptive preoperative diagnosis based on the biopsy was discordant with the final resection diagnosis (ie, benign-malignant), this was labeled as incorrect diagnosis. When the presumptive preoperative diagnosis based on the biopsy was indeterminate, this was labeled as indeterminate diagnosis.

## Clinical Features of Benign and Malignant Tumors
Patient age, sex, tumor size (greatest dimension in cm), tumor depth (superficial to fascia, or deep to fascia), tumor location (upper extremity, lower extremity, or trunk), pain at presentation (yes or no), and incidental discovery of tumor (yes or no) were collected from the medical record. These factors were determined a priori by the authors as clinically relevant when evaluating a patient with a presumed myxoid tumor on presentation. Statistical analysis was done to compare the baseline features between the patients with benign and malignant diagnosis on final resection pathology.

## Adverse Alterations in Care, Positive Margins, and Local Recurrence
Adverse alterations in care were collected for patients with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection. An adverse alteration in care was defined as a deviation from the preferred treatment that a patient would have received had a diagnosis of benign or malignant been made before resection. For patients with a benign tumor, adverse alterations in care included an extramorbid resection (i.e., wide resection) or an unnecessary resection. For patients with a malignant tumor, adverse alterations in care included a missed opportunity for neoadjuvant therapy for tumors that would have otherwise been indicated or an inadequate resection (ie, planned marginal resection). Adverse alterations in care were identified through manual review of the medical record, including multidisciplinary sarcoma tumor board notes and surgical reports. An extramorbid resection was defined as when the surgeon documented an intentionally wide resection to obtain clear margins given the indeterminate preoperative diagnosis. To meet these criteria, the resection had to be more morbid than simply taking a cuff of normal tissue around the specimen, as is typical for an excisional biopsy. To be classified as an extramorbid resection, documentation by the surgeon indicated that some specific aspect increased morbidity, such as resection of an entire muscle belly, resection of bone, or dissection around a major neurovascular bundle that was performed to achieve a negative margin. An unnecessary resection was defined as when the surgeon documented that the patient would not have had the tumor resected had a benign diagnosis been known preoperatively. A missed opportunity for neoadjuvant therapy was defined as when a malignant tumor on final resection pathology that was >5 cm was not radiated preoperatively, which is the preferred treatment pathway for deep or superficial soft-tissue sarcoma at our institution. An inadequate resection was defined as when the surgeon documented that the resection was intentionally marginal in nature, not wide, and positive margins resulted for the malignant tumor on final resection pathology, requiring the need for reresection or adjuvant treatment. All classifications were reviewed by the senior author (J.M.L.).
For all patients with myxoid sarcomas on final resection pathology and minimum 2-year follow-up, the rate of positive margins and local recurrence in patients with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection was compared with that of patients with a malignant diagnosis known preoperatively. Positive margins were assessed from the pathology reports during the definitive resection surgery. Local recurrence was identified through manual review of clinical notes, including sarcoma tumor board notes. Advanced imaging and/or tissue confirmation of recurrence was not required in every case, although definitive documentation of recurrence by the treating clinician was necessary. Kaplan-Meier analysis was used to trend local recurrence–free survival (LRFS) over time and time to local recurrence.

## Patient Characteristics
The entire patient cohort included 439 patients with a myxoid soft-tissue tumor based on final resection histology (Table ). The mean patient age was 56.1 ± 16.1 years, and 54% (236/439) of patients were female. Final resection confirmed the tumor as benign in 30% (131/439) of patients, and malignant in 70% (307/439) of patients; one patient was there with an indeterminate diagnosis even after final resection. Most tumors were of the extremity (90%, 394/439) and were deep to fascia (76%, 335/439). Patients presenting with pain accounted for 43% (190/439) of the cohort, and only 8% (33/439) of tumors were discovered incidentally. Mean tumor size was 7.9 ± 5.6 cm. Within the study cohort, 54% (235/439) of patients had a preoperative biopsy (FNA, CNB, or IB) read by a pathologist at our institution (Figure ). Because patients who underwent more than one biopsy were there, a total of 263 preoperative biopsies in 235 patients was recorded, including 84 FNA, 110 CNB, and 69 IB.
 

## Statistical Analysis
Accuracy of each biopsy type included the number and proportion of correct diagnoses, incorrect diagnoses, and indeterminate diagnoses. When comparing features between benign and malignant cohorts, Wilcoxon rank-sum or Kruskal-Wallis tests were used to compare continuous variables, and Fisher exact tests and chi-square tests were used to compare categorical variables. When comparing diagnostic yield by biopsy type, a chi-square test was used to identify difference in proportions between the groups. Univariate and multivariable logistic regression was used to determine the association between patient and tumor variables with benign or malignant final diagnosis. Initial multivariable regression models included all variables from univariable analyses. Variables were then eliminated in a stepwise manner in order of increasing z-score until remaining variables were notable at α = 0.1. The “Firth” correction for small or imbalanced samples was used for bias reduction in logistic regression. Chi-square tests were used to compare rate of positive margin and local recurrence between the groups. Time to local recurrence was calculated using Kaplan-Meier survival analysis. All tests were two sided, and a P value of less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant. All statistical calculations were done using R (version 4.0.2).

## Results

## Prevalence of Indeterminate Diagnosis Before Resection
Of the 235 patients with a biopsy before definitive resection, 28% (66/235) had an indeterminate diagnosis before resection, and 3% (7/235) had an incorrect diagnosis before resection. This yielded a combined indeterminate or incorrect diagnosis rate before resection of 31% (73/235) (Figure ). On final resection pathology of the 66 patients with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection, 31 patients with a benign diagnosis and 35 patients with a malignant diagnosis were found. The most common benign diagnosis was myxoma (84%, 26/31), and the most common malignant diagnosis was myxofibrosarcoma (57%, 20/35) (Table ). Among these patients, 50% (33/66) only had FNA, 35% (23/66) only had CNB, and 5% (3/66) only had IB before resection. The remaining patients had a combination of FNA + CNB (6%, 4/66), FNA + IB (2%, 1/66), CNB + IB (2%, 1/66), and FNA + CNB + IB (2%, 1/66).
 

## Diagnostic Accuracy by Biopsy Type
The relative proportions of correct, incorrect, and indeterminate diagnosis markedly differed between FNA, CNB, and IB (P < 0.001), as shown in Table . Of the 84 FNA performed, the correct diagnosis rate was 38% (32/84), and the rate of indeterminate diagnosis was 55% (46/84). The rate of incorrect diagnosis was 7% (6/84). Of the 110 CNB performed, the correct diagnosis rate was 69% (76/110), and the rate of indeterminate diagnosis was 30% (33/110). Only one patient with a CNB had an incorrect diagnosis (1%) with the biopsy reporting a benign tumor but final resection pathology showing a malignant tumor. Of the 69 IB performed, the correct diagnosis rate was 88% (61/69), and the rate of indeterminate diagnosis was 9% (6/69). The rate of incorrect diagnosis was 3% (2/69).
 

## Clinical Features of Benign and Malignant Tumors
Malignant tumors had a higher proportion of men (53% versus 31%, P < 0.001), were larger (median: 7.3 cm versus 4.5 cm, P < 0.001; mean: 8.9 cm versus 5.4 cm, P < 0.001), were more likely to be superficial to fascia (30% versus 9%, P < 0.001), and were less likely to be discovered incidentally (5% versus 15%, P < 0.001) (Table ). On multivariable analysis (Table ), superficial tumor depth (odds ratio [OR]: 8.85, P < 0.001), tumor location of the trunk (OR: 3.26, P = 0.023), male sex (OR: 2.18, P = 0.001), and tumor size (OR: 1.28, P < 0.001) were independent predictors of a malignant diagnosis. Incidental discovery of the tumor (OR: 0.34, P = 0.010) was associated with a benign diagnosis.
 

## Adverse Alterations in Care, Positive Margins, and Local Recurrence
The overall rate of an adverse alteration in care for patients with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection was 48% (32/66) (Figure ). Of the patients with a benign tumor on resection, 39% (12/31) patients received an alteration in care—nine patients with an extramorbid resection and three patients who had a surgical resection that was unnecessary. Of the patients with a malignant tumor on resection, 57% (20/35) received an alteration in care—17 patients who missed the opportunity to initiate neoadjuvant therapy before resection and three patients who had an inadequate resection for a malignant tumor. In patients with a final diagnosis of a myxoid sarcoma (Table ), the positive margin rate at resection surgery was 37% (10/27) in patients with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection, compared with 15% (11/74) in patients with a malignant diagnosis known preoperatively (P = 0.049). In patients with a final diagnosis of a myxoid sarcoma, the 5-year LRFS was 73% (95% confidence interval [CI], 58 to 92) in patients with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection, compared with 92% (95% CI, 86 to 98) in patients with a malignant diagnosis known preoperatively (P = 0.022). In patients with a final diagnosis of a myxoid sarcoma, the 10-year LRFS (95% CI) was 73% (58 to 92) in patients with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection, compared with 80% (68 to 93) in patients with a malignant diagnosis known preoperatively (P = 0.1). In patients with a final diagnosis of a myxoid sarcoma, the median time to local recurrence was 2.0 years (interquartile range; IQR 0.36 to 3.5) in patients with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection compared with 2.2 years (IQR 0.7 to 5.8) in patients with a malignant diagnosis known preoperatively. Log rank comparison demonstrates overall difference in survival estimates (P = 0.041) (Figure ).
 

## Discussion
In the largest series of myxoid soft-tissue tumors reported to our knowledge, the prevalence of indeterminate diagnosis before resection was 28%, affirming the suspicion that myxoid tumors are associated with a relatively high level of preoperative diagnostic uncertainty. The correct diagnosis rate varied markedly depending on the biopsy type (FNA, CNB, or IB), ranging from 38% to 88%. Patients with a malignancy were more likely to be male and have tumors that were larger, superficial to fascia, and less often to be discovered incidentally. Approximately half of patients with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection were found to have an alteration in care secondary to the uncertainty of their preoperative diagnosis. Importantly, in patients with a myxoid sarcoma on final diagnosis and a minimum of 2-year follow-up, patients with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection had two times higher the rate of positive margins at resection surgery and markedly shorter LRFS at 5-year postresection compared with patients with a malignant diagnosis known preoperatively.
The diagnostic accuracy varied considerably between FNA, CNB, and IB. As expected, FNA was the least accurate (38%, 32/86) with the most indeterminate diagnoses (55%, 46/86), whereas IB was the most accurate (88%, 61/69) with the least indeterminate diagnoses (9%, 6/69). Although the comparative accuracy and diagnostic yield of FNA, CB, and IB parallels prior studies in musculoskeletal tumors, these data have yet to be reported in such a large cohort of patients with musculoskeletal myxoid soft-tissue tumors. Most clinically relevant was the 30% (33/110) rate of indeterminate diagnosis with CNB, which is the most common biopsy type used for musculoskeletal soft-tissue tumors. At our institution, we quote approximately a 10% to 15% rate of an indeterminate diagnosis with a CNB of a musculoskeletal soft-tissue neoplasm. This is supported by Klein et al in 2021 who reported a 13% (19/196) rate of indeterminate diagnosis in a sample of 196 soft-tissue sarcomas. In the same study, they reported a 27% (5/19) rate of indeterminate diagnosis in myxofibrosarcomas. Their relatively higher rate of indeterminate diagnosis in myxoid sarcomas parallels the present study (28%, 66/235). The data reported here reiterate that each biopsy type possesses a unique accuracy profile that should be considered when working up a suspected myxoid tumor.
The rate of indeterminate diagnosis after biopsy varies based on the tumor subtype, with myxoid tumors carrying a higher error rate than other subtypes. - This study found that approximately one in four patients with a musculoskeletal myxoid tumor had an indeterminate preoperative diagnosis before resection, illustrating the diagnostic dilemma they can present, and the need for improvement in the workup of these tumors. We found that IB has excellent diagnostic yield at a rate of 93 to 96%, but the cost and morbidity incurred by an additional trip to the operating room make percutaneous options more favorable first-line choices. , FNA is the least invasive, but is not as clinically reliable and often requires repeat biopsy to confirm a diagnosis, especially in myxoid tumors. , For most tumors, CNB is an accurate and inexpensive alternative to IB with rates of indeterminate biopsy remaining relatively consistent (∼13% to 16%) over the past 30 years. , - Although myxoid histology is associated with a lower diagnostic yield of CNB as shown in this study (30% indeterminate result), it is still a reasonable first-line diagnostic option for suspected myxoid tumors. , A practice change we have made after considering these data is the near complete elimination of FNA in the workup of possible myxoid tumors, as it has shown to be mostly ineffective in these tumors at our institution, although we recognize this may not translate to other centers. The results of this study coupled with prior literature have guided the development of a proposed diagnostic algorithm for suspected myxoid soft-tissue tumors in Figure .
 Patient and tumor features were explored for association with final diagnosis that could be used to assist in making a diagnosis preoperatively. In this 439-patient cohort, the clinical features associated with malignancy included male sex, superficial tumor depth, trunk tumor location, and larger size; incidental discovery of the tumor was associated with a benign diagnosis. Although deep masses are more often associated with malignancy than superficial masses, myxofibrosarcomas are often superficial, whereas benign intramuscular myxomas are deep. Historically, features that favor a myxoid sarcoma are older age, male sex, and larger tumor size, whereas features that favor an intramuscular myxomas are young age, female sex, and smaller tumor size. , , Although patient sex and tumor size were parallel with our study, patient age was not predictive of diagnosis in our cohort. Furthermore, soft-tissue sarcomas are often not painful, which was demonstrated in the no difference found in the rate of pain between benign and malignant tumors.
To our knowledge, this is the first study evaluating the clinical import of an indeterminate preoperative diagnosis on the treatment received and oncologic outcome in myxoid tumors. In this cohort, 32 of the 66 patients (48%) with indeterminate preoperative diagnosis underwent treatment that was adversely altered by the indeterminate nature of the preoperative diagnosis. Patients with a benign tumor on final resection received an extramorbid resection in 29% (9/31) of cases or underwent resection that would have otherwise not been performed in 10% of cases (3/31). Potentially more impactful from an oncologic standpoint are the 49% (17/35) of patients with a malignant tumor on final resection who did not undergo neoadjuvant radiation therapy and the 9% (3/35) of patients with a malignant tumor on final resection who had a planned marginal resection and positive margins. Two of these three patients went onto local recurrence. In the 17 cases who missed an opportunity for neoadjuvant radiation, four went onto local recurrence. Most importantly, for patients with myxoid sarcomas on final resection and 2-year clinical follow-up, patients with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection had two times higher the rate of positive margins at resection surgery and markedly shorter LRFS at 5-years post-resection compared with patients with a malignant diagnosis known preoperatively. This signifies an association between preoperative diagnostic uncertainty and worse oncologic outcome in myxoid sarcomas. Additional association with other adverse outcomes such as decreased function from extramorbid resection or patient dissatisfaction from unnecessary surgery requires further investigation.
This study has several limitations. Biopsy and resection pathology diagnoses were determined retrospectively from pathology reports, and tissue specimens were not rereviewed, which limits the diagnostic accuracy reported in this study to the pathologist assigned to reading the slide at the time. As well, the study spans over a long period, as histologic diagnosis and classification of myxoid soft-tissue tumors have evolved. For myxoid tumors, it is common practice to use immunohistochemistry to exclude myxoid variants of specific tumors (eg, myxoid neurofibroma or spindle cell lipoma), fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) to exclude bland-appearing malignant tumors with a given translocation (eg, DDIT3 FISH to exclude myxoid liposarcoma), and next-generation sequency to determine if there is a novel or existing translocations or mutations (eg, GNAS1 mutations), point potential therapeutic targets, or a complex genotype (ie, the complex genetics found in myxofibrosarcoma). In most cases, immunohistochemistry and FISH are used, and next-generation sequency is reserved for diagnostically challenging cases. Being a retrospective review of a cohort over 2 decades, we acknowledge the inherent limitation in differences in the workup and diagnostic specificity between tumors diagnosed early in the study period, compared with more recently. Acknowledging this limitation, biopsy and resection specimens included in this study were reviewed primarily by a bone and soft-tissue expert pathologist at our sarcoma center. The retrospective nature of this study also required us to determine perceived adverse alterations in care only by what was documented in the medical record. This left interpretation of an alteration in care up to the authors, although definitions were proposed in best faith effort to reduce bias, though undoubtedly not eliminating bias. It is also possible that alterations in care occurred but were not documented and were therefore missed.

## Conclusion
The prevalence of indeterminate diagnosis before resection of musculoskeletal myxoid soft-tissue tumors was 28%, notably higher than other musculoskeletal neoplasms based on prior reports. For those that ended up being myxoid sarcomas on final resection, patients with an indeterminate diagnosis before resection had two times higher the rate of positive margins at resection surgery and markedly worse LRFS at 5-year postresection compared with patients with a malignant diagnosis known preoperatively, signifying the association between accurate preoperative diagnosis and improved oncologic outcome.
