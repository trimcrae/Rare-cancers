## BACKGROUND
Perineuriomas are benign, typically solitary tumors with intraneural and extraneural subtypes presenting distinct clinical manifestations and symptomatology. Here, the authors report a unique case of multifocal, infiltrative extraneural soft tissue perineuriomas localized to the shoulder.

## OBSERVATIONS
A 33-year-old female presented with 1 year of right neck and shoulder pain accompanied by a new onset of numbness and tingling down her right arm. MRI of a mass apparent over her right trapezius and scapula revealed several mixed cystic and solid neoplasms encroaching on the C5–6 nerve roots and brachial plexus. The tumors were biopsied and confirmed by immunohistochemical staining to be extraneural soft tissue perineuriomas. Further excision was carried out to relieve the patient of her symptoms associated with mass effect on the brachial plexus.

## LESSONS
This case highlights the importance of a thorough workup of soft tissue and peripheral nerve sheath masses. There is extraordinary difficulty in diagnosing and treating extraneural perineuriomas, given their wide array of clinicopathological presentations, and it is crucial that these tumors are accurately identified to avoid overtreatment.

Intraneural perineuriomas are limited to the boundaries of the peripheral nerve and manifest with motor or sensory deficits secondary to expansion of the affected nerve. Extraneural perineuriomas present within the skin and soft tissue and, depending on their specific clinicopathological features, can be further divided into soft tissue, sclerosing, or reticular subtypes. 
Soft tissue variants are commonly found in the trunk and extremities but have also been reported in the brain, kidneys, and intestines. – Their clinical symptoms can be nonspecific, and many patients are asymptomatic at the time of diagnosis. Any motor or sensory deficits occur secondary to mass effect as the tumor expands to encroach on neighboring structures. , Benign extraneural perineuriomas in general have classically been reported in the literature as solitary painless nodules, with only one report detailing an example of multifocality in the sclerosing subtype. Here, we report the unique case of a multifocal, infiltrative extraneural soft tissue perineurioma presenting with pain and sensory dysfunction.

## Illustrative Case

## History and Examination
A 33-year-old female was referred to our musculoskeletal oncology clinic due to concern for malignancy as the root cause of 1 year of right neck, shoulder, and trapezius pain and swelling. The patient presented with acute exacerbation of her pain accompanied by a new onset of tingling and numbness radiating down her right arm. Physical examination demonstrated an irregular, fixed, firm, and nontender mass approximately the size of an orange extending over the right trapezius and scapula. The overlying skin was intact without any changes in color or appearance. The patient also presented with swollen supraclavicular lymph nodes, but she denied any recent fevers, chills, weight loss, or other constitutional symptoms. The patient had no history of prior trauma to the area, and she also did not have a family history of similar pathology.

## Magnetic Resonance Imaging
The patient was initially being seen by the general surgery team for these issues, and around 2 months prior to her presentation to the clinic, she had undergone MRI, which showed a lobulated, septated soft tissue mass encroaching on the C5–6 nerve roots and the superior trunk of the brachial plexus ( ). There was also some concern for necrotic metastasis to the lower cervical lymph nodes.
 

## Biopsy
Fine needle aspiration by interventional radiology shortly after MRI demonstrated neoplastic cells, and a follow-up core biopsy suggested that the mass was a myxoid liposarcoma. Unfortunately, there was not enough tissue yield from the needle biopsies to check for the t(12;16)(q13;p11) translocation to confirm the mass as a myxoid liposarcoma. Given the lack of definitive diagnosis from these biopsies, the decision was made to perform an open, incisional biopsy of the mass to confirm the diagnosis prior to beginning any treatment.
We carefully dissected through the superior portion of the supraspinatus muscle belly to visualize the mass, which was separated from the surrounding muscle by a white tissue capsule. A 5 mm × 5 mm × 1–cm deep biopsy was collected and sent along with both previous biopsy specimens to an outside pathologist for further evaluation. Immunohistochemical staining of the new biopsy specimen was consistent with a diagnosis of extraneural soft tissue perineurioma, staining positive for smooth muscle actin (SMA), epithelial membrane antigen (EMA), and human erythrocyte glucose transporter-1 (GLUT-1) and negative for S100 and SOX-10. However, the two earlier specimens could not be definitively identified as perineuriomas and were instead labeled as atypical spindle cell masses.

## Surgery
Once the pathology results came back, we moved forward with surgical removal of the masses. Her tumor involved both the periclavicular and periscapular regions, and initially, it seemed that her symptoms were related solely to the periclavicular portion. Thus, a staged approach was utilized. The first operation was conducted in the supraclavicular region where most of the patient’s symptoms were. During this operation, three large masses were excised along with some smaller nodules ( ). The first mass was located within the supraspinatus muscle, extending underneath the acromion and filling the entire space above the scapular spine. The second mass was lateral to the clavicular insertion of the sternocleidomastoid and extended deep and anterior to the trapezius muscle. The final large tumor was removed from underneath the lateral head of the sternocleidomastoid. Great care was taken to protect the neurovascular structures as the tumors were extracted. Although the tumors abutted several of the nerve roots from the brachial plexus, they did not appear to enter or exit the nerve fascicles. Grossly, the tumor nodules ranged in size from 0.6 cm to 4.2 cm, with the involvement of adjacent lymph nodes by direct extension ( ).
 
H&E-stained sections showed a proliferation of bland-appearing spindle cells in whorls and storiform patterns within a fibromyxoid stroma ( ). The spindle cells had bland elongated and plump nuclei with inconspicuous nucleoli and delicate bipolar eosinophilic cytoplasmic processes. No significant mitotic activity or tumor necrosis was present. Scattered lymphocytes and plasma cells were present in the background.
 
On immunohistochemical staining, the spindle cells were positive for SMA, desmin (focal), EMA (focal), ALK-1 (focal), GLUT-1, and SATB2. The spindle cells were negative for CD21, CD23, CD34, clusterin, cytokeratin cocktail, GFAP, INSM1, MUC4, MyoD1, myogenin, PR, S100, SOX-10, SS18-SSX, and signal transducer and activator of transcription-6. In addition, H3K27me3 expression was retained, ruling out a malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumor. INI-1 expression was retained, ruling out poorly differentiated chordoma, epithelioid sarcoma, and malignant rhabdoid tumors. The Ki-67 proliferation index was low and was estimated to be approximately 5.0%, supporting a low-grade lesion or benignity.
By fluorescent in situ hybridization analysis, the tumor was negative for the DDIT3, NR4A3, and EWSR1 rearrangements seen in myxoid liposarcoma, extraskeletal myxoid chondrosarcoma, and Ewing sarcoma, respectively. Aside from the focal desmin positivity, which can be nonspecific, the overall staining pattern supported a diagnosis of perineurioma. Genetic sequencing conducted on the samples revealed mutations in KRAS, LRRK2, FUS-ATF1, and NTRK, but no mutations associated with neurofibromatosis were identified.
The patient’s postoperative recovery was uncomplicated, and repeat MRI 4 months later showed no sign of tumor recurrence. The patient’s symptoms from the tumors in her anterior and cervical region resolved. During her second surgery, the remaining tumors over her posterior scapula and shoulder were removed. These warranted a posterior approach with a separate incision from her first surgery. Postoperatively, the patient noted that her shoulder pain had completely resolved, no focal weaknesses were present, and she reported only mild paresthesias over the dorsal aspect of the long finger. Overall, she was very satisfied with the results of her procedures.
No formal postoperative or surveillance imaging was performed for follow-up. The tumor was identified as benign by immunohistochemical testing, and there are no specific guidelines for the surveillance of these tumors. The patient was told to return for follow-up if she developed symptoms but has yet to do so at more than 1 year postoperatively, despite multiple emergency department visits for other minor issues (e.g., sinusitis).

## Discussion

## Observations
Extraneural soft tissue perineuriomas are uncommon tumors that present exceptional challenges in their diagnosis and management. Their ambiguous presentations require a clinician to have a broad differential diagnosis in mind when evaluating a patient with a potential perineurioma. This patient in particular had a unique presentation of multiple lobulated perineuriomas exerting mass effect on her brachial plexus. This primarily increased the difficulty of initial diagnosis because extraneural perineuriomas are normally not associated with nervous system involvement and are typically present in the superficial soft tissues. , , – Additionally, multifocality has been documented in only a handful of cases previously and has typically been associated with malignant or hybrid tumors, unlike the benign homogeneous perineuriomas demonstrated in this patient. , , 
Rubin et al. reported a prior case of multifocal extraneural perineuriomas of the sclerosing variety that presented with multiple papules on the bilateral hands and forearms. The patient had no nervous symptomatology and was diagnosed by biopsy alone. Similar to the tissue samples obtained in our patient, biopsy revealed cells staining positive for EMA but negative for S100 and CD34. Wang et al. reported the case of a patient with multifocal intraneural perineuriomas who had symptoms similar to those in our patient—primarily presenting with pain, paresthesias, and aches in the affected nerve distributions. MRI in their case also revealed hyperintensity on T2-weighted imaging, although they were able to identify nerve enlargement. Immunohistochemical analysis of their specimen further revealed S100-positive cells surrounded by EMA- and CD34-positive cells, which differ from the S100- and CD34-negative cells biopsied from our patient.
CT and MRI scans can be helpful in distinguishing perineuriomas from other soft tissue masses, but there are several imaging features that are shared with other benign and malignant soft tissue lesions. – On CT without contrast, extraneural perineuriomas demonstrate homogeneous hypodensity comparable to that of muscle, and they appear minimally changed when visualized on CT with contrast. Better visualization is achievable on MRI, on which these masses will appear hypo- or isointense on T1-weighted images and hyperintense on T2-weighted images. In both modalities, the tumor will appear well circumscribed. Schwannomas and neurofibromas also appear well circumscribed with isointensity on T1 and hyperintensity on T2, but both might also demonstrate heterogeneity of signal enhancement on T2 due to cystic degeneration in the case of schwannomas and bony erosion in the case of neurofibromas. Accurate recognition and diagnosis of these neoplasms require correlation of clinicopathological features with immunohistochemical staining exhibiting differentiation of the perineurium.
Previous studies have determined several sensitive, but nonspecific, immunohistochemical markers for extraneural perineuriomas including SMA, EMA, and CD34. , – No single marker is sufficient to identify a mass as a perineurioma because there is significant overlap with the classic immunohistochemical profiles of similar tumors such as dermatofibrosarcomas and soft tissue meningiomas. More recent data support checking for positive staining of the markers claudin-1 and GLUT-1 for additional confirmation in the diagnosis. – 
Once the tumor has been identified, the management of extraneural and intraneural perineuriomas is very similar, with most cases being managed with marginal resection as they are both benign in nature. However, with intraneural perineuriomas, emphasis should be placed on careful excision and preservation of nerve function, given the precarious location. – , 

## Lessons
We report a unique case of benign multifocal extraneural perineuriomas with infiltrative soft tissue involvement outside of the neurovasculature. This differs widely from the usual presentation of smaller, solitary peripheral nerve tumors. The discordance between the benign biopsy results and the more aggressive and infiltrative appearance on imaging emphasizes the importance of verifying pathology with repeat biopsies (or, in this instance, an open biopsy) and evaluation by an appropriately specialized pathologist before surgical intervention. This allowed a meticulous but marginal resection of the tumor and avoided significant patient morbidity.
