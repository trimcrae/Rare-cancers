## Abstract
Extraskeletal myxoid chondrosarcoma (EMC) is characterised by recurrent NR4A3 gene rearrangements, most commonly EWSR1::NR4A3, and accounts for approximately 1–3% of soft-tissue sarcomas (STS). It typically arises in the deep soft tissues of the proximal lower limb, particularly the thigh. Diagnosis is best established by integrating morphology and immunophenotype with molecular confirmation; in particular, NR4A3 break-apart fluorescence in situ hybridisation (FISH) provides a practical single-assay solution. For localised disease, complete surgical excision remains the cornerstone of treatment. Radiotherapy (RT) improves local control when margins are close or tumours are large. Recurrence-free survival (RFS) varies: local recurrence (LR) rates range from 13 to 42% across studies, and distant metastases develop in around 35–45% of patients, primarily in the lungs. The median time to metastasis is approximately 28 months. Overall survival (OS) reflects the typically indolent yet metastatic course: 5-year OS 66–88%, and 10-year disease-specific survival approximately 85%. In advanced disease, anthracycline-based chemotherapy yields a low objective response rate (ORR), although occasional partial responses occur. By contrast, the anti-angiogenic tyrosine kinase inhibitor pazopanib produced an ORR of 18% and a median progression-free survival (PFS) of 19 months in a multicentre phase 2 study (NCT02066285). No clinically validated agents directly target NR4A3. This review summarises contemporary diagnostics and treatment, emphasising high-quality surgery, selective RT, and consideration of anti-angiogenic tyrosine kinase inhibitors in advanced disease.

## Introduction
Extraskeletal myxoid chondrosarcoma (EMC) is a rare malignant neoplasm of the soft tissues (Li et al. ; Stacchiotti et al. ). It is categorised as an ultra-rare sarcoma subtype, with an incidence of less than 1 per 1,000,000 inhabitants per year (Brown et al. ). It was first described by Stout and Verner ( ) and formally defined as a distinctive entity by Enzinger and Shiraki ( ). EMC accounts for around 1% of all soft tissue sarcomas (STS) (Brown et al. ; Shao et al. ; Wang et al. ; Fice et al. ). Historically misclassified as a low-grade chondrosarcoma, EMC is now recognised as a distinct sarcoma of intermediate malignancy. Long-term studies have shown it has high rates of local recurrence (LR) and distant metastasis (M1), suggesting it behaves more like an intermediate- to high-grade tumour of uncertain origin (Fice et al. ; Bishop et al. ; McGrory et al. ; Kemmerer et al. ; Antonescu et al. ). Advances in molecular analysis have also enhanced the accuracy of diagnosing this rare tumour type. The majority of cases of EMC are diagnosed in adults, typically within the fourth to seventh decades of life (Yoshida et al. ). The median age at diagnosis is approximately 50 years (Stacchiotti et al. ). However, the age range at diagnosis can be quite wide, with cases reported in individuals from 5 to 90 years of age (Saleh et al. ). While the disease manifests more frequently in adults, there have been some documented cases in children and adolescents (Stacchiotti et al. ; Wakely ). In a particular study encompassing 40 cases, only a single patient was an adolescent, with the remaining patients comprising adults (Shao et al. ). A further study focusing on cases in the head and neck region reported that most people affected were over 40 years of age (Romañach et al. ; Fidele et al. ). A slight male predilection is evident, with a reported male-to-female ratio of approximately 2:1 (Saleh et al. ). However, a study of head and neck cases reported a ratio of 1.7:1 (Shao et al. ), while another study reported a ratio of 1.5:1 (Li et al. ; Fidele et al. ). A plethora of studies have been conducted on EMC, yet, given its rarity, a significant proportion of these are limited to case series or retrospective reviews. Previously the most extensive study of EMC to date encompassed 117 cases (Meis-Kindblom et al. ). However, a recent study has utilised SEER database and identified 791 cases diagnosed as myxoid chondrosarcoma, with 439 cases meeting the inclusion criteria following exclusions (Wagner et al. ).

## Anatomy
EMC most often arises in deep soft tissue of the proximal lower limb, particularly the thigh (Finos et al. ; Chiusole et al. ). Together, the lower limbs and limb girdles account for around 60% of cases, with the thigh being the most frequent site (Shao et al. ; Finos et al. ; Kapoor et al. ). While the extremities are the most common sites, EMC can occur in a variety of other locations. Approximately 10–20% of EMC cases are found in the trunk (Shao et al. ). The following specific locations have been reported: (a) lower extremity: thigh (Xia et al. ), buttocks (Ji et al. ), foot (Nahal et al. ) and ankle (Bhamra et al. ); (b) upper extremity: hand (Okamoto et al. ), forearm (Handa et al. ), shoulder (Improta et al. ), arm (Vergara-Lluri et al. ), scapula (Barth ; Wang et al. ); (c) trunk (Wang et al. ; Potkrajcic et al. ): chest wall (Bustinza-Linares et al. ), pulmonary arteries (Gadabanahalli et al. ), abdominal wall (Fotiadis et al. ; Ueda et al. ; Gaudier et al. ), paravertebral soft tissues (Farhane et al. ), pancreas (Fotiadis et al. ), vulva (Omote et al. ); (d) head and neck: cranium (Narayanan et al. ), scalp (Powers et al. ), nasopharynx (Purkayastha et al. ), buccal space (Gupta et al. ), the gingival (Li et al. ), nasal cavity (Ceylan et al. ), and neck (Oh et al. ); (d) other: pleura (Goetz et al. ), mediastinum (Suster and Moran ), retroperitoneum (Fukuda et al. ), pelvis (Antonescu et al. ; Honda et al. ), perineum (Mavrogenis et al. ), urethra (Yi et al. ), dura (Rao et al. ), and masticator space (Romañach et al. ).  EMC tumours are characteristically large at the time of diagnosis. The mean size of the lesion is reported to be approximately 7–9.3 cm (Zhang et al. ), with a reported range from 1.3 to 25 cm (Meis-Kindblom et al. ; Hidai et al. ). The largest tumour size documented in the extant literature is 25 cm (Meis-Kindblom et al. ).
The symptoms exhibited at the time of presentation are often associated with the location of the tumour. EMC is often characterised by the presence of a slow-growing, deep-seated soft tissue mass, which may be accompanied by pain and tenderness (Stacchiotti et al. ; Chiusole et al. ; Ren et al. ). Some tumours may mimic a haematoma, and when located close to joints, can cause functional impairment (Shao et al. ; Fice et al. ; Zhang et al. ). Conventional X-rays are not typically specific for EMC (Hirasawa et al. ). The utilisation of ultrasound for EMC characterisation is not frequently documented in the extant literature (Starostin et al. ). Positron emission tomography (PET) scans are not particularly effective in the diagnosis and staging of EMC (Hirasawa et al. ).
There is no established staging system specific for EMC (Kemmerer et al. ; Bhamra et al. ; Dotlic et al. ). Staging is generally based on the American Joint Committee on Cancer (AJCC) staging system used for soft tissue sarcomas, which considers tumour size, grade, and the presence of regional lymph node or M1 (Hachitanda et al. ; Agaram et al. ; Enneking et al. ; Aigner et al. ).
The diagnosis of EMC is complicated by its rarity and its tendency to mimic other conditions. The diagnosis of EMC can be challenging due to its lack of specific clinical, radiological, and immunohistochemical (IHC) features. A combination of histopathological, IHC, and molecular findings is usually required for diagnosis. A definitive diagnosis is often reached after molecular analysis, which demonstrates a characteristic gene fusion involving the NR4A3 gene (Antonescu et al. ; Yoshida et al. ; Vergara-Lluri et al. ; Ren et al. ; Flucke et al. ; Hisaoka and Hashimoto ; Benini et al. ; Noguchi et al. ).
The differential diagnosis of EMC is broad and depends on the location of the tumour and its specific features. Clinically and radiologically, EMC may be mistaken for a variety of benign and malignant conditions, including: benign cysts, particularly when located near a joint (Starostin et al. ), myoepithelial carcinoma (Flucke et al. ), soft tissue chordoma (Antonescu et al. ; Tirabosco et al. ), myxoid liposarcoma (Antonescu et al. ), myxoid sclerosing epithelioid fibrosarcoma (Meis-Kindblom et al. ), epithelioid variant of myxofibrosarcoma (Hisaoka and Hashimoto ; Okamoto et al. ), chordoid meningioma (Narayanan et al. ; Siraj et al. ; Sangoi et al. ), myxoid variants of ossifying fibromyxoid tumour of soft tissues (Petscavage-Thomas et al. ; Bovee and Hogendoorn ), Extraskeletal mesenchymal chondrosarcoma (Zhou et al. ), pulmonary myxoid liposarcoma (Yanagida et al. ), rhabdomyosarcoma (Yoshida et al. ; Subramanian et al. ), leiomyosarcoma (Kikuchi et al. ; Rubin and Fletcher ), primary and metastatic chondrosarcoma of the lung (Finos et al. ), pulmonary carcinosarcoma (Gadabanahalli et al. ), haemangiopericytoma (Purgina and Lai ), extraosseous plasmacytoma (Walker et al. ; Peker et al. ), synovial sarcoma (Vergara-Lluri et al. ; Oshiro et al. ), malignant small round cell tumours (Wei and Siegal ), such as PNET/Ewing’s sarcoma (Rao et al. ), other myxoid neoplasms (Vergara-Lluri et al. ; Jakowski and Wakely ).
EMC is associated with a significant risk of LR, occurring in 30–50% of cases, and M1, reported in 35–45% of cases (Meis-Kindblom et al. ). While the lungs are the most frequent site of metastasis in EMC (Meis-Kindblom et al. ), other sites, including lymph nodes, bone, and the pancreas, have also been documented (Fotiadis et al. ; Takeda et al. ).

## Prognosis
The prognosis of EMC is variable. It is classified as a sarcoma of intermediate malignancy, with an indolent but metastatic course (Patel et al. ), it is associated with a risk of LR and M1 (Drilon et al. ). Long-term survival is possible, but there is a high incidence of locoregional recurrence (Gusho et al. ). Overall survival (OS) rates vary, with studies reporting 5-year OS rates ranging from 71 to 100% (Shao et al. ; Drilon et al. ; Gusho et al. ; Kawaguchi et al. ), and 10-year OS rates ranging from 60 to 88% (Drilon et al. ). One study on 42 patients (Kawaguchi et al. ) have reported 5-year survival as high as 100% with a 10-year survival of 88%. The presence of M1 at diagnosis significantly reduces survival, with a median survival after detection of M1 of approximately 17.8 months (Stacchiotti et al. ). Metastasis is common, with rates varying from 13 to 90% across different studies (Saleh et al. ; Drilon et al. ), with the most common site of metastasis being the lungs, followed by bone, lymph nodes and soft tissue. Progression-free survival (PFS) and disease-free survival (DFS) are impacted by atypical such as atypical histology and positive surgical margins (Giner et al. ).
Recurrence-free survival (RFS) is also variable, with some studies reporting LR rates of 13–42% (Chiusole et al. ; Gusho et al. ; Kawaguchi et al. ; Giner et al. ; Oliveira et al. ), and a 26% rate of distal recurrence with a median time of 3.2 years (Drilon et al. ); it is also impacted by positive surgical margins. There is some conflicting data, with some studies suggesting higher cellularity correlates with worse outcomes while others do not find such an association; there is also some disagreement about how other factors like tumour size, location, and depth of the tumour affect disease-free survival, metastasis-free survival, and RFS. These differences may arise due to the differences in methodologies and patient groups. Study sizes have varied widely, ranging from single cases to studies with more than 100 patients. Some studies have focussed on patient groups with surgical resection of primary EMC, others have looked at the outcomes with surgical treatment and chemotherapy. Studies have also varied based on geographic location. Overall, the prognosis for EMC is complex and influenced by multiple factors. Further research is needed to refine risk stratification and improve outcomes for patients with this rare sarcoma. Exploratory analyses suggest EWSR1::NR4A3 may identify tumours with longer progression-free survival on pazopanib (ORR 18%, median PFS 19 months), although validation is required (Stacchiotti et al. ). ## Morphology
EMC consists of cords, clusters and trabeculae of polygonal to oval neoplastic cells arranged within a rich myxoid matrix (Fig.  ) (Oliveira et al. ; Harris et al. ). Despite its name, EMC does not exhibit true cartilaginous differentiation and is now classified as a mesenchymal tumour of uncertain differentiation rather than a conventional chondrosarcoma (Saleh et al. ; Lucas et al. ; Goh et al. ). The lobular architecture of the tumour is often demarcated by fibrous septa, giving rise to its characteristic growth pattern. Tumour cells have a relatively monotonous morphology, being uniform in size, with round to oval nuclei, occasionally showing nuclear indentations, grooves or inclusions (Meis-Kindblom et al. ). In more cellular regions, tumour cells may form solid sheets where the myxoid matrix is less prominent (Hisaoka and Hashimoto ). In these areas, epithelioid or rhabdoid morphologies may be observed, characterised by abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm and peripherally displaced nuclei (Oshiro et al. ). Dedifferentiated EMC variants have been described, showing increased cellularity, greater nuclear atypia and more aggressive behaviour (Lucas et al. ). While hyaline cartilage differentiation is typically absent, some tumours show areas of chondroid metaplasia, which may complicate the differential diagnosis. Mitotic activity is usually low, although high-grade variants show increased mitoses and necrosis (Antonescu et al. ).
 The main diagnostic challenge is to differentiate EMC from other myxoid soft tissue tumours, including myxoid liposarcoma (MLPS) (Yoshida et al. ; Okamoto et al. ), myoepithelial carcinoma (MEC) (Flucke et al. ) and chordoma (Purgina and Lai ; Folpe et al. ; Ulici and Hart ). MLPS shares the myxoid stroma and may occasionally show cartilaginous differentiation. However, MLPS is defined by the FUS-DDIT3 or EWSR1-DDIT3 fusion, in contrast to EMC, which harbours NR4A3 rearrangements (Flucke et al. ). MEC often expresses cytokeratins (AE1/AE3, KRT14) and myoepithelial markers (S100, GFAP, calponin, p63), whereas EMC is usually negative for cytokeratins (Flucke et al. ). Chordoma may histologically mimic EMC due to its lobulated growth pattern and myxoid stroma, but is distinguished by its brachyury positivity (Agaram et al. ). In contrast to the diffuse membranous CD99 of Ewing sarcoma, EMC is typically CD99-negative or only focally positive (Stacchiotti et al. ).
IHC staining is essential to confirm the diagnosis of EMC (Lenz et al. ). Among the positive markers, vimentin consistently shows strong positivity in EMC, highlighting its mesenchymal origin. Neuron-specific enolase (NSE) and synaptophysin are also frequently expressed, indicating neuroendocrine differentiation. INSM1 (Agaram et al. ), another neuroendocrine marker, shows characteristic nuclear staining in EMC (Goh et al. ). Other markers such as CDK4, SATB2 and HNK-1 show variable expression within EMC and may help in its identification. Some markers show variable positivity in EMC, including S-100 protein, which is detected in 20–58% of cases (Flucke et al. ). Epithelial membrane antigen (EMA) may be focally positive in some cases, further complicating the differential diagnosis. PGP 9.5 is expressed in approximately 40% of EMC cases and may be co-expressed with synaptophysin in some tumours (Oliveira et al. ; Harris et al. ). Negative IHC markers are critical in differentiating EMC from other neoplasms. Cytokeratins, including AE1/AE3, CAM5.2 and KL1, are typically negative and differentiate EMC from carcinomas and myoepithelial carcinomas (Flucke et al. ). EMC is typically GFAP-negative by IHC, which rules out glial neoplasia (Stacchiotti et al. ). In addition, EMC is consistently negative for KIT (CD117), which helps to differentiate it from gastrointestinal stromal tumours (GISTs) and other KIT-positive sarcomas (Agaram et al. ).  A study of 16 cases of EMC demonstrated the utility of FISH for identifying EWSR1 rearrangements, which are present in the majority of cases. However, NR4A3 break-apart FISH is the preferred diagnostic assay, as it detects all NR4A3 rearrangements regardless of fusion partner (Wang et al. ). Of the 16 cases studied, 15 were interpretable and 14 of these (93%) tested positive for an EWSR1 gene rearrangement. In all positive cases, more than 50% of the tumour nuclei showed the rearrangement, with a median of 80%. Break-apart NR4A3 FISH is the preferred single assay for EMC because it detects any NR4A3 rearrangement irrespective of partner; EWSR1 break-apart FISH identifies only the EWSR1-rearranged subset and lacks disease specificity. In addition to its diagnostic specificity, FISH is also a practical tool because it can be performed on routine FFPE samples. Unlike reverse transcription-polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR), which requires fresh or frozen tissue to detect specific fusion transcripts, FISH can be used on archived tissue samples, making it more accessible to pathology laboratories. While highly sensitive, RT-PCR has limitations because different EMC cases may have different fusion partners for NR4A3, requiring multiple primer sets for accurate detection. Break-apart FISH for NR4A3 is the preferred single assay because it detects any NR4A3 rearrangement irrespective of partner; by contrast, EWSR1 break-apart FISH identifies only the EWSR1-rearranged subset and lacks specificity across sarcomas. Another major advantage of FISH is its applicability to small biopsy samples, which is particularly important in cases where a limited amount of tissue is available for analysis. As EMC is often diagnosed using core needle biopsies, which only sample a small part of the tumour, FISH provides a robust method for detecting EWSR1 rearrangements even in small tissue fragments. This makes it an invaluable tool for early and accurate diagnosis, allowing appropriate treatment planning without the need for larger surgical resections solely for diagnostic purposes.

## Genetics and molecular biology
The hallmark genetic feature of EMC is the t(9;22)(q22;q12) translocation (Fig.  ), which results in the fusion of the EWSR1 gene on chromosome 22q12 with the NR4A3 gene on chromosome 9q22 (Hirabayashi et al. ). The NR4A3 gene is a nuclear receptor involved in cellular differentiation and apoptosis. The EWSR1::NR4A3 fusion produces a chimeric protein that has the transactivation domain of EWSR1 and the DNA binding domain of NR4A3, resulting in aberrant transcriptional activity that causes tumourigenesis (Hisaoka and Hashimoto ; Broehm et al. ). This fusion protein is oncogenic and leads to deregulation of downstream target genes, particularly those associated with proliferation, differentiation and survival pathways. Rearrangements involving the NR4A3 gene are the hallmark of EMC, most commonly fused with EWSR1, but other fusions with NR4A3 have been described, including TAF15::NR4A3 and TCF12:: NR4A3. Variant partners appear to shape phenotype rather than independently dictate outcome; in particular, TAF15::NR4A3 is characterised by larger tumours and rhabdoid or epithelioid morphology (Agaram et al. ; Warmke et al. ). The variant t(9;17)(q22;q11.2) yields the 5′-TAF15-3′-NR4A3 fusion (historically RBP56/CHN) in extremely rare cases of EMC with neuroendocrine features according to Harris et al. ( ). Other variant translocations including t(7;9;17)(q32;q22;q11) and t(9;15)(q22;q21) have also been noted which highlights the genetic heterogeneity of EMC (Sjögren et al. ). These and other genetic alterations have been summarised in Table  . Gene expression analysis revealed upregulation of genes involved in chondrogenesis, such as CHI3L1 and CHST1, as well as genes associated with neural-neuroendocrine differentiation, such as SCG2, NEF3, GFAP and GAD2 (Sjögren et al. ). Although transcript-level upregulation has been reported for some genes related to the nervous system, this does not imply GFAP protein expression by IHC. This suggests that EMC may have a dual differentiation pathway. In addition, the cells can show variable expression of synaptophysin, S-100 protein, epithelial membrane antigen and neuron-specific enolase, suggesting a neuroendocrine component in some cases (Fletcher et al. ). Microtubule-associated protein-2 (MAP-2) and class III beta-tubulin, components of microtubules, are also expressed in EMC (Hisaoka et al. ). It is currently unclear whether the TEC-containing fusion genes induce neural-neuroendocrine differentiation or whether this is an intrinsic feature of the progenitor cell of EMC. CHI3L1, which encodes the glycoprotein YKL-40, was found to be significantly upregulated. YKL-40 is involved in several cancer related processes including angiogenesis, inflammation and extracellular matrix degradation and may be involved in the pathogenesis of EMC (Hisaoka and Hashimoto ).
 

## Treatment in locally advanced disease
Surgery remains the cornerstone of treatment for localised and locally advanced EMC. Modern management is MDT-centred: image-guided core biopsy with radiology–pathology correlation, pre-operative MRI to map fascial planes and neurovascular relationships, and joint planning with plastic surgery for primary flap coverage when wide resection would jeopardise function; this approach reduces unplanned excisions and facilitates margin-negative, limb-sparing surgery in expert centres (Umer et al. ; Gronchi et al. ). The goal of surgery is complete resection with wide margins to minimise the risk of LR. Tumours considered resectable typically lack extensive vascular or neural invasion and do not involve vital structures, making en bloc resection feasible (Bishop et al. ). Wide local resection with negative microscopic margins remains the standard treatment for localised STS, consistent with established therapeutic approaches for localised adult STS (Stacchiotti et al. ; Daigeler et al. ; ESMO/European Sarcoma Network Working Group ). Where critical structures preclude a wide cuff, planned marginal excision after pre-operative RT is acceptable in sarcoma guidelines and can achieve durable local control with function preservation in experienced units (Umer et al. ; Gronchi et al. ). This is standard for all sarcomas larger than 5 cm, deep and intermediate/high grade lesions as reported in the European Society of Medical Oncology (ESMO) (Gronchi et al. ) and National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines (Gronchi et al. ; Mehren et al. ). All suspected cases should be referred to a sarcoma centre before incision; unplanned excision is associated with worse outcomes and usually mandates wide re-excision along oncological planes. EMC exhibits higher LR than many other STS despite adequate margins, particularly when surgery is used alone; in the largest series, 10-year local control was 63% with surgery alone vs 100% with combined surgery-RT (Stout and Verner ; Enzinger and Shiraki ; Stacchiotti et al. ; Saleh et al. ; Kawaguchi et al. ; Lucas et al. ; Paioli et al. ; Mahyudin et al. ). For pulmonary oligometastases, video-assisted thoracoscopic metastasectomy is increasingly preferred when complete resection is feasible, offering low morbidity and short hospital stay with 5-year survival around 50% in sarcoma series; selection requires an MDT with thoracic surgery and radiology (Stamenovic et al. ).
Key prognostic factors for LR include tumour size, previous unplanned excision and lack of adjuvant radiotherapy (Liang et al. ). Risk increases continuously with size—in particular at ≥ 10 cm—and non-extremity location is independently adverse (Brown et al. ; Gusho et al. ). Further studies highlight additional prognostic factors, such as deep tumour location and high histological grade, which are associated with a higher likelihood of LR (Zhuang et al. ). In addition, time to LR (TLR) is an independent predictor of overall survival, with early LR (< 12 months) associated with worse outcomes than late recurrence (Liang et al. ).
Resection margin status remains a critical factor in disease control, as positive surgical margins significantly increase the risk of recurrence (Kainhofer et al. ). When a natural barrier (intact fascia or periosteum) is present, a close margin on that barrier is acceptable; otherwise, it is reccomended to aim for ≥ 1 cm of healthy tissue or convert to a planned close margin after pre-operative RT. LR rates are significantly lower with R0 (complete resection, negative margins). In one study, only 2 of 24 patients (8.3%) with R0 resections had a LR (Drilon et al. ). In a series of 31 cases, the rate of local recurrence was significantly higher with involved margins than with free margins (58% versus 7%) (Giner et al. ). With R1 (complete macroscopic resection, positive microscopic margins) the recurrence rates are much higher representing 41.6% in one study (Gusho et al. ) and 25% in another (Drilon et al. ). On the other hand, R2 (incomplete resection) has the highest recurence rates (5 out of 7 patients in one study which represents 71%) (Drilon et al. ). In addition, larger tumours (> 10 cm), high-grade malignancy and high Ki-67 index (> 20%) are strongly correlated with poor prognosis and increased risk of recurrence (Yıldırım et al. ). P53 also plays a role (Giner et al. ). The other prognostric factors have been summarised in detail in the chapter Prognosis.
Despite a high rate of eventual distant recurrence after surgery, the available data to support improved outcomes with perioperative chemotherapy is very limited. In practice, this lack of evidence means that adjuvant chemotherapy is not part of the standard treatment for EMC (Chiusole et al. ; Drilon et al. ; Gronchi et al. ; ESMO/European Sarcoma Network Working Group ; Mehren et al. ). Even large trials of neoadjuvant chemotherapy in soft tissue sarcomas have included few, if any, cases of EMC, given the intermediate behaviour and rarity of EMC. Some patients with very high risk features (e.g. large tumour burden or high-grade histology) have received neoadjuvant chemotherapy on an individualised basis, but the results do not suggest a survival benefit. For example, in a multicentre review, 7 patients received preoperative chemotherapy; however, no complete pathological responses were observed and there was no significant improvement in long-term DFS or OS attributable to chemotherapy (Chiusole et al. ). Consistent with this, a recent SEER analysis concluded that chemotherapy (in the adjuvant setting or in any setting) does not confer a discernible survival benefit in localised EMC. Therefore, the current evidence does not support the routine use of neoadjuvant/adjuvant chemotherapy in EMC. Surgery (with RT if necessary) remains the definitive treatment for localised disease, and systemic therapy is generally reserved for unresectable or metastatic cases (Brown et al. ).

## Radiotherapy
While surgery remains the primary treatment modality, radiotherapy (RT) plays an important role in various clinical scenarios, including neoadjuvant, adjuvant, radical and palliative settings. Given the limited prospective data on EMC, most recommendations for RT are extrapolated from retrospective studies and case reports. Using data from the Surveillance, Epidemiology, and End Results (SEER) database from 1973 to 2012, the study identified cases of localised EMC originating in the soft connective tissues of the trunk and extremities (Kemmerer et al. ). Patients were included if they had undergone surgical treatment and/or EBRT. Inverse probability treatment weighting was used to adjust for confounding variables, while survival analysis was performed using weighted Cox regression and Kaplan–Meier methods, with log-rank tests to assess statistical significance. The primary outcome measure was CSS. A total of 172 patients diagnosed between 2004 and 2012 were included in the analysis. Of the 156 evaluable patients, 94% underwent surgery and 32% received EBRT. Propensity score weighting was applied to adjust for age, sex, race, tumour grade, T stage, N stage, receipt of surgery and anatomical site. The results showed improved CSS in patients who received EBRT, with survival rates of 97% versus 85% at 3 years and 94% versus 85% at 5 years (P = 0.01), at a median follow-up of 33 months. There was also a trend towards an overall survival benefit with EBRT (P = 0.06). Even after further adjustment for the type of surgical resection performed, the CSS advantage persisted, with survival rates of 97% versus 85% at 3 years and 94% versus 85% at 5 years (P = 0.02), with a continuing trend towards improved overall survival (P = 0.08). These results suggest that EBRT is associated with a significant CSS benefit in patients with localised EMC.
Neoadjuvant RT is primarily considered in cases where surgical margins are expected to be narrow or inadequate due to anatomical constraints (Fig.  ). In a case study by Improta et al. ( ), a patient with locally recurrent EMC of the shoulder underwent preoperative RT with a total dose of 50 Gy delivered in 25 fractions over 5 weeks. Histological examination after surgery showed no viable tumour cells, suggesting a complete pathological response. Also, a single institution series (Bishop et al. ) studied 41 consecutive patients with localised EMC, of whom 56% (n = 23) received preoperative radiotherapy with a median dose of 50 Gy (range, 50–50.4 Gy), 24% (n = 10) received postoperative radiotherapy with a median dose of 60 Gy (range, 60–65 Gy), and 20% (n = 8) underwent surgery alone. The median age was 53 years (range, 20–84 years) and the median follow-up was 94 months (range, 8–316 months). Ten-year local control was 90%, disease-specific survival 85%, DMFS of 69% and overall survival 66%. LR occurred in 12% of patients at a median of 75 months, with surgery alone having a significantly higher 10-year local failure rate (37%) than those treated with combined modality therapy (range, 0–10%, resulting in 63% vs 100% local control at 10 years, P = 0.004). On multivariate analysis, surgery alone conferred a significantly increased risk of LR (HR 12.7, P = 0.02), and LR itself was the only factor associated with worse DMFS (HR 3.9, P = 0.04). The findings support the use of both surgery and—often given neoadjuvantly—to reduce the likelihood of local failure and thereby reduce the risk of subsequent M1.
 Adjuvant RT is typically recommended for cases with positive or close surgical margins (R1 or R2 resections) or when tumours are located in areas with a high risk of recurrence. Pre-operative RT can also be used deliberately to facilitate nerve- or vessel-sparing surgery with a planned close margin on a robust anatomical barrier, an approach endorsed by STS guidelines and supported by the superior local control observed with combined-modality therapy in EMC (Gronchi et al. ). A retrospective analysis by Ogura et al. ( ) included 23 patients treated at a single centre, where three patients who underwent R1 resection followed by adjuvant RT experienced no LR. Of the three patients who underwent R1 resection (microscopically positive margin) followed by adjuvant RT, none experienced LR over a mean follow-up of 104 months. This suggests an effective role for RT in preventing LR when surgical margins are inadequate. In addition, one patient with an inoperable primary tumour received definitive radiotherapy (60 Gy) and achieved local control for 48 months before developing M1. In addition, three patients with metastatic disease received palliative RT (50 Gy each) and showed objective clinical responses with PFS of 20, 43 and 62 months, respectively. While adjuvant RT appeared to be effective in reducing the risk of LR after R1 resection, it did not significantly affect long-term survival. The 5-year, 10-year and 15-year LR-free survival rates were 89%, 62% and 21%, respectively, reflecting a trend towards late recurrence. OS at 5, 10 and 15 years was 91%, 84% and 84% respectively. The study highlights that larger tumours (> 10 cm) and metastatic disease at diagnosis significantly worsened prognosis. Furthermore, in a study of 41 patients (Bishop et al. ), 80% received combined modality therapy (CMT) with surgery and RT, while 20% received surgery alone. Adjuvant RT was given postoperatively to 10 patients (24%) with a median dose of 60 Gy, ranging from 60 to 65 Gy. This dose was higher than the preoperative RT dose, which had a median of 50 Gy (range: 50–50.4 Gy). The 10-year local control rate was 90% overall, but significantly lower at 63% for those who had surgery alone compared to 100% for those who received CMT (p = 0.004). Multivariable analysis confirmed that surgery alone was associated with a 12.7-fold increased risk of LR (p = 0.02, 95% CI 1.4–115.3). LR was also associated with an increased risk of M1, with a HR of 3.9 (p = 0.04, 95% CI 1.1–14.7). The 10-year DMFS rate was 69% and 32% of patients developed M1, mainly in the lung, at a median of 28 months. OS rates were 88% at 5 years and 66% at 10 years, while DSS was 100% at 5 years and 85% at 10 years. Despite high relapse rates, the disease was indolent, leading to prolonged survival even in the presence of metastatic disease.
In cases where surgery is not feasible due to tumour size, location or patient comorbidities, radical RT has been investigated as an alternative. A case report by Qiu et al. ( ) describes the patient, diagnosed with a 12 × 7 × 6 cm tumour in the right adductor magnus muscle, underwent surgical resection followed by multiple courses of radiotherapy to both primary and metastatic sites. Spinal radiation was initially administered with 30 Gy in 10 fractions to T10-L3, resulting in an estimated spinal dose of 36 Gy₂ at T9/T10. Later, new M1 at T7-T9 were treated with 12 Gy in 4 fractions, followed by an 18 Gy boost in 6 fractions, bringing the cumulative spinal cord dose to 65 Gy at T9/T10, while carefully managing the risk of radiation myelopathy. Lung M1 were treated with 40 Gy in 10 fractions with SBRT, achieving a partial radiographic response with a spinal cord dose of 26.4 Gy at T6-T8. Recurrent pelvic and limb M1 required multiple courses of RT, including 30 Gy in 10 fractions to L4–L5 and sacrum, 50 Gy in 10 fractions by SBRT to the right leg, and 35 Gy in 10 fractions to the right groin and left flank, resulting in a complete clinical response in the leg. Thoracic and clavicle lesions were also treated with 40 Gy in 10 fractions to the right paratracheal node, right chest wall mass and left lower chest wall mass, 45 Gy in 5 fractions to the left chest wall and 36 Gy in 9 fractions to a large left clavicle mass. Over 10 years, the patient underwent repeated radiotherapy and surgical procedures, significantly exceeding the expected survival of 17.8 months for metastatic EMC. The use of hypofractionated RT, delivering 3–5 Gy per fraction instead of the conventional 1.8–2 Gy per fraction, may have contributed to overcoming the intrinsic radioresistance of EMC. Despite the high cumulative radiation dose, the patient experienced excellent pain relief with no radiation-related spinal complications. Similarly, Honda et al. ( ) published a case of EMC of the pelvis was successfully treated with proton beam radiotherapy combined with intra-arterial chemotherapy instead of wide surgical resection. The 11 × 6 × 13 cm tumour was treated with three-dimensional conformal radiotherapy at 36 Gy in 10 fractions, followed by proton beam radiotherapy at 30.8 Gy in 11 fractions. Intra-arterial cisplatin (CDDP) was administered at 120 mg/m2 weekly during RT, but was reduced to 100 mg/m2 after two cycles due to grade 4 thrombocytopenia. After RT, intra-arterial carboplatin (450–650 mg per course) was infused every 4 weeks, with intervals gradually increased to 8 weeks over 29 months, for a total of 19 courses. The tumour shrank significantly to less than 1% of its original volume 7 years after treatment. The patient maintained a complete response with mild functional impairment, suggesting that definitive RT combined with intra-arterial chemotherapy is a viable alternative for inoperable cases or those at high risk of post-operative disability.
In cases where surgery is not feasible due to tumour size, location or patient comorbidities, radical RT has been investigated as an alternative. A case report by Takagawa et al. ( ) describes the patient, diagnosed with a 12 × 7 × 6 cm tumour in the right adductor magnus muscle, underwent surgical resection followed by multiple courses of radiotherapy to both primary and metastatic sites. Moreover, in one study (Takagawa et al. ), high-dose-rate interstitial brachytherapy (HDR-ISBT) was used as as a treatment for metastatic EMC in an 87-year-old woman. The treatment consisted of a regimen of 30 Gy in 2 fractions over 1 day per metastatic site, targeting lesions in the inguinal lymph nodes, breast and popliteal fossa. HDR-ISBT was chosen because the patient was unable to undergo prolonged external beam radiotherapy (EBRT), providing an alternative that balanced symptom relief and tumour control. Over a follow-up period of 41 months for the inguinal lymph nodes and 36 months for the breast and popliteal fossa, no tumour regrowth was observed at any treated site. The biologically effective dose (BED) of HDR-ISBT, calculated at 142 Gy (α/β = 4), was significantly higher than conventional EBRT regimens, which typically deliver BED levels insufficient for long-term control. The treatment resulted in significant regression of metastatic lesions with no serious late toxicity, and only grade 2 dermatitis was reported. These findings suggest that HDR-ISBT may be an effective treatment option for metastatic EMC, but further studies are needed to optimise dose and fractionation strategies. Furthermore, Filannino et al. ( ) reported that the combination of RT with trabectedin resulted in near-complete regression of metastatic EMC lesions, with overall survival extending beyond 41 months after metastatic diagnosis.

## Treatment of metastatic/unresectable disease
EMC represents a significant tendency for LR and M1. The rate of metastatic spread following radical treatment is significant. According to a retrospective cohort study, 40.8% of 49 patients treated with curative intent developed M1, with higher rates observed in patients who underwent R1 resection compared to R0 resection (Chiusole et al. ). Similarly, another study reported that 59% of patients eventually developed metastatic disease after initial local treatment (Brodsky et al. ). In terms of stage at diagnosis, 77–85% of patients have locoregional disease, while 15–23% have M1 at initial diagnosis (Wagner et al. ). These values suggest that although EMC is typically slow-growing, there is a high likelihood of late-stage progression.
The most common site of metastasis is the lung, followed by soft tissue, bone and rare sites such as the testis and breast (Lubana et al. ; Gupta et al. ). Pulmonary M1 remain the most common and are present in up to 50–90% of patients at some point during their disease (Filannino et al. ). The time to metastatic progression varies. In one study, the median time from initial diagnosis to M1 was 28 months (Bishop et al. ). In another case, metastasis was reported to have occurred 13 years after initial diagnosis (Yoshino et al. ). Median survival after metastasis has been reported to be approximately 45 months (McGrory et al. ). Following radical resection, 5-year event-free survival has been reported at 43%, with only 14% achieving event-free survival at 10 years (McGrory et al. ). Even in patients who have undergone complete resection (R0), M1 can occur in up to 15.4% of cases (Chiusole et al. ).
EMC grows slowly, but its slow progression suggests that it may gradually evolve into a more dangerous form, leading to multiple metastatic recurrences. A small retrospective study showed that patients who underwent complete surgical removal of their metastatic lesions had a 100% survival rate at 5 years; those who did not have complete removal had a 92% survival rate, supporting its indolent nature (Paioli et al. ). More than 80% of patients who underwent complete resection developed a new distant recurrence with increasingly shorter intervals between failures.
Although definitive evidence on metastasectomy for EMC is limited (Chiusole et al. ), some case reports and small studies suggest that surgical removal of metastatic lesions, particularly pulmonary M1, may be considered in selected cases. To reduce practice variability, we suggest: observe indolent, asymptomatic metastatic disease; use doxorubicin ± ifosfamide first-line for symptomatic or rapidly progressive disease per STS guidance; favour pazopanib after anthracycline failure in view of EMC-specific activity—objective response rate 18% and median PFS 19 months—and anticipate chiefly disease stabilisation with cytotoxic chemotherapy (disease control ~ 40–60%, partial responses uncommon) (Chiusole et al. ; Drilon et al. ; Stacchiotti et al. ; Gronchi et al. ). In one reported case (Mavrogenis et al. ), despite the presence of lung M1, extensive surgical resection of the primary tumour including the perineal mass and adjacent ischiopubic ramus was performed. The patient’s postoperative course was uneventful. One month after the initial surgery, a thoracotomy was performed and approximately 20 pulmonary nodules were removed. Histological examination confirmed the presence of EMC M1 in the lung tissue. Remarkably, the patient remained disease-free 2 years after surgery, with no evidence of LR or M1. Despite these findings, the role of surgery in the advanced phase of EMC has not been specifically investigated, and current treatment approaches are based on the general algorithm for STSs (Paioli et al. ). Due to the slow growth of EMC, local therapies such as surgery or ablation should always be considered, especially in cases of isolated M1. However, as metastatic EMC can remain stable for months or even years, the rate of disease progression should also be considered when deciding on surgical intervention. Stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT) may also be an option instead of surgery. In addition, a study analysing the long-term outcomes of EMC suggests that, given the slow progression of the disease, close monitoring of metastatic lesions without immediate systemic intervention may be appropriate in selected patients (Masrouha et al. ). However, there is no consensus on the survival benefit of metastasectomy and more studies are needed to evaluate its role in disease management.
Recent data suggest that chemotherapy can stabilise disease in some EMC patients, but high-grade responses are rare (Stacchiotti et al. , ; Wagner et al. ; Chiusole et al. ; Zhuang et al. ; Ogura et al. ). A retrospective Italian series of 10 molecularly confirmed cases of EMC observed 4 partial responses (40%) to first-line anthracycline-based chemotherapy, with a median PFS of approximately 8 months (Stacchiotti et al. ). In a 2020 multi-institution trial, first-line chemotherapy (mostly anthracycline-based) achieved a partial response in only 1 in 10 evaluable cases, but approximately 60% achieved disease control (partial response or stable disease) (Chiusole et al. ). Across all regimens in this series, the overall first-line disease control rate was 50% (Chiusole et al. ). Median PFS with first-line chemotherapy has been in the order of 5–9 months in most reports (Stacchiotti et al. , ). For example, Drilon et al. ( ) reported a median PFS of ~ 5.2 months with various chemotherapies. These modest PFS durations reflect the palliative (non-curative) role of chemotherapy in EMC.

## Systemic palliative therapy
The role of chemotherapy in EMC has been widely debated, with most studies suggesting limited efficacy (Patel et al. ; Ogura et al. ). Traditional cytotoxic chemotherapy, particularly anthracycline-based regimens such as doxorubicin and ifosfamide, have been widely used as first-line treatment options (Omote et al. ; Patel et al. ; Ogura et al. ; Bacchetti et al. ). Doxorubicin is typically given at a dose of 75 mg/m2 intravenously every 3 weeks, while ifosfamide is given at 9 g/m2 over 3 days every 3 weeks. This anthracycline-alkylator combination has been used as the standard first-line treatment for metastatic EMC. However, responses are generally limited to stable disease, with only a fraction of patient achievinsg a partial resptse (Bacchetti et al. ). PFS with this regimen typically ranges from 3 to 6 months, while median OS varies from 18 to 45 months, depending on the extent of M1 at diagnosis (Brodsky et al. ). Despite these regimens, the overall response rate is around 10–20%, with many patients experiencing stable disease rather than significant tumour shrinkage (Bacchetti et al. ). Despite these poor results, there are cases where chemotherapy has shown remarkable efficacy (Han et al. ). One such case was a 15-year-old girl with inoperable sacrococcygeal EMC who achieved complete remission after four cycles of high-dose ifosfamide (5 g/day for 3 days) and doxorubicin (100 mg on day 1). Following this regimen, the tumour completely regressed and the patient remained in remission for over 6 months. One study (Wagner et al. ) analysed the effectiveness of different treatments for EMC using data from the SEER database (1973–2016). It found that chemotherapy did not improve OS and was associated with worse outcomes in both univariable (HR: 1.90, 95% CI 1.11–3.27) and multivariable analyses (HR: 2.54, 95% CI 1.07–6.08 in surgically treated cases). No specific chemotherapy regimens or dosages were reported, but previous studies suggested a lack of response to standard cytotoxic agents. The 10-year OS was only 10% for metastatic disease, with a median OS of 48 months. Surgery was the only treatment associated with significantly improved survival (HR: 0.27, 95% CI 0.16–0.47), while radiotherapy showed no clear benefit. In the retrospective series by Chiusole et al. ( ) patients with metastatic EMC who received palliative chemotherapy had a shorter median OS (72 months) than those who did not receive chemotherapy (81 months). This probably reflects selection bias (patients with more aggressive disease are more likely to receive chemotherapy), but it underlines that chemotherapy is not curative in EMC. Median OS for patients with metastatic EMC is in the order of 5–7 years in modern series, and the disease often follows an indolent course even without immediate systemic treatment. In some cases, watchful waiting or delayed therapy is used for slow-growing M1 (Stacchiotti et al. ; Kawaguchi et al. ). Local treatments such as metastasectomy or ablation can be used for oligometastatic disease, sometimes resulting in long remissions. Therefore, the decision to start systemic chemotherapy in advanced EMC must take into account the rate of tumour growth and symptom burden. For small, peripheral nodules not amenable to wedge resection or in patients unfit for surgery, percutaneous image-guided ablation (cryoablation or microwave) achieves high local control for lesions ≤ 1 cm and may be used within an MDT strategy alongside stereotactic ablative RT (Efthymiou et al. ; Bourgouin et al. ).
Trabectedin, a marine-derived cytotoxic agent, has also been studied in EMC. This drug is administered intravenously at 1.5 mg/m2 every 3 weeks and has demonstrated prolonged PFS in selected patients (Morioka et al. ). The study (Morioka et al. ) included five patients diagnosed with EMC or MCS who were treated with trabectedin and three patients with MCS who were assigned to the BSC group. The median follow-up for the entire study population was 22.7 months. Median PFS in the trabectedin arm was 12.5 months (95% confidence interval [CI]: 7.4 months-not reached), compared to 1.0 months (95% CI 0.3–1.0 months) in the BSC arm. The six-month progression-free rate (PFR) in the trabectedin arm was 100%. Assessment of tumour response according to RECIST v1.1 criteria showed that one patient with MCS achieved a PR, while the remaining four patients in the trabectedin arm had SD. No patient in the trabectedin group had a PD as their best response. OS analysis showed a median of 26.4 months (range: 10.4–26.4 months) in the trabectedin arm. Two of the five patients in this group were alive at the final data cut-off. In contrast, OS was significantly shorter in the BSC group. The longest duration of trabectedin treatment was observed in a patient with MCS who remained on treatment for over 2 years and showed a tumour shrinkage of 58% at 11 months after enrolment. One case report (Filannino et al. ) describes a patient who maintained stable disease for over 12 months when treated with trabectedin in combination with radiotherapy. However, trabectedin remains an investigational option and is not widely accepted as a standard of care. One of the challenges in assessing the effectiveness of chemotherapy in EMC is the lack of large-scale clinical trials. Most data come from small case reports or retrospective analyses, making it difficult to draw definitive conclusions.
Targeted therapy for EMC remains largely experimental due to a lack of large-scale clinical trials. Most of the available data comes from case reports and small observational studies. The notable exception is the multicentre phase 2 study (Stacchiotti et al. ) that evaluated pazopanib (800 mg daily) in advanced EMC in 26 patients, 23 of whom met modified intention-to-treat criteria. The median follow-up was 27 months. Four patients (18% [95% CI 1–36]) achieved an objective response according to RECIST 1.1, 16 (73%) had stable disease and two (9%) experienced progression. Median PFS was 19 months (95% CI 11–27), with 12-month and 24-month PFS rates of 74% and 40%, respectively. The 12-month and 24-month overall survival rates were 96% and 90%, respectively. Molecular analysis showed better outcomes in patients with the EWSR1::NR4A3 fusion, while TAF15-NR4A3 cases showed a poor response. Transcriptional profiling linked pazopanib sensitivity to upregulated VEGF and Notch pathways. The most common grade 3 adverse events were hypertension (35%), increased liver enzymes (23%) and anaemia (4%), with one patient discontinuing treatment due to toxicity. Pazopanib demonstrated clinically meaningful antitumour activity in EMC—objective response rate 18% and median progression-free survival 19 months—with a prespecified exploratory signal of greater benefit in EWSR1::NR4A3-positive tumours; validation is required (Stacchiotti et al. ).
Immune checkpoint inhibitors such as pembrolizumab and nivolumab, which have shown efficacy in other sarcomas (Anastasiou et al. ), have not been systematically evaluated in EMC; isolated reports of responses to interferon-α exist, but these do not establish a role for immunotherapy. One case study (Rubinger et al. ) documented a significant and durable response to interferon-alpha-2b in a patient with metastatic EMC, with disease control maintained for 16 months. However, this response remains an isolated observation and no consistent objective response rates have been reported in larger cohorts. Without randomised trials, it remains unclear whether immunotherapy can provide a meaningful survival benefit, although it remains an active area of investigation.

## Conclusions and summary
EMC is a sarcoma driven by fusion and characterised by NR4A3 rearrangements, as well as an indolent yet metastasising course. The primary step in achieving favourable outcomes is to exercise control over the local disease. In the context of localised disease, complete excision with an R0 margin constitutes the definitive intervention. For large tumours, close or positive margins, or other high-risk features, the addition of radiotherapy is recommended. In the largest series, the 10-year local control rate was 63% with surgery alone versus 100% with surgery plus RT, with 10-year local failure at 37% versus 0% (Bishop et al. ). Neoadjuvant or adjuvant cytotoxic chemotherapy has not been demonstrated to enhance survival or reduce M1, and its utilisation outside of clinical trials is not recommended. In advanced EMC, anthracycline-based regimens have been shown to stabilise the disease. Across retrospective series, cytotoxic chemotherapy chiefly achieves SD in 25% for ≥ 6 months and in 41% for < 6 months with no objective responses—and a multi-institutional cohort reported ∼60% first-line disease control; population-based data do not show an OS benefit (Wagner et al. ; Chiusole et al. ; Drilon et al. ) The utilisation of cytotoxic chemotherapy should be reserved for cases where its use is intended for the management of symptoms or in instances of rapid progression, rather than as a standard treatment option. Anti-angiogenic tyrosine kinase inhibition has been identified as the most consistently effective systemic strategy. Variant NR4A3 fusions shape phenotype—TAF15::NR4A3 associates with larger, rhabdoid or epithelioid tumours—while exploratory analyses suggest longer PFS with pazopanib in EWSR1::NR4A3 disease—ORR 18%, median PFS 19 months (Agaram et al. ; Stacchiotti et al. ). Consequently, pazopanib is considered a suitable initial systemic treatment option for progressive metastatic disease, with other VEGFR-directed TKIs being evaluated on an individual basis. At present, there are no clinically validated agents that directly target NR4A3.
Due to the rarity of EMC, we recommend standardising care in line with the ESMO–EURACAN–GENTURIS and NCCN STS guidelines (Gronchi et al. ; Mehren et al. ). This should include an MDT-led work-up involving image-guided core biopsy and MRI mapping, prioritising R0 resection and anatomic barriers, and preoperative radiotherapy of 50 Gy in 25 fractions (or postoperative 60–66 Gy) where margins are threatened. First-line therapy should be anthracycline-based for symptomatic or rapidly progressive advanced disease, with pazopanib as the preferred post-anthracycline agent given its EMC-specific activity. Metastasis-directed local therapy should follow uniform MDT criteria. This protocol reduces unwarranted variation and enables consistent reporting across centres.
