## Abstract
Major oncovascular resections involving irradiated or infected groins often preclude standard in-line arterial or venous reconstruction because of hostile local conditions and high reinfection risk. In these complex scenarios, trans-iliac bone tunnel bypass (TIBB) represents an alternative extra-anatomic solution, providing a protected lateral route that avoids contaminated fields while ensuring durable limb perfusion or venous outflow. We describe the technical principles, perioperative management, and clinical applicability of TIBB through two representative cases. The first case involved an arterial reconstruction performed for groin blowout following neoadjuvant radiotherapy and multiple prior graft infections. The second case concerned venous reconstruction after en bloc pelvic oncovascular resection for leiomyosarcoma. The technique is based on retroperitoneal iliac exposure, creation of a 10-mm tunnel through the iliac wing, and lateral routing of a ring-reinforced polytetrafluoroethylene graft in a subcutaneous plane, distant from infected or irradiated tissues. In both cases, TIBB achieved satisfactory hemodynamic results, effective infection control, and durable patency. At mid-term follow-up, arterial reconstruction remained patent without reinfection, whereas venous bypass resulted in significant limb edema reduction and maintained graft patency. These experiences suggest that TIBB is a reproducible and effective limb-salvage strategy in selected oncovascular patients when conventional reconstructive options are unsafe or unfeasible.

Major oncovascular resections involving the groin or pelvis represent one of the most demanding scenarios in vascular surgery. The dual objectives are complete oncologic clearance and durable limb perfusion, often pursued in a hostile setting shaped by previous operations, chronic infection, and especially neoadjuvant radiotherapy (nRT). Although nRT improves local tumor control, it compromises tissue vascularity, predisposing to wound complications and infection. , Additional risk factors—tumor size >10 cm, proximal thigh or groin location, and contaminated fields—further increase morbidity. , Reported postoperative infection rates range from 1.9% to 37.1%, highest in large, high-grade tumors requiring flap coverage or prosthetic reconstruction. In a recent multicenter analysis, surgical site infections occurred in 23.2% of patients—mostly within 90 days—and were linked to longer operations, greater blood loss, and wide soft-tissue defects. When vascular reconstruction is added, infection risk rises further: among 14 patients undergoing major vessel reconstruction for soft-tissue sarcoma, graft infection and wound dehiscence were common, sometimes necessitating graft removal and reoperation. 
Beyond the oncologic setting, hostile groin revascularization remains a recognized challenge in vascular surgery, with reported vascular surgical groin wound infection rates of 0.5% to 10%. In such circumstances, conventional in-line reconstructions carry a prohibitive risk of reinfection, hemorrhage, and graft blowout, prompting the development of extra-anatomic alternatives.
Trans-iliac bone tunnel bypass (TIBB) has emerged as a valuable option in this complex scenario.

## How I Do It

## Preoperative considerations
Patient selection. Ideal candidates include patients with infected femoral grafts, groin blowouts, irradiated or nonhealing wounds, and those undergoing en bloc tumor resection with vascular involvement. Patients should have reconstructible inflow and outflow vessels.
Imaging. CTA with three-dimensional reconstruction is mandatory. We evaluate iliac wing thickness (≥10 mm preferred), plan the tunnel trajectory to avoid belt compression, and map distal targets. In venous reconstructions, magnetic resonance venography helps confirm outflow patency. Planning software can be used to simulate tunnel trajectory.

## Operative technique
Two representative cases highlight both the indications and the technical step-by-step execution of the TIBB. The description is intentionally comprehensive, aiming to provide a practical, reproducible reference for surgeons undertaking this challenging procedure.

## Patient positioning and field preparation
The patient is positioned supine on a radiolucent table with the ipsilateral leg slightly flexed and internally rotated. This positioning enables simultaneous access to both the retroperitoneum and the distal target vessels without the need for redraping. The entire abdomen, pelvis, groin, thigh, and leg are prepared and draped within a wide sterile field, ensuring maximal flexibility for potential proximal or distal revisions ( ).
 Normothermia is maintained using a warming blanket and fluid warmers. Systemic heparin (100 U/kg) is administered before clamping to achieve an activated clotting time greater than 250 seconds.

## Consent
Written informed consent for publication of clinical details and associated images was obtained from the patients.

## Case 1 – arterial TIBB for groin blowout

## Background
A 54-year-old man with an extraskeletal myxoid chondrosarcoma involving the right groin underwent nRT followed by wide en bloc excision of the adductor compartment, iliopsoas, external obturator, quadratus femoris, and pectineus muscles, with partial resection of the inguinal ligament. The external iliac and common femoral veins were occluded and resected without reconstruction due to extensive collateralization. The iliac and femoral arteries were preserved and protected using sartorius muscle transposition and mesh reconstruction of the inguinal ligament ( ).
 At 1 month, the patient developed a wound infection caused by methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus and Enterococcus, complicated by massive external iliac artery bleeding that required emergency prosthetic interposition grafting. The prosthesis was later replaced with a cryopreserved arterial homograft, which subsequently became infected and degenerated. Hemostasis was achieved using three Viabahn stent grafts bridging the arterial gap, but within 3 weeks, the patient developed acute limb ischemia due to iliofemoral thrombosis. A Fogarty thrombectomy was performed, two distal stents were explanted, and a contralateral great saphenous vein (GSV) interposition graft was constructed. Recurrent hemorrhage occurred at homograft–GSV anastomosis due to extensive infection, prompting a multidisciplinary decision to proceed with definitive extra-anatomic revascularization.

## Retroperitoneal exposure
A 10-cm right pararectal retroperitoneal incision was made. The peritoneum was mobilized medially to expose the external iliac artery, which was circumferentially dissected after ligation of lumbar branches. The ureter was identified and protected throughout the dissection. Vessel loops were placed for proximal control.

## Tunnel creation
The iliacus muscle was detached over approximately 4 cm to expose the internal surface of the iliac bone. A 10-mm cannulated drill bit was used to create a transiliac tunnel from the iliac fossa ( ) to the lateral thigh at a 25° caudolateral trajectory, emerging just above the anterior superior iliac spine ( ). Bone wax was applied circumferentially to reduce bleeding and to shield the graft from sharp bone margins. The tunnel’s patency and smooth trajectory were verified with a chest tube.
 

## Graft selection and routing
A 6-mm Gore ring-reinforced polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) graft was selected to prevent kinking within the bony tunnel. The graft was passed through the tunnel using a tunneler and routed in a gentle curve superficial to the fascia lata to facilitate postoperative palpation and duplex ultrasonography surveillance ( ).
Care must be taken to prevent injury to the tibial nerve during the lateral supragenicular approach, as it is the first structure encountered when accessing the lateral side.

## Anastomoses
The proximal anastomosis was fashioned end-to-side to the common iliac artery, just above the hypogastric artery origin, with a continuous 5-0 polypropylene suture. After flushing both proximally and distally, clamps were released in a controlled sequence. Distally, through a lateral thigh incision, the supragenicular popliteal artery was exposed, mobilized, and encircled. The distal anastomosis was performed in an end-to-side fashion with 6-0 polypropylene, ensuring correct graft orientation without torsion or redundancy. Completion angiography demonstrated brisk antegrade flow and no evidence of anastomotic stenosis. The estimated blood loss was 600 mL.

## Outcome
Postoperative recovery was uneventful. The patient completed 6 weeks of culture-directed antibiotic therapy and remained infection-free. At 12 months, the ankle-brachial index was 0.95, and computed tomography angiography (CTA) confirmed full graft patency with no evidence of stenosis or reinfection ( ).
 

## Case 2 – venous TIBB after pelvic oncovascular resection

## Background
A 70-year-old patient presented with a self-detected right inguinal mass associated with ipsilateral leg swelling. Contrast-enhanced computed tomography (CT) revealed a deep iliac fossa lesion extending into the inguinal region along the vascular axis. An ultrasound-guided core biopsy confirmed leiomyosarcoma. Staging chest CT demonstrated at least three right pulmonary nodules suspicious for metastases.
The multidisciplinary tumor board recommended three cycles of systemic chemotherapy with doxorubicin and dacarbazine. Following referral to our sarcoma reference center, histopathological review confirmed leiomyosarcoma G2 D2 M1 N2. Due to uncertainty regarding pulmonary secondaries, three additional cycles of neoadjuvant chemotherapy were administered. Restaging CT excluded distant metastases but confirmed a 30-cm hypervascular mass arising from the right iliac venous axis (common and external iliac veins), extending caudally into the obturator, common femoral, superficial, and deep femoral veins, with infiltration of the inguinal, adductor, and quadriceps compartments.
Finally, the patient underwent wide en bloc resection of right femoro-inguinal-iliac soft tissues with bilateral adnexectomy.
To prevent the onset of phlegmasia during the perioperative period, the patient underwent an extra-anatomic bypass performed simultaneously.
A more conventional Palma procedure was excluded due to extensive infiltration of the common, superficial, and deep femoral veins, which would have required harvesting a longer segment of the contralateral GSV, thereby increasing the risk of thrombosis. Additionally, performing a Palma procedure would have precluded the use of the vertical rectus abdominis myocutaneous flap for soft-tissue coverage.

## Tunnel creation
The iliac bone tunnel was created as described in Case 1, with particular attention to avoid posterior cortical breach given the large resection cavity.

## Graft selection and routing
Venous reconstruction was achieved using an 8-mm ring-reinforced PTFE graft, passed through the transiliac tunnel and routed into the lateral thigh subcutaneous plane ( ).
 

## Anastomoses
The inferior vena cava was partially clamped by a Satinsky vascular clamp at the level of the iliocaval confluence. The anastomose between the graft and the inferior vena cava was performed end-to-side using a continuous 5-0 polypropylene suture. Distally, the graft was anastomosed end-to-side to the above-the-knee popliteal vein, preserving major collaterals to optimize venous return. To enhance early graft flow and prevent thrombosis, two temporary arteriovenous fistulas (AVFs) were created: a side-to-side AVF at the superficial femoral level and a small branch-to-branch AVF at the popliteal level.

## Outcomes
Estimated intraoperative blood loss was 900 mL, requiring transfusion. Anticoagulation was started immediately postoperatively. Limb edema progressively improved over 6 weeks. At 9 months’ follow-up, duplex ultrasonography confirmed full graft patency with a peak velocity of 40 cm/s, minimal reflux, and marked reduction in limb swelling. The vertical rectus abdominis myocutaneous flap used for soft-tissue coverage healed primarily without complications ( ), as documented on CTA ( ).
 

## Discussion
This technical report highlights the role of TIBB as a practical and effective bailout strategy in oncovascular surgery, which represents one of the most demanding areas in vascular reconstruction. Extensive soft-tissue sarcoma resections and nRT converge to create an ideal setting for wound complications, infection, and vascular fragility. The deleterious effects of nRT on wound healing are well-documented: large series reported wound dehiscence in 35% of patients treated preoperatively, compared with 17% in those treated postoperatively (P = .01). , Tumor size greater than 10 cm and proximal location were also identified as independent predictors of complications in a separate cohort study. Even with prophylactic flap coverage, irradiated groins remain highly susceptible to infection, as demonstrated by catastrophic graft contamination despite aggressive soft tissue reconstruction in our patient. 
When infection and tissue loss preclude in-line reconstructions, extra-anatomic solutions become indispensable. The classic axillofemoral bypass, although familiar, is penalized by its long and tortuous path, prone to kinking and suboptimal flow. The obturator bypass offers a more protected, deep route, yet requires extensive pelvic dissection and carries substantial risks—bleeding, nerve injury, and graft contamination—particularly when the tumor involves the medial thigh or pelvic sidewall. In such settings, the TIBB emerges as a technically elegant and hemodynamically superior alternative.

## Technical considerations
The defining step of TIBB is drilling a 10-mm channel through the iliac wing to establish a direct conduit from the iliac to the popliteal artery. Compared with the obturator canal, this trajectory is shorter, straighter, and lateralized—crucial for avoiding irradiated or infected fields. As first detailed by Lounes et al, this geometry minimizes flow disturbance and facilitates duplex surveillance.
Ring-reinforced PTFE grafts are preferred to withstand compression within the osseous tunnel and maintain luminal patency. This choice, validated by contemporary data, outperforms plain PTFE or Dacron conduits historically used in obturator and axillofemoral bypasses.
In contrast to the series by Enzmann et al, where the distal anastomosis was performed to the superficial femoral artery in six patients, to the above-knee popliteal artery (PA I) in one, and to the below-knee popliteal artery (PA III) in another, with the GSV used as the most common conduit (six of eight cases), our approach differs both in graft selection and trajectory. After crossing the iliac wing, the graft is tunneled laterally in a subcutaneous plane above the fascia lata, rather than medially toward the thigh or leg as described by Enzmann and colleagues. This lateral route provides several advantages: it maintains a safe distance from the irradiated or contaminated groin, allows palpation and duplex surveillance along its entire course, and ensures a short, direct, and hemodynamically favorable path to the distal target vessel. This configuration effectively combines the accessibility of the axillofemoral bypass with superior hemodynamic performance due to its reduced graft length and straighter alignment. In our experience, this technical modification facilitates postoperative monitoring and early detection of graft complications, while maintaining durable perfusion even in previously infected or irradiated fields.
An end-to-side anastomosis to the common or external iliac artery offers robust inflow with minimal ischemia time. This approach is simpler and safer than the deep pelvic dissection required for obturator bypasses, avoiding injury to obturator vessels and nerves, which can cause significant bleeding or neuropathy in up to 15% of cases. 
The supragenicular popliteal artery represents the optimal outflow site—readily accessible, of suitable caliber, and remote from infected zones. In venous reconstructions, extension to the below-knee popliteal vein may preserve collateral flow and minimize edema.
Temporary AVFs can augment flow in venous reconstructions, reducing early thrombosis and improving graft maturation. These are ligated 6 months postoperatively, once stable patency is confirmed and complete healing of the surgical wounds has been achieved. Intraoperatively, meticulous hemostasis, activated clotting time maintenance above 250 seconds, and sequential clamp release are essential to minimize thrombosis and blood loss—typically 600 to 900 mL, less than that reported in obturator routes.

## Comparative and clinical implications
The TIBB offers a compelling alternative to traditional extra-anatomic reconstructions by directly addressing their inherent limitations. The shorter graft trajectory improves hemodynamic performance and reduces the risk of thrombosis by minimizing flow resistance. Its lateral, extra-anatomic course avoids previously irradiated or infected fields, thereby substantially decreasing the likelihood of reinfection—an especially critical advantage in complex oncovascular reconstructions.
Another important benefit lies in the graft’s superficial subcutaneous routing, which allows for reliable postoperative surveillance. The use of ring-reinforced PTFE conduits ensures mechanical stability within the bony tunnel, preventing graft collapse or kinking. Additionally, the straight-line alignment achieved through precise iliac wing drilling contributes to favorable flow dynamics by minimizing turbulence and optimizing distal perfusion.
In our experience, TIBB enabled complete removal of infected prior grafts, effective infection control, and sustained limb revascularization in hostile local environments. These attributes make TIBB a valuable and physiologically sound alternative for limb salvage in patients where conventional in-line reconstructions are contraindicated.

## Limitations and outlook
Despite encouraging outcomes, the evidence remains limited to small series and case reports. Broader multicenter studies are warranted to define long-term durability and refine technical details. Integration of biologic grafts may further reduce infection rates.

## Conclusions
TIBB represents a technically demanding but reproducible solution for complex oncovascular scenarios, where conventional reconstructions are unfeasible due to infection, irradiation, or extensive tumor resection. Each procedural element—precise tunnel drilling, lateralized routing, and reinforced conduit selection—acts synergistically to optimize flow, minimize infection risk, and ensure durable revascularization. For surgeons facing irradiated or contaminated groins, TIBB expands the reconstructive armamentarium, offering a feasible and physiologically sound alternative in limb-salvage vascular surgery.
