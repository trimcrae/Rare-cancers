https://orcid.org/0000-0001-9536-6504Dias e SilvaDouglasConceptualizationFormal analysisWriting - original draft , According to the 2020 World Health Organization Classification of Soft Tissue and Bone Tumors, there are more than 100 different histological and molecular subtypes, each with distinct prognosis, clinical behavior, and response to systemic treatment. 
Although surgery is the standard initial treatment, many patients present with locally advanced or metastatic disease, requiring systemic therapy. However, the heterogeneity of these malignancies makes it challenging to develop robust, evidence-based treatment strategies. Most of the current therapeutic approaches are not specific to the different histologies and do not consider the molecular profiling of tumors. For most advanced STS cases, standard therapy still relies on cytotoxic chemotherapy. Anthracycline-based regimens have been used for over 30 years and continue to play a significant role in the first-line seating for patients with chemo-sensitive tumors. However, many histological subtypes of STS are known to be resistant to cytotoxic chemotherapy, and, despite intensive regimens, the 5-year survival in metastatic disease ranges from 15% to 30%, with median overall survival of 1.5-2 years. , 
Recently, immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) have emerged as a promising therapeutic strategy for multiple malignancies, representing a major advance in cancer treatment. , PD-1 and PD-L1 antibodies, either as monotherapies or in combination with anti-CTLA-4, have demonstrated some level of antitumor efficacy in advanced sarcomas, particularly in specific STS subtypes, such as alveolar soft part sarcoma (ASPS), angiosarcomas (AS), undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcomas (UPS), and dedifferentiated liposarcoma (DDLPS). , However, the response rate remains modest, with fewer than 20% experiencing significant clinical benefit.
A key factor contributing to the low response of STS to immunotherapy is its inherently cold tumor microenvironment. This is characterized by poor immune cell infiltration and low expression of neoantigens, which limit the effectiveness of immune-based treatments. Consequently, overcoming these barriers remains a major challenge in optimizing immunotherapeutic strategies for these tumors. 
One of the mechanisms related to an immunosuppressive microenvironment is tumor angiogenesis. Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) has been studied in other malignancies, where it contributes to immune evasion. However, its precise impact on the STS microenvironment remains poorly understood. In many STS cases, high VEGF expression has been associated with chemotherapy resistance and poor prognosis, and tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) targeting VEGF have demonstrated activity in phase II and III trials, suggesting a potential avenue for therapeutic intervention. 
Based on these findings, combination therapies integrating VEGFR-TKIs with ICIs have shown promising results in tumors such as renal cell and hepatocellular carcinomas. These combinations work by simultaneously inhibiting angiogenesis and enhancing antitumor immunity, potentially converting a cold tumor microenvironment into a more immunologically active one. However, the true clinical benefit of this approach in STS remains uncertain. To address this gap, we conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis to assess the efficacy and toxicity profile of this combination in advanced STS.

## Methods
This study was registered on PROSPERO in August 2024 (protocol CRDID 571653) and adhered to the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines and the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions. , 

## Search strategy
We performed an electronic search of databases including PubMed, Embase, and Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials from inception to August 2024. We used the following search strategy: (“soft tissue sarcoma” OR “sarcoma”) AND (“Pembrolizumab” OR “Nivolumab” OR “Avelumab” OR “ipilimumab” OR “ipilimumab plus nivolumab” OR “Durvalumab”) AND (“axitinib” OR “Sunitinib” OR “Regorafenib” OR “Cabozantinib” OR “Pazopanib”). Two investigators (D.D.S. and L.S.P.) independently performed a literature search. We excluded duplicated studies after the preselection process. All publications that were not excluded after preselection were read in full to verify their eligibility further. The investigators then compared the results and discussed and corrected them as necessary.

## Eligibility criteria
To ensure optimal homogeneity within the sample, the inclusion criteria for this meta-analysis were as follows: (1) patients over 18 years old diagnosed with STS, except GIST; (2) prospective clinical trials; (3) studies evaluating combination therapy with VEGFR-targeted TKIs and ICIs; and (4) reporting at least one relevant endpoint of interest. Studies were excluded if they met any of the following criteria: (1) overlapping patient populations; (2) reviews, systematic reviews, meta-analyses, case reports, conference abstracts, animal studies, or laboratory-based research; (3) inability to access the full text; (4) inclusion of patients with gastrointestinal tumors; or (5) non-English publications. The search included articles submitted up to January 2024.

## Endpoints and data extraction
D.D.S. and L.S.P. extracted outcome data independently, and disagreements were resolved by consensus. The primary endpoints included the objective response rate (ORR), progression-free survival (PFS), and overall survival (OS). OS was defined as time from intervention until death of any cause or last follow-up; PFS was defined as time from intervention until the first occurrence of tumor progression or death of any cause; ORR was defined as a confirmed complete or partial response, according to Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST 1.1) assessment. In addition, we extracted the grade ≥ 3 adverse events (AE) as the safety outcomes of interest. Moreover, we performed a subgroup analysis including alveolar soft part sarcoma (ASPS).

## Quality assessment
The risk of bias in each study was independently assessed by 2 authors (M.F. and R.C.P.) using the Cochrane ROBINS-1 tool for non-randomized trials. Any discrepancies between authors were resolved by consensus or with input from a third author (F.M.). A risk of bias plot was generated using the ROBVIS tool ( ). A funnel plot to assess publication bias was not created due to the small number of included trials (fewer than 10), as per Cochrane guidelines. The quality of the included studies was assessed using the Methodological Index for Non-Randomized Studies (MINORS).

## Statistical analysis
We performed this single-arm proportion meta-analysis for continuous endpoints and reported them in percentage events were pooled using the mean difference (MD) with 95% CI. P values < .05 were considered statistically significant. Cochran’s Q test and I2 statistics were used to assess heterogeneity. Values of P < .10 and I2 >25% were considered significant for heterogeneity. Different doses of the same drug were grouped using Cochrane’s combining method. Finally, a subgroup analysis of ASPS histology was performed to investigate the source of heterogeneity. All analyses were conducted using R software (meta and metafor packages). A 2-sided P-value <.05 was considered statistically significant. To explore the influence of individual studies, leave-one-out sensitivity analyses were performed, sequentially excluding one trial at a time. Additionally, Baujat plots were generated to visualize each study’s contribution to overall heterogeneity and its influence on the pooled effect size. Statistical analysis was carried out using Brown’s University OpenMeta-analyst. DerSimonian and Laird’s random-effects model was used in all analyses. The results were presented as a pooled analysis in forest plots.

## Results
We identified 735 studies through our search strategy. After the removal of duplicates and exclusion by title and abstract, 15 studies were included for a comprehensive analysis. Finally, 8 studies met our eligibility criteria and all met a minimum quality threshold, with total MINORS scores ranging from 14 to 17 out of 24. The literature selection process for this systematic review adhered to the PRISMA guidelines, as illustrated in .
 Collectively, the studies enrolled 332 patients and multiple histologic subtypes, including ASPS, leiomyosarcoma (LMS), synovial sarcoma (SS), undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma (UPS), angiosarcoma (AS), and others. Most included studies focused on adult patients, with ages ranging from 19 to 92 years. The sex distribution across trials was not uniformly reported but included both male and female participants. All trials included were non-randomized, phase II studies, reflecting the exploratory nature of research in this therapeutic area and the rarity of the disease. Detailed characteristics of included studies are presented in .
 

## Progression-free survival
Four studies reported PFS outcomes and were included in the pooled analysis. The combined mean PFS was 7.07 months (95% CI, 5.70-8.44; P < .0001) ( ). However, between-study heterogeneity was high (I2 = 81.9%), largely driven by Broto 2020 and Liu 2022, as shown in the Baujat plot. Sensitivity analyses demonstrated robust results, with PFS estimates ranging from 6.5 to 7.6 months when individual studies were excluded (see for sensitivity and heterogeneity analyses).
 

## Overall objective response rate
Eight studies were eligible for the pooled ORR analysis. The overall ORR was 30% (95% CI, 15%-51%) ( ). Considerable heterogeneity was observed (I2 = 88.6%). Trials by Van Tine 2023 (79%) and Grilley-Olson 2023 (72%) reported the highest response rates, while Wilky 2019 (12%) and Cousin 2022 (8%) showed the lowest. Baujat analysis confirmed that heterogeneity was mainly explained by these outlier studies. Leave-one-out analyses confirmed the stability of the pooled estimate, with ORR values ranging from 23% to 34% (see for sensitivity and heterogeneity analyses).
 

## Subgroup analysis: alveolar soft part sarcoma
Four trials provided data for patients with ASPS. The pooled ORR in this subgroup was 73% (95% CI, 63%-81%), with no heterogeneity detected (I2 = 0%) ( ). All individual trials reported consistently high response rates (range, 55%-79%). Sensitivity and Baujat analyses confirmed the robustness of the result and the absence of influential outliers (see for sensitivity and heterogeneity analyses).
 

## Safety and adverse events
Five studies reported safety data suitable for pooled analysis. The incidence of grade ≥3 adverse events was 37% (95% CI, 30%-44%) ( ). Heterogeneity was low to moderate (I2 = 22.6%), and leave-one-out analysis confirmed stable results (range, 35%-40%). Baujat plots showed no single study had a disproportionate impact on heterogeneity (see for sensitivity and heterogeneity analyses).
 

## Discussion
Systemic treatment of advanced STS remains challenging, largely due to the histological and molecular heterogeneity across subtypes. The combination of ICIs and VEGF-targeting TKIs has emerged as a promising approach, particularly considering the limited efficacy of conventional therapies in advanced disease settings. 
To our knowledge, this is the first systematic review and meta-analysis evaluating ICIs combined with TKI for STS, providing valuable insights into a new therapeutic approach for a disease with limited treatment options. Our analysis included 8 phase II trials, encompassing 332 patients with various histological STS subtypes. The pooled ORR was 33% and the PFS was 7.1 months. Notably, in patients with ASPS, the ORR reached 73%, underscoring the potential of this combination to deliver meaningful benefit in this rare but immunoresponsive subtype and offering support for treatment personalization.
Regarding safety, the incidence of grade ≥3 adverse events was 40% in our meta-analysis. The most frequently reported toxicities included diarrhea, mucositis, hypothyroidism, ALT/AST elevation, hypertension, and maculopapular rash. While the toxicity profile was non-negligible, it was generally manageable and less severe than that of doxorubicin-based chemotherapy, suggesting acceptable tolerability.
Historically, doxorubicin monotherapy has remained the cornerstone of first-line therapy, with an ORR of approximately 14%-20%, PFS of 4.6 months, and OS of 12.8 months. More intensive regimens, such as doxorubicin plus ifosfamide, improve ORR to 26% and OS to 14.3 months but at the cost of high hematologic toxicity. Combinations like gemcitabine plus docetaxel, used in later lines, yield ORRs of 16% and OS around 12.1 months. Notably, most studies beyond first-line therapy report ORRs below 20% and PFS between 2 and 4 months, with no new regime demonstrating superiority over doxorubicin in terms of OS in the past 40 years. 
Inspired by advances achieved with ICIs and targeted agents in other malignancies, similar strategies have been investigated in advanced STS, particularly in later-line settings. However, their clinical outcomes have been less promising to date. ICIs have demonstrated limited efficacy in STS when administered as monotherapy, largely owing to the immunologically “cold” tumor microenvironment characteristic of most subtypes. In a pooled analysis of 9 clinical trials, Italiano et al. reported an ORR of 15.1%, which decreased to 9.8% when ASPS was excluded. Similarly, a meta-analysis by Saerens et al. confirmed these findings, with an ORR of approximately 15%. 
In the context of targeted approaches, VEGFR-TKIs, such as pazopanib and regorafenib, have also been widely investigated as therapeutic options for advanced STS. However, despite regulatory approval, their clinical efficacy remains modest, with an ORR of approximately 8%, median PFS of 4.0 months, and OS of 11.0 months. 
A promising strategy to overcome these limitations is combination therapy. The rationale for combining ICIs and TKIs lies in their dual mechanism: TKIs not only inhibit angiogenesis but also reprogram the tumor immune microenvironment by reversing VEGF-induced immunosuppression, enhancing T cell infiltration and activation. The clinical activity of this approach has been demonstrated in several phase II trials, showing an ORR around 30%. A meta-analysis published by Cao et al. further corroborated these findings, reporting an ORR of 28% with ICI–TKI combinations in STS. 
In our meta-analysis, the combination also outperformed ICI or TKI monotherapy. The benefit appears particularly notable in ASPS, where the ORR exceeded 50% in multiple phase II trials. The exceptional sensitivity of ASPS to VEGF-targeted TKIs is well documented, with median PFS values ranging from 10.8 to 18.2 months in prior studies. ASPS is characterized by the ASPSCR1–TFE3 gene translocation, which dysregulates hypoxia-inducible factor-1α and upregulates VEGF expression. This molecular context may explain the enhanced efficacy of ICI+TKI combinations in ASPS, particularly when compared with atezolizumab monotherapy, which yielded ORRs around 37%, below what was observed in our pooled analysis. 
Recently, the phase II REGOMUNE trial evaluated the combination of regorafenib (a multi-target VEGFR-TKI) with avelumab (an anti-PD-L1 antibody) in patients with advanced STS considered immunologically “cold”, defined by the absence of mature tertiary lymphoid structures (mTLS-negative). In this study, the ORR was only 11%, with a PFS of 1.8 months. Importantly, translational analyses revealed a significant increase in intratumoral CD8+ T-cell and B-cell infiltration, along with enhanced PD-1 expression during treatment, indicating that the combination was capable of remodeling the tumor microenvironment, although without translating into substantial clinical benefit.
These findings contrast with our results and underscores the critical role of histological and immunological context in determining treatment efficacy. While ASPS appears highly sensitive to ICI-TKI combinations, other subtypes lacking immunogenic features derive more limited benefit. The modest activity observed in mTLS-negative tumors suggests that, although anti-angiogenic agents may partially reverse VEGF-mediated immunosuppression, this intervention alone may be insufficient to generate durable clinical responses.
Nonetheless, our findings must be interpreted with caution. All included studies were non-randomized phase II trials, most with small sample sizes and predominance of patients with ASPS, which may have introduced bias into the results. The histologic diversity and wide variation in clinical behavior hinder generalized treatment approaches, and reliable biomarkers for response prediction in STS are still lacking, complicating patient selection.
Although TKI–ICIs regimens are not yet routine, there is a strong rationale to explore them in STS. Anti-VEGF TKIs can “normalize” aberrant tumor vasculature, reduce hypoxia, and reverse VEGF-driven dendritic-cell dysfunction, thereby improving T-cell trafficking and priming. Clinically, related combinations have already translated into survival gains in other solid tumors—for example, atezolizumab–bevacizumab–carboplatin–paclitaxel in metastatic non-small cell lung cancer, pembrolizumab plus platinum–taxane (± bevacizumab) in advanced cervical cancer, and atezolizumab–bevacizumab in unresectable hepatocellular carcinoma. In sarcoma, VEGF-TKI in combination with ICIs has shown clear activity in ASPS and prospective microenvironment-modulating signals in “cold” STS with regorafenib–avelumab. , Epigenetic therapies offer a complementary route to inflame immune-excluded STS: DNA-methyltransferase inhibitors can induce a dsRNA/interferon “viral-mimicry” program, and EZH2 inhibition up-regulates MHC-I/antigen-presentation and can overcome PD-1 resistance. , EZH2 inhibition already has clinical precedent in sarcoma. Ongoing translational studies are evaluating markers such as PD-L1 expression, CD8+ T cell density, mTLS, and IFN-γ-related signatures as potential correlates of response. , 

## Limitations
Possible important issues to note in our study are as follows. The high heterogeneity in results (I2 = 93.5% for ORR and 81.88% for PFS) indicates significant variation between the included studies, which may restrict the broader applicability of the findings and suggests a need for more homogeneous studies to confirm these results. OS data were limited due to insufficient events and short follow-up periods in the included studies, hindering a comprehensive assessment of long-term efficacy of the combination therapy.
The analysis focused on advanced STSs as a whole and on ASPS but did not consider other STSs subtypes that may respond differently to the therapy, due to the low number of patients per histology. This limits the applicability of the results to all STSs subtypes. Also, the review included only phase II trials, which, while providing important preliminary data, do not offer the same level of evidence as phase III trials, potentially affecting the reliability of the results.

## Conclusion
This systematic review and single-arm meta-analysis demonstrates that the combination of ICIs and TKIs yields clinically meaningful outcomes in patients with advanced STS, particularly in those with ASPS.
Importantly, while the combination is generally tolerable, a considerable proportion of patients experience grade ≥3 adverse events, reinforcing the need for careful patient monitoring and proactive toxicity management strategies.
Despite encouraging results, current evidence remains limited by non-randomized trial designs, small sample sizes, and histological heterogeneity. Future randomized controlled trials, biomarker-driven patient selection, and integrated translational studies are urgently needed to better define the role of this therapeutic strategy and to expand its benefit to broader STS populations.
