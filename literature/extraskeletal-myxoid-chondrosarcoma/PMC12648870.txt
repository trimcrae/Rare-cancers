## Abstract
SMARCB1 (INI-1) deficient vulvar neoplasms are rare tumors with scarce available literature. These tumors can be comprised of proximal type epithelioid sarcoma (ES), myoepithelial carcinoma (MEC), myoepithelioma-like tumor of the vulvar region (MELTVR), and myxoepithelioid tumor with chordoid features (METC). A subset of vulvar yolk sac tumors (VYSTs) also exhibit INI-1 deficiency. Unlike other vulvar germ cell tumors, some researchers speculate these to be of non-germ cell origin. Herein, we report a spectrum of INI-1 deficient vulvar tumors comprising VYST (1 case), ES (1 case) and MELTVR (2 cases) which showed variable histomorphological features and concomitant lack of INI-1 expression on immunohistochemistry. The clinical course ranges from indolent to aggressive and all tumors warrant close clinical follow up. Management includes surgical excision with or without adjuvant therapy. Recognition of these unique neoplasms can aid in refining the classification scheme for such uncommon vulvar tumors.
Keywords: Epithelioid sarcoma, INI-1, MELTVR, SMARCB1, Vulva, Yolk sac tumor

## Introduction
The INI1 (SMARCB1/BAF47/SNF5) gene is a member of the Switch/sucrose nonfermentable (SWI/SNF) chromatin-remodeling complex which is involved with DNA transcription. Biallelic inactivation of this gene results in loss of the gene product that is normally expressed in all human tissues. Such a loss is easily recognized by performing immunohistochemistry (IHC) for INI-1 [ ]. Loss of INI-1 expression may be partial or complete and is seen in a host of neoplasms ranging from mesenchymal to even some epithelial malignancies [ ]. Mesenchymal neoplasms include both benign tumors like epithelioid schwannoma to malignant neoplasms such as malignant rhabdoid tumors, epithelioid sarcomas (ES), epithelioid malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumors (EMPNST), myoepithelial carcinomas (MEC) and extraskeletal myxoid chondrosarcomas (EMC). Soft tissue neoplasms with SMARCB1/INI-1 loss are typically characterized by the presence of an epithelioid/rhabdoid morphology. In most of these cases, an aggressive treatment plan is pursued.
INI-1 deficient vulvar neoplasms are rare. This subgroup most commonly encompasses proximal type ES, followed by MEC, then newly described entities like myoepithelioma-like tumor of the vulvar region (MELTVR) and myxoepithelioid tumor with chordoid features (METC). Unlike other INI-1 deficient tumors, these emerging neoplasms follow an indolent to intermediate course. They warrant an accurate diagnosis to most optimally guide patient management.
Herein, we describe four cases of INI-1 deficient vulvar neoplasms which include an ES, a case of vulvar yolk sac tumor (VYST) and two cases of MELTVR. One of these MELTVR cases has been previously reported [ ].

## Case details

## Case 1
 A 20-year-old nulliparous girl presented with a vulvar mass that had been bothering her for 15 days. On examination, the right upper labia majora showed induration with no palpable inguinal lymph nodes. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) showed subcutaneous fat stranding in the vulvar region along with bilateral polycystic ovaries. Positron emission tomography-computed tomography (PET- CT) scan showed a fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) avid irregular soft tissue thickening involving the vulvar vestibule, labia minora, and part of right labia majora with few mildly avid mesenteric lymph nodes. She was also found to have bilateral bulky ovaries with heterogeneously increased tracer uptake, suggestive of polycystic ovaries. Serum AFP level was slightly raised (77.25 ng/ml) while all other serum tumor markers were within normal limits.
Histopathological examination of the incisional biopsy showed a large infiltrative tumor of the vulva. This lesion involved the dermis as well as deeper tissue layers while the overlying epidermis appeared unremarkable. The tumor cells were arranged in sheets, solid nests, micropapillary patterns, and microcystic patterns. Their cytoplasmic appearances ranged from eosinophilic to clear with large vesicular nuclei having conspicuous nucleoli. Schiller-Duval bodies were also identified with focal extracellular mucin pools (Fig.  a-c). Lymph-vascular emboli and areas of necrosis were also present.
 On IHC, tumor cells diffusely expressed CK (clone composed of AE1/AE3 cocktail), SALL4, and Glypican-3 while being negative for CK7, CK20, CD30, ER, PR, SOX2, and PAX8. EMA showed focal staining. The case was diagnosed as vulvar yolk sac tumor. Subsequently, IHC for INI-1 showed loss of expression in the tumor cells (Fig.  d-g). Interphase FISH analysis was performed using a locus-specific probe to ETV6 (12p13) and a centromere 12 (CEP12) probe which showed 3 ETV6 signals and 2 CEP12 signals in 35% (35/100) of the scored tumor nuclei. This pattern indicates a relative gain of 12p (consistent with isochromosome 12p or other 12p amplification) and supports a germ-cell tumor genotype.
The patient underwent Bleomycin-Etoposide-Platinum (BEP) based chemotherapy, later changed to Vinblastine-ifosfamide-cisplatin (VeIP) regimen after 5 months due to local recurrence. It was then switched to radiotherapy (internal beam followed by systemic) 7 months later due to another recurrence. The patient remained disease free for a short interval, however, after only one month, she was found to have rising AFP levels. The subsequent PET-CT showed progressive disease. After a multidisciplinary tumor board, the patient was started on Gemcitabine-Oxaliplatin based chemotherapy and currently remains on treatment. She is one year and six months out from her initial presentation.

## Case 2
 A 22-year-old female presented with a painless lump in the mons pubis with gradual development of associated pain over 15 days. Following incision and drainage at an outside facility, the drained tissue was reported as a poorly differentiated carcinoma with round cell morphology. The patient was referred to our hospital with a non–healing wound. MRI revealed a large ill-defined hypoechoic soft tissue lesion measuring approximately 9.3 cm X 9.1 cm X 5.7 cm. The tumor was centered within the subcutis involving the mons pubis and left labia while abutting the abdominal muscles posteriorly. Inguinal and pelvic lymphadenopathy was noted and the largest lymph node measured 3.4 cm x 2.6 cm. A well-defined hypodense area measuring 4.4 cm x 4.4 cm was also seen in the right adnexal region. PET-CT showed a metabolically active intramuscular deposit in the right proximal anterior thigh, along with mild uptake in the bone marrow of axial and appendicular skeletons in addition to local disease.
On examination, a hard nodular mass was palpable in the lower abdomen and mons, extending superiorly to bilateral inguinal regions. All serum tumor markers were within normal limits.
Excisional biopsy showed a skin covered tissue with an infiltrative tumor involving the dermis and deeper tissue. Microscopically, the tumor showed an undifferentiated round to epithelioid cell morphology arranged in diffuse sheets and lobules. Areas of tumor necrosis with increased mitosis and apoptosis were also noted (Fig.  a-c).
 On IHC, the tumor cells showed patchy CK and EMA expressions with diffuse positivity for SALL-4, Glypican 3, and SOX2. Immunostains for LCA, CD20, NKX2.2, SOX10, Desmin, SMA, Myod1, Myogenin, p40, TdT, CD34, WT1, NUT, PAX8, ERG1, AFP, and CD117 were negative. CD99 showed a heterogenous membranous expression. Focal S100, Cyclind-1, and Heppar-1 expressions were also seen. BRG1 expression was retained, while INI-1 expression was lost in the tumor cells (Fig.  d-h). A final diagnosis of SMARCB1 (INI-1) deficient vulvar neoplasm favoring proximal type epithelioid sarcoma was rendered. The patient underwent palliative chemotherapy and was subsequently lost to follow up after five months of treatment.

## Case 3
 A 47-year-old premenopausal lady presented with pain and swelling in the perineum for one year. On examination, a mobile swelling measuring 3 cm x 3 cm was noted in the subcutaneous region of her right labia majora, with intact overlying skin. There were no palpable lymph nodes. High Risk Human papilloma virus (HPV) assay was negative and PET-CT showed localized disease.
The patient underwent a wide local excision of the vulvar mass. This showed a 4.6 cm circumscribed encapsulated lobulated tumor with a solid grey-white appearance and focal yellowish areas involving the subcutaneous fat. On microscopic examination, the tumor was composed of solid sheets of cells with a predominant epithelioid to rhabdoid morphology and focal areas of spindling (Fig.  a, b). The cells showed vesicular nuclei, prominent nucleoli, moderate-sized cytoplasm and increased mitotic activity (~ 10–12/mm2). Scattered histiocytic giant cells, few foamy histiocytic collections, and cholesterol cleft formations were also seen. The stroma appeared myxoid at places with peripheral lymphoid aggregates (Fig.  c). Focal necrosis was noted (~ 5–10%), however, no significant nuclear pleomorphism was seen. No lympho-vascular or perineural invasion was seen. The inked margins were free of tumor.
 Morphologic differentials included poorly differentiated carcinoma, malignant rhabdoid tumor, melanoma and sarcomas with an epithelioid morphology including epithelioid sarcoma. On IHC, the tumor cells expressed EMA (strong patchy expression), SMA and focal SMMH, while being negative for CK, CD34, CA125, S100, Desmin, p40, SOX10, ERG1, SALL4, GFAP, INSM1, and Myod1. GATA3 showed a heterogenous positive expression. Cathepsin showed patchy positivity and calponin also showed focal positivity. ER and PR showed strong positive expression in 95% of tumor cells. INI-1 showed complete loss of expression in the tumor cells (Fig.  d-g).
In view of strong and diffuse ER and PR immunoexpression with concomitant lack of CK and CD34, a diagnosis of Myoepithelioma-like tumor of the vulvar region (MELTVR) was favored and a close clinical follow up was advised on account of significant mitosis and focal necrosis. Following a multidisciplinary tumor board discussion, the patient was planned for regular surveillance with no adjuvant therapy and remains disease free post one year of follow up.

## Case 4
 A 34-year-old lady presented with an insidious onset pubic swelling which was progressively increasing in size for the last 1.5 years. On examination, a round 3 cm x 3 cm, firm, mobile and non-tender swelling was palpated above the pubic region. Ultrasonography showed a well-defined heterogenously hypoechoic lesion in the subcutaneous plane and was initially reported to likely be a trichilemmal cyst. Excision showed a pseudo-encapsulated soft tissue mass measuring 3.5 cm x 3 cm x 2 cm. Microscopic examination showed a circumscribed tumor with spindled to epithelioid cells having vesicular nuclei with prominent nucleoli (Fig.  a-c). Intermingled mixed inflammatory cell infiltrate was present. There was no evidence of any significant atypia, mitosis, or necrosis. No lympho-vascular or perineural invasion was noted.
 On IHC, the tumor cells expressed EMA (strong and diffuse), H-Caldesmon, ER, Calponin, and Cathepsin (focal, heterogenous expression), while being negative for CK, CD31, CD34, Desmin, ALK-1 S100, SMA, WT-1, and TLE-1. INI-1 expression was lost (Fig.  d-g). A final diagnosis of MELTVR was rendered. The patient remains disease free at six months post surgery.

## Discussion
Mutations in the subunits of switch/sucrose nonfermentable (SWI/SNF) chromatin-remodeling complex have been identified in various female genital tract malignancies. This includes undifferentiated and de-differentiated endometrial carcinomas, uterine sarcomas, small cell carcinomas of the ovary (hypercalcemic type), and clear cell carcinomas of the ovary [ ]. The SMARB1 (INI-1/BAF47) gene located on chromosome 22q11.2 is a subunit of the SWI/SNF complex, which directly influences gene expression. Cytogenetic tests and molecular analyses show 22q aberrations, deletions, or mutations of the SMARCB1 gene in these tumors [ ].
SMARCB1 (INI-1) deficient vulvar neoplasms are infrequent tumors with an unknown exact incidence due to their rarity. Proximal type epithelioid sarcoma (ES) is the most common tumor, followed by myoepithelial carcinoma (MEC). Newly described entities, like myoepithelioma-like tumors of the vulvar region (MELTVR) and myxoepithelioid tumors with chordoid features (METC) are the least common [ , ]. These neoplasms are known to occur in young to middle aged women (median age 41) and are associated with local recurrence and distant metastasis [ ]. The patients in this study had an age range of 20 to 47 years.
Epithelioid sarcoma is a malignant mesenchymal neoplasm exhibiting partial or complete epithelioid morphology with two clinicopathological subtypes: classic (or distal) and proximal (large cell) subtype. ES of the vulva mostly occurs as the proximal subtype, showing a diffuse or multinodular infiltrative growth pattern with a predominant epithelioid to rhabdoid morphology. It is a rare albeit well described entity with 57 cases currently described in the literature [ , ]. It follows an aggressive clinical course with metastatic potential. According to the World Health Organization (WHO) blue books, the diagnostic criteria include expression of epithelial markers like keratin or EMA with variable extent and intensity and loss of INI-1 [ ].  showed these features along with necrosis and significant mitosis, fulfilling these essential criteria. CD34 positivity is seen in up to 70% of ES cases and can further substantiate the diagnosis when present. Our case was negative for CD34 and instead showed positivity for SALL4, Glypican 3, and SOX2. Though expression of germ cell markers has been documented in INI-1 deficient vulvar tumors including ES [ ], diffuse expression, as seen in our case, is exceptional. The lack of typical reticular/microcystic pattern, which is seen in most of the vulvar YSTs [ , ], lends credence to the diagnosis of ES.
Our patient had metastatic disease at presentation and hence underwent palliative treatment and was lost to follow-up after five months of therapy.
Extrarenal rhabdoid tumors of the vulva have essentially been reclassified as ES (proximal type) of the vulva in recent times [ ]. MEC is a close differential of ES with overlapping features on both morphology and immunohistochemistry. INI-1 loss is seen in 50% of MECs. In addition to expression of epithelial markers, variable positivity for ME markers (S100, SMA, p63, calponin, GFAP, SMA) is noted [ ]. No significant ME marker expression was seen in our reported case of ES ( ).
The other morphologic differentials at this site include a poorly differentiated squamous cell carcinoma (SCC), melanoma, malignant perivascular epithelioid cell tumor (PEComa), malignant glomus tumor, epithelioid malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumor (MPNST), epithelioid leiomyosarcoma (LMS), and a cellular variant of extra skeletal myxoid chondrosarcoma (EMC). The lack of p40, melanocytic markers and myogenic markers ruled out SCC, melanoma, PECOMA, glomus tumor and epithelioid LMS. Moreover, none of these tumors are associated with an INI-1 loss. Epithelioid MPNST shows INI-1 loss, however, complete absence of SOX10 with insignificant S100 expression precludes this diagnosis. Cellular EMC can exhibit a high-grade morphology with epithelioid features and INI-1 loss, however, the absence of any myxoid stroma or significant expression of S100/CD117 together with the presence of epithelial marker expression ruled it out.
MELTVR is a recently described, emerging entity with few cases reported in literature. This tumor is characterized by an indolent course and typically presents with a circumscribed mass with myoepithelioma-like morphology, lobular pattern, and low mitotic activity on histopathology. INI-1 loss and EMA positivity, together with ER and PR expression along with concomitant lack of CK and CD34 on IHC, is characteristic for this tumor [ – ]. Cathepsin K and calponin positivity are also noted, however, these markers alone are not specific. Yoshida et al. [ ] in their cases of MELTVR reported negative CK in 7/9 cases, with focal expression only noted in 2/9 cases. Additionally, all of their described cases were negative for S100 and CD34.
 showed the archetypal morphology with low mitosis and a circumscribed nature, whereas  showed some concerning features such as significant mitosis and nuclear atypia. Despite these findings, the overall clinical profile (long standing history), morphology (circumscribed encapsulated mass) and immunophenotype (strong ER, PR expression and EMA positivity with concomitant lack of CK and CD34) favored a diagnosis of MELTVR over ES.
Few researchers have described cases of MELTVR exhibiting high grade nuclear atypia, significant mitosis, and infiltrative borders [ ]. The lack of recurrence or metastasis with an indolent course along with other pathological features proved crucial in designating them as MELTVR. Both of our cases of MELTVR remain disease free at the time of last follow-up, although the follow-up durations of six months and one year, were limited.
METC is an INI-1 deficient tumor with an intermediate prognosis and is believed to encompass the category of MELTVR by some authors. The largest study currently in the literature characterized these tumors as occurring in the puboinguinal region with myoepithelioma-like morphology and expression of Brachyury, ER, PR, and EMA [ ].
To add to this spectrum, vulvar yolk sac tumors (YSTs) are also beginning to be recognized to be INI-1 deficient. Traditionally, these rare extragonadal germ cell tumors were believed to arise from germ cells in the vulva that persisted due to an error in migration [ ]. Recent studies, however, postulate vulvar YST as variants of SMARCB1-deficient tumors having YST-like morphology and a non-germ cell origin [ ]. Nevertheless, primary vulvar YSTs are usually seen in young-to-middle-aged women and follow an aggressive clinical course. Recurrence and metastasis confer a poor prognosis. They also show poor response to chemotherapy compared to their gonadal counterparts [ , ]. YSTs express Glypican-3 & AFP as specific markers, along with the germ cell marker SALL4. YST-like morphology has also been reported in other INI-1 deficient tumors, like renal medullary carcinomas and 25% of INI-1 deficient sinonasal carcinomas [ , ].
Kolin et al. analyzed 3 cases of vulvar YSTs (43, 32, and 25 years of age). They all exhibited classical morphology of YST with expression of SALL4, CK, and Glypican 3, along with loss of INI-1 [ ]. Targeted molecular study, done in two of these cases, showed two copy deletions of SMARCB1 in both, with no genomic alteration typical of YST. The third case underwent FISH for isochromosome 12p which was negative.
Hammer et al. [ ] studied ten cases of SMARCB1-deficient vulvar neoplasms, with a median age of 41 years. These included seven cases of ES, one of MEC and two of MELTVR. All cases of ES showed the characteristic morphology with sheets of large epithelioid cells and necrosis. Osteoclast-like giant cells were seen in one case. Both MEC and MELTVR cases showed spindled to epithelioid cells in a myxoid stroma, however, the MELTVR cases were typically well circumscribed tumors. All cases showed loss of INI-1 with significant expression of germ cell markers such as SALL4, OCT3/4, and Glypican-3 in areas with YST-like morphology. This led the authors to conclude that vulvar YSTs do not represent true germ cell neoplasia and are simply somatically derived SMARCB-1 deficient neoplasms, similar to other researchers [ ]. These findings are discordant with the case of VYST included in the current study which showed a relative gain of 12p (ETV6) signals in comparison to CEP12, in 35% of nuclei, consistent with isochromosome 12p or partial 12p gain favoring a germ cell origin. The pathogenesis of VYST thus remains questionable and needs further research.
In conclusion, we report four cases of SMARCB1 (INI-1) deficient tumors of the vulva including ES, VYST, and MELTVR. These cases offered differing clinicopathological features that can be utilized to refine a differential diagnosis which includes poorly differentiated carcinoma, melanoma and various other mesenchymal neoplasms with an epithelioid/rhabdoid morphology. The vulvovaginal area is home to a distinctive set of mesenchymal neoplasms, including the newly described entity of INI-1 deficient vulvar tumors. These lesions have evolved to include MELTVR and METC which show an indolent to intermediate prognosis and do not warrant adjuvant therapy. SMARCB1 (INI-1) deficient tumors of the vulva are underdiagnosed due to rarity and recognition can aid in refining the classification scheme for such uncommon vulvar tumors. Making a distinction between these neoplasms and other neoplasms is important because of different treatment strategies depending upon their biological behaviour. A high clinical index of suspicion with awareness about these entities together with knowledge of their characteristic morphological features and application of ancillary testing including a panel of immunohistochemistry can aid in separating them from other common differentials at this site.
