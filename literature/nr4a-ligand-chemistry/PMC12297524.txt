Using c-Fos mapping and machine learning, we identified the claustrum (CLA), a subcortical region reciprocally connected with the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), as key mediators of both METH taking and seeking in self-administering male rats. Chemogenetic inhibition of CLA suppressed both drug consumption and cue-induced reinstatement, while ACC inhibition selectively reduced drug-seeking. Circuit tracing and manipulation revealed that the CLA-ACC circuit supported drug-taking, whereas the ACC-CLA circuit was specifically recruited during drug-seeking. Activity-dependent labeling showed that ACC ensembles activated by cues overlapped with those engaged during prior drug use. These findings suggest that CLA drives METH reward through the ACC, while the ACC gains cue salience and feeds back to CLA, reinforcing relapse. Targeting this bidirectional CLA-ACC circuit may provide novel therapeutic strategies for treating METH addiction.

Here, authors show both CLA and ACC as key mediators of methamphetamine (METH) use and relapse. Specifically, the CLA → ACC circuit modulates intake, while reciprocal ACC → CLA circuit drive cue-induced relapse, revealing a maladaptive positive feedback loop that perpetuates METH addiction.

## Introduction
Methamphetamine (METH), as a major psychostimulant, produces euphoria, enhances attention, and elevates arousal, and its use has surged in many countries over the past decades . METH use disorder (MUD) is a chronic and debilitating condition that significantly contributes to the global burden of disease. It is characterized by cycles of compulsive drug-taking and drug-seeking, with relapse rates ranging from 40% to 60% within one year, resulting in severe health, economic, and criminal justice consequences , . Despite the significant global impact of MUD, effective therapeutic interventions remain scarce.
Addiction as a recurring three-stage cycle—binge, withdrawal, and craving—where the primary neural systems involved shift from the mesocorticostriatal DA system during drug use to the prefrontal glutamatergic system during drug-seeking . METH intake increases extracellular dopamine (DA) and inhibits its reuptake, thereby enhancing DA signaling within the mesocortical and mesolimbic pathways , . This process induces long-lasting neurobiological changes in the brain’s reward system, where cues associated with drug-taking become strongly tied to its pleasurable effects, gradually acquiring greater incentive salience . These cues provoke intense drug cravings after withdrawal, raising the risk of relapse. Now, two parallel projections from the prelimbic (PL) region of the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) to the nucleus accumbens (NAc) core and from the infralimbic (IL) region of the mPFC to the NAc shell mediate cue-induced METH-seeking behavior . Emerging research has identified the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) as a key region involved not only in drug-seeking behaviors but also potentially in drug-taking processes . As part of the salience network, the ACC regulates attention, goal setting, emotion, and motivation-cognitive domains associated with cue-induced craving. Both preclinical and clinical evidence showed that ACC activity increased upon psychostimulant intake and cue presentation, with this hyperactivity predicting greater dependence severity – .
The ACC receives substantial projections from the claustrum (CLA), a subcortical structure that has gained growing attention for its extensive reciprocal connections with the neocortex. Anatomically, the CLA is a sheet-like region located between the putamen and insula, oriented rostral-caudally, and composed predominantly of glutamatergic neurons (83–90%) . Human diffusion tensor neuroimaging study revealed that the CLA possesses the highest connectivity in the brain by regional volume, suggesting a critical integrative role in network coordination . Historically, CLA may be a key region involved in consciousness generation, emphasizing its potential as a hub for cortical integration . More recent studies have expanded our understanding on the CLA’s role, implicating it in various mechanisms such as action control , pain processing , , emotion regulation , , resilience to distraction , slow-wave sleep , and interactions with external sensory stimuli . In the context of addiction, the CLA regulates cocaine susceptibility and relays context-related signals to ACC and orbitofrontal cortex, contributing to cocaine conditioned-place preference and its contextual associations of reward , . However, whether and how CLA transmission is essential for METH taking or seeking, and whether this circuit modulates specific behaviors related to drug reward and relapse, remain unclear.
To elucidate the key brain regions underlying METH intake and cue-induced drug-seeking behaviors, we conducted a comprehensive analysis of neural activation patterns across 18 brain regions in rats following METH self-administration and cue-induced reinstatement. Unbiased whole-brain mapping identified the CLA and ACC as critical regions selectively engaged in both METH taking and cue-induced seeking, distinguishing them from saline controls. Functional validation revealed that the CLA is integral to both processes, while the ACC is specifically required for drug-seeking but not drug-taking. Based on these observations, we hypothesized that the reciprocal projections between the ACC and CLA might subserve dissociable roles in these behaviors. Using viral-based retrograde tracing and projection-specific chemogenetic manipulations, we found that CLA neurons projecting to the ACC (CLA-ACC neurons) selectively modulate drug-taking, whereas ACC neurons projecting to the CLA (ACC-CLA neurons) are essential for cue-induced drug-seeking. These results establish the CLA and ACC as pivotal hubs in the neural circuitry regulating METH taking and seeking behaviors, with their reciprocal connections exhibiting functionally distinct roles.

## Results

## Activating traits of CLA and ACC in METH taking and seeking
We employed an intravenous METH self-administration (SA) model to investigate the key brain regions involved in METH intake and drug-seeking behaviors (Fig.  ). Rats in the METH group acquired and maintained stable METH self-administration behavior, displaying increased infusions across sessions (Fig.  ). Following 14 days of home-cage abstinence and five extinction sessions, the METH-trained rats exhibited significantly higher active nose-poke responses when exposed to conditioned cues compared to the saline-trained controls, confirming successful reinstatement of drug-seeking induced by cues (Fig.  ). 
To analyze brain region activation following drug taking and seeking, we first performed brain-wide immediate early gene c-Fos mapping. Rats were euthanized one hour after their final session, and c-Fos-positive cell density was quantified in 18 brain regions. We found that METH SA significantly increased the intensity of c-Fos positive cells in the ACC, central nucleus of the amygdala, CLA, dorsal medial striatum, globus pallidus, nucleus accumbens core, retrosplenial cortex, ventral pallidum, and ventral tegmental area compared to control rats that underwent saline self-administration (Saline SA) (Fig.  and Supplementary Fig.  ). In cue-induced reinstatement, rats re-exposed to conditioned cues (METH CS) exhibited significantly greater c-Fos expression in 13 out of 18 brain regions examined, including the ACC, amygdala, CLA, infralimbic cortex, and prelimbic cortex than those of control rats exposed to saline conditioned cues (Saline CS) (Fig.  and Supplementary Fig.  ).
We applied a support vector machine with recursive feature elimination (SVM-RFE) for the c-Fos-positive cell counts across the 18 regions. The scatter plot of the absolute coefficient values for the brain regions examined revealed that the CLA, ACC, and nucleus accumbens core were the top contributors distinguishing METH SA from Saline SA, as well as METH CS from saline CS (Fig.  ). We then performed double immunofluorescence to identify the specific cell type contributing to the activation of the CLA and ACC during METH taking and seeking. To label glutamatergic neurons, we used neurogranin, a neuron-specific protein highly enriched in pyramidal neurons and known to bind calmodulin, as a marker . METH SA rats presented with a significant increase of the colocalization of c-Fos and glutamatergic neurons in both ACC and CLA compared to the saline SA controls (Fig. ). In addition, the percentage of c-Fos-positive and neurogranin-positive neurons in both regions were higher in METH CS rats than saline CS rats (Fig.  ). These results showed that excitatory neurons in the ACC and CLA were consistently activated during both METH taking and seeking, highlighting their potential involvement in regulating METH reward and relapse.

## CLAGlu neurons control METH taking and seeking
To investigate the functional involvement of CLA glutamatergic (CLAGlu) neurons in METH taking and seeking behavior, we utilized a Designer receptor exclusively activated by designer drugs (DREADDs)-based approach, injecting rAAV-CaMKII-hM4Di-mCherry or control vectors into the bilateral CLA of rats (Fig.  ) to enable selective, temporary modulation of neuronal activity with clozapine-N-oxide (CNO). The viral targeting specificity was confirmed through co-localization with CLA-enriched marker, the nuclear receptor related 1 protein (Nurr1) (Fig.  ). The efficacy of neuronal inhibition was confirmed by a significant reduction in the proportion of c-Fos-positive neurons among mCherry-labeled neurons in the CLA (Fig.  ). We assessed drug-taking behavior through a dose-response test following the maintenance of METH SA and seeking behavior via cue-induced reinstatement after abstinence and extinction (Fig.  ). For each test, animals expressing mCherry received CNO (5 mg/kg, i.p.) while animals expressing with hM4Di were randomly assigned to received either vehicle (VEH) or CNO 20 min before the session started. Inhibition of CLAGlu neurons resulted in a downward shift in the METH dose-response curve, indicating a diminished METH reinforcement efficacy (Fig.  ), while inactive nose-pokes remained unaffected (Fig.  ). 
For drug-seeking behaviors, chemogenetic inhibition of CLAGlu neurons significantly reduced seeking when exposed to METH-associated cues (Fig.  ) without affecting inactive nose-pokes (Fig.  ). Chemogenetic inhibition of CLA pyramidal neurons failed to change the locomotor activity in the open field test (Supplementary Fig.  ). Collectively, CLA pyramidal neurons positively regulated both METH intake and seeking behavior.

## ACCGlu neurons selectively control seeking behavior
Extensive studies have demonstrated the functional contribution of the ACC in cue reactivity and drug-seeking behavior , . To further explore whether ACC neurons directly modulate METH taking or seeking behavior, we silenced ACC neurons using tetrodotoxin (TTX, 5.0 ng per side), a potent sodium channel blocker that inhibits action potential generation and propagation (Supplementary Fig.  ). For the METH taking and seeking tests, two independent cohorts of METH SA-trained rats were used. Each cohort was randomly assigned to receive intra-ACC microinjections of either vehicle (VEH) or TTX 30 minutes prior to testing (Supplementary Fig.  ). Inactivation of ACC via TTX did not affect drug-taking in a dose-response curve test (Supplementary Fig.  ). While intra-ACC infusions of TTX significantly attenuated drug-seeking behavior, as evidenced by a decrease in active nose-poke responses, without affecting inactive nose-poke responses (Supplementary Fig.  ). These results provide evidence that the ACC plays a critical role in modulating cue-induced drug seeking.
Building on insights from brain-wide c-Fos analysis and TTX inhibition, we next aimed to determine how activation or inhibition of ACC glutamatergic (ACCGlu) neurons simultaneously influences drug-taking and drug-seeking behaviors. First, AAV-CaMKII-hM4Di-mCherry or control vectors were infused into the bilateral ACC two weeks before METH SA (Fig.  ). The specificity of virus infection was confirmed by immunostaining, showing colocalization of CaMKII and mCherry in the ACC (Fig.  ). In an independent cohort, CNO treatment significantly reduced c-Fos expression in rats expressing inhibitory hM4Di in the ACC, confirming effective neuronal inhibition (Fig.  ). After the acquisition of stable METH self-administration, rats underwent a dose-response test and then, following extinction, a cue-induced reinstatement test. For both tests, rats expressing hM4Di were randomly assigned to receive either VEH or CNO (5 mg/kg, i.p.) 20 minutes before session onset, and rats expressing mCherry received CNO as a control (Fig.  ). Chemogenetic inhibition of ACCGlu neurons did not affect METH-taking (Fig.  ), suggesting that ACC excitatory activity is not essential for METH consumption. Rats underwent sessions of extinction showed comparable reductions in active nose-pokes (Fig.  ). Chemogenetic inhibition of ACCGlu neurons led to a significant reduction in cue-induced drug-seeking, without affecting inactive nose-poke responses (Fig.  ) and locomotor activity (Supplementary Fig.  ). 
To test whether direct activation of ACC pyramidal neurons is sufficient to regulate METH-taking or seeking, we bilaterally expressed hM3Dq in ACCGlu neurons to enable reversible activation, with AAV-CaMKII-mCherry vectors serving as controls (Fig.  ). Following METH SA or extinction, CNO (5 mg/kg) was administered 20 min to activate ACCGlu neurons before either the dose-response test or the cue-induced reinstatement test (Fig.  ). Immunostaining confirmed effective hM3Dq activation, showing a significantly higher number of c-Fos-positive cells in the ACC of the hM3Dq group compared to controls (Fig.  ). No significant differences in drug-taking behavior were observed between the mCherry and hM3Dq groups during METH SA (Fig.  ), and ACC activation did not affect METH taking across multiple doses (Fig.  ). There was also no difference in context extinction between the mCherry and hM3Dq groups (Fig.  ). Activation of ACCGlu neurons produced a trend toward increased active nose-poke responses during cue-induced seeking, without affecting inactive nose-pokes (Fig.  ) and locomotor activity (Supplementary Fig.  ). Taken together, these results demonstrate that while ACCGlu neuronal activity is not necessary for regulating METH reward or consumption, it supports cue-driven drug-seeking behavior, suggesting a selective role in relapse-related processes. 
Then, to address whether drug-taking and seeking behaviors recruit overlapping neuronal ensembles in the ACC, we employed an activity-dependent neural tagging approach by injecting a mixture of rAAV-E-SARE-CreERT2 and rAAV-hSyn-DIO-hM4Di-mCherry into the bilateral ACC (Supplementary Fig.  ). This strategy utilizes the estrogen receptor-dependent Cre recombinase (CreERT) under the control of the synaptic activity–responsive element (SARE), which regulates the induction of the immediate-early gene Arc , . Administration of the estrogen receptor ligand 4-hydroxytamoxifen (4-OHT) enabled temporal recombination of target alleles, allowing for neuronal tagging during a defined behavioral window of several hours . Following viral induction for CreERT expression, rats underwent 14 days of METH SA, and on day 10, 4-OHT (20 mg/kg, i.p.) was injected to label METH SA-activated ensembles with mCherry (Supplementary Fig.  ). After SA training, rats were assigned to home-abstinence for 14 days and context extinction for 5 days to extinguish nose-pokes (Supplementary Fig.  ). Re-exposure to drug-associated cues significantly increased active nose-pokes, after which rats were euthanized to assess hM4Di-mCherry and c-Fos expression (Supplementary Fig.  ). Cue-induced reinstatement evoked c-Fos expression, reflecting neuronal activity during drug-seeking, which partially overlapped with the mCherry + neurons representing drug-taking ensembles (Supplementary Fig.  ). Immunofluorescence revealed that 26.18% of mCherry + (SA-tagged) neurons in the ACC were immunoreactive for c-Fos during seeking, indicating partial reactivation. While 68.52% of c-Fos + (seeking-activated) neurons overlapped with mCherry + ensembles, demonstrating preferential recruitment of drug-taking associated neurons. SA-tagged (mCherry +) neurons showed significantly higher c-Fos induction during seeking compared to non-tagged (mCherry-) neurons among all DAPI + cells (Supplementary Fig.  ). In summary, these results provide evidence that cue-induced drug-seeking preferentially reactivates ACC ensembles previously engaged during the drug-taking phase, suggesting a functional overlap in neuronal populations governing both taking and seeking phases.

## Reciprocal connectivity between CLA and ACC
The selective involvement of ACC pyramidal neurons in drug-seeking behavior may reflect modulation by upstream reward-processing circuits, particularly the CLA-ACC pathway. Given the well-documental reciprocal connectivity between the CLA and ACC , we systematically mapped these projections using retrograde tracing, anterograde transsynaptic labeling, and cell-type-specific profiling. We injected the retrograde tracer Fluorogold (FG) into the ACC, allowing us to identify projections from the CLA to the ACC (Fig. ). This resulted in significant retrograde labeling of CLA-ACC cells, with approximately 83.68% co-localized with glutamatergic neurons marker CaMKII, confirming a predominantly glutamatergic projection (Fig.  ). We further took advantage of a recently established AAV1-mediated anterograde transsynaptic tagging approach , . By injecting a CaMKIIcre anterograde monosynaptic tracing virus into the CLA and a Cre-dependent mCherry-expressing virus into the ACC, we selectively labeled ACC neurons receiving inputs from the CLA (Fig.  ). We determined the specific cell type of ACC neurons receiving CLA innervation and found the majority (76.66%) of them co-expressed with glutamatergic neuron marker neurogranin (NG), while the minority (6.78%) were parvalbumin positive, indicating selective innervation of pyramidal neurons (Fig.  ). To explore whether ACC projects back to the CLA, we injected retrograde scAAV-hSyn-tdTomato into the CLA (Fig.  ). The results showed a robust labeling of tdtTomato+ neurons within the ACC (Fig.  ). Double immunostaining revealed that 85.57% of ACC-CLA neurons were pyramidal cells, indicating a strong reciprocal excitatory loop between the CLA and ACC (Fig.  ). 

## METH taking recruits CLAGlu-to-ACC circuit
To examine the activation pattern of the CLA-ACC circuit during METH taking, we injected FG into the ACC (Fig.  ). After two weeks, rats underwent 14 days of saline or METH SA and were perfused one hour after the final session (Fig.  ). We used triple-labeling staining, NG for glutamatergic neurons, c-Fos for neuronal activation, and FG for tracing. The METH group showed a significant increase in the co-expression of NG, c-Fos, and FG in CLA-ACC neurons compared to the saline group (Fig.  ), demonstrating METH-driven recruitment of CLA-ACC glutamatergic neurons. To further investigate the functional involvement of the CLA-ACC circuit in METH-related behaviors, we employed a projection-specific silencing approach (Fig.  ). AAV-Retro-CaMKII-Cre was injected into the ACC, while AAV-hSyn-DIO-hM4Di-mCherry was injected into the CLA to selectively inhibit glutamatergic CLA-ACC neurons (Fig.  ). Systemic chemogenetic intervention (VEH or 5 mg/kg CNO, i.p.) was implemented 20 minutes before both drug-taking and seeking tests (Fig.  ). CNO-mediated inhibition was confirmed by a significant reduction in the percentage of mCherry-positive neurons co-expressed with c-Fos in the CNO-treated group compared to controls (Fig.  ). In addition, we observed a significant decrease in c-Fos expression relative to mCherry-labeled axon terminals in the ACC after inhibition of glutamatergic CLA-ACC neurons (Supplementary Fig.  ). After acquisition of stable METH SA or extinction, rats were randomly assigned to VEH or CNO groups (Fig.  ). The results revealed that silencing of glutamatergic CLA-ACC neurons significantly decreased METH intake across doses without altering inactive nose-poke responses (Fig.  ). To determine whether this effect was behavior-specific, we assessed locomotor activity in an open field, where no significant differences in total distance traveled were observed between the VEH and CNO groups (Supplementary Fig.  ). In contrast to the effects on METH-taking, inhibition of glutamatergic CLA-ACC neurons had no significant influence on the cue-induced drug-seeking (Fig.  ). These findings indicate that CLA-ACC glutamatergic neurons are selectively engaged during METH taking but dispensable for seeking. 
To assess the behavioral specificity of the CLA-ACC neuronal circuit, we examined whether its inhibition alters natural reward consumption using a food self-administration paradigm (Supplementary Fig.  ). Both mCherry and hM4Di expressing rats showed comparable learning curves across training sessions (Supplementary Fig.  ). In the food-taking task, CNO (5 mg/kg) was administered 20 min before the session started. CLA-ACC neurons silenced rats exhibited similar levels of nose-poke responses to control animals (Supplementary Fig.  ). The task completion times were also similar between groups (Supplementary Fig.  ), indicating that CLA-ACC circuit inhibition has intact operant conditioning, unimpaired motivation for natural reward, or motor function. These results demonstrate that the CLA-ACC circuit is selectively involved in METH-taking behavior, without influencing natural reward consumption.

## ACCglu-to-CLA circuits control METH seeking
Given the established role of the ACC in cue-reactivity and its involvement in top-down control through the CLA, we hypothesized that ACC pyramidal neurons might regulate cue-induced seeking through their projections to the CLA , , . To test this hypothesis, we employed a virus retrograde tracing into the CLA combined with neuronal activity mapping by assessing the percentage of retrogradely labeled ACC neurons expressing c-Fos (Fig.  ). Following SA training and subsequent extinction, rats underwent cue-induced reinstatement testing as previously described (Fig.  ). Consistent with our predictions, exposure to METH-associated cues (METH CS) elicited significantly stronger seeking behavior compared to saline-associated cues (Saline CS) (Fig.  ). At the neural level, we observed a marked increase in the proportion of ACC-CLA neurons co-labeled with c-Fos, NG, and tdTomato in the METH CS group relative to the saline CS group (Fig.  ). This triple-labeling pattern demonstrates that drug-related cues selectively recruit ACC-CLA pyramidal neurons. 
To elucidate the causal role of the ACC-CLA excitatory circuit in both taking and seeking behaviors, we employed circuit-specific chemogenetic silencing. We selectively manipulated ACC-CLA by injecting AAV-Retro-CaMKII-Cre into the CLA and AAV-hSyn-DIO-hM4Di-mCherry into the ACC (Fig.  ). Immunostaining for c-Fos confirmed that CNO effectively inhibited the ACC-CLA excitatory circuit (Fig.  ). VEH or CNO (5 mg/kg, i.p.) was administered 20 min before both the METH-taking and cue-induced reinstatement tests, following METH SA and extinction training, respectively (Fig.  , m–o). Inhibition of the ACC-CLA circuit did not affect METH-taking behavior (Fig.  ), but significantly reduced cue-induced drug-seeking (Fig.  ), without impacting inactive nose-pokes and locomotor behavior (Fig.  and Supplementary Fig.  ). To further delineate the function of ACC-CLA pathway, we implanted bilateral cannulas in the CLA to directly inhibit ACC-CLA terminals in a separate cohort of rats expressing hM4Di in CLA-projecting ACC neurons (Supplementary Fig.  ). Cannula placements and suppression of CLA activity following CNO infusions were confirmed after behavior tests (Supplementary Fig.  ). Rats were randomly assigned to receive intra-CLA microinjections of either VEH or CNO (1 mM) 20 min before the METH-taking test following METH SA, and again before the drug-seeking test after extinction (Supplementary Fig.  ). Consistent with systemic inhibition results, terminal specific inhibition of ACC-CLA projections did not alter drug-taking behaviors (Supplementary Fig.  ) and affect inactive nose-pokes (Supplementary Fig.  ). Thus, the ACC-CLA glutamatergic transmission is selectively required for cue-driven seeking, not METH consumption. This pathway represents a behaviorally discrete node in relapse circuitry, and the dissociation between taking and seeking circuits highlights the functional specialization of ACC outputs.
Taken together, our findings delineate a functionally specialized reciprocal loop between glutamatergic neurons in the CLA and ACC that differentially regulates distinct phases of METH addiction, with CLAGlu-ACC projections modulating drug-taking and ACCGlu-CLA projections controlling drug-seeking (Fig.  ). 

## Discussion
Our comprehensive c-Fos mapping analysis revealed distinct activation patterns across multiple brain regions, with particularly robust engagement of both CLA and ACC pyramidal neurons during METH self-administration and cue-induced seeking. Through chemogenetic inhibition approaches, we demonstrated that suppressed neuron activities in the CLA significantly decreased focal c-Fos expression, reduced both drug taking and seeking, confirmed the necessity of CLA glutamatergic neurons activation (as indexed by c-Fos) for maintaining METH consumption and seeking behaviors. Together with previous studies showing that reduced c-Fos expression in the CLA is associated with alleviated relapse-like behavior , this finding expands our understanding of the CLA’s role across different phases of addiction. In addition, studies suggest that the CLA supports cue salience and resilience to distraction , , which may further contribute to its role in maintaining drug-seeking behaviors induced by cues after abstinence. Intriguingly, a recent study on fentanyl self-administration identified that the CLA-ACC circuit appears to function as a brake, generating inhibitory signals that limit opioid use . This divergence could be attributed to the high expression of opioid receptors in the CLA, which may alter the circuit’s functional output in response to opioid exposure, possibly leading to different circuit dynamics compared to psychostimulants.
The CLA is extensively connected with the cortical area, providing excitatory inputs to both inhibitory and excitatory neurons in the cortex , . Our anterograde trans-synaptic tracing data revealed that CLA glutamatergic neurons predominantly innervate pyramidal neurons within the ACC, consistent with previous electrophysiological evidence of a functional connection of glutamatergic neurons from CLA to ACC in gating visceral pain processin . Expanding upon this anatomical connectivity, we demonstrated that inhibition of the CLA-ACC circuit attenuated the ascending limb of the METH dose-response curve, reducing the rewarding effects of METH. Two recent studies demonstrated the CLA-PFC circuit’s involvement in regulating psychostimulant-induced impulsivity and the acquisition of drug-contextual associations . Our findings advance the understanding of CLA-mediated cortical mechanisms in psychostimulant addiction by uncovering a previously unrecognized role of the CLA-ACC circuit in modulating METH reward efficacy and its critical contribution to METH escalation.
The ACC serves as a critical regulatory hub that exerts top-down control over subcortical structures, including the CLA, dorsal striatum, amygdala, VTA, and thalamus, thereby modulating sensory integration , attention, and motivation processes . While previous studies have revealed that ACC encodes valence information and suppress natural reward consumption , . Here, we observed no direct modulatory effect of ACC pyramidal neurons on METH taking. One explanation may be due to the ACC’s influence on reward through distinct output pathways, and our manipulation of all ACCGlu neurons could obscure specific effects. For example, studies have found that the ACC-nucleus accumbens core supports reward taking while the ACC-basal lateral amygdala pathway suppress reward consumption , . In addition, reward encoding in the ACC may be more related to long-term memory processing, as supported by its selective role in remote, but not recent memory retrival . Using activity-dependent labeling, we found preferential reactivation of METH SA-activated ensembles in ACC during cue-induced seeking after abstinence and context extinction, suggesting that the ACC plays a critical role in the storage and retrieval of drug-taking memories. Notably, the majority of seeking-activated neurons colocalized with METH SA-activated ensembles, indicating potential involvement of ACC in encoding drug memory and cue salience. These findings, along with evidence linking mPFC neuronal ensembles to relapse behaviors in alcohol addiction , highlight the ACC’s pivotal role in integrating drug-associated cues with reward memory, supporting its involvement in cue-driven relapse.
Our study provides direct evidence that the ACC-CLA glutamatergic circuit and CLA glutamatergic neurons are essential for cue-induced seeking behavior following abstinence and extinction, as demonstrated through circuit-specific tracing and chemogenetic inhibition. Prior work by White et al. highlights the role of the ACC-CLA circuit in cognitive control, showing that ACC activation drives high-frequency firing in CLA neurons to amplify outputs . Electrophysiological studies further demonstrate that the ACC-CLA circuit projects to multiple cortical areas, including the mPFC, orbitofrontal cortex, and restrosplenial cortex, to regulate cortical synchronization , . More recent findings suggested that the CLA is predominantly active during synchronized cortical states under physiological conditions, such as rest or non-REM sleep, and plays a key role in coordinating cortical activity across regions , . We hypothesize that repeated activation of the CLA and ACC by METH exposure and drug-associated cues may disrupt CLA’s normal synchronizing role, leading to widespread cortical dysfunction. Based on our findings, we propose a “maladaptive cycle” model of addiction, linking binge drug taking and drug cue-induced drug-seeking. During the drug-taking phase, METH activates the CLA-ACC circuit, facilitating escalating drug use and encoding drug-associated learning and memory in the ACC. Following abstinence and extinction, re-exposure to drug-associated cues reactivates both ACC engrams and the ACC-CLA circuit, driving cue-induced seeking. Prior studies have demonstrated that discrete cues with strong incentive properties are sufficient for the incubation of drug seeking and that prolonged abstinence increases activation in corticostriatal structures in a food craving incubation model – . These findings align with our proposed “maladaptive cycle” model, which may also contribute to the incubation of drug-seeking through CLA-mediated cortical network dysregulation. Although our projection-specific inhibition experiments support a role for the ACC-CLA pathway in cue-induced reinstatement, ACC projections to other regions, such as the dorsal medial striatum and VTA, have also been implicated in cognitive control and opioid withdrawal symptoms, respectively , . Thus, ACC-mediated regulation of drug-seeking likely engages multiple parallel downstream pathways in addition to the ACC-CLA projection, with the CLA potentially serving as a critical hub to integrate signals from these diverse collaterals.
Our findings demonstrate that ACC synaptic outputs are critically involved in drug-seeking behavior, extend previous rodent and human research implicating the ACC in relapse mechanisms , . This discovery highlights several promising therapeutic avenues of targeting ACC activity for intervention. The state-dependent activation pattern of ACC during drug-seeking makes it particularly suitable for closed-loop interventions, such as neurofeedback or repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation, both modalities have shown efficacy in reducing craving and preventing relapse , . Combining these strategies with virtual reality-based cue exposure therapy could further enhance treatment precision for substance use disorders by recalibrating craving-associated neural circuits.
While this study provides important insights into the neural circuitry underlying METH addiction, several limitations should be considered. Firstly, the study was conducted exclusively in male rats, which may limit the generalizability of the findings across sexes. Rodent and human studies have shown that females exhibit stronger drug-seeking behaviors, heightened craving, and differences in voluntary reinforcement intake – . Future studies should include female subjects to assess whether similar mechanisms apply across sexes. In addition, although we used region- and circuit-specific viral-based manipulations with careful control of injection volume and speed, the possibility of viral spread to surrounding areas cannot be fully excluded. The insular cortex, a key component of the salience network, is anatomically adjacent to the CLA and may also contribute to reward and relapse. Employing transgenic models targeting CLA-specific neurons in future studies would enhance the precision of these manipulations and help differentiate the function of CLA and insula , . Future studies should incorporate in vivo recordings in CLA to monitor its real-time activity dynamics and use optogenetic activation to test its causal role in the execution of reward-related behaviors. Given that CLA neurons collateralize to multiple downstream targets beyond the ACC, future studies employing terminal-specific manipulations will be necessary to precisely delineate the contribution of individual CLA projections.
In conclusion, this study investigated the functional involvement of a CLA-ACC bottom-up circuit and ACC-CLA top-down circuit in METH-related addictive behaviors. Our findings reveal CLA’s involvement in both drug-taking and drug-seeking phases, identifying a brain region critical for cue-induced reinstatement. These insights offer a mechanistic basis for targeting frontal circuits through neuromodulation, with potential implications for treating craving in clinical settings.

## Methods

## Animals
Male SPF-graded Sprague-Dawley rats (8–10 weeks of age) weighing 280–300 g were purchased from Zhejiang Provincial Experimental Animal Center (Hangzhou, China). All rats were housed in a temperature-controlled environment at 22 ± 2 °C and humidity-controlled at 50 ± 10% under a 12 h light/dark cycle, with lights on from 6:00 PM to 6:00 AM. Food and water were provided ad libitum for all rats. All animal experiments were performed in accordance with the National Institutes of Health (NIH) Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals (8th Edition) and approved by the Affiliated Kangning Hospital of Ningbo University Experimental Animal Center Ethics Committee.

## Virus
Viruses used were purchased from BrainVTA Co., Ltd: rAAV2/1-CaMKIIa-CRE (PT-1731), rAAV2/9-EF1α-DIO-mCherry (PT-0013), scAAV-hSyn-tdTomato (PT-4708A), rAAV2/9-CaMKIIa-mCherry (PT-0108), rAAV2/9-CaMKIIa-hM4D(Gi)-mCherry (PT-0017), rAAV2/9-CaMKIIa-hM3D(Gq)-mCherry (PT-0049), rAAV2/Retro-Camkii-CRE (PT-0220), rAAV2/9-hSyn-DIO-hM4D(Gi)-mCherry (PT-0020), rAAV-E-SARE-CreERT2-PEST-WPRE-hGH polyA (PT-0596). The titer of viruses ranged from 5.00E + 12 vector genomes (vg)/ml to 5.5E + 12 vg/ml. All viral vectors were aliquoted and stored at − 80 °C until use.

## Stereotaxic surgery
Rats were anesthetized with Zoletil 50 (50 mg/kg, Virbac, France) and their heads were secured in a stereotaxic apparatus (RWD Life Science Co. Ltd.). Erythromycin ointment was applied to the eyes to prevent dryness. A small incision was made on the scalp to expose the skull surface. The skull was gently wiped with H2O2 to identify the bregma, and burr holes were drilled into the skull. The following stereotaxic coordinates were used to target specific brain areas: ACC (Anterior-Posterior (AP): + 2.5, Medial-Lateral (ML): ± 0.65, Dorsal-Ventral (DV): − 2.6) and CLA (AP: + 2.2, ML: ± 4.15, DV: − 6.4). Viral injections were performed using a microsyringe pump (World Precision Instruments, Florida, USA) and a blunt needle microliter syringe (10 µL, Hamilton Company) at a speed of 100 nL/min. The needle was left in place for an additional 5 min to allow diffusion of AAV vectors, after which it was carefully removed. Post-surgery, rats were allowed to recover for two weeks before undergoing behavioral tests.
For retrograde tracing of CLA-ACC cells, rats received bilateral injections (150 nL per side) of Fluorogold (UElandy, #F4040, 4% in saline) into the ACC. For retrograde tracing of ACC-CLA neurons, bilateral injections (400 nL per side) of scAAV-hSyn-tdTomato were administered into the CLA. For anterograde trans-synaptic tracing from the CLA to the ACC, rAAV2/1-CaMKIIa-CRE was injected (500 nL per side) into the CLA, and rAAV2/9-EF1α-DIO-mCherry was ipsilaterally injected (500 nL per side) into the ACC. Four weeks post-injection, the rats were euthanized for imaging.
For chemogenetic manipulations, 500–800 nL of AAV vector was bilaterally injected into the ACC, and 400 nL for the CLA. To specifically regulate CLA-ACC pyramidal neurons, rats were injected with 500 nL of rAAV2/Retro-Camkii-CRE in the ACC and 400 nL of rAAV2/9-hSyn-DIO-hM4D(Gi)-mCherry bilaterally in the CLA. For chemogenetic inhibition of ACC-CLA pyramidal neurons, 400 nL of rAAV2/Retro-CaMKII-CRE was injected in the CLA, and 800 nL of rAAV2/9-hSyn-DIO-hM4D(Gi)-mCherry bilaterally in the ACC. For activity-dependent cell labeling, 800 nL of rAAV2/9-hSyn-DIO-hM4D(Gi)-mCherry and rAAV-E-SARE-CreERT2-PEST-WPRE-hGH polyA mixture was injected into bilateral ACC. Behavioral procedures were conducted at least two weeks post-injection to ensure adequate expression of the viral constructs.
For intracranial injection experiments, bilateral cannulae (OD: 0.48 mm, ID: 0.34 mm, RWD Life Science Co. Ltd.) were implanted above the ACC at stereotaxic coordinates (AP: + 2.5 mm, ML: ± 0.65 mm, DV: − 1.6 mm) or CLA at stereotactic coordinates (AP: + 2.2 mm, ML: ± 4.1 mm, DV: − 5.9 mm). The guide cannulas were secured to the skull using stainless steel screws and dental cement. A stainless-steel dummy cannula was inserted to maintain patency and prevent infection. Rats were allowed to recover for one week before behavioral procedures.

## Jugular vein catheterization
Self-administration catheter implant surgery was performed as previously described , . The rats were anesthetized with Zoletil 50 (50 mg/kg). Silastic catheters were inserted in the right jugular vein, passed subcutaneously to the back, and attached to an end cap. Rats received daily 0.3 mL of heparinized saline (50 USP/mL in 0.9% saline) to maintain catheter patency. Rats were allowed to recover for at least 7 days before the training procedure.

## METH self-administration training
Methamphetamine (Institute of Forensic Science of China, 0.2 mg/mL) was dissolved in saline for self-administration (SA) experiments. Rats underwent daily 4-hour METH or saline self-administration sessions in a two-nose-poke operant chamber for 14 days, as previously described . Each chamber contained one active and one inactive nose-poke. Active nose-pokes, paired with light and tone cues, triggered an intravenous infusion of METH (0.05 mg/kg/infusion), followed by a 20 s timeout period. Inactive nose-pokes yielded no outcomes. METH injections were delivered under a fixed ratio 1 (FR1) schedule. There was a 20-minute extinction (0 mg/kg METH) phase prior to each session of SA training, during which active nose-pokes did not result in drug and cue delivery. Acquisition of the self-administration behavior was defined as a minimum of 10 infusions per session, at least 80% of total nose-pokes being active nose-pokes over two consecutive sessions, and exhibiting less than a 20% variation in the mean number of METH infusions over three consecutive sessions.

## FR-1 dose-response curve
Following the acquisition phase, we tested the dose-response curves to evaluate the effect of chemogenetic manipulation of specific brain regions or circuits on self-administration maintained by a full range of METH doses (0, 0.031, 0.0625, 0.125, 0.25, 0.5, 1, and 2 mg/kg/infusion) , . Each session consisted of eight sequential 25-minute trials, with each trial separated by a 5-minute intertrial timeout period. The dose was adjusted by altering the infusion volume via the duration of pump activation.

## Extinction and cue-induced reinstatement
Rats were kept abstinent in their home cages for 14 days following the dose-response curve test. After this withdrawal period, they underwent five 2 h extinction sessions in the self-administration chamber. During these sessions, nose pokes did not produce any outcomes, the house light was turned off, and only the number of nose pokes was recorded. The cue-induced reinstatement test commenced with the non-contingent presentation of infusion-associated cues for 5 s, including the house light and pump sound. Poking the active nose-pokes activated the cues, but no drug was delivered. Responses on the inactive nose-pokes were recorded but had no programmed consequences. The reinstatement test lasted for 2 h.

## Locomotor test
Rats were allowed to freely explore in an open field arena (60 cm*60 cm) for 60 min. The total distance traveled was recorded and measured by the ANY-maze automated detection system.

## Food self-administration
For food self-administration, rats were trained to self-administer palatable food pellets (45 mg/pellet, Bio Serv. #F0021) in a two-nose-poke operant chamber during daily 1h-sessions, starting from a FR1 schedule as previously described . Active nose-pokes resulted in delivery of a palatable food pellet, accompanied by the presentation of the house light and a tone, whereas inactive nose-pokes had no programmed consequences. Training progressed from FR1 to FR2, FR3, and ultimately to FR5 when responding to the previous scheduled trainings were stable. Each session ended either when 1 h had elapsed or when the rat had obtained 100 pellets. Testing was conducted when rats show less than 20% variability in responding over three consecutive days. During the food-taking test, rats received CNO (5 mg/kg, i.p.) 20 minutes prior to the FR5 session.

## Chemogenetic manipulations
To fully express the virus in the target brain region, we performed manipulation at least four weeks after virus injection. Clozapine N-oxide (CNO, MedChem Express, HY-17366) was dissolved in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO, Absin, abs9184), then in saline to a final concentration of 6% DMSO , . For neuronal activation, rats expressing mCherry (control group) or hM3Dq-mCherry were given an intraperitoneal injection of CNO (5 mg/kg, 1 mg/mL) at 20 minutes prior to the behavioral tests. For inhibition experiments, hM4Di-expressing rats were randomly assigned to either a CNO or vehicle (VEH) group prior to each test phase. An additional control group (mCherry + CNO) was included in certain experiments to control for off-target effects of CNO. All injections were administered intraperitoneally 20 minutes before the METH-taking or cue-induced seeking test.
For intracranial CNO injections, rats were assigned to either the CNO or VEH group separately for each behavioral phase. Rats were habituated for intracranial injections two days before the tests via inserting sham microinjection needles that were 0.5 mm beyond the guide cannulae for 2 minutes. For actual microinjections, 1 mM CNO in a vehicle of 5% DMSO and artificial cerebrospinal fluid (Tocris, 35-252) or vehicle was infused through CLA guide cannula (0.4 µL/side, 100 nL/min) 20 min before testing . Cannula placements were confirmed following all behavioral tests.

## Neuropharmacological manipulations
Tetrodotoxin (TTX) was purchased from the Qingdao Institute of Marine Biology. Two days prior to the microinjection, sham microinjectors were inserted 1 mm beyond the guide cannulae for 2 min to acclimate the rats to the microinjection procedure. For the actual microinjection, TTX (5.0 ng per side, dissolved in 0.5 µl of isotonic saline) or an equivalent volume of vehicle was infused into the ACC. The infusion was conducted using 30-gauge microinjection needles (RWD Life Science Co. Ltd.), positioned 1 mm below the guide cannula. The infusion was performed slowly for over 2 min, and the injection needle was left in place for an additional 3 min to facilitate diffusion. Behavioral tests were conducted 30 min following the drug delivery. Cannula placements were confirmed following all behavioral tests.

## Activity-dependent labeling and 4-OHT treatment
The ensemble tagging strategy used in this study was adapted from previously established protocols , . Twenty-one days after the stereotactic surgery, rats underwent METH self-administration training for 14 days. 4-Hydroxytamoxifen (4-OHT; TargetMol, Cat. No. T6743) was dissolved in a solution of 5% DMSO and 95% corn oil to a final concentration of 3.1 mg/mL. On day 10, animals received an intraperitoneal injection of 4-OHT (20 mg/kg) immediately after the self-administration training session as previously study described . Following the injection, rats were left undisturbed in their home cages for 24 h before the next training session, allowing for specific tagging of neuronal ensembles associated with self-administration.

## Immunofluorescence
For c-Fos staining, rats were anesthetized with Zoletil (50 mg/kg, i.p.) 1 h after the behavioral test and were perfused with saline followed by paraformaldehyde (PFA, 4% wt/vol in PBS, pH = 7.4). The rat brain was immersed in 4% PFA at 4 °C overnight for post-fixation and then dehydrated with 30% sucrose solution until tissues sank. The brain sections (30 μm) were prepared with a cryostat (Leica, Germany) and stored in 0.1 M PBS. The sections were incubated in blocking buffer containing 0.3% Triton X-100 and 5% normal goat serum/PBS for 60 min at room temperature and then with primary antibodies in blocking buffer overnight at 4%°C. The primary antibodies used were: anti-c-Fos (1:1000, 226008, SYSY), anti-CaMKII (1:500, ab22609, abcam), anti-Neurogranin (1:500, 845602, BioLegend), anti-Nurr1 (1:50, sc-376984, Santa Cruz). After three 10 min washes with PBS, the slices were incubated for 2 h to fluorophore-conjugated secondary antibodies (goat anti-Rabbit IgG Alexa Fluor 488, Invitrogen, cat. #A11008; goat anti-Mouse IgG Alexa Fluor 594, abcam, cat. #150116; goat anti-Rabbit IgG Alexa Fluor 594, Invitrogen, cat. #A11012; goat anti-Rabbit IgG Alexa Fluor 647, abcam, cat. #ab150083). After five washes, the slices were mounted on slides with Mounting Medium with DAPI (ab104139, abcam). Fluorescence images were captured using an Olympus BX53 or Leica TCS SP8 confocal microscope. Images were analyzed with ImageJ (National Institutes of Health).
For c-Fos quantification, 18 brain regions (refer to Supplementary Table  ) were analyzed. For each brain area of interest, 3-6 brain slices were selected based on the antero-posterior extent of the region. Images were background subtracted and matched to the corresponding planes of the rat brain atlas (Paxinos & Watson, 6th edition). Investigators were blinded to the treatment status. Both hemispheres were counted, and the total number of c-Fos positive cells was divided by the area size of the region of interest to obtain the density of c-Fos positive cells per square millimeter (c-Fos + /mm²).

## Machine learning classifiers
To identify potential key brain regions in regulating reward or reinstatement behavior, a support vector machine with a recursive feature elimination (SVM-RFE) algorithm was employed using the ‘caret’ package in R . The number of c-Fos-positive cells of each brain area was normalized and transformed into z-score data separately for the self-administration groups and the reinstatement groups. The datasets between saline self-administration and METH self-administration, and between saline cue-induced reinstatement and METH cue-induced reinstatement were compared. Feature importance was ranked using the ‘VarImp’ function, which provided the absolute values of the coefficients, indicating the contribution of each brain region to the model’s predictive capability.

## Statistical analysis
Sample sizes were determined based on common practice in animal behavioral studies. All data were analyzed using GraphPad Prism 9.0 (GraphPad Software, San Diego, CA) and presented as mean ± SEM unless otherwise stated. Data normality was assessed using the Shapiro-Wilk test. For comparisons between two groups, either an unpaired t test, Welch’s test, or the nonparametric Mann-Whitney test was applied where applicable. For comparisons among three groups with a single factor, one-way ANOVA followed by Bonferroni’s post hoc analysis or Brown-Forsythe ANOVA with Dunnett’s T3 multiple comparisons test was performed, where applicable. For comparisons involving two factors, two-way ANOVA with repeated measures followed by Bonferroni’s post hoc analysis was used. All tests were two-tailed, and p-values < 0.05 were considered statistically significant. The details are shown in Supplementary Table  .
