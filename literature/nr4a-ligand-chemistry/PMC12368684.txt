## Abstract
Natural products are the main source of drug development
and nutritional
supplements due to their abundant bioactivity and guaranteed safety.
Nonetheless, their journey from the laboratory to market faces formidable
hurdles, primarily stemming from issues such as poor solubility and
limited bioavailability. To combat these hurdles, coamorphous (CAM)
technology has emerged as a recent solution, offering advantages in
solubility enhancement, stability, drug loading capacity, and combination
with other drugs. Coamorphization of natural active pharmaceutical
ingredients (APIs) holds promise for enhancing their druggability
and presents an opportunity for combining them with synthetic drugs.
This review encapsulates recent advancements in natural API CAM systems,
covering their preparation and characterization techniques and diverse
applications, summarizing reported systems, and offering an outlook
on future possibilities and perspectives.

## Introduction
The solubility of a drug is intricately
intertwined with its absorption
and distribution in vivo, playing a crucial role
in determining the efficacy of drugs in clinical practice. However,
approximately 90% of drug candidates and 40% of marketed drugs possess
poor water solubility, which are categorized as Class II (low solubility
and high permeability) or Class IV (low solubility and low permeability)
drugs based on the Biopharmaceutical Classification System (BCS). Enhancing the solubility of poor water-soluble
drugs remains one of the great challenges in their development. Generally,
salt formation is the first choice for improving their solubility.
However, the strategy is not applicable to drugs that lack an ionizable
center. In recent years, various strategies,
such as chemical modification, inclusion techniques, nanotechnology,
micronization, crystal engineering, amorphization, and others, have
also been widely employed to tackle this challenge.

Transforming insoluble drugs from crystalline to
amorphous forms
is a widely employed strategy for enhancing their water solubility. In contrast to crystalline drugs, amorphous drugs
exhibit extended disordered molecular packing, resulting in a notable
advantage in solubility. However, amorphous
drugs exhibit low physical stability and are prone to recrystallization
due to their elevated internal energy, consequently diminishing their
solubility advantage. Thus, polymer excipients are utilized to hinder
recrystallization tendencies and preserve the solubility advantages
of amorphous drugs, resulting in the formation of what is known as
an amorphous solid dispersion (ASD) ( left). Incorporating polymers enhances the
physical stability of amorphous drugs by elevating the glass transition
temperature (T
g) and decreasing the mobility
of the drug molecules within the systems. This strategy has proven
highly successful in the pharmaceutical industry for its convenience
and effectiveness, leading to the introduction of a series of ASDs
on the market. However, challenges arise
with the utilization of polymeric carriers as stabilizers. Polymers often exhibit restricted drug loading
capacity, resulting in a larger bulk volume/mass of the final dosage
forms and reduced patient compliance. Moreover, polymers tend to absorb
moisture under ambient conditions, accelerating the phase transition
of drugs from their amorphous to crystalline forms. Hence, the focus
has shifted toward pharmaceutically acceptable low-molecular-weight
compounds, including amino acids, organic acids, food additives, drugs,
and others, which are utilized as stabilizers to tackle the challenges
associated with polymers in the research and development of ASDs. The obtained homogeneous single-phase amorphous
systems that emerge are referred to as coamorphous (CAM) systems ( right).
 Natural products serve as a vital wellspring for
the discovery
of new drugs. According to statistics, between 1981 and 2019, 22.7%
of drugs approved by the United States Food and Drug Administration
(FDA) were derived from natural active pharmaceutical ingredients
(APIs) or their derivatives. Unfortunately,
approximately 90% of natural APIs are excluded from new drug screening
processes due to insufficient water solubility, limited bioavailability,
and poor stability in clinical applications. Recently, there has been a notable surge in interest in CAM systems
for natural APIs, in both academic and industrial circles. In this
research field, natural APIs fulfill two key roles in CAM systems:
as primary APIs to enhance their own druggability and as CAM coformers
to improve the druggability of insoluble synthetic drugs.
CAM
systems offer a unique avenue for improving the druggability
of insoluble natural APIs while preserving their molecular structures,
thereby facilitating their commercialization and clinical application.
Using natural APIs as coamorphous coformers also provides an opportunity
to enhance the druggability of other insoluble drugs. In this work,
we provide a comprehensive overview of the preparation methods and
characterization techniques utilized in natural API CAM systems. We
then discuss the applications of natural API CAM systems in detail.
In reported natural API CAM systems, natural APIs have been studied
as both primary drugs and coformers. Hence, we categorize reported
natural product CAM systems based on their roles in CAM systems and
discuss some of them in detail. Finally, we address the opportunities
and challenges associated with the advancement of natural API CAM
systems. We hope this review inspires progress in the development
and utilization of natural APIs, especially those sourced from traditional
Chinese medicine.

## Preparation of Natural API CAM Systems
The preparation
method for natural API CAM systems is categorized
into two types based on the use of solvent: solvent-based and nonsolvent-based
methods. In the solvent-based method, the raw materials of CAM systems
are first dissolved in a suitable solvent, which are then rapidly
removed using various techniques. Conversely, in the nonsolvent-based
method, CAM systems are obtained by either directly disrupting the
crystal lattice of crystalline materials or cooling molten raw crystalline
materials. The choice of the preparation method for natural API CAM
systems is dictated by the physicochemical properties of the parent
materials.

## Solvent-Based Method
The solvent-based method is widely
employed in the production of
CAM systems, which is categorized as a solvent removing method and
solvent-assisted grinding method ( ). For the solvent removing method, the components
of the CAM system are initially dissolved in an appropriate solvent,
after which the solvent is removed using various techniques. Based
on the method of solvent removal, the solvent-based approach is classified
into solvent evaporation, spray drying, and freeze-drying methods.
The solvent-assisted grinding method, just as its name implies, is
conducted by grinding the physical mixture constituted drug CAM system
with the presence of drops of solvent.
 

## Solvent Evaporation Method
The solvent evaporation
method stands out as one of the most frequently employed techniques
for fabricating CAM systems in laboratory settings owing to its simplicity
and minimal equipment requirements. Among the various approaches,
reduced-pressure rotary evaporation is the most commonly used. This
method facilitates the formation of coamorphous systems by rapidly
removing the solvent from a solution containing coamorphous raw materials.
The rapid solvent evaporation prevents crystallization, leading to
the precipitation of solutes in an amorphous state. Several critical
parameters must be carefully controlled when this method is employed,
including temperature, pressure, rotation speed, and solvent type.
Gao et al. fabricated CAM repaglinide–saccharin by slowly evaporating
a methanol solution containing a mixture of repaglinide and saccharin
under ambient conditions. They examined
the formation of CAM repaglinide–saccharin at various molar
ratios, including 3:1, 2:1, 1:1, 1:2, and 1:3, observing that the
CAM system only formed at a molar ratio of 1:1. To bolster the manufacturing
efficiency of CAM systems, the solvent may be removed from the solution
containing a mixture of the API and its CAM coformer using a vacuum
rotary evaporator and elevated temperature. In our previous research,
we developed CAM systems of sinomenine with phenolic acids, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, tranilast, and
antibiotics using anhydrous methanol
as the solvent via vacuum rotary evaporation at 60 °C. The resulting
samples were subsequently dried under vacuum in an oven overnight
to eliminate residual solvent for further investigation.

## Spray Drying
Spray drying is distinguished as a continuous
and large-scale method for manufacturing natural API CAM systems.
In this process, a solution containing the raw materials required
for CAM formation is atomized into fine droplets, which rapidly evaporate
upon contact with hot air. The fast solvent removal prevents the solutes
from adopting a crystalline structure, resulting in the formation
of an amorphous powdered product. Several key parameters influence
the production of CAM systems via spray drying including solvent type,
feed rate, and drying temperature. Alhajj and colleagues dissolved
ciprofloxacin and quercetin in water and ethanol, respectively. They
subsequently obtained amorphous forms of ciprofloxacin and quercetin,
as well as their coamorphous systems with molar ratios of 2:1, 1:1,
and 1:2, by carefully controlling the feedstocks during spray drying. The in vitro aerosolization
study demonstrated that the spray-dried coamorphous ciprofloxacin–quercetin
powders exhibit favorable aerosol performance, suggesting their potential
as inhalants for treating cystic fibrosis. However, this approach
is limited to coamorphization of thermally stable drugs and faces
challenges such as solvent residue, high costs, and low yields.

## Freeze-Drying
Freeze-drying presents itself as a low-temperature
method for producing CAM systems, particularly suitable for coamorphization
of thermally unstable natural APIs. In this process, solutions containing
the raw materials for CAM formation are first prefrozen into a solid
state, leading to the random distribution of solutes. As the solvent
undergoes sublimation under reduced temperature and pressure conditions,
the solutes retain their noncrystalline arrangement, ultimately yielding
coamorphous samples. Using this method, four CAM systems involving
sinapic acid and amino acids have been successfully prepared using
freeze-drying. In short, sinapic acid
and amino acids were initially dissolved in a mixture of methanol
and water (30:70, v/v). Subsequently, the resulting solutions were
prefrozen and subjected to freeze-drying at −55 °C. However,
the initial dissolution of water-insoluble natural APIs involves the
use of organic solvents. Commonly used organic solvents, such as methanol
(−97.8 °C), have low melting points, which impose greater
demands on the equipment utilized in the prefreezing and freeze-drying
procedures. Fortunately, tert-butanol (TBA) is one
of the few organic solvents suitable for freeze-drying due to its
high melting point of 25 °C. Mixtures of TBA and water in different
volume ratios, or pure TBA, have been used to manufacture stabilized
powders of marketed pharmaceutical products, suggesting their promising application in the production of CAM
systems in the future.

## Solvent-Assisted Grinding
Solvent-assisted grinding
finds application in the preparation of natural API CAM systems on
a small scale in laboratory settings. This method combines solvent-mediated
and mechanical-force-induced lattice disruption, effectively breaking
the periodic molecular arrangement of the crystalline raw materials
and thereby facilitating the formation of CAM systems. The efficiency
of CAM system formation via solvent-assisted grinding depends on key
parameters, such as solvent type and volume, grinding duration, and
intensity. Skieneh et al. employed this method to craft a CAM system
of curcumin with folic acid dihydrate, resulting in enhanced solubility. In brief, the curcumin–folic
acid dihydrate CAM system was achieved by cogrinding equimolar mixtures
of curcumin and folic acid dihydrate using a mortar and pestle, with
the inclusion of 5 drops of methanol. When no solvent is involved,
this method transforms into a nonsolvent-based method, which will
be discussed in detail in the following section.

## Nonsolvent-Based Method
The solvent-free method offers
an environmentally friendly approach
for preparing natural API CAM systems by employing both kinetic and
thermodynamic methods ( ). The kinetic approach involves converting crystalline drugs
into amorphous forms by directly disrupting their lattice under mechanical
stress. Common strategies include ball milling and cryo-milling. On
the other hand, classical thermodynamic methods such as quench cooling
and hot melt extrusion are utilized in the thermodynamic approach.
 

## Ball Milling and Cryo-Milling
Ball milling is a widely
employed method for screening and preparing drug CAM systems. The
intense mechanical forces generated during milling disrupt the periodic
molecular arrangement of crystalline materials, facilitating their
direct transition into the amorphous state. The successful formation
of CAM systems depends on key parameters, such as milling frequency,
duration, ball size, and temperature. Minode et al. utilized a planetary
ball mill to prepare CAM systems of quercetin and amino acids. Their experiments were conducted under argon
gas conditions for various periods, following a cycle procedure of
10 min of milling, followed by 10 min of cooling. This cooling interval
is implemented to prevent system overheating and the recrystallization
process of formed CAM systems at elevated temperatures. However, due
to the thermal effects generated during milling, routine ball milling
may not be suitable for coamorphization of all drugs. The heat produced
can lead to the degradation of thermolabile drugs and impede the conversion
of crystalline drugs into their amorphous states.
Cryo-milling
effectively mitigates the aforementioned issues. Moreover, drugs become
more brittle under cryogenic conditions, facilitating their transition
from crystalline to amorphous forms. This
technique has been applied to produce CAM systems for certain synthetic
drugs. For instance, CAM systems of indomethacin and several amino
acids were prepared via cryo-milling to identify the most suitable
low-molecular-weight stabilizer for amorphous indomethacin. In exploring the coamorphization of natural
APIs, cryo-milling offers a dependable approach, particularly beneficial
for thermolabile compounds like artemisinin.

## Quench Colling and Hot Melt Extrusion
Quench cooling,
a method used to rapidly prepare small batches of amorphous samples,
is commonly used in laboratory environments for screening CAM systems.
In this method, the raw materials are heated until a homogeneous molten
mass is formed, followed by rapid cooling using liquid nitrogen or
dry ice. This rapid cooling inhibits crystallization, preserving the
glassy state and enabling the formation of coamorphous products. Key
parameters, such as the melting temperature and duration, are crucial
for ensuring complete melting while minimizing the risk of thermal
degradation of the raw materials. For instance, in the production
of CAM systems involving resveratrol and piperine, 1000 mg of crystalline
mixtures with fixed molar ratios of resveratrol and piperine was initially
melted at 160 °C, followed by immediate quenching using liquid
nitrogen.

Hot melt extrusion is
a technology enabling mass and continuous production of amorphous
products, sharing similar principles to the quench cooling method
in the fabrication of CAM systems. This approach has demonstrated
significant success in producing amorphous solid dispersions (ASD),
offering an alternative for preparing drug CAM systems. For instance,
Butreddy et al. successfully enhanced the solubility of nifedipine
by preparing a hydroxypropyl methylcellulose acetate succinate-based
ASD using this method. However, it is
important to note that both quench cooling and hot melt extrusion
are only suitable for thermostable drugs due to their high-temperature
operation.

## Characterization of a Natural API CAM System
To ensure
the successful acquisition of a natural API CAM system
for further study, the prepared samples must undergo characterization
by using various techniques. The commonly employed methods are outlined
below.

## Powder X-ray Diffraction (Powder XRD)
Powder XRD is
among the most commonly utilized techniques for characterizing natural
API CAM systems, providing insights into the complete amorphization
of raw materials. Crystalline materials typically display numerous
diffraction peaks in their PXRD patterns, whereas amorphous samples
exhibit characteristic halos. For instance, in the case of the platensimycin–berberine
chloride CAM system, platensimycin and berberine chloride were initially
codissolved in absolute methanol and evaporated using a vacuum rotary
evaporator. The resulting sample was
then analyzed via PXRD. The coevaporated sample lost the characteristic
diffraction peaks of its raw materials, with a distinct amorphous
peak emerging in its PXRD pattern, indicating successful amorphization.
Moreover, PXRD serves as a crucial method for assessing the physical
stability of natural API CAM systems. Following storage under various
conditions for different durations, the PXRD patterns are collected
and examined. The presence of crystalline diffraction peaks indicates
poor physical stability, whereas their absence suggests excellent
physical stability. For example, Chen et al. developed CAM systems
of simvastatin and nifedipine utilizing epigallocatechin-3-gallate
as a coformer via the spray drying method. These CAM systems were stored under three conditions: dry conditions
at 25 and 40 °C and 75% relative humidity (RH) at 40 °C.
After 6 months of storage, neither simvastatin–epigallocatechin-3-gallate
nor nifedipine–epigallocatechin-3-gallate exhibited any crystalline
signals in their PXRD patterns, indicating satisfactory physical stability.

## Thermal Analysis
Differential scanning calorimetry
(DSC), modulated temperature DSC (mDSC), and thermogravimetric analysis
(TGA) stand out as commonly employed thermal analysis techniques for
exploring natural API CAM systems. The glass transition temperatures
(T
g) derived from DSC and mDSC curves
not only yield crucial thermodynamic parameters but also furnish vital
insights to verify the formation of homogeneous amorphous forms. For
instance, in the investigation of the docetaxel–myricetin CAM
system, the coevaporated sample exhibited a single T
g, accompanied by the disappearance of the melting peaks
of its raw materials, affirming the creation of a homogeneous docetaxel–myricetin
CAM system. However, it is worth noting
that the glass transition process of an amorphous sample is reversible,
which can sometimes pose challenges in its identification from the
DSC curve due to the presence of nonreversible heat flow interference.
In comparison to DSC, mDSC offers three distinct heat flow curves:
total heat flow, reversing heat flow, and nonreversing heat flow.
Total heat flow conveys information identical to that from DSC. The
reversing heat flow curve unveils reversible thermal events during
the heating process, such as the glass transition, facilitating the
straightforward identification of T
g signals
in amorphous samples. For instance, Hu et al. investigated a tranilast–matrine
CAM system, where a single T
g emerged
on its reversing heat flow curve, accompanied by the absence of melting
peaks, indicating the complete coamorphization of the raw materials.

TGA stands as another pivotal thermal
analysis technique for natural
API CAM systems, furnishing valuable insights into parameters such
as thermostability and solvent residuals in the prepared samples.
These findings are instrumental in optimizing manufacturing techniques
for natural API CAM systems. For instance, in the investigation of
the docetaxel–myricetin CAM system, TGA was employed to explore
its thermal degradation behavior.

## Scanning Electron Microscopy
Scanning electron microscopy
(SEM) is utilized to analyze the surface micromorphology of natural
API CAM systems. CAM systems exhibit distinctly different micromorphological
characteristics compared to those of their raw crystalline materials,
sometimes influenced by their preparation techniques. In microtopography
measurements of ursolic acid–piperine CAM systems prepared
via the solvent evaporation method, CAM samples displayed spherical
characteristics with particle sizes ranging from 500 to 1250 nm. Similarly, sphere-shaped morphology was observed
in solvent-assisted ground curcumin–piperazine CAM systems. Conversely, the quench-cooled curcumin–piperine
CAM system exhibited irregular block shapes, differing from the columnar-like
and spherical or ellipsoidal shapes of crystalline curcumin and piperine,
respectively. However, SEM is not ideal
for rapidly collecting surface microcosmic information on CAM systems
due to its complex sample preparation process and strict instrument
requirements.

## Infrared and Raman Spectroscopy
Fourier transform infrared
(FTIR) and Raman spectroscopy are commonly employed technologies for
elucidating the dynamics of intermolecular interactions within compounds
constituting natural API CAM systems. Following coamorphization, the
FTIR characteristic stretching vibration signals of raw materials
undergo alterations corresponding to changes in their molecular arrangement.
The original intermolecular interactions are disrupted, giving rise
to new interactions within the CAM systems. In instances in which
CAM systems exhibit salt-forming interactions, FTIR spectroscopy enables
the identification of signals from ionized functional groups. For
example, in the FTIR spectra of CAM systems involving sinomenine and
phenolic acids, the emergence of symmetrical and asymmetric stretching
vibrations of CO2
– suggests the formation
of salts between sinomenine and phenolic acids.

Raman spectra are generated by changes in polarizability
during the vibration process, offering insights complementary to those
of FTIR spectra. In FTIR spectra, polar groups usually manifest strong
signals, while they appear weaker in Raman spectra. Conversely, nonpolar
groups may present strong signals in the Raman spectra. Han et al.
synthesized a curcumin–magnolol CAM system and analyzed its
Raman spectrum. Within the curcumin–magnolol
CAM system, the vibration of the phenolic OH group of curcumin vanished,
while the CO vibration signal was weakened and broadened at
1600 cm–1. Additionally, the absorption peak of
C–H (1316 cm–1) in magnolol shifted to 1310
cm–1, indicating potential interactions, such as
hydrogen bond interactions, between curcumin and magnolol.
It
is important to note that the intermolecular interactions among
compounds in CAM systems may be misidentified when using FTIR or Raman
spectroscopy. This issue arises particularly when comparing the spectral
data of CAM systems to those of their original crystalline materials.
The amorphization process of crystalline drugs disrupts their original
intermolecular interactions, resulting in different spectral characteristics.
Consequently, changes in the spectra cannot be definitively attributed
to new intermolecular interactions within the CAM systems unless there
is evidence of new functional group stretching vibration signals.
Therefore, obtaining the spectra of the amorphous forms of raw
materials is crucial for accurately exploring the intermolecular interactions
within the CAM systems. By comparing these spectra, researchers can
better understand whether observed spectral changes are due to new
interactions within the CAM systems or simply a result of the amorphization
process. However, if obtaining spectra of amorphous raw materials
is not feasible, comparing the spectra of CAM systems to those of
the raw crystalline materials can still offer insights into the intermolecular
interactions within the CAM systems. This is because the coamorphization
of crystalline raw materials inherently involves the formation of
intermolecular interactions. These considerations underscore the complexity
of characterizing CAM systems and emphasize the need for careful analysis
and interpretation of the spectroscopic data.

## X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy
X-ray photoelectron
spectroscopy (XPS) serves as another powerful tool for uncovering
the underlying intermolecular interactions within natural API CAM
systems by measuring changes in the bonding energies of relevant elements.
Upon formation of CAM systems, the chemical environment of raw materials
undergoes alterations, resulting in changes in the bonding energy
of their elements. Significant changes in the bonding energy often
occur in elements undergoing valency changes. In the investigation
of sinomenine–tranilast CAM systems, three CAM systems with
molar ratios of 1:2, 1:1, and 2:1 were prepared and analyzed using
XPS. The tertiary nitrogen peak of sinomenine
shifted from 399.187 to approximately 402 eV in each CAM system, indicating
its protonation and the formation of a salt between sinomenine and
tranilast.

## Nuclear Magnetic Resonance
Nuclear magnetic resonance
(NMR) plays a pivotal role in gaining insights into the formation
of natural API CAM systems. Depending on the sample state, NMR techniques
are categorized into solid-state NMR (ssNMR) and liquid-state NMR.
Among them, 13C ssNMR is the most widely utilized ssNMR
technology in studying natural API CAM systems, providing carbon chemical
shift (ppm) information to elucidate intermolecular interactions.
The transition from raw crystalline materials to CAM systems induces
alternative molecular aggregation modes, triggering changes in the
chemical environment of carbons and resulting in chemical shift movements.
For instance, Yu et al. fabricated a CAM system of ursolic acid with
piperine and analyzed its 13C ss NMR spectrum. Compared with crystalline ursolic acid and piperine,
the ursolic acid–piperine CAM system exhibited significantly
broadened carbon peaks. The chemical shifts of C31 and C2 in ursolic
acid shifted from 178.5 and 80.15 ppm to 186.6 and 78.9 ppm, respectively,
while the C8* peak of piperine shifted from 119.0 to 119.3 ppm. Due
to a wide range of molecular orientations, amorphous samples display
broader carbon peaks compared to their crystalline counterparts. These
peak shifts of carbon atoms provide reliable evidence for revealing
the intermolecular interactions within the components of natural API
CAM systems.
In addition, 15N ssNMR is a powerful
technology for exploring intermolecular interactions among solid-state
samples and is widely used in the investigation of drug crystalline
forms, such as cocrystals and salts. However,
this technology is still rarely applied in the study of CAM systems.
To our knowledge, the rare case is the omeprazole–amoxicillin
1:1 CAM system. Therefore, 15N ssNMR should be more widely utilized to explore the potential intermolecular
interactions within CAM systems.
However, ssNMR spectra may
also provide inaccurate intermolecular
interaction information in CAM systems, similar to the limitations
observed with FTIR and Raman results. The amorphization of crystalline
drugs destroys the initial intermolecular interactions, such as hydrogen
bonding, altering the chemical environment of each element and causing
shifts in their signal peaks. When analyzing the ssNMR spectra of
CAM systems using those of their crystalline raw materials as references,
it cannot be confirmed whether the peak shifts of certain atoms are
due to the amorphization of the crystalline raw materials or coamorphization.
Therefore, to draw accurate conclusions about the intermolecular
interactions within CAM systems, it is essential to employ the ssNMR
spectra of amorphous raw materials as references or combine the ssNMR
spectra with other solid-state characterization techniques, such as
FTIR, Raman, and XPS, to provide a comprehensive understanding of
the intermolecular interactions within CAM systems. However, when
amorphous raw materials are unavailable, the ssNMR spectra of crystalline
raw materials can still serve as a reference, providing valuable insights
into the intermolecular interactions within the CAM system.
Liquid-state NMR provides valuable insights into the intermolecular
interactions among components within natural API CAM systems when
the systems are in solution. For instance, in the study of sinomenine–tranilast
CAM systems, both 1D (1H NMR) and 2D NMR spectra (including
2D NOESY and 2D DOSY) were collected. Significant chemical shift variations observed in proton peaks within
the 1H NMR spectra suggested strong intermolecular interactions
between sinomenine and tranilast. Additionally, the appearance of
cross-correlation peaks between protons in sinomenine and tranilast
in the 2D NOESY spectra, along with changes in diffusion coefficients
observed in the 2D DOSY spectra of all CAM systems, indicated the
association and aggregation between the compounds.
This technique
is also employed to elucidate the mechanism behind
changes in the solubility of compounds within CAM systems at the intermolecular
interaction level. For instance, Hu et al. dissolved a tranilast–matrine
CAM system and its raw materials in deuteroxide and obtained their 1H NMR spectra. In the mixed tranilast–matrine
system, all proton peaks of tranilast shifted upfield, with the most
significant shift observed in H2 (Δδ = 0.51 ppm), indicating
strong intermolecular interactions between tranilast and matrine.
These robust intermolecular interactions may play a crucial role in
the considerable enhancement of tranilast solubility in the tranilast–matrine
CAM system.

## Applications
The coamorphization process disrupts the
periodic regular arrangement
of molecules in crystalline natural APIs, resulting in the formation
of CAM systems characterized by short-range order and long-range disorder
molecular aggregation. Alterations in molecular stacking patterns
lead to varied physicochemical properties, including solubility, permeability,
and stability, consequently influencing the bioavailability and pharmaceutical
efficacy in vivo. With the potential for enhanced
physical properties, the applications of CAM systems are numerous
and expanding continuously.

## Solubility Management
Due to the absence of lattice
energy constraints, compounds within CAM systems typically exhibit
easier release in aqueous mediums and demonstrate higher solubility
compared to their crystalline forms. Consequently, this approach is
being explored with the primary goal of enhancing the water solubility
of poorly soluble drugs. A series of previous studies have confirmed
successful enhancements in the solubility of both BCS Class II and
Class IV drugs through coamorphization. Similarly, the solubility
of some insoluble natural APIs has also been improved following coamorphization.
For instance, curcumin, an insoluble phytochemical polyphenol found
in turmeric rhizomes (Curcuma longa), exhibited enhanced solubility through the formation of CAM systems
with piperine, folic acid dihydrate, ascorbic acid, and chlorogenic
acid.
,,,,
 Furthermore, natural
APIs are utilized as CAM coformers to improve the solubility of insoluble
synthetic drugs. For example, the solubility of florfenicol was enhanced
after coamorphization with oxymatrine at molar ratios of 1:1 and 1:2. Both CAM systems also exhibited significantly
improved intrinsic dissolution rates compared with crystalline florfenicol.
Recently, several reports have highlighted that the formation of
CAM systems does not always lead to an improvement in drug solubility.
In fact, instances of decreased solubility have been observed in CAM
systems. For example, after coamorphization, both curcumin and magnolol
in a curcumin–magnolol CAM system exhibited lower solubilities
compared to their crystalline forms. This
decrease in solubility can be attributed to the aggregation behavior
in aqueous mediums. Similar reductions in solubility have been reported
in CAM systems of sinomenine, attributed to the formation of N+–H-mediated gels upon contact with water.
−

 Other CAM systems involving natural APIs, such as platensimycin–berberine
chloride and matrine-type alkaloids–resveratrol, have also demonstrated similar solubility reduction
behavior.

## Release Behavior Management
The formation of CAM systems
also presents an opportunity to modulate the release behaviors of
drugs. Typically, natural API CAM systems exhibit an increased release
rate compared to their crystalline counterparts due to their enhanced
solubility. For instance, in the ursolic acid–piperine CAM
system, ursolic acid demonstrated higher solubility compared to its
crystalline form in simulated gastric fluid (SGF) and simulated intestinal
fluid (SIF). In release experiments,
51% and 64% of ursolic acid were released from its CAM system into
SGF and SIF after 2 h, respectively, significantly higher than the
release from crystalline ursolic acid (6.7% and 7.45%). However, since
solubility is a thermodynamic property and release behavior is a kinetic
property, compounds with enhanced solubility in CAM systems may sometimes
exhibit decreased release behavior. For example, in the CAM system
of sinomenine and naproxen, the solubility of sinomenine increased
from 4.6 to 7.5 mg/mL in pH 6.8 phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), while
its cumulative release decreased from 84.1% to 67.1% after 6 h of
release experiments.

In natural
API CAM systems exhibiting a decreased solubility, sustained release
behaviors are often observed. The mechanisms underlying the decreased
solubility and sustained-release behavior of CAM systems are incompletely
understood. However, current literature suggests that gelatinization
of CAM systems during dissolution, as
well as the rapid recrystallization of insoluble components leading
to the formation of a “shell-like” structure, plays a key role in decelerating drug dissolution.
For instance, in the investigation of the platensimycin–berberine
chloride CAM system, while crystalline platensimycin completely released
into pH 6.8 PBS within 2 h in in vitro release experiments,
only 15.9% of platensimycin was released from the platensimycin–berberine
chloride CAM system after 6 h of testing. This sustained release behavior of platensimycin was further validated in vivo, where the half-life of platensimycin (2.2 h) from
the platensimycin–berberine chloride CAM system was twice as
long as that of crystalline platensimycin (0.96 h) in rats. Therefore,
coamorphization offers a convenient and promising approach for the
development of sustained-release formulations of drugs.

## Bioavailability Enhancement
The bioavailability of
drugs is a critical factor influencing their druggability and clinical
applications, often constrained by their solubility and permeability.
For BCS Class II drugs, a solubility improvement directly enhances
bioavailability. For instance, coamorphization with epigallocatechin-3-gallate
significantly enhanced the bioavailability of BCS class II drugs like
simvastatin and nifedipine through solubility enhancement. Compared to crystalline simvastatin, the C
max and AUC0–24h of simvastatin
from its CAM system improved from 8.43 ± 3.31 ng/mL and 19.11
± 8.83 ng·h/mL to 15.32 ± 8.79 ng/mL and 31.10 ±
9.81 ng·h/mL, respectively. CAM nifedipine–epigallocatechin-3-gallate
exhibited a C
max and AUC0–24h of nifedipine at 2495.00 ± 325.99 and 11571.21 ± 1898.56
ng·h/mL, respectively, significantly higher than those of crystalline
nifedipine (438.33 ± 70.87 and 2527.86 ± 1268.29 ng·h/mL,
respectively).
However, the bioavailability of BCS Class IV
drugs faces a double restriction imposed by their limited solubility
and permeability, making them less amenable to enhancement through
solubility improvement alone. BCS Class IV drugs typically serve as
substrates for transporters (P-gp) and metabolic enzymes (CYP3A4),
rendering them prone to efflux and metabolism. Consequently, coformers
such as flavonoid compounds and piperazine, known P-gp or CYP3A4 inhibitors,
are employed in CAM systems with BCS Class IV drugs to provide a dual
strategy for enhancing both solubility and permeability.
,,
 For example, talinolol, used
for hypertension and heart failure, is a P-gp substrate. Teja et al.
developed CAM systems for talinolol using the natural P-gp inhibitor
naringin. In pharmacokinetic experiments,
the 1:1 talinolol–naringin CAM system exhibited C
max and AUC0‑t
 values
of talinolol at 778.35 ± 263.92 ng/mL and 2352.38 ± 164.35
ng·h/mL, respectively, representing 8.6- and 5.4-fold increases
compared to crystalline talinolol, respectively.

## Synergy Therapy
Natural APIs harbor abundant bioactivities,
presenting an opportunity for synergistic disease therapy through
coamorphization with other APIs. Li et al. developed a CAM system
utilizing the natural anti-inflammatory ingredients apigenin and oxymatrine. In in vitro anti-inflammatory
activity experiments, the apigenin–oxymatrine CAM system notably
decreased the expression inhibition of inflammatory factors such as
IL-6, TNF-α, MCP-1, and COX-2, compared to apigenin or oxymatrine
alone. Furthermore, three CAM systems containing first-line nonsteroidal
anti-inflammatory drugs (indomethacin, naproxen, and sulindac) along
with the natural antirheumatic sinomenine were prepared, offering
a potential strategy for combination treatment of rheumatoid arthritis. In an effort to provide synergy therapy for
cystic fibrosis triggered by P. aeruginosa-induced recurrent lung infections, Alhajj et al. formulated inhalable
CAM systems using the fluoroquinolone antibiotic ciprofloxacin and
the flavonoid antibiotic quercetin.
In vitro aerosolization studies demonstrated that ciprofloxacin–quercetin
CAM systems exhibited desirable aerosol performance (ED ∼ 72–94%;
FPF ∼ 48–65%), whereas quercetin-only amorphous powder
had an ED of 36% and an FPF of 22%.

## Physical Stability Improvement
The physical stability
of amorphous drugs is a critical factor that affects their storage
and application. For instance, their solubility advantage can be compromised
by their transformation from a high internal energy amorphous state
to a stable crystalline form. The formation of CAM systems offers
a promising approach for stabilizing the solid state of amorphous
drugs, facilitated by mechanisms such as ideal miscibility, reduced
molecular mobility, enhanced T
g, and strong
intermolecular interactions. Han et al.
investigated the physical stability of the curcumin–piperine
CAM system using PXRD and further elucidated its underlying mechanisms
through molecular dynamics (MD) simulations at 298 and 313 K. Amorphous curcumin exhibited noticeable crystalline
diffraction peaks after 30 days of storage under 40 °C conditions.
Similarly, after 10 days of storage at both 25 and 40 °C, amorphous
piperine showed strong diffraction peaks. However, even after 90 days
of experimentation, the curcumin–piperine CAM system still
maintained a typically amorphous PXRD pattern, indicating its improved
physical stability. MD simulation revealed that the binding energies
between curcumin and piperine were 261.157 and 278.152 kcal/mol at
298 and 313 K, respectively, higher than those between curcumin and
curcumin (232.494 and 258.349 kcal/mol) or piperine and piperine (165.917
and 193.520 kcal/mol). This suggests that the curcumin–piperine
CAM system exhibits stronger intermolecular interactions compared
with amorphous curcumin and piperine, contributing to its superior
physical stability. In the case of CAM systems involving sinomenine
with platensimycin or sulfasalazine, after six months of storage under
25 °C and 75% relative humidity (RH) conditions, both sinomenine–platensimycin
and sinomenine–sulfasalazine remained free from recrystallization,
while amorphous sinomenine almost completely recrystallized after
2 weeks. The reduced molecular mobility
of both CAM systems compared with amorphous sinomenine, as revealed
by enthalpy relaxation studies, was identified as one of the reasons
for their improved physical stability.

## Reported CAM Systems Containing Natural APIs
In recent
years, the druggability of insoluble natural APIs has
been enhanced through the construction of their CAM systems, facilitating
their further commercial development and clinical application. Additionally,
some natural APIs are utilized as coformers for coamorphization with
other drugs based on their physicochemical properties and bioactivities.
In this section, we provide a summary and discussion of several natural
API CAM systems, categorizing them according to the role of natural
APIs within the CAM systems.

## CAM Systems with Natural Products as the Primary APIs
Natural APIs represent a cornerstone in drug development, owing to
their diverse bioactivities. Nonetheless, their limited druggability
presents a significant obstacle to their broader therapeutic application.
To address this challenge, we employed CAM technology to refine the
physicochemical properties of certain natural APIs. The corresponding
CAM systems reported thus far are summarized in .
 Curcumin ( ), extracted from turmeric rhizomes, boasts a range
of notable physiological
activities, including antioxidative, antitumor, hypolipidemic, hypoglycemic,
antiulcer, antibacterial, anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and antifungal
properties, suggesting potential for treating cancer, diabetes, coronary
heart disease, arthritis, Alzheimer’s disease, and other chronic
ailments. However, its therapeutic efficacy
is severely hampered by its exceedingly low water solubility. To address
this challenge, various curcumin-based CAM systems have been developed,
including curcumin–folic acid dihydrate, curcumin–piperazine, curcumin–artemisinin, curcumin–piperine, and curcumin–ascorbic acid. These systems have demonstrated an improved
curcumin solubility. For instance, the curcumin–piperazine
CAM system exhibited an enhanced dissolution rate. Further investigations indicated that the interaction forces
between curcumin and bovine serum albumin weakened upon CAM formation,
potentially increasing the unbound fraction of curcumin in bovine
serum and thereby influencing its absorption, distribution, metabolism,
and excretion properties. Additionally, the oral bioavailability of
the curcumin–piperine CAM system was evaluated in Sprague–Dawley
(SD) rats, revealing a 2.64-fold increase in C
max and a 2.16-fold increase in AUC0–24h compared
to crystalline curcumin. Furthermore,
the bioactivity of the curcumin–artemisinin CAM system against
tumors was assessed using an in vivo xenograft model
of PANC-1 tumors. The CAM system inhibited
approximately 62% of tumor growth at an oral curcumin dose of 100
mg/kg, demonstrating efficacy comparable to that of the positive control
drug doxorubicin (2 mg/kg), administered intravenously.
 Recent studies have reported an unexpectedly decreased
dissolution
behavior of amorphous curcumin, prompting the exploration of gelation-elimination
strategies. Mannava et al. developed a CAM system, which exhibited
significantly reduced solubility compared to crystalline curcumin
due to aggregation during dissolution. This aggregation phenomenon was effectively mitigated by incorporating
small amounts of polymers (HPMC, HPC, and PVP K30) into the curcumin/magnolol
CAM system. Similarly, Han et al. aimed to enhance the curcumin solubility
by preparing its amorphous form. However, it unexpectedly exhibited
lower dissolution than its crystalline counterpart due to gel formation
during dissolution. This gelation issue
was successfully addressed through coamorphization with chlorogenic
acid, resulting in 3.10- and 3.13-fold solubility enhancements compared
to crystalline curcumin in pH 1.2 and pH 6.8 dissolution media, respectively.
Sinomenine ( ), a natural API derived from Caulis sinomenii,
boasts a range of bioactivities, including anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive,
anticancer, heart-protective, pain-relieving effects, and others. Its hydrochloride-based formulations have been
used in China for years to treat rheumatoid arthritis. However, sinomenine
hydrochloride suffers from issues such as low oral bioavailability,
short half-life in vivo, and side effects due to
histamine release. These challenges can potentially be mitigated by
slowing its release rate. Consequently, a series of sinomenine CAM
systems incorporating phenolic acids, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, tranilast, and antibiotics were developed to tackle these challenges, offering
a novel potential strategy for synergistic therapy of rheumatoid arthritis
simultaneously. Each CAM system exhibited sustained release behavior
compared to that of commercial sinomenine hydrochloride. The decreased
dissolution behavior was attributed not only to the properties of
salt-forming ligands but also to the gel formation observed during
the dissolution process of sinomene CAM systems. For instance, while
sinomenine hydrochloride completely dissolved within 10 min in pH
6.8 PBS, only 36.4%, 67.1%, and 45.3% of sinomenine were released
from the sinomenine–indomethacin, sinomenine–naproxen,
and sinomenine–sulindac CAM systems, respectively, after 6
h of experimentation. Furthermore, some
sinomenine CAM systems demonstrated even slower release rates than
crystalline sinomenine, suggesting that CAM technology holds promise
as an approach for developing sustained-release preparations.
 To mitigate histamine release-mediated side effects,
tranilast,
an antihistamine, was utilized to construct dual-drug CAM systems
with sinomenine. These systems exhibited
satisfactory physical stability under 75% relative humidity at 25
°C and demonstrated sustained-release properties. Furthermore,
other dual-drug CAM systems were developed to address complex therapeutic
needs: sinomenine-NSAID CAM systems were designed for multitargeted
rheumatoid arthritis therapy, while sinomenine–antibiotic
CAM systems were formulated to treat infection-associated rheumatoid
arthritis.

Moreover, the CAM systems
of other natural APIs were also reported
in the literature. Most of these systems demonstrated enhanced druggability
due to improved physicochemical and pharmacokinetic properties. For
instance, Yu et al. developed a CAM system of ursolic acid and piperine
to enhance the solubility and oral bioavailability of ursolic acid, resulting in a 5.8-fold improvement in the AUC(0–24) value compared to that of crystalline ursolic
acid. The increased bioavailability is attributed to the enhanced
water solubility and permeability of ursolic acid, along with inhibition
of its metabolism by cytochrome P450 isozymes. A few CAM systems exhibited
decreased dissolution behaviors, indicating their potential application
in the development of sustained-release formulations. Hu et al. developed
CAM systems of water-soluble matrine-type alkaloids, such as matrine,
oxymatrine, and sophoridine, with resveratrol. Each of them exhibited a sustained release of matrine-type
alkaloids, potentially enhancing their bioavailability and prolonging
their half-life. The formation of a “shell-like” structure
due to the fast recrystallization of resveratrol during dissolution
hindered the release of matrine-type alkaloids, enabling sustained
release. In conclusion, CAM technology offers a promising approach
for the development and clinical application of natural APIs due to
its effective druggability enhancement capabilities.

## CAM Systems with Natural Products as Coformers
Due
to their safety and bioactivity, natural APIs are also utilized as
coformers in the formation of CAM systems ( ). The solubility of the coformer plays a
critical role in determining the solubility of the drug, as soluble
coformers typically enhance the solubility of poorly soluble drugs.
Consequently, water-soluble natural APIs ( ), such as matrine, oxymatrine, and sophoridine, have been utilized for CAM system fabrication.
For instance, Li et al. developed CAM systems of florfenicol with
oxymatrine at 1:1 and 1:2 molar ratios, resulting in improved dissolution behavior and permeability. In
this combination, oxymatrine also contributed its bioactive properties,
including antibacterial, metabolism-regulating, and antifibrotic effects,
potentially enhancing the clinical efficacy of florfenicol in treating
respiratory and digestive tract diseases such as respiratory infections
in pigs.
 The bioavailability of insoluble drugs remains
a major challenge
for the pharmaceutical industry, directly affecting their in vivo efficacy. A rational selection of CAM systems offers
an effective approach to enhancing drug bioavailability. Flavonoid-based
natural APIs, abundantly found in vegetables, fruits, and herbs, exhibit
diverse biological activities. As natural P-glycoprotein
inhibitors, these flavonoids are incorporated into CAM systems to
inhibit drug excretion and improve the bioavailability of BCS Class
IV drugs. Among them, naringin ( ) is the most frequently used coformer for addressing
drug bioavailability issues.
,,−

 For example, Yarlagadda et al. designed CAM systems
of ceritinib, a BCS Class IV drug, with naringin using molecular simulations
and prepared them at molar ratios of 1:1, 1:2, and 2:1 via the solvent
evaporation method. The resulting CAM
systems exhibited enhanced solubility in FaSSIF and FeSSIF. In vivo pharmacokinetic studies confirmed that the 2:1 ceritinib–naringin
CAM system achieved the highest bioavailability, attributed to the
dual CYP3A4 inhibition by ceritinib (autoinhibition) and naringin.
Additionally, this combination demonstrated proapoptotic effects in
A549 cell lines, significantly inhibiting lung cancer cell proliferation,
migration, and metastasis in vitro compared with
pure ceritinib.
 To reduce drug toxicity and enhance therapeutic
efficacy, natural
APIs have been incorporated into CAM systems due to their diverse
bioactivities. Prajapati et al. developed a pyrazinamide–naringin
CAM system to mitigate the hepatotoxicity of pyrazinamide, leveraging
naringin’s liver-protective properties. In an in vivo hepatotoxicity study, SD
rats treated with 500 and 1000 mg/kg pyrazinamide exhibited abnormal
hepatic cord arrangement, central vein congestion, cytoplasmic vacuolation,
and nuclear degeneration, indicating severe liver damage. In contrast,
rats receiving the pyrazinamide–naringin CAM system at the
same doses showed restored liver tissue, well-defined hepatocytes,
and prominent nuclei, demonstrating reduced hepatotoxicity of pyrazinamide
through coamorphization with naringin. Kanp et al. constructed a CAM
system of dasatinib with gallic acid, a natural antioxidant and antitumor
agent, to provide an effective synergistic strategy for breast cancer
treatment.
In vitro cytotoxicity
assessments on MCF-7 cells revealed that the CAM system had a 3.42-fold
lower IC50 than free dasatinib. Further studies confirmed
that the CAM system significantly improved mitochondrial membrane
depolarization, apoptotic index, and reactive oxygen species formation
in MCF-7 cells, suggesting a promising alternative approach for enhanced
antitumor activity. Furthermore, Wang et al. designed a platensimycin–berberine
chloride CAM system to enhance the antimicrobial and pharmacokinetic
properties of platensimycin. Killing
checkerboard assays demonstrated that the CAM system exhibited a synergistic
antibacterial effect against methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA). Additionally, in vivo studies revealed a sustained-release profile, with
the CAM system extending the half-life of platensimycin 2-fold compared
to its crystalline form. In summary, incorporating natural APIs into
drug CAM systems offers a formulation strategy that provides dual
benefits in terms of druggability and therapeutic efficacy.

## Concluding Remarks: Limitations and Future Perspectives
Recently, natural APIs have played a significant role in the construction
of CAM systems aimed at enhancing their own druggability or those
of other synthetic drugs. Through coamorphization, their physicochemical
properties such as water solubility, release behaviors, and permeability
can be effectively adjusted, resulting in improved bioavailability
and bioactivities compared to their crystalline counterparts. Overcoming
the poor druggability of natural APIs holds promise for their commercial
development and clinical application. When employed as CAM coformers,
natural APIs contribute to the enhancement of the druggability of
synthetic drugs due to their favorable physicochemical properties
or bioactivities. Furthermore, the prepared CAM systems offer potential
combination drug strategies for the treatment of specific diseases.
Interestingly, some CAM systems involving natural APIs surprisingly
exhibit decreased solubilities and sustained release behaviors in
certain drugs, suggesting the potential of CAM technology in the development
of sustained-release preparations.
CAM technology has shown
promising potential in the development
of insoluble natural APIs and synthetic drugs. However, the design
of CAM systems still relies heavily on trial and error, lacking a
clear theoretical basis for guiding the selection of coformers. Therefore,
there is a pressing need to establish a systematic theoretical framework
for the design of CAM systems in future research endeavors. Additionally,
the intermolecular interactions within CAM systems are not yet fully
understood. For instance, commonly used technologies such as FTIR,
Raman, and ssNMR sometimes provide inaccurate information, as it is
difficult to identify whether the spectral changes in CAM systems
are triggered solely by the amorphization of raw materials or by their
coamorphization in certain CAM systems. Therefore, faster and more
efficient analytical methods, based on experimental devices or theoretical
calculations, should be introduced or developed to address this issue.
Stability is one of the most important parameters of amorphous
forms, including physical and chemical stability. However, to the
best of our knowledge, almost all reported CAM systems have only explored
their physical stability. Therefore, further investigation into the
chemical stability of natural API CAM systems, such as their photostability
and thermostability, is necessary, similar to that studied in drug
crystal engineering.
Furthermore, most reported natural API
CAM systems primarily focus
on their preparation, characterization, and in vitro physicochemical property evaluation, neglecting in vivo studies of their pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics. More in vivo studies should be conducted in future research to
validate the hypothesis of their enhanced bioavailability and therapeutic
efficacy in vivo. The ultimate goal of CAM systems
research is to realize their applications in the pharmaceutical industry.
However, certain crucial aspects of the drug formulation development
process for natural API CAM systems are often overlooked, such as
the compatibility of raw and auxiliary materials, physical stability
under pressure, powder property evaluation, and others. Researchers
in this field should devote more attention to these aspects in order
to bridge the divide between scientific research and medical production.
As more CAM systems are reported, some researchers have observed
that CAM systems prepared by different methods, even with the same
raw materials at the same molar ratio, can exhibit varied physicochemical
properties, including T
g, molecular mobility,
and physical stability.
,
 This observation suggests
the possibility of polymorphism in the CAM systems. However, the potential
polymorphism of CAM systems has not received adequate attention so
far, underscoring the need for future investigations to prioritize
this aspect. Such research efforts are crucial to identifying the
most suitable CAM systems for further commercial development and confirming
their preparation technology. A comprehensive understanding of these
factors is essential for advancing their commercial development and
clinical application.
