## Significance
New classes of orally bioavailable drugs capable of reversing a dysregulated cytokine milieu could be useful therapies for immune disorders like inflammatory bowel disease. The salt-inducible kinases (SIKs) are promising targets in this regard, but developing chemically derived inhibitors of SIK enzymatic function has been hampered by the inability to selectively target subsets of SIK isoforms 1–3. Here, we describe the development of inhibitors that are selective for SIK isoforms 1 and 2 versus 3 and non-SIK kinases. These inhibitors suppress pro-inflammatory while inducing anti-inflammatory cytokine responses and ameliorate disease in a mouse model of colonic inflammation. These studies identify a strategy for developing SIK1/2-selective inhibitors and demonstrate their ability to suppress pathological intestinal inflammation.
Keywords: structure-based drug design, immunological disorders, kinase inhibitors, inflammatory bowel disease, medicinal chemistry

## Abstract
The salt-inducible kinases (SIK) 1–3 are key regulators of pro- versus anti-inflammatory cytokine responses during innate immune activation. The lack of highly SIK-family or SIK isoform-selective inhibitors suitable for repeat, oral dosing has limited the study of the optimal SIK isoform selectivity profile for suppressing inflammation in vivo. To overcome this challenge, we devised a structure-based design strategy for developing potent SIK inhibitors that are highly selective against other kinases by engaging two differentiating features of the SIK catalytic site. This effort resulted in SIK1/2-selective probes that inhibit key intracellular proximal signaling events including reducing phosphorylation of the SIK substrate cAMP response element binding protein (CREB) regulated transcription coactivator 3 (CRTC3) as detected with an internally generated phospho-Ser329-CRTC3-specific antibody. These inhibitors also suppress production of pro-inflammatory cytokines while inducing anti-inflammatory interleukin-10 in activated human and murine myeloid cells and in mice following a lipopolysaccharide challenge. Oral dosing of these compounds ameliorates disease in a murine colitis model. These findings define an approach to generate highly selective SIK1/2 inhibitors and establish that targeting these isoforms may be a useful strategy to suppress pathological inflammation.

The salt-inducible kinases (SIK1, SIK2, and SIK3) are serine (Ser)/threonine (Thr) kinases in the adenosine monophosphate–activated kinase (AMPK) subfamily that are expressed in many human tissues and are linked to diverse physiological processes including metabolism, bone formation, circadian rhythm, skin pigmentation, and tumor formation ( ) and SIK inhibitors may have therapeutic applications in osteoporosis ( ) and oncology ( ). In addition, a compelling body of work has identified the SIKs as key regulators of innate immune activation. Antagonizing SIK function by several structurally unrelated chemical SIK inhibitors with varying kinase selectivity profiles suppresses production of pro-inflammatory cytokines [e.g., tumor necrosis factor (TNF), interleukin (IL)-12/23 p40, and IL-23], and stimulates the expression of the key immuno-regulatory cytokine IL-10 in activated primary human and murine myeloid cells ( – ). Studies with the pan-SIK inhibitor YKL-05-099 demonstrated that the SIK-mediated cytokine effects are recapitulated in vivo without the metabolic effects observed in Sik2 knockout mice ( ). The critical role of SIK inhibitor–responsive cytokines for the pathogenesis of certain chronic inflammatory conditions such as inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) is clearly established by genetic associations of IL12B, IL23R, IL10, or IL10RA variants with IBD ( , ) as well as the clinical successes of neutralizing TNF, IL-12/23p40, and IL-23 p19 ( ). Thus, selective SIK inhibitors have the potential to suppress pathological inflammation associated with aberrant activation of innate immune cells, reverse dysregulation of these cytokines, and restore immune balance in inflammatory conditions, such as Crohn’s disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC) ( ). Of note, Galapagos NV has advanced three SIK family kinase inhibitors into clinical studies for treating immune diseases, with pan-SIK (GLPG3312), SIK2,3 (GLPG3970), and SIK3-selective profiles (GLPG4399) ( ); however, detailed safety and efficacy studies have not been published to date.
Investigations of SIK biology in primary human immune cells have utilized multi-kinase inhibitors that have potent off-target effects on additional kinases linked to immune signaling (e.g., SRC-family kinases) at cell-active concentrations ( , , , ), raising the possibility that polypharmacology may have contributed to the cellular responses. In addition, cell-active SIK inhibitors typically exhibit similar potencies across all three isoforms, and a recently disclosed SIK inhibitor is selective for SIK2/3 ( ). Consequently, there are limited chemical tools available to advance the understanding of the biological consequences of inhibiting specific SIK isoforms. Progress toward elucidating SIK isoform-specific biology has been made by analyzing mice in which specific SIK isoforms were either genetically removed or rendered catalytically inactive. While Sik1/Sik2 kinase-dead knock-in mice are viable, fertile, and healthy ( ), Sik3 kinase-dead knock-in and Sik3 knock-out mice show high neonatal mortality and bone malformation with dwarfism ( , , ). Mice with post-natal global deletion of both Sik2 and Sik3 are viable and display increased trabecular bone mass ( ), whereas global, conditional deletion of all three SIK isoforms leads to hypercalcemia and lethality ( ). Also, rare loss-of-function mutations in the catalytic domain of SIK3 are associated with recessively inherited skeletal dysplasias in humans ( ). Thus, targeting SIK3 alone, or in combination with SIK1 and 2, appears to carry the most severe toxicity risk. Robust up-regulation of IL-10 and suppression of TNF and IL-12/23p40 was observed in murine bone marrow–derived macrophages (BMDMs) from Sik1/Sik2 kinase-dead knock-in mice ( ). Therefore, we hypothesized that SIK1/2-selective inhibitors would maintain potent immunomodulatory activity and potentially confer a safety advantage relative to pan-SIK inhibition.
The development of SIK-selective inhibitors has been limited by potent, off-target inhibition of SRC family kinases, which have high sequence homology with the SIKs and share a small Thr gatekeeper residue (Thr96 in SIK2) in the catalytic site ( , , ). To address this challenge, we identified the small Thr gatekeeper and a nearby charged glutamic acid residue (Glu103) as a combination of amino acids that differentiate the catalytic site of SIKs from other kinases with high homology in the kinase domain. We then employed structure-based drug design to develop a SIK inhibitor chemotype that interacts with both features. This strategy yielded the potent and selective SIK1/2 inhibitors JRD-SIK1/2i-3 and JRD-SIK1/2i-4 that perturb SIK-dependent intracellular signaling leading to elevation of IL-10 and suppression of several pro-inflammatory cytokines, including TNF, IL-12/23p40, and IL-23 in activated human and murine myeloid cells. Oral dosing of JRD-SIK1/2i-4 recapitulated the cytokine modulatory activity of pan-SIK, multi-kinase inhibitors in an acute inflammatory mouse model and suppressed disease in a mouse model of experimentally induced colitis driven by activated myeloid cells. Together, these findings establish a strategy for developing SIK1/2-selective inhibitors and provide proof-of-concept that inhibitors targeting these isoforms may be a viable approach to treat IBD and other chronic inflammatory diseases.

## Results

## Molecular Recognition Strategy to Develop SIK-Selective Inhibitors.
A high-throughput screen (HTS) of the Janssen compound collection was performed using dephosphorylated SIK2 protein (containing kinase and UBA domains) in a cascade assay. The screen identified compound 1 (SIK2 ADP-Glo™ Ki = 0.022 ± 0.013 μM, , Top), an attractive starting point for optimization of a SIK inhibitor chemotype. Compared to previously reported SIK inhibitors, compound 1 has improved ligand efficiency (LE = 0.44) ( ) and lipophilic ligand efficiency (LLE, based on cLogP = 4.6) ( ) and 18-fold selectivity for SIK2 over SIK3 (Ki = 0.406 μM).
 A model of compound 1 bound to SIK2 identified hydrogen bond interactions from the amide NH and pyrazole nitrogen of 1 with the alanine of the SIK2 hinge backbone (Ala132) while the phenyl group is oriented toward the back pocket and the conserved lysine (Lys 49) ( , Bottom). The gatekeeper for all three SIKs is a Thr residue (Thr96 in SIK2), a smaller amino acid residue than the methionine or leucine gatekeeper in other AMPK family kinases (SI Appendix, Table S1). We hypothesized that occupying this back pocket with small substituents would lead to potent SIK inhibitors with selectivity over other AMPK family kinases.
Back pocket substituents were not anticipated to preclude binding to off-target kinases that share the same Thr gatekeeper, such as SRC family kinases (SI Appendix, Table S2). We examined the sequences of the SIK and SRC kinases for residues in the catalytic sites of SRC family kinases that differed significantly from the corresponding SIK residue. The SIK2 Glu103 residue is unique because it corresponds to Glu in all SIKs yet is Ser or cysteine (Cys) in all SRC family kinases and other high homology kinases including BTK and BMX. We hypothesized that the alternate size, shape, and charge of these residues would differentiate this region of the ATP-binding site between SIKs and high homology kinases. Thus, a design strategy was pursued to evaluate the potential for positively charged moieties to achieve differential interactions with the SIK Glu103 versus SRC family Ser or Cys residues. By combining favorable Glu103 interactions with a back pocket substituent, we envisioned identifying SIK inhibitors with very high SIK kinase selectivity.
Screening hit 1 was amenable to testing the selectivity hypothesis, with a position on the phenyl ring situated for directing substituents toward the small SIK Thr gatekeeper residue and the methoxy substituent with a vector for designing selectivity interactions with the unique SIK Glu residue. Initial structure-based optimization of 1 ( ) yielded compound 2 ( ), elaborated with a nitrile substituent directed toward the back pocket, a nitrogen atom positioned to engage in a hydrogen bonding interaction with conserved Lys49, and a one-carbon extension of the methoxy substituent to demonstrate the potential for larger alkoxy substituents. Together, these changes imparted a 20-fold increase in SIK2 potency ( ). Consistent with the high homology between SIK versus SRC family kinases, off-target activity for LCK as a representative SRC family kinase was detected with 19-fold selectivity for SIK2.
The predicted binding pose of compound 2 ( ) identified the alkoxy substituent as the most direct vector to making interactions with the SIK2 Glu103 residue. The hydroxymethyl pyrrolidine analog 3 ( ) was designed to engage the Glu103 carboxylate in a salt bridge ( ) retaining comparable SIK2 affinity but increasing selectivity for SIK versus LCK to 360-fold. The increased selectivity with the incorporation of the basic pyrrolidine group suggests that the ligand was able to form the desired favorable interaction with the SIK2 Glu103 residue that was not similarly accommodated when binding LCK. Unfortunately, the hydroxymethyl pyrrolidine substituent that imparted the high selectivity for 3 was rapidly metabolized by mouse liver microsomes, limiting the utility of 3 for in vivo studies. Truncating the Glu vector substituent to 4-hydroxyazetidine in compound 4 ( ) maintained SIK affinity, achieved 100-fold selectivity for SIK versus LCK and improved mouse liver microsome stability, making it an attractive compound for evaluating SIK pharmacology in vivo.

## MARK2-SIK2 ATP-Binding Pocket Chimera.
Multiple efforts to purify and crystallize wild-type (WT) or a maltose binding protein (MBP)-fusion protein of the SIK2 kinase and ubiquitin-associated (UBA) domains in insect and bacterial expression systems were unsuccessful. Therefore, a chimeric protein was designed to enable the structure-based design of selective SIK1/2 inhibitors. We used microtubule affinity regulating kinase (MARK) 2, a human paralog of SIK2 sharing 54% sequence identity in the kinase and UBA domains, as the basis of our chimera. The WT MARK2 kinase and UBA domains express well in an Escherichia coli expression system and its structure had been determined at 2.8-Å resolution ( ). A sequence alignment of human MARK2 and SIK2 proteins identified non-conserved residues in the ATP-binding pocket that were then mapped onto the MARK2 crystal structure. Nine mutations (I59L, K66V, V81I, L97I, V113I, M129T, S133K, G134N, and S197G) were required to create a MARK2 ATP-binding pocket chimera with 100% sequence identity to the SIK2 ATP-binding pocket within 6 Å of a bound ATP molecule. Although different conditions were developed for crystallization of the MARK2-SIK2 chimera, it retained the P61 space group with the analogous MARK2 construct and had similar overall 3-dimensional structures with an RMSD of 0.59 Å. Further optimization yielded conditions that reproducibly formed MARK2-SIK2 chimera crystals that ligands could be soaked into and routinely diffracted to 2–2.5 Å resolution.
An X-ray crystal structure of 3 bound to the MARK2-SIK2 chimera shows compound 3 in the predicted binding orientation, engaged in a two-point interaction with the hinge Ala132 of SIK2 ( , Bottom). The pyridine nitrogen of 3 makes a hydrogen bond with the conserved Lys49, and the nitrile substituent fits in a pocket adjacent to the small SIK Thr gatekeeper. The pyrrolidine substituent is positioned to make a favorable electrostatic interaction with the SIK2 Glu103 residue at a distance of 3.4 Å, consistent with the designed selectivity interaction that differentiates SIK from SRC kinases.

## Kinase Selectivity.
The SIK isoform selectivity was assessed in SIK1, SIK2, and SIK3 ADP-GloTM Max assays in the presence of 3 mM ATP ( ), determining SIK2 Ki’s for compounds 3 and 4 that are comparable to the Ki’s from the SIK2 ADP-GloTM assay run at Km ATP ( ). Compounds 3 and 4 are SIK1/2-selective, with SIK2 versus SIK1 selectivity within threefold and SIK2 versus SIK3 selectivity over 30-fold based on Ki ( ). The kinase profiles of 3 and 4 were assessed in a radiometric kinase activity panel (Eurofins Scientific, KinaseProfilerTM) with the concentration of ATP at Km for each kinase. Only four non-SIK kinases (1.4%) out of 327 human wild-type kinases screened were inhibited >50% at a 1 μM inhibitor concentration for both compounds (SI Appendix, Table S3), and selectivity ratios versus SIK2 were determined based on Ki values for each off-target kinase ( ). All four off-target kinases (ABL, LCK, RIPK2, and YES) share the same Thr gatekeeper residue as the SIKs. Compound 3 was 71-fold selective for SIK2 versus the SRC family kinase YES and 317-fold selective versus RIPK2. Compound 4 had a similar selectivity profile, with 82-fold selectivity versus YES and 50-fold selectivity versus RIPK2. The selectivity ratios for the remaining off-target kinases LCK and ABL were ≥100-fold. AMPK family kinases have the highest overall sequence homology with the SIKs but were not detected as off-targets, supporting the success of the selectivity strategy leveraging the small SIK Thr gatekeeper versus the larger AMPK methionine gatekeeper. Compounds 3 and 4 demonstrate improved kinome selectivity versus the literature SIK inhibitor YKL-05-099 which is reported to inhibit more than 60 kinases >90% at 1 μM in a panel of 468 kinases ( ) and compared to the published SIK inhibitor MRIA9 ( ), with 8 non-SIK kinases having selectivity ratios of <100-fold versus SIK2.
 

## SIK1/2-Selective Inhibitors Relieve Inhibitory Phosphorylation on SIK-Substrate CRTC3.
To determine whether SIK1/2-selective inhibitors modulate key SIK-dependent pathways, we developed assays to quantitatively monitor the intracellular localization or phosphorylation status of CRTC3, a CREB co-activator that translocates from the cytoplasm to the nucleus and CREB-dependently enhances IL-10 production when relieved of inhibitory phosphorylation by the SIKs (SI Appendix, Fig. S1A) ( , ). For one assay, we monitored the capacity of SIK inhibitors to modulate nuclear translocation of CRTC3 in the macrophage-like THP-1 cell line by high-content immunofluorescence microscopy using a commercially available anti-CRTC3 antibody. Compounds 3 (JRD-SIK1/2i-3) and 4 (JRD-SIK1/2i-4) induced nuclear translocation of CRTC3 at low micromolar concentrations that are expected to saturate SIK1 and SIK2 but not SIK3 (SI Appendix, Fig. S1B and ).
To demonstrate that our SIK1/2-selective inhibitors induced nuclear translocation of CRTC3 by relieving inhibitory phosphorylation, we set out to measure the levels of CRTC3 that are phosphorylated at a known SIK-dependent phosphorylation site ( ). To this end, we generated a monoclonal antibody (mAb) that specifically binds to the phosphorylated, but not to the unphosphorylated Ser residue 329 of human and murine CRTC3. This effort resulted in recombinant mAb#82 ( ) that specifically recognized recombinant WT CRTC3 protein but not a S329A mutant (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A). In addition, mAb#82 produced a strong signal of the expected molecular size in western blots of lysates from untreated THP-1 cells, whereas this signal was greatly diminished in lysates derived from THP-1 cells treated with the pan-SIK multi-kinase inhibitor dasatinib ( ) (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A). To enable quantification of pCRTC3 levels, we developed a pair of customized electrochemiluminescence-based sandwich immunoassays that utilize mAb#82 to selectively capture CRTC3 phosphorylated at Ser329 and a commercially available antibody to capture total CRTC3 protein independent of its phosphorylation state. JRD-SIK1/2i-3 induced a concentration-dependent reduction in Ser329 phosphorylation at sub-micromolar concentrations whereas the levels of total CRTC3 remained unaltered (SI Appendix, Fig. S2B). Together, these data demonstrate that JRD-SIK1/2i-3 and JRD-SIK1/2i-4 potently trigger these key downstream consequences of SIK inhibition in intact cells.

## SIK1/2 Inhibitors Modulate Cytokine Production in Activated Myeloid Cells.
Small molecule multi-kinase inhibitors that potently inhibit the SIK1-3 isoforms enhance IL-10 production and reduce the levels of inflammatory cytokines, including TNF and IL-12, in activated murine and human dendritic cells and macrophages ( – ). We tested our SIK1/2-selective inhibitors in short-term cell cultures of peripheral blood mononuclear cell (PBMC)-derived human macrophages and murine bone marrow–derived dendritic cells (BMDCs) that had been activated with the Toll Like Receptor (TLR)-4 ligand lipopolysaccharide (LPS). In LPS-stimulated myeloid cells from either species, JRD-SIK1/2i-3 and JRD-SIK1/2i-4 strongly induced IL-10 release at concentrations comparable to those obtained in our CRTC3 nuclear translocation assay and suppressed TNF production ( , , and SI Appendix, Fig. S3A).
 Given that our LPS-stimulation assays had been optimized for the IL-10 endpoint, we established conditions using the TLR7/8 agonist R848 at a concentration that corresponded to ~80% maximal activity to more precisely assess our compounds’ potencies at suppressing the release of key clinically relevant inflammatory cytokines from human macrophages. In this assay, JRD-SIK1/2i-4 suppressed TNF, IL-12/23 p40, and IL-23 at IC50’s of 0.11 µM, 0.25 µM, and 0.47 µM, respectively ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S3B). Both compounds were tested in the SIK1, SIK2, and SIK3 ADP-GloTM Max assays in the presence of 3 mM ATP ( ), approximating cellular ATP levels ( ). These data indicate that in PBMC-derived human macrophages, both compounds induce IL-10 after LPS treatment and JRD-SIK1/2i-4 suppresses TNF following R848 stimulation at concentrations that are consistent with the enzymatic IC50’s of SIK1 and SIK2 but below the enzymatic IC50 of SIK3 at cellular ATP levels ( ). Next, we established assays that were sensitive to the consequences of SIK inhibition in human and murine whole blood to inform in vivo studies with JRD-SIK1/2i-3 and JRD-SIK1/2i-4. Assessing the effect of SIK1/2-inhibition on TNF release in blood from both species 3 h after an ex vivo LPS challenge, we found that our SIK1/2-inhibitors suppressed TNF production at sub-micromolar IC50’s that were comparable to potencies observed in most other cell culture–based assays for these compounds ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S3 C and D).
IL-10 signaling can suppress production of inflammatory cytokines including TNF during innate immune activation ( ). To determine whether enhanced autocrine IL-10 signaling contributes to the reduction in TNF release in the presence of SIK1/2 inhibitors, we compared the responses to JRD-SIK1/2i-3 in LPS-stimulated BMDCs derived from Il10−/− mice ( ) or WT C57BL/6 littermates. In the absence of SIK inhibitors, LPS induced low levels of IL-10 from WT BMDCs, while this cytokine was not detected in culture media from Il10−/− BMDCs (SI Appendix, Fig. S3E). Treatment with JRD-SIK1/2i-3 enhanced LPS-induced IL-10 release from the WT BMDCs at concentrations comparable to other assays ( , , and SI Appendix, Fig. S3F). Similar levels of TNF were detected in media from both WT and Il10−/− BMDCs (SI Appendix, Fig. S3E). Although a trend was observed to more potent TNF suppression by JRD-SIK1/2i-3 in WT versus Il10−/− BMDC, the IC50s in both genotypes are consistent with other assays and a similar maximum effect was achieved ( , , SI Appendix, Fig. S3F). These data suggest that autocrine IL-10 signaling is not required for suppression of LPS-induced TNF release.

## SIK1/2 Inhibitors Induce Expression of Putative CREB Target Genes in Multiple Immune Cell Types.
To more comprehensively define the functional effects of SIK1/2 inhibitors in primary immune cells, we profiled the transcriptional responses elicited by JRD-SIK1/2i-3 in PBMCs responding to TLR4 activation at single-cell resolution. PBMCs from two donors were incubated with 1 or 10 µM of JRD-SIK1/2i-3 for 1 h, stimulated with LPS and, to focus on primary transcriptional responses to SIK1/2 inhibition, harvested 1 h later. Single-cell RNA-seq (sc-RNA-seq) analysis of these samples identified cell clusters corresponding to myeloid cells, CD4 T cells, CD4 memory T cells, cytotoxic cells, regulatory T cells, and B cells ( ). Consistent with being the sole cluster to robustly express TLR4 (SI Appendix, Fig. S4A), myeloid cells displayed the only strong transcriptional response to LPS stimulation with a total of 426 genes significantly modulated ( and Dataset S1).
Of the SIK isoforms, SIK3 was expressed to the greatest extent, but SIK1 and SIK2 were also present in appreciable fractions of all clusters. The proximal mediators of SIK signaling CREB1 and CRTC 1–3 ( ) were also detected in all clusters suggesting that both myeloid cells and lymphocytes are poised to respond to SIK inhibitors (SI Appendix, Fig. S4A). Few genes were significantly modulated in LPS-stimulated myeloid cells preincubated with 1 µM of JRD-SIK1/2i-3 compared to LPS stimulation alone ( ), a concentration preferentially targeting SIK1 and SIK2 versus SIK3. Strikingly though, four of the five upregulated genes NR4A2, NR4A3, AREG, and notably IL10 are in loci predicted to be CREB targets ( ). Likewise, at 10 µM of JRD-SIK1/2i-3, which is expected to substantially inhibit all three SIK isoforms, 33% (20 of 61) of upregulated genes were putative CREB targets, again including IL10. This observation contrasts with the transcriptional changes induced by LPS alone where only 19% (59 of 304) of induced genes are potential CREB targets ( ). Of note, the 22 transcripts reduced by 10 µM of JRD-SIK1/2i-3 in myeloid cells include mRNA encoding TNF and IL-12/23 p40.
CD4 T cells showed the greatest number of genes modulated by 1 µM of JRD-SIK1/2i-3 in the presence of LPS (75 genes up/30 genes down), followed by CD4 memory T cells (20 genes up/5 genes down; ). Querying these transcriptional responses for underlying biology using Ingenuity Canonical Pathways identified no or only limited pathway associations at 1 µM or 10 µM, respectively, in CD4 or CD4 memory T cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S4B). A greater number of processes were identified for myeloid cells stimulated with 10 µM of JRD-SIK1/2i-3, including IL-10 signaling and several negative correlations with IL-17 signaling (SI Appendix, Fig. S4B). Consistent with the role of the SIKs in suppressing the CRTC-mediated transcriptional activity of CREB ( ), these sc-RNA-seq data suggest that upregulation of CREB target genes is a primary transcriptional consequence elicited by SIK1/2 inhibitors.
The inability of LPS to directly activate T lymphocytes limits the utility of our sc-RNA-seq study to understand whether the observed transcriptional responses to SIK1/2 inhibitors in T cells may alter responses to T cell receptor (TCR) stimulation. Hence, we tested JRD-SIK1/2i-3 and JRD-SIK1/2i-4 in short-term cultures of anti-CD3/anti-CD28-activated human CD4+ T cells. In contrast to the potent cytokine modulation observed in myeloid cells, we found that our compounds suppressed IL-2 secretion in stimulated T cells considerably less potently. JRD-SIK1/2i-3 and JRD-SIK1/2i-4 inhibited IL-2 production at IC50’s that were approximately 8- to 12-fold greater than the EC50’s for IL-10 induction in primary human macrophages (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 and ). We confirmed that our compounds did not exhibit toxicity in T lymphocytes across the titration range with a CellTiter-Glo® viability assay. We therefore conclude that our compounds do not grossly interfere with TCR-mediated T cell activation at concentrations that inhibit SIK1 and SIK2 but note that both compounds are likely to inhibit the SRC-family kinase LCK at high concentrations ( ), a kinase with a critical role in TCR-mediated T cell activation ( ).
Together, these data identify upregulation of putative CREB target genes as a primary transcriptional response elicited by SIK1/2-selective inhibitors in multiple immune cell types. The most significant functional consequence of the induction of these genes appears to be modulation of key pro- and anti-inflammatory cytokines in activated myeloid cells.

## In Vitro ADME and Pharmacokinetics.
JRD-SIK1/2i-3 and JRD-SIK1/2i-4 were further profiled for their in vitro absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) and in vivo pharmacokinetics (PK) properties to determine their suitability for in vivo studies. Although JRD-SIK1/2i-3 is highly SIK1/2 selective, this compound showed low metabolic stability in mouse liver microsomes supplemented with NADPH and moderate permeability (SI Appendix, Fig. S6A) leading to a pharmacokinetic profile that was not suitable for PK/pharmacodynamic (PD) and efficacy studies (SI Appendix, Fig. S6B). In contrast, JRD-SIK1/2i-4 had improved metabolic stability in mouse liver microsomes and higher permeability (SI Appendix, Fig. S6A) resulting in improved oral exposure in mice and rats (SI Appendix, Fig. S6 A and C). At 80 mg/kg PO, JRD-SIK1/2i-4 plasma concentrations exceeded the mouse BMDC IL-10 EC50 and whole blood TNF IC50 for 14–17 h and at 20 mg/kg PO for 8–11 h (SI Appendix, Fig. S6C). Thus, this compound could achieve 24 h coverage of the mouse IL-10 EC50 with b.i.d. dosing.

## JRD-SIK1/2i-4 Displays Activity In Vivo Consistent with Potent SIK1/2 Inhibition.
Next, we evaluated JRD-SIK1/2i-4 in a LPS-induced cytokine release model in mice to establish the ability of a SIK1/2 inhibitor to modulate acute inflammatory responses in vivo and PK/PD relationships. In this model, JRD-SIK1/2i-4 was orally administered 1 h prior to systemic LPS challenge (see for schematic) with a broad dose range from 1 to 80 mg/kg to define a full concentration–response for cytokine modulation in vivo. The compound was found to exposure-dependently elevate IL-10 and reduce TNF levels in the circulation ( ). Comparing plasma concentrations of JRD-SIK1/2i-4 to levels of IL-10 or TNF enabled an estimation of the EC50 of IL-10 induction (1.14 µM) and the IC50 of TNF inhibition (0.39 µM) which were comparable to the potencies of JRD-SIK1/2i-4 in primary human macrophages, murine BMDCs, and whole blood assays ( ). We made similar observations with the expression of Il10 and Tnf in the colons from these mice, with significant induction of Il10 mRNA at 20 and 80 mg/kg and significant reduction of Tnf mRNA at 5, 20, and 80 mg/kg ( ). Compared to the published pan-SIK inhibitor YKL-05-099 ( ) dosed at 80 mg/kg, SIK1/2-selective JRD-SIK1/2i-4 suppressed TNF to a similar extent and elevated IL-10 at least as potently in the plasma ( ).
 To test whether JRD-SIK1/2i-4 demonstrated proximal target engagement in intestinal tissue, we quantified pCRTC3 levels in resected colon tissue lysates from the same mice using the electrochemiluminescence immunoassays described above. We found significant reductions of pCRTC3 levels with YKL-05-099 (80 mg/kg) and the highest dose groups of JRD-SIK1/2i-4 (20 and 80 mg/kg; ). These are the same doses where significant elevation of Il10 mRNA was observed, indicating proximal target engagement and downstream activity in a tissue in which macrophages play an essential role in maintaining homeostasis ( ).
Together, these data demonstrate that orally administered SIK1/2-selective inhibitor JRD-SIK1/2i-4 suppresses SIK kinase activity in vivo and potently modulates acute inflammatory responses in tissues that are affected in IBD.

## SIK1/2-Inhibitor JRD-SIK1/2i-4 Ameliorates Systemic and Tissue Inflammation in an Experimental Colitis Model.
The anti-CD40 colitis model ( ) involves both systemic and intestinal inflammation that results from activation of myeloid cells in lymphopenic Rag2−/− mice by injection of an agonistic antibody to CD40. The systemic inflammatory response peaks 3 d post-injection and results in body weight loss that is sensitive to TNF blockade ( ), while colitis is mediated by gut-resident macrophages and can be blocked by a neutralizing antibody targeting the p19 subunit of IL-23 ( ). Therefore, this model is well suited to test whether suppression of TNF and IL-12/23 p40 production by SIK1/2 inhibitors translates into reduced inflammatory pathology in vivo.
 Oral administration of JRD-SIK1/2i-4 dose dependently reduced CD40-induced body weight loss such that by day 7, body weights in animals treated with the highest dose of 80 mg/kg b.i.d. recovered to nearly those of naive mice that did not receive anti-CD40 mAb ( ). Other manifestations of anti-CD40-induced systemic inflammation, such as liver pathology and splenomegaly, were also dose-dependently suppressed by SIK inhibition ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S7A). Importantly, SIK inhibition also dose-dependently prevented the development of colitis in these mice, and colons from mice treated with 80 mg/kg b.i.d. showed minimal inflammation and gland loss by histological examination ( ). Thus, JRD-SIK1/2i-4 protected against both systemic and intestinal disease manifestations, whereas an anti-IL23p19 antibody only ameliorated intestinal disease ( ), as expected in this model ( ). While protection against systemic and intestinal disease was seen with both b.i.d. and q.d. dosing, the b.i.d. regimen showed a greater impact on PD parameters such as reversing inflammation-associated cytokine increases, e.g., TNF, IL-1β, and IL-6 (SI Appendix, Fig. S7B).
Efficacy in this model was achieved at 20 mg/kg b.i.d. and 40 mg/kg q.d. when trough concentrations of the inhibitor (80.4 nM and 66.8 nM, respectively) approached 24 h coverage above the IC50 of TNF inhibition in the LPS challenge model, whereas the 80 mg/kg dose b.i.d. provided maximal efficacy while maintaining 24 h trough coverage (1.56 µM) above IC80 of TNF inhibition.

## JRD-SIK1/2i-4 Is Susceptible to Thiol Adduct Formation.
JRD-SIK1/2i-4 was investigated for chemical or metabolic reactivity through incubations in human liver microsomes containing the trapping agent glutathione (GSH). JRD-SIK1/2i-4 formed a GSH adduct during incubations both in the presence and absence of the cytochrome P450 cofactor NADPH, suggesting the likelihood for inherent chemical reactivity rather than P450-mediated reactivity (SI Appendix, Fig. S8). Further optimization for this chemotype would be needed to remove this potential liability.

## Discussion
We describe a strategy to develop highly SIK1/2-selective inhibitors, exemplified by JRD-SIK1/2i-4, with cellular potency and pharmacokinetic profiles that enabled in vivo studies in rodents using oral dosing. High-throughput screening of SIK2 with a SIK3 counter-screen identified a SIK2 inhibitor chemotype originating from screening hit 1 with selectivity versus SIK3. Optimization to JRD-SIK1/2i-4 was accomplished using a kinase selectivity hypothesis based on the amino acid differences between SIK2 and the AMPK family as well as SRC family kinases with structurally similar catalytic sites ( ). By targeting both differentiating residues Thr96 and Glu103 in the SIK2 catalytic site, we were able to develop inhibitors with a high level of SIK selectivity. The lack of X-ray crystallography systems for the SIK family has hampered development of SIK isoform-selective inhibitors ( ). We addressed this issue by developing a crystallography system suitable for crystal soaking based on a chimera of MARK2 bearing mutations corresponding to the SIK2 catalytic site including the key Thr96 and Glu103 residue combination and applied it to demonstrate the key selectivity interaction for JRD-SIK1/2i-3. We anticipate that this system will be useful for iterative structure-based optimization of other highly kinase-selective SIK inhibitor chemotypes with different SIK isoform profiles for further interrogating SIK biology.
The SIKs act through a dual mechanism in which reducing SIK kinase activity converts pro-inflammatory myeloid cells to an anti-inflammatory phenotype ( – ). Selectively targeting SIK1 and SIK2 was pursued since myeloid cells from dual SIK1 and SIK2 kinase-dead knock-in mice robustly up-regulate IL-10 and produce reduced amounts of inflammatory cytokines during innate immune activation ( ) and to avoid potential tolerability concerns associated with inactivating SIK3 ( , – , , ). Our results demonstrate that selective SIK1/2 inhibitors recapitulate intracellular signaling in myeloid cells induced by pan-SIK inhibitors, such as reducing inhibitory phosphorylation of pSer329-CRTC3 and subsequent nuclear translocation of CRTC3. Likewise, our SIK1/2-selective inhibitors suppress pro-inflammatory cytokines such as TNF or IL-23 and induce expression of many CREB target genes including the anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10 in activated human and murine myeloid cells as well as in mice following a LPS challenge ( , , ). Our data in Il10−/− BMDCs indicate that autocrine IL-10 signaling is not required for suppression of LPS-induced TNF release by SIK1/2 inhibitors. This is consistent with a report that TNF release is suppressed in Il10−/− bone marrow–derived macrophages by indirect inhibition of SIK activity via activation of cAMP signaling ( ) and may be mediated by reduced inhibitory phosphorylation of other SIK substrates (e.g., class 2a histone deacetylases) that negatively regulate inflammatory cytokine production ( , ). These activities of SIK1/2-selective inhibitors provide pharmacological validation that targeting these two isoforms is sufficient to recapitulate the marked immunomodulatory activity of pan-SIK inhibitors in both activated mouse and human myeloid cells.
Oral dosing of the optimized analog JRD-SIK1/2i-4 suppressed both early systemic wasting disease and subsequent colitis in the anti-CD40 mouse model. The inhibitory effects of JRD-SIK1/2i-4 on systemic inflammation, as indicated by prevention of body weight loss, are expected to result from reduced TNF production by activated myeloid cells because efficacious doses of JRD-SIK1/2i-4 maintained trough coverage above the IC50 for TNF inhibition in the acute LPS challenge PK/PD studies for 12-24 h, and neutralizing TNF has been shown to inhibit systemic wasting disease, but not colitis, in the anti-CD40 model ( ). Likewise, suppression of colitis by JRD-SIK1/2i-4 most likely resulted from direct effects on activated myeloid cells in the colon rather than reduced systemic inflammation. Production of IL-23 by colonic myeloid cells responding to CD40 stimulation is a necessary upstream driver of the inflammatory cascade leading to colitis, but not wasting disease, in this model ( , ). Consistent with this hypothesis, JRD-SIK1/2i-4 suppressed IL-23 production in activated human macrophages with an IC50 in the range of the exposures achieved by efficacious doses of this inhibitor, and the highest doses of JRD-SIK1/2i-4 suppressed colitis to a similar degree as an IL-23p19 neutralizing antibody.
The activities of JRD-SIK1/2i-4 in the anti-CD40 colitis model are similar to the activity of the pan-SIK multi-kinase inhibitor HG-9-91-01 in the chemically induced trinitrobenzene sulfonic acid (TNBS) and chronic dextran sulfate sodium (DSS) colitis models ( ). Amelioration of colitis by HG-9-91-01 was associated with increased expression of Il10 by colonic macrophages, and HG-9-91-01 was unable to protect against colitis that spontaneously develops in Il10−/− mice suggesting a key role for IL-10 induction in reducing disease in these models ( ). While we found elevated colonic IL-10 levels in challenged, vehicle-treated animals against unchallenged mice on termination of the anti-CD40 model (P = 0.0115 for vehicle b.i.d. versus naive and P < 0.0001 for vehicle q.d. versus naive using a one-tailed t-test; SI Appendix, Fig. S7B), we did not observe a further increase in IL-10 concentrations in colon tissue from mice treated with efficacious doses of JRD-SIK1/2i-4. The lack of an increase in IL-10 in bulk tissue from mice at doses of JRD-SIK1/2i-4 where levels of the pro-inflammatory cytokines IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF were reduced may be due to a corresponding decrease in infiltrating immune cells. Experiments to determine the cytokine production by intestinal immune cells on a per-cell basis could help clarify this point. In addition, it is possible that JRD-SIK1/2i-4 may have up-regulated IL-10 in activated colonic myeloid cells during the early, acute response to CD40 stimulation, which would be consistent with the increase in colon and plasma IL-10 levels observed in our PK/PD model 1 h after LPS challenge. Overall, activity of JRD-SIK1/2i-4 in the anti-CD40 colitis model extends the findings with HG-9-91-01 in chemically induced colitis models ( ) by demonstrating that a SIK1/2-selective inhibitor can suppress colonic inflammation driven by activated innate immune cells.
Together, these findings establish a strategy for developing SIK1/2-selective inhibitors, including use of a chimeric MARK2/SIK2 X-ray crystallography system suitable for iterative compound optimization. This approach was utilized to identify the highly selective SIK1/2 inhibitor JRD-SIK1/2i-4 that is amenable for repeat oral dosing. Increases in trabecular bone mass in mice with post-natal, global deletion of both Sik2 and Sik3 are supported by similar findings with repeat dosing of a SIK2/3-biased small molecule inhibitor in the absence of short-term toxicities ( ). However, alteration of bone metabolism can limit the use of immunomodulatory drugs (e.g., glucocorticoids) for chronic inflammatory conditions ( ). Our data provide proof-of-concept that inhibitors targeting SIK1 and SIK2 may be a viable strategy to treat IBD and perhaps other chronic inflammatory diseases while avoiding potential tolerability challenges associated with targeting SIK3. Last, while JRD-SIK1/2i-4 will enable investigating the function of these kinases in a range of biological contexts, future efforts will seek to maintain the profile of this inhibitor while eliminating the propensity to form glutathione adducts.

## Materials and Methods
See SI Appendix, Materials and Methods for detailed descriptions of the methods. Briefly, potent SIK1/2 selective inhibitors were developed by docking a HTS hit to a SIK2 model followed by iterative medicinal chemistry optimization (SI Appendix, Figs. S9 and S10). Effects of these inhibitors on SIK activity were evaluated with biochemical kinase assays using 6His-tagged MBP-tagged fusions with the kinase domains of SIK1-3. The binding pose of JRD-SIK1/2i-3 was determined by crystallization with a MARK2-SIK2 chimera generated by making nine mutations (I59L, K66V V81I, L97I, V113I, M129T, S133K, G134N, and S197G) to create a MARK2 variant with 100% sequence identity within a 6-Å shell surrounding the SIK2 ATP-binding pocket. Inhibitor-induced changes in phosphorylation of the SIK substrate CRTC3 were determined using a pSer329-CRTC3-specific monoclonal antibody generated by immunization of rabbits with the CRTC3-derived peptide GLQSSRpSNPSIQ conjugated to keyhole limpet hemocyanin or ovalbumin followed by isolation of hybridomas that secreted antibodies with apparent selectivity for the phosphorylated but not the unphosphorylated peptide. Effects of SIK1/2-selective inhibitors on cytokine responses in vitro were assessed in human monocyte–derived macrophages stimulated with LPS or R848, human CD4+ T cells stimulated with anti-CD3/anti-CD28-coated beads, mouse BMDCs activated by LPS, and mouse or human whole blood treated with LPS. sc-RNA-Seq analyses on whole blood-derived PBMCs after exposure to JRD-SIK1/2i-3 and LPS were done using 10x Genomics technology. Effects of SIK1/2-selective inhibitors on acute inflammatory responses in vivo were determined in mice following oral administration of JRD-SIK1/2i-4 prior to LPS stimulation and assessment of cytokine levels in serum or colon lysates. The ability of JRD-SIK1/2i-4 to suppress intestinal inflammation was evaluated by repeat dosing for 7 d in lymphopenic Rag2−/− mice in which colitis was induced by an agonistic antibody to CD40.
