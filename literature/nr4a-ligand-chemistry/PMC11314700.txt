## Purpose
The purpose of this study was to identify potential drug targets for myopia and explore underlying mechanisms.

## Methods
Mendelian randomization (MR) was implemented to assess the effect of 2684 pharmacologically targetable genes in the blood and retina on the risk of myopia from a genomewide association study (GWAS) for age-at-onset of spectacle wearing-inferred mean spherical equivalent (MSE; discovery cohort, N = 287,448, European), which was further validated in a GWAS for autorefraction-measured MSE (replication cohort, N = 95,619, European). The reliability of the identified significant potential targets was strengthened by colocalization analysis. Additionally, enrichment analysis, protein-protein interaction network, and molecular docking were performed to explore the functional roles and the druggability of these targets.

## Results
This systematic drug target identification has unveiled 6 putative genetically causal targets for myopia—CD34, CD55, Wnt3, LCAT, BTN3A1, and TSSK6—each backed by colocalization evidence in adult blood eQTL datasets. Functional analysis found that dopaminergic neuron differentiation, cell adhesion, Wnt signaling pathway, and plasma lipoprotein-associated pathways may be involved in myopia pathogenesis. Finally, drug prediction and molecular docking corroborated the pharmacological value of these targets with LCAT demonstrating the strongest binding affinity.

## Conclusions
Our study not only opens new avenues for the development of therapeutic interventions for myopia but may also help to understand the underlying mechanisms of myopia.

It was estimated that in regions such as East and Southeast Asia, the prevalence of myopia in the youth has soared to an astounding 80% to 90%, with cases of high myopia—posing greater risks and complications—constituting 10% to 20% of these figures. This epidemic not only places an immense strain on healthcare systems but also underscores the urgency for effective myopia control and prevention strategies. 
Myopia is a multifaceted disease caused by the complex interaction of genetic susceptibility and environmental exposure. Previous studies have identified more than 400 genetic loci, , and identified multiple environmental risk factors associated with myopia. However, given the complex pathogenesis of myopia, there is no single fundamental treatment method. Currently, optical interventions using single-vision lenses are the most widely used management for myopia, but they neither can stop the progression of myopia nor prevent the pathological complications caused by high myopia. In contrast, drugs for myopia have few options and uncertain efficacy, such as atropine. Previous studies have proved that low-concentration atropine has a clear control effect on the onset and progression of myopia in children. However, some researchers question the long-term effectiveness of atropine in slowing myopia progression or axial elongation in America and Asia. , Research also showed that the myopia progression rate rebounds after stopping atropine eye drops in a concentration-dependent manner. , In addition, side effects like blurred vision, photophobia, and reduced accommodation are also reported to be accompanied by atropine. These limitations all underscore the necessity for the development of new drugs for myopia prevention and control.
Mendelian randomization (MR) analysis utilizes genetic data to deduce causal associations between an exposure and an outcome variable. Recently, MR has been found useful in drug target identification. Given that many circulating proteins encoded by druggable genes serve as the actual biological efficacy targets for numerous approved or experimental drugs, Expression Quantitative Trait Locus (eQTL) information, which is associated with alterations in drug-responsive gene transcription, can be used as the exposure to predict therapeutic targets.

## Methods
This study followed the specification of the Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology Using Mendelian Randomization ( ). The overview of this study is presented in .
 

## Expression Quantitative Trait Loci
Blood eQTLs were obtained from eQTLGen Consortium (https://eqtlgen.org/), and retina cis-eQTLs were obtained from a mega analysis. The druggable genes were obtained from a list of 4479 druggable genes, which integrated genomewide association study (GWAS) loci implicated in complex diseases and biomarkers with an array of druggable protein-coding genes for drug development.
In this study, eQTLs were limited to the cis-region of druggable genes. Specifically, cis-expression quantitative trait loci (cis-eQTLs) included for MR analysis are restricted to those located within 5 kb upstream of the transcription start site or 5 kb downstream of the transcription termination site of druggable genes. Finally, eQTLs for 2434 circulating druggable genes and 250 druggable genes in the retina remained for the following analysis.

## GWAS Summary Statistics of Myopia
The discovery stage MR used GWAS summary statistics for age-at-onset of spectacle wearing (AOSW)-inferred mean spherical equivalent (MSE; N = 287,448, European ancestry, UK Biobank), whereas the replication was conducted based on a GWAS for autorefraction-measured MSE (N = 95,619, European ancestry, UK Biobank). In the discovery phase, the model for AOSW-inferred MSE was derived using summary statistics of both autorefraction and AOSW samples. Detailed information is summarized in .

## Mendelian Randomization Analysis
In this study, we used the R package “TwosampleMR” to estimate the associations between eQTLs of drug targets and GWAS summary statistics of myopia. The MR strategy rested on a triad of postulates: (1) the genetic instrumental variables (IVs) used exhibit, a correlation with the exposure, specifically the expression magnitude of the druggable gene; (2) the IVs maintain no association with any undisclosed confounders that might influence the exposure-outcome relationship; (3) the IVs influence the outcome exclusively via the exposure, thereby excluding any instances of pleiotropy. We also performed several sensitivity analyses as quality control. First, we only used linkage disequilibrium clumping at R2 ≤ 0.01 for eQTLs with P < 5e-06 to choose instruments for each gene expression level. Second, to avoid bias from weak instruments, IVs with F statistic less than 10 were excluded and F-statistics were calculated by the formula , F = (beta/se)2. Third, for the druggable genes, the Wald ratio was applied if only one eQTL was available, whereas inverse variance weighted (IVW), MR-Egger, and weighted median were used if two or more eQTLs were available. The estimates from different methods should be in the same direction. The MR-Egger intercept test was conducted to detect the bias due to directional pleiotropy. Moreover, Cochrane Q was tested for heterogeneity. In discovery analysis, Bonferroni correction was used to adjust for multiple testing, setting a threshold P value at 2.05E-05 (0.05/2434) in the blood and 2.00E-03 (0.05/250) in the retina. In the replication analysis, the corrected P value threshold at 2.08E-03 (0.05/24) in the blood and 8.33E-02 (0.05/6) in the retina were used to validate the drug targets.

## Bayesian Colocalization Analysis
To assess the probability that two traits share the same causal single-nucleotide variation, Bayesian co-localization analysis was used through the R package “coloc” with default arguments. If a genetic variation in a particular gene region is associated with both exposure and outcome, it may be that the same genetic variation simultaneously exerts a causal influence on both the exposure and the outcome. However, the associations may be driven by different causal variables that are correlated due to linkage disequilibrium. Under this circumstance, colocalization serves as a useful sensitivity analysis when an MR analysis is based on a single gene region. The Bayesian approach delineates five distinct hypotheses for each genetic locus: (1) H0 = the absence of any association with both exposure and outcome; (2) H1 = an association exclusively with exposure; (3) H2 = an association solely with outcome; (4) H3 = both exposure and outcome are associated, albeit with separate causal variants for the two traits; and (5) H4 = both exposure and outcome are associated, sharing a common causal variant for the traits. In this study, we set the threshold of significance at SNP.PP.H4 > 0.80, in which genes could be categorized as potential targets for the following analysis.

## Gene Ontology Enrichment Analysis
We performed a Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analysis using the R package “clusterProfiler.” 

## Protein-Protein Interaction Network Construction
By constructing a protein-protein intereaction (PPI) network, we can obtain a more comprehensive mechanism picture of the identified targets. We performed PPI analysis using GeneMANIA (http://www.genemania.org), a website that allows for hypothesis generation about gene function, gene list analysis, and prioritization of genes for functional assays. 

## Candidate Drug Prediction
To assess the druggability value of the identified target genes, we first predicted potential candidate drugs in the Drug-Gene Interaction database (DGIdb, https://old.dgidb.org/), a database that describes drug-gene interactions and gene druggability. The identified target genes are incorporated into the DGIdb database to foresee promising pharmacological agents.

## Molecular Docking
We adopted the structure-based computer-aided drug design strategy using molecular docking to explore the interactions between candidate drug targets and predicted drugs at the atomic level. By mimicking the interactions between receptors and ligands, molecular docking can predict the position and direction of the ligand in the binding pocket and compute the binding affinity of these drug-target pairs. The software Autodock 4 (https://autodock.scripps.edu/) was used to complete the process of docking. For the structure of the drug, data were downloaded from the PubChem Compound Database (https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/). For the structure of target genes encoded proteins, we downloaded the structure data from the Protein Data Bank (PDB, https://www.rcsb.org/) and EMBL-EBI Alphafold-based structure data (https://www.ebi.ac.uk/). Finally, we obtained the structures of three proteins and seven drugs. We removed all the water molecules of the target proteins and ligands and added hydrogen atoms to them. Structural domains of each protein were included in the center of the grid boxes, with unrestricted molecular mobility allowed. The grid boxes were adjusted to contain all the domains of each protein macromolecule and unrestricted molecular mobility was allowed. The result of molecular docking was visualized by Autodock 4.

## Results
Using different MR methods, we identified 24 significant druggable genes in the blood (P < 2.05E-05, Bonferroni test) and 6 in the retina (P < 2.00E-03, Bonferroni test) that were causally related to myopia in the discovery cohort ( , ). In the sensitivity analyses, all genes passed the directional pleiotropy test and were included in the following replication analysis ( , ).
 In the replication phase, we successfully replicated 15 genes in the blood (P < 2.08E-03, Bonferroni test) and 4 in the retina (P < 8.33E-03, Bonferroni test; ,  ). IGF1R failed to pass the heterogeneity test and was excluded from the subsequent analysis (P value of Q test < 5.00E-02, , S6). Among these 19 replicated genes (see ), most are already reported to be related genetic loci associated with refractive error in GWAS , except for PSMA4 and GPX1. Although there is no direct overlap of results between the blood and retina, BTN3A1 in blood and BTN3A2 in retina both belong to Butyrophilin subfamily 3 and show functional overlap in adaptive immune response. 
 

## Colocalization Analysis
In the above analysis, the significant myopia-drug genes association identified through MR analysis may arise from a site in which SNPs of eQTLs and myopia GWAS are in close linkage disequilibrium, or from an SNP associated with gene expression and myopia at the same time. The GWAS-AOSW cohort was used for Bayesian colocalization analysis on the 18 replicated genes, which further verified 6 genes, including CD34, CD55, WNT3, LCAT, BTN3A1, and TSSK6, as showing significant evidence of colocalization (SNP.PP.H4 > 0.8). These genes were considered candidate targets for further analysis ( ).
 

## Gene Ontology Enrichment Analysis
GO enrichment analysis was used to evaluate the roles of the genes of interest. We found that BTN3A1 and CD34 were both significantly associated with the external side of the plasma membrane, WNT3 was significantly associated with Wnt signalosome, and LCAT was significantly associated with plasma lipoprotein pathways ( ).
 

## Protein-Protein Interaction Networks
The network shown in  delineates the function of these 6 target genes and another 20 genes that may functionally interact with these targets. The network comprised 199 interaction links, which encompassed a diverse array of associations including 80.30% physical interactions. Network functional analysis showed that Wnt3 was significantly associated with midbrain dopaminergic neuron differentiation (false discovery rate [FDR]-corrected P = 8.77e-3), whereas CD55 was significantly associated with positive regulation of cell adhesion (P = 1.36e-2).
 

## Prediction of Candidate Drug
Based on the interaction score, two to three drugs were selected for each target for subsequent analysis ( ). Of them, prednisolone was linked to both LCAT and CD34.
 

## Molecular Docking
We first utilized Autodock 4 to determine the binding sites for the 7 potential chemical compounds predicted by DGIdb. The binding energies were then computed between the predicted pharmacological agent and protein complexes translated from the target genes ( ,  ). Drug candidates linked to their targets through hydrogen bonds and electrostatic interactions could successfully occupy the binding pocket of drug targets. LCAT and testosterone showed the lowest binding energy (−8.27 kcal/mol), indicative of stable binding. 
 

## Discussion
This study applied a novel method to systematically identify drug targets for myopia and explore the molecular mechanism of myopia pathogenesis. Six genes (CD34, CD55, WNT3, LCAT, BTN3A1, and TSSK6) with a putative causal relationship with myopia were identified by MR combined with colocalization analysis.

CD34 is a specific marker of endothelial cells and angiogenesis. Liu et al. found a negative correlation between vascular density of choriocapillaris and myopia, which was associated with hypoxia and collagen remodelling in the sclera. A biochemical study also suggested CD34 as a potential key gene in high myopia. In this analysis, we identified CD34 as a potential risk gene for myopia.

CD55 inhibits complement activation by disrupting and preventing the formation of C3 and C5 convertases. Studies using animal models of myopia found that in the early and late stages of myopia, genes in the complement-coagulation system showed differential expression, and the serum complement markers were elevated in patients with pathologic myopia, which may support the putative protective role of CD55 in our analysis.

Wnt3 plays a pivotal role in canonical Wnt signaling cascade. In support of our finding, a previous study suggested that targeting Wnt signaling pathways hold promise as a therapeutic strategy for myopia. However, the role of the Wnt3/β-catenin pathway in myopia remains inconclusive. An animal study has found a dynamic change in the expression of Wnt-associated proteins and β-catenin, initially increasing and then decreasing. In this study, we found that Wnt3 plays a putative protective role in myopia. Further research is needed in the future.

LCAT is involved in the metabolism of cholesterol, an important precursor for steroid hormones including testosterone. A previous study reported higher levels of testosterone in the blood of myopic adolescents compared to non-myopes, and another study reported positive associations between testosterone levels and axial elongation, which is consistent with our finding that LCAT is a potential risk gene for myopia. Although testosterone, which targeted LCAT in our network pharmacology analysis, emerged as a promising therapeutic target for myopia based on our findings, it is crucial to note that it is not the only effective drug for this target. Our analysis primarily served as an initial screening for potential myopia drug targets, and further research is necessary for drug development and validation.

BTN3A1, also known as CD277, is expressed in multiple immune cells and plays a role in T-cell activation and adaptive immune response. Currently, little is known regarding the potential association between immune responses and myopia, but a connection between immune disorders and incident myopia has been suggested by Xu et al. In our analysis, BTN3A1 is speculated as a risk gene for myopia.

TSSK6, testis-specific serine kinase 6, is a biomarker of masculinity. We found a negative association between the expression of TSSK6 and MSE. Although this gene might play a role in the gender differences observed in myopia risk, current literature suggests that these differences are more likely attributed to behavioral variations. Further research is essential to deepen our understanding of how genetic and environmental factors interact and contribute to the gender disparities in myopia risk.
This study used genetic variants identified by GWAS, which typically have minimal effects on complex heterogeneous phenotypes. However, these variants may significantly influence molecular phenotypes, such as gene expression, making drugs affecting gene expression viable candidates for repurposing. Historically, drugs with genetic support have shown higher approval rates for clinical trials, particularly those linked to causal genes from Mendelian traits and GWAS. It should be noted that our current methodology did not permit the estimation of effects if these genes were targeted pharmacologically. Nonetheless, the identified targets provide valuable insights for developing potential treatments, either as agonists or antagonists, depending on their risk or protective roles in myopia. Further research is needed to assess whether the impacts of these variants justify the potential side effects or the costs of drug development, especially for targets like LCAT.
Our study has several advantages. First, we used two GWAS cohorts, one as the discovery cohort and the other for replication, which significantly reduced false positive results in identifying potential drug targets for myopia. Second, we strengthened the evidence of the association by integrating MR and colocalization analysis. Third, due to the unclear molecular mechanisms of myopia, we conducted an enrichment analysis and constructed a PPI network to reveal regulatory connections and promote the development of novel therapeutics. Last, we used molecular docking to assess the binding affinity of target genes with potential therapeutic agents, further confirming the druggable values of these targets.
However, this study still contains several limitations. First, the effect size estimated by MR may not accurately reflect the true magnitude of a drug's benefit from a shorter duration of clinical intervention. Second, the drug targets identified using protein targets through MR could be biased by horizontal pleiotropy, despite our use of cis-eQTLs which are less pleiotropic than trans-eQTLS. Third, no positive results were obtained in the analysis of retinal tissue, possibly due to the limited sample size. In contrast to TSSK6, which is mainly specifically expressed in the testis, the other 5 genes-CD34, CD55, Wnt3, LCAT, and BTN3A1 can be detected in the ocular tissue, which may be promising for local drug delivery. Last, the eQTL data were obtained using adult tissues while myopia develops during childhood. When eQTL data from children become available in the future, it will be beneficial to conduct a reanalysis using this specific dataset.
