CaoZhiyuan PuChengfei JiangXianyang HanGuiting ShenXiaogang WangWensheng DingWei HuangZhipeng HuangXi JiaBeibei LuVictor X TianLe WuZhao XiaoLei However, CAR-T therapy has shown limited efficacy in the treatment of solid tumors. One major challenge in the treatment of solid tumors is the lack of specific surface targets, which severely limits the application of CAR-T cells in solid tumors. Prostate cancer is the second most common cancer among men worldwide, and patients with late-stage metastatic prostate cancer have a 5-year survival rate of only 30%. Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop effective treatments for advanced prostate cancer. Currently, several targets, such as prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA), have been tested for CAR-T cell therapy in prostate cancer. However, since PSMA is also expressed in the central nervous system, it poses potential safety risks related to neurotoxicity. 
Another major challenge in the treatment of solid tumors is insufficient expansion of solid tumor-targeting CAR-T cells. In hematological malignancies, CAR-T cells can recognize tumor antigens in the bloodstream, such as CD19 or B-cell maturation antigen (BCMA), which allows for rapid expansion and significant antitumor effects. However, in patients with solid tumors, CAR-T cells often cannot effectively access tumor antigens shielded by the tumor microenvironment, resulting in a lack of expansion signals and poor CAR-T cell proliferation, which limits their antitumor activity. 
Here, we used a novel target screening approach and characterized prostatic acid phosphatase (PAP) as a feasible target for the treatment of prostate cancer with CAR-T cell therapy. An approach called CoupledCAR was also developed to expand solid tumor-targeting CAR-T cells without the need for solid tumor antigens, resulting in less exhaustion, better memory status and better killing ability than conventional CAR-T cell technology. Recently, GCC19CAR-T, based on the CoupledCAR technology, showed impressive efficacy in a clinical study targeting guanylate cyclase-C in third-line metastatic colorectal cancer. In this study, we further clarify the underlying mechanism of CoupledCAR and demonstrate that CoupledCAR functions as a platform technology, showing similar efficacy when applied to the PAP target.

## Methods

## Preparation of transmembrane PAP antigen and screening of anti-PAP specific single-chain variable fragment
The transmembrane PAP (tmPAP) gene (Sino Biological, HG10959-M) was cloned into the recombinant antigen expression vector PTSE-His (Beijing Bettermab Biotechnology). The constructed recombinant plasmid was transfected into HEK293 cells (Beijing Bettermab Biotechnology), and the recombinant tmPAP-His protein expressed by the HEK293 cells was purified using a HisTrap FF affinity chromatography column (GE HealthCare Bio-Sciences). The purified recombinant tmPAP-His protein was used as an antigen for screening a human single-chain antibody library (Beijing Bettermab Biotechnology). After three rounds of screening, 40 monoclonal single chain antibodies that specifically bind to the tmPAP antigen were obtained, from which seven distinct antibody sequences were identified and designated as S4C7, S5D1, S5F2, S9F5, S10E12, S12D4, and S12D9. These single-chain antibody fragments were inserted into phage vectors (Beijing Bettermab Biotechnology) to generate monoclonal single-chain antibody phages, followed by antigen-binding analysis using the recombinant tmPAP-His protein.

## Characterization of fully humanized PAP antibodies
The variable regions of the heavy and light chains of the selected single-chain variable fragment (scFv) antibodies were cloned into the eukaryotic expression vectors pTSEG1n and pTSEK (Beijing Bettermab Biotechnology), respectively, and transfected into HEK293 cells to produce fully humanized antibodies. The recombinant humanized antibodies were purified using a protein A affinity chromatography column. TmPAP-His antigen was coated onto a plate, and after blocking, gradient-diluted purified recombinant humanized antibodies were added. Anti-human IgG-HRP secondary antibody (Beijing Bettermab Biotechnology) was added for detection. The binding was quantified via a colorimetric reaction, and monoclonal antibodies that specifically bound to the tmPAP-His antigen were identified.

## Binding ability and affinity analysis of monoclonal antibodies
In a flow cytometry-based assay, PC3-tmPAP cells were labeled separately with each of the seven fully recombinant humanized antibodies for 0.5 hours at 4°C. After washing, the cells were stained with goat anti-human IgG-AF488 (Abcam, Ab96907) in stain buffer for 0.5 hours at 4°C. Following two washes, the cells were analyzed on a CytoFlex flow cytometer (Beckman Coulter) through the FITC (Fluorescein Isothiocyanate) channel. The binding of PAP antibodies was determined by the mean fluorescence intensity of Alexa Fluor 488 on live PC3-tmPAP cells. In the affinity analysis experiment, the S5D1 antibody was coated onto a pretreated chip, and different concentrations of recombinant human tmPAP protein were added as the mobile phase. Antibody affinity was measured using the BIAcore X-100 (GE HealthCare Bio-Sciences) system.

## Healthy donors and cell lines
Peripheral blood samples were collected from healthy donors who provided informed consent. The human prostate cancer cell line PC3 was cultured in F12-K medium (Gibco, 21127–022) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS, Gibco, 16000–044). PC3 cells were transduced with a lentiviral vector expressing the tmPAP gene to generate PC3-tmPAP cells. The CD19-positive Nalm6 cell line was cultured under standard conditions (F12-K medium supplemented with 10% FBS). The human embryonic kidney cell line 293T (Thermo, A35347) was cultured in DMEM medium (Dulbecco's Modified Eagle Medium) (Gibco, 11995–040) supplemented with 10% FBS.

## Vector construction
First, the signal peptide sequence of CD8α (GenBank number AAH25715.1, aa 1–21) was fused to a human PAP-specific scFv derived from S5D1. Second, the scFv fragment was fused to the human CD8α transmembrane domain (GenBank number NP_001759.3, aa 138–206), followed by the human 4-1BB costimulatory domain (GenBank number NP_001552.2, aa 214–255), human CD3ζ signaling moieties (GenBank number NP_000725, aa 52–163), and green fluorescence protein (GFP, GenBank number AAB02572.1, aa 1–239). The CD19-CAR was constructed with a humanized antibody (L2D8, humanized from FMC63). 

## Lentivirus production
Lentiviral plasmids and packing plasmids were transfected into 293 T cells (Thermo, A35347). Virus-containing supernatant was collected 48 hours after transfection and then concentrated and purified with a tangential flow filter (Repligen, S02-S05U-05-S). Lentivirus was stored at −80°C, and their titers were determined using 293 T cells.

## Human T-cell and B-cell sorting
Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were isolated from the blood of four healthy donors by gradient centrifugation in Lymphocyte Separation Medium (MP Biomedicals LLC, 50494). After washing twice with MACS (Magnetic-Activated Cell Sorting) buffer (Miltenyi, 130-091-221) containing 5% human serum albumin, T cells were selected by using a CD4/CD8 separation kit (Miltenyi, 130-045-101/130-045-201) and suspended at 1 million cells/mL in TexMacs serum-free medium (Miltenyi, 170-076-309) supplemented with 12.5 ng/mL interleukin-2 (IL-2, R&D, 202-IL-500). B cells were selected by using a B Cell Isolation Kit II (Miltenyi, 130-091-151) and similarly cultured.

## Generation of CAR-T cells
24 hours after separation and stimulation with Transact (Miltenyi, 130-111-160), T cells were transduced with lentiviruses encoding CD19 CAR or PAP CAR (with/without GFP). After 24 hours of transduction, the supernatants were removed, and the culture media were changed every 48 hours. Cells were maintained in TexMacs medium with 12.5 ng/mL IL-2.

## PAP CAR-T cells in vivo assay
6-week-old female NOG mice (Beijing Vital River) were subcutaneously injected with 2.0×106 PC3-tmPAP cells. 20 days later (Day 0), the total 10 inoculated mice were randomly divided into a non-transduced (NT, n=5) and PAP CAR-T (n=5) groups, receiving 1.0×106 NT or PAP CAR-T cells via tail vein injection. The individual mouse was considered the experimental unit within the studies. Retro-orbital blood was collected to assess the expansion of T cells in the peripheral blood by flow cytometry on Day 14. Tumor volumes were measured every 2–3 days until Day 21.

## Cytotoxicity assay using the Agilent RTCA system
NT cells, CD19 CAR-T cells and PAP CAR-T cells were cultured in TexMacs medium supplemented with 12.5 ng/mL IL-2 until Day 9. To mimic the in vivo environment, on Day 9, cells were divided into groups and cultured alone or mixed with B cells in different ratios in TexMacs medium without IL-2. Meanwhile, PC3-tmPAP cells (1.0×104 per well) were cultured in a 96-well plate (xCELLigence RTCA system). After 48 hours, the previously cultured cells, either alone or in combination with B cells, were co-cultured with the PC3-tmPAP cells according to their respective groups in the 96-well plate. The cell index was automatically measured every 15 min for 120 hours. The ratio of the difference between the values of the sample and control to the value of the control was defined as the specific lysis value.

## Flow cytometry and immunohistochemistry
CAR expression on CAR-T cells was assessed using rabbit anti-human IgG (Jackson ImmunoResearch, 309-065-006) or mouse IgG (Jackson ImmunoResearch, 315-065-006). The monoclonal antibodies used for human T cells and PBMC staining included: CD45-PE (BD, 555483, clone: HI30), CD3-PerCPCy5.5 (BioLegend, 300430, clone: UCHT1), CD4-FITC (BioLegend, 317408, clone: OKT4), CD8-APC-CY7 (BioLegend, 344714, clone: SK1), CD19-APC (BD, 555415, clone: HIB19), CD45RA-A700 (BD, 560673, clone: HI100), CD62L-PE-CY7 (BioLegend, 304822, clone: DREG-56), CD69-FITC (BioLegend, 310904, clone: FN50), CD80-BV786 (BD, 564159, clone: L307.4), CD86-BV605 (BD, 562999, clone: FUN-1), CD137-APC (BD, 550890, clone: 4B4-1), PD-1-APC (BD, 558694, clone: MIH4), TIM3-APC-CY7 (BioLegend, 345044, clone: F38-2E2), LAG-3-PC5.5 (BioLegend, 369312, clone: 11C3C65), and goat anti-human IgG-AF488 (Abcam, Ab96907). A rabbit mAb (D3Y5P, #12861, Cell Signaling) was used for immunohistochemistry (IHC) analysis of PAP expression. Formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded specimens were purchased in the form of commercially available tissue microarrays (HOrgN090PT02, Shanghai Outdo Biotech Company). PAP antibody (BioIntron, B21482901) was used to measure PAP expression on the surfaces of PC3 cells. IHC analysis of tumor tissue sections obtained from animal experiments was performed using the following primary antibodies: CD3 (Cell Signaling Technology, #85061), CD4 (CST, #48274), CD8 (CST, #85336S), and GFP (CST, #2956S). Subsequent quantitative evaluation of stained sections was conducted using QuPath software (V.0.5.1). 

## Cytokine cytometric bead array
In vitro co-culture supernatants and murine peripheral blood plasma were collected, and cytokine levels were analyzed by flow cytometry microspheres (Beckman CytoFLEX). FCAP Array software was used to analyze the levels of the cytokines, including IL-2 (BD, 558270), interferon gamma (IFN-γ, BD, 560111), tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-α, BD, 558273), and granzyme-B (BD, 560304).

## CoupledCAR in vivo assay
6-week-old female NOG mice were subcutaneously injected with 2.0×106 PC3-tmPAP cells. After 24 days (Day 0), a total of 63 mice were randomly divided into nine groups (n=7 per group): MOCK, NT, B cells, CD19 CAR-T, PAP CAR-T, PAP CAR-T+CD19 CAR-T, CD19 CAR-T+B cell, PAP CAR-T+B cell, and PAP CAR-T+CD19 CAR-T+B cell. Each group was infused via the tail vein with 200 µL DPBS (Gibco, 14190–136), containing 1.0×106 NT cells, 1.0×106 B cells, 1.0×106 CD19 CAR-T cells, 3.3×105 PAP CAR-T cells, or their combinations as specified. On Day 3, 6 and 9, the mice in the B-cell group, CD19 CAR-T+B cell group, PAP CAR-T+B cell group and PAP CAR-T+CD19 CAR-T+B cell group were infused with 1.0×106 B cells. The individual mouse was considered the experimental unit within the studies. Retro-orbital blood was collected on Day 14 to assess T-cell expansion and cytokine levels by flow cytometry. Tumor volumes were measured every 3 days, and infiltration of CD3+, CD4+, CD8+, and PAP-CAR T cells into tumors was assessed by IHC.

## Single-cell sequencing analysis
Single-cell sequencing was performed by NovelBio Bio-Pharm Technology. The single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-Seq) libraries and V(D)J libraries were generated using the 10x Genomics Chromium Controller Instrument and Chromium Single Cell 5’ library and gel bead kit, along with the V(D)J enrichment kit (10x Genomics, Pleasanton, California, USA). The cells (~1,000 cells/µL) were loaded into each channel to generate single-cell gel bead-in-emulsions (GEMs). After reverse transcription, GEMs were disrupted, and barcoded complementary DNA (cDNA) was purified, amplified, and used to construct 5’ gene expression and T-cell receptor (TCR)-enriched libraries. For the 5’ library and the V (D) J library construction, the amplified barcoded cDNA was fragmented, A-tailed, ligated with adaptors and index PCR amplified. The final libraries were quantified using the Qubit High Sensitivity DNA assay (Thermo Fisher Scientific), and the size distribution of the libraries was determined using a High Sensitivity DNA chip on a Bioanalyzer 2200 (Agilent). All libraries were sequenced on an Illumina sequencer (Illumina, San Diego, California, USA) using a 150 bp paired-end run.
Barcode processing, alignment, and gene quantification were conducted using Cell Ranger Suite with GRCh38 as the reference genome. The pretreated messenger RNA (mRNA) data matrices were then imported into R, and downstream analysis was performed with the Seurat package. Low-quality cells (<200 genes, >2,500 genes, or >10% mitochondrial genes) were filtered. Data were normalized and scaled using the Seurat package. T cells were classified as PAP CAR-T or CD19 CAR-T based on integrated gene fragments. Differential gene expression analysis was performed using non-parametric t-tests, and significant genes were visualized with ggplot2.
GSEA (Gene Set Enrichment Analysis) was conducted with the MAST R package using Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes gene sets from the MSigDB database and T-cell-related pathways. The CellChat R toolkit (V.1.1.3) was used to assign cellular ligand-receptor interaction cycle scores to PAP CAR-T cells, CD19 CAR-T cells and B cells.

## CellTrace expansion assay
Different combinations of CAR-T cells, NT cells and B cells were stained with CellTrace Violet (Thermo Fisher, C34557) or the CellTrace Far Red Cell (Thermo Fisher, C34564). Cell expansion was analyzed via flow cytometry every 24 hours for 144 hours.

## Statistical analysis
GraphPad Prism software was used for all the statistical analyses. The data are presented as the mean±SEM. Data shows technical replicates unless otherwise stated. Statistics were calculated using unpaired t-test or one-way analysis of variance as stated in the figure legends. Correlations were estimated by the calculation of two-tailed Pearson coefficients and significance. All statistical analyses were performed with Prism software V.8.3.0 (GraphPad). The following symbols indicate statistical significance: *p<0.05, **p<0.01, ***p<0.001, ****p<0.0001, and p≥0.05; NS, no significance.

## Results

## Identification of PAP as a potential prostate cancer target for CAR-T cell therapy
Based on the premise that the prostate is generally surgically removed from most patients with advanced prostate cancer, we tried to identify genes specifically expressed in both normal prostate cells and prostate tumor cells as targets for CAR-T cell therapy. Transcriptome data from the Human Protein Atlas database (www.proteinatlas.org/about/download) of all the genes that encode proteins expressed in 31 normal tissues and organs were downloaded, and the average protein-coding transcripts per million values of the genes in the tissue samples were collected to normalize the mRNA levels. A group of transmembrane genes specifically expressed in the prostate was defined and selected based on their mRNA levels being at least fivefold higher than those in all other tissues. Among the selected genes, we found that PAP was specifically expressed in normal prostatic epithelial cells and prostate cancer cells but was expressed at very low or undetectable levels in other normal tissues ( ). PAP expression was confirmed in 480 prostate cancer tissues based on an analysis of prostate cancer tissues from The Cancer Genome Atlas database ( ). PAP has two variants, tmPAP and secretory PAP. Here, we focused on tmPAP, which can be targeted by CAR-T cells. We verified that the PAP protein was specifically expressed on the membranes of normal prostate cells and prostate cancer cells but not those of other normal tissues by IHC staining ( ). These results indicated that PAP is a specific prostate cancer target for CAR-T cell therapy.
 

## PAP-specific CAR-T cells show antitumor activity both in vitro and in vivo
The PC3 cell line is commonly used to study prostate cancer. Some previous studies indicated that the PC3 cell line expressed PAP, while the majority of studies found that it does not. We verified that PAP is not expressed in the PC3 cell line. Therefore, we transduced the PC3 cell line with a lentiviral vector to express tmPAP and established the PC3-tmPAP cell line for PAP-specific antibody screening and the CAR-T cell activity study ( ). To generate antibodies against PAP, we screened the human single-chain antibody library using recombinant tmPAP protein. After three rounds of screening, we identified seven single-chain antibodies that specifically bind to recombinant tmPAP-His antigen ( ). We cloned the seven single-chain antibodies into eukaryotic expression vectors to generate fully humanized antibodies and all the antibodies demonstrated binding ability to the tmPAP-His antigen ( ). Furthermore, we evaluated their binding ability to the PC3-tmPAP cell line. Interestingly, only two antibodies demonstrated binding to tmPAP, with S5D1 showing the best binding ability ( ). The affinity measurement of the S5D1 antibody also showed high affinity ( ). Based on scFv of S5D1, second-generation PAP-targeting CAR-T cells were constructed with or without the GFP reporter ( ). We found that PAP CAR-T cells could efficiently kill PAP-positive cells ( ) and release significant amounts of IFN-γ, IL-2, and TNF-α in vitro ( ). No killing or detectable cytokine release was observed when PAP CAR-T cells were co-cultured with PAP-negative cells ( ). To assess the in vivo antitumor activity of PAP CAR-T cells, NOG mice were subcutaneously injected with 2.0×106 PC3-tmPAP cells 20 days before the intravenous injection of 1.0×106 PAP CAR-T or NT cells ( ). Significant expansion of PAP CAR-T cells in peripheral blood was observed on Day 14 ( ). IFN-γ production was detected on Day 14 in the PAP CAR-T cell group ( ). However, IL-2 and TNF-α were not detected in the peripheral blood of any group, maybe because these two cytokines were locally released ( ). The tumor volumes injected with PAP CAR-T cells were substantially reduced compared with those in the mice injected with NT cells ( ). These results demonstrated that PAP is a valid target for CAR-T cells and that PAP CAR-T cells are very efficient in killing PAP-positive cells both in vitro and in vivo.
 

## Anti-CD19 CAR-T cells induce remarkable expansion of non-transduced normal T cells
Although second-generation PAP CAR-T cells were shown to have a reasonable killing ability in the animal experiments, conventional solid tumor-targeting CAR-T cell therapies have shown limited efficacy in patients based on clinical trials. The lack of efficacy in solid tumors is due to the limited number of antigens outside the tumor microenvironment that can stimulate CAR signaling, resulting in very poor CAR-T cell activation and expansion. Therefore, further improvements to the activation and/or expansion of solid tumor-targeting CAR-T cells without antigens may be helpful. In our previous clinical trial on the use of CD19 CAR-T cells in the treatment of leukemia, we unexpectedly observed a phenomenon in which NT cells were expanded together with CD19 CAR-T cells ( ). This phenomenon suggested that the killing of CD19-positive tumor cells or B cells by CD19 CAR-T cells can facilitate the expansion of NT cells without NT cell-specific antigens. To confirm this phenomenon, we performed an in vitro assay in which CD19 CAR-T cells were co-cultured with NT cells with or without the addition of B cells or CD19 positive tumor cells (Nalm6), and found that NT cells exhibited significant expansion without specific antigen stimulation ( ). We hypothesized that replacing some NT cells with solid tumor-targeting CAR-T cells could enhance the expansion of solid tumor-targeting CAR-T cells when CD19 CAR-T cells kill CD19-positive cells.
 

## The CoupledCAR approach enhanced the expansion and functionality of PAP CAR-T cells in vitro
We conducted a co-culture assay using three different cell populations: CD19 CAR-T cells, PAP CAR-T cells, and NT cells. On adding B cells, we observed a significant expansion in the populations of CD19 CAR-T cells, PAP CAR-T cells, and NT cells, both in terms of the percentage of proliferating cells and the absolute cell numbers ( ). The expression levels of activation markers, such as CD137 and CD69, were shown to be upregulated in CD19 CAR-T cells, but NT cells and PAP CAR-T cells showed very slight upregulation ( ). Moreover, CD19 CAR-T cells exhibited significantly upregulated levels of the exhaustion markers PD-1 (Programmed Cell Death Protein 1) and LAG-3 (Lymphocyte Activation Gene-3), while PAP CAR-T and NT cells had much lower expression levels of these exhaustion markers ( ). In addition, PAP CAR-T cells and NT cells maintained higher percentages of cells with the stem-like central memory T cell (CD45ROnegCD62Lpos) and central memory T cell (CD45ROposCD62Lpos) phenotypes and lower percentages of cells with the effector memory cell (CD45ROnegCD62Lneg) and effector T cell (CD45ROposCD62Lneg) phenotypes than CD19 CAR-T cells ( ), while CD19 CAR-T cells exhibited higher percentages of cells with the effector T cell (CD45ROposCD62Lneg) phenotype. In the cytotoxicity assay, the PAP CAR-T cells+CD19 CAR-T cells+B cells groups with different CD19 CAR-T: B cell ratios showed better tumor cell lysis abilities than the PAP CAR-T group ( ). These data suggest that PAP CAR-T cells could expand without antigen, exhibit lower exhaustion and higher memory-like status, and achieve better killing when co-cultured with CD19 CAR-T cells and B cells. We named this system for expanding solid tumor-targeting CAR-T cells CoupledCAR, and this system includes CD19 CAR-T cells and solid tumor-targeting CAR-T cells.
 

## The CoupledCAR approach enhanced the efficient expansion and enhanced the functionality of PAP CAR-T cells in vivo
To determine whether the CoupledCAR approach enhances the anti-solid tumor efficacy of PAP CAR-T cells in vivo, we tested the CoupledCAR system in NOG mice with subcutaneous tumors. CD19 CAR-T cells or PAP CAR-T cells were infused alone or in combination via the tail vein 24 days after the transplantation of tumor cells, and selected groups were infused with B cells at four time points ( ). CD19 CAR-T cells were found to expand rapidly on encountering B cells in both the CD19 CAR-T cell+B cell group and the CD19 CAR-T cell+PAP CAR-T cell+B cell group ( ). The PAP CAR-T cells in peripheral blood in the CD19 CAR-T cell+PAP CAR-T cell+B cell group expanded by more than fivefold compared with those in the PAP CAR-T cell group on Day 14 ( ). The peripheral blood levels of IFN-γ and granzyme B were significantly increased in the CD19 CAR-T cell+PAP CAR-T cell+B cell group ( ). Moreover, the CD19 CAR-T cell+PAP CAR-T cell+B cell group showed better tumor killing than all the other groups ( ). In addition, the CD19 CAR-T cell+PAP CAR-T cell+B cell group showed the highest degree of CD3+, CD4+, CD8+, and PAP CAR-T cell infiltration into tumors ( ). In conclusion, the CD19 CAR-T cell+PAP CAR-T cell+B cell group showed better CAR-T cell expansion, infiltration and antitumor effects than all the other groups.
 

## Expansion of PAP CAR-T cells was mediated by costimulatory and cytokine/chemokine signaling
To determine the mechanism by which PAP CAR-T cells substantially expand in the CoupledCAR system, we conducted scRNA-Seq analysis. We sequenced the mRNA of each single cell from a co-culture assay at different time points ( ).
 Previous studies showed that the expansion and activation of T cells depended on three signals, including TCR signals (Signal 1), costimulatory signals (Signal 2) and cytokine signals (Signal 3). We found that TCR signaling and costimulatory signaling were highly activated in CD19 CAR-T cells at 4, 24, and 48 hours ( ). Interestingly, PAP CAR-T cells exhibited no TCR signaling activation ( ). The expression of CD80 and CD86, ligands for CD28, was significantly upregulated in B cells at 24 and 48 hours, and the expression of CD70, ligand for CD27, was significantly upregulated in both B cells and CD19 CAR-T cells at 24 and 48 hours, and also, the expression of OX40L, ligands for OX40, was significantly upregulated in CD19 CAR-T cells at 24 and 48 hours ( ), providing a source of the costimulatory signal. Moreover, the expression levels of cytokines or chemokines that promote T-cell expansion, such as IL-2, IL-3, IL-4, IL-6, IL-15, TNF and CXCL9, were significantly upregulated in CD19 CAR-T cells or B cells ( ). Our data suggest that PAP CAR-T cell expansion should be mediated by Signal 2 and Signal 3, which are provided by CD19 CAR-T cells and B cells, but not by Signal 1.
Previous studies have shown that TCR-based expansion results in CAR-T cell exhaustion, while an expansion approach based on Signals 2 and 3 results in reduced exhaustion. The expression of genes associated with exhaustion and memory was analyzed, and we found that PAP CAR-T cells showed a memory-like status and reduced exhaustion ( ), which can enhance the efficacy of CAR-T cells. The expression of the memory-related genes CCR7, IL-7R, SELL, TCF7 and LEF1, etc., was upregulated in PAP CAR-T cells and the expression of the exhaustion-related genes PDCD1, LAG-3, ID3, NR4A1, NR4A2, and NR4A3, etc were downregulated in PAP CAR-T cells compared with CD19 CAR-T cells. Unexpectedly, we found that the expression of the trafficking-related genes KLF2, CXCR6, ITGAL, and ITGB7, etc were also significantly upregulated ( ), suggesting that PAP CAR-T cells had better infiltration ability than CD19 CAR-T cells. This is consistent with the results mentioned above ( ). These data suggest that PAP CAR-T cells exhibit lower exhaustion and maintain higher memory-like status in the CoupledCAR system, which may result in better antitumor ability.

## Analysis of cell–cell communication using CellChat
To verify the signaling networks, the R toolkit CellChat was used to analyze ligand-to-receptor interactions among PAP CAR-T cells, CD19 CAR-T cells and B cells. The results showed that PAP CAR-T cells received CD80 and CD86 signals mainly from B cells at 4 hours, 24 hours and 48 hours and received decreased CD80 signals from CD19 CAR-T cells at 24 hours and 48 hours ( ). PAP CAR-T cells also received CD70 signals from B cells at 4 hours and 24 hours and received CD70 signals from CD19 CAR-T cells at 4 hours, 24 hours and 48 hours ( ). We found that both B cells and CD19 CAR-T cells delivered IL-2/15-mediated signals to PAP CAR-T cells at 24 hours and 48 hours ( ). Moreover, B cells delivered CXCL-mediated signals starting at 4 hours ( ). These data indicate that CD19 CAR-T cells and B cells provide Signals 2 and 3 to PAP CAR-T cells. To confirm protein expression in B cells, we also analyzed the remaining B cells in the CoupledCAR system after co-culture using flow cytometry. The results revealed upregulated expression of CD80 and CD86 in B cells ( ), which was consistent with the transcriptome results. This suggests that the B cells were activated and may serve as antigen-presenting cells, providing costimulatory signals that promote the expansion of PAP CAR-T cells. 
 In conclusion, our study shows that PAP CAR-T cells were expanded in the CoupledCAR system by costimulatory signals and cytokine/chemokine signals without stimulation by antigens, and these cells showed less exhaustion, more memory-like status, and better trafficking abilities than conventional CAR-T cells.

## Discussion
Our findings have the following important significance.
First, our study indicates that PAP is a specific and valid target for CAR-T therapy and may be a potentially better target than PSMA due to its stringent specificity. Although PAP is a biomarker for prostate cancer and the target of Provenge, a cancer vaccine by Dendreon, previous studies suggested it could not be used as a target for antibody drugs or cell therapies for prostate cancer, because PAP was reported to be not specific to the prostate but rather also expressed in normal bladder, kidney, pancreas, cervix, testis, lung and ovary tissues, which limits its usefulness as a specific target for prostate cancer. However, these earlier findings were likely influenced by false-positive staining caused by the non-specificity of polyclonal antibodies used in those studies. In contrast, our database analysis and IHC staining based on specific antibody confirmed that PAP is expressed in only normal prostate epithelial cells and not in any other normal tissues. Our study indicates that PAP is a suitable prostate cancer target and can be used for CAR-T cell therapy, which may greatly increase the prospect of clinical application. Moreover, the strategy of screening surface antigens expressed on corresponding normal tissues that are dispensable for life, such as the prostate, may serve as a novel method for expanding possible solid tumor-specific targets.
Second, we showed that the CoupledCAR approach provides a novel and feasible strategy for expanding CAR-T cells without any direct antigen stimulation. Recently, different approaches have been used to promote the antigen-specific expansion of solid tumor-targeting CAR-T cells. While antigen-dependent expansion methods can be used to expand solid tumor-targeting CAR-T cells, these methods simultaneously may cause rapid cell exhaustion due to direct stimulation via CD3ζ signaling. CAR-T cell exhaustion leads to insufficient therapeutic efficacy, while memory-like status CAR-T cells are more capable of sustaining persistence and survival, which enhances their antitumor activity and efficacy. In contrast, the CoupledCAR approach enhances the memory-like status of CAR-T cells and does not lead to rapid cell exhaustion, which may enhance their efficacy in the treatment of solid tumors. Additionally, the adverse events induced by the CD19 CAR-T cell-mediated elimination of B cells were minimal, and elimination of regulatory B cells from the solid tumor microenvironment may be beneficial in the treatment of solid tumors. Therefore, the antigen-independent expansion of solid tumor-targeting CAR-T cells may be superior to antigen-dependent expansion with regard to the killing of solid tumor cells in patients.
Finally, the antigen-independent expansion approach is not based on a specific tumor antigen, suggesting that it should work as a platform to support not only PAP CAR-T therapies but also other solid tumor-targeting CAR-T therapies. The tumor microenvironment is a barrier that limits not only CAR-T cell therapy but also other types of adoptive T-cell therapy that also require direct physical interactions between T cells and tumor-specific target antigen(s) for the activation and expansion of T cells. Therefore, this novel approach can also be incorporated into TCR-T or tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte-based therapies to facilitate the expansion of infused T cells in vivo. Moreover, CD19 CAR-T cells in the CoupledCAR system may also be replaced with CAR-T cells that target other white blood cell antigens (eg, CD20, CD22, and BCMA) as long as the CAR-T cells can engage these targets in the circulating blood. This strategy may provide feasible and flexible opportunities for solid tumor cell therapies.
This antigen-independent expansion approach is the key for advancing CAR-T cell therapies against solid tumors. CoupledCAR approach represents a universal platform technology that can be effectively extended to other solid tumor CAR-T applications. Here, we are currently conducting clinical studies to further validate the efficacy of PAP-targeted CoupledCAR-T therapy in patients with prostate cancer.
