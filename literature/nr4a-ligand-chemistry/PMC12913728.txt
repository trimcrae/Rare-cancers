## Abstract
In this phase 2 study (NCT05047991), patients with unresectable metastatic pancreatic adenocarcinoma were randomized to receive NALIRIFOX (liposomal irinotecan, 5-FU, leucovorin, and oxaliplatin) or gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel. The primary endpoint was progression free survival (PFS). Secondary endpoints included other efficacy outcomes (overall survival, objective response rate, disease control rate, and duration of response), as well as safety, pharmacokinetic parameters, and evaluation of the relationship between UGT1A1*6 and UGT1A1*28 polymorphisms and safety. A total of 117 patients were enrolled and randomly assigned to NALIRIFOX (n = 78) or gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel (n = 39). At a median follow-up of 18.7 months (interquartile range [IQR], 7.5–22.1) for NALIRIFOX and 12.1 months (IQR: 6.4–14.8) for the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel, median PFS was 7.6 months (95% CI 5.52–9.23) with NALIRIFOX versus 3.7 months (95% CI 3.38-5.32) with gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel (hazard ratio, 0.56; 95% CI, 0.35–0.88; P = 0.0115). ≥ Grade 3 treatment-emergent adverse events (TEAEs) occurred in 73.1% of patients receiving NALIRIFOX and 84.6% of patients receiving gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel, respectively. Despite the premature termination (predetermined sample size of n = 153 not reached) of the study, NALIRIFOX demonstrated improvement in PFS compared with gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel, with a manageable safety profile in Chinese patients with advanced pancreatic adenocarcinoma.
Subject terms: Pancreatic cancer, Pancreatic cancer

In the phase III NAPOLI-3 trial, first-line treatment with NALIRIFOX was associated with improved overall survival compared to gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel in patients with metastatic pancreatic adenocarcinoma. Here the authors report the results of a randomized phase II trial comparing the two regimens, NALIRIFOX versus Gemcitabine plus Nab-Paclitaxel, in Chinese patients.

## Introduction
Pancreatic adenocarcinoma is the sixth leading cause of cancer-related mortality in China, accounting for approximately 85,000 deaths annually . The poor prognosis is mainly attributable to the asymptomatic nature of the early-stage disease, resulting in 80%–85% of patients being diagnosed at an advanced, unresectable stage , . Therapeutic options for patients with advanced or metastatic pancreatic cancer remain limited, and outcomes are dismal.
For patients without actionable molecular alterations, standard first-line chemotherapy regimens include FOLFIRINOX (a combination of 5-fluorouracil [5-FU], leucovorin, oxaliplatin, and irinotecan) , gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel or gemcitabine monotherapy – , Despite extensive clinical research over the past decade, the majority of trials in pancreatic adenocarcinoma have yielded negative results , and few meaningful advances in systemic therapy have been achieved. Immunotherapeutic approaches, in particular, have not demonstrated consistent clinical benefit in this disease context .
The phase III NAPOLI-3 trial demonstrated that first-line treatment with NALIRIFOX—a regimen comprising liposomal irinotecan, 5-FU, leucovorin, and oxaliplatin—was associated with a significant improvement in overall survival compared to gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel in patients with metastatic pancreatic adenocarcinoma . This was the first positive phase III study in this setting in over a decade and established NALIRIFOX as a therapeutic option for this population. While no head-to-head trials have directly compared NALIRIFOX with FOLFIRINOX, recent meta-analyses suggest comparable survival outcomes between these regimens when evaluated against gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel .
However, Asian patients were underrepresented in the NAPOLI-3 trial, comprising only 5% of the study population , and the efficacy and safety of NALIRIFOX in Asian populations—particularly Chinese patients—remain inadequately characterized.
This randomized, open-label phase II trial was conducted to evaluate the efficacy and safety of first-line NALIRIFOX compared with gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel in Chinese patients with advanced or metastatic pancreatic adenocarcinoma. In this work, we demonstrate that NALIRIFOX shows improvement in PFS compared with gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel, with a manageable safety profile in Chinese patients with advanced pancreatic adenocarcinoma.

## Results

## Patient disposition and baseline characteristics
Between November 3, 2021, and November 15, 2022, 161 patients were screened, and 117 were enrolled and randomized: 78 to the NALIRIFOX group and 39 to the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group (Fig.  ). At the data cutoff (June 30, 2024), three patients in the NALIRIFOX group and none in the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group remained on treatment.
 Baseline demographic and disease characteristics were generally well-balanced between treatment groups (Table  ). The majority of patients had an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status (PS) of 1, stage IV disease, and liver metastases.
 

## Efficacy
At the data cutoff (June 30, 2024), the median follow-up time was 18.7 months (interquartile range [IQR], 7.5–22.1) for NALIRIFOX and 12.1 months (IQR: 6.4–14.8) for the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel. 53 patients (67.9%) in the NALIRIFOX group and 30 (76.9%) in the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group had experienced disease progression or death. Median progression-free survival (PFS) was significantly longer in the NALIRIFOX group (7.6 months; 95% confidence interval [CI], 5.52–9.23) compared to the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group (3.7 months; 95% CI, 3.38–5.32), with a hazard ratio (HR) of 0.56 (95% CI, 0.35–0.88; nominal P = 0.0115) (Fig.  ). PFS benefit was consistently observed across prespecified subgroups (Fig.  ), including those with liver metastasis, ECOG PS of 1, female sex, and stage IV disease. A numerically greater PFS benefit was seen in patients with baseline CA19-9 levels <37 U/mL.
 At the time of analysis, 60 patients (76.9%) in the NALIRIFOX group and 32 (82.1%) in the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group had died. Median overall survival (OS) was 12.9 months (95% CI, 8.84–16.43) in the NALIRIFOX group versus 8.9 months (95% CI, 6.90–12.62) in the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group (HR, 0.72; 95% CI, 0.47–1.11; nominal P = 0.1371) (Fig.  ). OS trends favored NALIRIFOX across most subgroups (Fig.  ), with particular benefit in patients with liver metastases, ≥3 visceral metastases, and stage IV disease at baseline. A numerically greater OS benefit was also observed in patients with CA19-9 < 37 U/mL.
The confirmed objective response rate (ORR) was higher in the NALIRIFOX group than in the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group (32.1% vs. 20.5%; between-group difference, 11.5%; 95% CI, 6.91 to 27.17) (Table  ). The median time to response (TTR) was 3.7 months in both groups. duration of response (DoR) was notably longer with NALIRIFOX (8.5 months vs. 3.8 months, P = 0.0234) (Fig.  ). Overall, 37 (47.4%) of 78 patients in the NALIRIFOX group and 21 (53.8%) of 39 patients in the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel received subsequent anticancer therapy (Supplemental Table  ).
 Both groups received a median of 4.0 treatment cycles. The median treatment duration was 4.71 months with NALIRIFOX and 3.45 months with gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel. The mean relative dose intensities were comparable between the two regimens: liposomal irinotecan (81.3% ± 14.8%), 5-FU (76.5% ± 14.6%), leucovorin (82.8% ± 14.1%), and oxaliplatin (80.9% ± 15.0%) in the NALIRIFOX group, and gemcitabine (82.8% ± 21.2%) and nab-paclitaxel (82.9% ± 21.4%) in the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group.

## Safety
All patients in both treatment groups experienced at least one treatment-emergent adverse event (TEAE) (Table  , Supplemental Table  ). Grade ≥3 TEAEs were reported in 73.1% of patients in the NALIRIFOX group and 84.6% in the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group. Common all-grade TEAEs included neutropenia (87.2% vs. 82.1%), leukopenia (82.1% vs. 87.2%), anemia (79.5% vs. 84.6%), thrombocytopenia (66.7% vs. 69.2%), nausea (73.1% vs. 48.7%), and fatigue (65.4% vs. 51.3%).
 Grade ≥3 neutropenia was more frequent with gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel (66.7%) than with NALIRIFOX (46.2%). Leukopenia and hypokalemia were the next most common grade ≥3 events with NALIRIFOX (25.6% and 23.1%, respectively), while leukopenia was also frequent in the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group (48.7%) (Table  ). Serious TEAEs occurred in 39.7% of patients in the NALIRIFOX group and 33.3% in the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group (Supplemental Table  ). 16 patients (20.5) in the NALIRIFOX group and 6 (15.4%) in the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group experienced TEAEs leading to treatment discontinuation. TEAEs leading to death occurred in five patients (6.4%) receiving NALIRIFOX and two (5.1%) receiving gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel. Among these, three deaths in the NALIRIFOX group—two due to gastrointestinal bleeding and one of unknown cause—were deemed treatment-related.
Delayed diarrhea (onset ≥24 h after administration) occurred in 43 patients (55.1%) in the NALIRIFOX group, more frequently among those aged <65 years compared to ≥65 years (37.2% vs. 17.9%). The median duration of delayed diarrhea was 4 days (95% CI, 3–6).

## Pharmacogenomic and exploratory analyses
Among the 78 patients treated with liposomal irinotecan, eight carried homozygous or compound heterozygous UGT1A1 variants, 22 were single heterozygotes, and 35 were wild-type. The incidence of grade ≥3 neutropenia was highest in the homozygous/compound heterozygous group (62.5%) compared with the single heterozygous (45.5%) and wild-type groups (40.0%) (Fig.  ). Grade ≥3 diarrhea was observed only in the wild-type group (2.9%) (Fig.  ).
 Post hoc analysis suggested a trend toward greater survival benefits with NALIRIFOX in patients with baseline CA19-9 levels <37 U/mL across both PFS and OS endpoints, which may support further investigation into the potential role of CA19-9 as a prognostic or predictive marker in this population.

## Discussion
FOLFIRINOX and gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel are both recommended as first-line therapy for metastatic pancreatic adenocarcinoma . While some retrospective and real-world studies , , suggest that FOLFIRINOX may be superior or equivalent to gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel, a prospective head-to-head comparison trial—JCOG1611, GENERATE —demonstrated significantly better efficacy for gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel. Nonetheless, the clinical utility of FOLFIRINOX is often limited by its considerable toxicity profile, including grade 3–4 vomiting and diarrhea in 14.5% and 12.7% of patients, respectively . Consequently, gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel remains a commonly used regimen in clinical practice, particularly among patients less suited for intensive combination chemotherapy. In real-world settings, the administration schedule of gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel is frequently modified—from three times in four weeks to twice in four weeks—to improve tolerability , . Therefore, there is an urgent need for alternative regimens that offer enhanced efficacy with an improved safety profile.
Supported by the global phase III NAPOLI-3 trial, NALIRIFOX (liposomal irinotecan plus 5-FU, leucovorin, and oxaliplatin) demonstrated superior OS over gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel and subsequently received US FDA approval in February 2024 for the first-line treatment of metastatic pancreatic adenocarcinoma . It has since been incorporated into the 2024 CSCO guidelines as a Category 1 A Level II regimen and designated as a preferred Category 1 option for metastatic disease in the NCCN 2025 guidelines .
In this randomized phase II study, NALIRIFOX yielded clinically meaningful improvements in PFS compared with gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel in Chinese patients with metastatic pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, with consistent benefit observed across all prespecified subgroups. These findings are congruent with those of the NAPOLI-3 trial, which primarily enrolled patients of non-Asian descent . The underrepresentation of Asian patients in NAPOLI-3—only 5% of the total population—has limited the generalizability of its findings to Asian cohorts and is a primary reason why NALIRIFOX has remained a Level II recommendation in Chinese treatment guidelines. The present study directly addresses this gap, providing high-quality evidence from a Chinese population and supporting an upgrade in the recommendation level of NALIRIFOX in national guidelines.
The median PFS in our study for NALIRIFOX and gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel (7.6 and 3.7 months, respectively) was comparable to that reported in NAPOLI-3 (7.4 and 5.6 months, respectively) . Similarly, the median OS of 12.9 months for NALIRIFOX and 8.9 months for gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel in our cohort aligns with the NAPOLI-3 results (11.1 vs. 9.2 months). Notably, our population had a higher proportion of patients with ECOG PS 1—83% versus 57% in NAPOLI-3—which generally portends a worse prognosis. This difference may also explain the shorter PFS observed in the control arm of our study. In addition, subgroup analysis revealed a particularly pronounced PFS benefit in patients with liver metastases (7.4 vs. 3.7 months; HR 0.38), reinforcing the robustness of NALIRIFOX in high-risk subgroups.
The PFS and OS achieved with NALIRIFOX in this study were also consistent with those reported for FOLFIRINOX in the phase III PRODIGE 4/ACCORD trial (6.4 and 11.1 months, respectively) . Although NALIRIFOX and FOLFIRINOX have not been directly compared in randomized studies, a recent meta-analysis concluded that their efficacy is comparable . In our study, a subgroup analysis suggested that patients with baseline CA19-9 levels <37 U/mL may derive greater benefit from NALIRIFOX, indicating a potential role for CA19-9 as a predictive biomarker.
Importantly, NALIRIFOX demonstrated an acceptable safety profile in this Chinese cohort. Grade ≥3 TEAEs occurred in a numerically lower proportion of patients receiving NALIRIFOX compared to those receiving gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel (73.1% vs. 84.6%). The overall safety profile of NALIRIFOX was consistent with that observed in NAPOLI-3, with the most common adverse events being diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, and fatigue . While direct comparisons between NALIRIFOX and FOLFIRINOX are lacking, meta-analyses suggest that NALIRIFOX is associated with significantly lower rates of grade ≥3 hematologic toxicities and peripheral neuropathy . In our Chinese population, the incidence of grade ≥3 neutropenia and leukopenia (46.2% and 25.6%, respectively) was higher than in the predominantly white population of NAPOLI-3 (24% and <5%, respectively) . Although the incidence of reduced or deficient dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase (DPYD) activity is relatively low among Chinese patients and its correlation with 5-FU toxicities remains to be confirmed , this study did not mandate pre-therapeutic DPYD testing. However, individuals with known low or deficient DPYD activity were excluded. Nevertheless, these rates are comparable to those observed in the PRODIGE 4/ACCORD study with FOLFIRINOX (neutropenia: 45.7%) . Notably, an observational study of FOLFIRINOX in Japanese patients reported even higher incidence of grade ≥3 neutropenia (64%) and leukopenia (36%) , suggesting that Asian patients may be more susceptible to hematologic toxicities from irinotecan-based regimens. However, in our study, no prophylactic growth factor support was implemented; the introduction of such supportive care measures may mitigate hematologic toxicity.
Although the NALIRIFOX group was associated with a slightly higher incidence of TEAEs leading to treatment discontinuation (20.5% vs. 15.4%) or death (6.4% vs. 5.1%) compared to the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group, the mean relative dose intensities were comparable between the two regimens. A post hoc analysis of the NAPOLI 3 trial indicated that a considerable proportion of long-term survivors underwent dose reductions or treatment delays of liposomal irinotecan and/or oxaliplatin, yet maintained prolonged drug exposure and received high cumulative doses of both agents. Importantly, despite the modestly higher discontinuation rate, NALIRIFOX demonstrated superior efficacy outcomes, suggesting that treatment benefit was maintained.
Delayed diarrhea is a known adverse effect of irinotecan and typically occurs ≥24 h after administration. When irinotecan is combined with 5-FU/ leucovorin, the incidence of delayed diarrhea can reach 70%–80%. In our study, 55.1% of patients experienced delayed diarrhea, with a notably lower incidence among patients aged 65 years or older (17.9%), suggesting that NALIRIFOX may be more tolerable in older individuals.
Polymorphisms in UGT1A1 are known to influence the metabolism and toxicity profile of irinotecan , . In our study, grade ≥3 neutropenia occurred in 62.5% of patients with homozygous or compound heterozygous UGT1A1 variants, 45.5% of single heterozygotes, and 40.0% of wild-type individuals. This trend is consistent with prior reports, although a retrospective Japanese study of FOLFIRINOX reported even higher neutropenia rates in wild-type (80%) and heterozygous (89%) patients . As severe hematologic toxicity can negatively impact dose intensity and treatment compliance, UGT1A1 genotyping may help guide the selection of irinotecan-based regimens and support risk-adapted dosing. Notably, the small sample size in our genetic subanalysis warrants validation in a larger cohort.
The gastrointestinal safety profile of NALIRIFOX in this study was also favorable, with grade 3–4 vomiting and diarrhea each occurring in <5% of patients. These rates were lower than those observed in NAPOLI-3 and comparable to those reported in our prior bioequivalence study in Chinese patients and in the Asian subgroup of NAPOLI-1, where nearly one-third of patients were Asian . This suggests that regional differences in pharmacogenetics or drug metabolism may contribute to improved tolerability in Asian populations. Collectively, these findings support NALIRIFOX as a well-tolerated, effective first-line therapy for Chinese patients with advanced pancreatic cancer.
The main limitation of this study is its premature termination, which prevented us from reaching the predetermined sample size. This was predominantly attributable to disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic, which adversely affected both patient recruitment and protocol adherence. Consequently, the statistical power of our analyses was reduced, and the results should therefore be interpreted with appropriate caution. Additionally, no stratification factors were indicated in this study due to the limited sample size. However, based on baseline characteristics, the incidence of liver metastases was similar between the NALIRIFOX and gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel groups. In addition, the proportion of subjects with an ECOG score of 1 was slightly higher in the NALIRIFOX group compared to the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group. Despite this imbalance, the NALIRIFOX group still demonstrated significantly superior efficacy over the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group. Moreover, the 2:1 randomization ratio resulted in a relatively smaller control group, which may have limited the statistical power for between-group comparisons. While the use of a two-sided alpha level of 0.10 exceeds the conventional threshold, it is appropriate for proof-of-concept research aimed at evaluating the efficacy and safety of NALIRIFOX versus gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel in Chinese pancreatic cancer patients. Furthermore, the absence of BRCA status information means that whether the findings of this study can be applied to BRCA-mutated subjects needs to be further validated. Patient enrollment in this study was restricted to those aged <70 years, unlike the NAPOLI 3 trial, which had no upper age limit. Therefore, the generalizability of our findings to older populations warrants further investigation. Despite these limitations, the observed efficacy and safety outcomes were consistent with those reported in the global NAPOLI-3 trial, reinforcing the robustness of our findings.
In conclusion, this randomized phase II study demonstrated that NALIRIFOX provides meaningful clinical benefit and acceptable tolerability as a first-line treatment for Chinese patients with metastatic unresectable pancreatic adenocarcinoma. These findings support its adoption as a preferred therapeutic option and provide critical data to inform regional treatment guidelines.

## Methods

## Study design
This multicenter, randomized, open-label phase 2 study was conducted at 30 tertiary hospitals across China. Eligible patients were enrolled between November 3, 2021, and November 15, 2022. The study adhered to the principles outlined in the Declaration of Helsinki and the principles of good clinical practice, with ethical approval obtained from the review boards at each participating center (Tianjin Cancer Hospital, Changzhi People’s Hospital, Cangzhou Central Hospital, Hebei University Affiliated Hospital, Cancer Hospital Affiliated to Harbin Medical University, Jilin Provincial Cancer Hospital, The First Affiliated Hospital of China Medical University, Liaoning Provincial Cancer Hospital, The Fourth Hospital of Hebei Medical University, Jilin University Second Hospital, Fujian Provincial Cancer Hospital, Shandong Cancer Hospital, Jiangxi Provincial Cancer Hospital, The First Affiliated Hospital of Xinjiang Medical University, Huazhong University of Science and Technology Tongji Medical College Affiliated Union Hospital, Huazhong University of Science Tongji Hospital, Tongji Medical College, Jinan Central Hospital, Yunnan Provincial Cancer Hospital, The First Affiliated Hospital of Bengbu Medical College, Dongyang People’s Hospital, Hunan Provincial People’s Hospital, Hainan Third People’s Hospital, Tianjin Medical University General Hospital, Hubei Provincial Cancer Hospital, The First Affiliated Hospital of Chongqing Medical University, Shanxi Cancer Hospital, The Second Affiliated Hospital of Guangzhou Medical University, Sun Yat-sen Memorial Hospital of Sun Yat-sen University, Beijing University First Hospital, and Huashan Hospital Affiliated to Fudan University) (Supplemental Table  ) and registered at ClinicalTrial.gov (NCT05047991, date of registration: September 17 2021). The study protocol is available in the Supplementary Information.

## Participants
Adult patients aged 18 to 70 years with histologically or cytologically confirmed unresectable locally advanced or metastatic pancreatic adenocarcinoma and at least one measurable lesion as per the Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST) v1.1 were eligible for inclusion. Additional inclusion criteria included an ECOG PS of 0 or 1, expected survival ≥3 months, and adequate organ function. Patients had not received prior systemic therapy for pancreatic cancer. Those who had received neoadjuvant or adjuvant chemotherapy with local therapy were eligible if the time to progression was >6 months. Written informed consent was obtained from all participants prior to enrollment.
Exclusion criteria included acinar cell carcinoma, pancreatoblastoma, solid pseudopapillary tumors, pancreatic neuroendocrine tumors, confirmed central nervous system metastasis, or hepatic encephalopathy during screening. Additionally, subjects with known reduced or deficient dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase (DPYD) activity were also excluded.

## Randomization and masking
Eligible patients were randomized in a 2:1 ratio to receive either NALIRIFOX or gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel. Randomization was not stratified. Statisticians used SAS v9.4 to generate randomization tables with block randomization. An Interactive Web Response Randomization System (IWRS) was used to assign patients to their respective treatments. Treatment allocation was not blinded.

## Procedures
Patients in the NALIRIFOX group received liposomal irinotecan (50 mg/m²), 5-FU (2400 mg/m²), leucovorin (400 mg/m²), and oxaliplatin (60 mg/m²), delivered sequentially by intravenous infusion on days 1 and 15 of a 28-day cycle. The gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel group received gemcitabine (1000 mg/m²) and nab-paclitaxel (125 mg/m²) by intravenous infusion on days 1, 8, and 15 of a 28-day cycle. Treatment continued until disease progression, unacceptable toxicity, investigator or patient decision, or other reasons, whichever occurred first.
Tumor assessments were performed at baseline, approximately every 8 weeks (±3 days) during treatment and every 8 weeks (±3 days) after treatment discontinuation. Imaging during screening included contrast-enhanced CT of the neck, chest, abdomen, and pelvis, as well as whole-body bone emission computed tomography (ECT). Subsequent imaging evaluations were based on target and non-target lesions, consistent with the screening imaging protocol. CT or MRI confirmed any new lesions discovered by bone scan. For patients achieving a complete response (CR) or partial response (PR), confirmation was required 4 weeks (±3 days) later. A post-treatment follow-up was conducted 30 days after treatment discontinuation.
Blood samples were obtained for analysis of the UGT1A1 gene polymorphism (Sanger sequencing) before the first administration of NALIRIFOX.

## Endpoints and measurements
The primary endpoint was PFS, which was defined as the time from randomization to the earliest occurrence of either radiologically documented tumor progression (per RECIST 1.1) or death from any cause. If a patient had neither disease progression nor death by the data cutoff date or before initiating any subsequent anti-tumor therapy, PFS would be censored at the date of the last tumor assessment prior to the data-cutoff date or prior to the start of the subsequent anti-tumor therapy, whichever occurred first. If a patient experienced progression or death after missing two or more consecutive tumor assessments, PFS would be censored at the date of the last valid radiological tumor assessment prior to the missed assessments. Secondary efficacy endpoints included OS (defined as the time from the start of the study drug to death from any cause), ORR (defined as the proportion of subjects with a best overall response of complete response or partial response), disease control rate (DCR, defined as the proportion of subjects with a best overall response of complete response, partial response, and stable disease), and DoR (defined as the time from the start of the first tumor assessment of complete response or partial response to the first assessment of disease progression or death from any cause). Tumor response was evaluated using RECIST v1.1 criteria. Additional secondary endpoints included safety, pharmacokinetic parameters, and evaluation of the relationship between UGT1A1*6 and UGT1A1*28 polymorphisms and safety. Adverse events (AEs) were classified using the Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities (MedDRA) version 25.1 and graded according to the Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE) version 5.0.
Exploratory analyses aimed to investigate correlations between UGT1A1 genotypes and the incidence of grade ≥3 diarrhea and neutropenia in patients receiving liposomal irinotecan therapy, as well as the association of efficacy with CA19-9 levels.

## Statistical analysis
This trial was designed as a randomized, open-label phase II study to evaluate the PFS benefit of NALIRIFOX compared with gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel in Chinese patients with advanced pancreatic adenocarcinoma. Based on prior data from the MPACT trial and real-world studies, the median PFS for the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel regimen was assumed to be approximately 5 months . To detect an HR of 0.63—corresponding to a median PFS improvement from 5 months to 7.9 months—with 80% power and a two-sided alpha of 0.10, a total of 125 PFS events were required. To account for patient attrition and ensure a sufficient number of PFS events, the total planned sample size was set at 153 patients, assuming an accrual duration of 12 months and 125 PFS events expected within 24 months. The 2:1 randomization ratio was chosen to provide more information on the safety and pharmacogenomic profile of the investigational regimen.
Time-to-event endpoints, including PFS, OS, and DoR, were analyzed using the Kaplan–Meier method. The 95% CIs for median time-to-event outcomes were calculated using the Brookmeyer–Crowley method. Between-group comparisons of PFS and OS were performed using two-sided log-rank tests, and HRs and associated 95% CIs were estimated with Cox proportional hazards models. The ORR and disease control rate (DCR) were estimated using the Clopper–Pearson exact method.
All efficacy analyses were performed in the intention-to-treat (ITT) population, defined as all randomized patients, regardless of whether they received study treatment. Safety analyses were conducted in all patients who received at least one dose of study medication. Pharmacokinetic and pharmacogenomic analyses were conducted in all patients who received liposomal irinotecan and had valid plasma samples or UGT1A1 genotype data, respectively.
Patients who were alive without documented progression at the time of data cutoff were censored as of the date of their last radiographic assessment. Similarly, patients lost to follow-up were censored at the date of last contact. Missing data for binary endpoints (e.g., response status) were not imputed; patients without post-baseline tumor assessments were considered non-responders in the response analyses. Sensitivity analyses were conducted to assess the robustness of primary endpoint results under various censoring scenarios.
Subgroup analyses were conducted using multivariable Cox models to explore the interaction across prespecified subgroups, including age (<65 vs.≥65 years), sex, ECOG performance status (0 vs. 1), number of metastatic sites, and liver metastases. Additionally, baseline CA19-9 level (<37 vs. ≥37 U/mL) was analyzed as a post hoc subgroup. A chi-square test was used for comparison of the incidence of grade ≥ 3 neutropenia/diarrhea in patients with three UGT1A1 genotype status.
All analyses were performed using SAS version 9.4 (SAS Institute, Cary, NC). All statistical tests were two-sided, with p-values < 0.05 considered nominally significant. As this was an exploratory phase II study, adjustments for multiplicity were not prespecified.
This study was registered at ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT05047991).
