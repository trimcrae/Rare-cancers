## Background
Cognitive impairment affects prognosis and quality of life in patients with trigeminal neuralgia (TN). Modulating hippocampal microglial activation is a promising approach for addressing TN-related cognitive deficits. Nuclear receptor subfamily 4 group A member 1 (Nr4a1) is involved in hippocampal-dependent cognition and microglial homeostasis. However, its role in trigeminal neuralgia-associated cognitive dysfunction remains unclear. We aimed to investigate the role of Nr4a1 in regulating neuroinflammation mediated by microglial hyperactivation, and to establish the relationship between the resulting synaptic plasticity impairment and cognitive dysfunction.

## Methods
A TN rat model was established using chronic constriction injury of the distal infraorbital nerve. Cognitive function and hippocampal microglial activation were evaluated over time. Transcriptomic sequencing was performed to identify differentially expressed genes related to microglial activation. Nr4a1 was overexpressed in hippocampal microglia using adeno-associated virus and lentivirus in TN rats and BV2 cells, respectively. Multi-omics analyses, co-immunoprecipitation, and mass spectrometry were used for mechanistic assessments.

## Results
Here, we observed that TN rats exhibited time-dependent cognitive impairment accompanied by excessive activation of hippocampal microglia and significant downregulation of Nr4a1 expression. Nr4a1 overexpression suppresses excessive activation of microglia and the neuroinflammation they mediate. These effects correlate with restored neuronal autophagy, enhanced synaptic plasticity, and improved cognitive function in TN rats. Mechanistically, we first revealed aberrant alterations in the phosphoinositide 3-kinase/protein kinase B (PI3K/Akt) signaling pathway through multi-omics sequencing. Furthermore, we identified Nr4a1 primarily targeting Akt2 among the Akt isoforms in the rat hippocampus. Restoring Nr4a1 expression reversed PI3K/Akt2 signaling downregulation in vivo and in vitro, whereas PI3K/Akt2 signaling pharmacological inhibition abrogated Nr4a1 overexpression-mediated benefits.

## Conclusion
Collectively, Nr4a1 may suppress excessive microglial activation by restoring the PI3K/Akt2 signaling pathway to alleviate neuroinflammation, thereby improving neuronal autophagy and synaptic plasticity. Our findings provide a deeper theoretical basis and molecular target for future TN-related cognitive impairment treatment.

## Background
Trigeminal neuralgia (TN) is a chronic neuropathic facial pain disorder. It is defined by paroxysmal attacks of electric shock-like or stabbing pain, which occur spontaneously or are triggered in the region supplied by one or more divisions of the trigeminal nerve [ ]. Beyond pain, TN adversely affects multiple cognitive domains, including attention, working memory, executive function, and information processing speed [ , ], causing reduced treatment adherence, diminished quality of life, and poor overall health outcomes. Therefore, identifying potential therapeutic targets and interventions for TN-related cognitive impairment is crucial. Chronic neuropathic pain, including trigeminal neuropathic pain, negatively affects the hippocampus and its distinct subregions, which are critically involved in learning, memory, and neural plasticity [ , ]. Compared with healthy individuals, patients with TN exhibit reduced bilateral hippocampal volume and structural abnormalities across the hippocampal subfields [ , ]. However, the mechanisms underlying hippocampal-dependent cognitive dysfunction in TN remain poorly understood.
Neuroinflammation has been implicated in TN progression [ , ]. Synaptic plasticity, which underlies cognitive functions, encodes declarative and spatial memory within the hippocampal synapses [ , ]. Moreover, neuroinflammation impairs hippocampal synaptic plasticity by altering long-term potentiation (LTP), reducing dendritic complexity, and decreasing spine density [ , ]. Microglia play a central role in regulating neuroinflammation. Dysregulated microglial activation drives chronic neuroinflammation [ , ]. During homeostasis, microglia secrete cytokines at physiological levels that promote synaptic plasticity [ ]. However, persistent activation triggers the excessive release of pro-inflammatory factors, including interleukin-1β (IL-1β), IL-6, and tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α), which exacerbate neuroinflammation [ , ]. In TN models, hippocampal microglia are substantially activated, releasing abundant pro-inflammatory cytokines [ , ]. Inhibiting microglial activation with minocycline improved cognitive impairment in rats with TN [ ]. Although the association between hippocampal microglial activation, neuroinflammation, and cognitive impairment in TN is established [ – ], the underlying mechanisms remain unclear. Elucidating the targets modulating microglial activation in TN is essential for developing targeted therapeutic strategies.
Nuclear receptor subfamily 4 group A member 1 (Nr4a1, also known as NGFI-B or Nur77) belongs to a gene family that encodes orphan nuclear receptors (Nr4a1, Nr4a2, and Nr4a3) and participates in processes such as inflammation, apoptosis, autophagy, and metabolism. Nr4a1 plays a significant role in cognitive functions, including learning and memory [ , ]. In humans with cognitive deficits, NR4A1 mRNA expression in peripheral blood mononuclear cells declines with age [ ]. Additionally, Nr4a1 is notably upregulated in the hippocampus within minutes after learning [ ]. Conversely, reduced hippocampal Nr4a1 expression associated with cognitive impairment has been observed in several neuropsychiatric disorders [ , ]. These findings highlight the pivotal role of Nr4a1 in hippocampal-dependent cognition. However, its role in cognitive dysfunction associated with TN remains underexplored. Nr4a1 also regulates microglial activity; our sequencing data revealed significant Nr4a1 downregulation in the hippocampus of a TN model. Nr4a1 maintains microglial homeostasis, and its deficiency promotes a pro-inflammatory state that exacerbates inflammatory responses [ – ]. Conversely, Nr4a1 overexpression or agonist treatment suppresses microglial activation and excessive release of pro-inflammatory factors [ – ]. Although Nr4a1 is also expressed in neurons, most studies of its cognitive effects have been focused on neuronal mechanisms, whereas its role in regulating cognition via microglial activation has been largely overlooked.
In this study, we systematically analyzed the dynamic changes in cognitive function and hippocampal microglia in a rat model of trigeminal neuropathic pain. We further investigated the role of Nr4a1 in regulating neuroinflammation mediated by microglial hyperactivation and we established the link between resulting synaptic plasticity impairment and cognitive dysfunction. Chronic constriction injury of the distal infraorbital nerve (dION-CCI) model, although reliably inducing sustained mechanical allodynia and serving as a valuable tool for studying chronic trigeminal neuropathic pain [ ], it does not fully replicate hallmark clinical features of classical TN such as paroxysmal pain and neurovascular compression. Therefore, in this study, we aimed to elucidate the role of Nr4a1-mediated hippocampal microglial regulation in TN-associated cognitive impairment within the chronic pain state induced by the dION-CCI model, thereby facilitating the development of Nr4a1-based diagnostic and therapeutic strategies.

## Methods

## Animals and ethics statement
Male Sprague–Dawley rats (4–5 weeks old) were obtained from the Experimental Animal Center of Ningxia Medical University. Animals were housed under standard conditions (12 h light/dark cycle; 45–65% humidity; 22–25 °C) with ad libitum access to food and water. All procedures followed the National Institutes of Health Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals (NIH Publication No. 8023, revised 1978) and were approved by the Ethics Committee of Ningxia Medical University (approval No. KYLL-2025-0230). This study was planned and is reported following the ARRIVE 2.0 guidelines for reporting animal research. Animals were randomly assigned using a random number table. Only male rats were included in this study to minimize sex-related variations. Juvenile rats (4–5 weeks old at procurement) were used to align with the standard timeline for the dION-CCI model and to complete necessary pre-conditioning and stereotaxic surgeries prior to pain induction.

## Modeling
A TN model was established via dION-CCI as previously described [ ]. Briefly, rats were anesthetized with 1% sodium pentobarbital (0.1 g/kg, ip) (P3761; Sigma-Aldrich), and an incision (0.5 cm) was made along the upper edge of the right vibrissal pad to expose the infraorbital nerve. The nerve was ligated twice using 4 − 0 polyglycolic acid sutures at intervals of approximately 2 mm. The incision was sterilized and sutured after ligation. Baseline measurements were obtained before surgery.

## Experimental animal groups and drug treatment
Three sequential animal experiments were performed to assess phenotypic progression, therapeutic intervention, and mechanistic pathways (see Figure for the overall design). Group sizes were determined based on effect sizes from pilot experiments and are consistent with previous studies employing similar models and endpoints [ , , ]. A formal power analysis was precluded for the multi-omics exploratory phase.

## Experiment 1 (Phenotypic time course)
Rats were randomized into Sham or dION-CCI groups and were analyzed at multiple time points post-surgery. Detailed group sizes, subgroup allocations for multi-modal analyses, and the behavioral testing timeline are provided in Supplementary Notes .

## Experiment 2 (Nr4a1 intervention)
Rats received stereotactic hippocampal injections of either control or Nr4a1-overexpressing adeno-associated viruses (AAV), generating four experimental groups (Sham or dION-CCI, each with AAV⁻ or AAV⁺). Full experimental details are in Supplementary Notes .

## Experiment 3 (Pathway inhibition)
To pharmacologically block the PI3K/Akt2 pathway, dION-CCI + AAV⁺ rats were administered the PI3K inhibitor LY294002 (M1925; AbMole, Shanghai, China) or the Akt2 inhibitor MK-2206 (M1837; AbMole). The distinct treatment regimens (dose and duration) for each inhibitor were selected based on prior studies demonstrating their efficacy [ – ] and were confirmed to effectively suppress the target pathway in our TN model during pilot testing. Complete preparation and administration details are provided in Supplementary Notes .

## Behavioral tests
Behavioral tests were conducted in rats sequentially as follows: Mechanical pain threshold detection, Open field test (OFT), Novel object recognition test (NORT), and Morris water maze test (MWMT). The detailed methods are described in the Supplementary Notes . Rats were acclimated to the testing room for 30 min before each experiment. All behavioral tests were conducted between 7:00 AM and 7:00 PM under controlled light and noise conditions. Tests were performed in order of invasiveness, from least to most invasive. The apparatus was cleaned with 75% ethanol between trials. Behavioral data were analyzed using Smart 3.0 (Panlab, S.L.U., Barcelona, Spain) or manually scored by a blinded experimenter.

## Western blotting
Western blotting was performed as previously described [ ]. Total protein was extracted from hippocampal tissues or cultured cells using radioimmunoprecipitation assay lysis buffer (KGB5303; Keygentec, Nanjing, China) supplemented with protease inhibitors, and protein concentration was determined using a bicinchoninic acid protein assay kit (KGB2101; Keygentec). Equal amounts of protein (30 µg per sample) were separated using sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis and transferred onto polyvinylidene fluoride membranes (0.22 μm; Immobilon-P, Millipore). Membranes were blocked with 5% skim milk for 2 h at room temperature and incubated overnight at 4 °C with primary antibodies. Detailed information on primary antibodies is listed in Supplementary Notes . Subsequently, membranes were incubated with horseradish peroxidase-conjugated secondary antibodies (goat anti-mouse IgG, 1:5000, A21020, Abbkine, Wuhan, China; goat anti-rabbit IgG, 1:5000, A21040, Abbkine) for 1 h at room temperature. Protein bands were visualized using an enhanced chemiluminescence (ECL) detection system and imaged with a chemiluminescence imaging system (Amersham Imager 600; GE Healthcare). Band intensities were quantified using ImageJ (National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, MD, USA). Uncropped blot images are provided in Supplementary Document .

## Real-time quantitative polymerase chain reaction
Total RNA was extracted using TRIzol reagent (15596026; Invitrogen, CA, USA). cDNA was synthesized from mRNA using a reverse transcription kit (R412; Vazyme Biotech, Nanjing, China). Real-time quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) was performed using the SYBR Green reagent (11201ES03; Yeasen Biotech, Shanghai, China). Gene expression levels were quantified using the 2−ΔΔCt method. The primer sequences used are listed in Supplementary Table .

## Immunofluorescence staining
Immunofluorescence was performed on tissue sections and BV2 and HT22 cells, following established methods [ ]. The rats were deeply anesthetized and transcardially perfused with ice-cold saline, followed by 4% paraformaldehyde (P0099; Beyotime, Shanghai, China). The brains were collected, postfixed in 4% paraformaldehyde, and sequentially dehydrated in 15% and 30% sucrose solutions. Coronary brain sections (thickness, 10 µm) were prepared using a cryostat (CM1950; Leica, Wetzlar, Germany). Sections were blocked using a solution containing 10% donkey serum and 0.3% Triton X-100 in phosphate-buffered saline for 1 h at room temperature and incubated overnight at 4 °C with the following primary antibodies: Iba1 (1:300; ab178846, Abcam), Iba1 (1:300; ab283319, Abcam), GFAP (1:300; ab7260, Abcam), NeuN (1:300; ab177487, Abcam), and Nr4a1 (1:100; sc-365113, Santa Cruz). After washing the sections thrice, they were incubated for 1 h in the dark at room temperature with Alexa Fluor® 488 goat anti-rabbit IgG (1:200; ab150077, Abcam) or Alexa Fluor® 594 goat anti-mouse IgG (1:200; ab150116, Abcam). Nuclei were counterstained with 4’,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (P0131; Beyotime). BV2 and HT22 cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde and permeabilized with 0.5% Triton X-100 (P0096; Beyotime). Cells were blocked with 2% bovine serum albumin and incubated overnight at 4 °C with primary antibodies against Iba1 (1:300; ab283319, Abcam), p-Akt2 (1:200; 8599, Cell Signaling Technology), or Postsynaptic density protein 95 (PSD95) (1:200; 3409, Cell Signaling Technology). Subsequently, cells were incubated in the dark for 1 h with fluorescent secondary antibodies Alexa Fluor® 555 goat anti-mouse IgG (1:200; ab150118, Abcam) or Alexa Fluor® 647 goat anti-rabbit IgG (1:200; ab150079, Abcam), followed by staining with 4’,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole. Brain and cell sections were imaged using a confocal (LSM980; Carl Zeiss, Germany), an automated fluorescence (FV4000; Olympus, Japan), or an inverted fluorescence (IX83; Olympus, Japan) microscope. Image analysis was performed using ImageJ. To ensure objectivity, cell counting and quantitative analyses were conducted in a blinded manner, with slides coded and analyzed by independent investigators blinded to the experimental conditions and treatments.

## Transmission electron microscopy
Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was performed as previously described [ ]. Fresh hippocampal tissue was fixed in 2.5% glutaraldehyde and postfixed in 1% osmium tetroxide. After ethanol dehydration, samples were embedded in epoxy resin and polymerized at 60 °C. Ultrathin sections were prepared and stained with uranyl acetate and lead citrate. Images were acquired using a Hitachi HT7700 TEM microscope (Tokyo, Japan). The postsynaptic density (PSD) thickness was measured using ImageJ. Two blinded investigators performed measurements to ensure reproducibility.

## Golgi staining
Golgi staining was performed to visualize dendritic spines using the FD Rapid GolgiStain Kit (PK401, FD NeuroTechnologies, Columbia, MD, USA) following the manufacturer’s protocol [ ]. Briefly, freshly dissected brain tissue was rinsed in ddH₂O and impregnated in a 1:1 mixture of Solution A and B for 2 weeks at room temperature in darkness. Subsequently, the tissue was transferred to Solution C and immersed for ≥ 3 days. Coronary sections (150 μm) were cut using a Leica CM1950 cryostat, mounted on gelatin-coated slides, and stained. Hippocampal dendritic spines were imaged using an Olympus VS200 slide scanner and analyzed using ImageJ.

## RNA sequencing and bioinformatic analysis
Total RNA was extracted from rat hippocampal tissues using TRIzol reagent (15596018; Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA), as previously described [ ]. RNA sequencing was performed using an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform by LC-Bio Technologies Co., Ltd. (Hangzhou, China). Sequencing data were aligned to the reference genome using the HISAT2 aligner. Raw count and fragments per kilobase of transcript per million mapped fragments (FPKM) values for each gene were quantified using high-throughput sequence read count. Genes were considered significantly differentially expressed based on the following criteria: Benjamini–Hochberg-adjusted p-value (FDR) ≤ 0.05 and |log₂(fold change)|≥ 1. Functional enrichment analyses were conducted using Gene Ontology, Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG), and gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA).

## Metabolomics analysis
Metabolomic analysis was performed as previously described [ ]. Approximately 50 ± 5 mg of rat hippocampal tissue was transferred to a 2.0-mL EP tube containing 500 µL of 80% ice-cold methanol solution and stainless-steel beads for homogenization. Samples were incubated at − 20 °C for 30 min to precipitate proteins and centrifuged at 20,000×g for 15 min. The supernatant was transferred to an injection vial, and a quality control sample was prepared by pooling 15 µL of extract from each sample. Analysis was conducted on a UPLC-HRMS system comprising an UltiMate 3000 UPLC system (Thermo Scientific) with an ACQUITY UPLC T3 column (100 × 2.1 mm, 1.8 μm; Waters, UK) coupled to a TripleTOF® 6600 high-resolution mass spectrometer (SCIEX, Framingham, MA, USA). Each sample was analyzed in positive- and negative-ion modes. Data processing was performed by LC-Bio Technologies Co., Ltd. Metabolite identification was based on the KEGG, Human Metabolome Database, and LipidMAPS databases. Sample quality was assessed using partial least squares-discriminant analysis and Spearman correlation, and differences in metabolite levels were determined using t-tests.

## Protein–protein interaction studies
Mass spectrometry (MS) and bioinformatic analyses were performed as previously described [ ]. Rat hippocampal tissues were collected, and co-immunoprecipitation (Co-IP) was conducted using a commercial kit (88804; Thermo Fisher Scientific) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Immunoprecipitates were separated using sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis, and gels were silver-stained (G7210; Solarbio, Beijing, China). Protein bands of interest were excised, digested with trypsin, and resuspended in 1% formic acid for liquid chromatography–MS. Peptide separation was performed on a nanoACQUITY UPLC system (Waters Corporation, Milford, MA, USA) coupled to a Q Exactive Hybrid Quadrupole-Orbitrap mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). MS/MS spectra were processed with Peaks Studio version 8.5 (Bioinfor Inc., CA, USA) and searched against the Rattus database (UniProtKB/Swiss-Prot) for Nr4a1.

## Molecular Docking
Molecular docking was performed as previously described [ ]. The NR4A1 protein structure was obtained from UniProt, and predicted AKT1, AKT2, and AKT3 structures were retrieved from the AlphaFold database. Molecular structures, including dehydration and hydrogenation, were prepared using AutoDockTools-1.5.7. Docking between Nr4a1 and AKT isoforms was performed using GRAMM-X. Resulting complexes were optimized using AutoDockTools-1.5.7. Protein–protein interactions were predicted and visualized using PyMOL v2.4.

## Stereotaxic injection of AAV
AAV vectors for Nr4a1 overexpression (HBAAV2/9-CX3CR1-R-Nr4a1-3flag-ZsGreen; 1.8 × 10¹² vg/mL) and empty vector control (HBAAV2/9-CX3CR1-ZsGreen; 1.9 × 10¹² vg/mL) were obtained from HanBio (Hanheng Biotechnology Co., Ltd., Shanghai, China). Rats were anesthetized and secured in a stereotaxic apparatus (RWD Life Science, China) [ ]. A burr hole was drilled through the skull, and 1 µL of AAV solution was slowly injected into the hippocampus using a NanoFil needle, NanoFil syringe, and microinjection pump controller (World Precision Instruments, Sarasota, FL, USA). The stereotaxic coordinates for the hippocampus were as follows: anteroposterior coordinate, -3.6 mm; mediolateral coordinate, ± 2.2 mm; and dorsoventral coordinate, -3 mm, with an infusion rate of 0.1 µL/min. The needle was left in place for 10 min before withdrawal, after which the incision was sutured. Rats recovered on a heating pad and were returned to cages for 1 week before experiments.

## Cell culture
BV2 murine microglial and HT22 neuronal cell lines were obtained from Baidi Technology (Hangzhou, China). Cells were cultured in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (Gibco) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (Gibco), 25 mM glucose, and antibiotics and maintained at 37 °C in a humidified incubator with 20% O₂ and 5% CO₂.

## Cell viability
Cell viability was assessed using the Cell Counting Kit-8 assay (CCK-8, HY-K0301, MCE, Shanghai, China). Cytotoxicity in BV2 cells was evaluated after exposure to various concentrations of lipopolysaccharides (LPS) (0, 0.1, 0.5, 1, 2, or 5 µg/mL), LY294002 (0, 5, 10, 15, 30, or 60 µM), and MK-2206 (0, 0.1, 0.5, 1, 2, or 5 µM). After treatment, CCK-8 working solution was added to the cells, followed by incubation for 1 h at 37 °C. Absorbance was measured at 450 nm using a microplate reader (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA).

## Flow cytometry
Flow cytometry was performed to assess CD86 and CD206 expression in microglial cells. Cells were resuspended in flow staining buffer containing 5% fetal bovine serum and incubated with Fc block (anti-CD16/32 antibody; 214228, BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA, USA) for 15 min at room temperature in the dark. Subsequently, 100 µL of the cell suspension was labeled with PE-conjugated anti-mouse CD86 and FITC-conjugated anti-mouse CD206 antibodies (K009926M, K015735M; Solarbio) for 30 min. Isotype-matched antibodies served as controls. Samples were analyzed on a CytoFLEX flow cytometer (Beckman Coulter, Indianapolis, IN, USA). Single-stain controls were used for fluorescence compensation before acquisition.

## Lentivirus infection
To achieve stable Nr4a1 expression, BV2 cells were transfected with LV-Nr4a1 or an empty lentiviral control vector, which was designed and synthesized by HanBio Co., Ltd. Briefly, 1 day before lentiviral infection, BV2 cells were seeded in six-well plates at a density of 1 × 10⁵ cells per well. Subsequently, cells were transfected with the lentiviral vectors at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 50, based on experimental requirements and the manufacturer’s instructions. After 4 h, the medium was replaced with fresh Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium, and cells were further cultured for 72 h. Stable Nr4a1-overexpressing cell lines were selected using 4 µg/mL puromycin (HB-PU-500; HanBio Co., Ltd.), and overexpression efficiency was confirmed through western blotting.

## Cell grouping and drug intervention
Figure shows a flowchart of cell experiments. To assess the role of Nr4a1, cells were assigned to four groups: Control, LPS (1 µg/mL, 055:B5; Sigma-Aldrich), LPS + Vec (transfected with empty vector + LPS), and LPS+Nr4a1OE (Nr4a1-overexpressing + LPS), treated for 24 h [ ].
To investigate the involvement of the PI3K/Akt2 pathway, five groups were used: Control, LPS, LPS+Nr4a1OE, LPS+Nr4a1OE+LY294002 (10 µM, M1925; AbMole; Shanghai, China), and LPS+Nr4a1OE+MK-2206 (0.5 µM, M1837; AbMole). Inhibitors were dissolved in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), with controls receiving equivalent DMSO (< 0.2%) [ , ]. Conditioned media from BV2 cells were collected and applied to HT22 neuronal cells for 12 h to assess downstream effects on autophagy and synaptic plasticity.
Detailed protocols for cell culture, transfection, treatment timelines, and media transfer are provided in Supplementary Notes .

## Statistical analysis
Data were analyzed using GraphPad Prism 10 (La Jolla, CA, USA) and are expressed as mean±standard deviation. Comparisons between two groups with normally distributed data were conducted using the unpaired Student’s t-test. Multiple group comparisons were performed using a one-way or two-way analysis of variance with repeated measures, followed by Tukey’s post hoc test. Non-parametric tests were applied when normality or variance assumptions were not met. A p-value of < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

## Results

## TN rats exhibited time-dependent cognitive impairment and hippocampal microglial overactivation
The experimental timeline is depicted in Fig.  a. dION-CCI surgery successfully induced persistent trigeminal neuropathic pain, as indicated by a sustained reduction in mechanical pain threshold from days 3 to 28 post-operation compared to Sham controls (Fig.  b). Cognitive function was assessed longitudinally. Critically, dION-CCI rats developed significant hippocampal-dependent cognitive deficits by day 28. This was demonstrated by impaired performance in the NORT (reduced discrimination index; Fig.  l, n) and the MWMT (prolonged escape latency, fewer platform crossings, and less time in the target quadrant; Fig.  o-r). Notably, the observed cognitive impairment was not confounded by motor dysfunction, as locomotor activity remained comparable across groups (see Supplementary Notes for comprehensive analysis).

 

In parallel, hippocampal microglia in dION-CCI rats underwent progressive activation over time. Immunofluorescence analysis revealed a time-dependent increase in Iba1⁺ cells with a shift toward an amoeboid, pro-inflammatory morphology (Figure a, b). Consistent with this, Western blotting showed a parallel increase in protein markers associated with microglial activation, with peak levels at day 28 (Figure c-f). See Supplementary Notes for the expanded background on microglial activation markers.
The co-occurrence of peak cognitive impairment and peak microglial activation at the 28-day time point established its relevance for subsequent mechanistic investigations. These results suggest that hippocampal microglial overactivation is a temporally aligned pathological feature that may contribute to cognitive dysfunction in this model of chronic trigeminal neuropathic pain.

## Microglial overactivation-mediated neuroinflammation causes dysregulated autophagy and impaired synaptic plasticity in the hippocampus of TN rats
GSEA of hippocampal transcriptomic data revealed significant enrichment of inflammation-related genes (Figure g). Western blotting further confirmed elevated levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-1β, TNF-α) and decreased anti-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-4, IL-10) in the dION-CCI group compared to the Sham group (Figure h-m). Hippocampal metabolomics further revealed significant alterations, with pathway enrichment analysis highlighting autophagy, phosphatidylinositol signaling, and LTP as key disturbed processes (see Supplementary Notes for detailed analysis) (Fig. a-d). Pro-inflammatory cytokines such as TNF-α, IL-1α, and IL-1β can induce autophagy and disrupt autophagy [ ]. During autophagy, the cytosolic form of microtubule-associated protein 1 A/1B-light chain 3 (LC3-I) is lipidated to form LC3-II [ ]. The adaptor protein p62 (SQSTM1) binds cargo, and LC3 is degraded by autolysosomes [ ]. Under pathological hyperactivation, LC3-II accumulates, whereas p62 decreases [ – ]. Western blotting showed increased LC3-II levels and decreased p62 expression in the dION-CCI group compared with the Sham group (Fig. e-g). Using TEM, the dION-CCI group showed increased autophagosome numbers compared with the Sham group. Additionally, we observed dilated endoplasmic reticulum cisternae and increased lysosome numbers (Fig. h).

 

Excessive autophagy compromises synaptic structure and function, impairing synaptic plasticity [ , ]. GSEA of hippocampal transcriptomic data confirmed enrichment of genes related to synaptic plasticity and cognition (Fig. i). Western blotting revealed significant downregulation of PSD95 and synaptophysin (SYN)—crucial markers of postsynaptic and presynaptic membranes, respectively—in the dION-CCI group (Fig. j-l) [ , ]. TEM showed blurred or absent synaptic clefts and reduced PSD thickness in TN rats, in contrast to the clear synaptic clefts observed in Sham rats, indicating altered synaptic ultrastructure (Fig. m-n).
Our results revealed that hippocampal microglial overactivation in TN rats drives neuroinflammation, which in turn induces dysregulated autophagy and ultimately leads to structural and functional impairment of synapses.

## Nr4a1 is a key gene that regulates microglial activation during cognitive impairment in TN rats
Transcriptomic sequencing identified 660 differentially expressed genes in the hippocampus (159 upregulated and 501 downregulated) between the Sham and dION-CCI groups (Fig.  a). Nr4a1 was among the most significantly downregulated genes associated with cognitive function (Fig.  b-c). RT-qPCR and western blotting of hippocampal tissue revealed significant downregulation of Nr4a1 expression at the mRNA and protein levels in the dION-CCI group compared with those of the Sham group (Fig.  d-e). Immunofluorescence revealed decreased Nr4a1 levels in the Cornu Ammonis area 1 (CA1), CA3, and dentate gyrus (DG) of the hippocampus in the dION-CCI group (Fig.  f-g). Double immunofluorescence labeling further revealed that this reduction occurred in microglia and neurons but not in astrocytes (Figure a-f).

 

We further injected an AAV carrying Nr4a1 under the microglia-specific CX3CR1 promoter (AAV⁺) into the hippocampus to achieve cell-type-specific Nr4a1 overexpression. The dION-CCI + AAV⁻ and dION-CCI + AAV⁺ groups exhibited significantly lower pain thresholds than the Sham group on days 3, 7, 14, 21, and 28 after modeling, confirming successful establishment of the TN model. However, on days 14, 21, and 28, the dION-CCI + AAV⁺ group exhibited higher pain thresholds than the dION-CCI + AAV⁻ group, potentially owing to reduced neuroinflammation in the brain (Fig.  i). Efficient Nr4a1 overexpression in the hippocampus was confirmed through immunofluorescence staining and western blotting 5 weeks after viral injection (Fig.  k and Figure b). Moreover, double immunofluorescence labeling showed that ZsGreen fluorescence was detected only in microglia, but not in astrocytes or neurons, indicating successful and specific Nr4a1 overexpression in the hippocampal microglia of TN rats (Figure a).

## Microglia-specific overexpression of Nr4a1 alleviates hippocampal microglial overactivation and its mediated neuroinflammation in TN rats
To determine whether Nr4a1 is a crucial regulator of hippocampal microglial overactivation in TN rats, we conducted in vivo and in vitro studies. Because microglial overactivation is typically accompanied by morphological transformations [ ], we applied a three-dimensional morphological quantification approach to precisely assess microglial states. Immunofluorescence revealed that compared with the Sham + AAV⁻ group, the dION-CCI + AAV⁻ group exhibited a significant increase in Iba1⁺ microglia with amoeboid morphology, reduced terminal endpoint voxels, and shortened total branch length in the CA1 (Fig. a-d), CA3, and DG (Figure a-h) of the hippocampus. Notably, microglial-specific Nr4a1 overexpression mitigated these changes (Fig. a-d and Figure S6a-h). Western blotting of Iba1 confirmed these observations (Fig. k). Furthermore, compared with the dION-CCI + AAV⁻ group, the dION-CCI + AAV⁺ group displayed reduced expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β) and increased levels of anti-inflammatory cytokines (IL-4 and IL-10) (Fig. e-j).

 

We confirmed this regulatory role of Nr4a1 in vitro using LPS-stimulated BV2 microglial cells [ ]. Stable Nr4a1-overexpressing cells were established and subjected to an inflammatory challenge (see Supplementary Notes for construction details). Mirroring the in vivo findings, Nr4a1 overexpression mitigated the LPS-induced pro-inflammatory morphological transformation and corrected the imbalance between pro- and anti-inflammatory cytokines (Figure g-m).
Collectively, these complementary in vivo and in vitro results demonstrate that Nr4a1 functions as a crucial regulator in microglia, restraining their overactivation and subsequent neuroinflammatory response in the context of trigeminal neuropathic pain.

## Microglial overexpression of Nr4a1 improves neuronal autophagy, synaptic plasticity, and cognitive function in TN rats
We further investigated the effects of restoring Nr4a1 expression on hippocampal neuronal autophagy and synaptic plasticity. First, TEM observation of hippocampal neurons revealed that compared with the dION-CCI + AAV⁻ group, the dION-CCI + AAV⁺ group showed reduced autophagosomes and lysosomes, along with ameliorated dilation of endoplasmic reticulum cisternae (Fig. a). Furthermore, western blotting indicated that Nr4a1 overexpression decreased LC3-II levels and increased p62 expression in TN rats (Fig. b-d). The brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF)/tropomyosin receptor kinase B (TrkB) signaling pathway enhances the expression of SYN and PSD95, promoting synapse formation, growth, and maintenance [ ]. Western blotting revealed that compared with the dION-CCI + AAV⁻ group, the dION-CCI + AAV⁺ group exhibited increased expression of synaptic plasticity-related proteins, including BDNF, TrkB, SYN, and PSD95 (Fig. e-j). TEM results showed that Nr4a1 overexpression restored synaptic cleft integrity and increased PSD thickness in the hippocampus of TN rats (Figure a-d). Furthermore, we performed Golgi staining to analyze the number and morphology of dendritic spines in the hippocampus. Compared with the Sham + AAV⁻ group, the dION-CCI + AAV⁻ group exhibited a decrease in spine number across hippocampal subregions, decreased density of mature spines (mushroom and stubby types), and increased density of immature spines (filopodia and thin spines). However, Nr4a1 overexpression ameliorated these alterations in TN rats (Fig. k-t).

 

Further, we evaluated the effect of microglial Nr4a1 overexpression on the cognitive function of TN rats. A flowchart of the behavioral experiments is shown in Fig.  H. Motor function did not significantly differ between the four groups in the OFT (Figure a). NORT revealed a significantly increased discrimination index in the dION-CCI + AAV⁺ group compared with that in the dION-CCI + AAV⁻ group, with no significant differences in locomotor activity between the groups (Figure b-d). Furthermore, in the MWMT, the dION-CCI + AAV⁺ group exhibited shortened escape latency, made more platform crossings, and spent more time in the target quadrant than did the dION-CCI + AAV⁻ group (Figure e-h).
In summary, the deficiency of Nr4a1 in microglia hyperactivates microglia, mediates abnormal autophagy and impaired synaptic plasticity through neuroinflammation, and ultimately leads to cognitive decline in TN rats.

## Multi-omics sequencing reveals aberrant changes in the phosphoinositide 3-kinase/protein kinase B (PI3K/Akt) signaling pathway in the hippocampus of TN rats
To elucidate the molecular mechanisms through which Nr4a1 regulates microglial overactivation-mediated neuroinflammation in TN rat hippocampi, we performed transcriptomic and metabolomic sequencing to identify aberrantly altered signaling pathways. Differential gene expression analysis revealed significant enrichment in certain pathways, including the PI3K/Akt signaling pathway, ubiquitin-mediated proteolysis, and the NOD-like receptor signaling pathway (Fig.  a-b). Gene ontology indicated enrichment in molecular functions such as phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase regulatory subunit binding, ATP binding, and phosphatase activator activity (Fig.  c). Furthermore, GSEA revealed significant enrichment in the PI3K/Akt signaling pathway, with an overall downregulation of gene expression (Fig.  d-e). Metabolomic analysis showed downregulated expression of phosphatidylinositol, as illustrated in the clustering heatmap of differential metabolites and boxplot of phosphatidylinositol levels. KEGG pathway analysis further confirmed enrichment in the phosphatidylinositol signaling system (Fig.  f-h). Subsequently, we validated the downregulation of the PI3K/Akt signaling pathway using RT-qPCR and western blotting. Compared with the Sham group, the dION-CCI group exhibited decreased mRNA levels of PI3K and Akt in the hippocampus (Fig.  i-j). Similarly, western blotting showed reduced protein expression of p-PI3K, p-Akt, and p-mTOR in the dION-CCI group (Fig.  k-n).

 

## Nr4a1 primarily targets Akt2 among Akt isoforms; restoring Nr4a1 rescues PI3K/Akt2 pathway downregulation in the hippocampus of TN rats and in an LPS-stimulated BV2 cell model
To investigate whether Nr4a1 interacts with the PI3K/Akt pathway, we performed Co-IP/MS on rat hippocampal tissues (Fig.  a). First, Co-IP was used to capture all proteins bound to Nr4a1 (Fig.  c). MS revealed potential Nr4a1-binding proteins. Notably, Akt2, among the Akt isoforms, was bound to Nr4a1 (Fig.  d). We used the STRING database to predict protein–protein interactions between Nr4a1 and Akt2, revealing a structural association (Fig.  b). Furthermore, molecular docking revealed that Akt2 (cyan) binds Nr4a1 (dark blue) at multiple sites via hydrogen bonds and van der Waals forces. Among the Akt isoforms, Nr4a1 showed the strongest binding to Akt2, with a binding free energy of − 626 kcal/mol, indicating a stable interaction (Fig.  e). Double immunofluorescence confirmed co-localization of Nr4a1 and p-Akt2 with or without Nr4a1 overexpression (Fig.  f). Therefore, Akt2 was selected for further study. Additionally, western blotting confirmed downregulation of key PI3K/Akt2 pathway components in LPS-stimulated BV2 cells and TN rat hippocampi. Restoring Nr4a1 expression increased PI3K/Akt2 pathway activity (Fig.  g-n). These data establish a molecular link between Nr4a1 and Akt2, suggesting that Nr4a1 regulates microglial activation, at least in part, by modulating the PI3K/Akt2 signaling axis.

 

## Downregulation of PI3K/Akt2 signaling reverses the beneficial effects of Nr4a1 overexpression on microglial overactivation-mediated neuroinflammation
We administered PI3K (LY294002) and Akt2 (MK-2206) inhibitors after Nr4a1 overexpression in vivo and in vitro. The clinically used Akt inhibitor MK-2206 exhibits higher specificity for Akt2 and downregulates phosphorylated Akt2; thus, it was used for targeting the Akt2 phosphorylation site at Ser474 [ – ]. Rats received intraperitoneal injections based on previously reported dosages of LY294002 [ , ] and MK-2206 [ , ]. Western blotting confirmed the effective inhibition of PI3K/Akt2 signaling in the hippocampus of TN rats after AAV-mediated Nr4a1 overexpression (Figure S11a-d). Similarly, immunofluorescence and western blotting showed that compared with the dION-CCI + AAV⁺ group, the dION-CCI + AAV⁺+LY294002 and dION-CCI + AAV⁺+MK-2206 groups had increased Iba1⁺ microglia, reduced terminal endpoint voxels and total branch length (amoeboid morphology), and elevated Iba1 protein expression (Figure a-l, s). These groups also showed higher expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β) and lower expression of anti-inflammatory cytokines (IL-4 and IL-10) than the dION-CCI + AAV⁺ group (Figure m-r).
Subsequently, we validated these findings in vitro. Based on previous studies [ , ] and on our CCK-8 and western blot results, BV2 cells were treated with 10 µM LY294002 or 0.5 µM MK-2206 (Figure e-h). Western blotting confirmed successful inhibition of the PI3K/Akt2 pathway in BV2 cells after LV-Nr4a1OE (Figure i-l). Compared with the LPS+Nr4a1OE group, the LPS+Nr4a1OE+LY294002 and LPS+Nr4a1OE+MK-2206 groups exhibited higher expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β) and decreased levels of anti-inflammatory cytokines (IL-4, IL-10) (Figure m-r).
These findings demonstrate that Nr4a1 may restore microglial homeostasis and suppress neuroinflammation by activating the PI3K/Akt2 signaling pathway.

## Inhibition of the PI3K/Akt2 pathway eliminates the beneficial effects of Nr4a1 overexpression on autophagy, synaptic plasticity, and cognitive function in TN rats
To determine whether the PI3K/Akt2 axis is necessary for the beneficial effects of Nr4a1, we inhibited this pathway following Nr4a1 overexpression. Notably, pharmacological inhibition of either PI3K (LY294002) or Akt2 (MK-2206) significantly attenuated the ability of Nr4a1 to restore cellular homeostasis in TN rats. The improvements in neuronal autophagy (Figure a-d), synaptic protein expression (Figure S12e-j), synaptic ultrastructure (Figure k-n), and dendritic spine architecture (Figure o-x) conferred by Nr4a1 were largely reversed by pathway inhibition. This finding was further assessed in a neuron-microglia co-culture system. The protective capacity of the conditioned medium from Nr4a1-overexpressing BV2 microglia was compromised when PI3K/Akt2 signaling was blocked. Under these conditions, the improvements in autophagy and synaptic protein markers were no longer observed in HT22 cells (Figure a-l). Critically, the cognitive improvements driven by Nr4a1 overexpression were also compromised by PI3K/Akt2 pathway inhibition. Behavioral analysis revealed that the gains in novel object recognition and spatial learning/memory were significantly diminished in inhibitor-treated groups compared to those receiving Nr4a1 overexpression alone (Figure d-j).
Complete experimental data are available in Supplementary Notes . Together, these loss-of-function experiments support the conclusion that the PI3K/Akt2 signaling pathway serves as an important downstream mechanism through which Nr4a1 exerts its protective effects, contributing to the rescue of autophagy, synaptic plasticity, and cognitive function.

## Discussion
Patients with TN develop cognitive impairment [ , ], with persistent deficits observed even after surgical pain relief, underscoring the need for more comprehensive treatment strategies [ ]. However, studies exploring the molecular mechanisms underlying TN hippocampal-dependent cognitive impairment are lacking. In this study, we demonstrated cognitive decline in TN rats, along with overactivation of hippocampal microglia, abnormal neuronal autophagy, and impaired synaptic plasticity. We identified a novel mechanistic axis: Nr4a1 deficiency in microglia may lead to an aberrant PI3K/Akt2 pathway, allowing microglial hyperactivation to mediate exacerbation of neuroinflammation, thereby indirectly disrupting neuronal autophagy and synaptic plasticity. Overall, in vivo and in vitro experiments revealed that Nr4a1 regulates microglia hyperactivation-mediated neuroinflammation to improve neuronal synaptic plasticity, suggesting that targeting this pathway could represent a potential therapeutic strategy for ameliorating TN-related cognitive impairment. 
In this TN model, hippocampal microglial activation increased in a time-dependent manner, reaching a significant level by day 28, coinciding with impaired cognitive function. These findings suggest that hippocampal microglial overactivation plays an important role in TN-related cognitive deficits. However, further studies are required to fully elucidate the relationship between pain and cognitive impairment. Activated microglia release inflammatory mediators that drive neuroinflammation [ ]. In TN rats with cognitive dysfunction, we detected elevated hippocampal levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β) and reduced expression of anti-inflammatory cytokines (IL-4 and IL-10), consistent with findings from previous studies [ , ]. Neuroinflammation and disrupted synaptic plasticity are established mechanisms underlying cognitive impairment in animal models [ , ]. Microglial-driven inflammation exacerbates synaptic dysfunction and cognitive decline in sepsis-associated encephalopathy [ ], whereas reducing neuroinflammation improves synaptic plasticity and cognitive outcomes in Alzheimer’s disease models [ ]. Similarly, in TN rats, we observed decreased expression of synaptic plasticity-related proteins and synaptic ultrastructural abnormalities, indicating impaired synaptic plasticity.
The results indicate that autophagy may link neuroinflammation and synaptic dysfunction. In this study, macroautophagy (or autophagy) was specifically examined. Autophagy is regulated by pro-inflammatory cytokines, including IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-17, and IL-6, enhancing autophagy when upregulated [ ]. Basal autophagy supports synaptic homeostasis, whereas excessive autophagy causes the degradation of critical synaptic proteins and organelles, ultimately impairing synaptic plasticity [ , ]. Therefore, autophagy exerts protective and detrimental effects depending on its magnitude, in TN-related cognitive impairment. Our metabolomic sequencing revealed enrichment of autophagy-related genes in the hippocampus of TN rats. Protein levels of key autophagy markers and ultrastructural analysis indicated pathological autophagy activation suggesting that hippocampal microglial overactivation drives neuroinflammation, disrupts neuronal autophagy, and causes synaptic deficits and cognitive dysfunction. Therefore, regulating hippocampal microglial activation is important for preserving neuronal function and cognitive health in TN.
Transcriptomic sequencing revealed that Nr4a1 is a crucial gene associated with microglial activation in TN rats with cognitive impairment. The role of Nr4a1 in cognitive impairment associated with TN has remained unexplored, prompting our interest. Nr4a1 downregulation promotes microglial activation and exacerbates neuroinflammation in central nervous system disorders. Compared with wild-type microglia, Nr4a1−/− microglia exhibit significantly elevated TNF, IL-1β, and IL-6 mRNA levels, and Nr4a1 directly binding to and destabilizing TNF mRNA in an m6A-dependent manner, mediating its anti-inflammatory effects [ ]. Our results showed that Nr4a1 expression is downregulated in the hippocampal microglia of TN rats. We overexpressed Nr4a1 in the hippocampal microglia of TN rats using AAV with a microglia-specific CX3CR promoter. This intervention attenuated microglial activation and neuroinflammation. Additionally, lentivirus-mediated Nr4a1 overexpression in BV2 cells reduced the inflammatory responses to LPS, supporting our hypothesis and being consistent with previous findings [ ]. These findings revealed a novel and important role of Nr4a1 in regulating hippocampal microglial activity during trigeminal neuropathic pain.
Subsequently, we evaluated the effects of Nr4a1 overexpression on autophagy and synaptic plasticity in TN rats. Neurons exhibited reduced autophagosomes and lysosomes, improved endoplasmic reticulum structures, and attenuated pathological autophagy. Nr4a1 overexpression also increased the hippocampal levels of synaptic proteins and promoted synaptic cleft integrity and PSD thickness restoration. We performed Golgi staining, given that Nr4a1 has previously been shown to regulate dendritic spine density [ ] and that changes in the morphology, structure, and number of dendritic spines are crucial components of synaptic plasticity [ ]. TN rats showed decreased dendritic spine density, fewer mature spines, and more immature spines, which were ameliorated by Nr4a1 overexpression. Nr4a1 is essential for hippocampal-dependent cognition [ , ]. Its expression is upregulated in the hippocampus after hippocampal-dependent long-term memory tasks and is induced by spatial and contextual exploration [ ]. Furthermore, Nr4a1 is induced in cortical and hippocampal GABAergic interneurons during associative learning and is necessary for normal memory storage [ ]. In this study, microglial-specific Nr4a1 overexpression in the hippocampus was primarily reflected in restoring hippocampal-dependent spatial learning and working memory in TN rats. Chen et al. [ ] reported that decreased Nr4a1 expression in hippocampal CA1 pyramidal neurons contributes to age-related cognitive impairment through impaired synaptic transmission and plasticity, whereas Nr4a1 overexpression improves cognitive deficits in aged mice, consistent with our findings. Similarly, Chatterjee et al. [ ] found that hippocampal Nr4a1 overexpression ameliorated long-term memory deficits in a tau-based mouse model of Alzheimer’s disease and related dementias [ ]. Furthermore, Lei et al. [ ] reported that hippocampal Nr4a1 overexpression induces age-related tau hyperphosphorylation and cognitive decline [ ]. These studies, focused on hippocampal neurons, have reported that maintaining physiologically appropriate levels of Nr4a1 is critical for neuroprotection, as excessive and insufficient expression can be detrimental. Building on this concept, our findings extend the understanding of Nr4a1’s role in cognition to the non-neuronal compartment. We demonstrate that maintaining appropriate Nr4a1 levels specifically in microglia is crucial for restoring cellular homeostasis and suppressing neuroinflammation in the hippocampus. This regulatory action in microglia is linked to the observed recovery of neuronal autophagy, synaptic plasticity, and, ultimately, cognitive function in TN rats.
Because the mechanisms through which Nr4a1 regulates microglial overactivation and neuroinflammation remain poorly understood, we investigated potential downstream pathways and identified a novel role for the PI3K/Akt2 pathway in this process. PI3K activation generates phosphatidylinositol-3,4,5-trisphosphate, which acts as a second messenger for Akt activation [ ]. The Akt family comprises three isoforms (Akt1–3) [ ]. Once activated, Akt promotes essential cellular functions, including transcription, proliferation, and survival, via downstream substrates such as mechanistic target of rapamycin (mTOR) [ ]. Notably, the PI3K/Akt signaling pathway is considered a crucial regulator of microglial activity [ , ]. Dysregulation of this pathway can drive chronic neuroinflammation and exacerbate inflammatory responses [ ]. Furthermore, hippocampal PI3K/Akt activation inhibits microglial overactivation and neuroinflammation, ameliorating neuropathic pain-induced cognitive deficits [ ]. In this study, multi-omics sequencing revealed abnormal changes in the PI3K/Akt signaling pathway in the hippocampus of TN rats. We further validated the downregulation of PI3K/Akt pathway expression by examining the mRNA and protein levels of its key components. Nr4a1 induces epithelial–mesenchymal transition and enhances the osteogenic differentiation capacity of jaw bone marrow mesenchymal stem cells by activating the PI3K/Akt signaling pathway [ , ]. Thus, we hypothesize that the PI3K/Akt pathway may represent a potential molecular mechanism through which Nr4a1 regulates hippocampal microglial overactivation and its associated neuroinflammation in TN rats.
Nr4a1 interacts with Akt, promoting tau hyperphosphorylation during aging [ ]. However, whether this interaction occurs in the hippocampus of rats remains unknown. Co-IP/MS on hippocampal tissue revealed that Nr4a1 binds specifically to Akt2. Reduced Akt2 expression impairs learning and memory, disrupts hippocampal LTP, and alters mTOR-related signaling [ ]. Based on these findings, we selected Akt2 for further investigation. PI3K/Akt2 signaling components were downregulated in Nr4a1-overexpressing BV2 cells and TN rat hippocampi. Restoring Nr4a1 levels enhanced PI3K/Akt2 activity, suggesting that Nr4a1 modulates microglial overactivation via this pathway. Pharmacological inhibition of PI3K (LY294002) or Akt2 (MK-2206) attenuated the protective effects of Nr4a1 against microglial activation, neuroinflammation, and its benefits for autophagy and synaptic plasticity in vitro and in vivo. Similarly, the behavioral improvements conferred by Nr4a1 were reduced following pathway inhibition. These findings are consistent with the view that PI3K/Akt2 signaling acts as a key downstream effector of Nr4a1, contributing to its regulation of hippocampal microglial overactivation, neuroinflammation, and synaptic dysfunction in TN rats. Notably, a previous study reported using AKT302–312, a peptide that competitively inhibits the Nr4a1–AKT interaction, to block Nr4a1–AKT binding in vitro and reverse Nr4a1-induced tau hyperphosphorylation [ ]. Whether similar strategies targeting Nr4a1–Akt2 binding produce effects comparable to our observations warrants future investigation.
Our study has some limitations. First, we employed the dION-CCI model, which primarily simulates chronic trigeminal neuropathic pain but does not fully replicate the clinically defining features of classical trigeminal neuralgia (e.g., paroxysmal pain). Consequently, our findings cannot be directly extrapolated to all subtypes of patients with TN. Nevertheless, given that cognitive decline is indeed observed in patients with TN, investigating the pathophysiological alterations occurring under such chronic pain conditions holds significant clinical relevance. Second, the inability to obtain human brain tissue precluded direct examination of the relationship between hippocampal Nr4a1 expression and cognitive status in patients with TN. Third, although we identified an interaction between Nr4a1 and Akt2, the specific binding domains and the intrinsic regulatory mechanisms require further elucidation. Finally, this study included young adult rats, whereas clinical TN predominantly affects older adults. Future investigations using aged animal models will be essential to elucidate the interaction between aging processes and the pathways identified here.

## Conclusions
In TN rats, hippocampal microglial Nr4a1 is downregulated. Our findings suggest that this downregulation is associated with impaired PI3K/Akt2 signal transduction, potentially through reduced interaction with Akt2 and decreased Akt2 kinase activity. Restoring Nr4a1 expression restores PI3K/Akt2 pathway activity, thereby suppressing microglial overactivation and neuroinflammation. These effects, in turn, might promote the recovery of neuronal autophagy and synaptic plasticity, accompanied by attenuated cognitive decline. Therefore, maintaining appropriate Nr4a1 activity is crucial for hippocampal microglial homeostasis in TN rats. Collectively, these insights highlight the Nr4a1–PI3K/Akt2 axis as a promising candidate therapeutic target for TN-related cognitive impairment.
