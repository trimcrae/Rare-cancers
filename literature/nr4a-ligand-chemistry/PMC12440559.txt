The work cannot be used commercially without permission from the journal.Given the increasing incidence and disability rate of rheumatoid arthritis (RA) year by year, RA has become a common cause of disability. Danggui Niantong Decoction (DGNTD) has been shown to have therapeutic effects on RA. However, to date, its bioactive components and potential targets remain unclear. To systematically explore the potential mechanisms of DGNTD in the treatment of RA, we utilized a combination of network pharmacology, Mendelian randomization, molecular docking, and molecular dynamics simulation. We used the Traditional Chinese Medicine Systems Pharmacology Database and Analysis Platform database to identify DGNTD’s active ingredients and potential targets, and GeneCards to screen RA-related targets. Common targets were identified via Venn analysis. We built a protein–protein interaction network and a drug-ingredient-target map with Cytoscape. Based on shared targets, we conducted gene ontology and Kyoto encyclopedia of genes and genomes enrichment analyses to reveal key pathways. To validate results, we used R for Mendelian randomization to assess core targets’ correlation with RA. We used Autodock for molecular docking and GROMACS for dynamics simulations to verify complex stability. DGNTD contains 316 active ingredients and 276 potential targets. Through protein–protein interaction network analysis and drug-ingredient-target network screening, we identified 215 active ingredients and 200 common targets, with quercetin and naringenin as core active ingredients. The core targets included RAC-alpha serine/threonine-protein kinase, cellular tumor antigen p53, signal transducer and activator of transcription 3, mitogen-activated protein kinase 1, mitogen-activated protein kinase 3, and Myc proto-oncogene protein. The Kyoto encyclopedia of genes and genomes and gene ontology enrichment analyses revealed 186 signaling pathways, with core targets mainly involving the PI3K/AKT pathway, lipid metabolism, and atherosclerosis-related biological processes. After validation through Mendelian randomization, RAC-alpha serine/threonine-protein kinase and mitogen-activated protein kinase 3 may be key targets for the treatment of RA by DGNTD. The molecular docking and dynamics simulations showed that the complexes formed by the core active ingredients and key targets exhibited strong stability. DGNTD exerts its therapeutic effects on RA through the synergistic action of multiple components, targets, and pathways, with its anti-inflammatory effects and potential molecular mechanisms being particularly noteworthy and warranting further investigation.

## 1. Introduction
Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is an autoimmune disease characterized primarily by erosive arthritis. Its pathological basis includes persistent synovial inflammation, synovial cell proliferation, angiogenesis, cartilage damage, bone erosion, and potential joint deformities.[ ] The disease mainly manifests as symmetrical swelling and pain in multiple joints, with stiffness, primarily affecting the small joints of the hands. In 2020, there were an estimated 38,300 deaths (95% UI 33,500–44,000) due to RA globally. Between 1990 and 2020, the global age-standardized YLD rate for RA increased by 13.8% (12.3–15.6).[ ] Moreover, as the disease progresses, the disability rate in RA patients continues to rise.[ ] Current treatment strategies for RA include nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, corticosteroids, disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs, and biologics. However, these treatment approaches have limitations,[ , ] including adverse reactions, drug resistance, long-term safety of medication, and individual variability in response to treatment.
Danggui Niantong Decoction (DGNTD) is a classic Traditional Chinese Medicine formula, which originates from the book “Yixue Qiyuan” written by Yuansu Zhang, an outstanding doctor of the Jin Dynasty. Interestingly, the symptoms described in the chapter for treatment with DGNTD are strikingly similar to the clinical manifestations of RA. These symptoms include redness, swelling, and pain in the small joints of the hands and feet, with progressive involvement leading to pain and limited mobility in larger joints such as the shoulders, elbows, and knees.Traditional Chinese Medicine categorizes RA as “Bi syndrome.” Patients may develop this condition due to congenital physical weakness, prolonged exposure to damp and cold environments, overwork, emotional distress, or improper diet, which can lead to the obstruction of the meridians (a type of channel analogous to the human vascular system) by wind, cold, and dampness. This may result in joint invasion and damage, eventually progressing to RA. According to the Traditional Chinese Medicine theory, DGNTD has the effect of relieving dampness and clearing heat, soothing wind and relieving pain. It has been widely used in the clinical treatment of RA in China due to its significant improvement of symptoms and major physicochemical indicators in RA patients and its relatively high safety profile.[ ]
Modern studies have shown[ ] that the pathological process of RA is characterized by the activation of autoreactive immune cells, the production of autoantibodies, the formation of immune complexes, and the activation of the complement system. Under the influence of chemokines, inflammatory cells migrate in large numbers to the synovial joints and deposit there, releasing cytokines to recruit more inflammatory cells. The proliferation of synovial cells forms an invasive pannus, which in turn releases proteases and cytokines, activates osteoclasts, destroys bone, and leads to joint deformity and functional impairment. DGNTD has shown significant efficacy in anti-inflammation,[ ] immune regulation,[ ] alleviation of arthritis symptoms,[ ] and restoration of joint function.[ ] However, the specific mechanisms underlying its therapeutic effects remain unclear. In this study, we utilized network pharmacology, Mendelian randomization, molecular docking, and molecular dynamics simulations to explore the potential therapeutic targets and signaling pathways of DGNTD in improving RA. We aimed to elucidate the molecular mechanisms underlying the therapeutic effects of DGNTD on RA and provide a theoretical basis for its clinical application and potential internationalization.

## 2. Materials and methods

## 2.1. Screening of active ingredients and their target sites for DGNTD
Using the Traditional Chinese Medicine Systems Pharmacology Database and Analysis Platform (TCMSP), we retrieved the active components of 15 Chinese medicinal herbs in DGNTD, including Baizhu, Cangzhu, Fangfeng, Qianghuo, Shengma, Gegen, Huangqin, Kushen, Renshen, Danggui, Zhuling, Zexie, Yinchen, Zhimu, and Gancao. The active components were screened based on the criteria of oral bioavailability ≥ 30% and drug-likeness ≥ 0.18. Meanwhile, the relevant targets of these 15 herbs in DGNTD were directly collected from the Relate Targets in the TCMSP platform. The UniProt database was used to standardize the names of all targets.

## 2.2. Prediction of disease-related targets and acquisition of drug-disease common targets
Using the GeneCards (https://www.genecards.org), OMIM (https://omim.org), DrugBank (https://go.drugbank.com), and TTD (https://db.idrblab.net) databases, we searched for targets related to RA using the keyword “rheumatoid arthritis.” In the GeneCards database, targets were filtered by a relevance score ≥ 1. After removing duplicates, these targets were converted to corresponding gene names on the UniProt platform. The drug targets of DGNTD were compared with the RA-related targets on the E Venn platform.[ ] The intersection targets were extracted and visually presented through a Venn diagram.

## 2.3. Construction of the DGNTD regulatory network
The target information of DGNTD for the treatment of RA was imported into Cytoscape 3.10.3 software to construct a “DGNTD-RA-Target” network diagram.

## 2.4. Construction of protein–protein interaction (PPI) networks and screening of core targets
The targets obtained were imported into the STRING database (https://cn.string-db.org/), with the species set as Homo sapiens and the highest confidence set to >0.9, to obtain the PPI network. Subsequently, the CytoNCA plugin in Cytoscape 3.10.3 software (University of California, San Diego) was used for topological analysis of the network. Using R 4.5.0 software (R Foundation and the R Core Team, Vienna, Austria), genes greater than the median value of the topological parameters (Betweenness, Closeness, Degree, Eigenvector, LAC, and Network) were identified as core targets. A core target network diagram was created using Cytoscape 3.10.3.

## 2.5. Gene ontology (GO) function and enrichment analysis of the Kyoto encyclopedia of genes and genomes (KEGG) pathway
Using R 4.5.0 software, the bioconductor biological data package in R was employed to conduct GO function and KEGG pathway enrichment analysis on the drug-disease intersection targets obtained in section 1.2, with P-value < .05 and Q-value < 0.05 as filtering criteria. The results were visualized using bubble charts and pathway diagrams.

## 2.6. Mendelian randomization analysis
The TwoSampleMR package in R 4.5.0 software was used for Mendelian randomization analysis. The eQTLGen consortium provided a comprehensive set of cis-eQTLs data and allele frequency statistics. The eQTL data corresponding to the 6 druggable genes were used as exposure data. In our study, eQTLs were limited to SNPs located within 100 kb upstream or downstream of the druggable genes. The genome-wide association study (GWAS) analysis from the RA dataset in the GWAS database (https://gwas.mrcieu.ac.uk) was used as the outcome variable, including genotype counts from 16,314 RA cases and 315,115 controls. The inverse-variance weighted method was used to assess the causal relationship between core target genes and RA. Additionally, sensitivity analysis was performed using the MR-Egger method to ensure the reliability of the study results. A P-value < .05 indicated a significant causal relationship between the core target genes and RA.

## 2.7. Molecular docking and molecular dynamics
The 2D structures of the active components corresponding to the core targets were retrieved from the PubChem database (https://pub-chem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/) and then converted into 3D structures using ChemOffice software (CambridgeSoft, Cambridge). The core protein IDs were searched in the UniProt database, and their 3D structures were downloaded from the PDB database (https://www.rcsb.org/). These structures were processed in PyMOL software (DeLano Scientific LLC, San Carlos) to remove water molecules and ligands. After adding hydrogen atoms using AutoDockTools software (Olson Laboratory, San Diego) to determine the active pockets, molecular docking was performed using AutoDock Vina software to calculate the binding free energy. Finally, compounds with lower binding energy and stable conformations were visualized with their targets using PyMOL software.
Molecular dynamics simulations were performed using the GROMACS 2025.1 software (University of Groningen, Groningen, Netherlands) package on the receptor proteins with the highest binding energy to the ligand molecules obtained from molecular docking, to obtain their binding modes and binding energies with the ligands. The receptor proteins used the CHARMM36 force field included in GROMACS, while the ligand molecules used the force field provided by the CGenFF database (https://cgenff.com/). The TIP3P water model was employed for the water molecules. The molecular docking results were used as the initial structures, which were first subjected to energy minimization, followed by 100 ns molecular dynamics simulations in an aqueous solution. The simulations were conducted under NVT (N = constant number of particles, V = constant volume, T = constant temperature) and NPT (N= constant number of particles, P = constant pressure, T = constant temperature) conditions, with the temperature set at 300 K.

## 3. Results

## 3.1. Screening of active ingredients and targets for DGNTD
A total of 316 active components of DGNTD were obtained through the TCMSP database platform. After removing 101 components without matched targets, 4 active components were obtained from Baizhu, 4 from Cangzhu, 2 from Danggui, 18 from Fangfeng, 85 from GAancao, 3 from Gegen, 34 from Huangqin, 23 from Kushen, 12 from Qianghuo, 16 from Renshen, 7 from Shengma, 13 from Yinchen, 7 from Zexie, 11 from Zhimu, and 7 from Zhuling. After removing duplicate active components, 215 potential active components were obtained. The targets corresponding to the effective components of this formula predicted by the database were sorted out, and the duplicates were removed. After conversion through the UniProt database, a total of 276 potential targets were finally obtained.

## 3.2. Target screening for RA
A total of 4698 RA-related targets were collected through the GeneCards, OMIM, DrugBank, and TTD databases, including 4338 from the GeneCards database, 100 from the TTD database, 247 from the DrugBank database, and 13 from the OMIM database. After removing duplicates, 4450 disease targets were obtained.

## 3.3. Venn analysis of potential therapeutic targets of DGNTD for RA
Using the E Venn platform, the drug targets of DGNTD were matched with the RA targets to obtain the intersection targets, and a Venn diagram was drawn. The results showed that there were 200 core targets between the drug and the disease (Fig. ).
 

## 3.4. Construction of the DGNTD regulatory network
The 215 active components of DGNTD and the 200 core targets shared between the drug and disease were imported into Cytoscape 3.10.3 software to construct a “Drug-Disease-Target” network, which included 430 nodes and 2879 edges (Fig. ).
 

## 3.5. Construction of PPI networks and screening of core targets
The 200 potential targets of DGNTD for the treatment of RA were imported into the STRING database, with the species limited to “Homo sapiens,” to generate a PPI network (Fig. ), which included 200 nodes and 695 edges. The TSV format file was downloaded and imported into Cytoscape 3.10.3 software to construct the Core diagram of the PPI network (Fig. A). The CytoNCA plugin was applied, and topological parameters including Betweenness, Closeness, Degree, Eigenvector, LAC, and Network were used. Based on the criterion that each parameter value exceeded the median, a topological analysis of the network was conducted, yielding 10 targets (Table ). The targets were sorted in descending order of degree, and targets with a degree value >10 were selected as core targets. These included RAC-alpha serine/threonine-protein kinase (AKT1), cellular tumor antigen p53 (TP53), signal transducer and activator of transcription 3, Mitogen-activated protein kinase 1, apoptosis regulator Bcl-2, mitogen-activated protein kinase 3 (MAPK3), and Myc proto-oncogene protein (MYC), which are the key targets of DGNTD for the treatment of RA (Fig. B).
 

## 3.6. GO and KEGG pathway enrichment analyses
Using R 4.1.1 software, GO function and KEGG pathway enrichment analyses were conducted on the 200 drug-disease intersection targets, with a threshold of P < .05 for screening. The GO function enrichment analysis yielded a total of 2928 entries, including biological process (BP), cellular component (CC), and molecular function (MF), with 2606 BP entries, 80 CC entries, and 242 MF entries. The top 10 significant results for BP, CC, and MF were selected to draw bubble charts (Fig. A). The main BPs involved in the treatment of RA with DGNTD include response to external stimuli, response to lipopolysaccharides, and response to oxidative stress. The CCs mainly include membrane microdomains, membrane rafts, and cytoplasmic vesicles. The MFs primarily consist of DNA-binding transcriptional activator activity and DNA-binding transcription factor binding. These findings suggest that DGNTD may exert its therapeutic effects on RA by modulating cellular responses to external stimuli, lipopolysaccharides, and oxidative stress to alleviate inflammatory responses. The targets of action are mainly distributed in the membrane rafts and cytoplasmic vesicles of cells.
 A total of 186 pathways were enriched in the KEGG analysis, involving pathways related to immune response, oxidative stress response, lipid metabolism, and atherosclerosis. The top 30 pathways most closely related to the improvement of RA by DGNTD were selected to draw a bubble chart (Fig. B). The pathways with the lowest P values include lipid and atherosclerosis, PI3K–AKT signaling pathway, hepatitis B, and human cytomegalovirus infection. Among them, the PI3K–AKT signaling pathway may play the most significant role (Fig. ).
 

## 3.7. Evaluating the potential association between key targets and RA through Mendelian randomization analysis
Through Mendelian randomization analysis, we identified 3 key targets, AKT1, and MAPK3, which are significantly associated with RA (Fig. A and B). Using the inverse variance weighting method, the odds ratio for AKT1 was 1.089 (95% confidence interval [CI]: 1.014–1.169, P = .018), and for MAPK3 was 0.933 (95% CI: 0.912–0.955, P < .001), all of which were significantly associated with RA (Fig. C). The funnel plots showed a roughly symmetrical distribution, indicating consistency of causal effects (Fig. C). Therefore, AKT1 and MAPK3 were determined to be the main anti-RA key targets of DGNTD.
 

## 3.8. Results of molecular docking and molecular dynamics
Based on the identified key targets, the corresponding active components of the drug were obtained (Table ). Quercetin and Naringenin were found to have interactions with many key targets and were selected as the core active components of DGNTD for molecular docking analysis. The molecular docking results showed that the lowest binding energies between the 3 target proteins and the 2 ligands were all less than −5 kJ·mol−1. Hydrogen bonds formed at the docking sites had distances within 2 Å to 3 Å, indicating a relatively stable binding between the core active components and the key target proteins. Finally, the docking results were visualized using Pymol software (Fig. ).
 To further validate the binding stability of the complex with lower binding energy and research significance, molecular dynamics simulations were performed on the quercetin–AKT1 complex. The root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) reflects the fluctuation of protein conformation and can be used to assess the stability of ligand binding to the target protein, with smaller values indicating more stable binding. Figure A shows that the RMSD value of the complex is relatively low, with fluctuations ranging from 0.6 to 0.8 nm, indicating high stability and equilibrium in the binding mode of the system. The root-mean-square fluctuation (RMSF) reflects the fluctuation of protein amino acid residues. Higher RMSF values indicate stronger interactions between the protein residues and the small molecule. Figure B shows significant peaks around Res120 and Res460 in the complex, suggesting that these residues may be key residues. The radius of gyration (Rg) can assess the compactness of the complex, with smaller values indicating tighter binding. Figure C shows that the Rg value of the complex is relatively low, indicating a compact protein structure and a relatively stable complex. The solvent-accessible surface area (SASA) describes the interaction between the protein molecule and the surrounding water molecules, reflecting changes in the hydrophobicity of the protein. Figure D shows that after binding with the quercetin ligand, the SASA of AKT1 fluctuates slightly and remains stable, indicating that the binding of the ligand has a minimal impact on the structure of AKT1. Hydrogen bonds are one of the strongest non-covalent interactions, and the number of hydrogen bonds formed during the simulation reflects the binding strength. Figure E shows that the number of hydrogen bonds changes during the simulation, with the complex forming at least 3 to 4 hydrogen bonds and gradually stabilizing.
 

## 4. Discussion
Considering the multifaceted nature of the pharmacological actions of Traditional Chinese Medicine formulas, we employed network pharmacology to examine the active components and protein targets of DGNTD for the treatment of RA and analyzed the “herb-disease-target” interaction network to address the complexity of the active components and protein targets of the herbal formula. Moreover, we utilized extensive GWAS data and Mendelian randomization techniques to identify key targets for the treatment of RA. This approach enhances the predictive efficacy of treatment by leveraging cis-eQTL and RA risk association data to demonstrate causal relationships between exposure and outcomes. Finally, to demonstrate the druggability of these target genes, we predicted the corresponding drugs and conducted molecular docking and molecular dynamics simulations.
Based on the results of this study, 7 core protein targets (AKT1, MAPK3, MYC, TP53, signal transducer and activator of transcription 3, mitogen-activated protein kinase 1, and apoptosis regulator Bcl-2) and 8 major active components in DGNTD (with 2 core components being naringenin and quercetin) were identified, indicating that DGNTD exerts its therapeutic effects on RA through multiple components and targets. Modern pharmacological studies have shown[ ] that quercetin has antioxidant properties and can inhibit pyroptosis and inflammatory cascade reactions. A meta-analysis and systematic review of 17 studies involving 251 animals on the efficacy of quercetin in treating RA demonstrated that quercetin can reduce arthritis scores and paw swelling and has anti-inflammatory, antioxidant stress, and bone-protective properties.[ ] Naringenin has been proven to have a series of biological effects, including anti-inflammation, free radical scavenging, and immune regulation.[ , ] Although there is no direct evidence yet that naringenin has significant therapeutic effects on RA, the pathogenesis of RA is closely related to signaling pathways such as PI3K/AKT, MAPK, and NF-κB.[ – ] Many studies have shown that naringenin exerts anti-inflammatory effects by regulating signaling pathways such as PI3K/AKT and NF-κB.[ , ] A study by Xu et al[ ] demonstrated that naringenin can inhibit the activity of the NF-κB signaling pathway by downregulating the expression of TLR4 and NF-κB p65 proteins and reducing the nuclear translocation of p65 protein. Interestingly, naringenin can also increase the expression of Bcl-xl and Bcl-2 and inhibit the PI3K/AKT pathway to alleviate NF-κB-mediated inflammatory responses in a manner similar to DGNTD-mediated FLS cell apoptosis.[ ] AKT is a protein kinase that regulates cell survival and apoptosis. As a key downstream target of PI3K, AKT binds to and is phosphorylated by PI3K, activating the PI3K/AKT signaling pathway, which promotes angiogenesis and pannus formation.[ ] An increasing number of studies have shown that the MAPK signaling pathway can regulate inflammatory cytokines and downstream cell signaling pathways, thereby affecting joint inflammation and destruction.[ , ] It has been reported that shikonin exerts antiangiogenic effects on RA in vivo, ex vivo, and in vitro by interrupting the PI3K/AKT and MAPKs signaling pathways.[ ] The MYC gene family contains multiple members, among which c-Myc is an important proto-oncogene. A study by Xu et al[ ] found that the activation of acid-sensing ion channel 1a subunit during acidosis promotes the proliferation and migration of RA fibroblast-like synoviocytes, and the mechanism may be related to the Wnt/β-catenin/c-Myc pathway. TP53 is an anti-inflammatory factor associated with autoimmune diseases. Dysregulation of TP53 expression may enhance the invasiveness of RA fibroblast-like synoviocytes or the auto-reactivity of lymphocytes, thereby contributing to the inflammatory phenotype in RA.[ ]
The GO analysis of key targets indicates that DGNTD is involved in processes such as immunity, inflammation, and oxidative stress. The KEGG analysis results show that many candidate pathways are involved, including the PI3K/AKT, lipid and atherosclerosis, NF-κB, and TNF signaling pathways. Among them, the PI3K/AKT signaling pathway is closely related to the pathological process of RA.[ ] Activation of the PI3K/AKT/mTOR signaling pathway inhibits ferroptosis in synovial fibroblasts of RA patients and promotes inflammation, angiogenesis, and disruption of the immune microenvironment.[ ] In addition, a study by Wang et al[ ] found that hydrogels with reactive oxygen species scavenging and pH regulation capabilities can protect cells from oxidative stress and induce macrophages to polarize to the M2 phenotype through the PI3K/AKT/NF-κB and MAPK pathways to reduce inflammatory cytokines, exert anti-inflammatory effects, and reshape the inflammatory microenvironment for the treatment of inflammatory diseases, including RA. A study[ ] that included 14 randomized controlled trials involving 1102 patients systematically evaluated the efficacy and safety of DGNTD in treating RA of the damp-heat bi syndrome type. The data showed that DGNTD significantly reduced laboratory indicators such as erythrocyte sedimentation rate, C-reactive protein, and rheumatoid factor in RA patients and alleviated joint swelling, pain, and morning stiffness. Lu et al[ – ] found that DGNTD could inhibit autophagy in fibroblast-like synoviocytes in adjuvant-induced arthritic rats and promote apoptosis via the mitochondrial pathway, thereby improving RA symptoms. In their subsequent studies, they discovered that DGNTD downregulated the expression of autophagy-related proteins LC3 and Beclin1, thereby inhibiting autophagy. Additionally, DGNTD may mediate apoptosis by regulating the Akt/Bax/Bcl-2 pathway, upregulating the expression of proapoptotic protein Bax, and downregulating the expression of antiapoptotic protein Bcl-2, thereby intervening in the progression of RA.
The molecular docking results showed that the binding energies of AKT1, MAPK3, and MYC with the core active components such as quercetin and naringenin were all <−5 kJ·mol−1. Among them, AKT1 had the lowest binding energy with quercetin, indicating that AKT1 has a higher binding affinity than the other key targets. Based on the above analysis, quercetin may target AKT1 to treat RA and alleviate inflammatory symptoms. The molecular dynamics simulation results of the quercetin–AKT1 complex, including RMSD, RMSF, Rg, and SASA, indicated that the conformation was tightly bound. These results, from both static and dynamic perspectives, revealed the stability and equilibrium of the binding between the key target proteins and the core active components, thereby validating that the active components of DGNTD can effectively treat RA by binding to the core targets.

## 5. Conclusion
In summary, this study preliminarily explored the potential molecular mechanisms of DGNTD in treating RA through network pharmacology, Mendelian randomization, molecular docking, and molecular dynamics simulation. The results suggest that DGNTD may primarily exert its therapeutic effects on RA by using core active components such as quercetin and naringenin to regulate key target proteins (AKT1, MAPK3, and MYC). This regulation may influence the upstream PI3K/AKT pathway, inhibit the activity of downstream inflammatory signaling pathways such as NF-κB and MAPK, reduce the expression levels of inflammatory factors such as IL6, IL1β, and TNF, mitigate the destructive effects of inflammatory cascades on joints, modulate autophagy and apoptosis, inhibit the proliferation of synovial cells and the formation of pannus, thereby achieving therapeutic effects on RA. These findings provide a theoretical basis for further studies and clinical applications of DGNTD in treating RA. Despite revealing the potential molecular mechanisms of DGNTD in treating RA, this study has some limitations. For example, it did not consider the differences in applicability among individuals of different constitutions and ethnicities, nor did it account for the synergistic effects between different active components. Future research will require further clinical trials and experimental studies to explore and validate the broad applicability of the findings and to strengthen the assessment of the safety of DGNTD, especially the potential risks associated with long-term and large-scale use.
