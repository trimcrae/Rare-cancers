## Significance
Strategies to optimize polyene antifungals (PAs) have primarily focused on modifying their mycosamine group and polyol chain. However, newly derived molecules face considerable challenges for timely clinical application. Here, we developed an adjuvant strategy that uses clinically approved drugs to target the polyene chain via hydrophobic coupling. This approach triggers conformational rearrangements in PAs, thereby enhancing their selectivity for the fungicidal target ergosterol over the off-target cholesterol and leading to both attenuated nephrotoxicity and enhanced efficacy. Given the irreplaceable lifesaving role of PAs in treating lethal fungal infections, the immediate clinical value of this strategy—exemplified by the amphotericin B and vitamin D3 combination—in saving millions of lives should not be underestimated.

Polyene antifungals (PAs) have long constituted an irreplaceable lifesaving treatment for lethal fungal infections. However, PAs induce significant dose-dependent nephrotoxicity, primarily because of their binding to cholesterol in the renal cell membrane, which is structurally similar to their fungicidal target ergosterol in the fungal membrane. Efforts to optimize PAs have focused mainly on their mycosamine group and polyol chain, while the potential of the polyene chain remains underexplored. Here, we report that the polyene chain can function as a molecular switch, enabling hydrophobic coupling with structurally compatible adjuvants repurposed from clinically approved drugs. This hydrophobic coupling triggers conformational rearrangements of PAs, which are characterized by a reorientation of the mycosamine group, and increases the rigidity and length of the polyene chain, which can asymmetrically modulate the binding of PAs to membrane cholesterol and ergosterol, thereby attenuating nephrotoxicity and increasing effectiveness. Among them, vitamin D3 demonstrated immediate clinical value, characterized by its broad safety profile and substantial dual adjuvant activities in multiple animal models and retrospective clinical data. Thus, exploiting the polyene chain as a molecular switch, modulated via specific polyene chain–coupled adjuvants, is a strategy to concurrently attenuate nephrotoxicity and potentiate the effectiveness of PAs.

However, the dose-dependent nephrotoxicity resulting mainly from the binding of these compounds to cholesterol (Cho) in the renal tubular cell membrane limits their clinical applications ( , ).
Structurally, PAs comprise two rigid fragments linked by a rotatable β-glycosidic bond: i) a mycosamine group and ii) a macrolide ring comprising a hydrophilic polyol chain and a hydrophobic polyene chain ( , ). The mycosamine group anchors PAs through hydrogen bonding with the 3β-OH of sterols ( , ), whereas the polyene chain confers selectivity by forming greater van der Waals (vdW) interactions with more rigid and elongated Erg than with Cho ( ). The structural optimization of PAs to reduce Cho binding and/or enhance Erg binding has focused primarily on modifying the mycosamine group or the polyol chain ( ), whereas modification of the polyene chain remains synthetically challenging and has achieved limited success to date ( , ).
Here, we developed an adjuvant strategy for optimizing PAs by targeting the polyene chain. In this strategy, the polyene chain is treated as a molecular switch, and hydrophobic coupling with specific adjuvants triggers conformational rearrangements in PAs to decrease Cho binding and increase Erg binding, thus attenuating nephrotoxicity and potentiating effectiveness.

## Results

## Vitamin D3 and Its Analogs as Adjuvants of PAs.
Based on the results of a phenotypic screen of 1,845 FDA-approved compounds ( ), we found that certain clinically approved drugs can function as adjuvants of PAs, attenuating cytotoxicity and potentiating fungicidal activity ( ).
 Vitamin D3 and its analogs, including vitamin D2, 25(OH)D3 and alfacalcidol, differentially attenuated the cytotoxicity of heptaenes (AmB, candicidin, hamycin, and mepartricin A; containing seven conjugated double bonds in the polyene chain) ( ) and degenerated heptaenes (nystatin; containing six conjugated double bonds in the polyene chain) ( ) in the human renal proximal tubular epithelial cell line HK-2, as evidenced by a twofold to eightfold increase in the half-maximal cytotoxic concentration (CC50) in cytotoxicity assays ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S1 A and B). No significant attenuation of cytotoxicity was observed for tetraenes (natamycin; containing four conjugated double bonds in the polyene chain) or pentaenes (filipin III; containing five conjugated double bonds in the polyene chain) ( ) ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S1 A and B). Among these adjuvants, vitamin D3 at 0.5 μg/ml, which is close to the concentration available in vivo ( ), markedly attenuated the cytotoxicity of heptaenes and degenerated heptaenes, resulting in a fourfold to eightfold increase in the CC50 value ( ). Furthermore, vitamin D3 increased the CC50 value of AmB by fourfold to eightfold in diverse mammalian cell lines, including the transformed human liver epithelial-2 cell line THLE-2, the human embryonic kidney 293 cell line HEK-293, and the murine macrophage-like cell line RAW264.7, from approximately 8 μg/mL to 32 to 64 μg/mL ( ). In addition, live/dead cell assays revealed that vitamin D3 reduced AmB-induced HK-2 cell death by 65.4% within 30 min at the CC50 level (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A).
The adjuvants potentiated the fungicidal activity of heptaenes and degenerated heptaenes against C. albicans, resulting in a twofold to eightfold reduction in the minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) ( ). No reduction was observed for tetraenes or pentaenes ( ). Vitamin D3 at 0.5 μg/mL exhibited broad synergistic effects with AmB, reducing the MIC value of AmB by fourfold to eightfold against fungal species listed as critical and high priority by the WHO ( ), including C. albicans, C. auris, C. parapsilosis, Cryptococcus neoformans, and Aspergillus fumigatus, with a fractional inhibitory concentration (FIC) index ≤ 0.5 ( ) ( ). In medium supplemented with fetal bovine serum and interstitial fluid, vitamin D3 maintained its synergistic effect with AmB, reducing its MIC by fourfold (SI Appendix, Fig. S2B). Live/dead cell assays revealed that vitamin D3 increased AmB-induced C. albicans death by 1.97-fold within 30 min at the MIC level (SI Appendix, Fig. S2C). Furthermore, vitamin D3 accelerated AmB-mediated fungal clearance, shortening the time required from 24 h to 12 h (SI Appendix, Fig. S2D). Clinically, azole pretreatment may induce partial resistance to AmB, which compromises its effectiveness as a salvage therapy ( ). We preincubated C. albicans with ketoconazole or fluconazole and found that vitamin D3 reversed this azole-induced AmB resistance and still potentiated the fungicidal activity of AmB (SI Appendix, Fig. S2 E–G).
By concurrently attenuating cytotoxicity and potentiating fungicidal activity, vitamin D3 and its analogs broadened the therapeutic window of PAs, as quantified by an 8 to 48-fold increase in the selectivity index (SI; CC50/MIC) for mammalian cells vs. fungi, indicating a significant improvement in both safety and effectiveness ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S2H).

## Adjuvants Reduced/Enhanced the Binding of PAs to Membrane Cho/Erg.
Binding to membrane Cho/Erg is considered the first step through which PAs damage renal cells or kill fungi ( , ). To elucidate the role of adjuvants in this process, we first embedded a fixed amount of Cho/Erg in 1-palmitoyl-2-oleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (POPC) to prepare large unilamellar vesicles (LUVs) that structurally resembled renal or fungal cell membranes, then performed an isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC) assay ( ) to assess the binding of AmB to sterols in the absence or presence of vitamin D3. In Cho-embedded LUVs, AmB bound to Cho with an association constant (Ka) of 8.87 × 104/M, whereas no binding was detected in the presence of vitamin D3 ( ). In contrast, in Erg-embedded LUVs, vitamin D3 enhanced the binding of AmB to Erg, resulting in a 15.03-fold increase in their Ka value ( ). To visualize this process in live cells, we fluorescently labeled AmB with Cy5 and vitamin D3 with FITC. Confocal microscopy revealed that they colocalized on the membrane of both HK-2 and C. albicans cells, with Pearson’s correlation coefficient value of 0.82 ± 0.04 and 0.86 ± 0.06, respectively ( ), indicating a strong colocalization ( ). Quantitative fluorescence analysis demonstrated that vitamin D3 (0.01 to 1 μg/mL) dose-dependently reduced AmB accumulation in HK-2 cells but increased its accumulation in C. albicans ( ). Furthermore, we isolated the membrane fractions of these cells via ultracentrifugation and quantified the AmB associated with them. Vitamin D3 reduced the content of AmB in HK-2 membranes by 25.4% but increased it in C. albicans membranes by 17.12-fold (SI Appendix, Fig. S3A). These results confirmed that vitamin D3 decreased the binding of AmB to membrane Cho while enhancing its binding to membrane Erg.
 Upon binding to membrane Cho/Erg, PAs can further damage renal cells/kill fungi by i) extracting sterols from the membrane ( ) and ii) inserting into the membrane to form transmembrane pores ( , , ). In sterol extraction assays, the AmB and vitamin D3 combination (AmB–vitamin D3) extracted less Cho from HK-2 than AmB alone did, whereas Erg extraction from C. albicans remained unchanged (SI Appendix, Fig. S3B). Moreover, compared with AmB alone, AmB–vitamin D3 decreased transmembrane pore formation in HK-2 cells, as evidenced by reduced K+ efflux and an increased intensity of the membrane potential-sensitive probe DiSC3(5) ( ) ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S3C). In contrast, AmB–vitamin D3 had the opposite effect in C. albicans, with K+ efflux increasing from 47.59% to 89.93% and DiSC3(5) decreasing by 63.9% ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S3C). Furthermore, by sequential administration, we found that vitamin D3 did not affect the DiSC3(5) level in C. albicans when administered alone but markedly reduced the DiSC3(5) level in C. albicans pretreated with AmB, confirming its direct promotion of AmB-induced transmembrane pore formation ( ). To exclude interference from other ion channels on the cell membrane, we assessed K+ efflux in Erg-embedded LUVs and observed that vitamin D3 still significantly increased AmB-induced K+ efflux ( ). Likewise, vitamin D2, 25(OH)D3 and alfacalcidol also enhanced AmB-induced transmembrane pore formation in C. albicans (SI Appendix, Fig. S3D). These results confirmed that the adjuvants reduced the binding of PAs to membrane Cho, subsequently decreasing Cho extraction and transmembrane pore formation in renal cells. Conversely, they enhanced the binding of PAs to membrane Erg and promoted transmembrane pore formation but did not increase Erg extraction in fungal cells.

## Adjuvants Modulate PA–Sterol Binding via Hydrophobic Coupling with the Polyene Chain.
We constructed phospholipid bilayer membrane models embedded with Cho or Erg and conducted molecular dynamics (MD) simulations combined with quantum mechanics/molecular mechanics (QM/MM) calculations to observe binding interactions among PAs, adjuvants, and sterols. In the Cho-embedded membrane model, AmB anchored to Cho through hydrogen bonds formed between the C3′–NH2 (2.9 Å)/C4′–OH (2.0 Å) of its mycosamine group and the 3β-OH of Cho in the absence of vitamin D3 and dissociated after a certain period ( and Movie S1). In the presence of vitamin D3, it rapidly precomplexed with the polyene chain of AmB via hydrophobic effects, forming a binary complex in the extracellular space ( and Movie S2). This complex subsequently anchored to Cho through a hydrogen bond between C3′–NH2 and 3β-OH (2.1 Å) ( and Movie S2). Quantitative analysis revealed that vitamin D3 reduced the contact frequency and increased the binding free energy between AmB and membrane Cho (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 A and B), indicating that the precomplexation of AmB–vitamin D3 interfered with AmB–Cho binding, particularly by impairing the hydrogen bonds required for membrane anchoring. Moreover, neither Cho extraction nor membrane insertion of AmB was observed, regardless of the absence or presence of vitamin D3 ( ).
 In the Erg-embedded membrane model, in the absence of vitamin D3, AmB anchored to Erg through the same mechanism as it anchored to Cho ( and Movie S3). No membrane insertion of AmB was observed ( ). When vitamin D3 was present, it rapidly formed a binary complex with AmB and anchored to Erg with the mycosamine group of AmB ( and Movie S4). In contrast to the behavior in the Cho-embedded membrane model, this binary complex gradually inserted into the phospholipid bilayer, forming a stable ternary complex with Erg through vdW interactions without subsequent dissociation ( ). Vitamin D3 facilitated membrane insertion by enhancing the vdW interactions between the binary complex and membrane Erg (SI Appendix, Fig. S4C). Molecules in each ternary complex aligned in parallel and were stabilized by vdW interactions (SI Appendix, Fig. S4D), and multiple complexes further assembled into a transmembrane pore: The hydrophilic polyol chain formed an inner liner, while the hydrophobic polyene chain, vitamin D3, and Erg formed an outer liner ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S4E). Quantitative analysis revealed that vitamin D3 increased the contact frequency and decreased the binding free energy of AmB and membrane Erg (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 F and G), indicating that the precomplexation of AmB–vitamin D3 enhanced AmB–Erg binding, primarily through enhanced vdW interactions with Erg. No Erg extraction was observed, regardless of the absence or presence of vitamin D3 ( ).
Moreover, vitamin D2, 25(OH)D3 and alfacalcidol also precomplexed with the polyene chain of AmB (SI Appendix, Fig. S5A). Vitamin D3 precomplexed with the polyene chains of all the tested heptaenes and degenerated heptaenes but not with tetraenes or pentaenes (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 B and C) and vitamin D3 could not modulate the binding of them to membrane sterols (SI Appendix, Fig. S5C). Hence, extracellular precomplexation of the adjuvants and PAs was essential for initiating the modulation of PA–sterol binding.
We further validated AmB and vitamin D3 precomplexation via dynamic light scattering (DLS) and ultraviolet-visible (UV-Vis) spectroscopy ( , ). DLS analysis revealed that while AmB and vitamin D3 independently formed particles of 37.55 ± 4.74 nm and 257.4 ± 46.71 nm, respectively, their mixture aggregated into distinct particles of 88.39 ± 18.97 nm ( ). In UV-Vis spectra, the AmB absorption band at approximately 329 nm exhibited a significant redshift, with the new peak intensity increasing proportionally with the vitamin D3 molar ratio ( ), indicating the formation of an AmB–vitamin D3 complex.
To clarify the nature of precomplexation, we performed detailed QM calculations on the AmB-vitamin D3 complex. In the two stable conformations identified by QM optimization, the steroid nucleus and the side chain of vitamin D3 were aligned parallel to the hydrophobic polyene chain of AmB ( ). Noncovalent interaction (NCI) analysis revealed extended green isosurfaces at the binding interface ( ), characteristic of vdW dominated interactions. Electrostatic potential analysis showed that the two molecules form a large and complementary contact interface dominated by neutral regions, with no strong charge pairing ( ). These results confirmed that the AmB–vitamin D3 complex was stabilized through hydrophobic coupling. No discernible interaction was observed between vitamin D3 and the mycosamine group or polyol chain of AmB.

## Polyene Chain Function as a Molecular Switch for Adjuvant-Mediated PA Modulation.
From MD simulations and QM calculations, we observed that upon hydrophobic coupling with vitamin D3, AmB underwent conformational rearrangements characterized by two key alterations: i) The mycosamine group reoriented, inducing a switch in AmB from a relatively constrained state to a more extended state, with the φ dihedral angle (C1′–O–C19–C18) ( ) rotated from –116.6° to –65.5° or –70.8° and the ψ dihedral angle (C2′–C1′–O41–C19) ( ) exhibiting minor changes ( ). This alteration exposed the polyene chain, which exhibited stronger vdW interactions with relatively rigid and elongated Erg than with more flexible Cho ( , ). ii) The rigidity and length of the polyene chain increased, as evidenced by an increase in its Rms fluctuation (RMSF) and an extension in length by 1.5 Å ( ), which further amplified the selectivity of AmB toward structurally compatible Erg ( ) ( , ). We further confirmed the conformational rearrangement by circular dichroism (CD) spectra. Vitamin D3 induced a hyperchromic effect and a blue shift in the AmB characteristic peaks 326 nm and 349 nm, suggesting that the polyene chain underwent a conformational rearrangement from a disordered random-coil state to a more rigid and ordered conformation ( ) ( ). Similarly, vitamin D3 hydrophobically coupled with all the tested heptaenes and degenerated heptaenes (SI Appendix, Fig. S6 A and B), as did its analogs with AmB ( ). Thus, the polyene chain of PAs functioned as a molecular switch: Hydrophobic coupling with adjuvants triggered conformational rearrangements and modulated PA–sterol binding.
 The length of the polyene chain, whether it was compatible with the adjuvants or not, was a critical determinant. Heptaenes and degenerated heptaenes possessed long hydrophobic polyene chains with lengths ranging from 13.47 ± 0.28 Å to 14.74 ± 0.24 Å, whereas those of tetraenes and pentaenes ranged from 8.52 ± 0.41 Å to 9.87 ± 0.50 Å (SI Appendix, Fig. S6C). The lengths of the adjuvants used in this study ranged from 15.37 ± 0.56 Å to 15.76 ± 0.74 Å (SI Appendix, Fig. S6C). Length compatibility with heptaenes and degenerated heptaenes allowed the adjuvants to exert their activity, whereas incompatibility with tetraenes and pentaenes prevented activity.

## Vitamin D3 Is a Valuable PA Adjuvant.
To evaluate the clinical potential of this adjuvant strategy, we assessed the ability of vitamin D3 orally administered at its clinically recommended dose (4 μg/kg/d) ( ) to attenuate nephrotoxicity and potentiate the effectiveness of intraperitoneally administered AmB across multiple mouse models ( ).
 In BALB/c mice systemically infected with C. albicans, coadministration of vitamin D3 increased the therapeutic index (median lethal dose/median effective dose; LD50/ED50) of AmB by 3.2-fold ( ). Compared with AmB (1 mg/kg/d) alone, AmB–vitamin D3 resulted in attenuated renal damage, as evidenced by a 42.42% reduction in serum creatinine (CREA) levels and a 34.17% reduction in blood urea nitrogen (BUN) levels ( ), decreased histopathological renal tubular damage (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 A and B), a 65.70% decrease in kidney injury molecule-1 (KIM-1) transcription (SI Appendix, Fig. S7C), and a 61.84% reduction in renal inflammation, as indicated by the decreased [18F]FDG signal intensity in the kidneys on microPET/CT images ( ) ( ). More importantly, compared with the same dose of attenuated liposomal AmB (L-AmB; 1 mg/kg/d or 3 mg/kg/d) ( ), AmB-vitamin D3 resulted in less or comparable renal damage (SI Appendix, Fig. S7D). In contrast to L-AmB, which reduced renal AmB distribution ( ), AmB–vitamin D3 maintained renal AmB levels comparable to those of AmB alone and was 1.7-fold higher than those of L-AmB ( ). To exclude renal damage associated with fungal infection, we assessed these renal damage indicators in uninfected mice following the same drug regimen. AmB–vitamin D3 yielded significantly lower levels than AmB alone did, with levels comparable to those elicited by L-AmB at the same dose (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 E–H).
In terms of effectiveness, vitamin D3 significantly improved survival outcomes in mice administered AmB (1 mg/kg/d), increasing the survival rate from 47.22% ± 4.81% to 91.67% ± 8.33% and extending the median survival time from 15.83 ± 5.92 d to more than 30 d, which surpassed the values achieved with L-AmB at the same dose and equaled those observed with L-AmB at 3 mg/kg/d ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S8A). Thus, using vitamin D3 as an adjuvant halved the dose of AmB required to achieve equivalent survival outcomes (SI Appendix, Fig. S8B). Compared with AmB alone, AmB–vitamin D3 treatment significantly reduced the fungal burden in the kidney, liver, brain, and spleen, resulting in accelerated systemic clearance of infection ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S8C). In mice systemically infected with A. fumigatus or C. neoformans, vitamin D3 increased the survival rates of those administered AmB by 40% or 30%, respectively ( ). In addition, a single topical application of nystatin and vitamin D3 was administered to mice with vaginal C. glabrata infection. Compared with nystatin alone, nystatin–vitamin D3 treatment significantly reduced tissue damage (SI Appendix, Fig. S8D), inflammatory infiltration (SI Appendix, Fig. S8E), and fungal load ( ).
Then we conducted a retrospective cohort study to investigate the clinical impact of vitamin D supplementation in patients receiving AmB for cryptococcal meningitis or encephalitis. After excluding patients aged <18 or those with incomplete clinical data, 66 patients were included. Among these, 22 patients receiving low-dose vitamin D supplementation (≤600 IU/d) constituted the vitamin D group, while the remaining 44 served as the control. Baseline demographic and clinical characteristics were comparable between the two groups, with the exception of albumin level, hemoglobin level, and immunosuppressant use (SI Appendix, Table S1). Compared to the control, the vitamin D group exhibited a significantly attenuated rise in serum CREA (34.65 ± 48.28 vs. 8.27 ± 21.37 μmol/L; P = 0.018) and a shorter median duration of AmB treatment (16.00 [11.25 to 21.75] vs. 20.50 [15.75 to 27.50] days; P = 0.012) ( ). Following multivariable adjustment for baseline imbalances, the vitamin D group maintained a significantly attenuated rise in serum CREA (mean difference: –27.67 μmol/L, 95% CI: –52.63 to –2.70, P = 0.030) and a shorter treatment duration (mean difference: –9.77 d, 95% CI: –17.52 to -2.02, P = 0.014) relative to the control (SI Appendix, Fig. S8 F and G). These data suggested that even low-dose vitamin D supplementation may attenuate renal damage and shorten the required duration of AmB treatment.

## Discussion
Although no newly derived PAs have reached clinical application, their irreplaceable lifesaving role has spurred decades of structural optimization ( , , , ). By leveraging a set of clinically approved drugs as adjuvants, we developed a PA optimization strategy targeting the polyene chain—diverging from structural optimization focused on the mycosamine group and polyol chain ( )—to increase the selectivity for Erg over Cho by triggering conformational rearrangements, leading to attenuated nephrotoxicity and potentiated effectiveness. This approach provides a mechanistic rationale for targeting the polyene chain in PA optimization, circumvents synthetic complexity, and establishes a conceptual framework for the rational discovery of adjuvants for PAs and other membrane-targeting amphiphilic drugs.
In addition to sterol binding, PAs are also known to damage cells by inducing an oxidative burst ( ). We found that the adjuvant reduced PAs-induced ROS in renal cells while increasing ROS in fungal cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S9). However, it is challenging to discriminate whether this represents a distinct mechanism of the adjuvant or is a consequence of mitochondrial dysfunction secondary to the modulation of sterol binding.
In the systemic circulation, the majority of vitamin D and AmB are transported by carrier proteins, such as vitamin D-binding protein (DBP) and albumin. The kidney serves as a primary organ for both AmB toxicity and systemic fungal infections, and its proximal tubules express megalin receptor that binds DBP, mediating the reabsorption of vitamin D and its metabolites ( ). Prior to binding their target at this site, vitamin D and AmB dissociate from their respective carrier proteins and exert effects through nanosecond-scale, dynamic coupling. This establishes an “Independent transport, in situ coupling” mechanism that is not constrained by specific administration route.
Here, we demonstrated that vitamin D3 offered multifaceted advantages in attenuating PAs toxicity: i) it directly coupled with the polyene chain of PAs via its intrinsic structure and hydrophobic properties to prevent nephrotoxicity, bypassing the need for its active form 1,25(OH)2D. This represents a mechanism underlying the health-promoting effects of vitamin D3; ii) it lowered the required dose of PAs via potentiating their efficacy, thereby mitigating their dose-dependent nephrotoxicity; iii) it exerted nonspecific renoprotective effects through its active form, such as inhibiting the RAAS system to induce vasodilation and anti-inflammation ( , ); and iv) it induced AmB from oligomers to polyaggregates, which were reported to possess lower hemolytic toxicity and a superior therapeutic index in vivo ( , ). These cumulative effects significantly attenuated the dose-dependent nephrotoxicity that restricts the clinical use of PAs. Also, the ability of vitamin D3 to change AmB aggregation state warrants significant attention in the future development of new drug formulations.
Given the staggering burden of lethal fungal infections ( , ), the high cost and limited accessibility of liposomal AmB ( , ), the challenges associated with developing new PAs ( ), and the established safety profile of vitamin D3, the clinical value of AmB–vitamin D3 as a life-saving, cost-effective, accessible, and safe strategy for lethal fungal infections should not be underestimated.

## Materials and Methods

## Cytotoxicity Assay In Vitro.
The cell cytotoxicity assay kit-8 (CCK-8) was conducted on HK-2, THLE-2, HEK293, and RAW 264.7 cell line. Approximately 5 × 103 cells per 100 μL were seeded onto 96-well plates and incubated overnight at 37 °C. The cells were treated with PAs (4 to 64 μg/mL; AmB, candicidin, hamycin, mepartricin A, nystatin, filipin III, and natamycin) alone or in combination with adjuvants (0.25 to 4 μg/mL; vitamin D3, vitamin D2, 25(OH)D3, and alfacalcidol) for 48 h. CCK-8 solution (MeilunBio®) was added to each well and incubated for 2 h. The absorbance was measured at 450 nm using a multifunction microplate reader (Varioskan LUX, Thermo Scientific).

## Antifungal Activity In Vitro.
The synergistic effect of adjuvants with PAs was measured by a chequerboard assay following the protocol from Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute document M27 ( ). Strains (SI Appendix, Table S2) were diluted to a final concentration of 5 × 103 cells/mL in RPMI 1640 medium (Gibco), RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum, or interstitial fluid, and treated with PAs (0.125 to 8 μg/mL; AmB, candicidin, hamycin, mepartricin A, nystatin, filipin III, and natamycin) alone or in combination with adjuvants (0.25 to 16 μg/mL; vitamin D3, vitamin D2, 25(OH)D3, and alfacalcidol) for 48 h. The vehicle-treated strains were used as a growth reference. Growth inhibition was determined by a visual and multifunction microplate reader (Varioskan LUX, Thermo Scientific). The MIC of each drug was defined as the lowest concentration of drug that resulted in no visible growth. The synergistic effect was evaluated by calculating the FIC index using MIC as follows ( ): FIC index = MICA combined/MICA alone + MICB combined/MICB alone. The value of the FIC index ≤ 0.5 was defined as a synergy. Off-scale MICs were converted to the next doubling concentration.

## ITC Assay.
ITC assay was performed to study the binding of AmB to membrane Cho/Erg in the absence or presence of vitamin D3 ( ). POPC (20 mg/mL; Avanti Polar Lipids), Cho, and Erg (4 mg/mL; APE×BIO) were prepared in a CHCl3 solution. Next, 1.2 mL of POPC solution and 350 μL of Erg or Cho solution were mixed in brown glass vials. The solvent was removed using a nitrogen stream, and the sample was stored in a vacuum environment for 12 h. The lipid film was hydrated with 1 mL of potassium buffer (5 mM HEPES, pH 7.4) and vortexed vigorously for 3 min. The lipid suspension was drawn into a Hamilton gastight syringe and passed through a 0.20 μm polycarbonate filter via a mini-extruder (Avanti Polar Lipids) more than 20 times to form LUVs. The determinations of phosphorus, Cho, and Erg contents were performed as described previously ( ).
The titration experiment was performed using the auto-ITC200 instrument (MicroCal, GE). AmB (100 μM) or AmB (50 μM)-vitamin D3 (50 μM) solution was placed in the sample cell while LUVs or LUVs-vitamin D3 (50 μM) were loaded into the injection syringe. The first injection of 0.4 μL was followed by 19 serial injections of 2 μL, with intervals of 150 s between each injection. The ITC curve, and Ka values were calculated using MicroCal Origin software.

## Fluorescence Colocalization of AmB and Vitamin D3 in Renal Cells and Fungi.
AmB and vitamin D3 were labeled with Cy5 and FITC, respectively, and their structures were confirmed by liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC–MS). HK-2 and C. albicans cells were collected and incubated with 10 μM AmB-Cy5 and varying concentrations of vitamin D3-FITC (0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, and 20 μM) at 37 °C for 1 h. The cells were washed three times to remove unbound drugs. Fluorescent images were obtained using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Olympus FV3000) with the excitation/emission wavelengths set at 650/670 nm for AmB-Cy5 and 488/525 nm for vitamin D3-FITC. Fluorescence intensity was quantified, and colocalization analysis was performed using ImageJ software from three independent experiments.

## Membrane Permeability Assays.
The percentage of K+ efflux was detected using inductively coupled plasma-atomic emission spectrometry (Thermo Scientific iCAP 7000 Series) ( ). HK-2, C. albicans, and LUVs were incubated with vehicle, vitamin D3 (5 µg/mL), AmB (1.25 µg/mL), or AmB–vitamin D3 for 4 h. The samples were centrifuged at 4,000× g for 5 min to separate the supernatant (extracellular) and pellet (intracellular). The pellet was subsequently digested with concentrated nitric acid overnight, heated, dried, and finally dissolved in sterile deionized water. The K+ concentration of the supernatant and pellet was detected to calculate the percentage of K+ efflux.
For membrane permeability to DiSC3(5) (Invitrogen) ( ), HK-2 and C. albicans cells were washed and resuspended at 2 × 107 cells/mL and incubated with DiSC3(5) (5 µM) under dark conditions for 15 min. Following this, the cells were washed thrice with PBS. HK-2 and C. albicans cells were then incubated with different drug combinations. At each point, samples were triply washed, and fluorescence intensity was determined by a multifunction microplate reader (Varioskan LUX, Thermo Scientific) at excitation/emission of 622/670 nm.

## MD Simulations of AmB, Vitamin D3, and Membrane Cho/Erg Interaction.
The phospholipid bilayers were assembled and hydrated using the Charmm-GUI Bilayer Builder (https://charmm-gui.org/). The phospholipid membranes were formed with POPC:Cho/Erg at 100:3 per layer. Water layers (50 Å) and neutralizing ions (0.15 M K+ and Cl−) were added between the two phospholipid layers. Models of POPC-Cho-AmB, POPC-Cho-AmB-vitamin D3, POPC-Erg-AmB, POPC-Erg-AmB-vitamin D3 were constructed based on the POPC-Cho/Erg membrane models with the water layers using the Molecular Operating Environment (MOE2020) program. One AmB molecule or it with four vitamin D3 were randomly inserted into POPC-Cho/Erg models. Water molecules with a distance of less than 2.5 Å from the AmB and vitamin D3 were removed. Hydrogen atoms were added to all molecules using the leap program integrated into the Amber20 package ( ). The complete system was then immersed in a rectangular box of the TIP3P water model. The force fields of Lipid17 and FF14SB were used to model POPC phospholipids and TIP3P water molecules, respectively. The general AMBER force field 2 (GAFF2) was applied to describe the behaviors of AmB, vitamin D3, and Cho/Erg.
The MD simulations were then performed by the GPU-accelerated pmemd.cuda module of Amber20. Each model was optimized as follows: i) The solvent (water molecules and neutralizing ions) was initially relaxed with 2,000 cycles of steepest descent and 2,000 cycles of conjugate gradient, while the entire POPC-Cho/Erg membrane, AmB, and vitamin D3 were constrained by a potential of 2,000 kcal/mol·Å2; ii) The entire model was then minimized using another 2,000 cycles of steepest descent and 2,000 cycles of conjugate gradient, with the heavy atoms of POPC-Cho/Erg membrane, AmB, and vitamin D3 molecules constrained by 500 kcal/mol·Å2; iii) The entire model was minimized again using 4,000 cycles of steepest descent and 4,000 cycles of conjugate gradient, without any constraint. Subsequently, the optimized models were heated from 0 to 300 K under the NVT ensemble for 200 ps, followed by another 150 ps of MD simulations to relax the system density to about 1.0 g/cm3 at 300 K under the NPT ensemble. Prolonged NPT MD simulations were further performed to produce trajectories (1.4 and 0.74 μs NPT MD simulations were performed for POPC-Cho/Erg-AmB models and POPC-Cho/Erg-AmB-vitamin D3 models, respectively) at a target temperature of 300 K under the periodic boundary condition. The cutoff for vdW and electrostatic interactions was set to 12 Å, while Langevin dynamics was used to control the system temperature (ntt = 3, gamma_in = 1.0). Additionally, the SHAKE algorithm was applied to constrain all hydrogen-containing bonds with a tolerance of 10−5.

## UV-Vis and CD Spectra Assays.
AmB and vitamin D3 were dissolved in DMSO to prepare the stock solutions. A fixed amount of AmB solution was mixed with varying ratios of vitamin D3, diluted with ddH2O to obtain sterol complexes. For the UV-Vis spectra, the final concentration of AmB was 0.02 mM. The absorbance of the samples was scanned using a TU-1950 UV–Vis spectrophotometer (Persee, Beijing, China) across the wavelength range of 250 to 500 nm ( ). For the CD spectra, the final concentration of AmB was 0.01 mM. CD spectra were recorded in a 10-mm quartz cuvette at 25 °C using the Chirascan Plus spectropolarimeter (Applied Photophysics, Leatherhead, UK) over a wavelength range of 250 to 500 nm, and the corresponding solvent baseline was subtracted from each spectrum ( ).

## DLS Assay.
Particle size was measured by dynamic light scattering using a Zetasizer Pro analyzer (Malvern Panalytical, UK) ( ). The median particle size was determined based on size distribution by number and volume. AmB and vitamin D3 (in DMSO) were mixed at a 1:1 molar ratio and diluted with deionized water to a final AmB concentration of 0.01 mM. The sample was loaded into a cuvette and equilibrated at 25 °C for 120 s prior to analysis.

## QM Calculation of AmB–Vitamin D3 Complex.
Based on the MD simulation trajectory of the AmB–vitamin D3 complex, a representative structure containing two molecules each of AmB and vitamin D3 was extracted and used as the initial model for QM calculations. 200 ps ab initio calculation was conducted using the PEB method under the density functional theory and the DZVP-MOLOPT-SR-GTH basis group. The QM calculations were performed using the CP2K software. Then, a representative structure of one AmB and vitamin D3 was extracted. QM optimization for the model was carried out by using the B3LYP-D3 method under the density functional theory ( ) and the 6-31G(d) basis group (Gaussian 16 software). The Reduced density gradient (RDG) analysis ( ) was adopted based on the wavefunction obtained from above quantum mechanics optimization to show the noncovalent interactions while the data were obtained with the aid of Multiwfn 3.8 (dev) software. The molecular visualizations were carried out using the VMD v1.9.4 (Visual Molecular Dynamics) software.

## Animal Experiments.
Female BALB/c mice aged 10 wk old (18 to 20 g) were obtained from SPF (Beijing) Biotechnology Co., Ltd. Mice were kept under standardized environmental conditions (12 h light/12 h dark cycle; 23 ± 3 °C) with free access to water and food. Animal experiments followed the 3R principles and were approved by the Animal Care and Use Committee of the Wuhan Food and Drug Safety Evaluation Center (202210178).
Nephrotoxicity experiments were conducted on mice systemically infected with C. albicans (strain SC5314; 1 × 105 cells per 20 g body weight ( ) and on uninfected mice (n = 4). Immunosuppression was induced by subcutaneous injection of cyclophosphamide (150 mg/kg) 3 d before infection. Vitamin D3 (Sigma-Aldrich) was administered by gavage at 4 μg/kg/d, starting from 3 d before infection to 8 d postinfection. AmB (Sigma-Aldrich) and L-AmB (Gilead Sciences, Inc.) were administered 24 h postinfection for 7 consecutive days. Blood samples were collected into heparinized tubes at each time point. Serum was obtained by centrifugation (4 °C, 1,000× g, 15 min) and the supernatant was used to measure CREA and BUN levels (Beckman Coulter AU5800). The right kidneys underwent histopathological examination with PAS and H&E staining. Histopathological analysis was performed according to standard protocols by two pathologists in a blinded manner, as previously described ( ). The percentage of tubules exhibiting cell necrosis, brush border loss, cast formation, and tubule dilatation was calculated and scored as follows: 0, none; 1, ≤10%; 2, 11 to 25%; 3, 26 to 45%; 4, 46 to 75%; 5, >76%. A minimum of ten fields were reviewed for each slide. The left kidneys were used to detect the transcription level of the KIM-1 by RT-qPCR. For microPET/CT imaging ( ), mice were anesthetized with pentobarbital sodium and were injected with [18F]FDG (Guangzhou HTA Isotope Medical Co. Ltd.) (10 to 11 MBq) via the tail vein at 48 h postinfection. MicroPET/CT (Inveon) scans were performed 40 min postinjection. Image analysis was conducted using the display software (Inveon Research Workplace, Siemens Medical Solutions Inc.). Regions of interest were drawn over the organs. The radioactivity density from image analysis was reported as the percent injected dose per gram (% ID/g).
For the assessment of in vivo efficacy, mice were systemically infected with C. albicans, C. neoformans (strain ATCC 32045; 1 × 105 cells;), or A. fumigatus (strain AF17; 2 × 104 cells), and administered with vitamin D3, AmB, or L-AmB as previously described ( ). The survival period of mice was recorded for 30 d. The left kidneys, liver, brain, and spleen were collected, weighed, and homogenized to determine fungal loads at each time point. The homogenates were serially diluted and plated on YPD plates. CFUs were calculated after 48 h of incubation. For the vaginal candidiasis model, β-estradiol 17-valerate (0.2 mg) was administered via subcutaneous injection 3 d before infection and 3 d postinfection ( ). Then, the mice were intravaginally inoculated with 5 × 105 cells of C. glabrata (strain ATCC 22019). A single dose of vitamin D3 (0.1 μg), nystatin (0.25 μg; APE×BIO), or nystatin-vitamin D3 was administered topically 4 d postinfection. Lavage fluid was harvested 3 d later, plated on YPD plates containing ampicillin (100 μg/mL; APE×BIO), and CFUs were calculated after 48 h of incubation. The vaginal tissues were stained with PAS and H&E for histopathological examination.
