## Abstract

## Background
The progression of bladder cancer (BC) from non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer (NMIBC) to muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC) significantly increases disease severity. Although the tumor microenvironment (TME) plays a pivotal role in this process, the heterogeneity of tumor cells and TME components remains underexplored.

## Methods
We characterized the transcriptomes of single cells from 11 BC samples, including 4 NMIBC, 4 MIBC, and 3 adjacent normal tissues. Bulk RNA-seq data were used to validate the clinical features of characteristic cells, and protein levels of these cells were further confirmed through immunohistochemistry (IHC) and multiplex immunofluorescence.

## Results
Bladder cancer progression was associated with distinct transcriptomic features in the TME. Tumor cells in MIBC displayed enhanced glycolytic activity and downregulation of chemokines and MHC-II molecules, reducing immune cell recruitment and facilitating immune evasion. This highlights glycolysis as a potential therapeutic target for disrupting tumor progression. We identified a T cell exhaustion pathway from naive CD8 + T cells (CD8 + TCF7) to terminally exhausted CD8 + STMN1 cells, with progressively declining immune surveillance. Targeting intermediate exhaustion states may restore T cell function and improve anti-tumor immunity. Macrophages polarized toward a pro-tumorigenic phenotype, while VEGFA + mast cells promoted angiogenesis in early-stage BC, suggesting their role as potential targets for therapeutic intervention in NMIBC. Furthermore, conventional dendritic cells (DCs) transformed into LAMP3 + DCs, contributing to an immunosuppressive microenvironment and enabling immune evasion.

## Conclusion
This study reveals dynamic changes in the TME during BC progression, including enhanced glycolysis, T cell exhaustion, and immune cell remodeling, which contribute to immune evasion and tumor progression. These findings identify critical pathways and cell populations as potential therapeutic targets, offering new strategies to improve treatment outcomes in BC patients.

## Introduction
Bladder cancer (BC) is a prevalent malignancy worldwide, with increasing incidence and mortality rates. According to global cancer statistics, over 500,000 new cases and more than 200,000 deaths occur annually due to BC [ ]. BC is primarily classified into non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer (NMIBC) and muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC), each exhibiting distinct pathological characteristics and clinical outcomes. NMIBC accounts for 70–80% of cases. Although it generally has a favorable prognosis, a significant proportion of NMIBC cases progress to the more aggressive MIBC [ ]. The progression from NMIBC to MIBC not only exacerbates the disease burden on patients but also significantly increases healthcare costs, emphasizing the need for a deeper understanding of the mechanisms driving this transition [ ].
The progression of NMIBC to MIBC not only exacerbates the disease burden on patients but also significantly increases healthcare costs. Therefore, understanding the biological differences between NMIBC and MIBC is crucial for developing effective therapeutic strategies and improving patient outcomes [ ]. The tumor microenvironment (TME), comprising tumor cells, stromal cells, immune cells, and their secreted cytokines and extracellular matrix, plays a pivotal role in tumor initiation, progression, immune evasion, and metastasis [ ]. In particular, the TME facilitates cancer progression by promoting immune suppression through the activation of regulatory T cells (Tregs), polarization of tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) into the M2 phenotype, and the upregulation of immune checkpoint molecules such as PD-L1 [ ]. These processes collectively impair T cell function, inhibit anti-tumor immunity, and enable cancer cells to evade immune surveillance. Moreover, hypoxic conditions and metabolic reprogramming within the TME further exacerbate immune escape mechanisms and tumor survival [ ].
The tumor microenvironment (TME), comprising tumor cells, stromal cells, immune cells, and their secreted cytokines and extracellular matrix, plays a pivotal role in tumor initiation, progression, immune evasion, and metastasis [ ]. While the TME is known to drive cancer progression through immune suppression and metabolic reprogramming, previous studies have relied heavily on bulk RNA-seq and cell-line models [ ]. Bulk RNA-seq averages signals from mixed cell populations, masking critical differences among individual cells, while cell-line models oversimplify the complexity and heterogeneity of the TME. These limitations hinder our understanding of the dynamic changes and cellular interactions within the TME during bladder cancer progression. To overcome these challenges, single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) offers a high-resolution approach to dissect epithelial and immune cell heterogeneity and their roles in shaping the TME. These findings underscore the pivotal role of the TME in driving bladder cancer progression and highlight the need for a detailed exploration of its cellular and molecular mechanisms.
Immune evasion is a hallmark of cancer progression, yet the specific mechanisms driving this process remain poorly understood. For example, the transition of CD8 + T cells from a naive state to an exhausted phenotype, characterized by diminished effector function and upregulation of inhibitory receptors, requires further investigation [ , ]. Similarly, the remodeling of myeloid cells, including macrophage polarization and functional shifts, has not been comprehensively characterized in bladder cancer. These processes are central to immune suppression in the TME but remain insufficiently explored. In addition to immune cell dynamics, epithelial cell populations, which are central players in bladder cancer, exhibit substantial heterogeneity that has not been fully characterized. While previous studies have identified general epithelial markers [ , ], they have failed to distinguish specific subpopulations with metabolic reprogramming, such as enhanced glycolysis, and their impact on immune suppression and tumor progression.
To address these limitations, this study employs single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) and bulk RNA-seq technologies to conduct an in-depth analysis of the TME during the progression from NMIBC to MIBC. By dissecting cellular composition, gene expression profiles, and intercellular interactions, we aim to uncover how epithelial cell heterogeneity, immune cell transitions such as T-cell exhaustion, and myeloid cell remodeling contribute to immune suppression and tumor progression. Furthermore, we investigate key pathways, including glycolysis activation and immune evasion mechanisms, to identify critical factors driving bladder cancer progression and to propose potential biomarkers and therapeutic targets for improving patient prognosis.

## Materials and methods

## Data collection and sample collection
The single-cell RNA-sequencing data were from our previous work and can be found in the GSA-Human database under the accession code HRA000212 and in the SRA datasets under BioProject PRJNA662018 [ ]. Bulk RNA-seq data were collected from TCGA and GEO. TCGA-BLCA dataset was obtained from UCSC Xena (https://xenabrowser.net/datapages/). GSE13507 was obtained from GEO (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/). Bladder cancer tissues from the First Affiliated Hospital of Xi’an Jiaotong University were used for immunofluorescence and Immunohistochemistry (IHC). The sample information are list on Table S1.

## Single-cell RNA-seq quality control and data integration
We processed the scRNA-seq data from 10× Genomics using Cell Ranger 7.1.0 against the GRCh38 human reference genome. The preliminary filtered data generated from Cell Ranger were then subjected to downstream analysis. Additional quality control steps were applied to cells based on four metrics: total UMI count, number of detected genes, proportion of mitochondrial gene count per cell, and erythrocyte gene expression. Specifically, cells with fewer than 2000 UMIs, more than 8000 UMIs, fewer than 500 detected genes, or more than 10% mitochondrial gene count were excluded from the analysis.
The gene expression data from individual samples were processed into a Seurat object using the Read10× function. For each sample, gene expression levels were normalized by converting them to fractions, multiplying by 10,000, and applying a natural logarithm transformation after adding 1 to avoid taking the log of zero. The top 3000 highly variable genes (HVGs) from the normalized expression matrix were identified, centered, and scaled before performing principal component analysis (PCA). Batch effects were corrected using the Harmony package (version 0.1.1) in R [ ], based on the top 50 PCA components. To remove potential doublets, cells with detected genes above 5,000 are also filtered out by DoubletFinder [ ].

## Dimension reduction and unsupervised clustering
Following batch effect correction and data integration using the Harmony package, PCA was performed on the integrated expression matrix with the RunPCA function. The first 20 principal components (PCs) were applied in the FindNeighbors function to facilitate clustering. Different resolution parameters were used for various cell types: 0.6 for all cells, 0.4 for T cells and myeloid cells, and 0.3 for epithelial cells. Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) was employed to visualize the clusters in two dimensions with the same PCs and default parameters. The FindClusters function was used to identify clusters within all cells, epithelial cells, T cells, and myeloid cells. Marker genes for each cluster were determined using the FindAllMarkers function. These marker genes were then compared with those reported in previous literature, allowing for the classification and annotation of the respective cell populations.

## CNV estimation
The InferCNV package was employed to detect CNVs in epithelial cells and to identify actual cancer cells, using the default settings (https://github.com/broadinstitute/infercnv). Two clusters, which were predominantly composed of non-malignant cells and identified through unsupervised clustering analysis, served as the control group. The CNV score for each cell was defined as the sum of the calculated CNVs for each gene within that cell.

## Differential expression analysis and gene set variation analysis (GSVA)
Differential expression analysis was conducted to compare cells from various types of BC using the FindAllMarkers function, with the parameters set to “min.pct = 0.25” and “thresh.use = 0.25.” Pathway activity in individual cells was assessed using GSVA (Gene Set Variation Analysis) with default settings in the GSVA R package (version 1.32.0). We analyzed a gene set comprising 50 hallmark pathways (h.all.v7.2.symbols.gmt), which was sourced from the Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) website (https://www.gsea-msigdb.org/gsea/index.jsp). The differential pathway activities between the groups were determined using the limma R package (version 3.42.2), with significantly perturbed pathways identified by a Benjamini-Hochberg-adjusted P value of ≤ 0.01. Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analysis was conducted to identify biological processes enriched in differentially expressed genes (DEGs) of specific cell types. The analysis was performed using the ClusterProfiler R package (version 4.2.0), focusing on biological processes (BP) from the Gene Ontology database. Enrichment significance was determined using adjusted p-values (q-values) < 0.05. Visualization of top enriched processes was generated using the dotplot function from the ClusterProfiler package.

## Cellular fraction calculation
For each sample, the cellular fraction of each major cell lineage (Epithelial cells, T/NK cells, and myeloid cells) was determined. The cellular proportions of subpopulations within these major lineages were then calculated relative to their corresponding primary immune lineages. To assess the differences in cellular fractions among different types of BC, we utilized an unpaired Wilcoxon rank sum test. P values were adjusted for multiple comparisons using the false discovery rate (FDR) method.

## Trajectory analysis
To delineate the cellular lineage trajectories of T cells, myeloid cells, and DCs, we utilized Monocle2 (version 2.14.0) and Monocle3 (version 1.3.6) [ , ]. Using the differentialGeneTest function, we selected the top 1000 signature genes with a q value < 0.001. The differentiation trajectories were subsequently inferred using Monocle’s default settings for dimensionality reduction and cell ordering. To model the CD8 T cell state transition, the diffusion map algorithm [ ], which preserves the global 20 relations and pseudotemporal ordering of cells, was applied to infer the differentiation trajectory.

## NicheNet analysis
NicheNet (version 2.1.5), a robust tool designed to predict ligands that drive transcriptomic alterations in target cells, was employed to identify potential ligands mediating the distinct phenotype of mast cells and their subsets. Following the established methodology, we utilized all expressed genes in VEGFA + mast cells as the background gene set and selected the top 50 genes ranked by log2FC as the gene of interest. Genes were considered expressed if they exhibited nonzero values in at least 10% of the cells within a given cell type. Specifically, we utilized only the expressed receptors in VEGFA + mast cells to construct the ligand-receptor interaction network and computed ligand activity using the predict_ligand_activities function.

## Cellular interaction analysis via CellChat
We utilized CellChat to investigate mast cell cellular interactions [ ]. This method infers the potential interaction strength between two cell subsets based on gene expression. The workflow began by inputting pre-processed expression profiles from the data slot of the Seurat object, along with their corresponding annotations, to create a CellChat object. CellChatDB.human was set as the ligand-receptor interaction database. We performed the CellChat analysis according to the standard procedure, using cells derived from mast cells and myeloid cells.

## SCENIC analysis
Single-cell regulatory network inference and clustering (SCENIC) [ ] was used to analyze gene regulatory networks. The workflow included three main steps: co-expression analysis, target gene motif enrichment analysis, and regulon activity evaluation. Activated regulons in mast cell subsets were analyzed using pySCENIC (v0.12.1) [ ] with raw count matrices as input. Co-expression networks were constructed using GRNboost2, identifying regulatory relationships between transcription factors (TFs) and target genes. Regulons were determined through motif enrichment analysis using RcisTarget with the hg38 motif database. Only motifs with an adjusted P-value ≤ 0.05 were retained for significance. Regulon activity was scored per cell using AUCell, with activity thresholds based on gene set size (minimum of 10 genes). Visualization of regulon activities included heatmaps and UMAP density plots to highlight differences across mast cell subtypes.

## Gene signature estimate in bulk RNA-seq
The cell signatures of distinct cell clusters were estimated utilizing the ssGSEA method implemented in the GSVA R package (version 1.48.3). This method translates specific gene expression patterns into quantitative estimates of cell populations present in individual tumor samples at the bulk level. The differently expressed genes among epithelial cells (Supplementary Table S2) and markers for epithelial cells (Supplementary Table S3) were employed as gene sets in the gsva function with the parameter “method = ssgsea”, while other parameters were set to default values. For the bulk RNA-seq cohort, we evaluated the prognostic significance of the epithelial cell, T cell, and myeloid cell signatures. Patients were stratified into high and low signature groups based on the optimal cutoff value calculated using the “surv_cutpoint” function. Kaplan–Meier survival curves were generated using the survminer (version 0.4.9) and survival (version 3.5–5) R packages.

## Multiplex immunofluorescence
The tissue samples were obtained from the First Affiliated Hospital of Xi’an Jiaotong University. The following antibodies were used to detect specific proteins: anti-EPCAM (mouse, 1:500, servicebio, GB15274-100), anti-CCND1 (rabbit, 1:1000, servicebio, GB111372-100), anti-TNFA (rabbit, 1:400, servicebio, GB11188), anti-VEGFA (mouse, 1:75, servicebio, GB14165), anti-TPSAB1 (rabbit, 1:1000, servicebio, GB15110), anti-CD3 (rabbit, 1:100, servicebio, GB13014-50), and anti-CD56 (rabbit, 1:600, servicebio, GB12041-100).

## Results

## Comprehensive scRNA-seq analysis reveals cellular heterogeneity in BC progression
The scRNA-seq fastq files included samples from 4 cases of NMIBC, 4 cases of MIBC, and 3 adjacent normal bladder tissue samples (Fig.  A, supplementary materials Table S1). Each sample was processed using Cell Ranger (version 7.1.0). To maximize cell retention, quality control was performed individually for each sample. Specifically, cells with mitochondrial content greater than 10% were removed, and to account for varying UMI distributions across samples, we retained approximately 90% of the cells for each sample. The final QC results are shown in Figure S1, with a total of 119,738 cells retained across 11 samples.
 Next, we used the harmony package to integrate the samples and employed DoubletFinder software to exclude doublets. Unsupervised clustering analysis divided the cells into 20 clusters, which were annotated using corresponding marker genes. The main cell types identified were T/NK cells (PTPRC, CD3E, CD56), epithelial cells (EPCAM), fibroblast cells (COL1A1), endothelial cells (VWF), B cells (CD79A), and myeloid cells (LYZ) (Fig.  B, C). We calculated the proportions of each cell type in the samples and found significant differences in the proportions of epithelial and immune cells between MIBC and NMIBC (Fig.  D, E). Specifically, the proportion of epithelial cells was significantly higher in MIBC compared to NMIBC, while the proportion of immune cells was higher in NMIBC. To validate this, we performed IHC staining on tissue samples, which revealed differential distribution of T (CD3+) and NK (CD56+) cells between tumor and adjacent normal tissues (Fig.  F). Notably, in NMIBC, the infiltration levels of T and NK cells in tumor tissues were similar to those in adjacent tissues, while in MIBC, the infiltration levels differed significantly (Fig.  F, supplementary Figure S2). These results highlight the crucial role of immune cells in the progression of both NMIBC and MIBC, warranting further investigation.

## Heterogeneity and functional remodeling of epithelial cells in BC progression
To further elucidate the differences of TME during progression of BC, we first concentrated on the epithelial cells of BC. The epithelial cells were reclustered into 20 clusters. Figure  A displays the differences in cell distribution across different samples and groups after clustering. We then calculated the marker genes for each cluster (Supplementary Figure S3A). Additionally, we performed GO enrichment analysis for each cluster and found that clusters 3, 6, and 16 were enriched in the ribose phosphate metabolic process, while clusters 1, 12, and 17 were enriched in cytoplasmic translation (Supplementary Figure S3B). Using the inferCNV method, we calculated the CNV values for the epithelial cells, finding that the normal group had relatively low CNV values corresponding to their clusters, which was consistent with our calculations (Fig.  B, Supplementary Figure S3C). Based on the CNV results and enrichment analysis, we classified the epithelial cells into 15 types (Fig.  C, Supplementary Figure S4A).
 Subsequently, in the calculated cell proportions, we observed that EP_OLFM4, EP_CRH, EP_CCND1, and EP_LCN15 cells were significantly enriched in MIBC, whereas EP_S100A2 and EP_CXCL1 cells were significantly enriched in NMIBC and normal groups (Fig.  D, Supplementary Figure S4B). Previous studies have also indicated the existence of heterogeneity in epithelial cells among different patients and grades [ , ]. These results suggest that epithelial cells exhibit significant differences between different types, and these cellular differences may play an important role during the progression of BC. To further substantiate the significant differences of these cells during BC progression, we used the Findallmarkers function to calculate markers for these clusters, and identified the top 10% of marker genes for each cluster as characteristic genes. Using the GSVA algorithm, we computed cell scores from the RNA-seq data of TCGA and GSE13507 datasets (Supplementary Figure S4C). We found that the EP_CCND1 is highly infiltrated in the tumor samples (Fig.  E) and showed significant differences in cell scores among different stages (Fig.  F). Moreover, survival analysis revealed that the cell group highly distributed in MIBC (EP_CCND1) was associated with poor overall survival (OS) (Fig.  G), whereas the cell groups highly distributed in NMIBC or adjacent normal tissue (EP_CXCL1, EP_CD74, EP_SNX31, EP_HS6ST3) were associated with better OS (Supplementary Figure S4D, E, F), suggesting that these two cell types have significantly different malignancy levels. Additionally, we found that the CCND1 protein of EP_CCND1 was expressed in a subset of epithelial cells (Fig.  H), confirming that EP_CCND1 cells are expressed in some tumor epithelial cells and are highly distributed in cancer tissues. This cell population may become a key focus for NMIBC treatment and represents a potential target cell type.

## Metabolic reprogramming and key molecular features of epithelial cells in BC progression
To delve into the molecular characteristics of epithelial cell types progressing in BC, we conducted differential analysis on MIBC and NMIBC cell populations, followed by GSEA enrichment analysis on the differentially expressed genes (Supplementary Table S2). The results revealed the activation of hypoxia and glycolysis-related pathways in MIBC cells (Fig.  A). Previous studies have demonstrated that during the progression of cancer, the metabolic pathways of tumor cells undergo significant changes. Specifically, HIF-1α enhances glycolytic flux by upregulating glycolytic enzymes and plays a pivotal role in angiogenesis and immune cell function [ ]. Our findings revealed that HIF1A (hypoxia inducible factor 1 subunit alpha) expression levels were higher in MIBC than in NMIBC and adjacent normal tissues (Fig.  B). Moreover, cell populations with high distribution in tumor tissues exhibited correspondingly elevated HIF1A expression (Supplementary Figure S5A), suggesting an association between tumor cell metabolism and BC progression. Notably, glycolysis-related genes, including HK1 (hexokinase 1), HK2 (hexokinase 2), and LDHA (lactate dehydrogenase A), were highly expressed in MIBC (Fig.  B, C), indicating corresponding changes in the metabolic profile of bladder tumor cells during BC progression.
 We further calculated the glycolysis score using the glycolysis gene set from BIOCARTA_FEEDER_PATHWAY (c2.cp.biocarta.v2024.1.Hs.symbols.gmt, download from https://www.gsea-msigdb.org/gsea/index.jsp) (Supplementary Table S4) and observed significant differences in the glycolysis score among different types of BC, with MIBC exhibiting a significantly higher score compared to NMIBC and adjacent normal tissues (Fig.  D, E). Furthermore, using TCGA-BLCA clinical data, cells with a positive correlation between epithelial cell scores and glycolysis-related genes were associated with poor prognosis (Fig.  F, Supplementary Figure S5B, C). These results indicate that during BC progression, bladder tumor metabolism undergoes reprogramming, with a greater reliance on anaerobic glycolysis for energy utilization, ultimately impacting patient prognosis.
Studies have shown that tumor cells regulate the composition and function of immune cells in the TME through metabolite-mediated secretion of growth factors [ ]. Additionally, tumor metabolic reprogramming can influence the composition of the TME, with glucose metabolism in cancer cells potentially exerting immunosuppressive effects on the TME [ ]. To further explore changes in epithelial cell populations during BC progression, we analyzed the expression of chemokines, cytokines, and their receptors. We found that the cell population (EP_CCND1, EP_LCN15) with high distribution in MIBC exhibited significantly higher LDHA expression than in NMIBC and adjacent normal tissues, further highlighting the role of glucose metabolism in BC progression (Supplementary Figure S5A). Notably, chemokine expression was significantly lower in MIBC compared to NMIBC and normal high-distribution cell populations (EP_CCND1) (Fig.  G). In contrast, the expression of growth factors and growth factor receptors was higher in the MIBC high-distribution cell population (EP_CCND1) (Fig.  G). Concurrently, the expression of MHC-II molecules (HLA-DPA1, HLA-DRA) was correspondingly lower (Fig.  H). Studies have indicated that increased glycolysis in tumor cells is associated with reduced expression of certain chemokines, such as CXCL10, which play a crucial role in recruiting cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) to tumor sites [ , ]. Thus, based on our analysis, we infer that the cell population with high distribution in MIBC, particularly those with enhanced glycolytic capacity, exhibit reduced secretion of chemokines, leading to decreased immune cell recruitment. Simultaneously, they downregulate their MHC-II molecule expression levels, promoting immune evasion.
Collectively, these studies further support our observation of the activation of hypoxia and glycolysis pathways in MIBC cells. HIF1A plays a critical role in the metabolic reprogramming of tumor cells, promoting tumor progression and resistance by enhancing glycolysis and regulating angiogenesis and immune evasion mechanisms. This provides new insights into the metabolic differences between MIBC and NMIBC cells and offers a theoretical basis for developing therapeutic strategies targeting tumor metabolic pathways.

## Functional remodeling of T/NK cells drives immune evasion in BC progression
Next, we explored the dynamics of immune cell lineages within the TME during the progression of BC. Since T cells are the most abundant tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes in the TME, we re-clustered T/NK cells and identified 18 clusters (Fig.  A, B, Supplementary Figure S6A). Based on previous studies and the marker genes of each cluster (Supplementary Figure S6B, C) [ , ], we annotated these clusters as follows: 2 subtypes of NK cells (NK_FCGR3A and NK_KLRC2), 8 subtypes of CD8 + T cells (CD8_HAVCR2, exhausted T cell; CD8_CCL5, exhausted T cell; CD8_STMN1, cycling T cell; CD8_GZMK, effector memory T cell; CD8_CREM, naive T cell; CD8_TCF7, naive T cell; CD8_LEF1, naive T cell), 6 subtypes of CD4 + T cells (including CD4_MAF, CD4_IL2RA, CD4_CCR7, CD4_ANK3, CD4_LTB, CD4_CXCL13), and 2 subtypes of Treg cells (Treg_CTLA4; Treg_SELL, naive-like Treg).
 Then, we calculated the cytotoxic and exhaustion scores of CD8 T cells for normal, NMIBC, and MIBC samples. Both cytotoxic and exhaustion scores were elevated in tumor samples (Fig.  C). Furthermore, the cytotoxic score was significantly higher in NMIBC than in MIBC, suggesting an early activation in tumor progression. Compared to adjacent normal samples, the proportions of effector memory T cells (CD8_GZMK) and cycling T cells (CD8_STMN1) were elevated in tumor samples, with a more pronounced increase observed in MIBC samples (Fig.  D). GZMK (Granzyme K) is a granule enzyme closely associated with the killing function of cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs). Studies have shown that an increase in CD8 + GZMK + cells indicates a stronger cytotoxic potential within the T cell population. Moreover, the number of CD8 + GZMK + cells significantly increases after anti-PD-1 treatment, indicating that these cells are one of the effector cells of anti-PD-1 therapy [ ]. In our study, CD8_GZMK cells were significantly higher in MIBC and NMIBC than in adjacent normal tissues (Fig.  D), and their infiltration abundance also increased with the grade of bladder cancer (Supplementary Figure S6D), suggesting that although CD8_GZMK cells play an important role in tumor killing, their function may affected by other cell types.
Exhausted T cells, which exhibit diminished effector functions and increased expression of inhibitory receptors such as PD-1 and TIM-3, play a critical role in tumor progression by failing to eliminate cancer cells effectively [ ]. We observed a higher fraction of Tex (CD8_HAVCR2) in both NMIBC and MIBC samples relative to adjacent tissue samples (Fig.  D). The transcription factors NR4A2 and NR4A3, which are associated with T cell exhaustion, were also highly expressed in adjacent tissue samples (Fig.  E). Recent studies have shown that NR4A transcription factors directly regulate inhibitory receptor expression, such as PD-1 and TIM-3, by binding to their promoter regions [ ]. Additionally, NR4A mediates T cell dysfunction through the NFAT signaling pathway, reinforcing the exhausted phenotype and promoting immune evasion [ ]. The observed high expression of NR4A2/3 in adjacent tissues may reflect an early regulatory mechanism by which NR4A primes T cells for dysfunction prior to tumor infiltration, consistent with their known role in promoting immune evasion. Although this hypothesis requires further validation, corresponding survival analysis indicates that a high exhausted T cell score (CD8_HAVCR2) is significantly associated with poor survival outcomes (Fig.  F), supporting the role of exhausted T cells in BC progression.
NK cells, known for their innate cytotoxicity, cytokine production, and versatility in combating various tumors, have emerged as a promising alternative immune effector. Consequently, our study shifts focus on NK cells. We found that during BC progression, the cytotoxic activity and exhaustion scores of NK cells increased, suggesting that NK cells continuously play a role in the transition from NMIBC to MIBC. The cluster NK_FCGR3A was most representative of cytotoxic cells and exhibited high expression of FCGR3A (CD16a), FGFBP2, and CX3CR1 (Fig.  B). Studies have shown that the CX3CL1-CX3CR1 axis plays a dual role in cancer, recruiting antitumoral immune cells to control tumor growth and activating a pro-tumoral response [ ]. Given the low expression of CX3CL1 in cancer cells EP_CCND1 from MIBC patients (Fig.  G), it was possible that NK_FCGR3A cells were downregulated in their recruitment into the TME by EP_CCND1, potentially resulting in immune evasion. Through cell connection analysis, we observed that the number of ligand-receptor interactions between NK cells and tumor epithelial cells (EP_CCND1) with low chemokine expression was significantly lower than that with other types of epithelial cells (Fig.  H). This suggests that tumor epithelial cells with low chemokine expression reduce their interactions with NK cells, potentially promoting tumor development.
Our study found that as bladder cancer progressed, the cytotoxic and exhaustion markers of NK cells were continuously activated. Meanwhile, the exhaustion markers and cytotoxic markers of CD8 + T cells showed an upward trend during the progression from adjacent normal tissue to cancerous tissue, but exhibited a downward trend during the progression from NMIBC to MIBC. These cellular changes and trends are characteristic of bladder cancer development.

## The transition of naive to exhausted CD8 + T cells drives immune dysfunction in BC progression
During the progression of BC, we observed that cycling T cells, particularly those expressing CD8_STMN1 and CD8_GZMK, were significantly more abundant in MIBC compared to NMIBC and adjacent non-tumor tissues (Fig.  D). Conversely, exhausted Tex cells (CD8_HAVCR2) were more prevalent in NMIBC than in MIBC. This observation suggests that changes in the functional state of CD8 + T cells are associated with tumor progression in BC. To further explore the dynamic changes of T cells during BC progression, we employed trajectory analysis using Monocle2 to analyze the development of CD8 + T cells. The results of the trajectory analysis, conducted using Monocle2, revealed that within the bladder tumor environment, CD8 + T cells can transition from naive T cells (CD8_TCF7) to exhausted or terminally exhausted T cells (Fig.  A). Additionally, the analysis with Monocle 3 indicated the path from CD8_TCF7 to CD8_STMN1 (Fig.  B). This developmental trajectory of T cells in bladder tumors was further validated using the diffusion map method (Supplementary Figure S6E), aligning with previous research findings [ , ].
 We conducted a deeper analysis of naive T cells (CD8_TCF7) within the T cell population and found that as BC progresses, these naive T cells gradually transition towards the CD8_GZMK. According to Zheng et al., TCF7 + Tex cells are more likely to represent committed Tex cells with certain stemness characteristics [ ], providing a rationale for the transition from TCF7 + to GZMK + in bladder cancer. Furthermore, we categorized the distribution of CD8_TCF7 cells based on pseudo-time and noticed that CD8_TCF7 cells can be divided into two phases. During the resting phase, CD8_TCF7 cells highly express NR4A1-3, while in the activated phase, they upregulate cytotoxic-related genes (such as GZMA, NKG7, and CCL5) and MHC-II genes (including CD74 and HLA-DRA) (Fig.  C). Additionally, we performed enrichment analysis on the differential genes of naive T cells (CD8_CREM) between tumor and normal tissues, and found that these genes played a crucial role in the regulation of leukocyte activation (Fig.  D). Moreover, naive T cells (CD8_TCF7) were associated with good prognosis, while terminally exhausted T cells (CD8_HAVCR2) were linked to poor prognosis (Fig.  E, Supplementary Figure S6F). This also suggests that naive T cells play a functional role in activating T lymphocytes and are important in early anti-tumor responses.
Synthesizing these observations, we inferred that during BC progression, the transition of T cells from the TCF7 phenotype to the GZMK phenotype may be accompanied by enhanced immune surveillance function. Furthermore, when T cells further transition from the CD8_GZMK phenotype to the exhausted CD8_HAVCR2 phenotype, their killing ability is further diminished, ultimately leading to enhanced immune evasion by tumor cells. Additionally, the poorer survival prognosis associated with high CD8_HAVCR2 distribution confirms that as naive T cells transition to the CD8_HAVCR2 and CD8_STMN1 phenotypes, their cytotoxic capabilities are indeed reduced.

## Immunosuppressive remodeling of myeloid cells during BC progression
The myeloid component in the TME exhibits significant heterogeneity. Based on canonical marker genes (Supplementary Figure S7A), it can be classified into 12 subgroups, including 3 monocyte subtypes, 6 macrophage subtypes, and 3 dendritic cell (DC) subtypes (Fig.  A, B).
 Previous studies have shown that CD14 + and CD16 + monocytes from various cancer types cluster into two main branches, suggesting minimal tissue-specific influence and heterogeneity [ ]. In our study, we observed similar findings in bladder cancer, where monocytes expressed both monocyte-related genes (CD14, CD16, FCN1) and macrophage-associated markers (CD68, CD163) (Fig.  B), indicating their transition into macrophages. Notably, Mono_CD14 and Monocyte_S100A9 were highly expressed in adjacent tissues, with both cell types expressing S100A8 and S100A9, genes associated with neutrophils (Fig.  C). This suggests these monocytes are likely early infiltrating cells, as they also exhibit high expression of inflammatory cytokines, chemokines (IL1B, CCL4, CXCL2, CXCR4), growth regulators (AREG, EGR1), tissue-resident markers (NR4A1, NR4A2, NLRP3), and nuclear factor κB signaling genes (NFKB1, NFKBIA) (Fig.  D).
Next, we focused on macrophages. Recent studies have highlighted significant transcriptomic heterogeneity among macrophage subsets across different cancer types, warranting separate analysis to better characterize their properties [ ]. Tissue-resident interstitial macrophages generally display gene expression profiles between monocyte-derived and alveolar macrophages, with the local microenvironment playing a crucial role in their differentiation and function [ , ]. However, in bladder cancer, marker genes for tissue-resident interstitial macrophages (FABP4, MCEMP1, MARCO) were not expressed (Fig.  B), suggesting that the macrophages present are primarily tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs). We identified several TAM subtypes in bladder cancer, including SPP1 + TAM, FOLR2 + TAM, MKI67 + TAM, and C1QC + TAM, LY6D + TAM.
Macrophages are traditionally classified into three subtypes in vitro: M0 (non-polarized), M1 (pro-inflammatory/anti-tumor), and M2 (anti-inflammatory/pro-tumor) [ ]. To further characterize the phenotype of macrophage subsets in bladder cancer, we calculated M0, M1, and M2 signature scores using CIBERSORT gene signatures [ ]. We found that several macrophage subsets expressed both M1 and M2 signatures (Supplementary Figure S7B). During BC progression, these macrophages exhibited significant phenotypic changes, with the majority showing a shift towards an M2-like phenotype (Supplementary Figure S7B). Particularly, Macro_SPP1, Macro_FOLR2, and Macro_C1QC had higher M2 scores, while in adjacent normal tissues, Macro_CRH had a higher M0 signature, and macro_MKI67 was skewed towards an M1 phenotype. However, in NMIBC and MIBC, MKI67 + macrophages shifted towards an M2-like phenotype, indicating a high degree of heterogeneity and functional transformation in macrophages during tumor progression.
Building on Cheng et al.’s study, which defined TAMs with dichotomous functional phenotypes [ ], we evaluated the angiogenic and phagocytic capabilities of the identified macrophage subsets, and observed that these traits varied across different stages of BC. In adjacent tissues, macro_SPP1 exhibited a phagocytic phenotype in NMIBC but shifted to an angiogenic phenotype in MIBC. As bladder cancer progresses, the proportions of Macro_LY6D, Macro_CCL13, and Macro_MKI67 increased, with Macro_LY6D and Macro_MKI67 consistently displaying angiogenic characteristics. Using TCGA-BLCA clinical data, we assessed the relationship between the signatures of LY6D + , and MKI67 + macrophages and patient prognosis, finding that higher signatures of Macro_LY6D, Macro_MKI67, and Macro_CCL13 were associated with poor prognosis (Fig.  F).
Trajectory analysis suggested two distinct differentiation pathways for monocytes: one leading from monocyte_CD14 to macro_C1QC, and another from other monocytes to macro_SPP1 and macro_FOLR2, with macro_LY6D as an intermediate state (Fig.  G). Previous studies have shown that macro_SPP1 is an immunosuppressive macrophage subtype, with its reduction associated with better responses to immunosuppressive therapies [ ]. LY6D, highly expressed in esophageal and head and neck cancers, has also been implicated in promoting tumorigenesis in skin basal cell carcinomas through interactions with TREM2 + myeloid cells [ ]. Therefore, the transition of macrophages in the bladder tumor microenvironment towards an angiogenesis-associated phenotype is closely linked to tumor progression, with pro-angiogenic macrophages contributing to the formation of an immunosuppressive microenvironment that facilitates bladder cancer development.

## Pro-angiogenic role of mast cells in early-stage BC progression
Next, we analyzed the Mast cell in BC samples. Previous studies have shown that mast cells in tumors may play a dual role in influencing the fate of tumor cells, with their effects varying across different types of cancer [ ].VEGFA and TNF are important markers for distinguishing mast cells. Tumors with TNF-enriched mast cells tend to have better patient outcomes [ ]. Mast cell-derived VEGFA is a key gene associated with tumor angiogenesis. Through the production and release of VEGFA, basic fibroblast growth factor (bFGF), and transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β), mast cells promote the formation of blood and lymphatic vessels [ ].
In our analysis of mast cell subtypes, we found that VEGFA was predominantly expressed in mast cells from adjacent normal tissues (Fig.  A, Supplementary Figure S8A). This is consistent with Cheng et al.’s findings, where tumors with VEGFA-enriched mast cells had poor prognoses [ ]. Mast-derived TNF has been reported to enhance the functionality of CD8 + dendritic cells (DCs) and facilitate the priming of CD8 + T cells, thereby improving anti-tumor capacity [ ]. We further classified mast cells in bladder tumors into 5 clusters (Fig.  B) and found that VEGFA expression varied significantly across different BC tissue types. Higher VEGFA expression was observed in mast cells from adjacent normal tissues compared to cancer tissues (Fig.  C), suggesting that VEGFA-enriched mast cells may play a promoting role in BC progression. IHC staining of tumor samples further confirmed the existence of VEGFA + mast cell in BC (Fig.  D).
 To further compare mast cells in different tissue types, we analyzed the ratio of VEGFA to TNF expression and found a dramatically higher frequency of VEGFA + mast cells compared to TNF + mast cells in adjacent normal tissues (ratio = 3.25) (Fig.  E). This suggests a higher tumor-angiogenesis capacity and indicates that mast cell-derived VEGFA may play a significant role in promoting bladder tumor progression. To validate our findings, we utilized clinical data from TCGA-BLCA and confirmed a significant correlation between the presence of mast cells and poor prognosis in bladder tumors (Fig.  F). In contrast, Cheng et al. found that in nasopharyngeal cancer (NPC) patients, mast cells were primarily TNF + and were associated with a better prognosis. Therefore, our study confirms that VEGFA-dominant mast cells, through their pro-angiogenic effects, play a crucial role in the progression of bladder tumors.
To further investigate the reasons for the enrichment of VEGFA + mast cells in the microenvironment, we utilized SCENIC to detect regulatory networks and found that NFKB regulons were significantly enriched in VEGFA + mast cells (Fig.  G, Supplementary Figure S8B). Studies have shown that NF-κB regulates VEGFA expression by increasing hypoxia-inducible factor-1 (HIF-1) transcription [ ], further suggesting that NFKB may be a key driver of VEGFA + mast cells. Then, we compared the transcriptomic profiles of VEGFA + and VEGFA- mast cells, and performed NicheNet analysis using genes highly expressed in VEGFA + mast cells. We identified IL-1B, highly expressed by TAMs, as a potential ligand driving the phenotype of VEGFA + mast cells (Fig.  H). Zhang et al. identified the IL1B-ADRB2 ligand-receptor pair as significantly enriched between mast cells and macrophages. ADRB2 has been reported to play a critical role in preventing the release of pro-inflammatory mediators from mast cells [ ]. In addition, we identified SPP1 and CD44 as other important ligand-receptor pairs between mast cells and macrophages (Fig.  I, J). Studies have shown that mast cells from CD44-deficient mice exhibited inhibited growth during co-culture compared to those from wild-type mice in vitro [ ]. Therefore, CD44 plays a crucial role in the growth of mast cells and may contribute to the enrichment of VEGFA + mast cells in bladder tumors. We can inferred that the aggregation of mast cells in bladder tumors is largely associated with macrophage-driven enrichment of VEGFA + mast cells. Macrophages, particularly those expressing FOLR2 and SPP1, promote the accumulation of VEGFA + mast cells, thereby contributing to the formation of a pro-angiogenic tumor microenvironment.
In summary, the results indicate that while mast cells significantly contribute to angiogenesis during the early stages of bladder cancer, their role diminishes as the tumor progresses. This suggests that mast cells are more influential in the initial formation and metastasis of bladder cancer, but their pro-tumor activity wanes in later stages. Consequently, targeting mast cells could be a promising approach to improving the effectiveness of early-stage bladder cancer treatments.

## Activation and immunosuppressive role of dendritic cells in BC progression
Dendritic cells (DCs) in BC were classified into three subtypes: conventional DCs (cDC_CD1C, cDC_CLEC9A) and LAMP3 + DCs (DC_LAMP3 +). LAMP3 + DCs, also known as “mature DCs enriched in immunoregulatory molecules” (mregDC), exhibit features of maturity (LAMP3), migration (CCR7, FSCN1), and immunoregulation (CD274, IDO1, PDCD1LG2, CD200) [ ]. These cells are characterized by a downregulation of Toll-like receptors (TLRs) and an upregulation of immune-regulatory genes (CD274, PDCD1LG2, CD200), which play crucial roles in modulating T cell responses (Fig.  A).
 In our study on BC, we observed that LAMP3 + cDCs exhibited an activated phenotype with a higher migratory capacity (Fig.  B), consistent with findings in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) by Zhang et al. [ ]. LAMP3 + DCs have been reported to interact with tumor-infiltrating Treg cells and inhibit CD8 + T cell-mediated tumor immunity, likely through the expression of immunosuppressive molecules such as PD-L1 and the production of cytokines like IL-4 [ ]. Trajectory analysis suggested that LAMP3 + DCs in bladder tumors may be derived from conventional DCs (cDCs) (Fig.  C), aligning with recent reports that mregDCs originate from cDC1s and cDC2s after tumor antigen uptake. Compared to adjacent normal tissues, tumor tissues had higher antigen-presenting signatures but also exhibited a higher immunosuppressive score, particularly in MIBC and high-grade tumors, where LAMP3 + DCs showed higher infiltration (Fig.  D). Using TCGA-BLCA clinical data, we found that LAMP3 + DC scores were significantly higher in tumor tissues than in normal tissues (Fig.  E). Previous studies have shown that cDC2-derived LAMP3 + DCs interact with Treg cells and suppress CD8 + T cells, promoting tumor immune evasion and metastasis [ ]. Our earlier research also reported a significant correlation between LAMP3 + DCs and Treg cell infiltration [ ]. Moreover, using SCENIC to explore the regulatory network, we found that a series of regulons related to LAMP3 + DCs, including STAT5A and CEBPB (Fig.  F). Furthermore, the STAT5A and CEBPB regulon signatures are significantly related to LAMP3 + DCs in TCGA-BLCA (Fig.  G). The results suggest that, despite the potential convergence of the two cDC subsets into LAMP3 + cDCs upon differentiation in BC, they retain distinct transcriptomic characteristics, possibly leading to diversification of their functions.
In summary, the progression of bladder cancer is associated with a shift from conventional DCs to LAMP3 + DCs, leading to the formation of an immunosuppressive microenvironment that promotes tumor immune evasion. This shift, characterized by increased mature DCs and reduced antigen-presenting capacity, contributes to the further development and potential metastasis of bladder cancer.

## Discussion
BC is primarily classified into two distinct types: NMIBC and MIBC, each exhibiting unique pathological characteristics and clinical outcomes. The progression of NMIBC to MIBC not only intensifies the disease burden on patients but also leads to a significant increase in healthcare costs. In this study, we focused on analyzing the biological differences between NMIBC and MIBC through single-cell transcriptomics, specifically examining the remodeling of key cellular components in the TME across different BC types and adjacent tumor tissues (Fig.  ). We further validated our findings with bulk RNA-seq and immunohistochemistry.
 We identified distinct transcriptional signatures in epithelial cells from different types of BC. Notably, epithelial cells in MIBC exhibited enhanced glycolysis, a metabolic shift often driven by hypoxia and mediated by HIF-1α. This shift supports the high energy demands of cancer cells, promoting tumor progression and impacting the immune response [ , ]. Glycolysis in tumor cells increases lactate production, which accumulates in the TME, impairing CTL activation and function, as well as the function of other immune cells like dendritic cells (DCs) and natural killer (NK) cells [ , ]. Our analysis revealed that MIBC cells with enhanced glycolysis showed elevated expression of glycolysis-related genes (HK1, HK2, LDHA) and increased levels of PDL1, while MHC-II expression was reduced, suggesting that glycolysis may contribute to immune evasion.
This dual mechanism, enhanced glycolysis and MHC-II suppression, aligns partially with findings in non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), where tumor cells also utilize glycolysis to suppress CTL activity [ ]. However, our study uniquely identifies a concurrent suppression of MHC-II expression in glycolytic bladder cancer cells, indicating a distinct immune evasion strategy compared to NSCLC. Key glycolytic enzymes, such as hexokinase-2 (HK2), have been shown to activate the NF-κB pathway, upregulate PDL1 expression, and suppress antigen presentation [ ]. Enhancing antigen presentation through the MHC-II pathway, or targeting glycolysis by inhibiting HK2 or modulating lactate metabolism, could represent potential therapeutic strategies to restore T cell-mediated immunity and improve responses to immunotherapy [ ].
Changes in cytotoxic T cells during tumor progression, such as the increase in CD8_GZMK cells, suggest that these cells, which are associated with immune surveillance, may play a critical role in bladder cancer progression. Our trajectory analysis revealed a progressive transition of CD8 + T cells from naive (CD8_TCF7) to terminally exhausted (CD8_STMN1) states, with intermediate effector memory populations (CD8_GZMK and CD8_HAVCR2) retaining partial cytotoxic functionality. These findings align with previous studies that identified GZMK + T cells as key mediators of immune surveillance before exhaustion. For instance, Zheng et al. demonstrated distinct exhaustion pathways across multiple cancer types, highlighting GZMK + T cells as a pivotal transition state in the tumor microenvironment [ , ]. Similarly, Hu et al. revealed that GZMK + CD8 + T cells undergo significant transcriptional reprogramming during immunotherapy, underscoring their role in modulating treatment response [ ]. Considering the role of the GZMK + pathway in T cell exhaustion, therapies targeting this transition may enhance the effectiveness of immunotherapy in BC. However, their function likely depends on interactions with other immune cells. We also acknowledge the potential limitations of our study, including the sample bias due to untreated patient samples.
The NR4A family, particularly NR4A2 and NR4A3, has been shown to play a critical role in regulating T cell exhaustion by directly binding to the promoter regions of inhibitory receptors such as PD-1 and TIM-3 [ ]. Additionally, NR4A mediates key metabolic shifts, such as increased glycolysis and reduced oxidative phosphorylation, which reallocate energy resources in T cells and reinforce the exhausted state [ ]. High NR4A2/3 expression observed in our study suggests that NR4A may prime T cells for dysfunction prior to tumor infiltration, thereby facilitating immune evasion. Beyond their role in T cell exhaustion, NR4A transcription factors also regulate Treg cell development and function by directly enhancing Foxp3 expression, a master regulator of Tregs [ ]. This promotes Treg differentiation and stabilization, contributing to immune homeostasis but also to immunosuppression in the tumor microenvironment. In BC, NR4A-regulated pathways likely play a dual role, driving both T cell exhaustion and Treg-mediated immunosuppression, thereby reinforcing tumor progression. The dual role of NR4A in driving T cell exhaustion and stabilizing Treg-mediated immunosuppression underscores its central role in shaping the immunosuppressive TME during bladder cancer progression. Targeting NR4A-regulated pathways could provide a novel therapeutic strategy to reinvigorate cytotoxic T cell function and modulate Treg activity. Future studies employing in vitro and in vivo models will be essential to validate these mechanisms and explore their potential for improving immunotherapy outcomes in bladder cancer.
Previous studies have shown that tumors enriched with VEGFA + mast cells are associated with poor patient outcomes [ ]. In BC, VEGFA + mast cells are predominant, while TNF + mast cells are minimally enriched. Our study further confirmed a significant association between VEGFA + mast cells and immunosuppressive macrophages, such as macro_SPP1, suggesting that this interaction may contribute to tumor progression and the poor prognosis observed in BC patients. VEGFA + mast cells and immunosuppressive macrophages, such as SPP1 + macrophages, have been implicated in promoting angiogenesis in multiple solid tumors, including breast and ovarian cancers [ , ]. Our findings further support this role in bladder cancer, where these cells may act synergistically to enhance tumor progression. This highlights the importance of targeting these pro-angiogenic cell populations as a potential therapeutic strategy to disrupt tumor vascularization and improve patient outcomes.
In the TME, myeloid cells, including LY6D + macrophages, exhibit significant heterogeneity. In bladder cancer, macrophages such as macro_SPP1 and macro_LY6D play a role in promoting angiogenesis, contributing to tumor progression. These findings suggest that pro-angiogenic macrophages are key players in the remodeling of the TME during bladder cancer progression. Further research is needed to elucidate the specific mechanisms by which these cells contribute to microvascular formation within the TME.
Based on the analysis above, BC progression exhibits distinctive features of TME remodeling. We propose the concepts of “positive response” and “negative response” within the immune environment’s influence on tumor cells. The “positive response” refers to an environment that prevents tumor cells from progressing into more malignant states, thereby inhibiting the development of MIBC. In this state, cytotoxic immune cells, particularly CD8 + T cells and NK cells, are activated, reducing tumor immune evasion, and suppressing angiogenesis and metastasis. Tumor cells in this environment express MHC-II molecules, facilitating antigen presentation and the activation of CD8 + T cell-mediated cytotoxicity. Conversely, in the “negative response” environment, pro-angiogenic mast cells play a promotive role in the early stages of tumor development, while metabolic reprogramming in tumor cells, particularly through glycolysis, produces byproducts that remodel the surrounding immune microenvironment. This shift contributes to the exhaustion of effector T cells, including their transition into terminally exhausted states, and an increase in immunosuppressive macrophages (such as macro_SPP1 and macro_LY6D), collectively promoting tumor immune evasion. Overall, in patients prone to progression to MIBC, the TME predominantly exhibits a “negative response”, where numerous immune cells evolve towards immunosuppressive phenotypes, and tumor-reactive T cells advance towards exhaustion and terminal exhaustion.
This study has three major limitations. First, the limited sample size in our single-cell data may have restricted the statistical power of some findings, potentially affecting the robustness of our conclusions. Future studies with larger datasets are needed to validate and expand on these results. Second, the bladder tumor samples used in this study were obtained from different patients, introducing potential heterogeneity that could influence the outcomes. To address this, future research should consider using tumor samples of varying grades from the same patient, which would help to minimize the impact of sample heterogeneity. Third, while CD4 + T cells play a critical regulatory role in the immune response, our study did not thoroughly investigate their function, particularly in relation to how they influence the cytotoxic activity of CD8 + T cells. Further research is necessary to explore the interactions between CD4 + and CD8 + T cells in the context of tumor immunity, which could provide deeper insights into immune regulation in bladder cancer.
This study has three major limitations. First, the limited sample size in our single-cell data may affect the statistical power of some findings. Future studies should include larger multi-center datasets to increase the generalizability of our conclusions. Integrating single-cell transcriptomics with complementary approaches, such as spatial transcriptomics, could provide additional validation and improve mechanistic insights. Second, the heterogeneity among patient samples, as they were obtained at different stages and from different individuals, may influence the outcomes. Future research should prioritize paired or longitudinal sampling from the same patients to minimize variability and better capture the dynamics of TME remodeling. Third, functional validation of key findings, such as the role of glycolysis in immune evasion or the contribution of GZMK + T cells to tumor progression, is still lacking. Experimental studies using co-culture systems or in vivo models will be essential to confirm these mechanisms and their potential as therapeutic targets.

## Conclusion
Our scRNA-seq analysis reveals key dynamics of the TME during BC progression. We identified unique epithelial cell subsets, such as EP_CCND1 cells, which exhibit enhanced glycolysis and reduced chemokine expression, contributing to immune evasion. Additionally, the progression of BC was associated with a transition of T cells from naive to terminally exhausted states, emphasizing the critical role of CD8_GZMK cells in immune surveillance. VEGFA + mast cells were found to promote tumor growth in early BC stages, while conventional DCs shifted to immunosuppressive LAMP3 + DCs during later stages. These findings provide a deeper understanding of the dynamic changes in the TME, shedding light on potential therapeutic strategies for bladder cancer. Future studies are warranted to validate these findings in larger cohorts and explore their applicability in broader cancer types, ultimately aiming to improve treatment outcomes for BC patients.
