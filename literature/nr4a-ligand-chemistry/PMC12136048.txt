## Abstract
Fibroblastic reticular cells (FRCs) are specialized fibroblasts that construct secondary lymphoid organs where they provide crucial signals for immune cell homeostasis and migration. While splenic FRCs are thought to support antiviral T cell responses, their role remains unclear. Here, we found that ablation of splenic FRCs impaired virus-specific CD8+ T cell responses during lymphocytic choriomeningitis virus (LCMV) infection. Immunofluorescence imaging revealed that FRCs promote CD8+ T cell clustering with type 1 conventional dendritic cells (cDC1) in the T cell zone before migration to the infected marginal zone. Without FRCs, T cells instead clustered with cDC1 and virus-infected cells in the marginal zone, leading to suboptimal priming. Mechanistically, FRCs coordinated early viral replication and the inflammatory milieu for optimal DC activation, and an intact FRC network was crucial for generating effector T cells and maintaining protective memory T cells. Thus, splenic FRCs provide essential lymphoid niches for antiviral T cell responses.

Fibroblastic reticular cells of the spleen coordinate inflammation for optimal antiviral T cell responses and protective memory.

## INTRODUCTION
The spleen is responsible for protection against systemic infections, facilitating capture and detection of pathogens present in the blood and the generation of immune responses ( ). The spleen is compartmentalized into red pulp (RP) and white pulp (WP), with these two anatomical zones separated by the marginal zone (MZ). The RP and MZ are rich in macrophages, including CD169+ MZ macrophages, and other innate immune cells, including type I conventional dendritic cells (cDC1s), monocytes, neutrophils, and natural killer (NK) cells, forming a first line of defense during infection. The WP acts as a hub where antigen presentation occurs with cDCs presenting antigens to naïve T cells in the T cell zone. During systemic viral infections, such as lymphocytic choriomeningitis virus (LCMV) infection, the organization of these anatomical spleen niches is important for pathogen control and cytokine responses ( ). In particular, type I interferon (IFN-I) production requires an organized splenic anatomy. In mice where the spleen architecture is disturbed, including the loss of lymphotoxin β receptor (LTβR), loss of LTα/β, or deletion of LTβ in B cells, viral replication and IFN-I responses are perturbed ( – ).
The splenic architecture and its functionality rely on a supportive network of stromal cells. Among the different types of stromal cells, splenic fibroblasts play a pivotal role by producing and ensheathing extracellular matrix components for the structure of the spleen that is also required for immune cell migration ( ). Subsets of fibroblasts have been identified in the spleen, based on anatomical location and functionality. Within the WP of the spleen, immune-fibroblasts called fibroblastic reticular cells (FRCs) that express Bst1 (CD157) and vascular cell adhesion molecule–1 (CD106) originate from CCL19+ mesenchymal precursor cells that require LTβR signaling for their development and maintenance ( , ). FRCs are heterogeneous, with subsets exhibiting distinct functions and locations. Within B cell follicles, B zone reticular cells include follicular DCs and other CXCL13-producing fibroblasts that support this compartment ( , ). In the T cell zone, T zone reticular cells produce CCL19, CCL21 to attract and retain DCs and T cells, facilitating their interactions, and interleukin-7 (IL-7) that promotes T cell survival ( – ). Supporting the MZ niche are marginal reticular cells that express CXCL13 and receptor activator of nuclear factor kappa-Β ligand ( ). Together, this diverse fibroblast architecture supports splenic functions and contributes to protective immunity via mechanisms that remain incompletely understood.
Inflammation alters the activation state and functions of lymph node FRCs that contribute to immune responses ( – ). Ablation of FRCs was found to inhibit the induction of T and B cell responses in lymph nodes ( – ). This suggests an important role for FRCs in mounting immune responses. However, removal of FRCs precipitates rapid loss of lymphocytes from lymph nodes within 1 to 2 days, thereby hampering detailed assessment of immune response generation in the absence of FRCs ( , ). In the spleen, lymphocytes traffic through distinct niches supported by RP fibroblasts or WP FRCs. Spleen CCL19+ FRCs guide lymphocyte and DC migration into and within the splenic WP via CCR7-dependent migration ( , ). Loss of the CCR7 ligands CCL21 and CCL19 in plt/plt mice was found to have only a minor impact on antiviral immune responses in the spleen, despite shifting T-DC interactions from the T cell zone to the MZ ( ) and also despite the capacity for CCL19 and CCL21 to assist with DC maturation ( ). This implicates additional mechanisms by which spleen FRCs support immune responses. We previously identified that during acute LCMV infection, spleen FRCs undergo a profound reprogramming and switch their homeostatic program to immune support ( ). Despite these insights, the extent of FRC contributions to immune responses during pathogen infection in the spleen remains poorly understood.
Here, we investigated the role of splenic FRCs in regulating the priming of CD8+ T cells during LCMV infection. We reveal the impact of FRC-orchestrated viral replication and inflammation that support DC activation and FRC-dependent generation of effector and memory antiviral T cells. Therefore, our results reveal how spleen FRCs contribute to the coordination of antiviral immune responses.

## RESULTS

## Splenic FRCs support the architecture of the WP
We and others have previously shown that CCL19-Cre x ROSA26–enhanced yellow fluorescent protein (eYFP) mice enable identification of splenic WP FRCs with minimal YFP expression in other stromal cell subsets ( , ). Here, to investigate the role of FRCs in immune responses, we crossed CCL19-Cre mice to ROSA26-inducible diphtheria toxin receptor (iDTR) mice (CCL19-DTR) enabling selective FRC depletion. To this end, CCL19-DTR (Cre+DTR) and control (Cre−DTR littermates) mice were injected with DT, and the splenic compartment was analyzed posttreatment ( ). DT treatment completely ablated WP CD157+ FRCs ( , and fig. S1A). Besides a small reduction in glial cells and an increase in pericytes, the cellularity of other subsets of stromal cells, including RP reticular cells, mesothelial, adventitial reticular cells, and blood endothelial cells was not changed following DT treatment ( ). Confocal microscopy confirmed the depletion of WP FRCs, including PDPN+CD157+ FRCs in the T cell zone and PDPN−CD157+ FRCs in B cell follicles ( , top). Because FRCs play a pivotal role in maintaining WP homeostasis by secreting chemokines and cytokines crucial for compartmentalization and immune cell survival, we performed quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) analysis on control and FRC-depleted spleens. Expression of the homeostatic chemokines Ccl19, Ccl21, and Cxcl13 was abrogated in the spleen of FRC-depleted mice, while expression of the T and B cell survival factors Il7 and Baff was significantly reduced ( ). Previous studies showed that FRC depletion leads to a paucity of CD45+ immune cells in LNs ( , , ). We thus analyzed the immune compartment of the spleen but found no significant loss of lymphocytes or myeloid cells, indicating that immune cells were retained in the spleen shortly after FRC ablation ( and fig. S1B). Confocal microscopy further revealed alterations to the architecture of the WP with B and T cells accumulating in the RP in CCL19-DTR mice ( , bottom). CD169+ MZ macrophages were retained after FRC depletion but found to be disorganized among T and B cells. Intravascular labeling of CCL19-DTR mice with anti-CD45 antibody, which only labels RP cells within the short-term labeling period ( ), confirmed an increased proportion of T lymphocytes accumulated in the RP of the spleen in FRC-depleted mice ( and fig. S1C). Collectively, our data confirmed that CCL19-DTR mice allow specific deletion of WP FRCs, leading to disruption of the architecture of the WP and displacement of T cells into the RP. This facilitated investigation of the induction of immune responses in the spleen when FRCs were absent but immune cells were present.
 

## Splenic FRCs facilitate the initiation of CD8+ T cell responses to viral infection
We then investigated how FRCs regulate T cell responses to acute virus infection in the spleen following systemic infection with LCMV Armstrong. For this, we transferred LCMV-specific TCR transgenic CD8+ P14 T cells into CCL19-DTR and control mice, treated with DT, and then infected these mice and analyzed the spatiotemporal dynamics of T cell priming. In Cre− mice, P14 T cells formed clusters in the T cell zone within 1.5 days of infection, followed by expansion and migration toward infected cells in the MZ and the RP between day 2.5 and 3 postinfection. In stark contrast, in CCL19-DTR mice, P14 T cells formed clusters at day 1.5 postinfection within the MZ where foci of infected cells were localized ( ). Localization of P14 T cells with virus-infected cells persisted for at least 3 days, but the P14 T cell accumulation was reduced compared to control mice, suggesting impaired expansion. Injection of CTV-labeled P14 T cells into mice revealed significantly reduced proliferation and decreased expression of the early activation marker CD25 (IL-2Ra) in FRC-depleted mice 3 days after infection ( ).
 To explore how FRCs supported T cell responses, we examined how the absence of FRCs influenced global responses to infection in the spleen assessed by single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq). We sort-purified stromal cells, myeloid cells, tdTomato+ P14 T cells, and total splenocytes from infected control and FRC-depleted mice at day 1.5 postinfection, corresponding to when P14 T cells formed clusters and thus received early activating signals ( and fig. S2A). We annotated 28 clusters encompassing subsets of stromal cells, T and B lymphocytes, DCs, and myeloid cells ( and fig. S2, B and C). As expected, FRCs were absent from Cre+DTR mice, accompanied by wide-spread alterations to the proportions of T cell, DC, and myeloid cell clusters in this cohort ( and fig. S2D). Gene expression analysis revealed higher up-regulation of gene modules involved in responses to virus, cell proliferation, and inflammatory responses in control mice ( and fig. S2E). Notably, gene modules associated with responses to viruses were enriched in neutrophils, stromal cells, and DCs, and proliferation genes were enriched in stromal cells and T cells, highlighting that these cell types are the early responders to LCMV infection that are affected by the loss of FRCs ( ).
 We next examined the impact FRC depletion on T cell activation following LCMV infection. We identified five distinct T cell clusters comprising naïve and activated T cells ( ). Two naïve clusters were defined by Tcf7, Il7r, Sell (CD62L), S1pr1, and Klf2 expression, and three activated T cell clusters with high Gzmb, Il2ra, and Irf4 expression (fig. S2F). The T cell–activated cluster 3 had higher expression of effector genes, including Xcl1 and Ifng, while the T cell–activated cluster 1 had higher expression of cell cycle genes, including Mki67, suggesting cells entering division. Few activated T cells were observed in FRC-depleted mice, indicating impaired T cell activation in the absence of FRCs (fig. S2G). TdTomato expression revealed that P14 T cells were found predominantly in the activated T clusters in mice with FRCs, whereas in FRC-depleted mice, P14 T cells were associated with the naïve T cell clusters ( ). Subclustering of tdTomato+ P14 T cells revealed that cells from control mice were enriched for genes associated with activated T cells and cell division, whereas splenic P14 T cells from mice lacking FRCs were enriched for naïve T cell genes and defense response genes ( ). Differential gene expression analysis demonstrated up- or down-regulation of 1678 genes by P14 cells in control compared to FRC-depleted mice ( ), including up-regulation of Il2ra, Gzmb, Cxcl9, and Ifng. Conversely, P14 cells from FRC-depleted mice expressed S1pr1 and Klf2 ( ). Our analysis thus suggested that early T cell activation during LCMV infection was substantially impaired in the absence of CCL19+ FRCs.

## FRCs regulate T-DC interactions and DC activation in vivo
To understand why T cell activation was impaired in the spleen in the absence of FRCs, we investigated how FRCs facilitated T-DC interactions early post-LCMV infection. We first confirmed CD11c+ DC-dependent T cell priming in this infection model using CD11c-DTR mice (fig. S3A). Because CD11c is also expressed in metallophillic and MZ macrophages that are depleted in the CD11c-DTR model ( , ), we used CD169-DTR mice and confirmed that depletion of CD169+ cells did not impair P14 cell activation (fig. S3B). To visualize T-DC interactions, we generated bone marrow chimeric mice by reconstituting irradiated CCL19-DTR mice with bone marrow from XCR1-Venus mice. In control mice, clustering P14 T cells transiently interacted with XCR1+ cDC1 and XCR1− CD11c+ cDCs in the T cell zone at day 1.5 postinfection ( , and fig. S3C). By day 2.5 postinfection when P14 T cells engaged with infected cells in the MZ, XCR1+ cDC1s were not recruited to the clusters of P14-infected cells. In contrast, in CCL19-DTR mice, localization of P14 cells with virus-infected cells was accompanied by the recruitment of XCR1+ cDC1s and XCR1− CD11c+ DCs to foci of virus infection between days 1.5 and 2.5 postinfection ( ). These data revealed that in FRC-ablated mice, P14 T cells, XCR1+ cDC1s, and infected cells were colocalized, but at this time point, T cell proliferation was impaired ( ). These results led us to hypothesize that although DCs and P14 cells could interact in the absence of FRCs, these cellular interactions were suboptimal.
 FRCs can contribute to the homeostasis of DCs in lymphoid organs at steady state, but it is not known whether FRCs contribute to DC maturation during virus infection ( ). To investigate this, we analyzed cDC1s, cDC2s, activated cDCs, and plasmacytoid DCs from our scRNA-seq dataset ( ). Activated DCs were distinguished by increased levels of costimulatory molecules, chemokines, and chemokine/cytokine receptors (fig. S4A). After FRC depletion, we found a reduction in the percentage and cellularity of activated cDCs compared to control mice, suggesting a more immature state in the absence of FRCs (fig. S4B). To understand potential cross-talk between FRCs, DCs, and T cells, we performed an analysis of predicted ligand-receptor interactions with FRCs. This revealed the largest number of predicted interactions between FRCs and plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDCs), activated DCs, and cDC1s with fewer interactions predicted to occur between FRCs, cDC2s, and P14 cells ( and fig. S4C). Predicted FRC-DC interactions included activated DC recruitment via Ccl21a/Ccr7, adhesion of DCs to extracellular matrix via multiple integrins, and modulation of DC functionality through pathways such as Pge2/Ptger4 or Thbs1-Thbs4/Cd47 (fig. S4, C and D) ( – ). This suggested that FRCs might influence T cell responses predominantly through interactions with DCs.
Given the potential for substantial FRC-DC interactions in the spleen during infection, we further investigated how FRC depletion affected DC activation. We compared gene expression in DCs from control and FRC-ablated mice. Gene Ontology (GO) term pathway analysis revealed that activated DCs, cDC1s, and cDC2s from control mice were enriched for genes associated with responses to type I IFN, innate immune response, and T cell costimulation ( ). We then analyzed DC activation using diffusion maps to define their differentiation trajectories ( ). Our analysis indicated the convergence of cDC1 and cDC2s toward an activated DC state upon LCMV infection ( ), corroborating the convergent genetic reprogramming of DC subsets upon maturation reported during murine cytomegalovirus infection ( ). This revealed that cDCs from control mice were more differentiated toward an activated DC state, while in FRC-depleted mice, DCs were in an intermediate maturation state ( ). Comparison of DC maturation and immunoregulatory genes revealed that cDCs from FRC-depleted mice had lower expression of the costimulatory molecules Cd80, Cd40, and Cd86, lower expression of the regulatory molecules Socs1 and Cd274 (programmed death-ligand 1), and lower expression of the chemokine Cxcl9 but similar expression of Ccr7 and major histocompatibility complex class I (MHC-I) and MHC-II molecules ( and fig. S4E). We then predicted interactions between P14 T cells and DCs, which revealed a reduction in the number of putative interactions between activated DCs and cDC1 with P14 T cells in FRC-ablated mice ( ). Collectively, these data suggest that FRCs might influence DC activation during LCMV infection, potentially affecting the delivery of activating signals to T cells and the priming of P14 cells in vivo.

## FRCs regulate DC maturation and T cell priming in the spleen
The above experiments suggested that FRCs contribute to DC functionality after infection. In line with this this, flow cytometry showed reduced DC maturation in FRC-depleted mice 1.5 days post-LCMV infection. This revealed a significant decrease in expression of the cell surface maturation markers CD86, CD80, and CD40 in cDC1 and cDC2 in FRC-depleted mice ( and fig. S5A). Intracellular IL-12 production in cDC1 remained unaffected upon LCMV infection, indicating that viral sensing by cDCs was intact (fig. S5B) ( ). However, the production of intracellular type I IFN and IL-12 by pDCs was reduced in FRC-depleted mice (fig. S5C). This indicated that alterations to the inflammatory milieu produced by multiple cell types in FRC-ablated mice contributed to reduced DC activation. In support of this, expression of IFN-α/β genes, interferon-stimulated genes, and cytokines, including TNF and IFN-γ, was significantly reduced in the spleens of FRC-ablated mice 24 hours after LCMV infection (fig. S5D). To ascertain whether increasing the inflammatory milieu, including the production of IFNs, could restore DC maturation in FRC-ablated mice, we injected infected FRC-depleted mice with CpG oligodeoxynucleotides, a Toll-like receptor 9 agonist that induces IFN-I–dependent cDC maturation ( ). This treatment restored expression of maturation markers in cDC1s to levels comparable to LCMV-infected control mice ( ).
 Next, we explored the capacity of DCs from FRC-ablated mice to prime CD8+ T cells. We enriched cDCs from control and CCL19-DTR mice and cocultured them with naïve P14 cells ex vivo. We observed significantly reduced T cell expansion and reduced up-regulation of CD25 and CD69 by P14 T cells when cocultured with cDCs from FRC-depleted mice ( ). We then examined T-DC interactions by performing flow cytometry to enumerate P14 cell–cDC1 conjugates in spleen cell suspensions. This revealed impaired T-DC conjugate formation in FRC-ablated mice ( and fig. S5E), indicating that despite being present within the same niche in the spleen after infection ( ), P14 T cells did not form stable conjugates with DCs when FRCs were ablated. We hypothesized that this may affect early T cell priming interactions. To investigate this, we used Nr4a3-Tocky mice crossed to P14 mice, where P14 cells express the fluorescent Timer protein under the control of Nr4a3, which is downstream of TCR signaling ( ). P14-Tocky cells were injected in control and CCL19-DTR mice before LCMV infection. Low expression of CD69 and the Tocky-Blue fluorescent protein was seen in depleted mice, confirming weak TCR signaling events in P14 cells primed in the absence of FRCs ( ).
Examination of virus replication in the spleen 3 days after infection revealed reduced viral load in mice depleted of FRCs, which could limit antigen availability and/or inflammation and subsequently DC and T cell responses ( ). Therefore, we next sought to distinguish between the role of reduced inflammation or viral load for the induction of T cell responses. To determine whether inflammatory signals were limiting T cell responses in FRC-depleted mice after infection, we supplemented inflammatory signals as in . But a single injection of CpG did not restore P14 cell expansion, possibly due to the nature of the inflammatory environment induced by CpG being different from natural LCMV infection (fig. S6A). To investigate this, we made use of the LCMV clone 13 strain A3, which lacks the gp33 epitope due to a mutation in the peptide sequence from KAVYNFATC to KAAYNFACTC, thereby preventing H-2Db binding and P14 T cell activation ( ). CCL19-DTR mice were either infected with wild-type (WT) LCMV CL13 [2 × 105 plaque-forming units (PFU)] or with mixed LCMV consisting of WT CL13 (2 × 105 PFU) combined with a high dose the A3 strain (1.8 × 106 PFU) ( ). In this setting, the antigen load remained the same across the two groups, but inflammation was increased in the mix LCMV group. This revealed that increasing virus-induced inflammation could restore early P14 T cell expansion in the absence of FRCs but did not increase expression of CD25 by P14 cells, possibly reflecting additional signals that could not be restored by inflammatory signals ( and fig. S6B).
We next sought to distinguish between the role of viral load and the FRC niche for induction of T responses. We infected WT mice with a low dose of LCMV (2 × 103 PFU) to match virus levels to that in FRC-depleted mice ( and fig. S6C). Normalizing viral load equilibrated cDC1 activation between control and FRC-depleted mice ( ). The early activation and proliferation of P14 T cells were largely equivalent in these mice, with FRC-depleted mice showing slightly faster responses ( and fig. S6D). However, we found that P14 cell responses at the peak of T cell expansion (day 8) were significantly reduced in FRC-depleted mice compared to viral load–matched control mice ( ). Hence, impaired T cell expansion in FRC-depleted mice was not attributed to the reduction in early virus replication. We conclude that the FRC niche supports virus replication and the inflammatory milieu responsible for rapid DC maturation and early T cell activation, and FRCs provide additional signals that are required to sustain the T cell proliferative response.

## Impaired generation of antiviral effector CD8+ T cells in FRC-ablated mice
To understand the consequence of altered T cell priming in the absence of FRCs, we assessed endogenous CD8+ T cell responses to LCMV infection at day 8. Depletion of FRCs led to a substantial reduction in endogenous CD8+ T cells specific for the LCMV immunodominant GP33 and NP396 epitopes, as well as their percentages within the total splenic cell population following LCMV infection ( ). Following ex vivo stimulation of splenocytes with GP33–41 or NP396–404 peptides, we observed a diminished capacity of CD44+ CD8+ T cells from FRC-depleted mice to produce IFN-γ and TNF ( ). We next investigated whether FRCs also influenced T cell differentiation by examining the expression of killer cell lectin-like receptor G1 (KLRG1) and IL-7R on endogenous T cells to discriminate between short-lived effector cells (SLECs) and memory precursor effector cells, respectively. CD8+ T cells primed in the absence of FRCs exhibited lower expression of KLRG1 and increased IL-7R expression indicative of impaired SLEC differentiation, with similar results observed in P14 T cells ( , and fig. S7, A to C). P14 T cells had increased CD62L expression in FRC-ablated mice, as well as altered cytokine production in the spleen of FRC-depleted mice (fig. S7, C and D). Granzyme B expression by P14 cells was not altered in these mice, and the virus was cleared by day 8 in the spleen, indicating that cytotoxic function was not abrogated (fig. S7D).
 We then asked whether the defect in CD8+ T cell responses in the absence of FRCs was observed during other infections. We injected CCL19-DTR mice with transgenic OT-I CD8+ T cells and infected these with vaccinia virus (VV) expressing the recombinant ovalbumin protein. We observed a significant decrease in the expansion of OT-I cells 8 days after infection ( ). Although OT-I T cell differentiation into KLRG1+ SLECs was unaffected, we found a decrease in IL-7R expression and an increase in CD62L expression indicative of also impaired CD8+ T cell differentiation during VV infection. Thus, priming of antiviral effector CD8+ T cell responses in the spleen required FRCs.

## FRCs are essential for the generation and maintenance of protective memory T cells
Having identified a defect in the expansion and differentiation of effector CD8+ T cells in the absence of FRCs, we next asked how FRCs affect the formation and functionality of memory T cells in the spleen. Circulating memory T cells encompass central memory T cells (TCM) and effector memory T cells (TEM) that exhibit distinct migratory, recall, and protective properties ( ). Recent work identified that within the pool of memory T cells exists a subset of long-lived effector cells (LLEC), characterized by the absence of CD62L and IL-7R expression ( ). We analyzed P14 cells from CCL19-DTR mice 30 days postinfection and found a substantial reduction in the formation of TCM in FRC-depleted mice ( ). We also observed a small but significant decrease in P14 TEM cells and no difference in the number of LLEC in the spleen of CCL19-DTR mice. We next examined the functionality of memory P14 cells by assessing their ability to produce effector cytokines upon ex vivo stimulation with their cognate peptide ( ). We found decreased capacity to produce the cytokines IFN-γ, TNF, and IL-2 upon restimulation ( ).
 We then asked whether the reduction in T cell memory differentiation and functionality in FRC-depleted mice that were treated with DT before infection were the result of altered signals during the effector phase of the response. For this, we transferred effector P14 cells from the spleens of control and CCL19-DTR mice 8 days after LCMV infection into infection-matched WT mice and analyzed the memory compartment ( ). This revealed normal formation of memory cell numbers, but these cells exhibited reduced effector functions upon restimulation ( ). This indicated that the defective priming of T cells in the absence of FRCs was critical in shaping the quality of the memory pool and that FRCs could not rescue memory T cell functions later during memory formation or maintenance. Next, we assessed the recall and protective properties of memory P14 T cells induced in FRC-deficient mice by transferring memory P14 cells from control or CCL19-DTR mice into naïve WT recipients before LCMV CL13 infection ( ). We did not observe differences in the expansion of memory P14 cells nor their differentiation into KLRG1+ secondary effector cells in the spleen of infected mice ( ). However, memory P14 T cells that were primed in the absence of FRCs were unable to control systemic viral load compared to memory P14 cells from control hosts ( ), possibly due to their reduced ability to secrete effector molecules ( ). Thus, splenic FRCs are required for the generation of memory CD8+ T cells that can protect against reinfection.
Last, we asked whether spleen FRCs also contribute to the maintenance of memory T cells. To investigate this, CCL19-DTR and control mice were infected with LCMV 30 days before depletion of FRCs with four weekly doses of DT ( ). We found that P14 cells were significantly reduced after FRC depletion, due primarily to a reduction in TCM cells and TEM cells, while LLEC numbers were not changed ( and fig. S7E). We found a similar but nonsignificant trend toward loss of endogenous TCM phenotype cells and TEM cells after FRC depletion (fig. S7F). Together, these data reveal a requirement for spleen FRCs for the maintenance of antiviral memory CD8+ T cells.

## DISCUSSION
The spleen is a key site for the induction and maintenance of immune responses against systemic pathogens. Spleen stromal cells respond dynamically during infections and undergo substantial transcriptional reprogramming ( , ). This suggests that FRCs help to coordinate responses to infection, yet it has remained unclear how FRCs contribute to antiviral T cell responses. Here, we reveal that spleen FRCs provide important niches that support viral replication and coordinate early inflammation required for the maturation of DCs and enable appropriate interactions with T cells for the efficient induction of cytotoxic T lymphocyte responses. We further unveil that support for T cell responses that is provided by FRCs is required for the generation and maintenance of protective memory T cells. Collectively, our results substantially broaden our understanding of how stromal cells support immune responses in the spleen and foster protective memory.
The spleen enables the rapid and robust induction of immune responses by supporting efficient virus infection that leads to IFN-I production. We identified a critical role for FRCs in regulating viral replication and early inflammation that were important for DC activation and spatial interactions with T cells. Mouse models that lack lymphocytes or lymphotoxins have developmental defects in spleen architecture that is required for efficient replication of LCMV and IFN-I responses ( , , ). Mechanistically, the requirement for an organized spleen microarchitecture for efficient antiviral immune responses remains incompletely understood, but our data support a model by which CCL19+ FRCs ensure viral replication in the spleen and early inflammation to promote appropriate responses by DCs for T cell priming. This indirect support for virus replication is consistent with a requirement for an intact spleen architecture for efficient infection ( , ). By promoting viral replication and IFN-I responses in the spleen, FRCs might also direct their own reprogramming to support the immune response. In the spleen during LCMV infection, we showed that FRCs regulate a large program of gene expression that could support the ongoing immune response ( ). How these responses by FRCs regulate FRC-DC and FRC-T cell interactions that support the host response needs to be investigated further. But our transcriptomic analysis of potential interactions between FRCs and DCs implicates several opportunities for direct FRC-DC interactions that could support the ongoing immune response.
Cellular communication between DCs and FRCs has previously been shown to play several roles in immune responses. In lymph nodes, inflammatory cues up-regulate C-type lectin-like receptor 2 expression on mature DCs that engages with podoplanin on FRCs ( , ). This engagement prompts the stretching of the FRC network, accommodating the increase of immune cellularity. This axis also regulates intranodal DC migration. During lymph node remodeling, DC-derived lymphotoxin β maintains FRC homeostasis and functionality required for the support of immune responses ( ). In addition, a subset of Grem1+ FRCs expresses multiple ligands with the potential to modulate DC functions at steady state, including molecules involved in homeostasis, recruitment, and matrix adhesion ( ). Our discovery that loss of FRCs in the spleen impairs DC maturation supports a role for FRC-DC communication during virus infection in the spleen. We found predicted receptor-ligand interactions shared by FRCs and DCs that could further support DC functionality during viral infection. However, the relative involvement of direct DC-FRC interactions versus indirect support for DC functions by FRCs, including coordination of the inflammatory milieu, requires further analysis.
Much knowledge of the roles played by FRCs in immune responses comes from studies of lymph nodes, including the demonstration that FRCs play inhibitory roles in T cell priming via production of inducible nitric oxide synthase and cyclooxygenase 2 production of PGE2 ( – ). But FRCs also positively regulate T cell responses, including via the production of the alarmin IL-33 that amplifies CD8+ T cell responses to LCMV infection in lymph nodes ( ). Since spleen FRCs also express IL-33 ( , ), it is likely that this is an additional mechanism by which they support antiviral T cells later in the response once activated T cells up-regulate the receptor serum stimulation-2 (ST2). Furthermore, interferon receptor signaling in lymph node FRCs has been shown to be important for regulating FRC functions and restricting viral spread during LCMV infection ( ). Although IFN-I signaling to spleen FRCs has not been directly investigated, it is likely that IFN-I contributes to the reprogramming of spleen FRCs that occurs after LCMV infection, which includes up-regulation of the inflammatory chemokines such as Cxcl10 and Cxcl5 ( ). Likewise, early after MCMV infection, RP reticular cells and FRCs were found to rapidly respond to IFN-I signals by regulating many IFN-stimulated genes ( ). It will be important in future studies to determine how spleen FRCs respond to disparate inflammatory signals and incorporate these to differentially regulate early and ongoing immune responses. For instance, during autoimmune-driven inflammation, IL-17 can metabolically reprogram FRC functions ( ).
Memory T cells that form upon resolution of infections protect from secondary rechallenges. Here, we found that the quality of the memory pool induced after LCMV infection was dependent on the early events regulated by FRCs. In the absence of FRCs, the defect in T cell priming resulted in the formation of memory T cells that could proliferate and differentiate into effector cells but were unable to protect mice from rechallenge with LCMV. This suggested that effector functions that are imprinted early in the formation of effector and memory T cells are dependent on an intact FRC network. The formation of TCM and, to a lesser extent, TEM was dependent on the continuous presence FRCs. Furthermore, by depleting FRCs at memory, we show that the maintenance of memory T cells in the spleen also requires an intact FRC network. Since spleen FRCs express IL-7 and IL-15, cytokines that are required for the maintenance of memory T cells, these signals might be provided predominantly to TCM cells in the WP FRC niche. This is in line with a previous report that revealed the location of circulating memory T cells in the T cell zone, interacting with PDPN+ FRCs while TEM were found within the RP ( ). In sum, our study provides crucial insight into the role of FRCs in orchestrating the spatio-temporal regulation of antiviral T cell priming and the generation of protective memory T cells.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

## Mice
CCL19-Cre, ROSA26-iDTR, P14.Ly5.1, P14.tdTomato, XCR1-Venus, CD11c-DTR, CD169-DTR, OT-I.GFP, and P14.Nr4a3-Tocky mice were bred in the Doherty Institute. CCL19-DTR mice were generated by crossing CCL19-Cre to ROSA26iDTR mice. For all experiments, Cre+DTR and Cre−DTR littermate controls were used. P14.Tocky mice were generated by crossing P14.Ly5.1 with Nr4a3-Tocky mice ( ). To generate bone marrow chimeric mice, CCL19-DTR mice were lethally irradiated with two doses of 550 rad, 3 hours apart, and mice were reconstituted with bone marrow cells from XCR1-Venus donors. Recipient mice were used at least 8 weeks postreconstitution. Animal experiments were approved by the University of Melbourne Animal Ethics Committee (approval 25292). Mice were maintained under specific pathogen–free conditions and housed in individually ventilated cages. All mice were sex- and age-matched, and both female and male mice were used between 7 and 14 weeks of age.

## Adoptive transfer of transgenic CD8+ T cells
Recipient mice were intravenously injected with 5 × 104 P14 cells at least 24 hours before treatment or infection. For early T cell activation experiments, P14 cells were first enriched and labeled with CellTrace Violet (Thermo Fisher Scientific) at a final concentration of 5 μM according to manufacturer’s instructions, and 106 cells were intravenously injected in recipient mice.

## Virus and infections
LCMV Armstrong and clone 13 strains and recombinant vaccinia virus Western Reserve strain expressing ovalbumin ( ) (VV-OVA) were used in this study. Unless stated otherwise, mice were infected intraperitoneally with 2 × 105 PFU of LCMV Armstrong. Mice were infected intravenously with 2 × 105 PFU of VV-OVA. The mixed-mutant infection was performed by coinfecting intraperitoneally 1.8 × 106 PFU of a gp33-deficient LCMV clone 13 mutant [A3 strain, encoding an H-2Db–binding–deficient, gp33-altered peptide ligand ( )] along with 2 × 105 PFU of WT LCMV clone 13. Titers of virus from homogenized spleen samples were determined by plaque assay on Vero cells as described ( ).

## DT treatment
CCL19-DTR mice and their littermate controls were injected with 10 ng/g of body weight of DT, twice, 24 hours apart. Mice were infected the following day posttreatment. CD11c-DTR and CD169-DTR mice were injected with 5 and 10 ng/g of body weight of DT, respectively, and, 24 hours later, infected with LCMV.

## CpG treatment
To analyze the effect of systemic inflammation on DC maturation, CCL19-DTR mice were infected with LCMV and, 24 hours later, injected intravenously with 50 μg of CpG combo, CpG generated by linking (5′ to 3′) CpG-2006 to CpG-21798 ( ). DC maturation was analyzed 16 hours post-CpG injection.

## Spleen processing for cell isolation
To isolate stromal cells, spleens were digested as per our published protocol ( ). Briefly, spleens were incubated with enzyme mix [collagenase D, Dispase II, and deoxyribonuclease (DNase)] for 25 min, and stromal cells were enriched by depleting CD45+ hematopoietic cells. For T cell responses to infection, spleens were mechanically dissociated through a 70-μm nylon mesh in RPMI 1640 supplemented with 2% fetal bovine serum. For myeloid cell isolation, spleens were digested with collagenase D and DNase for 25 min.

## Flow cytometry
Single-cell suspensions were stained in fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) buffer [phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), 1% bovine serum albumin, and 5 mM EDTA] containing CD16/32 Fc blocking antibody for 30 min at 4°C. Antibodies used for staining are detailed in table S1. Live cells were discriminated with a fixable LIVE/DEAD Fixable Near-IR Dead Cell Stain kit, propidium iodide, or 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole. Endogenous LCMV-specific CD8+ T cells were identified with H-2Db-gp33-41– and H-2Db-np396-404–restricted tetramer staining, respectively. For intracellular staining for DCs, spleens were harvested in cold RP-2 containing brefeldin A. Brefeldin A was also added during digestion. Cells were enumerated by adding SPHERO™ calibration particles to each sample before acquisition on a flow cytometer. Samples were acquired using FACSFortessa (BD) or Cytek Aurora, and FlowJo software was used for analysis. Details of antibodies can be found in table S1.

## Peptide stimulation and intracellular staining
Splenocytes were platted in a 96-well plate and incubated for 4 hours with 1 μM LCMV GP33-41 or NP396-404 peptides and brefeldin A before staining. Detection of intracellular molecules was performed using the BD Cytofix/Cytoperm Fixation/Permeabilization Kit according to manufacturer’s instructions.

## Real-time quantitative PCR
Total RNA was extracted from the spleen using the RNeasy Plus Mini Kit (QIAGEN) and converted to cDNA using the High-Capacity cDNA Reverse Transcription Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Gene expression was analyzed by real-time qPCR using Fast SYBR Green Master Mix (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Cycle threshold values were determined for genes individually, and gene expression was normalized to the housekeeping genes Hprt and Gapdh (ΔCt) and presented as 2−ΔCt (arbitrary units).

## Immunofluorescence and confocal imaging
Mouse spleens were harvested, cut in half, and fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde for 2 to 4 hours at 4°C, washed for 1 hour in PBS, incubated in 30% sucrose overnight, and embedded in optimal cutting temperature freezing media. Tissue sections were cut at 16-μm thickness with a cryostat (Leica, CM3050S), air-dried before being fixed in acetone for 5 min, dried, and then blocked for 30 min (Protein Block X0909, Dako) at room temperature. Sections were stained with primary antibodies for 1 hour at room temperature, washed in PBS, and, where required, stained with secondary antibodies for 45 min at room temperature. Stained sections were mounted in ProLong Gold antifade reagent and acquired on a LSM780 or LSM710 confocal microscope (Carl Zeiss), and images were processed with Imaris.

## Quantification of confocal imaging
Images were processed with Cecelia (https://github.com/schienstockd/cecelia) ( ). Individual channels were segmented using Cellpose ( ). Cell populations were gated using Cecelia’s histo-cytometry module and spatially analyzed. LCMV segmentation was converted into a branching network to discern patches of viral particles and signal noise. Cell contact was defined as cells being within a 5-μm distance from each other. Cell clusters were defined as a minimum of four cells within a 15-μm radius.

## In vivo conjugates
To analyze DC-T conjugates ex vivo, we adapted a published protocol ( ). Control and CCL19-DTR mice were infected with LCMV and, 24 hours later, injected with 5 × 106 P14 and 5 × 106 OT-I cells. Sixteen to 18 hours later, spleens were harvested and digested with collagenase D. Cells were resuspended in PBS with 2% fetal calf serum without EDTA and stained for DCs and transgenic T cells. Samples were acquired on a flow cytometer at no more than 3000 events/s. MHC-II+P14+ and MHC-II+OT-I+ doublets and the frequency of P14- and OT-I–conjugated fraction within XCR1+ cDC1 subset were analyzed.

## Intravascular labeling
Mice were injected with 4 μg of anti-CD45 biotin antibody intravenously and euthanized 5 min later. Spleens were harvested and digested as per digestion protocol.

## Single-cell RNA sequencing
Control and CCL19-DTR mice were injected with tdTomato+ P14 cells followed by DT treatment and LCMV infection. Spleens were digested, and six fractions were sorted comprising: tdTomato+CD8+ P14 cells; CD45−CD31−Ter119− cells that expressed at least one of the markers CD157 or PDPN or double negative for CD157 and PDPN (CD157+ and/or PDPN+ stroma and CD157−PDPN− stroma); TCR−NK1.1−CD19− CD11c+ and CD11c− myeloid cells; and total cells. Cells were mixed in equal ratios and encapsulated into emulsion droplets using a Chromium Controller (10x Genomics). scRNA-seq libraries were constructed using the Chromium Single Cell 3′ v3 Reagent Kit according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Libraries were run on the HiSeq 2500 for 100-bp paired-end Illumina sequencing.

## scRNA-seq analysis
Sequencing data were aligned to mouse genome mm10 using CellRanger v7.0.0 to generate feature-barcode matrices. Subsequent data analysis was performed using the Seurat R package v5.1.0 ( ). The sequencing data are available at the National Center for Biotechnology Information Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) under the accession number GSE274926.
Gene expression data were normalized using SCTransform v0.4.1 ( , ), regressing for cell cycle score [using R package gprofiler2 v0.2.3 ( )], percentage mitochondrial RNA, and percent ribosomal RNA to avoid these variables dominating the data.
The top 3000 most variable genes were used to calculate principal components of normalized and scaled expression data. From the principal components reductions, Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projections (UMAPs) were generated, and clusters were identified. Cluster-specific genes were identified using FindAllMarkers from Seurat with minimum percentage expression set to 10%. Cluster identity was annotated manually (table S2). Cell type proportions were tested using the propeller package. Differentially expressed genes were identified using FindMarkers from Seurat, and GO analysis was performed using DAVID (david.ncifcrf.gov). Signature enrichment analysis was performed using AddModuleScore from Seurat. Details of gene modules can be found in table S3.
Dimensional reduction with the Diffusion Map tool in the destiny package v3.16.0 ( ) was used to estimate differentiation trajectories. GO gene set enrichment analysis was performed using clusterProfiler package for R ( ). Cell-cell communication analysis was performed using LIANA ( ), making use of ligand-receptor networks from CellChatDB v2.1.2 ( , ), with additional filtering using a custom analysis (https://github.com/dn-ra/bespoke_interaction_analysis). The chordDiagram function from the circlize package v0.4.16 ( ) and ggplot2 v3.5.1 ( ) were used to visualize expression of ligands and receptors in clusters.

## Statistical analysis
Graphs and statistics were generated using Prism 10 (GraphPad). Samples were tested for normality, and two groups were compared using the two-tailed Mann-Whitney U test or the unpaired t test. Multiple groups were analyzed with one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA), followed by Tukey’s posttest comparison or Kruskal-Wallis, based on Gaussian distribution. All graphs depict means ± SEM. Details of statistical analysis are indicated in the figure captions and include the statistical test used. *P < 0.05, **P < 0.01, ***P < 0.001, and ****P < 0.0001.
