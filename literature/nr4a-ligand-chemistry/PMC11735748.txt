## Abstract
When the traditional random forest (RF) algorithm is used to select feature elements in biostatistical data, a large amount of noise data and parameters can affect the importance of the selected feature elements, making the control of feature selection difficult. Therefore, it is a challenge for the traditional RF algorithm to preserve the accuracy of algorithm results in the presence of noise data. Generally, directly removing noise data can result in significant bias in the results. In this study, we develop a new algorithm, standardized threshold, and loops based random forest (STLBRF), and apply it to the field of gene expression data for feature gene selection. This algorithm, based on the traditional RF algorithm, combines backward elimination and K-fold cross-validation to construct a cyclic system and set a standardized threshold: error increment. The algorithm overcomes the shortcomings of existing gene selection methods. We compare ridge regression, lasso regression, elastic net regression, the traditional RF algorithm, and our improved RF algorithm using three real gene expression datasets and conducting a quantitative analysis. To ensure the reliability of the results, we validate the effectiveness of the genes selected by these methods using the Random Forest classifier. The results indicate that, compared to other methods, the STLBRF algorithm achieves not only higher effectiveness in feature gene selection but also better control over the number of selected genes. Our method offers reliable technical support for feature expression analysis and research on biomarker selection.
Keywords: improved random forest algorithm, feature gene selection, noise data, standardized threshold, biomarker

## Introduction
Gene expression data refers to the data that records the expression levels of genes under specific conditions or in particular samples. It is used to uncover gene variations and regulations across biological processes, disease states, and environmental changes. Microarray technology and RNA-seq plays the pivotal role in acquiring gene expression data as it enables the detection of expression levels of a large number of known or unknown genes under a given condition and facilitates their conversion into numerical data for analysis and interpretation [ , ]. The advent and widespread application of DNA microarray and RNA-seq technologies have resulted in the mining and publication of a substantial amount of gene expression data. However, the challenge lies in identifying meaningful feature genes from the high-dimensional gene expression data. Feature genes refer to the genes that are associated with specific traits or phenotypes in a particular organism or population. They can control the phenotypic characteristics of an organism, differentiate and identify different biological states, disease types, or other important biological processes [ , ]. Identifying feature genes is crucial for studying diseases, especially those whose complete cure remains uncertain. Moreover, feature genes have routinely been employed to identify diagnostic, prognostic, and therapeutic biomarkers for many cancers [ , ]. Generally, the selection of feature genes is closely tied to the selection methods. Gene expression data possess extremely high dimensions and inherent noise, and the noise data was not always effectively regulated [ ]. In summary, under the influence of noise data, retaining the accuracy of results and selecting meaningful feature genes are quite challenging.
Previous studies have proposed several classic and popular multivariable gene selection methods. Ridge regression limits the complexity of the model by adding an L2 regularization term, thereby reducing the regression coefficients and mitigating the impact of multicollinearity on the model. Lasso regression incorporates an L1 regularization term into the loss function, which causes some regression coefficients to be compressed to zero. This selective elimination of unimportant features simplifies the model, facilitating feature selection and enhancing interpretability. Elastic Net regression combines the advantages of both L1 and L2 regularization, allowing for the selective compression of the coefficients of unimportant features to zero while retaining relevant features [ ]. Additionally, there are methods based on decision trees that integrate and analyze data for gene selection. Decision trees represent the mapping relationship between object attributes and object values, which can be primarily used for data analysis and prediction. Prominent algorithms in this category include Iterative Dichotomiser 3, Classifier 4.5, and Classification and Regression Tree. The traditional random forest (RF) algorithm, based on decision trees, is a classifier that trains and predicts samples using multiple decision trees. It falls under the Bagging (parallel ensemble learning method) class in ensemble learning. During the training of base learners, the traditional RF algorithm introduces sample perturbation through a process of random sampling with replacement and incorporates attribute perturbation. In the training process of base decision trees, the traditional RF method randomly selects a subset containing k attributes from the candidate attribute set, then identifies the optimal split attribute from this subset, facilitating feature selection. Due to its outstanding characteristics, such as high classification strength and broad applicability, the traditional RF algorithm has demonstrated excellent results in various fields, including biological prediction and fault detection [ ]. Although the traditional RF algorithm has broad adaptability, it still has several shortcomings. Since the parameters in the traditional RF algorithm are generally adjusted and optimized based on the model’s predictive ability, it cannot effectively balance the data [ ]. Furthermore, the traditional RF algorithm cannot make predictions beyond the training set data range, which inevitably generates randomness and a large amount of noise data, leading to situations where crucial features are overwhelmed by noise and significantly which affects the feature importance ranking results in RF calculations. In this study, an improved approach, STLBRF, was developed based on the theoretical foundation of the traditional RF algorithm. The STLBRF algorithm embeds a looping structure into the traditional RF algorithm, integrating backward elimination in multivariate statistics, K-fold cross-validation, and standardized parameters to iteratively establish, eliminate, and select models. Introducing a cyclic system enables iterative training of a RF model and averaging of results across multiple training sessions. This averaging process significantly mitigates the influence of randomness, enhancing the stability and reliability of the final predictions. While the impact of noise may fluctuate during each training iteration, the model effectively attenuates these irrelevant signals through repeated training and averaging, thereby enhancing its stability and generalization capability. During each iteration, backward elimination removes a batch of least important feature data, calculates the Area Under the Curve (AUC) across all models, sums these AUCs, and takes their mean. The decision to continue the iteration depends on comparing the mean difference with a standardized threshold, effectively addressing scenarios where important features are overwhelmed by noise. The introduction of standardized parameters ensures fair treatment of all features by the model, enhancing the traditional RF algorithm's capability to balance data. Compared to the traditional RF algorithm, the STLBRF algorithm significantly mitigates the impact of noisy data and randomness, markedly reducing subjective bias, optimizing parameter settings, and yielding superior feature selection results. The proposed STLBRF algorithm references and completes Yang's [ ] improvement ideas for the traditional RF algorithm and extends them to various gene datasets.
The evaluation of an improved algorithm could be as equally crucial and challenging as the algorithm improvement process itself. Random Forest algorithm is not only commonly employed for feature selection but also serves as a crucial classifier in the assessment of biological models [ , ].
In this study, we compared the STLBRF algorithm with ridge regression, lasso regression, elastic net regression, and the traditional RF algorithm from both visual and quantitative perspectives. We employed the Random Forest classifier to compare the classification accuracy results dominated by feature genes selected by the five methods. Through quantitative research analysis, we found that the STLBRF algorithm outperformed the other four methods, demonstrating higher accuracy.

## Materials and methods

## RF algorithm
The traditional RF algorithm is an ensemble learning method that utilizes a collection of decision trees, which has almost no assumptions about the relationships between variables and is able to capture complex interactions among variables commonly found in biological systems. In other words, the RF algorithm can handle a large number of predictor variables despite complex interactions, and has been successfully applied in genetics, clinical medicine, and bioinformatics in recent years [ ].
The RF algorithm serves two main functions: classification of samples and selection of features. It accomplishes sample classification by randomly selecting a subset of features and employing a majority voting principle. Specifically, the algorithm randomly selects a subset of features from the original feature set. Each decision tree within the RF algorithm votes for the classification based on its selected features. The final result is determined by the classification with the most votes. Additionally, the RF algorithm estimates the error rates during training. This involves using bootstrap samples to train trees and testing with samples not included in the bootstrap samples, which are known as out-of-bag (OOB) data. After learning from all the trees, predictions are aggregated to compute the OOB estimation of error rates [ ]. Moreover, the RF algorithm is interpretable, as variables are scored based on their impact on the decision trees generated within the forest. Consequently, variable importance values can be generated by calculating the change in mean squared error of the OOB data [ ], which enables feature selection. The following section elaborates on the construction of traditional RFs and the process of computing variable importance.

## Building a RF
In the first step, let X denote the total number of samples in the dataset, and Y denote the total number of attributes per sample. Randomly take x (where x < X) samples from the total number of samples with replacement, and train a decision tree using these x samples. When a decision tree node needs to be split, y (where y < Y) attributes are randomly selected from all Y attributes. The best attribute for the current node split is chosen from the y selected attributes based on the Gini index. The splitting process for each decision tree ensures that the impurity of every node is minimized, continuing until a node cannot be split (note that this process does not involve tree pruning).

A zero error increment indicated that the model’s accuracy had reached a relatively stable level. However, during the feature selection process, it was necessary to eliminate irrelevant features and to discard weakly correlated ones. ## Results

## Three real gene expression datasets
In this study, three sets of raw gene expression datasets were obtained from the official Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) database, including the tuberculosis dataset GSE6112, the gastric cancer microarray dataset GSE19826, and the gastric cancer RNA-seq dataset GSE184336. The microarray data platform for GSE6112 was GPL4475, the platform for GSE19826 was GPL570, and the platform for GSE184336 was GPL11154. Detailed sample information for all three datasets was presented in .
 The experimental process primarily consisted of two parts: the first part involved the selection of feature genes, while the second part entailed the discriminative classification of test samples using the selected feature genes. In the feature gene selection phase, five different algorithms were employed: ridge regression, lasso regression, elastic net regression, the traditional RF algorithm, and the STLBRF algorithm. Each algorithm identified the top 20 most significant genes for the selection of gene data. For the discriminative classification phase, the Random Forest classifier models were constructed using the selected top 20 genes to classify test samples, enabling a comparative analysis of the classification accuracy based on the feature genes extracted by the five algorithms and facilitating an in-depth exploration of the impact of different feature selection algorithms on classification performance.

## Performing feature selection on tuberculosis data using five algorithms

## Data processing
The tuberculosis data obtained from the GEO database were comprehensive and had already undergone log fold change processing. However, to alleviate potential dimensional discrepancies in gene expression data, we employed the limma package in R to normalize the raw gene expression dataset.
Moreover, the raw data were organized using probe IDs on the vertical axis. To align probe names with gene names, we conducted gene name conversion using annotation information from the microarray platform. In cases where multiple probes corresponded to a single gene, we calculated and selected the mean expression value for that gene. This process resulted in a dataset encompassing 6377 genes with their respective expression levels.

## Feature gene screening
This study divided the overall sample dataset of tuberculosis into training and testing sets. The primary purpose of the training set was to determine algorithm parameters, thereby establishing a trained model system, while the testing set served to evaluate the model’s quality and quantify differences between its predictions and actual events. Based on the sample function in the R language, 25 samples were randomly selected from a pool of 36 samples, which consisted of 12 control samples and 13 experimental samples, designated as the training set. The remaining 11 samples, consisting of seven experimental samples and four control samples, were designated as the testing set.
Based on the 25 training samples, five algorithms were employed for feature selection. Each algorithm computed the corresponding importance of genes where higher importance suggested a more significant role in distinguishing between the two sample types. Five algorithms were applied to select the top 20 genes for comparison, as shown in .
 The STLBRF algorithm identified two common genes with the results of the lasso regression: STK31 and AGTR1, two common genes with the outcomes of elastic net regression: STK31 and AGTR1, and five shared genes with the traditional RF algorithm: STK31, CENPF, STC1, NPY1R, and SYN2.

## Discussion
The traditional RF algorithm plays a crucial role in feature selection. However, during the modeling process, a large amount of noise data and parameters often influence the calculation of gene importance, which can be difficult to regulate. The preservation of algorithm results’ accuracy under the influence of noise data is a challenging issue for the traditional RF algorithm. Previous solutions had their limitations. Therefore, we developed a new algorithm: the STLBRF algorithm.
In our proposed algorithm, we established a nested loop structure by combining the traditional RF algorithm with backward elimination and K-fold cross-validation from multivariate statistics, thereby iteratively constructing, eliminating, and selecting models. Moreover, we introduced the termination signal for the iteration using the error increment. The results demonstrated that our improved algorithm significantly mitigated the impact of noise data, reduced subjectivity, optimized parameter settings, provided superior feature selection results compared to the traditional RF algorithm and other algorithms, and allowed for a fixed output of desired feature count.
Furthermore, the results from two examples in this study demonstrated that our proposed STLBRF algorithm exhibited higher effectiveness on feature selection compared to ridge regression, lasso regression, elastic net regression and the traditional RF algorithm. On the one hand, the number of commonly identified feature genes was few across the five algorithms, where the STLBRF algorithm identified distinctly different genes in comparison to the other four algorithms. What’s more, the stable classification accuracy of the RF model based on the 20 top feature genes from the STLBRF algorithm all exceeded 80%.
In addition, existing research revealed that many of the selected feature genes by the STLBRF algorithm played important roles in the related diseases from the biological point of view. The numbers of relevant genes identified in both data analyses were significantly higher compared to the other algorithms. In the analysis of tuberculosis microarray data, 13 out of the 20 selected genes were found to be associated with pulmonary diseases. Similarly, in the analyses of gastric cancer microarray data and RNA-seq data, 14 and 17 out of the 20 selected genes were related to gastric cancer and other cancers, respectively. Among them, in the analysis of tuberculosis data, CENPF was confirmed to be overexpressed and associated with poor prognosis in lung cancer and lung adenocarcinoma tissues [ ]. AGTR1 inhibited the progression of lung adenocarcinoma through the PI3K/AKT3 pathway [ ]. PKN3 siRNA reduced the growth of liver and lung metastases [ ]. ZNF326 promoted the proliferation of non-small cell lung cancer cells by regulating ERCC1 expression [ , ]. SERPINE1 and SERPINB7 served as potential biomarkers for intravenous vitamin C therapy in non-small cell lung cancer [ ]. GHRH antagonists and agonist analogs have been developed for clinical applications including the treatment of benign prostatic hyperplasia, breast, prostate and lung cancer, diabetes, and neurodegenerative diseases [ ]. ICAM1 initiated CTC cluster formation and trans-endothelial migration in breast cancer lung metastasis [ ]. Genes such as KCNS3 identify 14 new motifs and two new signals for known motifs NPNT and GPR126, providing a basis for new understanding of lung diseases with altered genetic determinants [ ]. Lung cancer is more prevalent in families carrying the c.9976A > T variant than in families that are carriers of pathogenic BRCA1 or BRCA2 [ ]. STK31 regulated the proliferation and cell cycle of lung cancer cells through the Wnt/β-catenin pathway and c-myc feedback regulation [ ].

Additionally, genes such as TFAP2B、ACVR1B、AVP、FLJ33534, and LAT provided reference significance for other types of cancer [ ].
Similarly, in gastric cancer RNA-seq data analysis, compared with normal gastric cells, the expression of CBX2 is increased in gastric cancer cell lines, and CBX2 depletion inhibits the proliferation, migration, and invasion of GC cells by inactivating the YAP/β—catenin pathway [ ]. UTP6 can serve as a new effective predictor of chemoradiotherapy sensitivity and a prognostic factor for survival in colorectal cancer patients [ ]. SNHG15 increases the expression of programmed cell death ligand 1 by inhibiting miR-141, thereby promoting the resistance of gastric cancer cells to immune response [ ]. SNHG15 exerts carcinogenic effects in gastric cancer progression by targeting miR-506-5p [ ]. Through analysis of a series of colon cancer samples from TCGA, it was found that the expression level of JAG2 increases in the late T stage, demonstrating prognostic value for colon cancer patients [ ]. Additionally, genes such as CBX2, UTP6, AHR, APMAP, PRRG1, and UBE2QL1 provided reference significance for other types of cancer [ ].
In this study, we primarily focused on the analysis of gene expression data, and the results indicated that the algorithm is applicable not only to microarray data but also to RNA-seq data. Given that the RF algorithm is scale-free, it is suggested that as long as it is appropriately normalized, it should be applicable to any type of omics data. Consequently, the STLBRF algorithm has the potential to be utilized across various omics datasets, suggesting its considerable potential for expansion. In addition, our STLBRF algorithm is iterative, employing a backward elimination approach that progressively removes genes during each selection iteration, thus, the data volume gradually diminishes. Hence, unlike the traditional RF algorithm, our STLBRF algorithm is not suitable for classification but solely for feature selection.
In genomic research, the influence of noise in high-throughput data is common and challenging. We aim for the STLBRF algorithm to efficiently select feature data, particularly for rare disease studies, thereby facilitating disease prediction, treatment, and biomarker research.

## Conclusions
We constructed an improved the traditional RF algorithm with standardized threshold and nested loop structure based on the traditional RF and applied it to feature selection. The experimental results indicated that the performance of the improved algorithm was significantly better than those of four current feature selection methods. Therefore, the improved RF had the potential to more effectively select feature data, which could help researchers to conduct more accurate research on rare diseases and other diseases.

## Key Points
Based on the traditional RF algorithm, we constructed an improved RF algorithm with a standardized threshold and a nested loop structure for feature selection.
The improved RF algorithm addressed two key shortcomings of the traditional RF algorithm.
Compared to four current algorithms in terms of feature selection, the improved RF algorithm achieved higher effectiveness, which provided us important method for selection of feature elements in biostatistical data.
