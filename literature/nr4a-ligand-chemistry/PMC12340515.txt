## Background
Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is a leading challenge of global public health that predominantly affects developing countries. Although smoking is the main risk factor, only a fraction of smokers develop COPD. This study aimed to identify biomarkers or therapeutic targets that would effectively aid early diagnosis and treatment of smoking-induced COPD.

## Methods and results
After retrieving GSE27597, GSE38974, GSE47460, GSE76925, and GSE239897 from the Gene Expression Omnibus, never-smokers were excluded from each dataset. Differential expression analysis and weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA) were employed to discern a reliable gene list. Subsequently, integrated data, which incorporated 120 control and 349 COPD samples, was analyzed by random forest (RF) and least absolute shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO) methods to identify key genes. Lastly, 6 genes with the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve exceeding 0.7 were selected as potential biomarkers of smoking-induced COPD. CCL19, FCRLA, CD79A, SLITRK6, GRM8, and KRT4 exhibited similar expression patterns across the datasets, reflecting their prominent contribution to COPD pathogenesis.

## Conclusion
These results suggested CCL19, FCRLA, CD79A, SLITRK6, GRM8, and KRT4 as key genes for the development of COPD among smokers. While these potential biomarkers were identified by computational analyses, experimental studies are needed to evaluate their clinical applicability.

## Introduction
Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is a heterogeneous disorder identified by persistent respiratory symptoms resulting from abnormalities in the airways or alveoli [ ]. COPD ranks third among the primary causes of death, predominantly affecting low- and middle-income countries [ , ]. While various factors, such as genetic variants [ ] and respiratory infections [ ], contribute to the development and progression of COPD, smoking is considered the main risk factor for the disease [ ]. Oxidative stress [ ], airway inflammation [ ], cellular senescence [ ], and mitophagy-dependent necroptosis [ ] are known interconnected molecular mechanisms through which cigarette smoke (CS) induces COPD.
The excessive presence of exogenous oxidants in CS disrupts the balance between oxidants and antioxidants, eventually leading to oxidative stress [ ]. Cellular components, including DNA, proteins, and lipids, can be directly damaged by reactive oxygen species (ROS) produced in the oxidative stress condition [ ]. Moreover, oxidative stress amplifies inflammation by reducing histone deacetylase-2 activity [ , ], accelerates cellular senescence through the activation of p53 [ ], and exacerbates airway remodeling by upregulating matrix metallopeptidase 9 expression [ ]. Macrophages and the alveolar epithelial cells detect inhaled CS particles through the binding of multiple types of pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) such as toll-like receptors (TLRs) and cytosolic NOD-like receptors (NLRs) [ ]. This recognition occurs in response to various stimuli such as damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) and ROS. The receptor induction activates intracellular signaling pathways, particularly NF-κβ and STATs, leading to the production and release of inflammatory mediators [ ]. Cigarette smoke-induced senescence primarily results from epigenetic regulation mediated by microRNAs [ ]. Malfunction of mitochondria caused by CS is responsible for mitophagy-dependent necroptosis in alveolar and airway epithelial cells [ ].
Lack of consideration for smoking history, coupled with the limited sample size and heterogeneous nature of COPD, has resulted in the constrained identification of shared differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in many of the previous transcriptomics studies [ ]. Moreover, there are limited feasible biomarkers for clinical application in COPD [ ]. The present study aimed to discover common differentially expressed genes in the lung tissue of smoker COPD patients relative to smoker individuals without evidence of the disease. Furthermore, the presence of shared DEGs in significant modules correlating with the disease was assessed using weighted gene co-expression network (WGCNA) analysis. Lastly, genes revealed to have a critical role in COPD pathogenesis by both analyses were subjected to random forest (RF) and the least absolute shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO) algorithms to identify key genes that may serve as biomarkers or therapeutic targets.

## Materials and methods
2.1

## Data acquisition and processing
The Gene Expression Omnibus database (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/) was explored to obtain lung tissue transcriptome datasets incorporating COPD and normal samples. Five datasets, including four microarray datasets and one bulk RNA sequencing dataset, were retrieved for further analysis. First, never-smokers and samples with no information on their smoking history were excluded from all datasets according to the sample phenotypic information retrieved using the “GEOquery” [ ] R package. Then, outlier detection was conducted by means of principal component analysis (PCA) and hierarchical clustering. GSM953102 and GSM953106 were detected as outliers in GSE38974. In GSE47460, six samples, including GSM1150187, GSM1150251, GSM1150226, GSM1150465, GSM1149948, and GSM1150147, were discarded. Meanwhile, no outliers were detected in the GSE27597, GSE76925, and GSE239897 datasets. The filtration process led to the acquisition of 469 samples, comprising 349 and 120 individuals classified as COPD and normal, respectively ( ). 

2.2

## Diagnosis criteria and demographic data on the study population
In GSE27597, COPD patients suffered from a severe form of COPD, as the sampling occurred during lung transplantation [ , ]. The Global Initiative for Obstructive Lung Disease (GOLD) staging was employed to classify patients in GSE38974 [ ]. A combination of evidence from clinical history, CT scan, and surgical pathology was utilized in GSE47460 for the diagnosis of COPD [ , , , ]. In GSE76925, according to the measurements of lung function, COPD cases were classified as GOLD stages 3–4, and control smokers exhibited normal lung function [ , ]. Several lung function criteria, including forced expiratory volume in 1 s (FEV1), forced vital capacity (FVC), FEV1/FVC ratio, and diffusion capacity of the lung for carbon monoxide (DLCO), were used to characterize COPD in GSE239897. Additionally, microCT imaging was conducted to classify COPD cores into mild, moderate, or severe categories. Alveolar destruction was assessed through tissue percentage and alveolar surface density measurements. Notably, COPD patients in this study had a history of inhaled corticosteroid use [ ]. The demographic data on the population composition in these five datasets is presented in . 

2.3

## Identification of shared DEGs and pathway analysis
Differential expression (DE) analysis was conducted on each dataset individually using the “limma” [ ] package. This method uses linear modeling and empirical Bayes moderation to improve p-value reliability by borrowing variance information across genes [ ]. Genes meeting the criteria of |LogFC| > 1 and adjusted p-value <0.05 were considered as DEGs. Finally, shared DEGs across the datasets were selected based on their consistent significant upregulation or downregulation in at least two of the five datasets. Finally, separate Venn diagrams were generated using the “ggVennDiagram” [ ] package to illustrate the overlap of downregulated genes across the datasets and the overlap of upregulated genes across the datasets.
After selecting shared DEGs, the “clusterProfiler” [ ] package was utilized to determine the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathways and Gene Ontology (GO) terms associated with these genes. The KEGG pathways and GO results with a false discovery rate (FDR) less than 0.05 were ordered based on the number of involved genes and their significance (FDR).

2.4

## Protein-protein interaction network
The “Cytoscape v3.10.2” [ ] was utilized to construct a PPI network for shared DEGs. Molecular interactions were retrieved from https://string-db.org/using the “STRING” [ ] plugin, with a confidence score threshold set above 0.4. Discrete nodes were excluded from the resultant network, and subsequent analysis was conducted to specify the degree of connectivity of each node within the network. Lastly, the “cytoHubba” [ ] plugin was employed to highlight hub genes based on the connectivity score.

2.5

## Construction of weighted gene co-expression network
Weighted gene co-expression networks were independently constructed for each dataset using the “WGCNA” [ ] R package to examine the presence of shared DEGs in modules correlating significantly with COPD. Initially, the quality of samples and genes was assessed using the goodSamplesGenes function. In the case of GSE239897, genes with FPKM values that were less than 10 in more than 90 % of the samples were discarded. Soft thresholds (β) were assessed using the pickSoftThreshold function, and optimal soft thresholds were selected based on the scale-free topology fit index above 0.8 and minimal mean connectivity. Then, scale-free networks were constructed using the blockwiseModules function by taking entries of a minimum number of genes equal to 30 and the optimal powers. Subsequently, dendrograms of the modules were drawn using the plotDendroAndColors function, and the correlations of module eigengenes with COPD status were evaluated. The corresponding correlation heatmaps were visualized using the “CorLevelPlot” [ ] package. Moreover, module membership (MM) and gene significance (GS), which are defined as the correlation of individual genes with module eigengenes and an COPD status, respectively, were assessed using Pearson's method. At last, shared DEGs found in significant modules across at least one dataset were identified to compile the final gene list for machine learning methods.

2.6

## Identification of key genes using machine learning methods
Random forest (RF) tree-based classification and logistic regression of the absolute shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO) methods were used to identify key genes. For this purpose, after merging the five datasets, the batch-effect correction was carried out by “sva” [ ] package. Principal component analysis (PCA) was employed to confirm the batch effect removal utilizing “FactoMineR” [ ] and “Factoextra” [ ] packages. Then, RF algorithm was used to limit the number of potential biomarkers or therapeutic targets based on the “MeanDecreaseGini” metrics which reflects the importance of genes. Subsequently using the “gmlnet” [ ] package, the selected subset of genes was subjected to LASSO regression, whereby genes with zero coefficient values were excluded from further consideration. Finally, the performance of the aforementioned classifier models was evaluated by drawing receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves and calculating the corresponding area under the curve (AUC) for each gene by the “proc” [ ] package. Genes with an AUC exceeding 0.7 were considered as candidate biomarkers.

2.7

## Evaluating the diagnostic efficacy of selected genes in blood specimens
Compared to lung tissue, which can be obtained by bronchial brushings during bronchoscopy [ ], blood is an easily accessible source for diagnosis. With this in mind, ROC analysis of diagnostic performance was carried out for the selected key genes using blood gene expression data. To this end, two blood transcriptomic data, including GSE54837 and GSE76705, were utilized considering their provided smoking history on samples. In GSE54837, seven samples, including six samples belonging to normal non-smokers and GSM1324621 as an outlier, were excluded from ROC analysis. In GSE76705, GSM2036035 and GSM2036094 were excluded from the dataset as outliers. Lastly, GSE54837 encompassed a total of 219 samples, including 135 COPD patients and 84 controls, and GSE76705 incorporated 227 samples, including 140 patients with COPD and 87 controls.

## Results
3.1

## Shared DEGs
Screening of DEGs between normal smoker individuals and smoker COPD patients in GSE27597, GSE38974, GSE47460, GSE76925, and GSE239897 identified 289, 429, 20, 453, and 170 DEGs, respectively ( ). The logFC values and adjusted p-values for the identified DEGs in GSE27597, GSE38974, GSE47460, GSE76925, and GSE239897 are presented in , respectively. The criteria for exhibiting consistent upregulation or downregulation (adjusted p-values <0.05 and |LogFC| > 1) in at least two datasets identified 64 shared DEGs. Shared DEGs comprised 40 upregulated and 24 downregulated genes. Among the genes, only SLCO4A1 showed opposite regulation in two datasets, while other genes demonstrated an identical direction of alterations across the datasets ( ). 

3.2

## Functional enrichment analysis of shared DEGs
KEGG pathway and GO functional enrichment analyses were employed to elucidate potential regulatory mechanisms of shared DEGs. It was evident that 21 pathways met the criteria of an adjusted p-value less than 0.05 ( A). Among the enriched pathways, those related to inflammatory responses in COPD comprised the TNF, IL-17, PI3K/Akt, C-type lectin receptor, NF-κB, and Toll-like receptor signaling pathways. According to the GO analysis, the functional role of shared DEGs was categorized into Biological Processes (BP), Cellular Components (CC), and Molecular Functions (MF). The most prominent terms in the BP category included response to molecules of bacterial origin, response to lipopolysaccharide, differentiation of mononuclear cells, and cell chemotaxis. The external side of the plasma membrane and the endoplasmic reticulum lumen were the CC terms in which shared DEGs were engaged. Shared DEGs were enriched in 15 MF terms, notably DNA binding transcription activator activity, DNA binding transcription activator activity RNA polymerase II-specific, integrin binding, and cytokine activity ( B). 

3.3

## Protein-protein interaction network
After removing 20 discrete genes, a PPI network was constructed for the remaining 44 shared DEGs to explore hub genes ( A). Hub genes are highly connected genes in a PPI network. Thus, the top five hub DEGs detected based on the degree of connectivity were interleukin 6 (IL6), interleukin 1 beta (IL1B), Fos proto-oncogene, AP-1 transcription factor subunit (FOS), prostaglandin-endoperoxide synthase 2 (PTGS2), and activating transcription factor 3 (ATF3). Moreover, the PPI of the top 10 genes was illustrated using the “cuytoHubbu” plugin ( B). 

3.4

## Weighted gene Co-expression network and identification of COPD-specific modules
To identify disease-specific modules and genes contributing to the pathogenesis of COPD, weighted gene co-expression networks were individually constructed for each dataset detailed in . Setting the fitting degree of the scale-free topology network model at 0.8 and considering minimal mean connectivity, the optimal soft thresholds (β) were computed as 14, 12, 10, 8, and 12 for GSE27597, GSE38974, GSE47460, GSE76925, and GSE239897, respectively ( ). Subsequently, block-wise construction of the gene modules led to the detection of 15, 29, 21, 26, and 18 modules in GSE27597, GSE38974, GSE47460, GSE76925, and GSE239897, respectively. Plots illustrating module membership versus gene significance for each module are presented in . Examination of the overlay of 64 shared DEGs and genes in modules significantly correlating with COPD in the datasets resulted in the verification of the key role of 63 DEGs by WGCNA analysis (detailed information is presented in ). In the GSE27597 dataset, 45 shared DEGs were spread across 7 significant modules (5 positively and 2 negatively correlated) with absolute correlation values ranging from 0.361 to 0.924. In the GSE38974 dataset, 38 shared DEGs were distributed in 8 significant modules (5 positively and 3 negatively correlated) with absolute correlation values ranging from 0.370 to 0.937. In the GSE47460 dataset, 18 shared DEGs were in 4 positively correlated significant modules and 1 negatively correlated significant module, with absolute correlation values ranging from 0.173 to 0.293. In the GSE76925 dataset, 20 shared DEGs were found in 7 significantly correlated modules (6 positively and 1 negatively correlated), with absolute correlation values ranging from 0.172 to 0.368. Among the modules significantly correlated with COPD in the GSE239897 dataset, 26 shared DEGs were identified in 2 positively correlated and 2 negatively correlated modules, with absolute correlation ranging from 0.346 to 0.505. 

3.5

## Determination of candidate biomarkers by RF and LASSO methods
The 63 intersecting genes identified by differential expression and WGCNA analyses underwent the decision tree-based RF algorithm and LASSO regression using batch-effect-corrected integrated data. As shown in , there was no obvious batch effect in the PCA graph of integrated data following “ComBat” normalization. Firstly, the top 30 genes were selected according to the classification of RF. Secondly, 5 genes with 0 coefficient by the LASSO regression were omitted, obtaining 25 genes. Ultimately, the top 6 genes, namely chemokine (C–C motif) ligand 19 (CCL19), Fc receptor-like A (FCRLA), CD79a molecule (CD79A), SLIT and NTRK-like family member 6 (SLITRK6), glutamate metabotropic receptor 8 (GRM8), and keratin 4 (KRT4), were singled out based on their AUC values from the ROC curve, which were 0.7137, 0.7106, 0.7102, 0.7102, 0.7089, and 0.7116, respectively. As illustrated in , each of the selected biomarkers demonstrated significant differential expression across a minimum of three datasets. Furthermore, all biomarkers exhibited differential expression within the integrated dataset. A summary of these potential biomarkers, demonstrating their protein products from UniProt [ ] and known functions from the gene database in NCBI [ ], is presented in . 

3.6

## Diagnostic performance of selected genes in blood samples
Despite their diagnostic power in lung tissue, none of the six identified key genes demonstrated diagnostic efficacy (AUC >0.7) in blood samples. Meanwhile, FCRLA, with AUCs of 0.6819 and 0.6908 in GSE54837 and GSE76705, respectively, showed the highest, yet still insufficient, diagnostic potential for COPD ( ).

## Discussion
The present study aimed to identify potential biomarkers or therapeutic targets responsible for the derivation of COPD in smokers using machine learning methods. Compared to studies utilizing similar expression datasets, this investigation benefits from consideration of smoking history, a larger sample size, and the use of machine learning methods to identify key genes.
A total of 64 genes differentially expressed in at least two datasets were defined as shared DEGs, including 40 upregulated and 24 downregulated genes. 21 KEGG pathways were revealed to be enriched with shared DEGs, among which TNF signaling and IL-17 signaling were the most significant pathways. TNF signaling is a well-established pathway contributing to the development and progression of COPD in smokers through multiple mechanisms, including recruitment and activation of leukocytes, production of inflammatory cytokines, remodeling of airways, hypersecretion of mucus, and apoptosis of bronchial epithelial cells [ ]. Similarly, IL-17 signaling is involved in the inflammatory response, remodeling of alveolar walls, and recruitment of neutrophils. Moreover, IL-17 level is elevated in the lungs of smoker COPD patients relative to smoker individuals without COPD, making it a potential biomarker for the development of COPD in smokers [ ].
As the GO analysis revealed, the functional roles of the 64 shared DEGs encompassed 401 BPs, 2 CCs, and 15 MFs, all with adjusted p-values less than 0.05. In the BP category, response to the molecule of bacterial origin (GO:0002237), response to lipopolysaccharide (GO:0032496), and mononuclear cell differentiation (GO:1903131) were the most important terms with 12 involved genes. Response to the molecule of bacterial origin is in line with the recent studies noted that microbiota imbalance is involved in the pathogenesis of COPD [ ]. Additionally, recognition of lipopolysaccharide originating from the outer cellular membrane of gram-negative bacteria by Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) can eventually lead to inflammation in the airways [ , ]. The external side of the plasma membrane and the endoplasmic reticulum lumen were the CC terms associated with shared DEGs (GO:0009897 and 0005788, respectively). In the BP category, noteworthy terms included DNA binding transcription activator activity (GO:0001216), DNA binding transcription activator activity, RNA polymerase II-specific (GO:0001228), integrin binding (GO:0005178), and cytokine activity (GO:0005125).
The top five hub genes identified by PPI network analysis were IL6, IL1B, FOS, PTGS2, and ATF3. Among them, IL6, IL1B, FOS, and PTGS2 are involved in the TNF-signaling pathway, the most enriched pathway by shared DEGs. This finding highlights the importance of the TNF-signaling pathway in the molecular pathogenesis of COPD and also suggests IL6, IL1B, FOS, and PTGS2 as potential therapeutic targets.
Co-expression analysis was employed to investigate the presence of shared DEGs within modules exhibiting a significant correlation with COPD. For this purpose, WGCNA analysis was conducted independently on each dataset, followed by determining the overlay of the 64 shared DEGs and modules correlated significantly with COPD. Finally, a more dependable gene list resulted from the overlay of DE and WGCNA analyses.
To efficiently identify the target genes from batch-effect-corrected integrated data, the top 30 genes based on the MeanDecreaseGini parameter of the RF algorithm were used as entries for LASSO regression. After rolling out the 5 genes with zero coefficients, ROC analysis was conducted to compute AUC values for the remaining 25 genes. Genes with AUC>0.7 were ascertained as target genes, including CCL19, FCRLA, CD79A, SLITRK6, GRM8, and KRT4.
CCL19 and its receptor (CCR7) have a central role in the recruitment and activation of T cells, B cells, and dendritic cells (DCs) [ ]. Comparing emphysema with non-emphysema COPD lung samples, it was observed that the expression of CCL19 is increased in all degrees of emphysema severity determined by CT scan [ ]. Moreover, in emphysematous COPD patients, upregulation of CCL19 was correlated with the destructive degree of emphysema [ ]. Multiplexed immunoassay showed that the serum level of CCL19 is increased in smoker COPD patients relative to healthy smokers [ ]. Furthermore, elevated CCL19 mRNA and protein levels were observed following chronic exposure to CS in the wild-type mouse model [ ].
FCRLA, a well-known B-cell protein located in the endoplasmic reticulum, is an intracellular member of the Fc receptor-like family [ , ]. Its expression correlated with the degree of destruction in the alveolar walls and was significantly upregulated in COPD patients [ ]. Similarly, enhanced expression of FCRLA was reported in peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) following exposure to cigarette smoke extract (CSE) for 24 h [ ]. An epigenomic study of newborn blood specimens whose mother had smoked throughout pregnancy identified FCRLA as a differentially methylated gene correlating with maternal plasma cotinine levels [ ].
Heterodimerization of CD79A and CD79B forms the signaling transduction moiety of the B-cell receptor (BCR), contributing to the maturation, survival, and activation of B lymphocytes [ ]. By conducting WGCNA on the lung transcriptome of smoker COPD patients and the control group, CD79A and FCRLA were identified as hub genes in the most prominent module correlating with COPD severity based on the Goddard score [ ]. Many transcriptomics studies of COPD lung samples have demonstrated an increased expression of CD79A [ , ]. However, the analysis of blood gene expression data samples showed a downward trend in expression with increased severity of COPD [ ]. The reason behind this inconsistency has remained vague; nevertheless, it is hypothesized that the reduced level of CD79A in blood stems from the recruitment of B-cells to the lung, as CD79A level in peripheral blood correlates with B-cell counts [ ].
SLITRK6, a transmembrane protein belonging to the SLITRK family, promotes cell division via the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway in lung adenocarcinoma. Furthermore, both in vivo and in vitro knockdown of SLITRK6 led to tumor suppression [ ]. On the contrary, the DE analysis finding of the current study revealed SLITRK6 was significantly downregulated in COPD smokers compared to healthy smokers in GSE38974, GSE47460, and GSE76925 with log2FC of −1.33, −0.84, and −1.22, respectively. Analysis of microarray expression of small epithelial airway cells has also demonstrated that SLITRK6 transcript levels drop in normal smokers in comparison with non-smokers. Moreover, SLITRK6 has been previously identified as a noteworthy biomarker for COPD based on machine learning results [ ].
Glutamate receptor 8, encoded by GRM8, is a G protein-coupled receptor that inhibits the enzyme responsible for producing cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) [ ]. On the one hand, the intracellular level of cAMP has a negative correlation with the production of pro-inflammatory mediators and a positive correlation with anti-inflammatory mediators in various immune cells. In contrast, this study observed a significant downregulation of GRM8 in lung tissue across the datasets. In accordance with this finding, a previous study underscored consistent downregulation of GRM8 across multiple stages of COPD [ ]. On the other hand, a constant increase in the cAMP level in T-cells leads to the cessation of the cell cycle at the G1 checkpoint [ ]. The latter suggests a potential link between the downregulation of GRM8 and COPD pathogenesis; however, as cAMP activity is cell-type-specific, further investigations at single-cell resolution are required to elucidate the role of GRM8 in COPD lungs. A genome-wide association study (GWAS) on cancerous lung tissues identified GRM8 as a gene associated with cigarette smoke-induced disorders [ ]. In the present study, GRM8 demonstrated the highest classification potential based on both Gini and accuracy metrics in RF classification, followed by KRT4.
KRT4, encoding cytokeratin 4, belongs to the keratin gene family. Cytokeratins are considered markers of cellular differentiation of epithelial cells and are involved in the maintenance of epithelial integrity [ , ]. KRT4 mRNA level is reported to be downregulated in the basal epithelial cells and lung tissue of COPD patients compared to controls [ , ]. Dysregulation of other cytokeratins in epithelial cells has also been observed in response to CS. For instance, disruption in the differentiation of primary human bronchial epithelial cells (pHBECs) has been documented by observing reduced protein levels of KRT5 and KRT14 after prolonged exposure to CSE [ ]. While determining the precise role of KRT4 in COPD requires further investigations, keratins have recently been found to be also involved in the immune response and inflammation [ ].
Emerging research has underscored the contribution of adaptive immune cells in COPD pathogenesis [ ]. Herein, FCRLA and CD79A were highlighted by multiple analyses, underscoring the pivotal role of B-cells in CS-induced COPD. Consistently, CCL19, which is involved in the recruitment of lymphocytes [ ], was upregulated and identified as a potential biomarker.
While smoking has been widely suggested as the main cause of COPD, a multitude of other factors, such as genetic mutations [ ], air pollution [ ], and autoimmune responses [ ], can favor the onset of the disease. With this heterogeneity in the pathogenesis of COPD in mind, the identification of applicable biomarkers for type-specific COPD not only facilitates the distinction of different types but also makes it possible to establish phenotype-specific therapeutic strategies [ ]. The key genes identified in the study, namely CCL19, FCRLA, CD79A, SLITRK6, GRM8, and KRT4, hold potential in detecting the onset of the pathogenesis of smoking-induced COPD among smoker individuals. However, these candidates should be tested in prospective cohorts to provide insight on the reproducibility of their diagnostic performance before being proposed as biomarkers. By removing non-smokers from both case and control groups, this study made an effort to identify molecular signatures that reflect the onset of COPD. Furthermore, the significant expression patterns of these genes reflect their substantial involvement in biological mechanisms that contribute to the disease progression. The study of biomarkers can additionally lead to the identification of these key pathways that could be potentially considered as therapeutic targets. In contrast to traditional diagnostic methods that centrally rely on the onset of clinical manifestations, RNA biomarkers are a valuable source of the assessment of disease presence. These phenotype-specific potential biomarkers can be utilized for early detection as well as patient classification, facilitating more targeted therapies.
None of the six key genes demonstrated acceptable diagnostic efficacy in blood specimens, which underscores tissue-specific transcriptomic alterations in COPD. For instance, prior studies have shown that the expression pattern of CD79A is not consistent in lung tissue and blood [ ]. Given the challenges associated with acquiring lung tissue samples, further transcriptomic analysis is needed to investigate potential blood-based biomarkers.
Besides the insights provided, this study faced considerable limitations. Notably, comprehensive clinicodemographic information was missing from most sample information tables accompanying the datasets. In particular, the filtering of individuals with smoking histories differs from the information recorded in the corresponding reference articles. In fact, factors such as disease severity, comorbidities, prior treatment history, disease progression (including stable and exacerbation phases), and inconsistencies in smoking history records may substantially impact the results, especially given the heterogeneous nature of COPD, which can lead to varying outcomes in independent investigations. Moreover, lung tissue consists of a mixture of cells, and substantial changes in sample composition may significantly impact the findings. Additionally, we included only datasets that provided smoking histories for both the case and control groups, and the study was limited to assessing the reproducibility of its findings in external datasets. Given the heterogeneity in COPD pathogenesis, further investigations are undoubtedly required to provide insights into the applicability of the suggested biomarkers.

5

## Conclusion
The present study aimed to identify key genes contributing to the development of COPD in smokers. CCL19, FCRLA, CD79A, SLITRK6, GRM8, and KRT4 were selected as potential biomarkers or therapeutic targets that exhibited significant dysregulation in the lung tissue of smoker COPD patients compared to healthy smokers. While the association of CCL19, FCRLA, CD79A, and SLITRK6 with COPD has been demonstrated in previous studies, GRM8 and KRT4 are novel biomarkers suggested by the results of the present study. Discovering applicable biomarkers for detecting the onset of COPD in smokers can not only facilitate early diagnosis and treatment but also help prevent disease progression and inappropriate therapeutic approaches. Herein, upregulated IL6 was identified as the central hub gene in the PPI network of DEGs in COPD, and being involved in the TNF-signaling pathway along with other hub genes such as IL1B, FOS, and PTGS2 underscored the pathway as a therapeutic target. Meanwhile, given that the findings rely on computational methods, it is crucial to assess the efficacy and potential side effects of the approach.
