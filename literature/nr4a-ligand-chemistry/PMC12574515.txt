AbbasMohamed SethiGautam KumarAlan Prem – ] It is among the top 20 most prevalent types of cancer worldwide, with an estimated 321,476 new cases and 248,305 deaths reported globally in 2022, according to GLOBOCAN data.[ ] This heterogeneous group of malignancies profoundly impacts psychosocial well-being and physical and cognitive functioning.[ , ] Depending on the tumor’s size and anatomical location, patients may experience a spectrum of cognitive and behavioral impairments, such as personality changes, difficulties with complex tasks, and impaired information processing.[ – ] Additional symptoms commonly include persistent headaches, alterations in speech, vision, and hearing, mood disturbances, reduced attention span, memory deficits, seizures, coordination difficulties, pronounced fatigue, symptoms of depression, and overall cognitive decline.[ , , , ]
There are various types of brain cancer, including brainstem glioma, craniopharyngioma, germ cell tumors, glioblastoma (GBM), glioma, and astrocytoma.[ , – ] According to the 2021 World Health Organization (WHO) classification of central nervous system tumors, these malignancies are broadly categorized into 12 distinct groups.[ ] The global age-adjusted prevalence of brain cancer is 5.57 per 100,000 individuals, with astrocytic tumors comprising more than half of all reported cases.[ ] GBM, a type of astrocytic tumor, is diagnosed more frequently than lower-grade astrocytomas.[ ] GBM is also the most common malignant brain tumor, and it accounts for up to 14.2% of all primary brain and other central nervous system tumors.[ ] It is highly invasive and is recognized as one of the most devastating and lethal neoplasms, characterized by a high mitotic index, extensive necrosis, and pronounced microvascular proliferation.[ , , – ] Due to their invasive properties, gliomas pose substantial challenges in treatment.[ – ] The current standard treatment regimen for GBM includes maximally safe surgical resection followed by radiotherapy, chemotherapy, targeted therapy, and tumor-treating fields.[ , ] To date, several drugs have received approval of Food and Drug Administration of the United States for GBM treatment, including bevacizumab for recurrent GBM and temozolomide (TMZ) for newly diagnosed cases.[ , ] However, these therapeutic interventions have shown limited efficacy in improving the prognosis of GBM patients and associated with various challenges.[ , , , – ] Therefore, a critical need remains to develop more effective and targeted treatment strategies for GBM.
One of the significant challenges in treating brain cancers is the ability to effectively traverse the blood–brain barrier and deliver anticancer agents to the tumor site.[ , ] Identifying delivery methods that are both minimally invasive and efficacious is critical for successfully managing brain tumors.[ , ] The resistance of brain tumors to both conventional and novel therapeutic approaches is, at least in part, attributable to the unique cell-intrinsic and microenvironmental properties of neural tissue.[ ] Targeted and combinational strategies offer considerable promise in overcoming these barriers.[ , , ] Novel approaches that specifically direct therapeutic agents to tumor sites in the brain may enhance treatment efficacy while minimizing systemic toxicity.[ ] Consequently, intensive research efforts are essential to develop and optimize innovative therapeutic strategies that can improve or potentially replace current treatment modalities for brain cancer. The exploration and identification of novel molecular targets remain imperative for advancing brain cancer management.
In addition, gut microbiota has emerged as a nongenetic factor influencing both the development of brain tumors and the efficacy of cancer therapies.[ ] Microbial populations can modulate the host immune system and promote inflammation, potentially contributing to sustained proliferative signaling, evasion of apoptosis, induction of angiogenesis, and tumor invasiveness.[ ] Furthermore, these microbes produce metabolites such as hormones, indole derivatives, and secondary bile acids that act as ligands for nuclear receptors (NRs), which are expressed in the gastrointestinal tract.[ ] This suggests that NRs may play a critical role in the development and regulation of brain cancer.
Several studies have demonstrated that NRs are essential in regulating cellular functions and are implicated in various pathological conditions, including cancer.[ – ] The NR superfamily comprises a group of ligand-dependent transcription factors (TFs) characterized by conserved domain structures and is broadly classified into three categories: classic endocrine receptors, orphan receptors, and adopted-orphan receptors.[ , , ] Members of this superfamily share a common structural organization consisting of a variable N-terminal domain (NTD), a DNA-binding domain (DBD), a conserved ligand-binding domain (LBD), and a variable C-terminal domain (CTD) [Figure ].[ , , ] Binding of ligands, either agonists or antagonists, induces conformational changes in specific helices within the LBD, resulting in either an active or repressive transcriptional state.[ – ] To precisely regulate target gene expression, NRs may function in concert with other transcription factors and coregulatory proteins.[ ] Additionally, they must integrate signals from key metabolic sensors such as activated protein kinase (AMPK) and mechanistic target of rapamycin (mTOR) complexes, while synchronizing their activity with circadian rhythm mechanisms.[ ]
 NRs are key regulators of numerous physiological processes, including development, reproduction, cellular differentiation, immune responses, stress adaptation, and metabolism.[ , , ] These receptors are integral components of complex signaling networks and modulate the expression of a broad range of genes.[ ] NRs also regulate genes involved in critical cellular processes such as energy production, autophagy, and mitochondrial biogenesis.[ ] Furthermore, NRs play a pivotal role in the pathophysiology of several diseases, including obesity, atherosclerosis, diabetes, and cancer.[ ] Despite their promising therapeutic potential, NRs can also induce adverse effects. For example, Meijer et al[ ] reported that the glucocorticoid receptor (GR), which is ubiquitously expressed in most cell types, mediates many of the classic side effects associated with glucocorticoid (GC) overexposure. Although both agonists and antagonists of GR can provide therapeutic benefits in various disease contexts, their widespread expression and involvement in multiple physiological processes often result in unintended side effects.[ ] Therefore, studies investigating the therapeutic potential of NR modulators should carefully consider and evaluate the associated toxicities.
It is well-established that various NRs are associated with cancer development.[ , , ] NRs have been extensively investigated and have emerged as promising therapeutic targets across a broad spectrum of cancer types.[ , ] Their capacity for selective modulation by small molecules makes them attractive as therapeutic candidates.[ , , – ] Several NRs have also been implicated in brain cancer, highlighting their potential as novel therapeutic targets. Despite the considerable body of research exploring the role of NRs in brain cancer, a comprehensive synthesis of studies delineating their functional roles and therapeutic relevance in this context remains lacking. This review examines the therapeutic relevance of NRs in brain cancer, with particular emphasis on receptor-specific agonists and antagonists as promising candidates for prevention and treatment.

## Nuclear Receptor Signaling
Although some NRs can bind DNA as monomers, many function more commonly as homodimers or heterodimers.[ – ] NRs are classified into four subgroups based on their mode of action: type I, type II, type III, and type IV.[ , ] Type I includes androgen receptors (ARs), estrogen receptors (ERs), glucocorticoid receptors (GRs), and progesterone receptors (PRs), which are associated with heat shock proteins in the cytosol.[ , ] Upon ligand binding, these receptors dissociate from their chaperone proteins, homodimerize, and translocate to the nucleus.[ ] In the nucleus, it recruits coactivator proteins, binds to DNA, and activates the transcription of target genes.[ – ] In contrast, type II receptors such as vitamin D receptors (VDRs), retinoic acid receptors (RARs), thyroid hormone receptors (TRs), peroxisome-proliferator activated receptors (PPARs), and liver X receptors (LXRs), heterodimerize with retinoid X receptors (RXRs) and remain bound to specific DNA response elements in the nucleus and associated with co-regulators.[ , – ] Upon ligand binding, corepressor complexes are displaced and replaced by coactivator complexes, leading to target gene transcriptional activation.[ , , ] Type III includes orphan receptors that function similarly to type I receptors, although they recognize distinct DNA response elements.[ , ] Type IV NRs also resemble type II receptors in their mechanism of action but bind DNA as monomers, recognizing extended half-sites within response elements.[ , , ] Ligand binding (agonist or antagonist) to NRs induces a conformational change in the receptor, resulting in the release of corepressors and recruitment of coactivators, which ultimately activate or suppress the transcription of target genes, including those involved in various hallmarks of cancer [Figure ].[ , , ] In conclusion, these NRs represent viable targets for therapeutic intervention, and the design of ligands that selectively modulate these receptors may offer a promising strategy for treating diverse diseases.
 

## Nuclear Receptors in Brain Cancer
NRs have gained substantial prominence in cancer research due to their established role in regulating disease processes. Numerous studies have suggested that NRs may contribute to brain cancer pathophysiology, with evidence pointing toward significant alterations in their expression profiles [Table ].[ – ] Several NRs have been reported to be involved in brain cancer, including ARs, ERs, estrogen-related receptors (ERRs), GRs, NR subfamily 4 group A (NR4A), NR subfamily 1 group D member 2 (NR1D2), NR subfamily 5 group A member 2 (NR5A2), LXRs, PPARs, PRs, RARs, TLX, TRs, VDRs, and RXRs. As such, this review focuses on NRs that are notably implicated in brain cancer, exploring their potential as prognostic markers and their therapeutic relevance through modulation by receptor-specific agonists or antagonists. This review aims to provide a comprehensive overview of the complex expression dynamics of NRs in brain cancer and the molecular mechanisms through which these receptors exert their effects on ligand binding [Table ].[ , , – , – , , – ]
 

## Androgen receptors
ARs are ligand-activated TFs belonging to the NR subfamily 3 group C, and they play a crucial role in the regulation of eukaryotic gene expression.[ , ]
AR is a 110 kDa protein-coding gene on the X chromosome (Xq12) and comprises eight exons.[ , ] It is typically expressed in tissues of several organs, including the breast, cervix, endometrium, epididymis, fallopian tube, kidney, seminal vesicle, and testis.[ , ]
Immunohistochemical analysis of tissues of patient with GBM has demonstrated a significant upregulation of AR expression.[ , ] The ligand-dependent activity of AR has been shown to regulate several cellular processes, including survival, proliferation, and invasion.[ , , ] For instance, AR knockdown via siRNA in U87 GBM cells reduced cell proliferation, glutathione peroxidase 4 (GPX4) expression, and increased lipid peroxidation.[ ] Treatment with the curcumin analog ALZ003 further suppressed AR levels and induced apoptosis by upregulating caspase-3/7, caspase-8, and caspase-9 expressions. In vivo administration of ALZ003, either alone or in combination with TMZ, in NOD-SCID mice transplanted with U87 cells led to a marked reduction in tumor volume, AR expression, and levels of oncogenic markers such as c-Myc, Ki-67, and proliferating cell nuclear antigen (PCNA), while improving survival independently of TMZ resistance.[ ]
Similarly, treatment of DBTRG-05MG cells with cedrol, a natural phytochemical, inhibited the 5α-dihydrotestosterone-induced nuclear translocation of AR and suppressed downstream targets such as KLK3 and TMPRSS2.[ ] Cedrol induced cytotoxicity by increasing intracellular reactive oxygen species (ROS) and DNA damage by increasing the expression of H2A histone family member X (γ-H2AX), triggering the ataxia telangiectasia mutated (ATM)/checkpoint kinase 2 (Chk2) and ataxia-telangiectasia mutated, and Rad3 related (ATR)/checkpoint kinase 1 (Chk1) pathways.[ ] It also induced G0/G1 cell cycle arrest and apoptosis by upregulating p53 and p21, and downregulating cyclins A, B1, D1, CDK2, CDK4, and PCNA.[ ] Cedrol increased apoptotic markers, including AIF, Bax, cleaved caspase-3, cleaved caspase-8, cleaved caspase-9, and Fas-L, while decreasing Bcl-2 levels. Co-treatment with TMZ further potentiated these effects and sensitized DBTRG-05MG cells to chemotherapy. These effects were validated in DBTRG-05MG xenograft mouse models.[ ]
Moreover, treatment with AR antagonists such as enzalutamide and seviteronel decreased colony formation in GBM cells and sensitized the cells to radiation.[ ] Enzalutamide combined with radiation in mice bearing LN18 and T98G cell xenografts resulted in delayed DNA damage repair.[ ] Another approach using compound I (N-[3-(2,5-Dichlorobenzyloxy)-4-(methanesulfonylmethylamino)-phenyl]-4-bromobenzamide), a nimesulide analog and HSP27 inhibitor, induced proteasome-mediated degradation of AR and significantly reduced tumor burden in vivo without observable toxicity.[ ] Furthermore, enzalutamide treatment was associated with a reduction in the glioma stem cell population, evidenced by decreased expression of CD133, OCT4, Nanog, SOX2, and CD90, along with key genes associated with the TGF-β pathway, cell cycle, and proliferation.[ ]
In vivo, enzalutamide treatment improved survival in GBM-bearing mice and reduced the proportion of CD133+, c-Myc+, and AR+ cells.[ ] Collectively, these studies indicate that targeting AR using specific agonists or antagonists, either as monotherapies or in combination with established chemotherapeutics such as TMZ, could serve as a promising alternative strategy for overcoming therapeutic resistance in brain cancer. However, further investigations are essential to validate these findings and establish their clinical efficacy.

## Estrogen receptors
ERs are members of the NR superfamily that primarily function as ligand-inducible TFs and mediate diverse biological effects through multiple mechanisms.[ – ] They exist as two main subtypes: ERα (NR3A1) and ERβ (NR3A2).[ – ] Structurally, ERs share the conserved domain architecture characteristic of the NR family, including DNA-binding, ligand-binding, and activation function domains.[ – ] Numerous ER ligands have been developed that effectively inhibit tumor growth and induce apoptosis in cancer cells.[ ] Several studies have reported overexpression of ERs at variable levels in brain cancer tissues and various cell lines, suggesting a functional role for ERs in brain tumor biology.[ – , – ] However, contrasting reports have also shown decreased ER expression in F98 rat glioma cells and astrocytic tumors, indicating potential context-dependent roles of ER signaling in brain cancers.[ , ]
Numerous studies have explored the roles of ERs and their modulators in brain cancer. For example, the inactivation of the novel ERα variant, ERα-36, has been shown to enhance tamoxifen (TAM) sensitivity, a selective ER modulator (SERM), and reduce autophagy in U87 glioma cells.[ ] TAM treatment increased the expression of autophagy-related genes such as Beclin1, ATG5, and ATG12, while it decreased P62 levels. In contrast, treatment with IC162, an ERα-36-specific modulator, in combination with TAM resulted in an elevated growth rate in these cells. Moreover, co-treatment with ICI182,780 (an ER antagonist), 1,3,5-tris (4-hydroxyphenyl)-4-propyl-1H-pyrazole (PPT) (an ERα-specific agonist), or autophagy inhibitors such as 3-methyladenine and chloroquine alongside TAM significantly reduced cell proliferation.[ ] In another study, genetic ablation of ERβ resulted in a more aggressive GBM phenotype in ERβ knockout (KO) GBM cells.[ ] The reintroduction of ERβ1 into ERβ-KO cells reversed these oncogenic effects, whereas ERβ5 reintroduction exacerbated tumorigenicity by modulating the mTOR and NF-κB pathways. Notably, ERβ1, but not ERβ5, expression prolonged survival in mice bearing GBM tumors.[ ] Additional research revealed that overexpression of ERβ led to the downregulation of genes involved in ATM signaling, DNA recombination and repair, and cell cycle checkpoint regulation.[ ] ERβ-overexpressing cells displayed increased γH2AX foci following TMZ treatment, suggesting a critical role for ERβ in impairing homologous recombination-mediated DNA repair by inhibiting ATM expression and activation. Enhanced survival outcomes were observed in both TMZ-sensitive and -resistant GBM models treated with TMZ in the presence of ERβ overexpression.[ ] Another study reported that treatment with TAM and CC-8490, a benzopyranone with known SERM activity, induced growth inhibition in glioma cell lines, including U87MG, U138MG, and U373MG.[ ] These treatments led to hallmark apoptotic changes such as nuclear condensation, fragmentation, and cell shrinkage. Moreover, intraperitoneal administration of these selective estrogen receptor modulators (SERMs) significantly reduced tumor weight and volume in murine models.[ ] Another study has demonstrated that treatment with LY500307, an ERβ agonist, significantly inhibited proliferation, viability, and survival of various GBM cell lines, including U87, U251, LN229, T98G, and patient-derived primary cells.[ ] LY500307 also promoted apoptosis and modulated key pathways in cell cycle regulation, DNA damage response, and programmed cell death. Importantly, LY500307 treatment significantly improved survival in mice bearing tumors in the GL26 syngeneic glioma model and induced apoptosis in orthotopic GBM tumor models.[ ]
Another study demonstrated that treatment with toosendanin (TSN), a bioactive extract derived from a medicinal herb, reduced cell proliferation, upregulated ERβ and p53 expression, and induced apoptosis in U87 and C6 GBM cells.[ ] TSN administration also significantly suppressed tumor progression and weight in a U87 xenograft mouse model.[ ] In a related study, hypoxic conditions markedly elevated the expression of ERβ isoforms—ERβ1, ERβ2, and ERβ5—in U87 cells.[ ] Overexpression of ERβ1 or ERβ5 in these cells led to reduced proliferation, decreased extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK) activation, and reduced matrix metallopeptidase (MMP) 2 expression.[ ] In another investigation, the treatment of liquiritigenin, an ERβ agonist, significantly increased ERβ expression in TMZ-resistant U138 glioma cells, thereby sensitizing them to TMZ-induced proliferation inhibition.[ ] However, this sensitizing effect was abrogated either by ERβ knockdown or activation of the PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling pathway via insulin-like growth factor I (IGF-I).[ ]
Similarly, another study reported that treatment with 12-O-tetradecanoylphorbol-13-acetate (TPA) increased the interaction and phosphorylation of ERβ with protein kinase C (PKC)α and PKCδ, resulting in reduced proliferation and invasion of Daoy medulloblastoma cells.[ ] In contrast, treatment with diarylpropionitrile (DPN), an ERβ agonist, significantly promoted proliferation and invasion in Daoy and D283 medulloblastoma cell lines.[ ] Another study reported that treatment with 17β-estradiol (E2) or DPN enhanced cellular proliferation and migration in Daoy and D283 cells.[ ] However, the use of the ER antagonist Faslodex (ICI182,780) effectively reversed all estrogen-mediated effects in both in vitro and xenograft models of human medulloblastoma.[ ] Interestingly, another study observed that high concentrations of E2 significantly reduced cell viability in LN229 and LN18 glioma cell lines compared to controls.[ ] Furthermore, these E2-pretreated cells displayed increased sensitivity to TMZ treatment.[ ] Another study has demonstrated that in the PFSK1 cell line, E2 treatment enhanced cell migration and activated ERK1/2 phosphorylation.[ ] However, these effects were inhibited by co-treatment with ICI182,780, suggesting an ER-mediated mechanism.[ ] Another investigation revealed that E2 and PPT significantly increased cell numbers in U373 and D54 cells, whereas DPN did not elicit this effect.[ ] Moreover, both E2 and ICI182,780 downregulated ERα expression in U373 cells.[ ] Conversely, treatment with tibolone, an ER agonist, led to a dose-dependent decrease in cell number and tumor inhibition in human glioma cell lines, highlighting its potential anti-proliferative activity.[ ] Additionally, the E2 treatment exhibited divergent effects on connexin 43 (Cx43) expression: it decreased Cx43 in C6 cells, promoting migration, but increased it in F98 glioma cells.[ ] E2 also upregulated ERβ in C6 cells while downregulating ERα in F98 cells. These differential responses are likely due to cell line-specific ER expression profiles.[ ] A separate study demonstrated that treatment with histone deacetylase inhibitors (HDACi) such as panobinostat and romidepsin upregulated ERβ1 expression in U87, U251, and patient-derived GSC-111010 primary GBM cells.[ ] Co-treatment with the ERβ agonist LY500307 significantly suppressed cell viability, colony formation, and invasion while promoting apoptosis. Importantly, the combination of LY500307 and panobinostat enhanced survival in GBM-bearing mice.[ ]
In patient samples, ERβ expression was found to be significantly reduced as astrocytic tumor grade increased, while levels of ER coactivators, amplified in breast cancer 1 (AIB1), proline, glutamic acid, and leucine-rich protein 1 (PELP1), and transcriptional mediators/intermediary factor 2 (TIF2) were elevated.[ ] These elevated coactivator levels were associated with poor prognosis of astrocytic tumors.[ ] Another clinical investigation demonstrated that low ERβ expression significantly correlated with high-grade astrocytic tumors, elevated cytoplasmic expression of Bcl-2-associated athanogene-1 (BAG-1), and poor patient survival. Multivariate analysis further confirmed the prognostic value of ERβ expression for overall survival.[ ] Moreover, a statistically significant association was observed between nuclear BAG-1 and cytoplasmic HSP70 expression.[ ] Collectively, these findings suggest that ERs, particularly Erβ, are frequently dysregulated in brain cancers and play crucial roles in modulating tumor cell survival, proliferation, invasion, and treatment responsiveness.

## Glucocorticoid receptors
GR, a member of the NR superfamily, is encoded by the NR3C1 gene and exists primarily as two protein isoforms: GRα and GRβ.[ ] GR plays a fundamental role in modulating gene expression by either directly binding to GC response elements or indirectly influencing the activity of other transcription factors.[ ] GR is ubiquitously expressed across nearly all human tissues and is composed of three structural and functional domains: the NTD, DBD, and LBD.[ – ]
The role of GRs, particularly GRβ, in the development and progression of brain cancer has been investigated extensively. For example, an in vitro study revealed a significant upregulation of GRβ in both primary glioma cells and established glioma cell lines, whereas GRα expression remained relatively unchanged.[ ] GRβ was shown to interact with β-catenin, a crucial mediator of cell proliferation and migration in both injured astrocytes and glioma cells. Further mechanistic analysis demonstrated that the GRβ/β-catenin interaction acts as a key modulator of astrocyte reactivity through the Wnt/β-catenin/T-cell factor (TCF) signaling pathway, exerting a dominant-negative effect on GRα-mediated transrepression via GSK-3β-independent mechanism. These findings highlight the GRβ’s pivotal role in promoting astrocyte proliferation and migration following injury, as well as in the pathogenesis of gliomas.[ ] Supporting this observation, another study reported that GRβ knockdown significantly reduced the proliferation of U118 and Shg44 glioma cells in vitro and inhibited tumor growth in vivo.[ ] Additionally, GRβ silencing was associated with decreased TCF/lymphoid enhancer factor (LEF) transcriptional activity, despite no disruption to the β-catenin/TCF complex itself. Interestingly, both GRα and GRβ were found to directly interact with TCF-4. However, only GRβ was necessary to sustain TCF/LEF activity under hormone-free conditions and was sufficient to maintain TCF/LEF signaling even in the absence of β-catenin stabilization, suggesting a unique crosstalk mechanism between GRβ and TCF-4 in regulating Wnt signaling and glioma proliferation.[ ]
Interestingly, a case report involving two patients with GBM demonstrated the antineoplastic potential of methylprednisolone (MTP) pulse therapy, a synthetic glucocorticoid, in GR-positive GBM cases.[ , ] Although initial tumor regression was observed, disease progression ensued despite an increased MTP dosage. This outcome was hypothesized to result from the selective elimination of GR-positive tumor cells, thereby favoring the expansion of GR-negative cell populations. This hypothesis was supported by a notable reduction in GR levels observed during reoperation.[ ] Similarly, Piette et al[ ] demonstrated that treatment with another synthetic glucocorticoid, dexamethasone, significantly inhibited the migration and invasion of U373 MG glioma cells via suppression of the ERK1/2 MAPK signaling pathway in a GR-dependent manner.[ , ] To validate whether this effect was GR-mediated, the GR antagonist mifepristone (RU486) was used. RU486 alone at a concentration of 10 μmol/L did not influence cell migration or invasion. However, it effectively reversed the dexamethasone-induced inhibition of both processes, confirming the involvement of GR in mediating these effects. Additionally, the study found that exogenous administration of macrophage migration inhibitory factor (MIF) counteracted the anti-migratory and anti-invasive effects of dexamethasone on U373 MG cells. Furthermore, in glucocorticoid-resistant Hs 683 glioma cells known to express high levels of endogenous MIF, treatment with ISO-1, a pharmacological inhibitor of MIF, significantly enhanced the sensitivity of these cells to the migration-inhibitory effects of dexamethasone.[ ]
In another study, chronic stress was shown to promote glioma growth in a U87MG xenograft mouse model, which was associated with significantly elevated serum levels of the stress hormones GC and noradrenaline.[ ] Mechanistically, GC was found to bind to the GR, leading to activation of the PI3K/Akt signaling pathway in vitro. Notably, treatment with the GR antagonist mifepristone (RU486) effectively suppressed the expression of p-Akt, thereby inhibiting stress hormone-induced glioma cell proliferation.[ ] Consistent with these findings, Pinski et al[ ] demonstrated that RU486 administration in U87MG xenograft models resulted in a significant, dose-dependent reduction in both tumor volume and weight, along with decreased cytosolic GR protein expression.[ ] RU486 also antagonized the proliferative effect of dexamethasone on U87MG cells in vitro, suggesting its potential as a therapeutic agent for brain tumors such as malignant gliomas.[ ] Supporting this, a case report documented the successful treatment of multiple meningioma patients with mifepristone, reporting up to a 72% reduction in tumor volume as assessed by magnetic resonance imaging.[ ]
Furthermore, studies using primary cultures of anaplastic astrocytoma cells revealed that dexamethasone significantly inhibited cell growth in a GR-dependent manner.[ ] However, despite its antiproliferative effects, dexamethasone has also been reported to induce resistance to various anticancer drugs in glioma cells.[ , ] In this context, a study by Gorman et al[ ] showed that dexamethasone diminished staurosporine-induced apoptosis and necrosis in a GR-dependent manner.[ ] Additionally, dexamethasone was found to attenuate the caspase activity of chemotherapeutic agents such as camptothecin and etoposide by upregulating the expression of the anti-apoptotic protein Bcl-xL in D384 astrocytoma cells. Given that synthetic glucocorticoids such as dexamethasone are frequently used to manage cerebral edema in patients with glioma, their interference with the efficacy of anticancer therapies may present a significant clinical concern.[ ]

## Liver X receptors
LXRs belong to the NR superfamily of ligand-activated TFs.[ , ] Two isoforms of LXRs have been identified: LXRα (NR1H3) and LXRβ (NR1H2).[ , ] LXRs regulate a broad range of physiological processes, including excretion, de novo synthesis of cholesterol, detoxification of bile acids or lipids, immunity, neurological functions, glucose homeostasis, skin development, and epidermal homeostasis.[ – ] Although LXRβ is ubiquitously expressed across various tissues, LXRα expression is more restricted and is predominantly found in tissues that are central to lipid metabolism, such as the liver, adipose tissue, kidney, small intestine, and spleen.[ , ]
A growing body of evidence has demonstrated that the deficiency or dysregulation of LXRs is associated with a range of pathological conditions in humans, including diabetes, skin disorders, inflammation, and cancer.[ – ] LXRs have also been implicated in the development and progression of brain cancers. For instance, a notable study revealed that treatment with LXR-623, a synthetic LXR agonist, induced cell death and upregulated ATP-binding cassette subfamily A member 1 (ABCA1), ATP-binding cassette transporter G1 (ABCG1), and E3 ubiquitin ligase-inducible degrader of the low-density lipoprotein receptor (IDOL), while simultaneously downregulating low-density lipoprotein receptor (LDLR) levels in both established GBM cell lines and patient-derived primary GBM cells.[ ] This effect was specifically dependent on LXRβ and not LXRα. Moreover, LXR-623 treatment in GBM xenograft models similarly resulted in suppressed tumor growth and improved survival outcomes.[ ] Additionally, another investigation demonstrated that LXRβ is essential for maintaining the survival of densely plated glioma cells.[ ] CRISPR/Cas9-mediated knockdown of LXRβ in TS543 and TS576 glioma cell lines led to a significant reduction in ABCA1 expression, impaired cell survival and cell cycle progression, and diminished feedback inhibition of the mevalonate pathway.[ ] Collectively, these findings provide preliminary evidence supporting the therapeutic potential of targeting LXRβ in brain tumors such as gliomas. Nevertheless, further preclinical and clinical studies are warranted to fully elucidate the role of LXRs in brain cancer and to validate their utility as therapeutic targets.

## Nuclear receptor subfamily 4 group A receptors
The NR4A family, classified as orphan NRs, comprises three members: Nur77 (NR4A1), Nurr1 (NR4A2), and neuron-derived orphan receptor 1 (NOR1, NR4A3).[ , ] These receptors exhibit context-dependent functions, acting as either tumor suppressors or promoters depending on the cellular environment.[ ] Several studies have suggested a role for Nur77 in the development of brain cancer. For instance, one investigation demonstrated that treatment with n-butylidene phthalide (BP), an anticancer compound, led to an increase in Nur77 expression in GBM 8401 GBM cells.[ ] Additionally, BP treatment triggered the translocation of Nur77 from the nucleus to the cytoplasm, which was associated with the release of cytochrome c from mitochondria and the activation of caspase-3, indicating the induction of apoptosis.[ ] Similarly, another study reported that treatment of (Z)-N-(2-(dimethylamino) ethyl)-2-(3-((3-oxoisobenzofuran-1(3H)-ylidene) methyl) phenoxy) acetamide (PCH4), a derivative of BP, induced Nur77 expression, its cytoplasmic translocation, and apoptosis in GBM 8401 cells.[ ] Notably, the knockdown of Nur77 reduced PCH4-induced apoptosis, confirming the involvement of Nur77 in mediating the apoptotic response. Furthermore, in vivo experiments using DBTRG-05MG GBM xenografts in nude mice demonstrated that PCH4 treatment significantly reduced tumor growth and promoted apoptotic activity.[ ] These findings highlight the therapeutic potential of targeting Nur77 in GBM through modulation of its expression and subcellular localization.
A substantial body of evidence has implicated Nurr1, also known as NR4A2, in the progression of various cancer types, including brain tumors.[ – ] For instance, it has been reported that treatment with BP induced Nurr1 expression, leading to growth arrest and apoptosis in DBTRG-05MG and GBM 8401 GBM cells.[ ] Similarly, another study demonstrated that treatment with PCH4, a synthetic derivative of BP, upregulated Nurr1 mRNA expression, which subsequently promoted apoptosis and reduced cell proliferation in DBTRG-05MG cells.[ ] Further supporting its role in glioma pathophysiology, the knockdown of Nurr1 in GBM cell lines, including 14015s, U87-MG, and 15037, resulted in diminished Nurr1 expression, accompanied by reduced cell proliferation, migration, invasion, and spheroid formation.[ ] Additionally, silencing NR4A2 expression led to the activation of apoptotic markers, such as cleaved caspase-8, caspase-7, and PARP.[ ]
In vivo, treatment with the synthetic compound 4-Cl significantly reduced tumor weight and increased cleaved caspase-8 levels in U87-MG-derived xenografts in mice, further highlighting Nurr1 as a promising therapeutic target in GBM.[ ]
NOR1, also known as NR4A3, is frequently downregulated across various cancer types.[ – ] In the context of brain cancer, treatment with BP was found to induce NOR1 mRNA expression, leading to growth arrest and apoptosis in DBTRG-05MG and GBM 8401 cells.[ ] Similarly, administration of PCH4 significantly increased NOR1 mRNA expression, inhibited cell proliferation, and promoted apoptosis in DBTRG-05MG cells.[ ] Collectively, these findings suggest that pharmacological induction of NR4A family members, Nur77, Nurr1, and NOR1 by agents such as BP and its derivative PCH4 exerts potent anti-tumor effects in GBM models.

## Peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors
PPARs are members of the nuclear hormone receptor superfamily and function as transcription factors regulating various aspects of energy metabolism.[ – ] There are three subtypes of PPARs: PPARα, PPARγ, and PPARβ/δ.[ , ] These receptors play pivotal roles in multiple physiological processes, including lipid metabolism and immune modulation.[ ] Dysregulation of PPARs have been documented in various malignancies, such as bladder, breast, colon, lung, prostate, and thyroid cancers, suggesting a potential role in tumorigenesis.[ – ]
Numerous studies have reported overexpression of both PPARα and PPARγ in brain tumor tissues and glioma cell lines, highlighting the significance of these receptors in brain tumor pathophysiology.[ – ] A notable study investigated the impact of manganese (Mn) exposure on PPAR isotypes expression and demonstrated that Mn neurotoxicity alters the subcellular distribution of PPAR isotypes in U87 and SK-N-SH cell lines.[ ] Another study has demonstrated that treatment with PPARα agonists, such as bezafibrate and gemfibrozil, reduced cell viability and modulated the expression of key regulatory proteins, including cyclins, p27Kip1, Mdm-2, p21Waf1/Cip1, Bcl-2, Bax, PARP, caspase-3, and ARs in several glioma cell lines.[ ] Fenofibrate, a PPARα-specific agonist, has been shown to inhibit IGF-I signaling and suppress cell motility in LN229 glioma cells.[ ] The anti-migratory effects were attributed not only to IGF-I inhibition but also to PPARα-mediated mitochondrial dysfunction and ROS accumulation.[ ] In another study, pharmacological inhibition of PPARα using AA452 enhanced radiosensitivity, induced cell death, and reduced viability, migration, and the expression of AOX1, c-Myc, and cyclin D1 in primary human GBM cells.[ ] Similarly, siRNA-mediated silencing of PPARα and PPARβ reduced proliferation and migration in U87-FABP7(+) cells.[ ] Besides, bexarotene, an RXR agonist, was shown to reduce cell proliferation, total antioxidant status, and NF-κB levels, while increasing levels of PPARβ, PPARγ, and 8-hydroxy-2-deoxyguanosine (8-OHdG) in C6 glioma cells.[ ] It also promoted apoptosis through caspase-3/7 activation.[ ]
Moreover, multiple PPARγ ligands, including all-trans retinoic acid (ATRA), troglitazone, ciglitazone (CGZ), pioglitazone (PGZ), rosiglitazone, LY171833, and prostaglandin J2, have been reported to induce PPARγ, upregulate Bax, Bad, N-cadherin, p21Cip1/Waf1, GFAP, COX-2, and downregulate Bcl-2, c-Myc, vimentin, thereby inhibiting cell growth and viability in various brain cancer cell lines.[ , , , – , – , – , ] In particular, PGZ was shown to increase excitatory amino acid transporter 2 expression and reduce extracellular glutamate levels in U251MG and U87 cells, effects that were reversed by the PPARγ antagonist GW9662, indicating a PPARγ-dependent mechanism.[ ] PGZ and troglitazone also reduced tumor growth and induced apoptosis in tumor-induced mice xenograft models.[ , , , ] Similarly, GW347845X (a PPARγ agonist) and conjugated linoleic acid markedly inhibited cell growth, proliferation, invasion, and migration, while inducing apoptosis, cytoskeletal remodeling, cell adhesion, nucleosome concentration, p21 activity and expression of A2B5, β-catenin, N-cadherin, GFAP, and caspase-9, as well as reducing the expression of nestin, intra-, and extracellular fibronectin, caspase-3, Bcl-2, cyclin D1, CDK4, VEGF 165, VEGF 121, and NOS II activity in Grade IV astrocytoma and ADF GBM cell lines.[ , ] Furthermore, the knockdown of PPARγ through siRNA reversed the inhibitory effects of anti-miR-130b in glioma cells.[ ] Moreover, PPARγ was identified as a direct target of miR-130b, indicating that the miR-130b-mediated enhancement of glioma cell proliferation and invasion is, at least in part, mediated through the downregulation of PPARγ in glioma cells.[ ]
These findings collectively highlight the potential of PPARs, particularly PPARα and PPARγ, as critical regulators of glioma progression. Activation of these receptors by pharmacological agents such as fenofibrate and pioglitazone offers a promising avenue for therapeutic intervention in brain cancers. Nonetheless, several studies have reported differential expression of PPARs in brain cancer patients, highlighting the need for comprehensive preclinical and clinical investigations to validate these findings and to optimize therapeutic strategies for glioma and other brain malignancies.

## Progesterone receptors
PR, a member of the steroid hormone receptor superfamily, is encoded by the NR subfamily 3 group C member 3 gene and comprises 933 amino acids.[ ] PR is widely expressed across multiple tissues, with particularly high levels in the uterus, where it plays a crucial role in regulating physiological processes essential to the female reproductive system.[ ] Beyond reproductive functions, PR is also involved in central nervous system homeostasis, modulating proteins associated with inhibitory neurotransmission, reducing apoptosis, and supporting neuronal integrity and survival.[ , ] The neuroprotective effects of progesterone suggest its therapeutic potential in various brain injuries.[ ] PR has also been increasingly implicated in the pathophysiology of brain cancers, particularly gliomas.
For instance, in silico analyses of the cancer genome atlas (TCGA) and genotype-tissue expression datasets have revealed significant downregulation of PR mRNA across astrocytoma grades.[ ] An in vitro study demonstrated that progesterone promotes cell growth and S-phase progression in U373-MG and D54 cells, and these effects are attenuated by co-treatment with mifepristone, a PR antagonist.[ ] Similarly, in another study, increased PR expression and progesterone-induced growth were observed in glioma sphere (GS) cultures.[ ]
In vivo studies further confirmed that progesterone treatment enhanced tumor growth and infiltration in U373 and U87MG glioma models, effects that were reversed by PR silencing using phosphorothioate antisense oligonucleotides or co-treatment with mifepristone (RU486) in U373 and U87MG rat brain implants.[ , ] Interestingly, a separate study showed that progesterone, alone or in combination with TMZ, promoted cell death and suppressed proliferation and migration in U87MG and U118MG cells by downregulating EGFR, p-Akt, and mTOR.[ ] Progesterone also enhanced TMZ sensitivity in these cell lines.[ ] Valdes-Rives et al[ ] demonstrated that stimulation of the lysophosphatidic acid (LPA) 1 receptor promoted a protein-protein interaction between PKCα and PR in GBM cells, resulting in PR phosphorylation at serine 400.[ ] In addition, treatment with LPA enhanced VEGF transcriptional activity, a well-recognized target of PR, and this effect was inhibited by mifepristone. Moreover, the activation of LPA1/PR signaling cascade was found to stimulate the migratory behavior of GBM cells. Notably, TCGA data revealed that LPAR1 expression increases with tumor grade and correlates with reduced survival in grade III astrocytoma, suggesting the role of LPA1/PR axis in regulating glioma progression.[ ] Similarly, another study demonstrated that tetradecanoylphorbol acetate (TPA)-induced PKC activation increased PR transcriptional activity, upregulated PR target gene progesterone-induced blocking factor (PIBF), and enhanced cell invasion and migration in U251MG and U87 cells.[ ] González-Arenas et al[ ] further showed that PKCα and PKCδ might mediate PR serine 400 phosphorylation, promoting PR degradation through the proteasome following TPA treatment.[ ] Progesterone treatment also substantially increased cell migration and invasion in D54 and U251 cells through PR-dependent mechanisms, as confirmed by antisense oligonucleotide-mediated silencing and RU486 co-treatment.[ ] Besides, progesterone enhanced cofilin phosphorylation, an actin-binding protein that plays a major role in the migration and motility of the cells, in a PR-independent manner.[ ] In a separate investigation, Bello-Alvarez and colleagues reported that progesterone and the PR agonist R5020 activated cSrc in a PR-dependent manner in U251 and U87 cells.[ ] Moreover, progesterone treatment led to increased phosphorylation of focal adhesion kinase (FAK) and decreased phosphorylated Paxillin, both key mediators of cell migration and invasion.[ ] Furthermore, the role of PIBF in gliomas has been investigated by a couple of studies.[ , ] Progesterone-induced PIBF expression which was effectively blocked by RU486.[ ] PIBF treatment promoted proliferation in U87 cells and enhanced invasion and migration in U87 and U251 cells.[ ] PIBF was also found to associate with the IL-4 receptor and activate the JAK1/STAT6 signaling cascade.[ , ] These observations indicate that progesterone regulates PIBF expression via PR and that PIBF may contribute to glioma progression via IL-4 receptor/JAK1/STAT6 signaling.[ , ]
Collectively, these findings underscore the multifaceted role of PR in glioma biology, influencing cell proliferation, migration, invasion, and treatment response. Further research into PR-targeted modulators is warranted to explore their potential as therapeutic agents for glioma management and to improve current treatment strategies for brain cancer.

## Retinoic acid receptors
RARs, members of the NR subfamily 1 group B, regulate gene expression in eukaryotic cells by forming heterodimeric complexes with RXRs.[ ] These RAR/RXR heterodimers bind to specific DNA sequences known as retinoic acid response elements, thereby activating gene transcription.[ ] Vitamin A derivatives such as retinoic acid serve as ligands for RARs and initiate signaling pathways involved in gene regulation, cellular differentiation, and embryogenesis.[ ] The RAR gene family encodes three distinct subtypes: RARα (with four isoforms), RARβ (five isoforms), and RARγ (two isoforms), each of which may exert unique biological effects through differential gene targeting.[ ] Structurally, RARs comprise three primary domains: The NTD, the DBD, and the LBD, connected by a hinge region that includes a nuclear localization signal. Isoform variation primarily occurs within the NTD, whereas the DBD and LBD are conserved across isoforms.[ ] RARs are ubiquitously expressed in human tissues.[ ]
RAR activation has demonstrated anti-tumorigenic potential in brain cancer models following treatment with retinoids. For instance, treatment of GBM cells with ATRA and (E)-4-[2-(5,6,7,8-Tetrahydro-5,5,8,8-tetramethyl-2-naphthylenyl)-1-propenyl] benzoic acid (TTNPB) led to a reduction in the secretion of hepatocellular growth factor (HGF), a key player responsible for tumor progression.[ ] In another study, combined administration of ATRA and pioglitazone inhibited cell growth and proliferation and induced apoptosis, as evidenced by downregulation of c-Myc and Bcl-2 and upregulation of Bax.[ ] Collectively, these findings suggest that RAR ligands, particularly in combination with other therapeutic agents, may serve as promising adjuvants in the treatment of brain tumors, including GBM. Further investigation into the therapeutic potential and mechanistic pathways of RAR activation in brain cancer is warranted.

## Retinoid X receptor
RXRs, members of the NR superfamily, function as heterodimerization partners with various other NRs to regulate gene transcription.[ , , ] RXRs are classified into three subtypes: RXRα (NR2B1), RXRβ (NR2B2), and RXRγ (NR2B3).[ , , ] RXR ligands, collectively referred to as “rexinoids,” can independently activate transcription in “permissive” heterodimers such as PPAR/RXR, LXR/RXR, and FXR/RXR. In contrast, activation of “non-permissive” heterodimers, including RAR/RXR, VDR/RXR, and TR/RXR, typically requires the co-binding of both RXR and partner ligands.[ – ]
Dysregulation of RXR expression has been observed in various brain tumor models. For example, treatment with 9-cis and 13-cis retinoic acids, agonists of RXR, led to significant inhibition of HGF secretion and expression, thereby disrupting a potential pro-tumorigenic autocrine signaling loop in U87 cells.[ ] In another study, treatment with UAB30, an RXR modulator, induced cellular differentiation and G1 cell cycle arrest and caused a marked reduction in cell viability, proliferation, invasion, and migration in medulloblastoma patient-derived xenograft (PDX) cell lines (D425, D384, and D341).[ ] UAB30 also significantly suppressed tumor growth and metastasis in a medulloblastoma in vivo model.[ ] In addition, it has been reported that treatment with CGZ and gemfibrozil, a PPARα ligand, significantly reduced RXR isoform expression in multiple GBM cell lines, including A172, U-118 MG, U-87 MG, and T98G.[ ] Moreover, studies have demonstrated the therapeutic potential of combination treatments involving 6-OH-11-O-hydroxyphenanthrene (IIF, an RXR ligand) and PGZ or CGZ.[ , ] These combinations were found to significantly reduce cell invasion and downregulate MMP-2, and -9 in a dose-dependent manner in C6, U-87 MG, and GL261 cells. Further, the combined treatments inhibited proliferation and induced apoptosis, as evidenced by increased expression of cytochrome c, cleaved caspase-3, Bax, and Bad.[ , ]
In vivo studies further confirmed that oral administration of PGZ and IIF led to reduced tumor volume and proliferation in glioma models.[ ]
Collectively, these findings highlight the therapeutic potential of RXR ligands as modulators of brain tumor progression. Nevertheless, further preclinical and clinical studies are necessary to fully elucidate their mechanisms of action and validate their efficacy and safety in brain cancer treatment.

## TLX
TLX (NR subfamily 2 group E member 1, NR2E1), also known as the Drosophila tailless gene homolog, is an orphan NR characterized by its transcriptional repressive activity.[ , ] TLX expression is predominantly localized in the brain, particularly the subventricular zone of the lateral ventricles and the sub-granular zone of the dentate gyrus.[ , , ] Accumulating evidence has demonstrated the critical involvement of TLX in the progression of brain malignancies.
For example, integrated in silico and clinical analyses have revealed a significant upregulation of TLX expression in glioma tissues compared with normal brain tissues.[ ] Mechanistically, TLX was shown to promote glioma malignancy by enhancing the transcriptional activation of programmed death-ligand 1, thereby contributing to the establishment of an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. In addition, inhibition of TLX expression markedly impaired the growth of glioma allografts and xenografts in vivo, restored anti-tumor immune responses, reduced the population of PD-L1-positive cells and glioma-associated macrophages, and enhanced infiltration by cytotoxic lymphocytes.[ ] In another study, TLX knockdown was found to enhance canonical TGF-β signaling response in T98G and U343MG cells.[ ] Specifically, loss of TLX led to increased stabilization of TGF-β receptor II, augmented nuclear translocation of Smad2/3, and upregulation of TGF-β target gene expression.[ ] Moreover, investigations using GBM stem cells (GSCs) demonstrated that TLX is essential for maintaining GSC growth and self-renewal.[ ]
TLX knockdown reduced GSC growth and self-renewal capacity, accompanied by upregulation of genes such as tet methylcytosine dioxygenase 3, thymine DNA glycosylase, and dicer 1, and downregulation of genes including inhibitor of DNA binding 3 and 4, and methyl-CpG binding domain protein 2. In vivo studies revealed that silencing TLX expression diminished tumor growth and size in orthotopic mouse models of GBM. Importantly, intratumoral delivery of TLX-targeting siRNAs inhibited GSC-driven tumorigenesis and significantly prolonged the survival of GSC-bearing mice.[ ]
Collectively, these findings highlight the pivotal role of TLX in modulating the tumor microenvironment, immune evasion, and GSC-mediated tumorigenesis, highlighting its potential as a promising therapeutic target for the treatment of aggressive brain tumors.

## Thyroid hormone receptors
TRs, members of the steroid hormone receptor superfamily, mediate the biological effects of thyroid hormone (T3) on cellular growth, development, and differentiation.[ , ] TRs exist in two major subtypes, TRα and TRβ, which are encoded by different genes.[ ] These receptors bind to thyroid hormone response elements located in the promoter regions of T3-regulated genes, thereby modulating their transcriptional activity.[ , ] For proper function, TRs form either homodimers or heterodimers with other NRs, including RARs, RXRs, and VDRs.[ ]
A study has demonstrated the involvement of TRs in brain tumor pathogenesis.[ ] This study reported that the expression levels of TRα1 and TRα2 inversely correlated with tumor grade, whereas TRβ1 expression was positively associated with increasing tumor grade in astrocytoma tissues. These findings suggest that the expression profiles of TR isoforms might be associated with the degree of malignancy in astrocytoma.[ ] Another study revealed that T3 treatment led to a reduction in the mRNA expression levels of TRα1, TRα2, and TRβ1 in the medulloblastoma-derived HTB185 cell line.[ ] Interestingly, co-treatment with actinomycin D, an inhibitor of RNA synthesis, caused an acute elevation in basal TR mRNA levels, yet the rate of TR mRNA degradation was enhanced in the presence of T3. Additionally, treatment with cycloheximide, a protein synthesis inhibitor, abrogated the T3-induced downregulation of TR mRNA, indicating that this regulatory effect is dependent on the synthesis of de novo proteins.[ ]
Together, these findings underscore distinct expression patterns of TR isoforms in brain tumors, suggesting a potential association with tumor grade. However, current evidence is limited to expression-level analyses, and comprehensive regulatory and functional studies are necessary to elucidate the precise role of TRs in brain tumor biology and assess their viability as therapeutic targets.

## Vitamin D receptors
VDRs, members of the steroid hormone receptor superfamily, play a pivotal role in mediating the biological effects of vitamin D, particularly in regulating gene expression involved in calcium and phosphate homeostasis, cellular differentiation, proliferation, and immune modulation.[ ] VDRs are expressed in various immune cell types, including neutrophils, regulatory T cells, dendritic cells, macrophages, and B cells.[ , ] Beyond their immunological functions, VDRs regulate diverse biological processes such as development, homeostasis, cell proliferation, and various other physiological functions.[ , ]
Numerous studies have demonstrated that VDRs are widely expressed across normal and malignant tissues.[ – ] In brain cancer, several investigations have reported elevated VDR expression in tumor tissues and cell lines.[ , ] However, another study indicated the downregulated expression of VDR in certain brain malignancies, such as GBM.[ ] Pharmacological studies using vitamin D analogs, such as tacalcitol (1α,24-dihydroxyvitamin D3) and calcipotriol, have shown marked inhibition of proliferation and migration in T98G cells, suggesting potential therapeutic efficacy.[ ] Similarly, another study demonstrated that treatment with 1α,25-dihydroxyvitamin D3 and tacalcitol suppresses p-p70-S6 kinase and enhances the expression of p-PLCγ, a downstream target in phospholipid signaling.[ ] These agents also inhibited STAT3 activation and anchorage-independent growth in T98G and U251 cells, further supporting their potential to suppress GBM progression.[ ] In addition, Salomon et al[ ] have shown that downregulation of VDR expression has been associated with enhanced cell survival and migratory capacity in T98G cells, whereas treatment with calcitriol, the active form of vitamin D, upregulated the expression of cell cycle inhibitors p57, p27, and p21, reduced cyclin D1 levels and decreased cell viability and migration.[ ] Moreover, positive VDR expression has also been associated with improved overall survival in patients with GBM.[ ] In meningiomas, a higher frequency of the Fok-I ff genotype in VDR has been observed, suggesting a possible genetic predisposition to tumor development.[ ] Further evidence from metastatic brain tumor models indicated that calcipotriol reduced the proliferation and survival of BR2 metastatic cells, with no significant effect on parental cells.[ ] This response was mediated through VDR-dependent mechanisms, including downregulation of Hippo pathway effectors, Yes-associated protein, and transcriptional coactivator with PDZ-binding motif. Further, VDR-overexpressing metastatic cells have been shown to have reduced tumor burden in the NOD/SCID mouse model.[ ]
Collectively, these studies provide preliminary insights into the tumor-suppressive potential of VDR signaling and the therapeutic efficacy of vitamin D analogs in brain cancer. However, these findings are predominantly based on expression and in vitro studies. Comprehensive mechanistic investigations, including in vivo validation and clinical evaluation, are necessary to elucidate the functional role of VDR in brain tumorigenesis and to assess its viability as a therapeutic target.

## Other NRs
Several additional NRs have also been investigated for their potential roles in brain cancer pathogenesis, including ERRβ, NR1D2, also known as REV-ERBβ, and NR5A2, also referred to as liver receptor homolog-1.
ERRs are orphan NRs that share considerable structural similarity with ERα. This receptor subgroup comprises three members, ERRα, ERRβ, and ERRγ, each possessing functional domains similar to other NRs.[ , ] ERRs have been implicated in tumorigenesis and modulation of estrogen signaling across various cancer types.[ ] Notably, the ERRβ2 isoform has been associated with GBM pathogenesis.[ ] A study demonstrated that ERRβ2 is highly expressed in multiple GBM cell lines. Treatment with DY131, an ERRβ agonist, exerted anti-migratory effects, induced actin cytoskeletal remodeling, and caused G2/M cell cycle arrest and apoptosis in 42MGBA-TMZres and T98G cells. In addition, the combination of DY131 with TG-003, a pan-CDC-like kinase inhibitor, enhanced ERRβ2 expression and suppressed both growth and migration of TMZ-resistant GBM cells. Importantly, co-treatment with TG-003 and DY131 showed significant antitumor efficacy in a zebrafish intracranial xenograft model, indicating permeability across the blood–brain barrier.[ ]
Additional studies have reported dysregulation of other NRs, including NR1D2, and NR5A2, in brain cancer tissues and cell lines.[ , ] NR1D2 was found to be highly expressed in GBM tissues and cell lines.[ ] Silencing of NR1D2 resulted in morphological alterations, inactivation of AXL, and suppression of cell proliferation, invasion, and migration in LN-18, T98G, and U118 MG cells. Moreover, NR1D2 silencing impaired focal adhesion maturation and F-actin assembly, as well as reduced levels of p-FAK and actin-related regulatory proteins such as p-Rac1/Cdc42, WAVE, and PFN2 in LN-18, T98G, and U118 MG cells.[ ] NR5A2 was also found to be overexpressed in GBM samples.[ ] Functional studies revealed that NR5A2 enhances cell proliferation, colony formation, and invasiveness, and induces a G0/G1 cell cycle shift in T98G and U251 cells. These effects were attributed to the co-activation of β-catenin/TCF4 signaling.[ ]
Collectively, these findings demonstrate the potential of NRs as key contributors to brain tumorigenesis and highlight their promise as novel therapeutic targets for brain cancer. Further mechanistic studies are warranted to validate these receptors and elucidate their roles in disease progression.

## Conclusions
Despite extensive research, brain cancer continues to be among the most lethal malignancies globally, emphasizing the urgent need to identify reliable biomarkers and therapeutic targets. In recent years, NRs have emerged as pivotal transcriptional regulators with multifaceted roles in brain cancer pathogenesis, particularly in glioma and GBM. This review provides a brief representation of the current literature, highlighting NRs such as PPARs, LXRs, RARs, and orphan receptors, including TLX and NR1D2, and their ability to modulate various hallmarks in brain cancer. A key strength of this review lies in its integrative approach, demonstrating how NR modulation through ligands can serve as a potential therapeutic strategy.
An increasing body of evidence indicates that NRs, including ARs, ERs, ERRs, GRs, NR4A, NR1D2, NR5A2, LXRs, PPARs, PRs, RARs, RXRs, TLX, TRs, and VDRs, are associated with brain cancer. Specific receptors such as GRs, NR1D2, NR5A2, PPARγ, TRβ1, and TLX are frequently overexpressed, whereas TRα1 and TRα2 are downregulated. Others, such as ARs, ERs, PPARα, PRs, and VDRs, exhibit differential expression across brain tumor types and cell lines [Figure ]. These receptors modulate key signaling pathways, including PI3K/Akt/mTOR, EGFR, p53, Notch, Wnt/β-catenin, TGF-β, and NF-κB, affecting critical cancer hallmarks including survival, proliferation, migration, invasion, and apoptosis. In addition, ligand-based modulation of NRs has demonstrated the potential to influence gene expression associated with tumorigenesis. Notably, receptors such as FXR remain underexplored in brain cancer, highlighting areas for future investigation.
 Although NR ligands show considerable promise in modulating tumor behavior, challenges such as limited blood–brain barrier permeability, receptor subtype specificity, and insufficient preclinical validation continue to hinder clinical translation. In addition, combination therapies incorporating NR modulators with chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and immunotherapy may offer synergistic therapeutic benefits. Furthermore, precision medicine approaches using genomic and proteomic profiling to tailor NR-targeted therapies hold the potential to enhance efficacy while minimizing adverse effects. Robust, randomized, double-blind, multicenter clinical trials are critically needed to establish the safety and therapeutic value of NR modulators in brain cancer.
Emerging evidence suggests that NRs may also play roles in epigenetic regulation and immune modulation within the tumor microenvironment, representing promising avenues for future research in brain cancer. Advanced technologies such as single-cell transcriptomics and AI-driven drug discovery could accelerate the identification of subtype-specific NR modulators and their optimal therapeutic contexts. Future research should also prioritize the identification of brain-penetrant, subtype-selective NR modulators and their rigorous evaluation in robust preclinical brain cancer models. Expanding the pharmacological repertoire for orphan NRs and elucidating their functional contributions to brain cancer pathogenesis may uncover novel therapeutic avenues. In conclusion, NRs represent an underexplored yet promising class of targets with significant potential for transforming the prevention and treatment of brain cancers.
