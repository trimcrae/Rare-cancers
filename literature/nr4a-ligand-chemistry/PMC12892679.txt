## Background
Genome-wide association studies (GWASs) have identified over 100 loci associated with rheumatoid arthritis (RA) risk. Nonetheless, the contribution of these loci to RA risk remains largely unknown, hampering the development of new therapeutics. As proteins are direct effectors of disease processes, we conducted the first large-scale proteome-wide association study (PWAS) to prioritize RA risk genes based on their effects on plasma protein abundance.

## Methods
We integrated RA GWAS summary statistics (discovery: 22,350 cases and 74,823 controls; replication: 31,313 cases and 995,377 controls) with precomputed protein expression weights generated from the Atherosclerosis Risk in Communities (ARIC) study (N = 7,213) and INTERVAL study (N = 3,301). Causal inference was performed using Mendelian randomization (MR) and colocalization analyses. Druggable target exploration was conducted to identify potential therapeutic targets for RA.

## Results
A total of 35 genetically regulated proteins associated with RA risk, including 10 potentially causal candidates, were identified. Notably, six potentially causal proteins (FCRL3, ICOSLG, MAPK3, WISP1, FAM213A, and IL1RN) were not implicated in the original GWASs. Druggable target exploration identified 160 drug-gene interactions, including a drug, AMG-557, targeting the PWAS protein ICOSLG, which possesses superior anti-inflammatory and anti-rheumatic activity in autoimmune diseases and may therefore be a candidate for RA treatment.

## Conclusions
Our results provide novel insights into RA pathogenesis and suggest promising targets for further mechanistic investigation and drug development.

## Introduction
Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is the most prevalent autoimmune disease characterized by persistent synovitis and systemic inflammatory responses, affecting up to 1% of the population [ ]. Given that the pathogenesis of RA has not been fully elucidated, it can only be managed by relieving pain and reducing inflammation, which generally necessitates lifelong therapy [ ]. The etiology of RA involves various risk factors such as genetics, sex, and the environment, among which genetic factors account for more than 60% [ ]. Considering the clear causal relationship between genetic factors and RA, understanding the pathological processes from a genomic perspective is important. Over the last decade, genome-wide association studies (GWASs) have identified more than 100 loci robustly associated with RA, making significant progress and invaluable utility in revealing the genetic architecture of RA [ ]. Nevertheless, these variants have only a small effect on RA, and deciphering the molecular mechanisms of the identified genetic effects remains challenging, which has impeded the advancement of new therapeutics [ ].
Over 80% of risk variants associated with RA are located in the non-coding regions of the genome [ ] and are enriched in various elements related to the regulation of gene function, indicating that these variants likely contribute to the underlying pathogenesis by regulating gene expression [ ]. Recently, transcriptome-wide association studies (TWASs) have been extensively proposed as an improved approach to investigate the association between genetically predicted gene expression and disease risk, showing elevated power in prioritizing disease-risk genes [ – ]. Taking advantage of TWASs, some studies have prioritized RA candidate genes [ , ]; however, the mediating effects of gene expression can only explain a small fraction of GWAS heritability [ ]. As the ultimate products of gene expression, proteins are closely related to the biological processes of pathogenesis and act as a major source of efficient biomarkers and therapeutic targets [ ]. Nonetheless, obstacles in obtaining large-sample genomic and proteomic data, such as sample acquisition, sequencing costs, and data storage, have limited the discovery of RA-related genes [ ].
Gene expression is regulated at multiple levels, including pre-transcriptional, translational, and post-translational, and studies have revealed a notoriously poor correlation between mRNA and protein expression levels [ , ]. Furthermore, genetic variation can affect protein abundance by altering the rate and stability of gene expression; however, these effects tend to be significantly reduced during the translation process, indicating that their potential effects on downstream phenotypes may be further attenuated or buffered [ ]. Therefore, it is critical to investigate whether the identified risk variants contribute to RA pathogenesis by regulating protein abundance. Protein quantitative trait loci (pQTLs) are genetic variants associated with variations in the abundance levels of proteins. With the widespread utilization of proteome sequencing technology, numerous pQTLs have been identified [ – ]. These pQTLs are more likely to exert effects on protein abundance by directly influencing the processes of translation, providing novel clues for exploring the causal associations between proteins and phenotypic variations from a genetic perspective. Inspired by TWASs, a proteome-wide association study (PWAS) was established to explore the association between protein expressions and phenotypes [ , ]. The PWAS leverages an external reference panel that measures both genetic variation and protein expression to train a predictive model of protein expression, enabling the estimation of association statistics between predicted protein expression and phenotype. The PWAS has been empirically verified and shown to be reliable in various complex diseases and traits, such as Alzheimer’s disease, heart failure, and colorectal cancer [ – ].
In the present study, we identified candidate proteins associated with RA by integrating the largest up-to-date RA GWAS [ ] with two independent human plasma pQTL datasets [ , ]. Then, Mendelian randomization (MR) and colocalization analysis were employed to investigate causal relationships between specific proteins and RA. Additionally, we performed TWAS and cell type-specific expression analyses to reveal the expression patterns of potential RA proteins identified by PWAS in different cell types. Finally, we explored potential drug targets for RA by analyzing the PWAS findings using the Drug-Gene Interaction Database (DGIdb). Our findings provide a novel perspective to identify RA-associated risk proteins as well as promising targets for further research on RA pathogenesis and the development of new therapeutics.

## Materials and methods

## GWAS summary statistics of RA discovery cohort
The GWAS summary data for the RA discovery cohort were derived from the most recent large-scale GWAS meta-analysis of RA reported by Ishigaki and colleagues [ ], which included 22,350 RA cases and 74,823 controls from 25 independent cohorts of European descent. All RA cases in each cohort satisfied the 1987 American College of Rheumatology (ACR), and 2010 ACR/European League against Rheumatism criteria. All cohorts were obtained following protocols approved by the institutional ethics committees, and informed consent was obtained from all participants. Detailed information on the genotyping, imputation, and association tests can be found in the Supplementary Materials.

## GWAS summary statistics of RA replication cohort
To increase the confidence of the PWAS results, replication of the PWAS of RA was performed using proteomes and independent GWAS summary data. The replication RA GWAS summary data were obtained from a recently published GWAS of RA, which contained 31,313 RA cases and approximately 1 million controls from six countries in Northwestern Europe [ ]. RA cases were diagnosed by rheumatologists and/or identified through nationwide Scandinavian rheumatology quality registries and/or the 10th revision of the International Statistical Classification of Diseases code-based registration of all inpatient and outpatient healthcare visits. A detailed methodological description is provided in the Supplementary Materials.

## Blood genetic and proteomic data
The blood proteomes integrated with genotyping data were derived from the Atherosclerosis Risk in Communities (ARIC) study [ ] and the INTERVAL study [ ]. The INTERVAL study is a large, prospective cohort of approximately 50,000 individuals, embedded within a pragmatic randomized trial of blood donors [ ]. The relative concentrations of plasma proteins or protein complexes in the blood samples from the two studies were measured using a slow off-rate modified aptamer (SOMAmer), a proteomic profiling platform. Genotyping for individuals in the ARIC study was performed on the Affymetrix 6.0 DNA microarray and imputed to the TOPMed reference panel. Genotyping for those in the INTERVAL study was conducted using the Affymetrix Axiom UK Biobank genotyping array and imputed to the 1000 Genomes Phase 3-UK10K reference panel. After quality control, 4,483 unique proteins from 7,213 European individuals in the ARIC study and 3,170 proteins from 3,301 European individuals in the INTERVAL study were included in the PWAS analyses. Quality control is detailed in Supplementary Materials.

## Proteome-wide association studies (PWAS)
The associations between plasma protein levels and RA were estimated using the adapted version of the functional summary-based imputation (FUSION) pipeline [ ] with GWAS summary statistics of RA and pQTL data from the ARIC and INTERVAL studies. For each protein, FUSION trained predictive models to estimate the effects of genetic variants on protein abundance using a cohort with both genotype and protein abundance data. The predictive model in the ARIC study was fitted using the elastic net al.gorithm [ ], whereas the penalized linear model was used for the INTERVAL study [ ]. Next, using the ImpG-Summary algorithm [ ] (Supplementary Materials), FUSION was used to estimate the association between predicted protein abundance and RA by integrating RA GWAS summary statistics and protein predictive models. Proteins within the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) region were excluded from PWAS analysis. PWAS association statistics were adjusted for false discovery rate (FDR) using the Benjamini-Hochberg (BH) method (P-value < 0.05). We counted the significant single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) (P < 5 × 10− 8) within ± 1 Mb for each PWAS proteins using RA discovery and replication GWASs. For each significant SNP, we extended to include all proxy SNPs in linkage disequilibrium (LD, r2 > 0.8) based on the 1000 Genomes Project EUR reference panel. Then, we annotated all significant SNPs to nearest genes using ANNOVAR [ ]. PWAS proteins were regarded as “not implicated in the original GWAS” only if their coding genes were neither annotated as the nearest genes by ANNOVAR nor located within ± 1 Mb of any LD-expanded SNP.

## MR analyses
To investigate whether the proteins identified by PWAS had causal relationships with RA, a two-sample MR analysis was performed using the GWAS summary data of RA and pQTL data from the ARIC and INTERVAL studies. MR analysis was executed with the “TwoSampleMR” package version 0.5.4 in R platform [ ], with the phenotypic trait (RA) serving as the outcome, the expression level of PWAS proteins acting as the exposure, and significant pQTL SNPs of PWAS proteins using as instrumental variables (IVs). Genome-wide significant SNPs (P < 5 × 10− 8) were selected, and followed LD clumping to obtain independent SNPs (R2 > 0.001) as IVs. To ensure the robustness of the MR results, four MR models were employed: Wald ratio, inverse variance weighted (IVW), MR Egger, and weighted median, depending on the number of IVs. The Wald ratio method was used to estimate causality from exposure to outcome when only one independent IV was accessible, whereas the IVW method was used in cases where multiple IVs were available. The MR-Egger intercept and Cochran’s Q statistics were further used to examine horizontal pleiotropy and heterogeneity, respectively (P > 0.05 indicating the absence of heterogeneity or horizontal pleiotropy). For each significant PWAS protein, MR was conducted using the GWAS summary data of the discovery RA and the corresponding pQTL data from the ARIC and INTERVAL studies. Other pQTL data can be used when no IV is accessible in the corresponding pQTL data. The proteins that reached a significance threshold of FDR < 0.05 was regarded as potentially causal PWAS associations in MR analyses.

## Bayesian colocalization analyses
Bayesian co-localization analyses were used to assess the posterior probability that the PWAS protein and RA risk loci shared the same genetic variant, rather than the variant shared coincidentally due to LD. Colocalizations on the shared SNPs from both the pQTL (ARIC or INTERVAL study) and RA discovery data were analyzed using built-in function (COLOC version 5.1.0) of FUSION with parameter “--coloc_P 0.05”, which indicated that only genes with P < 0.05 were included. The default parameters set in the colocalization analysis were followed: including p1 = 1 × 10− 4 (the prior probability for a SNP being associated with RA), p2 = 1 × 10− 4 (the prior probability for a SNP being a significant QTL, and p12 = 1 × 10–5 (for a SNP being associated with both QTL and RA). Five mutually exclusive hypotheses were tested in the colocalization analysis [ ]: H0, no causal association with either GWAS or pQTL (PPH0); H1, association with GWAS only (PPH1); H2, association with pQTL only (PPH2); H3, association with GWAS and pQTL in two independent SNPs (PPH3); and H4, association with GWAS and pQTL in one shared SNP (PPH4). PWAS proteins with posterior probability PPH4 > 0.7 were considered strong evidence for colocalization [ ]. Proteins with a causal role in both colocalization and MR were regarded as potentially causal PWAS associations. Regional plots showing GWAS and pQTL data for each potentially causal PWAS protein were generated using LocusZoom (http://locuszoom.org).

## Examination of the potentially causal proteins at mRNA level
Given that DNA is transcribed into messenger RNA and is ultimately translated into proteins, we further determined whether the potentially causal PWAS proteins had similar evidence at the transcript level by leveraging TWAS and gene expression profiles at the bulk and single-cell levels. TWAS analysis was conducted using the joint-tissue imputation approach [ ], which leverages genetic regulation shared by different tissues to improve prediction performance in a tissue-dependent manner. In this study, joint tissue TWAS was performed using RA GWAS summary statistics from the discovery cohort and three pre-computed gene expression weights of RA-relevant tissues, including whole blood, lymphocytes, and fibroblasts, from the GTEx cohort [ ]. Genes with FDR < 0.05 were considered significant.
Differential expression analyses were performed to verify the causal relationship between PWAS proteins and RA. We selected the three largest bulk RNA-seq datasets related to RA from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) database: GSE93272 (whole blood samples from 232 RA patients and 43 healthy controls), GSE45291 (whole blood samples from 493 RA patients and 20 healthy controls), and GSE120178 (whole blood samples from 127 RA patients and 20 healthy controls). The differentially expressed genes were analyzed using Student’s t-test with a significance threshold of P < 0.05, after FDR corrections. In addition, we examined the cell type-specific differential expression of PWAS proteins using human synovial single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data from Gupta et al. [ ]. The study applied scRNA-seq to 28,674 cells from the inflamed synovial tissue of 12 donors (11 RA patients and 1 OA patient as a control). The cells were filtered using stringent quality control metrics. We removed genes expressed in fewer than three cells and cells with high mitochondrial gene content (10%) or unique feature counts of more than 2,500 or less than 200. The RNA counts were normalized and scaled. After quality control, 28,674 cells were clustered into eight cell types: fibroblasts, myeloid cells, T cells, B cells, NK cells, endothelial cells, mural cells, and plasma cells. For the causal proteins identified by PWAS, the Wilcoxon rank-sum test was employed to determine cell type-specific differential expression in each cell type. The proteins that reached a significance threshold of FDR < 0.05 was regarded as significant in specific cell types.

## Gene set enrichment analyses
To explore the possible biological functions involved in RA-associated proteins identified by PWAS, we performed gene set enrichment analyses in gene ontology (GO), Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) and Reactome databases using the R package “clusterProfiler” version 4.12.0 [ ]. Gene set enrichment analysis is a computational method used to determine whether a previously defined set of genes is significantly enriched in biological processes and pathways. For each pathway in these databases, the P-value was calculated based on the hypergeometric test and corrected for multiple testing using the BH procedure. The pathway reached a significance threshold of FDR < 0.05, which was considered significant.

## Protein-protein interaction (PPI) network
To investigate potential PPIs among the RA-associated proteins identified by PWAS, a PPI network was constructed using the Search Tool for the Retrieval of Interacting Genes (STRING) database V12.0 (https://string-db.org/) [ ]. Proteins in the PPI network were clustered using a Markov clustering algorithm. The STRING webtool also provides gene set enrichment analyses that aim to detect GO terms and pathways that are significantly enriched in genes in the network versus random genes in the genome.

## Druggable targets exploration
To assess whether the proteins identified by PWAS could serve as potential therapeutic targets for RA, we explored the interactions between these proteins and drugs using DGIdb 5.0 (https://www.dgidb.org/) [ ]. The DGIdb contains more than 20,000 drugs and 10,000 genes involved in over 70,000 drug-gene interactions, which have been mined from 30 distinct sources, including DrugBank, ChEMBL, PharmGKB, the Therapeutic Target Database, and Drug Target Commons. To prioritize the most therapeutically plausible drug-gene interactions for RA, we developed a prioritization framework that integrates evidence from three complementary dimensions: the strength of the drug-gene interaction, the clinical status of the drug, and the known pathological relevance of the gene to RA (Supplementary Materials). The DGIdb can not only provide searches for established drug-gene interactions but also query whether the genes are “potentially” druggable according to their membership in gene categories associated with druggability (e.g., kinases).

## Results

## PWAS identified proteins associated with RA
To identify candidate proteins for RA, we performed the first large-scale PWAS by integrating the most recent GWAS of RA with human blood protein expression weights generated by the ARIC and INTERVAL studies. A total of unique 1,551 proteins (1,255 in the ARIC study and 929 in the INTERVAL study) were significantly heritable (P < 0.01) and included in the PWAS analyses. Among the total of 2,184 associations, we identified 35 proteins (comprising 42 associations) that were significantly associated with RA risk (Fig.  A, Table S1), including 20 proteins with negative associations and 15 proteins with positive associations (Fig.  B). To bolster the robustness of the PWAS results, we replicated the PWAS for RA using independent GWAS summary data. Of the 35 PWAS proteins, 24 proteins were replicated for significant associations with RA in replicated PWAS with the same directions (Table  , Table S2), confirming the robustness and reproducibility of our PWAS results. For example, several well-known risk proteins or drug targets for RA, CCL21 (PDiscovery =6.07 × 10− 10 and PReplication =7.54 × 10− 9), FCGR2A (PDiscovery =1.76 × 10− 7 and PReplication =2.40 × 10− 7), FCGR2B (PDiscovery =9.55 × 10− 7 and PReplication =1.85 × 10− 7) enjoyed the most significant PWAS results for RA. Furthermore, six proteins were implicated by both ARIC and INTERVAL proteomes, including FCGR2A, FCGR3B, IL6R, MAPK3, ICAM5, and FCRL3, indicating the repeatability of PWAS results across proteomes. Notably, among these PWAS identified proteins, we found 11 proteins (ICOSLG, TNFRSF1B, PCBD2, TGFBR3, LRCH4, FCRL3, MAPK3, FAM213A, WISP1, CCN4, IL1RN) were not implicated in the original GWASs (neither annotated as the nearest genes nor located within ± 1 Mb of any LD-expanded SNP, Table S3).

 

 

## MR analysis
To investigate whether the proteins identified by PWAS were causally associated with RA, we performed two-sample MR analyses for each PWAS protein using the discovery RA GWAS summary statistics and pQTL data. MR analyses revealed 15 proteins (CCL21, OLFML3, FCRL3, FCGR3B, ICOSLG, FCGR2A, PAM, MAPK3, WISP1, FCGR3A, HIBCH, PMEL, ICAM5, FAM213A, IL1RN) with strong evidence of causal associations (Fig.  ; Table S4). Among these 15 potentially causal proteins, six proteins (FCRL3, ICOSLG, MAPK3, FAM213A, WISP1, IL1RN) were not implicated in the original GWASs. Horizontal pleiotropy and heterogeneity were not detected for the 15 potentially causal proteins (Table S4).

 

## Colocalization of PWAS associations
To determine whether PWAS proteins and RA risk loci shared the same genetic variants, we conducted Bayesian colocalization analyses for the 15 potentially causal PWAS proteins. Bayesian colocalization analyses showed strong colocalization evidences for 10 proteins (CCL21, FCRL3, ICOSLG, FCGR3B, FCGR2A, MAPK3, WISP1, FCGR3A, FAM213A, IL1RN) that the same risk variants drove the GWAS and pQTL signals (PPH4 > 0.7, Fig.  ), including all six proteins (FCRL3, ICOSLG, MAPK3, WISP1, FAM213A, IL1RN) that were not implicated in the original GWASs, reinforcing the high-confidence causal associations between these proteins and RA risk. The regional plots for each potentially causal PWAS protein are shown in Figure S1 and Figure S2.
Taken together, we identified 10 potentially causal proteins by MR and colocalization analyses (Fig.  ), which were prioritized as potential RA risk proteins, including six proteins (FCRL3, ICOSLG, MAPK3, WISP1, FAM213A, IL1RN) that were not implicated in the original GWASs.

## Examination of the potentially causal proteins at mRNA level
Given that DNA is transcribed into messenger RNA and ultimately translated into proteins, we further investigated whether the 10 potentially causal PWAS proteins were associated with RA at the transcriptional level by leveraging differential expression analysis and TWAS. First, we conducted differential expression analysis between patients with RA and healthy controls from the three largest bulk RNA-seq datasets. After comparing causal PWAS proteins with expression genes, we found that all 10 potentially causal proteins were differentially expressed at the transcriptional level, of which two proteins (FCGR2A and IL1RN) were significant in all three datasets (Fig.  A). The majority of causal PWAS proteins were significantly differentially expressed in the same direction across different datasets. An exception was CCL21, which was significantly downregulated in GSE93272 but significantly upregulated in GSE45291. This discrepancy may be due to batch effects caused by the heterogeneity between samples and cross-platform experiments. Among the 10 potentially causal PWAS proteins, seven PWAS proteins (CCL21, FAM213A, FCGR3B, FCGR3A, FCRL3, ICOSLG, WISP1) were differential expressed at the transcriptional level with same direction of PWAS results (Figs.  B and A). Furthermore, except for three proteins (WISP1, FAM213A, and CCL21) that were not detected in the joint tissue TWAS, we identified three proteins (ICOSLG, FCRL3, and MAPK3) whose cis-regulated gene expression was associated with RA in the same direction as in PWAS (Fig.  B). MAPK3 was significantly associated with RA in all three tissues (whole blood, fibroblasts, and lymphocytes), whereas FCRL3 and ICOSLG were significantly associated with RA in the whole blood and lymphocytes.

 

Next, we investigated the cell type-specific differential expression of the potentially causal PWAS protein using human synovium scRNA-seq data from 12 donors (11 RA patients and 1 OA patient as a control) (Fig.  C). Except for two proteins (WISP1 and FAM213A) that were not detected in the scRNA-seq data, seven of the eight proteins showed cell type-specific differential expression in multiple cell types. For example, the PWAS protein FCGR2A was significantly upregulated in endothelial and T cells and significantly downregulated in myeloid cells. In addition, the PWAS protein ICOSLG was significantly upregulated in fibroblasts and significantly downregulated in the plasma and NK cells. IL1RN and MAPK3 were significantly downregulated in plasma cells, and MAPK3 was significantly upregulated in fibroblasts and myeloid cells.

## Gene set enrichment analyses
To determine the relevant biological pathways and molecular mechanisms of the PWAS proteins, 35 PWAS-significant proteins were subjected to gene set enrichment analyses using GO, KEGG, and Reactome database respectively. GO enrichment analyses identified several GO terms for PWAS proteins, such as regulation of leukocyte proliferation (P = 8.30 × 10− 6), regulation of lymphocyte proliferation (P = 5.22 × 10− 5), regulation of dendritic cell antigen processing and presentation (P = 4.41 × 10− 3), and negative regulation of B cell receptor signaling pathway (P = 4.31 × 10− 4) (Fig.  A). We also detected 17 significant KEGG functional pathways for PWAS proteins. The most significant KEGG pathways included Fc gamma R − mediated phagocytosis (P = 1.04 × 10− 4), cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction (P = 1.04 × 10− 4), and natural killer cell mediated cytotoxicity (P = 3.05 × 10− 4) (Fig.  B). Furthermore, enrichment analyses in the Reactome database identified lots of pathways associated with RA, including MAPK3 (ERK1) activation (P = 9.51 × 10− 7), signaling by interleukins (P = 2.81 × 10− 3), and signaling by FGFR1 (P = 8.29 × 10− 3) (Fig.  C). Identification of these pathways will contribute to a better understanding of the regulation of RA pathogenesis.

 

## Connectivity among the identified proteins
To explore the connectivity among the 35 RA-associated proteins identified by PWAS, we constructed a PPI network using the STRING database containing 16 nodes and 39 edges (Fig.  D). The edges in the PPI network are colored according to the PPIs based on different data sources. In particular, several well-known RA risk proteins identified by PWAS, such as IL6R, FCGR2A, and FCGR2B, were present at the center of the PPI network and formed more PPIs with other PWAS proteins, suggesting that these proteins play important roles in the regulation of RA pathogenesis. For example, the PWAS proteins FCRL3 and ICOSLG were experimentally determined to interact with the known RA risk protein FCGR3A, which is enriched in proteins involved in the regulation of lymphocyte activation and proliferation.

## Druggable targets exploration for RA therapy
As most drugs exert their therapeutic effects through targeting proteins, we finally explored whether the 10 potentially causal PWAS proteins could serve as potential therapeutic targets using DGIdb database. In the DGIdb, we observed 160 drug-gene interactions involving seven proteins (CCL21, ICOSLG, FCGR3B, FCGR2A, MAPK3, IL1RN, and FCGR3A) (Table S5). We then developed a framework to prioritize RA-relevant drug-gene interactions by integrating three criteria: interaction strength, drug clinical status, and target pathological relevance. Several first-line drugs for RA scored highly within this system, such as rituximab (targeting FCGR2A), tocilizumab (targeting FCGR3A), and methotrexate (targeting IL1RN), indicating the high credibility of our prioritization framework. Furthermore, several drugs that have not received regulatory approval were also highly ranked by our framework. For example, AMG-557, a human antibody against the T cell co-stimulator ligand (ICOSL), was identified as the drug interacting with the PWAS protein ICOSLG and achieved the top rank in our prioritization framework. Another highly ranked candidate is valziflocept (targeting FCGR2A), a recombinant, soluble human FcγRIIb, which has completed a Phase 2 trial for autoimmune disease therapy. Furthermore, we evaluated the potential druggability of the 10 potentially causal PWAS proteins. We found that two well-known drug targets of RA (FCGR2A and FCGR3A), coupled with the PWAS protein ICOSLG, were within the clinically actionable category (Table S6). Given that enzymes predominantly form the bulk of therapeutic targets, the PWAS protein, WISP1, was classified in the kinase category. These data suggest that the PWAS protein ICOSLG is a potential therapeutic target in RA.

## Discussion
In this study, we performed the first PWAS by integrating the largest up-to-date RA GWAS and human plasma pQTL data to identify candidate proteins associated with RA pathogenesis and potential therapeutic targets for RA treatment. Collectively, 35 proteins whose genetically regulated protein abundance was significantly associated with RA risk were identified. After causal effect inference, 10 of 35 proteins (CCL21, FCRL3, ICOSLG, FCGR3B, FCGR2A, MAPK3, WISP1, FCGR3A, FAM213A, and IL1RN) were potentially causal and were prioritized as RA-risk proteins. Of note, among the 10 potentially causal PWAS proteins, three (ICOSLG, FCRL3, and MAPK3) also showed evidence of association with RA at the transcriptional level, reflecting consistent findings at the mRNA and protein levels. The remaining four causal proteins (FCGR3B, CCL21, FCGR3A, and FCGR2A) are well-known risk proteins and drug targets for RA. These proteins were identified using PWAS alone, highlighting the importance and necessity of integrating proteomics with GWAS. These high-confidence risk proteins may serve as promising targets for further mechanistic investigation and drug development.
Of note, among the 10 potentially causal PWAS proteins, six (FCRL3, ICOSLG, MAPK3, WISP1, FAM213A, and IL1RN) were not identified by the original GWASs, underscoring the effectiveness of PWAS in identifying risk proteins for RA. The PWAS protein IL1RN inhibits the activities of interleukin 1 related immune and inflammatory responses, whereas MAPK3 participates in signaling cascades that regulate various cellular processes, such as proliferation, differentiation, and cell cycle progression. These two PWAS have been widely proven to play important roles in RA pathogenesis [ – ], indicating the effectiveness of PWAS in the identification of potential RA risk proteins. FCRL3 is one of several Fc receptor-like glycoproteins and a member of the immunoglobulin receptor superfamily that possesses tyrosine-based immunoregulatory functions. Studies have shown that regulatory T cells expressing FCRL3 exhibit a memory phenotype and high levels of PD-1, thus reducing their capacity to suppress the proliferation of effector T cells and contributing to the progression of autoimmunity [ , ]. In addition, FCRL3 has been shown to enhance the activation of NF-kappa-B and MAPK signaling pathways in TLR9 stimulated B-cells, ultimately promoting the proliferation, activation, and survival of B cells [ ]. The PWAS protein ICOSLG is a ligand for the T cell-specific cell surface receptor ICOS, which has been proven to not only co-stimulate T cell proliferation and cytokine secretion, but also induce B cell proliferation and differentiation into plasma cells [ ]. A recent study demonstrated increased expression of ICOS and ICOSLG in patients with RA (67 patients with RA compared to 40 patients with OA and 40 healthy controls) and collagen-induced arthritis (CIA) mice [ ]. Functional experiment showed that the adoptive transfer of CD19+ICOSLG+ B cells aggravated arthritic progression in CIA mice, and blocking ICOSL significantly inhibited proinflammatory responses and ameliorated arthritic progression. WISP1 is a member of the WNT1 inducible signaling pathway that attenuates p53-mediated apoptosis in response to DNA damage through the activation of AKT kinase. A study of synovium scRNA-seq from 36 RA patients revealed that WISP1 was highly expressed in HLA-DRAhi inflammatory sublining fibroblasts, which was dramatically expanded and pathogenic in RA synovium [ ]. Furthermore, a recent clinical case-control study showed that WISP1 was positively correlated with VCAM-1, MMP-3, and GM-CSF levels, and was elevated in non-treated patients with RA, which can be used to indicate the activity and therapeutic response associated with RA (AUC = 0.733) [ ]. These findings provide genetic and biological evidence that these PWAS proteins are likely to be functionally associated with RA pathogenesis, emphasizing the significance of linking genetic factors to RA etiology via specific proteins.
Exploring potential therapeutic targets is particularly vital for RA treatment, because some individuals do not respond adequately to currently pharmacologic therapies [ ]. Druggability exploration analysis revealed 160 drug-gene interactions involving seven PWAS proteins, including several drugs that have been commonly used as first-line RA treatment, such as rituximab (targeting FCGR2A), tocilizumab (targeting FCGR3A), and methotrexate (targeting IL1RN), demonstrating the effectiveness of PWAS proteins in the exploration of novel drugs for RA. Notably, AMG-557, a human antibody against the T-cell co-stimulator ligand, was found to interact closely with the PWAS protein ICOSLG. The ICOS/ICOSLG co-stimulatory axis plays a critical role in RA pathogenesis. Upon engagement, it drives activated T cells to differentiate into T follicular helper (Tfh) cells. These cells are pivotal for initiating germinal center reactions, which in turn promote the clonal expansion of autoreactive B cells and the production of pathogenic autoantibodies—a defining pathological processes of RA [ ]. In a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled study, AMG-557 showed safety and potential efficacy in patients with SLE accompanied by arthritis [ ], which supports further studies on AMG 557 as a potential therapeutic agent for autoimmune diseases. Another highly ranked candidate is valziflocept, a recombinant, soluble human FcγRIIb, which is target FCGR2A. The FcγR/IgG interaction drives a vicious cycle in autoimmunity. The process begins with FcγR/IgG-mediated tissue damage, whose debris enhances autoantigen presentation to stimulate autoreactive B cells, leading to the production of more autoantibodies that form new immune complexes, thereby re-engaging FcγRs and perpetuating the destructive cycle [ ]. Therefore, blocking immune complex binding to FcγRs could both protect tissue and suppress this pathogenic cycle by reducing the production of inflammatory mediators and autoantibodies. As a recombinant soluble FcγR, valziflocept showed encouraging results in Phase 2 trials for immune thrombocytopenia and SLE [ ]. Further pharmacological experiments and clinical trials are required to confirm the clinical safety and efficacy of these drugs for RA treatment.
Our study has several strengths. Primarily, we performed the first PWAS for RA using the largest and most comprehensive reference human proteomes integrated with the latest GWAS summary statistics for RA. After causal effect inference using MR and co-localization, ten proteins were prioritized as potentially causal proteins associated with RA, reducing the false-positive rate in our study. Furthermore, we identified six PWAS proteins for RA that were not implicated in the original GWASs, presenting prospective avenues for further mechanistic investigations and drug development for RA. Additionally, by targeting the PWAS protein, we identified a drug (AMG-557) that possesses superior anti-inflammatory and anti-rheumatic activities in autoimmune diseases, which could serve as a potential therapeutic agent for RA.
The results of this study should be interpreted with caution in conjunction with its limitations. First, although over 4,000 proteins or protein complexes were examined in the present study, it did not cover the entire plasma proteome [ ]. More comprehensive and larger proteomic datasets are required in the future to estimate the genetic effects on the abundance of the remaining proteins [ ]. Secondly, because of the absence of proteomes in other tissues, the present PWAS were only conducted using the plasma proteome as a reference. Assessing the role of genetically regulated proteins in other tissues, especially the synovium, will provide more insight into RA pathogenesis [ ]. Third, the present findings were restricted to European participants to avoid confounding population stratification, which limits the generalizability of our results. Further expansion of the scale and diversity of PWAS studies can provide more precise estimates and enable its broader applications [ ]. Fourth, the RA cohort is a heterogeneous group with diversity in age, disease duration, and treatment exposure, all of which could affect the plasma proteomic landscape and are potential, unmeasured sources of variability in our analysis. Future studies in more homogeneous populations will be essential to confirm our findings. Despite these limitations, PWAS proteins are key to understanding disease etiology, facilitating the biological interpretation of GWAS results, and prioritizing follow-up functional studies.

## Conclusions
In summary, using PWAS, we identified ten potentially causal proteins that were significantly associated with RA risk, including six proteins (FCRL3, ICOSLG, MAPK3, FAM213A, WISP1, and IL1RN) that were not implicated in the original GWASs. Our results elucidate the causal pathways and potential drugs for these PWAS proteins, highlighting promising targets for future mechanistic and therapeutic studies to identify effective treatments for RA.
