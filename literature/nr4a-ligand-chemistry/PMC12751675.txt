## Background
Inflammasomes are intracellular multiprotein complexes composed of NOD-like receptors (NLRs), the adapter protein apoptosis-associated speck-like protein containing a caspase recruitment domain (CARD) (ASC), and the effector protease caspase-1. Among these proteins, the NLR family pyrin domain-containing 3 protein (NLRP3) is the most extensively studied; it forms inflammasomes that activate caspase-1, which in turn mediates the maturation of the proinflammatory cytokines interleukin (IL)−1β and IL-18 and cleaves gasdermin D (GSDMD), leading to pyroptosis—a type of programmed cell death marked by membrane lysis [ – ]. Additionally, other NLRs, such as NLRP1, NLR family CARD-containing protein (NLRC3), NLRC4, NLRC5, absent in melanoma 2 (AIM2), and retinoic acid-inducible gene I, form inflammasomes in response to specific stimuli [ , , ].
The NLRP3 inflammasome plays a multifaceted role in cancer initiation, development, angiogenesis, and antitumor immunity [ ]. Depending on the context, it orchestrates multiple biological pathways, including nuclear factor (NF)-κB signaling, type I interferon responses, autophagy, and oxidative stress, thereby exerting multifaceted effects on cancer progression [ ]. Although IL-1β and IL-18 are commonly linked to NLRP3, they can be produced by other inflammasome- or non-inflammasome-mediated mechanisms. IL-1β often promotes cancer cell proliferation and metastasis [ , ], yet it may exert antitumor effects in certain contexts [ – ]. Thus, a more detailed understanding of inflammasomes, their signaling pathways, and effector molecules in cancer-specific contexts may lead to the development of targeted therapies for different cancer types and stages.
Pyroptosis, a proinflammatory form of programmed cell death, plays a complex, double-edged role in cancer, contributing to both antitumor immunity and tumorigenesis, depending on the context [ , , ]. While it can enhance tumor cell clearance by increasing the efficacy of immunotherapy, it may also drive cancer progression by creating conditions of chronic inflammation; its effects differ across cancer types [ , , ]. Inflammation is a defining hallmark of cancer development and progression [ – ]. Chronic inflammation sustained by inflammasome activation can influence all stages of tumor development, including immunosuppression, proliferation, angiogenesis, and metastasis, through the release of inflammatory cytokines that regulate diverse cell populations within the tumor microenvironment (TME) [ ]. Proinflammatory cytokines secreted by fibroblasts, endothelial cells, and macrophages contribute to cancer initiation, progression, and metastasis. This persistent inflammatory state may lead to conditions that favor tumor growth and survival by disrupting cell signaling and reshaping the TME [ , ]. Conversely, increased inflammasome activation and pyroptotic activity within tumors and the TME can strengthen immune surveillance through the release of danger signals that attract immune cells and promote antitumor immunity [ , , ]. Therefore, a more integrative insight into how inflammasomes, pyroptosis, and inflammatory pathways orchestrate the balance between tumor promotion and suppression under the distinct conditions of the TME is critical for uncovering new therapeutic opportunities against cancer.
This review summarizes current knowledge on the complex interplay between inflammasomes and cancer, highlighting the dual nature of inflammasome activation. We examine how chronic inflammation and inflammasome activity contribute to tumor initiation and progression, and investigate the diverse roles of inflammasome-associated immune dynamics within the TME. We also discuss recent discoveries of genetic variations in inflammasome and pyroptotic components, as well as the progress and future challenges of inflammasome-targeted therapeutics for cancer treatment.

## Overview of inflammasomes
Two main classes of inflammasomes—canonical and noncanonical—have been reported and are triggered by various inflammasome sensors that recognize distinct pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) or damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) or respond to diverse physiological changes [ , , ]. Canonical inflammasomes can be initiated by sensors such as NLRP3, NAIP-NLRC4, and AIM2, whereas noncanonical inflammasomes are activated by caspase-4/5/11 (caspase-4/5 in humans and caspase-11 in mice) [ – ].
In this section, we discuss the characteristics of key canonical inflammasomes, including the NLRP3, AIM2, NLRC4, CARD8, NLRP12, and NLRP6 inflammasomes, which play multifaceted roles in cancer (Fig.  ). Among these, the NLRP3 inflammasome is further subdivided into its structure, activation pathways, and Posttranslational modifications (PTMs) to provide a more comprehensive overview. 

## NLRP3 inflammasome

## Domain structure and activation pathways
NLRP3, a key component of the NLRP3 inflammasome, is a member of the nucleotide-binding domain (NBD)- and leucine-rich repeat (LRR)-containing protein (NLR) protein family [ ]. It consists of three domains: a) The N-terminal pyrin domain (PYD) interacts with the apoptosis-associated speck-like protein containing a caspase recruitment domain (ASC) protein to induce the formation of the inflammasome complex [ ]. b) The nucleotide-binding and oligomerization domain (NACHT domain) is the central domain of NLRP3 that binds to adenosine triphosphate (ATP) or dATP to regulate protein activation and oligomerization [ ]. c) The leucine-rich repeat (LRR) domain, the C-terminus of NLRP3, plays roles in ligand sensing and autoinhibition, the detection of external stimuli, and induction of signal transduction [ , ].
Activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome occurs through ATP hydrolysis by the ATPase activity of the NACHT domain, which induces the self-oligomerization of NLRP3 [ ]. Upon NLRP3 oligomerization, ASC, an adaptor protein comprising an N-terminal PYD and a C-terminal CARD, is recruited to clustered PYDs via PYD–PYD interactions. These events lead to the formation of prion-like filaments, which serve as signaling platforms called molecular “signalosomes”, to further recruit procaspase-1 through CARD–CARD interactions [ , , , ]. Recent advances in structural biology have greatly expanded our understanding of the tripartite architecture of NLRP3 and its organization with ASC into highly ordered supramolecular organizing centers, which subsequently activate caspase-1 through proteolytic cleavage [ , ]. These discoveries have provided insights into the molecular regulation of inflammasome activation. A deeper understanding of the structures of NLRP3 and its binding partners will open new avenues for developing targeted therapeutics aimed at specific domains or protein–protein interaction interfaces, offering promising strategies for treating a wide range of inflammasome-mediated diseases.

## Activation pathways (priming and activation)
Two tightly regulated signals are required for canonical NLRP3 inflammasome activation: an initial priming and licensing step (Signal 1), followed by an activation step that triggers NLRP3 inflammasome assembly (Signal 2). During priming, signals mediated by receptors such as Toll-like receptors (TLRs), tumor necrosis factor receptors (TNFRs), interleukin-1 receptor (IL-1R), or nucleotide-binding oligomerization domain-containing protein 2 (NOD2) activate the transcription factor NF-кB, leading to the upregulation of Nlrp3, Il1β, and Il18 transcription [ , , ]. Once translated, NLRP3 initially adopts a closed, autoinhibited conformation. PTMs, which are discussed in the following subsection, facilitate conformational changes that relieve this autoinhibition [ , , ].
During the activation step, a variety of inflammasome-stimulating signals promote NLRP3 oligomerization into a disc-like structure, exposing its PYD domain, which engages the PYD domain of ASC. This NLRP3–ASC interaction drives the formation of ASC specks, which are amyloid-like protein aggregates that serve as a platform for inflammasome assembly, enabling the recruitment and autocatalysis of caspase-1. Subsequent autoproteolytic cleavage of caspase-1 into its p20 and p10 catalytic subunits results in full enzymatic activation, enabling the processing of pro-IL-1β and pro-IL-18 into their mature, bioactive forms, as well as the cleavage and activation of GSDMD [ , , , , , , , , ]. ASC specks are also released extracellularly, where they retain their proinflammatory function and can be engulfed by neighboring cells, thereby perpetuating the inflammatory feed-forward cycle [ ].
To date, various PAMPs or DAMPs have been reported to activate NLRP3 inflammasome assembly [ ]. Examples include viral and bacterial infections, extracellular ATP (eATP), mitochondrial damage, metabolic dysfunction, and misfolded proteins [ – ]. At least three major mechanisms have been implicated in NLRP3 inflammasome activation: ionic flux, such as potassium (K+) efflux; mitochondrial damage with reactive oxygen species (ROS) production; cytosolic translocation of mitochondrial DNA; and stress to organelles, such as lysosomes [ , , , , , , , ].
Importantly, centrosomal NIMA-related kinase 7 (NEK7), a key regulator of mitosis, acts as a scaffold for NLRP3 activation independent of its catalytic activity [ , ]. The NEK7–NLRP3 interaction, which affects NLRP3 inflammasome activation, is well established in mouse models [ , , , ]. A recent study demonstrated that NEK7 phosphorylation at Thr190 and Thr191 is critical for NLRP3 inflammasome assembly in murine cells because these modifications increase its binding to NLRP3 [ ]. Cryogenic electron microscopy studies have further revealed the structure of the NLRP3–NEK7–ASC complex, in which NLRP3 assembles into large, disc-shaped oligomers in association with adenosine 5′-O-(3-thio) triphosphate, NEK7, and ASC, thereby recruiting pro-caspase-1 [ , ]. Although whether NEK7 functions as a universal sensor of NLRP3 inflammasome activation in humans remains unclear, a structural study showed that human NEK7 interacts with regions near NLRP3 to promote its activation [ ].
Additional spatiotemporal analyses suggest that inter-organelle contact sites play a critical role in NLRP3 inflammasome activation. Upon stimulation, NLRP3 is sequentially translocated from mitochondria and mitochondria-associated endoplasmic reticulum (ER) membranes to the trans-Golgi network. The overall activation process depends on several factors, including microtubule dynamics, protein kinase D, and glycogen synthase kinase 3β activity, ultimately facilitating sustained NLRP3 oligomerization and inflammasome assembly [ , – ]. Although considerable advances have been made in recent studies to improve our understanding of the molecular structures and the subcellular crosstalk underlying inflammasome activation, future studies integrating cryo-EM with live-cell super-resolution imaging will be essential for resolving the detailed spatiotemporal dynamics of this process.

## PTMs
NLRP3, the key component of the inflammasome complex, is regulated by extensive PTMs, including acetylation/deacetylation, ubiquitination/deubiquitination, phosphorylation, sumoylation, palmitoylation, and ISGylation, particularly during the licensing step of inflammasome activation [ , , – ]. PTMs are highly complex, and several comprehensive reviews have discussed the PTMs of NLRP3 inflammasome components in the context of inflammasome activation and pyroptosis in detail [ , , ]. Therefore, in this review, we briefly focus on the major PTMs of the NLRP3 inflammasome.
First, the phosphorylation of NLRP3 at Ser5 and Ser295, which is regulated by Akt and PKA, respectively, is involved in inhibiting inflammasome activation, while dephosphorylation of Tyr861 also contributes to this inhibitory effect [ – ]. Several other phosphorylation sites, including Tyr136, Tyr140, Tyr143, and Tyr168, which are regulated by Bruton’s tyrosine kinase, are associated with the activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome complex in inflammatory contexts such as postischemic inflammation [ , ]. Additionally, the phosphorylation of Ser194 by JNK1 is essential for NLRP3 deubiquitination and self-association [ ]. The phosphorylation of NLRP3 at Thr659 by p21-activated kinase 1 facilitates the NLRP3–NEK7 interaction and promotes inflammasome activation [ ].
Second, the ubiquitination of NLRP3, including both monoubiquitination and polyubiquitination, plays a pivotal role in the licensing step of inflammasome activation [ ]. The E3 ligases Pellino-2 and HUWE1 mediate K63-linked and K27-linked ubiquitination, respectively [ , ]. Notably, HUWE1-mediated K27-linked polyubiquitination of NLRC4, AIM2, and NLRP3 promotes inflammasome assembly and activation [ ]. In contrast, E3 ligases such as TRIM31 [ ], Cbl-b [ ], and Cullin 1 [ ] facilitate the inhibitory ubiquitination of NLRP3, thereby repressing inflammasome activation and mitigating pathological inflammation. Indeed, numerous enzymes and sites mediating the ubiquitination/deubiquitination of NLRP3, its interacting partners, and components of inflammasome assembly have been identified; however, it remains to be elucidated how the delicate balance between ubiquitination and deubiquitination, as well as other PTMs, influences the determination of NLRP3 licensing and activation.
Third, NLRP3 acetylation at Lys24 by lysine acetyltransferase 5 (KAT5) promotes NLRP3 oligomerization and subsequent inflammasome activation in response to diverse stimuli [ ]. In contrast, the deacetylation of NLRP3 by sirtuin 2 (SIRT2), an NAD⁺-dependent deacetylase, is essential for preventing aging-associated inflammation and insulin resistance [ ]. A recent study of macrophage glucocorticoid-induced TNFR-related protein (GITR) showed that GITR competes with NLRP3 to interact with the E3 ligase MARCH7. By recruiting MARCH7, GITR facilitates SIRT2 degradation, thereby increasing NLRP3 acetylation [ ]. Consequently, GITR in macrophages contributes to increased NLRP3 acetylation and activation, leading to systemic inflammation [ ]. Furthermore, in inflammatory macrophages, phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase expression is increased and is required for the epigenetic upregulation of IL-1β through the H3K9/27 acetylation-mediated transcriptional activation of TLR4, as well as for enhanced IL-1β maturation via NLRP3-K21/22/24 and ASC-K21/22/24 acetylation-dependent inflammasome activation [ ]. Notably, excessive NLRP3 acetylation and inflammasome activation have been linked to diverse pathological conditions, including neuroinflammation and cognitive impairment, in Tau-overexpressing mouse models [ ].
The acetylation/deacetylation of chromatin, which is modulated by acetylases/deacetylases, alters chromatin structure and thereby orchestrates the transcription of NLRP3, AIM2, and other inflammasome components, collectively driving inflammasome assembly [histone acetyltransferase families, histone deacetylase (HDAC) families, and SIRT families] [ , – ]. For instance, histone acetylation mediated by the HAT p300 can promote NLRP3 inflammasome activation through ORMDL3 signaling [ ]. Conversely, histone H3 and H4 acetylation induced by suberoylanilide hydroxamic acid, an inhibitor of histone deacetylases, can activate autophagy, thereby suppressing NLRP3 inflammasome activation and alleviating excessive neuroinflammation and cognitive decline in the aging mouse brain [ ]. In addition, the direct interaction of HDAC10 with NLRP3 promotes its deacetylation, thereby inhibiting NLRP3 inflammasome activation and mitigating acute inflammatory injury [ ]. It is just beginning to be understood that at least three PTMs (phosphorylation, ubiquitination, and acetylation) discussed above appear to be associated with cancer development [ , ]. Further evidence is warranted to determine whether these specific PTMs contribute to the complex roles of NLRP3 inflammasome in particular cancer types and stages.
Finally, small ubiquitin-like molecule (SUMO) family proteins play a critical role in orchestrating NLRP3 inflammasome activation. For example, the interaction of NLRP3 with the SUMO-conjugating enzyme UBC9 promotes the SUMOylation of NLRP3 at Lys 204 by small ubiquitin-like modifier 1, thereby increasing inflammasome activation, whereas SUMO-specific protease 3 mediates the deSUMOylation of NLRP3, leading to the attenuation of inflammasome activity [ ]. Conversely, NLRP3 SUMOylation by the SUMO E3 ligase MAPL suppresses NLRP3 inflammasome activation, whereas deSUMOylation by the SUMO-specific proteases SENP6 and SENP7 promotes NLRP3 activation [ ]. In addition to SUMOylation, palmitoylation, a reversible lipid modification catalyzed mainly by the DHHC family of palmitoyl acetyltransferases, regulates NLRP3 inflammasome activation by modulating the subcellular localization and interactions of inflammasome components [ – ]. Although this review does not provide a detailed discussion of the PTMs of other inflammasome components such as ASC and IL-1β, accumulating evidence underscores the importance of PTM-mediated fine-tuning of NLRP3 licensing and activation. A detailed molecular elucidation of the complex mechanisms underlying the integrative PTM regulation of NLRP3 inflammasome activation will shed light on an in-depth understanding of the pathogenesis and potential therapeutics of cancer, as well as other inflammasome-related diseases. ## NLRC4 inflammasome
NLR apoptosis inhibitory protein (NAIP)–NLRC4 was initially reported as IL-1β converting enzyme protease-activating factor (IPAF) [ ]. It functions as an adaptor and nucleator of NAIP inflammasomes to activate caspase-1 upon the recognition of NAIP by gram-negative bacteria and their cytoplasmic components [ – ]. NLRC4 is composed of an N-terminal CARD, a central NACHT domain, and a C-terminal LRR domain and is present only in NAIP inflammasomes. NAIP, a proximal sensor, recognizes distinct bacterial components, including flagellin and type III or IV secretion systems, thereby initiating NLRC4 oligomerization and inflammasome assembly to establish safeguards against infection [ , ]. A recent cryo-EM study showed that ASC and NLRP4 utilize the same mechanism to induce filament structures, leading to inflammasome assembly and caspase-1 activation [ ].
Early studies have shown that NLRC4 expression is upregulated by genotoxic and proinflammatory stimuli, including TNFα, UV radiation, and p53 activation [ , ], and that this process is related to the apoptosis of p53-positive tumor cells [ ]. Later, NLRC4 was revealed to participate in IPAF inflammasome activation [ , ]. In general, NLRC4 inflammasome activation is required for host defenses against several bacterial pathogens, including Salmonella, Legionella, and Pseudomonas [ – ]. NLRC4 phosphorylation at Ser533 is critical for its activation of caspase-1 and pyroptosis, thereby leading to host defense functions during Salmonella infection [ ]. Moreover, NLRC4 can interact with NLRP3 to activate inflammasomes, particularly in innate immune cells [ ]. Notably, NLRC4 negatively regulates immunological memory by suppressing NLRP3 inflammasome activation and protecting against Salmonella infection through Th1 immune responses [ ]. Thus, interactions between NLRC4 and other inflammasomes may influence or modify immune responses in a context-dependent manner.
NLRC4 inflammasome activation is closely associated with the release of signaling lipids such as prostaglandins and leukotrienes and uncontrolled inflammation triggered by flagellin-mediated systemic inflammasome activation [ ]. Moreover, human NLRC4 appears to be associated with autoinflammation in humans, specifically, hyperinflammatory subtypes called macrophage activation syndrome and infantile enterocolitis, which are caused by the hyperactivation of innate immune responses [ , ]. In colorectal cancer (CRC), NLRC4 is believed to act as a tumor suppressor by inducing cell death and regulating immune responses [ ]. Nevertheless, the effects of NLRC4 mutations on various cancers, including CRC, remain controversial [ – ].
NLRC4-driven inflammasome responses appear to protect against acute bacterial infections [ – , ], and dysregulation of this inflammasome has been implicated in the pathogenesis of various human diseases, including autoinflammatory diseases, colitis, and cancer [ – ]. Therefore, balanced activation of NLRC4 inflammasomes is critical for maintaining immune homeostasis and preventing unwanted immune-related damage to host tissues.

## AIM2 inflammasome
The AIM2 inflammasome was identified as an intracellular protein platform that recognizes cytosolic double-stranded DNA and the DNA of vaccinia virus [ , ]. The HIN200 and pyrin domains of AIM2 bind to DNA and ASC, respectively, thereby activating caspase-1 and inducing the secretion of IL-1β and IL-18 [ ]. In the context of infection, cytokine generation and pyroptosis during AIM2 inflammasome activation contribute to immune responses that protect against bacterial infections. However, dysregulation of the AIM2 inflammasome is associated with pathological responses in inflammatory diseases, including atherosclerosis, skin and liver diseases, and neuroinflammation during acute brain injury [ , ].
In the context of tumors, previous studies revealed the critical function of AIM2 in preventing CRC through inflammasome-dependent and inflammasome-independent functions, as well as gut microbiota remodeling [ , ]. AIM2 participates in the suppression of PI3K/Akt signaling and mTOR kinase activity, thereby leading to the suppression of colon tumorigenesis and increased apoptosis [ , ]. Additionally, AIM2 mutations and microsatellite instability of the AIM2 gene have been reported to be involved in the tumorigenesis of CRC, hereditary non-polyposis CRC, and small bowel cancer [ – ]. Moreover, the reduced expression of AIM2 in tumor cells is associated with poor clinical outcomes in patients with CRC [ ]. In summary, AIM2 is critically involved in innate immune signaling, and its altered expression is associated with the pathogenesis of various inflammatory diseases and cancers. However, a gap remains in our understanding of the regulatory function of AIM2 in tumorigenesis and the potential utility of targeting AIM2 for the treatment of cancers involving AIM2 expression and mutation.

## CARD8 inflammasome
CARD8, also termed CARD-inhibitor of NF-κB-activating ligand or tumor-upregulated CARD-containing antagonist of caspase 9, was recently reported to serve as the main inflammasome component [ ]. As one of the inflammasome-forming pattern recognition receptors, CARD8 is composed of three major components: an N-terminal disordered region, a function-to-find domain (FIIND) consisting of the UPA region and ZU5 region, and a CARD [ , ].
In the context of certain danger signals, including infections, such as human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), coronaviruses, and picornaviruses, CARD8 functions as an inflammasome sensor [ – ]. The CARD8 FIIND is divided into two noncovalent parts through posttranslational autoproteolytic cleavage and is subjected to proteasomal degradation, activating the CARD8 inflammasome via the interaction of reactive UPA-CARD fragments with pro-caspase-1 [ , , , ]. The inhibition of the serine dipeptidases DPP8 and DPP9 contributes to the degradation of FIIND N-terminal fragments, thereby promoting the formation of an active NLRP3 inflammasome from the C-terminal fragment [ , ]. Activation of the CARD8 inflammasome triggers the activation of GSDMD, leading to pyroptosis in myeloid leukemia cells and T cells [ , ].

## NLRP12 inflammasome
NLRP12 is a key regulator of PANoptosis (P, pyroptosis; A, apoptosis; N, necroptosis), a component of the innate immune response and a form of lytic cell death mediated by caspases and receptor-interacting serine/threonine-protein kinases, and plays important roles in immune defense during infections, inflammatory diseases, and cancers [ ]. Recent studies have shown that NLRP12 participates in the activation of both the inflammasome and the PANoptosome in response to heme combined with PAMPs or TNF. In this context, the inflammasome becomes part of the larger NLRP12–PANoptosome complex, driving inflammatory cell death through caspase-8 and RIPK3 [ ]. In mouse models, NLRP12 deficiency enhances inflammatory responses, increases autoantibody production, exacerbates kidney damage, and causes lymphoid organ enlargement in an IFN-I-dependent manner, contributing to lupus progression and emphasizing its role in maintaining immune homeostasis [ ]. Additionally, hematopoietic cell kinase has been identified as a critical regulator of NLRP12-mediated PANoptosis, suggesting its potential as a therapeutic target in inflammation [ ].
NLRP12 has context-dependent functions in cancer. In CRC, its expression is reduced in both mouse and human tissues, and it suppresses CRC development by limiting β-catenin activation in intestinal epithelial cells [ ]. In contrast, NLRP12 is highly expressed in gastric cancer tissues and cells and is correlated with a poor prognosis. In this context, it promotes tumorigenesis by preventing TRIM25-mediated hexokinase 2 degradation and regulating histone H3 lysine 18 lactylation, thereby increasing Myc transcription [ ]. Furthermore, NLRP12 is expressed primarily in tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), where it drives protumor polarization and is associated with poor outcomes in lung adenocarcinoma patients [ ]. Together, these studies indicate that NLRP12 plays multifaceted, context-dependent roles in normal and cancer cells, regulating inflammasome activation, PANoptosis, and tumorigenesis. Further studies are warranted to elucidate its precise functions in various cancers and inflammatory diseases.

## NLRP6 inflammasome
NLRP6 plays a central role in host defenses by maintaining epithelial integrity during microbial infection [ ]. It directly recognizes lipoteichoic acid from gram-positive bacteria and activates the inflammasome complex in a caspase-11- and caspase-1-dependent manner, driving the maturation of IL-1β and IL-18 [ ]. Upon sensing double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) produced by various viruses, NLRP6 also undergoes liquid–liquid phase separation, a process essential for inflammasome activation and antimicrobial responses during mouse hepatitis virus or rotavirus infection [ ].
In cancers, NLRP6 increases the activity of the PI3K/Akt pathway by recruiting the E3 ligase RBX1 to p85α, leading to Lys2566 ubiquitination and the autophagic degradation of p85α. The loss of NLRP6 expression suppresses glioblastoma cell growth in vitro and in vivo, underscoring its tumor-promoting potential [ ]. NLRP6 also promotes small cell lung cancer metastasis by driving tumor-derived exosome-induced macrophage polarization toward the M2 phenotype [ ]. Moreover, recent work has shown that Nlrp6 deficiency delays hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) growth in mice and that the adoptive transfer of NLRP6-deficient macrophages promotes HCC progression. Mechanistically, NLRP6 deficiency increases macrophage-mediated phagocytosis via E-Syt1 [ ]. Together, these findings suggest that NLRP6 can influence tumorigenesis and metastasis in a context-dependent manner through its interplay with tumor-infiltrating macrophages, modulating their infiltration, phagocytic activity, and phenotype switching.
Notably, NLRP6 deficiency induces gut microbiota dysbiosis, which promotes the expansion of hepatic monocytic myeloid-derived suppressor cells and reduces T-cell populations. This immunosuppressive shift ultimately diminishes antitumor immune surveillance and drives liver disease progression toward HCC [ ]. Collectively, these studies identify NLRP6 as a multifunctional sensor with context-dependent roles in host defense, microbiota regulation, and tumorigenesis.

## Overview of pyroptosis
Pyroptosis is an inflammatory form of programmed cell death triggered by inflammasome activation and the subsequent cleavage of GSDM superfamily members [ , , – ]. The human GSMD superfamily members include GSDMA, GSDMB, GSDMC, GSDMD, GSDME/DFNA5, and PVJK/DFNB59, whereas mice have five members, including GSDMA, GSDMC, GSDMD, GSDME, and PJVK/DFNB59 [ ]. In general, the C-terminal domain of GSDM members, except DFNB59, contains a repressor region that suppresses the N-terminal domain, which has pore-forming activity. Upon activation by caspases or granzymes, the N-terminal domain is liberated to induce pyroptosis [ , , ].
Although the controlled activation of pyroptosis is crucial for host defense, its excessive activation leads to persistent inflammatory responses in various inflammatory diseases [ , ]. In the context of cancer, pyroptosis is thought to play a dual role, exerting either anticancer or procancer effects depending on specific contexts, such as cancer types and the TME [ ]. Recent research has revealed at least four distinct forms of pyroptosis, namely, canonical, noncanonical, apoptotic caspase-dependent, and granzyme-mediated pyroptosis, depending on the cell type, stimulus, and microenvironment [ , , – ]. In the following subsections, we provide a brief overview of the four forms of pyroptosis, as illustrated in Fig.  . For a more detailed discussion, readers are referred to recent review articles that elaborate on the pyroptosis classification, mechanisms, functions, and disease relevance [ , , , , ]. 

## Canonical pyroptosis
Depending on the involvement of caspase-1 [ ], pyroptosis can be broadly classified into canonical and noncanonical pathways. In canonical pyroptosis, PAMPs or DAMPs are recognized by intracellular NLRs, including NLRP1, NLRP3, and NLRC4; AIM2; and pyrin [ , ]. This recognition leads to the assembly of the inflammasome complex, which activates caspase-1-mediated cleavage of GSDMD, the crucial executor of canonical pyroptotic cell death [ – ]. Cleavage of GSDMD results in the oligomerization of the N-terminal domain of GSDMD, which binds to membrane lipids and forms pores in the cell membrane. These events lead to cell swelling and rupture, the release of inflammatory cytokines and cellular contents, and chromatin degradation during cell death [ , , ]. Although only a few studies have reported PTMs of GSDMs in pyroptosis activation, a recent study demonstrated that GSDMD undergoes S-palmitoylation at Cys191 by the palmitoyltransferases ZDHHC5 and ZDHHC9, a process that is enhanced by mitochondria-derived ROS and inflammasome activation. Interestingly, cleavage-deficient GSDMD can still induce pyroptosis when palmitoylated, indicating that palmitoylation serves as a crucial checkpoint for GSDMD activation [ ]. However, further studies are needed to determine whether other PTMs also contribute to GSDMD-induced activation of pyroptosis.
In general, activation of the canonical inflammasome pathway and the resulting pyroptosis primarily occur in immune cells, contributing to host defenses against various pathogenic infections [ , , , ]. In cancer, accumulating evidence suggests that pyroptosis plays an anticancer role, and attempts have been made to combine adjunctive drugs and agents targeting pyroptosis with chemotherapy and immunotherapy to inhibit cancer cell proliferation, migration, and invasion. This topic will be discussed in the later sections of this review. Because multiple types of cell death pathways, such as apoptosis, necroptosis, ferroptosis, and necrosis, can occur simultaneously or sequentially with pyroptosis at tumor sites, future experimental and clinical studies should consider developing potential therapeutic strategies that potentiate anticancer effects through synergistic actions on multiple modes of cell death.

## Noncanonical pyroptosis
Noncanonical pyroptosis can be triggered by the direct recognition of lipopolysaccharide (LPS) released from gram-negative bacteria by caspase-4/5 (in humans) or caspase-11 (in mice) [ – ]. Activated caspase-4/5/11 directly cleaves GSDMD, inducing pyroptosis without the involvement of the inflammasome complex [ , ]. The recognition of intracellular LPS by caspase-4/5/11 depends on bacterial escape from the vacuole, which is triggered by the interferon-inducible GTPase-mediated lysis of bacterial vacuoles [ ]. The insertion of the N-terminal fragment of GSDMD into the cell membrane and the formation of a cytotoxic pore trigger potassium efflux, resulting in the activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome [ , ]. Notably, the nuclear orphan receptor Nur77 functions as an LPS-binding protein and interacts with NLRP3, thereby promoting noncanonical NLRP3 inflammasome activity and pyroptotic cell death [ ]. Moreover, noncanonical pyroptosis activation is mediated by the cleavage of the pannexin-1 channel and purinergic P2X7 receptor signaling [ ]. An interesting trial reported the use of bacterial outer membrane vesicles as carriers of LPS to efficiently trigger noncanonical pyroptosis and increase the efficacy of pyroptosis-mediated anticancer immunotherapy [ ]. More experimental studies are needed to develop anticancer therapeutic strategies involving the induction of noncanonical pyroptosis pathways.

## Apoptotic caspase-mediated pyroptosis
Recent studies have identified an apoptotic caspase-mediated pyroptosis pathway involving caspase-3/GSDME, caspase-8/GSDMC, and caspase-6/GSDMB [ ]. Chemotherapeutic agents can activate caspase-3-mediated apoptosis, which, in cells expressing GSDME, leads to its cleavage and the induction of pyroptosis [ ]. During Yersinia infection, the apoptotic caspase-8 cleaves GSDMD to induce pyroptosis [ , ]. Caspase-8 can also cleave GSDMC, forming pores in the cancer cell membrane and inducing pyroptosis [ ]. In addition, apoptosis-related caspase-3/6/7 can cleave GSDMB to generate membrane-disrupting pores, thereby initiating pyroptosis [ ].
In contrast, several caspases exert inhibitory regulatory effects on pyroptosis. During apoptosis, caspases-3 and −7 can cleave GSDMD at noncanonical sites, thereby preventing pyroptosis [ ]. Similarly, the cleavage of GSDMB isoforms by caspase-7 or neutrophil elastase can either suppress its pore-forming ability [ ] or fail to induce cytotoxicity [ ], indicating an inhibitory role in pyroptosis. Thus, apoptotic caspases can function as both activators and inhibitors of pyroptosis, highlighting the complex crosstalk between apoptosis and pyroptosis. Further studies are warranted to elucidate the multifaceted roles of various caspases in regulating the complicated interrelationship between apoptosis and pyroptosis, and their effects on cancer as well as diverse disease conditions.

## Granzyme-mediated pyroptosis
Recent studies have revealed the involvement of several granzymes in the activation of pyroptosis. Granzyme A derived from natural killer cells and cytotoxic T cells can cleave GSDMB and promote cancer cell pyroptosis [ ]. Granzyme B from tumor-infiltrating natural killer cells also triggers tumor cell pyroptosis through the direct cleavage of GSDME [ ]. Interestingly, in a murine model, programmed cell death protein (PD)−1⁺ CD8⁺ T cells in the liver increased hepatocyte cytotoxicity through pyroptosis mediated by granzyme B and perforin, further exacerbating autoimmune liver disease [ ]. In addition, human type 2 innate lymphoid cells have been identified as potent cytolytic effectors against acute myeloid leukemia cells and use granzyme B to induce pyroptosis and/or apoptosis [ ]. These findings highlight the critical role of several immune cell subsets in promoting granzyme-mediated pyroptosis in the specific context of cancers and the TME. However, certain tumor cells also evade pyroptotic cell death through the generation of noncytotoxic GSDMB isoforms [ ]. Therefore, a clearer understanding of the various pyroptosis mechanisms, particularly in the context of tumor-derived strategies to evade protective pyroptosis, is needed to identify new therapeutic opportunities.

## A paradoxical function of inflammasome activation in cancer
Despite their potential benefits, inflammasomes and pyroptosis have both positive and negative effects on various cancers. In this section, we examine the roles and mechanisms of inflammasomes and pyroptosis in enhancing anticancer effects, along with their influence on antitumor immune responses.

## Beneficial roles of inflammasome activity and pyroptosis in anticancer responses

## Anticancer effects of inflammasome and pyroptosis components
An early study showed that protein-tyrosine phosphatase-1B (PTP1B) promotes human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2)-positive breast cancer cell death under hypoxic conditions [ ], whereas the inhibition of PTP1B blocks this process. Recent work has demonstrated that loss of the phosphatase PTP1B induces pyroptotic cancer cell death by promoting RNF213 phosphorylation at Tyr 1275, which drives its oligomerization. Activated RNF213 ubiquitinates the NF-κB regulator CYLD/SPATA2, leading to NLRP3 inflammasome activation and pyroptotic cell death in HER2-positive breast cancer cells, even under hypoxic conditions [ ]. Additionally, AIM2 inflammasome activation prevents p21 degradation and inhibits autophagy, thereby increasing the efficacy of differentiation therapy against acute myeloid leukemia (AML) and improving treatment outcomes [ ]. Furthermore, radiofrequency ablation induces pyroptosis in HCC cells via AIM2 inflammasome signaling, suggesting that AIM2 is a promising therapeutic target for increasing the efficacy of radiofrequency ablation in HCC treatment [ ]. Low-dose chemotherapy induces DNA damage in ileal epithelial cells, activates the AIM2 inflammasome, and increases IL-18 secretion. Notably, AIM2 knockout or microbial depletion reduces chemotherapy-enhanced antitumor effects, suggesting that low-dose chemotherapy could serve as a valuable adjuvant in cancer immunotherapy by activating the AIM2 inflammasome complex [ ]. Moreover, a recent immunoprecipitation‒mass spectrometry study revealed that neutrophil cytosol factor 4 (NCF4), an ASC-binding protein, is critical in defending against CRC. NCF4 regulates the balance between ROS production and the activation of NLRP3 and AIM2 inflammasomes through phosphorylation and redistribution [ ]. In the context of radioresistant non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), overexpression of R-spondin 3 (RSPO3) enhances NSCLC tumor radiosensitivity by promoting β-catenin-NF-κB signaling and NLRP3 inflammasome activation, suggesting that RSPO3 is a potential therapeutic target in patients with radioresistant NSCLC [ ]. Collectively, inflammasome components and their regulators represent promising therapeutic targets, particularly in cancers where suppression of inflammation contributes to disease progression, opening new avenues for improving treatment outcomes.
Several reports highlight that pyroptosis and its regulatory molecules are key contributors to anticancer effects in specific contexts. The N-terminal fragment of GSDMD (GSDMD-NT) promotes antitumor immunity by directly targeting mitochondria through its interaction with cardiolipin. Notably, phospholipid scramblase-3 facilitates the externalization of cardiolipin to the mitochondrial outer membrane, enabling its binding to GSDMD-NT, triggering mitochondrial damage, and thereby inducing pyroptosis-mediated anticancer immune responses [ ]. Additionally, noncanonical cyclic GMP-AMP synthase-stimulator of interferon genes (cGAS-STING) signaling can be activated by dodecyl 6-hydroxy-2-naphthoate targeting the mitochondrial protein cyclophilin D, leading to mitochondrial DNA release via caspase-9 activation and GSDME cleavage, thereby inducing anticancer effects [ ]. Moreover, DNA methyltransferase and poly (ADP-ribose) polymerase inhibitors induce the upregulation of NFX1-type zinc finger-containing 1, which triggers a pathogen mimicry response in mitochondria and activates STING-dependent NLRP3 inflammasome signaling, contributing to ovarian tumor suppression [ ]. Together, these findings highlight mitochondrial damage and signaling as critical drivers of pyroptosis and key mediators of anticancer effects. Moreover, inositol-requiring enzyme 1α (IRE1α), an ER stress sensor and endonuclease, inhibits NLRP3 inflammasome-dependent pyroptosis by silencing taxane-induced dsRNA and regulating IRE1α-dependent decay in triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) [ ]. The inhibition of IRE1α in Trp53−/− TNBC cells results in the taxane-induced accumulation of dsRNAs, which are recognized by ZBP1, thereby triggering the activation of NLRP3–GSDMD-mediated pyroptosis in cancer cells [ ]. These findings strongly suggest that ER stress-associated modulation of pyroptosis signaling affects the effectiveness of combined treatment with chemotherapy and immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs). Further studies are needed to identify specific target molecules and pathways that can be used to activate pyroptosis for improved anticancer therapeutics. 

## Transcriptional and epigenetic regulators of inflammasome activation
Inflammasome activation can be transcriptionally regulated to influence anticancer responses. A recent study revealed that achaete-scute family bHLH transcription factor 1 regulates the expression of solute carrier family 6 member 13 (SLC6A13), thereby influencing inflammasome activity. Elevated SLC6A13 levels are associated with an improved liver cancer prognosis, increased inflammasome activation, and the inhibition of HCC cell proliferation [ ]. Another transcription factor, homeobox C8, is upregulated and negatively regulates caspase 1 expression in NSCLC. Silencing homeobox C8 results in increased pyroptotic cell death and suppresses the tumorigenesis of NSCLC [ ].
Epigenetic control of inflammasome and pyroptotic components plays a crucial and complex role in the development and treatment of cancers [ ]. In CRC, NLRP3 is epigenetically silenced by HDAC2, which suppresses GSDMD-mediated pyroptosis. HDAC2 reduces NLRP3 transcription by decreasing H3K27 acetylation and disrupting the formation of the BRD4/p-P65 complex. Inhibiting HDAC2 enhances NLRP3/GSDMD-mediated pyroptosis, promoting anticancer therapeutic effects against CRC [ ]. The N6-methyladenosine writer METTL3 inhibits GSDMD-induced pyroptosis, thus promoting breast cancer progression [ ]. METTL3 functions in the negative regulation of pyroptosis through the YTHDF2-dependent posttranscriptional silencing of GSDMD [ ]. These data indicate that components of inflammasomes, GSDMs, and regulators of inflammasome and pyroptotic pathways exert anticancer effects in specific contexts. However, the precise mechanisms by which the same signals contribute to tumor clearance rather than tumor growth under certain conditions remain unclear. ## Inflammasomes, cell death components, and tumorigenesis

## Chronic inflammation, inflammasome activity, and tumorigenesis
Accumulating evidence indicates that chronic inflammation, driven by inflammasome activation, is a key contributor to tumor growth and metastasis [ , ]. Inflammasome activation and chronic inflammation, which are associated with obesity, are critically involved in the carcinogenesis of prostate cancer (PCa) [ ]. Additionally, house dust mite-triggered chronic lung inflammation, particularly the activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome, is linked to the development of lung cancer [ ]. In patients with chronic myelomonocytic leukemia with KRAS mutations, activated RAS-GTP proteins promote sustained NLRP3 inflammasome activation and distinct inflammatory cytokine profiles. The IL-1 receptor blocker anakinra effectively reduces inflammasome activity, decreases the monocyte count, and improves clinical outcomes [ ].
In colitis-associated CRC (CAC), zinc finger protein 70 (ZNF70) binds to NLRP3, reducing its K48-linked ubiquitination and activating signal transducer and activator of transcription (STAT) 3 to increase IL-1β production. Reducing ZNF70 expression improves CAC outcomes in mouse models, highlighting its potential as a treatment target [ ]. Moreover, protease-activated receptor 2 (PAR2) plays a critical role in inflammatory responses and breast cancer metastasis [ ]. The CRISPR/Cas9 gene-editing system, delivered via anionic bovine serum albumin nanoparticles (tBSA/Cas9-PAR2), successfully targets PAR2 in both cancer and inflammatory cells. PAR2 deficiency suppresses NLRP3 inflammasome signaling while increasing STING activation and reversing the epithelial–mesenchymal transition (EMT) in the TME, effectively preventing metastasis [ ]. These findings suggest that persistent inflammasome activation and its drivers, such as ZNF70 and PAR2, represent promising therapeutic targets for carcinogenesis and metastatic progression.
A recent study revealed that GSDMC expression is increased in CRC tissues. Increased GSDMC2/3/4 expression induces tumor cell pyroptosis, triggering the expression of CXCL2, which promotes the recruitment of myeloid-derived suppressor cells into the TME and subsequently leads to tumorigenesis and CRC progression [ ]. Recent studies have focused on targeting the NLRP3 inflammasome–GSDMD-mediated pyroptosis pathway to improve the clinical response rates to ICI therapy. Ongoing research is exploring the potential of modulating pyroptosis to increase the efficacy of not only ICI therapy but also other immunotherapies, such as chimeric antigen receptor-T-cell therapy (CAR-T cell therapy) and tumor vaccines [ ]. Although ferroptosis can inhibit tumor growth, its low to moderate activation paradoxically promotes tumor recurrence by initiating the macrophage/IL-1β/neutrophil/vasculature axis. Ferroptotic cancer cells stimulate macrophages to release IL-1β, which subsequently activates neutrophils, creating a TME conducive to tumor growth and metastasis. Targeting this inflammatory response significantly increases the efficacy of sorafenib in treating HCC, underscoring the importance of modulating ferroptosis-induced inflammation to improve cancer therapies [ ]. These findings highlight the critical roles of different cell death pathways and their associated molecular functions in tumor progression and metastasis.
Kaposi sarcoma herpesvirus (KSHV)-driven inflammasome activation may play a critical role in the pathogenesis of KSHV-associated disorders (KADs), including Kaposi sarcoma, primary effusion lymphoma, multicentric Castleman disease, and KSHV-inflammatory cytokine syndrome. Compared with healthy volunteers or people with HIV without KADs, patients with HIV and KADs present elevated IL-18 levels and increased caspase-1/4/5 activity. Moreover, patients with primary effusion lymphoma, multicentric Castleman disease, and KSHV-inflammatory cytokine syndrome display distinct inflammasome profiles, highlighting KSHV-induced inflammasome activation as a potential therapeutic target [ ]. These findings suggest that chronic inflammatory responses triggered by either pyroptosis-mediated DAMP signaling or virus-mediated inflammasome activation contribute to tumorigenesis. Further mechanistic studies are needed to identify the specific components of the inflammasome and pyroptosis that could serve as therapeutic targets and to clarify how their regulation affects the progression of different types of cancer. ## Inflammasome-regulating molecules as regulators of tumorigenesis
Several molecules involved in regulating inflammasome activation have been implicated in tumorigenesis and related pathologies. In HCC, sorcin is overexpressed and interacts with the NLRP3 inflammasome, thereby promoting tumor cell proliferation, migration, and invasion. Sorcin silencing activates pyroptosis, whereas caspase-1 inhibition reverses this effect. These observations suggest that sorcin is a potential biomarker and therapeutic target for HCC, suggesting new treatment strategies [ ]. A study of patients with bile reflux gastritis and a murine duodenogastric reflux (DGR) model revealed that ubiquitin-specific protease 50 (USP50) was significantly upregulated in macrophages, promoting NLRP3 inflammasome activation and the release of the danger molecule high-mobility group box 1 (HMGB1). USP50 interacts with ASC to activate NLRP3, and HMGB1 release drives gastric tumorigenesis via the PI3K/AKT and MAPK/ERK pathways, providing new insights into DGR-associated gastric cancer (GC) development [ ]. Additionally, the suppression of SIRT 7 depletes DDX3X, thereby disrupting NLRP3 inflammasome assembly and resensitizing sorafenib-resistant HCC cells. Combined SIRT7 inhibition and sorafenib treatment suppress hyperactive ERK1/2 signaling and IL-1β-driven pro-survival pathways, highlighting its therapeutic potential to overcome chemoresistance and enhance antitumor effects on HCC [ ]. Further research is needed for the precise modulation of inflammasome-regulating molecules by clarifying their specific roles in different types of cancer, suggesting novel strategies for tumor control.
Nerve injury-induced protein 2 (NINJ2), a member of the ninjurin family, acts as a surface adhesion molecule in Schwann cells [ ] and contributes to tumor suppression, the regulation of inflammation and metabolism, and longevity in mice [ ]. Ninj2-deficient mice exhibit altered carbohydrate and lipid metabolism, and pyroptosis is induced through the activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome, thereby linking inflammation to the spontaneous development of tumors, such as lymphoma [ ]. In gastric mucosal lesions, hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF)−1α upregulates Drp1, which promotes mitochondrial fission and mitochondrial ROS production, leading to NLRP3 inflammasome activation and the aggravation of portal hypertensive gastropathy and GC. Additionally, HIF-1α enhances glycolysis, which further promotes NLRP3 inflammasome activation and drives the progression of gastric mucosal lesions [ ]. Moreover, disrupted bile acid homeostasis in HCC may contribute to inflammasome-associated tumor progression. Specifically, chenodeoxycholic acid induces excessive mitochondrial ROS production to activate the inflammasome by targeting hemoxygenase-1, resulting in hepatocyte transformation and HCC tumorigenesis [ ]. Interestingly, sustained activation of the transcription factor STAT3 upregulates AIM2 expression to promote GC development and epithelial cell migration; however, this tumor-promoting activity of AIM2 appears to be independent of its inflammasome activity [ ]. Table highlights inflammasome-regulating molecules and their diverse roles in tumorigenesis across various cancers. Although the precise mechanisms remain incompletely understood, these molecules can modulate inflammasome activation, thereby influencing both tumor progression and immune surveillance. Elucidating the molecular pathways through which specific inflammasome and pyroptosis-related molecules act is therefore critical for developing targeted therapies that suppress tumor growth while enhancing antitumor immune responses. 

## Inflammasome-associated immune dynamics within the TME

## Anticancer functions of inflammasome-associated molecules within the TME
In the TME, a complex interplay exists between tumor immune evasion and tumor cell death mediated by pyroptosis, which can be triggered by surrounding immune and nonimmune cells. Emerging evidence underscores the critical role of dendritic cells (DCs) in cancer immunotherapy. In particular, DC activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome leads to caspase-1 activation and the release of proinflammatory cytokines, processes that are fundamental for combating tumors and increasing the efficacy of DC-based cancer therapies [ ]. However, immunosenescence poses significant challenges to cancer immunotherapy in elderly patients, diminishing the efficacy of treatments such as PD-1 and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte–associated protein 4 (CTLA-4) blockade due to impaired DC migration and a reduction in the number of naive CD8 + T cells. Zhivaki et al. demonstrated that specific adjuvants can “hyperactivate” DCs via the NLRP3 inflammasome, leading to Th1-skewed CD4+ T-cell responses that effectively eliminate tumors in aged mice [ ]. Additionally, CD146+ TAMs exhibit antitumor activity through the activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome, partly via the suppression of the expression of TMEM176B, an immunoregulatory cation channel. The blockade of TMEM176B with an inhibitor promotes the antitumor function of CD146+ TAMs. Thus, targeting CD146 and TMEM176B in TAMs could serve as a promising approach for enhancing inflammasome-mediated activation of the antitumor functions of macrophages in the TME [ ]. Expanding our understanding of the modulation of inflammasome pathways in specific immune cells offers significant promise for improving the efficacy of cancer therapies, particularly in challenging patient groups such as elderly individuals.
NLRC4 is downregulated in epithelial CRC cells, and its loss in tumor cells is associated with poor immune cell infiltration and poor survival outcomes. Restoring NLRC4 expression in tumor cells initiates inflammasome-independent immune reprogramming, promoting type I interferon signaling and the activation of DCs and Th1-mediated antitumor responses. In various carcinoma types, increased NLRC4 expression correlates with increased type I interferon-associated immune cell infiltration and improved clinical outcomes, underscoring its potential as a promising therapeutic target in aggressive cancers [ ]. Additionally, inflammasome activation within the TME stroma plays a tumor-suppressing role by interfering with the Myc–Ras oncogenic axis. In the bone marrow stroma, inflammasome activity suppresses Ras signaling, limits HSC proliferation, and restrains B-cell lymphomagenesis [ ]. These insights highlight the therapeutic potential of strategies aimed at enhancing inflammasome activation and pyroptosis in cancer, immune, or stromal cells that are tailored to specific tumor types or an immunocompromised host status to increase anticancer responses.
Several molecules have been shown to regulate inflammasome activation and pyroptotic functions in immune cells within the TME, contributing to antitumor responses across various cancers. Arf1 ablation in CRC cells leads to the release of oxidized lipoprotein, HMGB1, and genomic DNA, which bind to the CD36/TLR2/TLR6 complex in DCs. This interaction activates the NF-κB, NLRP3 inflammasome, and cGAS–STING pathways, ultimately increasing CD8+ T-cell infiltration and cross-priming and thereby promoting tumor regression [ ]. Apolipoprotein C-III (ApoC3) transgenic mice show enhanced CD8+ T-cell antitumor activity through ApoC3-induced inflammasome activation, which in turn activates macrophages. ApoC3 mimetic peptides that bind to TLR2/4 further increase immune responses by activating inflammasomes and enhancing CD8+ T-cell responses [ ]. Thus, modulation of the TME to promote anticancer effects holds promise as a potential therapeutic approach for various cancers, including CRC and liver cancer. Interestingly, adropin, produced by energy homeostasis-associated gene, plays a dose-dependent role in CRC progression. Low-dose adropin activates inflammasomes and increases M1 macrophage activity, leading to tumor suppression, whereas high-dose adropin promotes a protumor macrophage environment [ ]. ## Inflammasome-associated immune dynamics in the TME promote tumorigenesis
The profiles of several inflammasome components and related genes in the TME are related to cancer progression. In patients with lung adenocarcinoma, AIM2 upregulation is correlated with an immunosuppressive environment marked by inactive DCs and T regulatory cells, contributing to lung adenocarcinoma progression [ ]. Additionally, NLRP3 inflammasome activation in the TME promotes pancreatic cancer growth by inducing Th2-type inflammation via IL1β and COX2/PGE2, thus suppressing CD8+ T-cell activity. The inhibition of NLRP3 expression restores Th1 immunity, enhances CD8+ T-cell responses, and limits tumor growth [ ]. The NLRP3/NF-κB/IL-1β inflammasome pathway is also activated in pancreatic cancer characterized by TREM2 depletion, resulting in the enrichment of proinflammatory macrophages and eliciting pathogenic inflammation to promote the progression of pancreatic cancer [ ]. NLRP3 is highly expressed in ovarian cancer, resulting in elevated levels of IL-1 and PD-L1 that further promote an immunosuppressive TME and tumor progression. The inhibition of NLRP3 leads to a decrease in the infiltration of immunosuppressive cells, including myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), TAMs, and PD-1⁺/CD4⁺ T cells, ultimately suppressing tumor growth [ ]. Moreover, a recent study showed that sphingosine kinase 1-expressing TAMs are linked to CRC liver metastasis through the release of sphingosine-1-phosphate, which activates the NLRP3 inflammasome and promotes IL-1β production, thereby increasing TAM infiltration and CD8⁺ T-cell dysfunction [ ]. Another recent study demonstrated that GSDMD-mediated release of IL-33 from senescent hepatic stellate cells in the liver TME facilitates obesity-associated HCC progression [ ]. Future studies are needed to fully elucidate the molecular mechanisms of inflammasome-associated tumorigenesis through immune–tumor–stromal interactions within the TME across multiple cancer types.
Several factors have been reported to modulate inflammasome activation, influencing immune cell behavior and shaping the TME to affect cancer progression. In CRC, elevated levels of Alu RNA, an endogenous nucleic acid, in exosomes promote the NLRP3 inflammasome, which drives the EMT, tumor invasion, and metastasis. Thus, elevated Alu RNA levels in cancer exosomes are linked to CRC progression and potentially serve as a prognostic biomarker [ ]. Additionally, infection with colibactin-producing Escherichia coli induces inflammasome activation to promote tumorigenesis, whereas ATG16L1 in myeloid cells suppresses tumor growth in this context by dampening inflammasome activation within the TME. This highlights the intricate balance between microbial influences and host immunity in colorectal tumorigenesis [ ]. Moreover, increased expression of LLT-1, a ligand of the NK cell inhibitory receptor NKRP1A, is strongly associated with NLRC4 inflammasome activation and a poor prognosis for patients with glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) [ ]. Therefore, an in-depth understanding of inflammasome activation in the context of tumor-associated immune dynamics may support its potential application as a valuable target for prognostic assessment, diagnostics, and potential therapeutic strategies for several tumors.
Human small heterodimer partner (NR0B2), a nuclear receptor that regulates cholesterol metabolism, modulates inflammasome activity and IL-1β production in myeloid cells. By re-educating these cells, NR0B2 suppresses the expansion of regulatory T cells, thereby reducing tumor growth and improving treatment efficacy in metastatic breast cancer [ ]. The orphan nuclear receptor estrogen-related receptor alpha, a known tumor-promoting factor in various cancers, drives cisplatin resistance in endometrial cancer by binding to the NLRP3 promoter and inhibiting downstream caspase-1/GSDMD signaling within the hypoxic TME. This suppression increases resistance to pyroptosis and cisplatin in endometrial cancer cells [ ]. The nuclear receptor Rev-erbα (NR1D1), a negative regulator of the circadian clock, plays a tumor-suppressive role in lung cancer development through the suppression of NLRP3 inflammasome activation. NR1D1 deficiency in the TME promotes tumorigenesis, which is downregulated by MCC950 [ ]. A deeper understanding of the roles of numerous nuclear receptors in tumorigenesis and their anticancer effects may expand the potential application of nuclear receptor ligands or antagonists for the development of therapeutics to treat various cancers. ## Genetic variation in inflammasome components and tumorigenesis
Emerging evidence suggests that genetic variations in inflammasome components and related molecules can significantly influence cancer development. A recent study analyzed 299 single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in 16 NLRP3-related genes and their association with the survival of 866 patients with HBV-related HCC. Two SNPs, PANX1 rs3020013 and APP rs9976425, were significantly linked to OS. These data strongly suggest that specific SNPs in NLRP3-related genes play a pathological role in the prognosis of patients with HCC [ ] and that genetic variations in NLRP3 inflammasome components are associated with tumor progression and the prognosis. However, further studies are needed to identify the specific genetic variations that contribute to heightened or defective inflammasome activation.
Clonal hematopoiesis, which is characterized by somatic mutations in blood stem cells, is associated with increased inflammasome activity. A recent study of 62 patients with non-Hodgkin lymphoma or multiple myeloma (MM) revealed that 24% exhibited pathogenic clonal hematopoiesis mutations. These mutations may serve as biomarkers for predicting the risk of cytokine release syndrome in patients receiving CD19 or B cell maturation antigen-targeted CAR-T cell therapy [ ]. Further research is needed to elucidate the effects of these mutations on inflammasome activation in hematopoietic tumors and to clarify how these mutations and genetic variations influence inflammasome activation and tumor pathogenesis in different cancers.
The LRRK2 G2019S mutation is associated with Parkinson’s disease and an increased risk of colon cancer. In a colitis-associated cancer model, the LRRK2 G2019S mutation promoted colorectal tumorigenesis and activated the inflammasome, exacerbating gut epithelial necrosis. Inhibiting LRRK2 kinase activity or using GSDMD inhibitors has been shown to reduce the severity of colitis and cancer progression, positioning LRRK2 as a potential therapeutic target for colon cancer characterized by hyperactive LRRK2 activity [ ]. Additionally, mutations in NLRP1 and related genes are associated with rare skin disorders and certain dermatological conditions, including skin cancers such as squamous cell carcinoma and melanoma [ ]. Further research involving larger populations is needed to explore additional genetic variations, identify potential biomarkers, and develop personalized therapies for various cancers. Such efforts are crucial for developing targeted inflammasome-based anticancer therapies. 

## Inflammasome-related molecules as prognostic markers for tumors
Recent studies have highlighted the prognostic significance of inflammasome-related gene signatures in various tumors and within the TME. Increased AIM2 and NLRP3 levels are associated with a poor prognosis for patients with lung adenocarcinoma [ , ], highlighting their potential influences on cancer progression and patient outcomes. High inflammasome risk scores were correlated with shorter survival of patients with osteosarcoma, especially in patients with elevated FGFR3 and RARB expression. In particular, the key genes CD36 and MYD88 were linked to increased tumor aggression [ ]. Elevated HMGB3 levels in glioma tissues correlate with increased levels of M2 macrophage markers and NLRP3 components, promoting macrophage polarization and pyroptosis [ ]. Moreover, bioinformatics analysis of 1681 low-grade glioma samples from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) and the Genotype-Tissue Expression Project revealed nine key hub pyroptosis genes (CASP4, TP63, TIRAP, CASP9, IL18, TNF, IL1A, PLCG1, and TP53). High-risk low-grade glioma patients exhibit increased immune activity, suggesting the potential for personalized treatments and novel immunotherapy targets [ ]. In patients with oral squamous cell carcinoma and potentially malignant oral disorders, tissue NLRP3 expression and serum IL-1β levels are significantly increased and closely correlated with tumor invasion and progression, particularly with increased degrees of dysplasia, suggesting their involvement in tumor dedifferentiation and metastasis [ ]. These observations suggest that various inflammasome-related molecules may serve as potential prognostic markers for specific solid cancers, depending on the stage.
ASC/NLRP3 specks in patients with myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) are increased compared with those in healthy individuals and similar to those in patients with autoinflammatory disorders. The inflammatory cytokines TNF, IL-6, and IL-1β correlate with MDS progression and disease markers, highlighting the potential application of these biomarkers in MDS diagnosis [ ]. In addition, the levels of the inflammasome components CASP1 and ASC are elevated in patients with MDS compared with those in patients with other leukemias, suggesting their potential role in MDS pathogenesis and diagnosis [ ]. Moreover, in patients with AML, higher NLRP3 expression is associated with shorter survival, whereas lower levels of TXNIP, NLRP3, and IL1B correlate with a better prognosis, suggesting the potential of these genes to guide the development of targeted therapies for AML [ ]. Collectively, these findings emphasize the potential prognostic value of inflammasomes and pyroptosis-related molecules in solid and hematopoietic cancer cells and in the TME for predicting cancer cell aggressiveness and patient outcomes. ## Potential anticancer therapeutics targeting inflammasomes and pyroptosis
Significant efforts have been made to develop potential anticancer therapeutics that target inflammasomes and pyroptosis, aiming to either inhibit tumor-promoting inflammation or increase immune responses, thus representing promising strategies for cancer treatment. In this section, we explore pyroptosis-inducing molecules and strategies for leveraging these pathways in anticancer therapies.

## Strategies and molecules that activate inflammasomes and pyroptosis
In this section, agents, including natural products, synthetic compounds, nanobiotechnology-based agents, and inflammasome-activating agents, which induce inflammasome activation or pyroptosis for cancer therapy, are discussed and summarized in Table  . 

## Natural products that activate inflammasomes and pyroptosis
Natural products, along with their diverse bioactive compounds, are emerging as promising agents that activate inflammasomes and pyroptosis, providing novel strategies for enhancing antitumor responses and blocking cancer progression. For example, quercetin, a natural compound, shows promise for treating colon cancer through the induction of pyroptosis through the GSDMD pathway. Quercetin inhibits tumor growth by activating GSDMD-mediated pyroptosis; this process is facilitated by NEK7 [ ]. The flavonoid kaempferol activates the NLRP3 inflammasome in AGS cells through the NF-κB pathway, resulting in caspase-1 cleavage and IL-18 release. It also induces pyroptosis by inducing mitochondrial damage, highlighting its potential as a novel treatment strategy for GC [ ]. Additionally, docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) triggers ovarian cancer cell death via caspase-1-dependent pyroptosis and GSDMD cleavage through the p38MAPK/Mnk1 signaling pathway [ ]. Moreover, in CRC, the inhibition of HDAC2-mediated histone deacetylation activates NLRP3 and triggers pyroptosis and enhances therapeutic outcomes; this effect is achieved by targeting HDAC2 with santacruzamate A in combination with antitumor agents [ ]. Reniformin A, a compound isolated from Isodon excisoides, inhibits NSCLC growth by inducing pyroptosis via the TLR4/NLRP3/caspase-1/GSDMD axis [ ].
In addition, Cistanche deserticola, a traditional Chinese medicine, exhibits anticancer activity through the induction of both apoptosis and pyroptosis in T-cell lymphoma cells via noncanonical pyroptosis pathways involving caspase-1/4 and GSDMD/GSDME [ ]. 2’,4’-Dihydroxychalcone, which is derived from the consumable medicinal herb Abrus cantoniensis Hance, impedes the dissemination and progression of CRC through the suppression of NF-κB signaling and NLRP3 inflammasome activation while simultaneously facilitating apoptosis and pyroptosis by promoting GSDMD cleavage through a noncanonical pathway that modulates caspase-3/4/11 in CRC cells [ ]. Oroxylin A, a compound derived from Scutellaria baicalensis, activates caspase-3-mediated ROCK1 signaling, promoting M1-like macrophage polarization and T-cell infiltration. Additionally, oroxylin A can elicit the expression of glycolytic proteins from HCC-derived extracellular vesicles to activate the NLRP3 inflammasome in macrophages, further enhancing anti-HCC immune responses and improving the efficacy of ICIs [ ]. Currently, research on the effectiveness of combining natural products with existing chemotherapeutic agents to enhance anticancer responses is lacking. Future studies should focus on developing natural products that induce various types of cell death, with the goal of increasing the anticancer effects of chemotherapy by strengthening antitumor immune responses.

## Synthetic compounds that activate inflammasomes and pyroptosis
Recent studies have suggested the possibility of using synthetic compounds to target inflammasome pathways, which can potentially lead to the development of more effective cancer therapies. In NSCLC cells, 9‐(2‐ethoxyethoxy)‐10‐methoxy‐5,6‐dihydro‐[1,3]dioxolo[4,5‐g]isoquinolino[3,2‐a]isoquinolin‐7‐ium bromide (EEBR), a novel synthesized compound, induces pyroptosis by activating the NF-κB/NLRP3 signaling pathway and suppresses tumor growth in animal models [ ]. Carvedilol, a β-adrenergic receptor antagonist, also triggers pyroptosis in PCa cells by activating the NLRP3 inflammasome and promoting NF-κB signaling, suggesting its potential as a therapeutic agent for inducing pyroptosis in PCa cells [ ]. Statins such as simvastatin, a mevalonate pathway inhibitor, enhance the anticancer effects of immune checkpoint blockade therapy in ARID1A-mutated clear-cell ovarian carcinoma by triggering pyroptosis in mouse models [ ]. Future approaches targeting specific metabolic pathways and promoting pyroptosis are warranted to develop new therapeutic strategies that increase antitumor immunity against various tumors and are not limited to ARID1A-mutated cancers.
Administering bacteria-derived outer-membrane vesicles, which are abundant in PAMPs, can significantly enhance tumor vaccination by inducing trained immunity. These vesicles activate inflammasome pathways and increase interleukin-1β secretion in mice, thereby promoting immune responses and T cell activation to improve the protection against tumors [ ]. Additionally, LTX-315, a synthetic oncolytic peptide, promotes anticancer immunity by inducing the maturation of tumor-infiltrating DCs through the release of DAMPs and direct activation of TLR7. This process involves inflammasome signaling and MyD88-mediated pathways, leading to improved antimelanoma immunity [ ]. Radium-223, an alpha-emitter used in cancer therapy, not only induces DNA damage but also triggers immunogenic cell death, particularly pyroptosis, through the activation of the STING pathway [ ]. These efforts to develop novel molecules and compounds for the TME represent a promising strategy for increasing anticancer inflammasome activation, improving therapeutic outcomes, and stimulating robust antitumor responses.
Metal-ion-chelating phenylalanine nanostructures consistently activate K+ channel efflux through metal-ion-chelating dimers, increasing K+ efflux, activating the NLRP3 inflammasome and thereby driving increased ICI-mediated breast and colon cancer cell death [ ]. AB598, a CD39 inhibitory antibody, preserves eATP, activates myeloid cells, and promotes inflammasome activation via P2X7 signaling, thus improving its anticancer effects on solid cancers [ ]. Additionally, the anti-IL-18 binding protein antibody COM503 increases IL-18 availability in the TME, increasing T- and NK-cell activation and inhibiting tumor growth in mouse models [ ]. Recently, the administration of the novel NLRP3 agonist BMS-986299 [ ] in combination with ICIs (nivolumab and ipilimumab) has been shown to augment antitumor effects on immunologically “cold” cancers. BMS-986299 increases the release of IL-1β and IL-18, which induce the formation of T-cell memory responses and NK cells, respectively. Notably, BMS-986299 polarizes macrophages toward the inflammatory M1 type, providing a new potential treatment option for patients who do not respond or who develop resistance to ICIs. This combined approach, which utilizes antibodies with immune-activating functions in the TME, represents a promising strategy for amplifying antitumor inflammasome responses.

## Nanobiotechnology to enhance anticancer pyroptosis
Several studies have demonstrated the potential of nanobiology to increase tumor sensitivity to immunotherapy. Nanomaterials are emerging as a promising approach for inducing pyroptosis in cancer treatment because they offer controlled drug release, extended circulation, and reduced toxicity [ , , ]. Tumor-targeting nanoparticles containing atorvastatin (CS-HAP@ATO NPs) disrupt mitochondrial function and induce pyroptosis via NLRP3 inflammasome activation, enhancing antitumor immune responses [ ]. Biodegradable vanadium-based nanoparticles (NSB NPs) induce excessive ROS production and consume overexpressed glutathione by reducing the disulfide bonds of NSB NPs in the TME, leading to ferroptosis and NLRP3 inflammation-mediated pyroptosis in cancer cells [ ]. Additionally, phospholipid-coated sodium citrate nanoparticles trigger dual pyroptotic pathways in tumor cells: (1) the release of citrate and Na⁺ ions activates GSDMD-mediated pyroptosis, and (2) citrate induces caspase-8-dependent GSDMC-mediated pyroptosis, together enhancing antitumor immune responses [ ]. Homomultivalent polymeric nanotraps, such as heptakis(2,3,6-tri-O-methyl)-β-cyclodextrin (TMCD), effectively disrupt lipid metabolism and trigger pyroptosis in tumor cells. The outstanding nanotrap PTMCD4 causes lysosomal stress, activating the NLRP3 inflammasome and initiating pyroptosis in different types of cancer cells [ ]. An lncRNA-edited nanovaccine combined with an anti-TIM-3 antibody amplifies DC inflammasome activation, improves CD8+ T-cell responses, and increases TIM-3 blockade efficacy, thus augmenting immune checkpoint blockade immunotherapy in colon cancer [ ]. Furthermore, biomimetic nanocarriers encapsulating the CD39 antagonist POM1 and metformin increase the P2X7 receptor-induced activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome and pyroptosis, potentiating antitumor immune responses in the TME [ ]. Similarly, Xiao et al. [ ] reported that nanomedicine-based activation of tumor cell pyroptosis, which partly depends on granzyme B, can promote immunogenic cell death and increase anticancer treatment efficacy in a mouse breast tumor model. Additionally, a nanoplatform coloaded with Mn2⁺ and paclitaxel has shown potential as a circulating immunotherapy, effectively targeting both primary and metastatic cancer models [ ]. Ongoing efforts to engineer synthetic compounds using nanotechnology may provide promising new strategies to overcome tumor resistance and increase the efficacy of cancer therapy, with minimal side effects by activating pyroptosis and other cell death pathways.

## Other approaches: inflammasome/pyroptosis-activating strategies
Radiotherapy can be used to reverse resistance to anti-PD1 treatment. Appropriately controlled radiotherapy can activate the NLRP3 inflammasome, thereby promoting antitumor responses. In addition, combining chemotherapy with an NLRP3 agonist and radiotherapy contributed to tumor control and immune priming in both wild-type and anti-PD1-resistant lung adenocarcinoma models. These successful results encourage well-controlled inflammasome activation during radiotherapy, providing a potentially innovative treatment for patients with ICI-refractory tumors [ ].
Photodynamic therapy can induce pyroptosis through ROS generation; however, its effectiveness is limited by its lack of specificity and deep tissue penetration. A recent study addressed this issue by developing the near-infrared-II photosensitizer Z1, which targets mitochondria and efficiently generates ROS, thereby triggering both pyroptosis and apoptosis [ ]. In addition, modified 5-aminolevulinic acid photodynamic therapy induces pyroptosis through the activation of the JNK pathway and NLRP3 inflammasome in cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma [ ]. Proteotoxic stress induced by cuproptosis, a type of cell death triggered by excessive copper accumulation, has attracted attention as a potential cancer therapeutic strategy [ ]. Oxidative damage to intracellular membrane proteins caused by an amphiphilic photocatalyst initiates noncanonical pyroptosis. This process activates the inflammasome and caspases, leading to GSDMD cleavage and subsequent cell lysis. Photocatalysis produces hydroxyl radicals and hydrogen peroxide and destabilizes membrane protein folding, particularly under hypoxic conditions [ ]. Moreover, hydrophobic radical-trapping antioxidants, such as JSH-23, alkylate cysteine residues in the N-terminal region of CARD8, leading to its destabilization and proteasome-mediated degradation, triggering CARD8 inflammasome activation [ ]. These emerging approaches, which are based on intracellular membrane protein oxidation-triggered inflammasome activation, proteotoxic signaling, and pyroptosis targeting, may contribute to the development of novel therapeutic strategies for various primary and metastatic tumors.

## Inflammasome-modulating molecules
In the context of the role of inflammasomes in tumorigenesis, emerging research has also shown that targeting inflammasome activation can disrupt inflammatory pathways that promote tumor growth, representing a novel approach to enhance cancer treatment and potentially improve patient outcomes. ## Natural products inhibiting inflammasome activity and pyroptosis
Resveratrol exerts antitumor effects on GBM by reducing cell viability, proliferation, and migration through the suppression of NLRP3 inflammasome activation through the JAK2/STAT3 pathway, revealing potential therapeutic targets for increasing the efficacy of GBM treatment [ ]. Curcumin, a well-known anti-inflammatory agent, downregulates CD39, CD73, and NLRP3 expression and enhances anticancer effects on metastatic melanoma cells, suggesting that it represents a promising adjuvant for antitumor therapeutics [ ]. Additionally, brusatol, a quassinoid compound derived from the traditional Chinese herbal medicine Brucea javanica, mitigates NLRP3 inflammasome activation and reduces tumor growth and cancer progression, indicating its potential as a therapeutic agent for pancreatic cancer [ ]. Oridonin, a diterpenoid isolated from Chinese herbal medicine [ ], is extensively used for its anticancer effects mediated by the inhibition of caspase-1 activity and pyroptosis through canonical pathways [ ]. Furthermore, oxymatrine, extracted from the Chinese herbal medicine Radix Sophorae tonkinensis, suppresses the tumorigenesis of CRC, tumor migration, and metastasis by inducing mitophagy and inhibiting the activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome [ ].
Vitamin D suppresses stem-like cells in TNBC cells by inhibiting NF-κB signaling and reducing NLRP3 inflammasome expression [ ]. In a high-fat diet-induced colitis-associated cancer model, exogenous butyrate inhibited the NLRP3/caspase-1 pathway, reversing inflammation and tumor growth, indicating that it is a promising therapeutic strategy for colitis-associated cancer [ ]. Coenzyme Q0, derived from Antrodia camphorata, exerts antitumor effects on head and neck squamous cell carcinoma by inhibiting the NLRP3 inflammasome and hypoxia-induced ROS production and driving metabolic reprogramming through HIF-1α inhibition. Additionally, coenzyme Q0 reduces the expression of inflammasome components (NLRP3, ASC/caspase-1, and IL-1β), leading to decreased tumor cell migration and invasion [ ]. Moreover, schisandrin B, a compound present in the herbal medicine Schisandra chinensis, shows promise for inhibiting TNBC growth, migration, and invasion. Schisandrin B suppresses inflammasome activation, reducing the production of IL-1β, a key driver of TNBC progression [ ].
In a liver metastasis model of breast cancer, euphorbia factor L2, a diterpenoid derived from the seeds of Euphorbia lathyris L., inhibited ascites formation and tumor cell metastasis by reducing NLRP3 expression and increasing immune cell infiltration [ ]. The major compounds exiguaflavanone A and exiguaflavanone B from the S. exigua ethyl acetate fraction reduce cell proliferation, migration, and invasion by downregulating IL-1β, IL-18, IL-6, and NLRP3 expression in NSCLC [ ]. The aqueous fraction of Bacopa monnieri exerts potent anticancer effects on oral cancer models by inducing PARKIN-dependent mitophagy and reducing NLRP3 inflammasome activation by blocking ROS production, highlighting its potential as an effective oral cancer treatment [ ]. Moreover, osthole, a compound derived from Cnidium monnieri, suppresses CRC progression by targeting mitochondrial metabolism and autophagy and impairing the ASC/caspase-1/IL-1β inflammasome pathway and autophagic flux [ ]. These findings suggest that various natural compounds, either individually or in combination, have significant potential for managing different cancers by modulating the inflammasome and mitochondrial functions. Future identification of additional natural products with the ability to modulate metabolic reprogramming, autophagy/mitophagy, and immune cell functions in various cancers and within the TME is warranted.

## Synthetic compounds that suppress inflammasome activity and pyroptosis
Numerous studies have shown that MCC950, an NLRP3 inflammasome inhibitor, has potential anticancer effects on various cancers. The palate, lung, and nasal epithelium clone (PLUNC) inhibits the progression of nasopharyngeal carcinoma by promoting the ubiquitination-mediated degradation of NLRP3 [ ]. Combining PLUNC overexpression with MCC950, an NLRP3 inhibitor, effectively reduced nasopharyngeal carcinoma invasion and lung metastasis in vivo [ ]. Moreover, MCC950 manipulates NLRP3 inflammasome activation and facilitates paclitaxel-targeted TNBC cell death [ ]. Tyrosine kinase inhibitors, such as sorafenib and regorafenib, which are commonly used to treat advanced HCC, have been shown to activate inflammasomes. Given that inflammasome activation correlates with a poor prognosis in male patients with HCC, MCC950 enhances the anticancer effects of sorafenib in vivo. These findings suggest that suppressing inflammasome activation could potentiate the effectiveness of tyrosine kinase inhibitors in patients with HCC [ ].
The NLRP3 inhibitors glibenclamide and miR-223, which target NLRP3 posttranslationally, inhibit NLRP3 activation and its downstream proteins, processes that lead to decreased levels of IL-1β and IL-18, indicating its potential effectiveness against CRC; however, it does not completely prevent metastasis [ ]. The recently developed dual pattern recognition receptor-inhibiting peptide rCT-S100A8/A9 blocked TLR4 and receptor for advanced glycation end products (RAGE) expression, effectively inhibiting NLRP3 inflammasome activation. In mouse models, rCT-S100A8/A9 improved survival, reduced colitis and CRC burden, and alleviated colonic damage, highlighting its potential as a promising therapeutic option for CRC [ ]. Another nanostrategy-based study aimed to modulate MDSCs, which are key cells involved in cancer immune escape, by reversing the immunosuppressive microenvironment. Zhu et al. [ ] developed 30 nm gold nanoparticles that disrupt NLRP3 inflammasome-mediated IL-1β production, thereby reducing the number of MDSCs and increasing T-cell activation in the TME. Collectively, these findings suggest the potential of inflammasome-modulating agents to augment anticancer efficacy through their cytotoxic and/or immune-adjuvant effects in combination with other strategies. More studies are warranted to examine their therapeutic efficacy in diverse cancer models in both preclinical and clinical settings.

## Clinical relevance of inflammasomes in cancer

## Clinical trials using IL-1 receptor antagonists
Considering the potential of targeting inflammasomes in cancers, recent studies have explored how inflammasome modulation can be used to maximize antitumor activity. Clinical research can be designed based on either the induction of pyroptosis, a form of programmed cell death characterized by cell swelling and lysis, or TME remodeling. To date, the development of direct therapeutic agents designed to activate pyroptotic tumor cell death remains limited, with most investigations focusing on the blockade of inflammasome-related downstream effectors, particularly IL-1β.
Clinical trials of the IL-1 receptor antagonist anakinra have produced promising results in multiple cancers. A 2016 clinical trial revealed that anakinra was well tolerated and significantly reduced the levels of CA19-9, a biomarker for pancreatic cancer, by up to 70% compared with the starting levels (NCT02021422). The combination of neoadjuvant chemotherapy with anakinra for the treatment of eight patients with locally advanced pancreatic cancer achieved promising results, with prolonged recurrence-free survival and overall survival (NCT02550327). Canakinumab, a monoclonal antibody that inhibits the NLRP3 inflammasome by targeting IL-1β, resulted in a 77% reduction in incidence (HR: 0.23; p = 0.0002) and a 67% reduction in mortality in lung cancer patients (HR: 0.33; p < 0.0001) (NCT01327846; [ ]). In a clinical trial of anakinra in patients with CRC, potential benefits were obtained when anakinra was administered with other conventional therapies. A phase II clinical trial exploring the efficacy of anakinra in combination with 5-fluorouracil (5-FU) and bevacizumab in patients with CRC (NCT02090101) reported a median progression-free survival (PFS) of 5.4 months and a median OS of 14.5 months [ ]. Anakinra is also being investigated in the context of locally advanced rectal cancer in an ongoing clinical trial (NCT04942626; [ ]) evaluating its effect in combination with concurrent chemoradiotherapy for rectal cancer management. These studies help extend the therapeutic paradigm toward integrating targeted anti-IL-1 interventions into the standard therapy for certain stages of colon and rectal cancer.
The therapeutic potential of anakinra has been studied in hematologic malignancies, particularly MM. A clinical trial (NCT02492750) assessing the tolerability and efficacy of anakinra in combination with lenalidomide and dexamethasone demonstrated that the combination was well tolerated by all patients and effectively exerted antimyeloma effects with only minor side effects. Moreover, the results of another phase II clinical trial (NCT00635154) with long-term follow-up revealed that treatment with anakinra combined with dexamethasone, a corticosteroid commonly used to reduce inflammation and induce apoptosis in myeloma cells, slowed the progression of active myeloma and improved the OS of patients [ ]. These findings underscore the benefits of IL-1-inhibiting adjunct therapeutics for MM, in which chronic inflammation is a key driver of tumorigenesis. ## Clinical trials using natural products as adjuvants for anticancer treatment
Clinical trials using curcumin, an inhibitor of NLRP3 inflammasome activity, documented its potential in combination with standard chemotherapeutic regimens such as FOLFOX, comprising 5-FU, leucovorin, and oxaliplatin; the combination treatment resulted in a significantly increased median OS in patients with CRC (502 days vs. 200 days, HR: 0.34, p = 0.02) (NCT01490996; [ ]). Another study showed that the combination of curcumin with bevacizumab and 5-FU is safe and beneficial, with a median OS of 30.7 months and a median PFS of 12.8 months for patients with rectal cancer (NCT02439385; [ ]). A phase II trial is assessing the combined effect of quercetin, a flavonoid that activates pyroptosis, and dasatinib in patients with chemoresistant TNBC (NCT06355037). This trial aims to explore the adjunct efficacy of quercetin in combination with the TKI dasatinib in TNBC, an aggressive subtype of breast cancer with a poor prognosis. Additionally, a phase I/II study evaluated the combination of docetaxel and quercetin in patients with castration-resistant PCa (NCT06615752). A phase II study is currently ongoing to evaluate the safety and effectiveness of combining quercetin with ICIs in patients with head and neck cancer (NCT05724329). Furthermore, the use of DHA-based therapies for the treatment of breast cancer, NSCLC, and melanoma is being evaluated. A phase II clinical study investigated the efficacy of DHA as a neoadjuvant chemotherapy in patients with localized breast cancer (NCT03831178; [ ]) and palliative chemotherapy with paclitaxel in NSCLC [ ]. However, in phase III trial of metastatic melanoma, DHA with paclitaxel failed to be superior to dacarbazine [ ], indicating more DHA studies is needed before it can be used clinically. These findings suggest that natural products could serve as valuable adjuvants to various cancer therapies, potentially increasing treatment efficacy and improving patient outcomes by targeting inflammasome and pyroptosis pathways and sensitizing cancer cells to chemotherapy and immunotherapy. ## Other drugs being tested in clinical trials
A clinical evaluation of LTX-315, a synthetic oncolytic peptide and inflammasome activator, demonstrated its remarkable efficacy against several malignancies. In a phase II clinical trial for melanoma, patients treated with a combination of LTX-315 and pembrolizumab exhibited an overall response rate (ORR) of 14% and a disease control rate of 43% (NCT04796194). The efficacy of LTX-315 in combination with ICIs against multiple solid tumors, including melanoma, breast cancer, and head and neck cancer, has been reported. This efficacy is evidenced by increased numbers of tumor-infiltrating T cells and significant reductions in the primary tumor size, with complete responses observed in 2 of 44 lesions, a more than 50% reduction in tumor size in 5 of 44 lesions, and stable disease in 20 of 44 lesions (NCT01986426; [ ]).
Several other inflammasome regulators, including DFV890 (NCT05552469), dimethyl fumarate (NCT02546440, [ , ]), disulfiram (NCT05667415, NCT03950830), AB598 (NCT05891171), and BMS-986299 (NCT03444753; [ ]), are being evaluated in current clinical trials. In a phase II clinical trial involving patients with refractory cutaneous TCL, dimethyl fumarate, which exerts anti-inflammatory effects by inhibiting GSDMD [ ], a favorable safety profile (severe adverse events, 1.7%) and clinical efficacy (ORR in skin lesions, 30.4%; ORR in patients with Sézary syndrome, 80%) were observed (NCT02546440; [ ]). A phase I study of GBM also showed that high doses of dimethyl fumarate are associated with an improved median PFS (11.8 months) and median OS (14.5 months) [ ]. Disulfiram, an aldehyde dehydrogenase inhibitor, when repurposed as an inflammasome modulator in cancer therapy, produced promising preliminary results [ , ]. However, other studies have reported unfavorable outcomes (NCT03950830; [ ]). Recently, a phase I trial of the first-in-class NLRP3 agonist BMS-986299 documented its safety and efficacy in combination with an ICI (NCT03444753; [ ]). Although BMS-986299 monotherapy failed to show clinical benefits (ORR, 5%; n = 1/21), its combination with nivolumab and ipilimumab achieved an ORR of 33%, with partial responses (n = 3/9) in immunologically cold tumors. Further clinical trials are necessary to identify novel, innovative agents that potentially affect the regulatory roles of inflammasomes and pyroptosis in the TME and tumor cells and thus to develop successful therapeutic regimens with conventional drugs in diverse cancer settings. ## Conclusions
Accumulating evidence has confirmed that chronic and sustained inflammation, driven by persistent IL-1β and IL-18 secretion and inflammasome signaling, favors a protumorigenic TME that promotes tumor cell proliferation, metastasis, and angiogenesis. In contrast, the activation of various inflammasomes contributes to immune surveillance against cancer by inducing pyroptosis and enhancing antitumor immunity. This dual role presents a major challenge in the development of inflammasome-targeting cancer therapy. A deeper understanding of the mechanisms by which distinct types of inflammasomes and pyroptosis pathways participate in crosstalk with different types and stages of cancer and the TME is critically important for the development of new therapeutic strategies targeting inflammasomes.
Genetic variations in NLRP3-related and inflammasome-regulating molecules shape tumor susceptibility and serve as tumorigenic drivers in certain cancers. However, large-scale genome-wide association studies are still needed to identify susceptibility or resistance variants related to inflammasomes and pyroptosis across a wide range of cancers. Such efforts will help elucidate the genetic association between inflammasomes and pyroptosis in the context of the causes and pathogenesis of tumors and metastasis. Recent studies have identified several inflammasome-related molecules as valuable prognostic biomarkers for predicting the outcomes of patients with various cancers and the TME; however, this field remains in its infancy. Further studies should be conducted to identify single or combined inflammasome biomarkers for predicting pathological processes in tumorigenesis and to understand how they underlie the creation of a protumorigenic microenvironment. Accumulating integrated information on tumor-specific genetic variants and expression profiles of inflammasomes and molecules involved in pyroptosis will influence the future direction of personalized preventive and therapeutic strategies against tumors.
Considering the multifaceted roles of inflammasomes in both tumorigenesis and anticancer effects, several experimental therapeutic approaches have focused on modulating inflammasomes and pyroptosis to develop promising therapeutic strategies for various cancers. Numerous in vitro and in vivo studies of natural products and synthetic compounds have shown the potential of employing inflammasome-modulating agents to treat cancers, depending on the type and stage of the disease. Additionally, extensive efforts have been made, including recent advances in nanomedicine delivery, to activate inflammasomes and pyroptosis to either eliminate cancer cells or enhance antitumor immunity in various cancers. Moreover, recent challenges in mitigating tumor-promoting chronic inflammation have shown significant potential for the treatment of specific cancers in a microenvironmental context. Although only a few inflammasome-targeting molecules, beyond IL-1 receptor antagonists, have reached clinical application, growing mechanistic insight continues to bridge the gap between preclinical discovery and translational potential. Ultimately, a holistic understanding of the dynamic molecular networks governing inflammasome and pyroptosis pathways across diverse cancers will be pivotal for designing next-generation therapies that not only suppress tumor growth but also reprogram the tumor immune landscape toward durable cancer control.
