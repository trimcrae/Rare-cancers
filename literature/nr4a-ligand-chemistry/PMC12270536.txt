## Abstract
Halogens play a fundamental role in drug design, influencing bioactivity, stability, and selectivity. However, their impact on toxicity, particularly genotoxicity, cardiotoxicity, and hepatotoxicity, remains a critical challenge in drug discovery. This study presents HD-GEM (Hybrid Dynamic Graph-based Ensemble Model), a novel machine learning framework integrating graph neural networks, descriptor-based molecular fingerprints, and ensemble meta-learning to predict the toxicity of halogenated aromatic compounds and drug scaffolds. HD-GEM demonstrates superior predictive power compared to conventional machine learning (ML) models and popular toxicity web applications like ProTox, ADMETlab, and admetSAR, achieving high accuracy and Receiver Operating Characteristic—Area Under Curve scores across diverse datasets. Importantly, a node perturbation analysis revealed that carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen atoms within the scaffold dominate toxicity predictions, whereas halogen contributions were minimal, challenging the conventional assumption that halogenation inherently increases toxicity in many pharmacological contexts. Among halogens, iodine-substituted compounds exhibit the lowest toxicity, a trend corroborated across single-, double-, and triple-ring scaffolds. Notably, polyhalogenated scaffolds show reduced toxicity, suggesting a stabilizing effect that mitigates reactive metabolite formation. This study presents an interpretable artificial intelligence-driven framework for toxicity prediction in the context of computational toxicology and cheminformatics. Atom-level and descriptor-based analyses reveal scaffold- and feature-specific contributions to toxicity.
Keywords: artificial intelligence, cardiotoxicity, graph neural network, halogen, hepatotoxicity, machine learning

## Introduction
Drug discovery is a lengthy and capital-intensive process, often requiring several years to develop a novel therapeutic agent. According to a study published in 2021, the costs of developing a new drug (prelaunch) range from US$161 million to US$4.54 billion, with an overall success rate of clinical drug development ranging from 10%–15% [ ]. These figures underscore the substantial financial and temporal investments required in drug development, coupled with relatively low success rates necessitating streamlining of the drug discovery pipeline. Toxicity is the highest contributor to drug attrition, accounting for a combined 31% failure rate including preclinical animal and human toxicity [ ]. Cardiovascular and liver associated risks with 27% and 15% respectively are the most common contributors to attrition in advanced molecules [ ]. Resource-intensive in vivo and in vitro toxicity tests hinder high-throughput screening of chemical compounds. In silico techniques involving modelling and structure–activity relationships provide a powerful alternative to traditional testing methods enabling efficient and economical identification of potential toxicophores [ ]
Traditional in-silico approaches involve quantitative structure–activity relationships (QSARs) and chemical grouping approaches with software and webserver applications like TOPKAT, PASS, MCASE, TEST, CAESAR, LAZAR, Derek Nexus, VEGA, and OECD QSAR toolbox among others [ ]. Artificial intelligence (AI) is transforming many fields, including toxicological risk assessment. Toxicology, being inherently data-rich, stands to benefit greatly from advanced AI integration of large and diverse data volumes, balanced accuracy, and probabilistic risk assessments despite flaws of black-boxing and dataset biases [ ]. Recent advancements in machine learning have led to the development of several web-based applications aimed at predicting cardiotoxicity and hepatotoxicity, enhancing early detection and drug safety assessments. For instance, ProTox 3.0 is a webserver that predicts the toxicity of chemicals, including human ether-a-go-go-related gene (hERG) blockers, which are associated with cardiotoxicity [ ]. Similarly, ADMET-AI, developed by Stanford University, utilizes graph neural networks (GNNs) to predict drug-induced cardiotoxicity [ ].
In the realm of hepatotoxicity, an ensemble model integrating machine learning and deep learning techniques has been developed for comprehensive prediction, leveraging datasets of less than 1600 compounds to assess liver toxicity risks [ ]. Additionally, the HeCaToS project has generated multi-omics data for repeated dose toxicity, facilitating the development of in silico models for predicting both cardiotoxic and hepatotoxic effects [ ]. Tools like Pred-hERG 2024, which focuses on cardiac ion channel blockade, and InterDILI, which classifies compounds based on Food and Drug Administration (FDA)’s hepatotoxicity risk categories, further enrich the computational landscape [ , ]. Webservers such as admetSAR3.0 and ADMETlab 3.0 also offer integrated predictions for both cardiotoxicity and hepatotoxicity, supporting interactive visualizations for medicinal chemistry optimization [ , ]. These web-based tools represent significant strides in the application of AI for toxicity prediction, offering accessible platforms for researchers and clinicians to evaluate potential adverse effects of compounds. Some drawbacks of these tools include limited molecule recognition, poor generalization, limited accuracy, and recall [ ]. An alternative approach with improved accuracy, robustness, and generalization is required in accordance with regulatory guidelines to improve toxicity risk assessment, which is the primary objective of this study.
This study introduces a novel hybrid dynamic GNN-descriptor and ensemble meta-learner model (HD-GEM) for predicting compound toxicity by integrating graph-based molecular representation learning with extended chemical descriptors offering a unique combination of structural and chemical insights. The framework employs a flexible GNN architecture, allowing the selection of convolutional layers such as graph attention networks (GATs), graph isomorphism networks (GINs), or GraphSAGE based on dataset characteristics, enabling effective capture of atom-level and bond-level interactions for downstream learning. Additionally, molecular structures encoded as SMILES strings are enriched with diverse descriptors, including MACCS keys, ECFP, and topological fingerprints. To enhance predictive performance, the model incorporates ensemble meta-learners, including CatBoost, Random Forest, and Logistic Regression, which combine their strengths through soft-voting strategies for robust and accurate predictions.
HD-GEM is a dynamic combination of adaptable GNNs, rich descriptors, and ensemble learning, complemented by advanced feature engineering, hyperparameter optimization, and techniques to address class imbalance. This integration makes the model a robust and adaptable tool for toxicity prediction, which was further used to evaluate the role of halogenation in influencing scaffold toxicity. The model was trained for only cardiotoxicity and hepatotoxicity citing the highest causative correlation to drug attrition [ , ], but is capable of training and predicting of other toxicity endpoints without requiring any modifications. HD-GEM was benchmarked against popular ML models including XGBoost, SVM, Random Forest, GAT, GCN, GIN, and GraphSAGE. The HD-GEM models were also benchmarked against other popular webserver applications prominently used for toxicity predictions.
Site-specific halogenation of a scaffold can have diverse effects on the pharmacological, toxicological, and therapeutic properties of the scaffold [ ]. Our lab reviewed several case studies showing strategic halogenation mitigates toxicity and improves the druggability of the scaffolds [ ]. We also investigated the antimicrobial effects of several halogenated compounds and discovered halogenation improved antimicrobial and antibiofilm activity of the base scaffolds [ ]. Since halogenation enhanced the antimicrobial and antibiofilm properties of scaffolds, the secondary objective of this study is to study the effect of site-specific halogenation on the toxicological profile of the antimicrobial scaffolds using HD-GEM models. Focusing on the entirety of scaffolds available would not be a feasible approach, therefore we decided to specifically focus on simple aromatic scaffolds prominently used in medicinal chemistry and drug discovery. Annotated single, double, and triple-ring scaffolds with various degrees and types of halogenations and noted antimicrobial usage were curated for the study. Also, drug databases were curated to analyze the effects of halogenation on drugs predicted to be hepatotoxic or cardiotoxic using the HD-GEM model. In addition, the effects of sulfonation and nitrogenation were also compared to that of halogenation.

## Methodology

## Dataset preparation and feature engineering
The toxicity datasets for cardiotoxicity and hepatotoxicity were obtained from the TOXRIC database (https://toxric.bioinforai.tech/home) [ ]. Additionally, hERG inhibition training sets from two previous studies [ , ] were added to the TOXRIC hERG inhibition dataset for the final cardiotoxicity training set. The cardiotoxicity dataset is a binary classification dataset with 0 and 1 values indicating nontoxic and toxic classes respectively. The assessment is based on the threshold of 10 μM IC50 inhibition of the human hERG channel [ ]. The hepatotoxicity dataset from TOXRIC is also a binary classification dataset and classified based on the drug-induced liver toxicity (DILI) as per US FDA drug labeling guidelines. The cardiotoxicity and hepatotoxicity datasets have 16 173 and 2889 entries, respectively, of which 100 compounds from both datasets have been assigned as validation datasets. The datasets comprise SMILES strings of the compounds along with their corresponding toxicity value.
To establish the relationship of toxicity to site-specific halogenation, experimental datasets were curated from PubChem and ZINC databases. We selected single-, double-, and triple-ring aromatic scaffolds and generated all possible halogenated derivatives viz. single, double, triple, poly (tetra, penta and hexa) halogenation and atom type of bromine, fluorine, chlorine and iodine were curated with their corresponding SMILES strings. Only the base scaffold and its halogenated derivatives were included to isolate structure-toxicity relationships without interference from other substituents. Datasets were curated from DrugBank, Drug Central, and the FDA to analyze trends in halogenation degree and type among drugs predicted to be hepatotoxic or cardiotoxic using the HD-GEM model. Additionally, the impact of halogenation on toxicity is compared to that of sulfonation and nitrogenation. Each SMILES string is processed to extract two categories of features, i.e. graph-based features and descriptor-based features.
For graph-based features, SMILES strings are converted into molecular graphs where atoms represent nodes and bonds represent edges. For each node, features of atomic number, degree, total hydrogens, aromaticity, hybridization, and ring membership are extracted whereas bond type and aromaticity are extracted for edges. Descriptor-based features included both fingerprint types and extended descriptors, with meaningful features selected based on SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) analysis (results not shown). SHAP analysis was employed as a post hoc interpretability approach following the initial development of the HD-GEM model using the complete set of molecular descriptors and fingerprints. This analysis was conducted outside the core model training pipeline to independently quantify the contribution of each feature to the model’s predictions. Features identified as having minimal or redundant influence based on their SHAP values were systematically removed. Only those descriptors and fingerprints demonstrating substantial predictive relevance were retained to construct the final optimized feature set for subsequent model training ( ). The fingerprints calculated were MACCS keys, extended connectivity fingerprints (ECFP), atom-pair fingerprints, and topological torsion fingerprints. An additional 19 extended descriptors are calculated including molecular weight, logP, topological polar surface area, rotatable bonds, valence electrons, etc. Invalid SMILES strings, duplicates, molecules with NaN values in feature extraction, or descriptors failing computation are excluded from the dataset. Once the features are extracted, descriptor-based features are standardized using StandardScaler to ensure uniformity across input features.

## Graph neural network design
A hybrid GNN architecture is designed to integrate graph-based learnings to descriptor-based features. The molecular graph is passed through multiple GNN layers like GAT, GIN, or GraphSAGE. Batch normalization layers are added after each graph convolution to stabilize training, and dropout layers prevent overfitting. After processing, node-level embeddings are aggregated into graph-level embeddings using global mean pooling. The graph-level embeddings are concatenated with the descriptor-based features to form a unified feature vector. This vector represents both structural and physicochemical properties. The concatenated feature vector is passed through a multilayer perceptron (MLP) to predict a proxy toxicity score, used only for optimizing architectural hyperparameters, not for final toxicity predictions. The final output is a single score interpreted as a probability of toxicity. A threshold derived from receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analysis is used to classify compounds as toxic or nontoxic. ROC analysis assesses the diagnostic performance of the model by plotting the true positive rate (sensitivity) against the false positive rate (1 − specificity) across a range of threshold values. The optimal cutoff point is selected based on the trade-off between sensitivity and specificity, ensuring that binary predictions reflect a data-driven balance between false positives and false negatives. The GNN components include:

## Graph attention network
GAT uses attention mechanisms to assign importance weights (αi,j) to neighboring nodes during aggregation [ ].

## Graph isomorphism network
GIN is specifically designed to learn expressive node embeddings by approximating injective mappings [ ].

## GraphSAGE
GraphSAGE aggregates features from neighboring nodes using a defined aggregation function (e.g. mean, max, or sum) [ ].

## Global mean pooling
After processing the molecular graph, node embeddings are aggregated into a graph-level embedding using global mean pooling [ ].

## Integration of descriptor-based features
The graph-level embeddings are concatenated with descriptor-based features to form a unified feature vector.

## Multilayer perceptron
The unified feature vector is passed through an MLP for proxy toxicity prediction. The MLP applies linear transformations, nonlinear activations, and dropout layers [ ].

## Class imbalance mitigation and hyperparameter optimization
Class imbalance, being a common occurrence in toxicity datasets, needs to be addressed. Synthetic minority oversampling (SMOTE) was used, which generates synthetic samples for the minority classes until a balanced dataset for training is obtained [ ]. Class weights were also calculated and incorporated into the binary cross-entropy loss function to maintain equal contribution of both the classes to model’s optimization. Hyperparameters are tuned using Optuna, to minimize the validation binary cross entropy loss across 50 trials [ ]. The hyperparameters tuned were GNN hidden channels (range 32–512), dropout rate (0.2–0.5), learning rate (log 1e-4 to 1e-3), weight decay (log 1e-5 to 1e-3), GNN layers (1–4) and GNN type (GAT, GIN, and GraphSAGE). Each trial is evaluated using five-fold cross-validation. Early stopping with a patience of 40 epochs is used to prevent overfitting.
The toxicity scores predicted by the GNN module post optimization are not used directly for final compound classification. Instead, the GNN outputs are employed solely for learning architecture-specific parameters such as learning rate, weight decay, hidden dimensions, and dropout rate via hyperparameter tuning. Final toxicity predictions are made solely by meta-learners (CatBoost, Random Forest, Logistic Regression), which are trained on descriptor-based features subjected to PCA and SMOTE resampling.

## Cross-validation, ensemble learning and threshold optimization
Stratified K-fold cross-validation is used to divide the dataset into training and testing subsets while maintaining the original class distributions [ ]. During each fold the model’s predictions are combined with descriptor-based features using an ensemble meta learner, comprising CatBoost, Random Forest and Logistic Regression [ ], Principal component analysis (PCA) is used for descriptor-based features to reduce dimensionality by reducing feature size while retaining critical information [ , ]. The meta-learner combines predictions from multiple classifiers using a soft-voting mechanism to further improve performance [ ].
Optimal classification thresholds are determined using the G-means criterion on the ROC curve [ ]. The threshold that maximizes the geometric mean of sensitivity (true positive rate) and specificity (1 – false positive rate) is selected for each fold. This ensures an ideal balance between detecting toxic compounds and minimizing false positives.

## Fine-tuning and model deployment
The fold with the highest ROC-AUC score during cross-validation is selected for fine-tuning the final model. PCA is applied dynamically to reduce dimensionality, and SMOTE is used to balance the dataset further. The final model is trained on the best fold with optimal hyperparameters and threshold.
For external validation datasets and experimental datasets, SMILES strings are processed. These features are scaled using the pretrained StandardScaler and reduced using the pretrained PCA model [ ]. Toxicity predictions are generated using the fine-tuned meta-learner, and results are appended to the dataset for further analysis.

## Benchmarking
Benchmarking of the models was done in two ways, evaluating the model with other machine learning models on various benchmark test sets and evaluating the model with other webserver applications using our validation datasets. We benchmarked against XGBoost, SVM, Random Forest, GAT, GCN, GIN, and GraphSAGE. For the XG-Boost, SVM and Random Forest models, the same molecular descriptors and fingerprints were used as previously used in the HD-GEM model and hyperparameters were tuned by Optuna. For the GNN models, molecular graphs were generated based on the SMILES strings of the compounds to build the models and hyperparameters were similarly tuned using Optuna.
The benchmark test sets for cardiotoxicity were obtained from previous hERG inhibition studies [ ]. The benchmark test sets for hepatotoxicity were obtained from DILIrank [ ], DILIst [ ], therapeutic data commons (TDC) [ ], LTKB [ ] and the test set from a deep learning DILI study [ ]. These datasets were compared to the respective training data and duplicates were removed. Multiple toxicity predicting web applications like ProTox-3.0 (https://tox.charite.de/protox3/), ADMETlab 3.0 (https://admetlab3.scbdd.com/) and admetSAR 3.0 (http://lmmd.ecust.edu.cn/admetsar3/) were used to benchmark performance against both cardiotoxicity and hepatotoxicity HD-GEM models. Specialized toxicity web applications like cardioToxCSM (https://biosig.lab.uq.edu.au/cardiotoxcsm/) for cardiotoxicity and DL-DILI server (http://www.pkumdl.cn:8000/DILIserver/DILIhome.php) and DILI PRedictor (https://dili.serve.scilifelab.se/) for hepatotoxicity were also used for benchmarking. All the webserver applications were accessed on 26 November 2024.

## Atomic toxicity contribution using node perturbation
For a selected molecular graph (compound), the graph’s features, including atomic and global descriptors, are extracted. The global descriptors are scaled using a pretrained StandardScaler and reduced using a pretrained PCA model to ensure consistency with the training pipeline. The scaled and reduced features are fed into a pretrained ensemble meta-learner model, consisting of classifiers like CatBoost, RandomForest, and LogisticRegression. The original prediction probability (baseline) is stored for reference. For each node (atom) in the molecular graph, the node’s features are perturbed by zeroing out its contributions [ ]. The modified graph is reprocessed through the same scaling and PCA transformation steps, followed by a prediction using the meta-learner. The difference between the baseline and perturbed prediction probabilities is calculated, quantifying the contribution of the perturbed node to the overall prediction [ ]. The contributions from all nodes are normalized to a 0–1 scale, ensuring a consistent representation of their relative importance across the molecular structure [ ].

## Model implementation and usage
The models were implemented in Python language on Google Colab Pro, leveraging NVIDIA Tesla T4 GPU with 16GB of VRAM for faster computations. Hyperparameter optimization was carried out using the Optuna library. Libraries like Pytorch, PyTorch Geometric, RDKit, Scikit-learn, Optuna, Imbalanced-learn, CatBoost, Joblib, Pandas, and NumPy were used in the implementation of the model. The complete schematic representation of the workflow of the HD-GEM model is represented in and a detailed model architecture description is provided in the supplementary. The HD-GEM model framework is user-friendly, particularly in the Google Colab environment, where it simply requires running notebook cells and uploading the necessary datasets. The model is pretrained, requiring users to simply upload the necessary datasets and PKL files to their Google Drive to run it without any additional setup. It is also easily adaptable to other Jupyter notebook environments. Furthermore, the model can automatically train and predict various toxicity endpoints upon uploading a binary classification dataset. Detailed usage instructions are provided in the repository’s README file.
 

## Ablation study
To systematically evaluate the individual contributions of each component in the HD-GEM architecture, we performed an extensive ablation study. The objective was to assess the role of GNNs, molecular descriptors, chemical fingerprints, data preprocessing strategies (SMOTE and PCA), and the meta-learner ensemble in overall toxicity prediction performance. A total of 10 ablated model configurations were constructed by selectively removing or using one component of the full HD-GEM framework while keeping the remaining architecture constant. A summary of the ablated models is provided in the .
To eliminate confounding effects due to suboptimal tuning, all models including ablated variants were trained using hyperparameters optimized through Optuna training. Each ablation model was trained independently on training dataset using five-fold stratified cross-validation. During training, SMOTE was applied to balance minority classes and PCA was used for dimensionality reduction of global features, where applicable. For models employing meta-learners, a soft-voting ensemble of CatBoost, Random Forest, and Logistic Regression classifiers was used. The final model and its ablation variants were evaluated on cardiotoxicity (1- validation and 4- benchmark) and hepatotoxicity (1- validation and 5- benchmark) datasets. Performance was assessed using accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and ROC-AUC metrics. To determine the individual contribution of each component, average values of these metrics were calculated across all predicted datasets for both endpoints.

## Cross-validation
The evaluation metrics for the cardiotoxicity prediction model across both the training and test datasets are detailed in . In the training set, the model consistently achieved excellent performance across all five folds, with accuracy, F1-scores, and ROC-AUC values close to 0.975, 0.975, and 0.997, respectively. Precision and recall values also remained high, ranging from 0.969 to 0.983, demonstrating robustness in minimizing false positives and negatives. Fine-tuning further enhanced these metrics slightly, yielding a remarkable ROC-AUC of 0.998 and an F1-score of 0.976. For the test set, the model showed strong generalization, with accuracy values ranging from 0.813 to 0.833 and F1-scores from 0.816 to 0.832 across folds. The ROC-AUC values remained high, between 0.893 and 0.908, indicating remarkable discriminatory power. Precision and recall metrics ranged from 0.81 to 0.85 maintaining consistency and ensuring balanced sensitivity and specificity. Fine-tuning on the test set aligned closely with training performance, reflecting the model's stability and efficacy in predicting cardiotoxicity across diverse data splits.
The evaluation metrics for the hepatotoxicity prediction model across both the training and test datasets are detailed in . In the training set, the model consistently achieved strong performance across all five folds, with accuracy values ranging from 0.957 to 0.968, F1-scores between 0.961 and 0.971, and ROC-AUC values consistently at or above 0.995. Precision and recall metrics remained well-balanced, with precision reaching as high as 0.978 and recall up to 0.970, highlighting the model's effectiveness in accurately predicting hepatotoxicity while minimizing false positives and negatives. Fine-tuning further improved these metrics, yielding a high ROC-AUC of 0.996 and an F1-score of 0.969. For the test set, the model showed good generalization, with accuracy values ranging from 0.706 to 0.758 and F1-scores from 0.724 to 0.785 across most folds. The ROC-AUC values, ranging from 0.752 to 0.820, reflect moderate to strong discriminatory power. Precision and recall metrics were balanced in some folds (e.g. Fold 3: Precision 0.762, Recall 0.809), though variability was observed, particularly in Fold 4, where the F1-score dropped to 0.518. Despite this, the fine-tuned model achieved metrics on the test set closely aligning with those from the training set, demonstrating improved stability and robust performance in predicting hepatotoxicity across diverse data splits.

## Halogenation-toxicity trends in aromatic ring compounds predicted by the HD-GEM model

## Halogenation-toxicity trends in single ring compounds
Halogenation-toxicity trends for individual scaffolds (benzene, toluene, phenol, aniline, nitrobenzene, benzoic acid, benzaldehyde, and benzonitrile) were predicted using HD-GEM models. Neither the base scaffolds nor their halogenated counterparts exhibited cardiotoxicity ( ). Only triple chlorination in benzene and double chlorination in benzonitrile increased marginal toxicity. Halogenation did not show any significant effect on cardiotoxicity of single ring compounds ( ).
 For hepatotoxicity, single-ring nonhalogenated scaffolds showed varied toxicity. Benzene, aniline, benzoic acid, benzaldehyde, and toluene were predicted not to have any hepatotoxicity while others scaffolds were hepatotoxic ( ). Halogenation had different effects on the hepatotoxicity of the scaffolds. The degree of halogenation had varied effects on hepatotoxicity, wherein in scaffolds of phenol ( ), benzonitrile ( ), benzaldehyde ( ) and toluene ( ) showed least toxicity when di-halogenated whereas monohalogenation imparted least toxicity in benzoic acid ( ). The type of halogen substitution also demonstrated varied effects ( ) but across all scaffolds, iodination as mono-type substituent or in combination with bromine displayed the least hepatotoxicity.

## Halogenation-toxicity trends in double ring compounds
Nonhalogenated double-ring compounds (naphthalene, indole, benzofuran, benzothiophene, and quinoline) did not demonstrate any cardiotoxicity ( ). Tri- and poly-halogenation of naphthalene, polyhalogenation of benzofuran, and quinoline are the only instances of cardiotoxicity of note. Mono and di-halogenation of the double-ring compounds did not show any cardiotoxicity. For the atomic type substitutions, chlorine and bromine mono-type substitutions were the only instances of cardiotoxicity observed. Other types and combinations of halogenation did not induce cardiotoxicity ( ).
 All the nonhalogenated double ring compounds excluding benzofuran demonstrated hepatotoxicity ( ). Contrary to the cardiotoxicity trend, tri- and poly-halogenation seems to demonstrate lower toxicities among naphthalene, quinoline, and benzofuran scaffolds. Also, there is no definitive common type of substitution that had lowest toxicities consistently. Although mono-type substitutions had scaffold-specific toxicities that are lower in comparison to combination-type substitutions ( ). Benzothiophene is hepatototoxic and halogenation had no effect in mitigation of toxicity ( ).

## Halogenation-toxicity trends in triple ring compounds
Triple ring nonhalogenated scaffolds like anthracene and phenanthrene were noncardiotoxic while carbazole, acridine, and fluorene demonstrated cardiotoxicity ( ). Poly-halogenation demonstrated the least cardiotoxicity among all the scaffolds excluding carbazole, wherein tri-halogenation did not demonstrate any cardiotoxicity ( ). For the type of substitution, iodine mono-type substitution showed least cardiotoxicity in phenanthrene, carbazole, acridine, and fluorene while fluorine mono-type substitution did not show any cardiotoxicity in either anthracene or phenanthrene ( ).
All the nonhalogenated triple ring scaffolds except quinoline were hepatotoxic ( ). Halogenation did not have any significant effect in mitigating the hepatotoxicity of the triple ring scaffolds ( ). Di-halogenation only had marginally less toxicity in comparison to the base scaffolds in anthracene, phenanthrene, carbazole, and acridine while tri-halogenation did not demonstrate any hepatotoxicity in fluorene ( ). Fluorine mono-type substitution had the least toxic profile among all the scaffold groups ( ).

## Halogenation-associated toxicity in drugs predicted by the HD-GEM model
The toxicity trends pertaining to atom-type substitution among drugs were evaluated by curating datasets across three databases of DrugBank, Drug Central, and FDA. The DrugBank, Drug Central, and FDA datasets have 14 642, 4099, and 4213 compounds respectively and HD-GEM model was used to predict cardiotoxicity and hepatotoxicity of these compounds. The percentages of toxic compounds per type of atomic substitution were calculated based on the total toxic compounds present within the databases. Halogen compounds exhibit only a moderate percentage of toxicity in all three databases while nonhalogenated compounds have a significantly higher percentage of toxic compounds ( ). Similarly, sulfur-containing compounds exhibited lower toxicity percentages of toxic compounds in comparison to their nonsulfur compounds ( ). In a stark contrast, nitrogen substitution had an extremely high percentage of toxic compounds (>90%) ( ) in comparison to non-nitrogenated compounds.
 From these dataset results, the effects of degree and atom-type substitution of halogens on these drugs were evaluated ( ). For both cardiotoxicity and hepatotoxicity, nonhalogen drugs had the highest percentage of toxic compounds among total toxic drugs. Mono-halogenation was the next highest with over 20% across all databases. Interestingly, percentage of toxic compounds decreased on increasing halogenation degree with poly-halogenation showing least percentage of cardio- and hepatotoxic compounds ( ). Similarly, increasing complexity of halogen atom type substitutions decreased percentage of toxic compounds ( ). The order of least toxicity among mono-type substitutions is iodine followed by bromine, fluorine and chlorine ( ).
 

## Halogen atom contribution to toxicity of the scaffold
Atomic contributions to toxicity were quantified on a normalized 0–1 scale. The threshold for contribution to toxicity is assigned as 0 and any contribution >0 in toxic compounds were considered as prediction contributor. Contribution occurrences in both cardiotoxicity and hepatotoxicity datasets for various atom types were calculated ( ). Here, an occurrence refers to each instance where a specific atom type in a toxic compound contributes positively (i.e. >0) to the predicted toxicity, as determined by the node perturbation analysis. For cardiotoxicity, carbon atoms exhibit the highest contribution occurrences, significantly dominating the other atom types with over 12 000 occurrences. This suggests that carbon atoms play a critical role in influencing cardiotoxicity. This is expected due to the higher percentage of carbon atoms usually present in most scaffolds. Nitrogen and oxygen atoms are the next most influential, with contribution occurrences of ~2000 each. Other atom types, such as fluorine, sulfur, chlorine, hydrogen, bromine, and iodine, show minimal contributions, with occurrences close to zero ( ). For hepatotoxicity, carbon atoms also show the highest contribution occurrences, with around 1200 occurrences. Oxygen and nitrogen atoms follow as significant contributors, with occurrences of approximately 500 and 300, respectively. Chlorine and sulfur show minor contributions, while fluorine, phosphorus, bromine, and iodine have negligible contribution occurrences ( ).
 The extent of the halogen atom contributing to the toxicity in toxic compounds is calculated ( and ). In the case of cardiotoxicity, the largest proportion of toxic compounds (51.38%) are nonhalogenated, indicating that nonhalogen atoms are the predominant contributors to predicted toxicity. Meanwhile, 46.89% of toxic compounds contain halogens, but these halogens do not contribute to the model’s prediction of cardiotoxicity. Only 1.71% of toxic compounds contained halogen atoms that contributed positively to influence the cardiotoxicity outcome ( ). For hepatotoxicity, a substantial majority of toxic compounds (71.2%) are nonhalogenated, emphasizing that nonhalogen atoms dominate hepatotoxicity predictions. Around 28.2% of toxic compounds contain halogens that do not contribute to the prediction, further highlighting their limited role. Only a negligible proportion (0.4%) of toxic compounds have halogen atoms contributing positively to hepatotoxicity predictions ( ). The analysis reveals that halogens generally play a limited role in directly contributing to toxicity predictions for both cardiotoxicity and hepatotoxicity. This underscores the significance of other atom types, such as carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen, in driving the toxicity outcomes predicted by the model. The predictions discussed in the sections 3.3, 3.4, and 3.5 are predictions by the HD-GEM model and require further experimental validation.

## Discussion
The present study offers a comprehensive exploration of the toxicological implications of site-specific halogenation on pharmacological scaffolds using HD-GEM while addressing several limitations of traditional in silico toxicity prediction methods, such as low generalization, limited molecular recognition, and inconsistent prediction accuracy [ , ]. The HD-GEM model outperformed both traditional machine learning models and web-based toxicity platforms ( , ).
Traditional QSAR and fingerprint-driven approaches often struggle with extrapolation to novel chemical spaces due to their reliance on predefined molecular descriptors and lack of structural context [ ]. In contrast, GNNs can capture atomic and bond-level topological relationships [ ]. However, GNNs alone may overfit or underperform when feature representation is suboptimal or data is sparse ( , , ) [ ]. The hybrid HD-GEM architecture addresses this by integrating chemically interpretable descriptors with topologically informed GNN embeddings, while decoupling their outputs to prevent overfitting. This architectural decoupling enhances robustness ( , ). This hybrid approach yields superior performance compared to standalone models, consistent with other toxicity studies [ ]. Although tools like ProTox 3.0, admetSAR 3.0, and ADMETlab 3.0 employ modern ML methods [ , , ], they rely on rigid pipelines and static thresholds, often applying binary cutoffs without class balancing, which limits F1-score and ROC-AUC ( ). HD-GEM leverages GNNs for scaffold-specific tuning and uses a stacked meta-learner trained on SHAP-selected, PCA-reduced features. SMOTE and G-means thresholding further boost performance on imbalanced data, yielding consistently higher F1 and ROC-AUC scores ( ).
In this study, we used the HD-GEM model to systematically deconstruct the impact of halogen substitution across simple and complex aromatic systems, revealing multilayered insights that challenge generalized assumptions and offer scaffold-specific interpretations. In monoaromatic systems such as benzene, aniline, benzoic acid, phenol, and toluene, the baseline cardiotoxicity was negligible ( ), with halogenation exerting only minor influence, consistent with the notion that single-ring scaffolds lack the structural complexity required for high-affinity interactions with cardiotoxic targets such as the hERG channel [ ]. For hepatotoxicity, phenol and benzonitrile scaffolds displayed reduced toxicity upon di-halogenation ( ), which may reflect an attenuation of phase I oxidative metabolism by cytochrome P450 enzymes [ , ]. Specifically, halogens can deactivate aromatic rings toward electrophilic substitution or hydroxylation via their strong inductive and mesomeric effects, depending on position [ ]. In contrast, the increased hepatotoxicity observed upon halogenation of scaffolds such as benzene, aniline, toluene, and benzoic acid ( ) may be attributed to enhanced bioactivation via cytochrome P450-mediated oxidative pathways, where halogen substitution at reactive positions can stabilize aryl radicals or promote the formation of electrophilic intermediates such as quinone methides or acyl halides, leading to covalent binding with hepatic proteins and potentially triggering hepatocellular stress [ , ]. Iodine, in particular, showed a consistent protective trend across scaffolds ( ) suggesting that the electronic and steric properties of iodine contribute to its reduced reactivity and toxicity [ ]. Due to its large size, high polarizability, and low tendency to form electrophilic intermediates, iodine generates fewer active metabolites compared to chlorine and fluorine [ ]. Also limited number of drugs with iodination due to their atomic size can also be a factor [ ]. Additionally, iodine’s polarizability could enable intramolecular stabilization through halogen bonding, reducing the reactivity of adjacent functional groups [ ].
The double-ring scaffolds naphthalene, quinoline, benzofuran, benzothiophene, and indole exhibited heightened sensitivity to halogenation ( ) due to increased conjugation and electron delocalization across extended π-systems [ ]. These features facilitate both higher affinity for biological targets and increased susceptibility to CYP450-mediated bioactivation, particularly via epoxidation of internal double bonds or oxidative ring cleavage [ , , ]. Polyhalogenation consistently mitigated toxicity in these systems ( ). This is likely achieved by multiple halogens reducing accessibility of metabolic enzymes to key reactive sites by steric shielding, cumulative halogenation diminishing π-electron density, disfavoring epoxidation thereby disrupting binding to hepatic or cardiac targets [ , , ]. Interestingly, di-halogenated and mono-halogenated derivatives sometimes retained hepatotoxicity, suggesting that partial substitution might not sufficiently disrupt metabolic activation pathways. For example, halogenated quinoline derivatives may still undergo two-oxidation to yield electrophilic quinone imines, depending on the substitution pattern [ , , ]. The differential responses across scaffolds underscore that while halogenation can stabilize a molecule against metabolism, partial substitution may preserve reactivity at critical positions, especially in heteroaromatics with innate metabolic liabilities.
Triple-ring systems such as carbazole, acridine, phenanthrene, anthracene, and fluorene represent archetypal polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), many of which are prototypical toxicophores due to their planar, lipophilic nature, and susceptibility to multistep metabolic activation [ ]. These molecules undergo bioactivation primarily via arene oxide or diol-epoxide pathways, leading to DNA adduct formation, oxidative stress, and long-term genotoxicity [ ]. Within this context, tri-halogenation had a dual effect ( ). For example, in compounds like fluorene and anthracene, tri-halogenation mitigated hepatotoxicity ( and ), likely due to inhibition of arene oxidation. Here, halogen substitution at electron-rich positions adjacent to metabolic “hot spots” such as the 9-position can sterically hinder CYP binding and electronically disfavor the transition state for epoxide formation in PAHs [ ]. In contrast compounds like, carbazole, acridine did not follow this trend ( and ), suggesting that its nitrogen-containing heterocycle at position 9 and 10 imposes unique metabolic liabilities that are not mitigated by halogenation at adjacent positions, highlighting the importance of scaffold-specific biotransformation routes [ ]. Notably, fluorinated derivatives frequently exhibited the lowest cardiotoxicity ( ), aligning with literature reports that fluorine, due to its small size and strong C–F bond, imparts metabolic inertness while subtly modulating local dipoles and binding affinity. Fluorine can also reduce basicity or block oxidation at sensitive aromatic sites, a property exploited in designing safer drug candidates [ ]. Conversely, the higher lipophilicity and potential bioaccumulation of brominated or iodinated derivatives may offset their reduced reactivity, explaining why toxicity mitigation in these cases is dose- and scaffold-dependent [ ].
The trends observed in drug datasets provided valuable insights into the broader implications of halogenation on toxicity ( ). Nonhalogenated drugs exhibited the highest percentage of toxic compounds across all databases. Mono-halogenation showed moderate toxicity, while increasing halogenation degree reduced the percentage of toxic compounds, with poly-halogenation being the least toxic. This trend is likely due to the stabilizing effects of multiple halogen substitutions, which may reduce the formation of reactive metabolites during biotransformation [ ]. Among halogen types, iodine exhibited the least toxicity, followed by bromine, fluorine, and chlorine ( ) aligning with observations in single, double, and triple ring compounds, suggesting that the electronic and steric properties of iodine contribute to its reduced reactivity and toxicity [ ]. Also limited drugs with iodination due to their atomic size can also be a factor [ ]. The high toxicity associated with nitrogen substitution (>90%) highlights that potential of nitrogen-containing compounds to form toxic reactive intermediates or interact with critical biological pathways, emphasizing the need for careful consideration in drug design [ ].
Analysis of atom-level contributions indicates that carbon atoms are the most frequently involved in model-predicted toxicity outcomes, with over 12 000 instances in cardiotoxicity and 1200 in hepatotoxicity datasets ( ). While this may appear self-evident given carbon’s ubiquitous presence in organic molecules, its prominence in our results derived from node-perturbation which measures how much the model’s predictions change when specific atoms are altered, reflecting its functional impact on toxicity prediction, not just elemental abundance in datasets. Carbon’s contribution highlights the model’s recognition of scaffold-level features like aromaticity, hybridization state, and chain length, factors known to significantly influence toxicity. Nitrogen and oxygen atoms also showed significant contributions ( ). These elements are prevalent in both halogenated and nonhalogenated drugs and toxicophores, reinforcing their mechanistic relevance to toxicity [ ]. This broader pattern suggests the chemical framework (carbon, nitrogen, oxygen) plays a more decisive role in toxicity than specific substituent atoms alone. Halogen atoms contributed minimally, with limited influence on toxicity predictions ( ). This finding is consistent with prior acute toxicity machine learning studies [ ], as well as an in vitro study on rat toxicity [ ], which proved that toxicity was more strongly associated with the core structure of aromatic and aliphatic compounds than with individual substituent groups. These findings confirm that the model primarily leverages scaffold-level, features particularly carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen atoms while halogens play a secondary, often negligible role in determining toxicity.
Overall, the findings highlight the nuanced role of halogenation in modulating toxicity across various compound scaffolds and drugs. While degree- and type-dependent halogenation demonstrated scaffold-specific toxicity mitigation, the results also indicate a limited role of halogenation in toxicity, with a more pronounced influence of the scaffold itself, an observation previously hypothesized by our lab [ ]. Halogenated inhaled anesthetics like halothane show hepatotoxicity via Cytochrome P450 (CYP) mediated metabolite formation [ ]. However, compounds with the same base scaffold but different halogenation (e.g. soflurane, sevoflurane) showed lower toxicity [ ]. Similarly, di-halogenated nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs demonstrated hepatotoxicity through quinone metabolite formation, but halogen type and degree influenced outcomes [ ]. Regarding cardiotoxicity, halogen groups attached to a drug’s phenyl ring can induce hERG inhibition, as observed in clofilium and ibutilide [ ]. Conversely, fluorine atoms have been used to mitigate hERG affinity in phenyl-containing 1H-pyrrolo-[3,2-c]quinolines by shifting specificity toward the MAO-B enzyme instead [ ]. A machine learning study also predicted that aromatic rings have a tendency to inhibit hERG channel, which is further accentuated by halogen substitutions [ ]. The primary cause of hepatotoxicity is metabolite formation via CYP interactions, while cardiotoxicity is primarily driven by a drug’s affinity for the hERG potassium channel [ , ]. Halogens have the propensity to stabilize interactions with both these targets through halogen bonding [ , ]. Therefore, the scaffold’s overall affinity or structural compatibility with these targets plays a much greater role in toxicity than the mere presence of halogens in the scaffold.
Despite the impressive predictive performance, some limitations of this study warrant careful consideration. Although extensive efforts were made to curate data from PUBCHEM, ZINC, DrugBank, DrugCentral, and FDA, future studies could benefit from the inclusion of larger datasets, particularly for novel halogenated drug-like scaffolds despite our goal of this study in isolating the impact of halogen substitution. A significantly larger dataset with more diverse chemical structures can further leverage the potential of the GNNs. While trends in halogenation degree and type are analyzed, the model does not address other critical factors, such as the metabolic transformations or specific target interactions that could influence toxicity outcomes. A promising future direction is to integrate in silico biotransformation models to predict toxic metabolite formation and assess CYP450-mediated metabolic risks. This would refine HD-GEM’s ability to distinguish parent compound toxicity from metabolite-induced toxicity, a crucial factor in regulatory toxicology.
The study is currently focused solely on predicting cardiotoxicity and hepatotoxicity, but the model has the ability to explore other toxicological endpoints such as nephrotoxicity, carcinogenicity, and mutagenicity. Prospects of our lab include expansion of this framework to encompass these additional toxicological endpoints. Another interesting prospect for the research community can be to evaluate structure toxicity relationships for other atoms like sulfur and nitrogen found in common toxicophores using this strategy. The current study focuses on halogenated aromatic compounds. Future work should extend to exploring additional functional groups and a broader range of molecular scaffolds including aliphatic classes. Additionally, validating toxicity trends through in vitro toxicity assays is crucial to confirm the reliability of model predictions, as machine learning models inherently carry biases and cannot guarantee perfect accuracy across all cases. Halogenation enhances antimicrobial potency while reducing the risk of resistance development [ ]. Halogens are among the most prevalent functional groups in modern drug discovery and are a key feature in many blockbuster drugs [ ]. Therefore, strategically incorporating halogens into molecular scaffolds to maximize efficacy with minimal toxicity and resistance potential is a promising approach. Key strategies to consider include optimizing the position and type of halogens to enhance antimicrobial activity while minimizing toxicity, designing compounds for bacterial specificity to reduce off-target effects on human cells, adjusting dosing regimens to balance antimicrobial efficacy with safety, and integrating halogenation with other functional groups to enhance selectivity and therapeutic potential. ## Conclusion
This study demonstrates the efficacy of the HD-GEM model for predicting the toxicological impact of site-specific halogenation on pharmacological scaffolds. The hybrid integration of GNN-based representations, descriptor-based features, and ensemble meta-learning offers a versatile and high-performing approach for cardiotoxicity and hepatotoxicity prediction. The study delineates the effects of halogenation degree and type on toxicity, and offers valuable insights for drug design, particularly for developing halogenated drug derivatives with reduced toxicity. A scaffold’s chemical framework such as aromaticity, hybridization state, chain length, and heteroatom substitutions like nitrogen and oxygen atoms, play a more decisive role to toxicity in comparison to halogens, which should be considered for rational drug design.

## Key Points
The HD-GEM model demonstrates superior performance compared to traditional machine learning models and popular toxicity webservers like ProTox, ADMETlab, and admetSAR.
Node perturbation analysis reveals that scaffold carbon, nitrogen and oxygen atoms primarily drive toxicity predictions, challenging the conventional belief that halogenation significantly increases toxicity.
Polyhalogenated compounds consistently show reduced toxicity, suggesting a stabilizing effect that mitigates reactive metabolite formation.
Iodine-substituted compounds exhibit the lowest toxicity across various scaffold classes and drug databases.
