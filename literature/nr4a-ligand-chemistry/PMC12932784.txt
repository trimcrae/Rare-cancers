This study delineates the tumor-suppressive functions of ALDH2, examining its enzymatic activity, post-translational modifications, potentially transcriptional regulation, and protein–protein interactions. In an oral squamous cell carcinoma (OSCC) cohort, high ALDH2 immunostaining independently correlated with improved clinical outcomes. Functional assays across four HNSC-derived cell lines revealed that ALDH2 inhibits anchorage-independent growth, migration, invasion, and endothelial tube formation effects mediated by suppression of the HRAS–AKT–NFκB signaling axis. Mutations at E504 and phosphorylation-deficient variants at T261 and S488 impaired ALDH2 enzymatic function and abolished its tumor-suppressive capacity by reactivating oncogenic signaling. Mechanistically, ALDH2-mediated inhibition of AKT1 reduced NR4A1 phosphorylation, thereby enhancing TGM2 transcription and translation and promoting apoptosis. Notably, ALDH2 directly interacts with ALDH6A1, and this association, independent of catalytic activity, synergistically amplifies anti-tumor signaling. Collectively, these findings identify ALDH2 as a key tumor suppressor in HNSC, including OSCC, and highlight the therapeutic potential of activating ALDH2, NR4A1, and TGM2. Moreover, stabilization of the ALDH2–ALDH6A1 complex may offer a viable strategy for disease prevention and treatment, even in the context of frequent ALDH2 mutations.

## Introduction
Head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSC) ranked as the third most prevalent cancer globally, with 1,464,550 new cases and 487,993 deaths reported in the Global Cancer Statistics of 2020 [ ], underscoring its significant impact on global healthcare systems. Both environmental exposures and genetic heterogeneity contribute to the progression of HNSC. Established risk factors include tobacco use, betel nut chewing, excessive alcohol consumption, and human papillomavirus (HPV) infection [ ]. Comprehensive genomic profiling by The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) consortium revealed the lack of recurrent mutations in actionable therapeutic targets. This limitation highlights the urgent need to explore alternative molecular mechanisms and novel therapeutic strategies for managing HNSC [ ].
In HPV-positive HNSC, molecular profiling has revealed two distinct subtypes characterized by unique transcriptomic signatures [ ]. In contrast, HPV-negative HNSC is predominantly derived by somatic mutation in genes regulating the cell cycle (CDKN2A, TP53, CCND1), growth signals (EGFR), survival pathways (PIK3CA, PTEN), WNT signaling (FAT1, AJUBA, NOTCH1), and epigenetic modulation (KMT2D, NSD1) [ ]. Despite the abundance of molecular data, deeper biological interpretation remains essential. Emerging evidence highlights aberrant redox metabolic regramming as a hallmark of cancer [ ]. Through integrative data mining, validation on clinical specimens, and in vitro functional assays, we identified ALDH2 as a promising biomarker for enhancing risk stratification and guiding personalized treatment strategies in HNSC, particularly within the HPV-negative subgroup [ ]. Alda-1, a known agonist and chemical chaperone of human aldehyde dehydrogenase 2, further supports the therapeutic potential of ALDH2 activation in patients with deficient ALDH2 activity [ ].
ALDH2, predominantly localized in the mitochondria, belongs to the aldehyde dehydrogenase family and serves as the second key enzyme of the major pathway of alcohol metabolism [ ]. Beyond its role in ethanol detoxification, ALDH2 plays a critical function in metabolizing both endogenous and exogenous aldehydes, including reactive species such as 4-hydroxynonenal (4-HNE) and malondialdehyde (MDA) [ ]. Reduced ALDH2 activity has been associated with increased susceptibility to esophageal cancer [ ] and other malignancies of the upper gastrointestinal tract [ ], as demonstrated in population-based cohort studies. A prevalent point mutation, rs671 (G > A in exon 12), resulting in a loss of ALDH2 activity and is notably common in Asia populations, reaching an allele frequency of approximately 45% in Taiwan. Numerous studies have reported a synergistic interaction between the rs671 variant and alcohol consumption, markedly elevating the risk of HNSC [ ]. Restoration of ALDH2 activity through post-translational modifications, particularly serine/threonine phosphorylation mediated by epsilon protein kinase C (PRKCE), has been shown to enhance its enzymatic function [ ]. Accordingly, we aimed to investigate the correlations of ALDH2 expression levels and key clinicopathological features in patients with oral squamous cell carcinoma (OSCC). In parallel, we examined the impact of ALDH2 level on cellular functions and molecular signaling pathways across multiple HNSC-derived cell lines, including one derived from OSCC. Furthermore, we assessed the biological consequences and downstream signaling effects of several ALDH2 mutants, especially the rs671 variant and two putative phosphorylated sites, and explored the functional role of a candidate ALDH2-interacting protein, ALDH6A1.

## Materials and methods

## Study population and immunohistochemistry
The retrospective study enrolled patients diagnosed with first primary OSCC who underwent curative surgery at Kaohsiung Chang Gung Memorial Hospital, Taiwan, between January 2011 and December 2016. Formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded tumor specimens were available for all included cases. The study was approved by the Medical Ethics and Human Clinical Trial Committees of Chang Gung Memorial Hospital (#202401183B0). All patients were reclassified according to the American Joint Committee on Cancer (AJCC) staging system. Exclusion criteria include: (1) prior history of any malignancy before OSCC diagnosis, (2) presence of distant metastasis at initial diagnosis, and (3) receipt of curative treatments such as induction chemotherapy, radiotherapy, or concurrent chemoradiotherapy (CCRT) before surgery. Comprehensive clinical and pathological data were collected. ALDH2 protein level was assessed by immunohistochemistry following standard protocols. Based on the median immunoreactive score, patients were stratified into two groups: low ALDH2 expression (≤ 3.33) and high ALDH2 expression (> 3.33), as detailed in the Supplementary Information.

## Cell culture, plasmids, site-directed mutagenesis, ShRNA clones, and lentiviral preparation
We utilized four HNSC-derived cell lines: 2A3 (pharynx, HPV-positive, CRL-3212™, ATCC, Manassas, VA, USA), Detroit 562 (pharynx, HPV-negative, CCL-138™, ATCC), FaDu (hypopharyngeal, HPV-negative, HTB-43™, ATCC), and SCC-25 (tongue, CRL-1628™, Food Industry Research and Development Institute, Hsinchu, Taiwan). All cell lines were cultured under standard conditions, Notably, 2A3 and FaDu share the same parental origin, with 2A3 distinguished by HPV positivity. We obtained the pLenti-C-Myc-DDK-P2A-Puro vector (pLenti, PS100092, Origene, Rockville, MD, USA) and the pCMV6-ALDH2 plasmid (RC200505, NM_000690, Origene). The ALDH2 coding sequence (1551 bp) was subcloned into the pLenti vector using homologous recombination via the In-Fusion cloning system (#121416, Takara, Mountain View, CA, USA), following the manufacturer’s protocol.
Site-directed mutagenesis was performed using the QuickChange II Lightning Site-Directed Mutagenesis Kit (#210518, Agilent, Santa Clara, CA, USA) to generate the following ALDH2 mutants: E504K (glutamic acid to lysine at residue 504), T2641 (threonine to alanine at residue 261), S488A (serine to alanine at residue 488), and the double mutant T261A/S488A. All constructs were verified by Sanger sequencing. Three shRNA clones targeting ALDH2, HRAS, and TGM2 were obtained from the National RNAi Core Facility (Institute of Molecular Biology/Genomic Research Center, Academia Sinica, Taiwan). For stable overexpression or knockdown, replication-incompetent lentiviruses were generated by co-transfection of the target plasmid with packing plasmids psPAX2 and pMD2.G into Phoenix-AMPHO (3 × 106 cells, ATCC). Viral supernatants were harvested and used to establish stable cell lines. Additional methodological details are provided in the Supplementary Information.

## Genotyping, chemicals, quantitative RT-PCR, and immunoblot analysis
Genotyping of all cell lines was performed using a custom single-nucleotide polymorphism assay targeting ALDH2_rs671 (TopGen, Kaohsiung, Taiwan) and analyzed with the Light Cycler 96 (Roche, Basel, Switzerland). Unless otherwise specified, all chemicals were procured from Merck (Burlington, MA, USA). Quantitative RT-PCR was conducted using TaqMan chemistry with predesigned primers and probes (Topgen) to quantify mRNA expression levels of target genes. Protein expression was assessed via standard immunoblot protocols, employing appropriate dilutions of primary and secondary antibodies, as detailed in the Supplementary Information.

## In vitro functional assays and next-generation sequencing
To evaluate the biological effects of gene expression alterations, we performed a series of in vitro functional assays, including soft agar colony formation, transwell migration and invasion assays, and angiogenic capacity using human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVEC) treated with conditioned media derived from gene-overexpressed or knockdown cells. Apoptosis was assessed via flow cytometry using Annexin V-fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC)/propidium iodide (PI) staining. These assays collectively measured anchorage-independent cell growth, cell motility, angiogenic potential, and apoptotic responses following modulation of target gene expression [ ] (Supplementary Information). For functional assays, cells were synchronized in medium containing 2% fetal bovine serum (FBS) for 24 h, followed by release into standard growth medium (10% FBS) for at least 24 h before phenotypic analysis. Next-generation sequencing (NGS) was performed by Genomics BioSci (Taipei, Taiwan, Supplementary Information) on stable ALDH2-knockdown Detroit 562 cells using the shALDH2#2 clone, with the shLuc group serving as controls. Bioinformatics analyses included read alignment using Bowtie2, transcript quantification via the RSEM package, and differential gene expression analysis. Enrichment analyses were conducted to identify significantly affected Gene Ontology (GO) terms and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathways, as detailed in the Supplementary Information.

## RAS pull-down assay
Active RAS (RAS-GTP) levels were analyzed using the Ras Pull-down Activation Assay Biochem Kit (Bead Pull-Down Format, #BK008, Cytoskeleton, Denver, CO., USA). Briefly, cells were lysed in lysis buffer (0.5 mg/mL), and 800 µg of total protein was incubated with 100 µg of RAF-RBD glutathione affinity beads at 4 °C for 1 h. Beads were washed with 500 mL of wash buffer and centrifuged at 4 °C for 3 min. The pellet was resuspended in 2X Laemmli sample buffer and boiled for 2 min. Active RAS was detected by immunoblotting using a pan-RAS antibody. GTPγS-treated lysates served as a positive control, while GDP-treated lysates were used as a negative control.

## ALDH2 and CASP3/7 activity assays
ALDH2 enzymatic activity and downstream oxidative stress markers were examined using the following kits: ALDH2 Activity Assay kit (substrate: acetaldehyde, ab155894, Abcam, Cambridge, MA, USA), 4-HNE ELISA Kit (E-EL-0128, Elabscience®, Houston, TX, USA), MDA Colorimetric/Fluorometric Assay Kit (ab118970, Abcam), and Elabscience® Caspase 3/7 (CASP3/7) Activity Assay Kit (Colorimetric Method, #E-CK-A383, Elabscience). Cell lysates were used to quantify ALDH2 activity toward acetaldehyde, intracellular MDA concentration, and CASP3/7 activity. For 4-HNE quantification, the culture medium was collected after 24 h of serum starvation. Detailed protocols and reagent dilutions are provided in the Supplementary Information.

## Dual-luciferase reporter assay
Relative promoter activities were examined using the Dual-Luciferase® Reporter Assay System (E1910, Promega). To evaluate NFκB transcriptional activity, cells were transfected with the pGL4.32[luc2P/NF-κB-RE/Hygro] vector (E8491, Promega), which contains five tandem NFκB response elements driving expression of the luc2P luciferase reporter gene (Photinus pyralis). Constructs encoding ALDH2 wild-type (WT) and mutants: ALDH2(E504K), ALDH2(T261A), ALDH2(S488A), and double mutant ALDH2(T261A/S488A) were co-transfected to assess their regulatory effects. The plasmid ratio of pGL4.32[luc2P/NF-κB-RE/Hygro] (700 ng) to pRL Renilla Luciferase Control Reporter Vector (E2231, Promega, control) (50 ng) was maintained at 14:1. Luminescence signals were measured using the GloMax® 20/20 Luminometer (E5311, Promega).
To investigate TGM2 promoter activity, two proximal promoter regions (−900 to + 100 and − 600 to + 100, with + 1 representing the transcription start site) were cloned into the pGL4.17[luc2/Neo] vector, generating pGL4.17-ATGM2(−900 to +100), and pGL4.17-BTGM2(−700 to +100) constructs. These were co-transfected with pRL Renilla into ALDH2-overexpressing FaDu cells (1 × 105) and ALDH2-knockdown Detroit 562 cells (1 × 105), maintaining a consistent 14:1 plasmid ratio. Renilla luciferase was used as an internal control for normalization.

## Pull-down and co-immunoprecipitation assays
Pull-down and co-immunoprecipitation (Co-IP) assays were performed using the Pierce™ Co-Immunoprecipitation Kit (#26149, ThermoFisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). Antibodies used included anti-ALDH2, anti-HaloTag, and anti-ALDH6A1, with anti-GFP serving as a negative control. For pull-down assays, we utilized ALDH2- and ALDH6A1-overexpressing FaDu cells, which exhibit low endogenous levels of ALDH2 and ALDH6. Co-IP assays were conducted using Detroit 562 and SCC-25 cells, which express high endogenous levels of both proteins. Detailed protocols and antibody dilutions are provided in the Supplementary Information.

## Statistical analysis
All statistical analyses were performed using the Statistical Package for the Social Sciences (SPSS, v. 18.0, IBM, Armonk, NY, USA), including evaluations of immunohistochemistry and in vitro experimental data. Survival probabilities were estimated using the Kaplan-Meier method, and statistical significance between groups was assessed via the log-rank test. A two-tailed p < 0.05 was considered statistically significant. Associations between clinical predictions and survival outcomes were analyzed using univariate and multivariate Cox proportional hazards regression models. Hazard ratios (HRs) and corresponding 95% confidence intervals (CIs) were calculated. For in vitro experiments, all assays were performed in triplicate or more (≥ 3 biological replicates). Data are presented as mean standard deviation (SD). Statistical differences between two groups were evaluated using Student’s t-test. For comparisons involving three or more groups, one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s post-hoc test was applied. A p-value < 0.05 was considered indicative of a significant difference.

## Results

## ALDH2 immunostaining predicts survival and correlates to prognostic significance in OSCC patients
Data mining using GEPIA and UALCAN platforms, based on The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), revealed that the ALDH2 mRNA levels were higher in normal (n = 44) compared to the tumor tissues in HNSC patients (p < 0.05; Supplementary Fig. A). Moreover, elevated ALDH2 expression levels were associated with improved overall survival (p = 0.016; Supplementary Fig. B). To validate these findings, we further analyzed a local cohort of patients with OSCC. Supplementary Table summarizes the clinicopathological characteristics of 150 patients with OSCC who underwent curative surgery at Kaohsiung Chang Gung Memorial Hospital, Taiwan, between January 2011 and December 2016. Among these patients, 74 specimens (49.3%) exhibited high ALDH2 immunostaining (Fig.  A). At the same time, 76 showed low ALDH2 expression levels (Fig.  B). The median follow-up duration was 60.6 months (range: 3.1 ~ 107.4 months), during which tumor recurrence occurred in 42 patients. The 5-year overall survival (OS), cancer-specific survival (CSS), and recurrence-free survival (RFS) rates for the entire cohort were 75.1%, 78.2%, and 71.1%, respectively. High ALDH2 immunostaining was correlated with tumors located in the buccal region (p = 0.045) and early pathological stage (p = 0.009), as shown in Supplementary Table . 
Univariate analysis revealed that patients with low ALDH2 expression exhibited markedly poorer 5-year survival outcomes compared to those with high expression levels (OS: 58.9% vs. 91.8%, p < 0.001; CSS: 62.1% vs. 94.5%, p < 0.001; RFS: 55.2% vs. 87.2%, p < 0.001). Additional unfavorable prognostic indicators, namely, advanced pathological stage, perineural invasion, lymphovascular invasion, and extranodal extension, were each significantly correlated with diminished 5-year OS, CSS, and RFS (Fig.  C and E, Supplementary Table ). Multivariate Cox regression analysis confirmed that low ALDH2 expression independently predicted poorer survival rates, with elevated hazard ratios for OS (HR = 4.239; 95% CI: 1.758–10.224; p = 0.001), CSS (HR = 6.199; 95% CI: 2.332–16.478; p < 0.001), and RFS (HR = 4.418; 95% CI: 2.069–9.435; p < 0.001). Additionally, extranodal extension emerged as an independent adverse prognostic factor for OS (p = 0.037), CSS (p = 0.019), and RFS (p = 0.023). Lymphovascular invasion was also independently associated with inferior CSS (p = 0.036) (Table  ). 

## ALDH2 functions as a tumor suppressor in four different OSCC-derived cell lines
To investigate the impacts of ALDH2 levels on cellular physiology and expression levels of related proteins, we conducted a series of in vitro functional and immunoblot analyses. Detroit 562 and SCC-25 cells exhibited relatively high ALDH2 mRNA and ALDH2 protein levels, whereas 2A3 and FaDu cells showed low expression (Fig.  A and B). Supplementary Fig. outlines the genetic backgrounds of these cell lines. Genotyping confirmed that all lines harbored the wild-type ALDH2 allele at rs671 hotspot (Supplementary Fig. ). Stable knockdown of the ALDH2 gene using replication-incompetent lentivirus carrying two distinct shRNA clones reduced ALDH2 mRNA (p < 0.001) and protein levels in Detroit 562 and SCC-25 cells (Fig.  C). Conversely, stable overexpression of the ALDH2 via transduction of viruses packaged from pLenti-ALDH2-Myc-DDK-P2A-Puro plasmid increased ALDH2 mRNA (p < 0.001), and ALDH2 protein levels in 2A3 and Fadu cells (Fig.  D). Accordingly, these genetically modified cell lines were utilized for downstream functional assays to evaluate the biological consequences of ALDH2 modulation. 
ALDH2 knockdown in Detroit 562 and SCC-25 cells enhanced anchorage-independent cell growth (p < 0.001; Fig.  E), accompanied by upregulation of MYC and E2F1, and downregulation of CDKN1C (p57) protein levels (Fig.  F). Cell migration (p < 0.001) and invasion (p < 0.001) were also increased (Fig.  G, Supplementary Fig. ), with elevated expression of SNAI1, CD44, and VIM (Fig.  H). ELISA revealed increased VEGFA secretion in conditioned media from ALDH2-knockdown Detroit 562 (p < 0.001 for both clones) and SCC-25 cells (p < 0.05 for shALDH2#3) (Fig.  I). When applied to HUVECs, conditioned media from these knockdown cells promoted tube formation after 8 h (p < 0.001; Fig.  J). In contrast, ALDH2 overexpression in 2A3 and FaDu cells reversed these phenotypes, suppressing anchorage-independent cell growth, migration, invasion, and HUVEC tube formation (Fig.  K and P, Supplementary Fig. ). These findings support a tumor-suppressive role for ALDH2 in HNSC-derived cell lines.

## ALDH2 suppresses tumorigenesis via inhibition of the HRAS-AKT axis
Our previous data mining using TIMER2.0 revealed that HNSC patients harboring HRAS mutations (30/408 = 4.90%) exhibited significantly reduced ALDH2 levels compared to non-mutant counterparts (p = 0.0068) [ ]. Given that HRAS mutations are associated with enhanced oncogenic activity and downstream signaling [ ], we investigated the impact of ALDH2 modulation on RAS pathway components. Silencing ALDH2 using two distinct shRNA clones in Detroit 562 cells led to upregulation of HRAS, TBK1, pCHUK(S176) (IKKα), and RELA (p65/NFκB); pAKT1(S473), FOXO3, pFOXO3(S253), pCREB1(S133), pNR4A1(S351) as well as pMAP2K1(S298) (MEK1), and pMAPK1/3(T202/Y204) (ERK1/2). At the same time, total NR4A1 (NUR77) protein levels were downregulated (Fig.  A). In contrast, ALDH2-overexpressing FaDu cells displayed the opposite expression profile (Fig. B). Additionally, ALDH2 knockdown elevated active RAS-GTP levels, whereas ALDH2 overexpression suppressed RAS activation in Detroit 562 and FaDu cells, respectively (Fig.  C, Supplementary Fig. ), indicating that ALDH2 negatively regulates RAS activity. 
To further delineate the role of ALDH2 in suppressing HRAS-driven tumorigenesis, we transiently transfected a constitutively active HRAS(G12D)-HaloTag plasmid into ALDH2-overexpressing 2A3 (HPV+) and FaDu (HPV-) cells (Fig.  D). Immunoblot analysis confirmed successful expression of HRAS(G12D)-HaloTag fusion proteins (Fig.  E). In 2A3 and FaDu cells, HRAS(G12D) overexpression enhanced the colony formation/anchorage-independent cell growth (p < 0.05, p < 0.05), cell migration (p < 0.001, p < 0.001), and invasion (p < 0.001, ns) and angiogenic potential via HUVEC tube formation (p < 0.001, p < 0.01) upon treatments with conditioned media collected from HRAS(G12D)-overexpressing cells compared to control treatments. Notably, ALDH2 overexpression mitigated these HRAS(G12D)-induced phenotypes (Fig.  F and G, Supplementary Fig. ), indicating a suppressive effect on HRAS-mediated oncogenic traits. Conditioned media from HRAS(G12D)-overexpressing 2A3 and FaDu cells promoted HUVEC tube formation (p < 0.001, p < 0.01), whereas media from ALDH2/HRAS(G12D)-coexpressing cells reversed this effect (p < 0.001, p < 0.001) (Fig.  H). At protein levels, HRAS(G12D) upregulated pCREB1(S133), pNR4A1(S351), pCHUK(S176), RELA, BCL2, pMAP2K1(S298), and pMAPK1/3(T202/Y204), while suppressing ALDH2 and NR4A1. ALDH2 overexpression counteracted these changes, restoring NR4A1 levels and downregulating HRAS(G12D)-induced proteins (Fig.  I).
To validate the dependency of ALDH2’s suppressive role on HRAS, we knocked down HRAS using three shRNA clones in ALDH2-deficient Detroit 562 cells. HRAS expression was significantly reduced at both mRNA and protein levels (p < 0.01), with shHRAS#3 selected for further assays (Fig.  J). In Detroit 562 and SCC-25 cells, HRAS knockdown suppressed colony formation/anchorage-independent cell growth (p < 0.001, p < 0.001), migration (p < 0.001, p < 0.001), invasion (p < 0.001, p < 0.001), and HUVEC tube formation (p < 0.001, p < 0.001), effects that were reversed upon concurrent ALDH2 knockdown (Fig.  K N, Supplementary Fig. ). Protein analysis confirmed that HRAS silencing downregulated pCHUK(S176), pAKT1(S473), pMAPK1/3(T202/Y204), while elevating ALDH2 and NR4A1 (Fig.  O). Interestingly, HRAS and ALDH2 appear to engage in a reciprocal regulatory loop, wherein constitutively active HRAS downregulates ALDH2 expression. Conversely, ALDH2 attenuates HRAS signaling activity, suggesting a negative feedback mechanism that may fine-tune the dynamics of oncogenic signaling. These findings demonstrate that ALDH2 suppresses tumorigenesis in vitro by attenuating the HRAS-AKT axis. Notably, NR4A1 expression positively correlated with ALDH2 and inversely with HRAS, suggesting its potential role as a downstream effector of ALDH2-mediated tumor suppression.

## ALDH2 mutations promote RAS-AKT-NFκB signaling and abolish tumor suppressive functions in vitro
Beyond its role in alcohol metabolism, ALDH2 detoxifies lipid peroxidation-derived aldehydes, including 4-HNE and MDA. Multiple sequence alignment identified four conserved phosphorylated sites: T214, T261, S263, and S488 (Supplementary Table ). T261 and S488 were selected for functional assays due to their spatial separation and low likelihood of coordinated regulation. To assess the impacts of ALDH2 mutations on enzymatic activity and tumor suppressive functions, we generated stable cell lines overexpressing ALDH2(WT), the clinical mutant ALDH2(E504K), phosphorylation-incapable mutants ALDH2(T261A) and ALDH2(S488A), and the double mutant ALDH2(T261A/S488A) in FaDu and ALDH2-knockdown Detroit 562 cells (Fig.  A). ALDH2(WT) overexpression increased acetaldehyde-metabolizing activity (p < 0.001), reduced 4-HNE (p < 0.001) and MDA levels (p < 0.001) (Fig.  B and D). In contrast, all mutants, ALDH2 (E504K), ALDH2(T261A), ALDH2(S488A), and ALDH2(T261A/S488A), reduced ALDH2(WT)-enhanced enzymatic activity (p < 0.001; Fig.  B) and reversed suppressed of 4-HNE (p < 0.01; Fig.  C). Notably, ALDH2(E504K) and ALDH2(T261A/S488A) induced MDA accumulation in both cell lines (p < 0.001, p < 0.05, Fig.  D), and single T261 or S488 mutations had milder effects. These results suggest that while all mutations impair acetaldehyde and 4-HNE metabolism, the loss of phosphorylation at T261/S488 does not fully compromise MDA detoxification. 
Functional assays revealed that mutant ALDH2 constructs reinstated ALDH2(WT)-suppressed colony formation/anchorage-independent cell growth (p < 0.001), migration (p < 0.001), invasion (p < 0.05), and HUVEC tube formation (p < 0.01) in both cell lines (Fig.  E and G, Supplementary Fig. ). Immunoblotting showed that mutants partially or totally restored expression of RAS-AKT-MAPK pathway proteins downregulated by ALDH2(WT), including HRAS, TBK1, pAKT1(S473), pCREB1(S133), pNR4A1(S351), pCHUK(S176), RELA, BCL2, pMAP2K1(S298), and pMAPK1/3(T202/Y204). Interestingly, ALDH2(WT) upregulated NR4A1, while all mutants suppressed its expression (Fig.  H). RAS-GTP pull-down assays confirmed that mutant ALDH2 constructs reactivated RAS signaling, especially in ALDH2(E504K) and ALDH2(T261A/S488A) cells (Fig.  I, Supplementary Fig. ). Dual-luciferase reporter assays showed that NFκB-RE activity, suppressed by ALDH2(WT), was significantly restored by all mutants, with the highest activity observed in ALDH2(E504K) and ALDH2(T261A/S488A) cells (p < 0.001; Fig.  J). These findings demonstrate that ALDH2 mutations, particularly E504K and T261A/S488A, impair aldehyde detoxification and promote RAS-AKT-NFκB signaling, thereby abolishing the tumor-suppressive functions of ALDH2 in vitro.

## ALDH2 induces apoptosis via transactivation of the TGM2 gene
Stable knockdown of ALDH2 in Detroit 562 cells downregulated proapoptotic proteins BBC3 (PUMA) and PMAIP1 (NOXA), while upregulating antiapoptotic protein BCL2. Conversely, ALDH2 overexpression in FaDu cells reversed these effects (Fig.  A), suggesting a proapoptotic role for ALDH2. To identify downstream targets of ALDH2, we performed RNA NGS in ALDH2-knockdown Detroit 562 cells. Among 303 significantly altered transcripts (posterior probability of error estimate, PPEE), KEGG pathway analysis yielded no significant enrichment. However, 59 GO biological processes were overrepresented. Within GO:0043277 (apoptotic cell clearance, p.adjust = 0.0181), transglutaminase 2 (TGM2) was significantly downregulated (log2 PostFC = −1.56, PPEE = 0.0238), also appearing in GO:0045785 (positive regulation of cell adhesion), implicating TGM2 as a potential ALDH2-regulated gene (Supplementary Tables , ). 
Quantitative RT-PCR confirmed that ALDH2 overexpression upregulated TGM2 mRNA in 2A3 (p < 0.001) and FaDu (p < 0.001) cells. At the same time, ALDH2-knockdown suppressed TGM2 expression in Detroit 562 (p < 0.001) and SCC-25 (p < 0.001) cells (Fig.  B). Immunoblotting validated corresponding changes in TGM2 protein levels (Fig.  C). TGM2 overexpression for 72 h enhanced apoptosis in FaDu cells, as shown in Fig.  D. To assess transcriptional regulation, we cloned two promoter regions of the TGM2: −900 to + 100 (pGL4.17-ATGM2) and − 600 to + 100 (pGL4.17-BTGM2) (the transcription start site defined as + 1). Promoter activity was higher for pGL4.17-A in Detroit 562 cells with high endogenous ALDH2. In FaDu cells, ALDH2 overexpression increased both pGL4.17-A (p < 0.001) and pGL4.17-B (p < 0.001) activities, while ALDH2 knockdown reduced them (Fig.  E), indicating that ALDH2 positively regulates TGM2 transcription. Further analysis of cell lysates from Fig.  D K revealed elevated TGM2 expression in ALDH2-overexpressing cells (lane 2). However, TGM2 levels were reduced in ALDH2/HRAS(G12D) dual-overexpressing 2A3 and FaDu cells (lane 4 vs. 2; Fig.  F), indicating a suppressive effect of HRAS(G12D) on ALDH2-induced TGM2 upregulation. Additionally, TGM2 levels were increased in HRAS-knockdown (lane 3), but reduced in ALDH2/HRAS double-knockdown cells relative to ALDH2-knockdown alone in Detroit 562 and SCC-25 cells (lane 4 vs. 3; Fig.  G). These findings suggest that ALDH2 positively regulates TGM2 protein expression, whereas HRAS(G12D) exerts an adverse regulatory effect, effectively abolishing ALDH2-induced TGM2 upregulation.
Mutant ALDH2 constructs, E504K, T261A, S488A, and T261A/S488A, failed to upregulate TGM2 mRNA (p < 0.001) and TGM2 protein levels compared to the ALDH2(WT) group in both FaDu and ALDH2-knockdown Detroit 562 cells (Fig. H and I), suggesting that acetaldehyde-metabolizing activity and phosphorylation at T261/S488 are required for TGM2 transactivation. Phylogenetic footprinting via JASPAR [ ] identified conserved NR4A1-responsive elements (NR4A1-REs) across human TGM2 and mouse Tgm2 promoters (Supplementary Fig. ), implying that ALDH2 might indirectly regulate TGM2 via NR4A1.
To clarify the functional relationships, we transiently transfected pTGM2-HaloTag into Detroit 562 and FaDu cells (Fig.  J, Supplementary ). Immunoblotting confirmed robust expression of the ~ 110 kDa fusion protein (Fig.  K, Supplementary S12A). TGM2 expression reduced colony formation/anchorage-independent cell growth (p < 0.01), cell migration (p < 0.001), and invasion (p < 0.01), and HUVEC tube formation (p < 0.001) (Fig.  L N, Supplementary Fig. B- F). It also increased apoptosis (p < 0.001), upregulated BBC3 and PMAIP1, and enhanced CASP3/7 activity while suppressing BLC2 in Detroit 562 cells (Fig.  P, Supplementary Fig. G, H). In ALDH2-knockdown Detroit 562 and FaDu cells, TGM2 overexpression reversed tumorigenic phenotypes and restored apoptosis markers (Fig.  J and P, Supplementary - ).
Conversely, TGM2 knockdown in SCC-25 cells using three shRNA clones resulted in reduced TGM2 protein and mRNA levels (p < 0.01) (Fig.  Q). The shTGM2#2 clone was then used for further assays. TGM2 knockdown increased colony formation/anchorage-independent cell growth (p < 0.001), cell migration (p < 0.001), invasion (p < 0.001), and HUVEC tube formation (p < 0.001) (Fig.  R and U, Supplementary Fig. I), reducing apoptosis (p < 0.01) (Fig.  V) and downregulating BBC3, PMAIP1, CASP3/7 activity (but non-significant), with BCL2 upregulation (Fig.  W). Significantly, ALDH2 overexpression in TGM2-knockdown SCC-25 cells partially rescued the tumor-suppressive phenotype, reducing cell growth (p < 0.001), migration (p < 0.001), invasion (p < 0.001), and angiogenesis (p < 0.001), while enhancing apoptosis (p < 0.001) and downregulating BBC3, PMAIP1, and CASP3/7 activity, with BCL2 upregulation (Fig.  R and U, Supplementary Fig. C). Moreover, in FaDu cells, co-overexpression of ALDH2 and TGM2 modestly enhanced suppression of tumorigenic traits and amplified apoptosis (p < 0.01 to 0.05), with significant upregulation of BBC3, PMAIP1, and CASP3/7 activity (Supplementary Fig. C- H). These findings demonstrated that ALDH2 exerts tumor-suppressive effects in vitro, at least in part through transcriptional activation of TGM2.

## ALDH2-ALDH6A1 interaction potentiates tumor suppression in vitro
Building on our previous findings that suggested a potential interaction between ALDH2 and ALDH6A1 [ ], we further investigated this relationship. Immunoblot analysis revealed elevated ALDH6A1 expression in Detroit 562 and SCC-25, with lower expression in 2A3 and FaDu cells, mirroring the expression pattern of ALDH2 (Fig.  A). To assess functional collaboration, we transfected pALDH6A1-HaloTag plasmids into ALDH2-overexpressing 2A3 and FaDu cells, resulting in robust expression of the ALDH6A1-HaloTag fusion protein (~ 91 kDa, Fig. B). Functional assays were conducted to evaluate the individual and combined effects of ALDH2 and ALDH6A1 (Fig.  C). Compared to control cells (Lenti/pHaloTag), ALDH6A1 overexpression alone (lane 3) inhibited colony formation/anchorage-independent cell growth (p < 0.001, p < 0.01), cell migration (p < 0.01, p < 0.01), invasion (p < 0.001, p < 0.001), and HUVEC tube formation following treatments with conditioned media from ALDH6A1-overexpressing cells (p < 0.01, p < 0.01) (Fig. D F, Supplementary Fig. ). Notably, co-overexpression of ALDH2 and ALDH6A1 further enhanced these inhibitory effects compared to either protein alone, indicating a synergistic tumor suppressive interaction. Consistent results were observed in Detroit 562 and SCC-25 cells, where ALDH6A1 overexpression (lane 2) reduced colony formation/anchorage-independent growth (p < 0.001, p < 0.001), cell migration (p < 0.001, p < 0.001) and invasion (p < 0.001, p < 0.001), and HUVEC tube formation following treatment with conditioned media from cells (p < 0.001, p < 0.001). Furthermore, ALDH6A1 overexpression in ALDH2-knockdown cells mitigated the pro-tumorigenic effects induced by ALDH2 depletion, restoring suppression of colony formation, migration, invasion, and angiogenesis potential (all p < 0.001) (Fig. G J, Supplementary Fig. ). These results were consistent across both Detroit 562 and SCC-25 cell lines, suggesting that ALDH6A1 overexpression can compensate for ALDH2 loss in vitro. 
To elucidate the molecular basis of this interaction, we performed in-silico docking analyses using PRISM [ ] and ClusPro [ ], both of which predicted a stable ALDH2-ALDH6A1 complex (Supplementary Tables , , Fig. ). ClusPro identified a high-probability interaction cluster (cluster 0) comprising 55 members, with a center and lowest energy of −1012.0 and − 1449.3, respectively, indicative of a favorable docking conformation. PyMOL visualization confirmed the predicted interaction (Fig.  K, 6 L). Experimental validation via pull-down assays in ALDH2/ALDH6A1-overexpressing FaDu cells using anti-ALDH2 and anti-HaloTag antibodies (anti-GFP as control) confirmed physical interaction between the two proteins (Fig. M). Coimmunoprecipitation in Detroit 562 and SCC-25 cells with high endogenous ALDH2 and ALDH6A1 substantiated this interaction (Fig.  N). Collectively, these results demonstrate that ALDH2 physically associates with ALDH6A1 and that their co-expression synergistically enhances tumor suppression in vitro.
To evaluate potential interactions between the previously described ALDH2 mutants (E504K, T261A, S488A, and T261A/S488A) and ALDH6A1, we analyzed the predicted interfacial residues mediating ALDH2–ALDH6A1 binding. Specifically, residues in ALDH2 (Ala19, Lys144, Asp299, Gln461, Leu463, Gln464, Val468, Asp471, Tyr485, Arg492, and Tyr497) and ALDH6A1 (Thr159, Tyr172, Lys510, Thr511, Thr513, Asp520, Ala527, Met533, Gly534, and Arg535) were identified as key contributors to the interaction interface (Fig.  L). Notably, the mutant residues in ALDH2 do not overlap with these predicted contact sites. We subsequently performed transient transfection of pALDH6A1-HaloTag plasmids into FaDu cells stably overexpressing ALDH2(WT), ALDH2(E504K), ALDH2(T261A), ALDH2(S488A), or the ALDH2(T261A/S488A) double mutant, as well as into Detroit 562 cells with ALDH2 knockdown (Fig.  O and P). Reciprocal pull-down assays confirmed that each of these ALDH2 variants retains the ability to interact with ALDH6A1 (Fig.  Q). These results suggested that the ALDH2-ALDH6A1 interaction is independent of ALDH2 catalytic activity or phosphorylation status; the tumor suppressive effect may be mediated by complex formation, not just enzymatic activity. ## Discussion
The present work underscores the potential clinical relevance of ALDH2 expression as a prognostic biomarker in OSCC. Patients with low ALDH2 levels exhibited significantly poorer survival outcomes, including OS, CSS, and RFS, even after controlling for established pathological risk factors. This supports the integration of ALDH2 immunostaining into routine histopathological evaluation to enhance risk stratification in surgically managed OSCC. Clinically, ALDH2 expression may help identify patients who, despite being classified as low-risk based on conventional pathology, harbor a heightened risk of recurrence. Such individuals may benefit from intensified surveillance, adjuvant therapy, or consideration of more aggressive treatment modalities. Conversely, patients with high ALDH2 expression, associated with a favorable prognosis, could potentially avoid overtreatment and its associated morbidity. Beyond its prognostic implications, ALDH2 also emerges as a promising therapeutic target due to its involvement in cellular metabolism and tumorigenesis, warranting further mechanistic and translational exploration. These findings emphasize the protective role of ALDH2, aligning with our previous observations on its downregulation in HNSC [ ] and corroborated by a recent systematic review [ ]. Functionally, ALDH2 exerts tumor suppressive effects through its enzymatic activity, particularly in metabolizing acetaldehyde and 4-HNE, a lipid peroxidation product. Our in vitro data, derived from four genetically distinct HNSC cell lines, strongly support the conclusion that loss of ALDH2 enzymatic activity is a key driver of tumorigenesis. This is further substantiated by a pan-cancer analysis of over 10,000 samples across 33 cancer types, which revealed widespread downregulation of ALDH2 [ ].
In HNSC-derived cell lines with low endogenous ALDH2 expression, introduction of ALDH2 mutants, including the rs671 (E504K) variant and two putative phosphorylation-deficient mutants (T261A and S488A, especially the T261A/S488A double mutant), resulted in marked reductions in both ALDH2 activity and tumor-suppressive function. These residues are conserved across 10 ALDH isoforms, suggesting a broader regulatory significance. Importantly, mutations at T261 and S488, analogous in effect to E504K, impaired ALDH2 phosphorylation and its ability to detoxify 4-HNE, while leaving its activity toward another lipid peroxidation product, MDA, unaffected. This differential effect suggests that MDA accumulation is unlikely to be a primary contributor to tumorigenesis. Phosphorylation emerges as a critical post-translational modification that regulates ALDH2 activity and protects it from aldehyde-induced inactivation. While previous studies have identified three key phosphorylation sites, T185, S279, and T412, targeted by PRKCE, our findings introduce two novel sites, T261 and S488, as essential for enzymatic function. The specific kinases responsible for these modifications remain to be elucidated.
We show that ALDH2 indirectly transactivates TGM2, which encodes a multifunctional enzyme implicated in diverse cellular processes and capable of inducing apoptosis in various HNSC-derived cell types. The TGM2 protein facilitates the post-translational modifications of multiple substrate proteins through a wide variety of enzymatic functions, including transaminase, deamidase, crosslinking, isopeptidase, GTPase, adhesion/scaffold, disulfide isomerase, and kinase activities [ ]. Among these, its disulfide isomerase function has been linked to the mitochondrial-dependent apoptotic pathway [ ]. Expression levels of TGM2 vary significantly across cancer types compared to normal tissues, highlighting its context-dependent molecular roles [ ]. Supporting our findings, Milakovic et al. demonstrated that cytosolic TGM2 promotes apoptosis, whereas nuclear-localized TGM2 exerts a protective effect against apoptosis [ ]. ALDH2 activation influences a broad spectrum of gene expression and cellular pathways, reinforcing its roles in maintaining cellular homeostasis, including the regulation of apoptosis [ ]. Although the TGM2 promoter also contains responsive elements to inflammation and hypoxic stimuli, such as NFκB-RE [ ], our data revealed that (1) ALDH2 expression positively modulated TGM2 promoter activity, mRNA abundance, and protein levels; and (2) wild-type ALDH2 suppresses, while mutant ALDH2 enhances NFκB-RE reporter activity. These observations suggest that NFκB is unlikely to mediate TGM2 transactivation in this context, thereby supporting the hypothesis that ALDH2 regulates TGM2 expression and apoptosis.
In detail, we demonstrate that ALDH2 suppresses AKT activation, thereby limiting AKT-mediated phosphorylation of NR4A1. Interestingly, total NR4A1 protein levels are elevated, suggesting stabilization in its unphosphorylated form. The elevation of NR4A1 is accompanied by upregulated TGM2 mRNA expression, identification of putative NR4A1-REs within the TGM2 promoter, and ALDH2-mediated enhancement of TGM2 promoter activity, collectively supporting a model in which NR4A1 directly transactivates TGM2. The NR4A family of orphan nuclear receptors, comprising NR4A1 (Nur77), NR4A2 (Nurr1), and NR4A3 (NOR1), functions as ligand-independent transcription factors that are responsive to diverse cellular stressors. NR4A1 expression and activity govern multiple molecular mechanisms, including sequence-specific DNA binding to NBRE and NurRE motifs [ ], chromatin remodeling via co-regulator recruitment [ ], and mitochondrial translocation [ ]. Indeed, AKT1 phosphorylates NR4A1 at serine 350 within its DNA-binding domain, suppressing NR4A1’s transcriptional activity and mitochondrial pro-apoptotic function in a PI3K-dependent manner [ ]. Mechanistic investigations have shown that NR4A1 translocates to mitochondria, where it interacts with BCL2, converting it from an anti-apoptotic to a pro-apoptotic effector. This interaction facilitates the release of cytochrome c and initiates intrinsic apoptotic signaling [ – ]. NR4A1 regulates a diverse array of target genes, with functional outcomes that are highly context-dependent, shaped by cellular stress levels, epigenetic accessibility, and the dynamics of cofactors [ ]. This regulatory relationship is reinforced by our data, which show a positive correlation between ALDH2 and NR4A1 expression, as well as a negative correlation with pNR4A1(S350). Significantly, elevated NR4A1 levels were associated with enhanced apoptosis, aligning with prior studies that link high NR4A1 expression to improved cancer-specific survival rate in patients with aggressive B-cell lymphomas, where NR4A1-mediated apoptosis pathways may counteract lymphomagenesis [ ]. Additionally, NR4A receptors have been shown to upregulate pro-apoptotic genes such as FASL, TRAIL, NDRG1, NDRG2, and BIM [ – ], further strengthening the proposed mechanism. These findings position NR4A1 as a downstream effector of ALDH2-mediated tumor suppression, linking metabolic regulation to transcriptional control of pro-apoptotic or anti-metastatic pathways.
Our data further revealed that NFκB-RE reporter activity peaked in cells overexpressing ALDH2(E504K) and ALDH2(T261A/S488A), but was lowest in cells overexpressing ALDH2(WT). This pattern suggests that ALDH2 enzymatic activity contributes to the suppression of RAS and its downstream AKT-NFκB signaling cascade. Mechanistically, activated RAS engages PI3K, which catalyzes the conversion of phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP2) into phosphatidylinositol 3,4,5-trisphosphate (PIP3). PIP3 serves as a membrane docking site for AKT, facilitating its phosphorylation and activation [ ]. Activated AKT subsequently phosphorylates IκB kinases, a protein complex comprising CHUK, IKBKB (IKKβ), and IKBKG (NEMO), which in turn phosphorylates NFKBIA (IκBα), an inhibitor of NFκB dimers (NFKB1/RELA). This phosphorylation targets NFKBIA for ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation, liberating NFκB dimers to translocate into the nucleus and initiate transcription [ ]. Consistent with this pathway, our findings showed that ALDH2(WT) downregulated, but ALDH2 mutants and knockdown upregulated, the protein levels of HRAS, RAS-GTP, pAKT1(S473), pCHUK(S176), and RELA, reinforcing the notion that ALDH2 suppresses RAS-AKT-NFκB signaling. NFκB is widely recognized for regulating a diverse set of genes that govern immunity, inflammation, cell proliferation, survival, and apoptosis [ ], including interleukins, BCL2 [ ], VEGFA [ ], FASL [ ], and TGM2 [ ]. The discrepancy indirectly reinforces the role of NR4A1 as a transcriptional modulator of TGM2 expression in the present system. Therapeutic activation of ALDH2 could attenuate oncogenic RAS signaling by restoring apoptosis pathways through NR4A1 and TGM2, thereby reducing proliferation and survival signaling.
To date, there is no direct evidence that ALDH2 regulates RAS signaling. However, our study demonstrated that ALDH2 suppressed tumorigenesis in vitro by disrupting RAS-AKT-NFκB signaling. A plausible mechanism is ALDH2-mediated reduction of oxidative stress. Although acetaldehyde and 4-HNE are not reactive oxygen species (ROS), their accumulation can induce oxidative stress and mitochondrial damage via adduct formation with proteins, DNA, and lipids [ ]. We observed that ALDH2 inactivated HRAS, AKT-NFκB, and MAPK proteins across multiple HNSC-derived cell lines, supporting the role of oxidative stress in activating RAS signaling [ ]. Mutations in the RAS family (HRAS, NRAS, and KRAS) are frequent oncogenic drivers [ ], with HRAS-activating mutations occurring in approximately 4–9% of HNSC cases [ ]. We further demonstrated that ALDH2 not only reduced total RAS protein levels but also inhibited RAS-GTP, as evidenced by the RAS Binding Domain (RBD) of RAF1, which specifically binds to the GTP-bound form of RAS [ ]. In the RAS GTPase cycle, guanine nucleotide exchange factors convert inactive RAS-GDP to active RAS-GTP, enabling downstream PI3K-AKT and MAPK signaling [ ]. Immunoblot analysis revealed that several AKT and MAPK downstream effectors were inversely correlated to ALDH2 levels and positively associated with constitutively active HRAS(G12D), further validating our observation. HRAS(G12D), a recurrent activating mutation in the RAS family, results in persistent HRAS activation and is associated with various cancers [ ]. These results establish ALDH2 as a negative regulator of oncogenic RAS signaling, likely through modulation of oxidative stress, and highlight its potential role in attenuating HRAS-driven tumorigenesis by suppressing the RAS–AKT–MAPK/NF-κB axis.
Previous studies [ ], data mining [ ], and molecular docking in our investigation suggested a functional interaction between ALDH2 and ALDH6A1. We confirmed this interaction through pull-down and Co-IP assays, which revealed synergistic tumor-suppressive effects in vitro. ALDH6A1 (malonate and methylmalonate semialdehyde dehydrogenase) encodes a mitochondrial enzyme involved in valine and thymine catabolism [ ]. ALDH2 and ALDH6A1 share several features: (1) both belong to the ALDH family; (2) both detoxify aldehydes; (3) both localize to mitochondria; (4) mutations or dysregulation of either gene are linked to metabolic disorders and cancer [ ]. Their combined activity enhances protection against aldehyde-induced oxidative stress. Given that ALDH2 functions as a dimer/tetramer [ ], we propose that ALDH2/ALDH6A1 heterodimer formation may exert similar protective effects. ALDH6A1 also participates in propionyl-CoA metabolism, and its downregulation has been linked to metabolic reprogramming and the progression of hepatocellular carcinoma [ ]. Elevated ALDH6A1 protein levels have been shown to suppress colon cancer by modulating the RAS/MAPK pathway [ ]. In line with these findings, overexpression of the ALDH6A1 gene in two HNSC-derived cell lines demonstrated its tumor-suppressive roles, including inhibition of colony formation/anchorage-independent cell growth, migration, and invasion, as well as HUVEC tube formation following treatment with conditioned media.
Notably, ALDH2 and ALDH6A1 form a stable protein–protein interaction that enhances tumor-suppressive signaling, independent of ALDH2’s catalytic activity or phosphorylation status. This interaction is preserved across wild-type ALDH2 and all tested mutants, including the catalytically impaired E504K variant and phosphorylation-site mutants T261A, S488A, and the T261A/S488A double mutant, suggesting that the ALDH2-ALDH6A1 interface is structurally robust and functionally conserved. These findings imply that the tumor-suppressive role of ALDH2 may be mediated, at least in part, through its association with ALDH6A1, rather than solely through aldehyde detoxification. The preserved interaction across mutants further supports a scaffolding or regulatory function for ALDH2 in modulating ALDH6A1-dependent pathways, with potential implications for therapeutic targeting in populations harboring ALDH2 polymorphisms. These findings open avenues for genotype-informed therapies that bypass the need for aldehyde detoxification and instead target protein-protein interaction, suggesting small molecules or biologics that stabilize or mimic the ALDH2-ALDH6A1 complex may enhance tumor-suppressive signaling, even in the absence of full enzymatic function.
In summary, our study identifies high ALDH2 immunostaining as an independent prognostic marker for improved survival in OSCC. The enzymatic activity of ALDH2, particularly its detoxification of acetaldehyde and 4-HNE, plays a pivotal role in suppressing tumorigenesis in HNSC-derived cells. Mutations at E504 and phosphorylation-deficient variants at T261 and S488 restore tumorigenic potential by activating HRAS–AKT–NFκB signaling. Mechanistically, ALDH2 suppresses AKT1 activity, elevates unphosphorylated NR4A1, and transactivates TGM2 to promote apoptosis. Moreover, ALDH2 interacts with ALDH6A1 to amplify their tumor-suppressive effects, independent of ALDH2’s catalytic function. These findings support the therapeutic potential of ALDH2 activation, via pharmacological agents, post-translational modulation, or lifestyle interventions, as a promising strategy for combating HNSC.
