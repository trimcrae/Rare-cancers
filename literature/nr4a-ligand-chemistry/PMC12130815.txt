## Significance
Despite initially high sensitivity and response rates to chemotherapy, small-cell lung cancer (SCLC) quickly develops resistance, leaving patients with limited treatment options. We identified Retinoid X receptor γ (RXRγ) as a key driver in the progression of SCLC from chemosensitivity to chemoresistance. Importantly, RXRγ antagonists effectively halt SCLC progression, restore SCLC susceptibility to chemotherapy, and prolong the survival of model mice.

Small-cell lung cancer (SCLC) is the most lethal type of lung cancer, characterized by rapid evolution from chemosensitivity to chemoresistance and limited treatment options. However, the mechanisms underlying this evolution remain poorly understood. Here, we show that Retinoid X receptor γ (RXRγ) is uniquely overexpressed in chemo-resistant SCLC tumors, and that RXRγ serves as an essential factor driving chemoresistance in SCLC. RXRγ forms phase-separated droplets with LSD1 in the nucleus, which enhances RXRγ-mediated gene transcription activity and reprograms gene expression, promoting tumor stemness and metastasis, and eventually driving SCLC chemoresistance. In turn, RXRγ antagonist disrupts RXRγ–LSD1 interaction, reducing their binding to the target gene locus, markedly suppressing the expression of the RXRγ target gene network. Finally, RXRγ antagonists strongly suppress tumor growth and metastasis and restore SCLC vulnerability to chemotherapy in multiple preclinical SCLC models, resulting in a substantial extension of survival in mouse models. Thus, these results establish RXRγ as a key player in SCLC by phase separation and as a potential therapeutic target for this deadly disease.

Tumor cells evade treatments and experience relapse through phenotypic switching driven by intrinsic or induced cell plasticity ( , ). Cancer stem cells (CSCs) are a highly plastic state that allows cells to rapidly adapt to changing microenvironments ( , ). A growing body of evidence suggests that CSCs, often characterized by their self-renewal and aberrant differentiation capabilities, exhibit inherent resistance to standard cancer therapies ( , ). The persistence of CSCs after treatment can fuel tumor regrowth and promote disease aggressiveness ( ). However, the underlying mechanisms of the acquisition and maintenance of CSC characteristics during the transition of cancer cells from chemosensitivity to chemoresistance remain poorly understood.
Small-cell lung cancer (SCLC) accounts for approximately 15% of all lung cancers and is a recalcitrant neuroendocrine carcinoma ( ). SCLC is notorious for its limited treatment options, earlier metastasis, rapid development of chemoresistance and dismal survival ( ). Platinum-based chemotherapy has remained the cornerstone of the first-line treatment for SCLC since the 1970s. Despite initially high sensitivity and response rates to chemotherapy, SCLC rapidly develops resistance, frequently leading to recurrence and a dismal prognosis, leaving patients with limited treatment options. Immunotherapy has resulted in only modest clinical benefits to date, while no targeted treatment was approved for SCLC. As a result, the 5-y survival rate of SCLC patients is less than 5% due to the absence of effective strategies to overcome chemoresistance ( , ). Recent improved molecular understanding has brought new promising opportunities for SCLC treatment, several inhibitors targeting MYC ( ), DLL3 ( ), EZH2 ( ), and LSD1 ( ) are currently being evaluated in clinical trial. However, little is known regarding the specific factors that drive the transition from chemosensitivity to chemoresistance in SCLC. Identifying the druggable drivers and developing pharmacological strategies to overcome chemoresistance are imperative.
The retinoid X receptors (RXRs) RXRα, β, and γ (with gene name RXRA, RXRB, and RXRG, respectively) present a subgroup of the nuclear receptor (NR) superfamily with conserved ligand binding domain and endogenous ligands. RXRα and RXRβ are widely expressed in a variety of tissues and controls numerous biological processes, including developmental, metabolic, and biochemical functions. They have emerged as valuable therapeutic targets for treating diseases such as neurological disorders, diabetes, and cancers ( , ). Accumulating evidence has established that RXRα and RXRβ act as tumor suppressors in various cancers ( , ). Bexarotene, a selective RXRs agonist, has been approved by the FDA for the treatment of cutaneous T cell lymphoma ( ). Intriguingly, RXRγ shows unique tissue expression patterns compared to RXRα/β and is likely to have distinct physiological functions ( ). RXRγ is highly expressed in muscle and brain tissues. Functional analyses revealed that RXRγ is essential for oligodendrocyte differentiation and myelin repair and might be a pharmacological target for regenerative therapy in the CNS ( , ). Like other RXRs, RXRγ binds to specific DNA response elements as either heterodimers or homodimer, transactivates its target genes and orchestrates pleiotropic physiological and pathological roles. RXRγ-deficient mice are relatively healthy and fertile ( ), which, together with its druggable characteristic, suggests that RXRγ may be an ideal therapeutic target. Recent studies from two groups suggested that RXRγ is required for cell proliferation in retinoblastoma and activates a neural crest stem cell (NCSC) transcriptional program in melanoma ( , ). However, the expression and function of RXRγ in human tumor cells remain largely unexplored.
In this study, we found that RXRγ is highly overexpressed in tumors of patients with SCLC, especially in chemo-resistant tumors of these patients. Using gain and loss of function studies, we identified RXRγ as an essential driver of tumor stemness and chemoresistance in SCLC. RXRγ antagonist inhibits the expression of cancer stemness genes and prometastatic genes, leading to cell death and overcoming chemoresistance both in vitro and in vivo. Thus, our findings establish RXRγ as a previously unexpected key player in advanced SCLC, underscoring its potential as a therapeutic target for this condition.

## Results

## RXRγ Is Associated with SCLC Chemotherapy Resistance and Its Overexpression Drives SCLC Tumor Chemoresistance.
Given that NRs are attractive therapeutic targets for various diseases including cancers, with lots of clinical-grade antagonists or agonists currently available, we reasoned that identification of NRs that play a crucial role in SCLC progression could lead to the development of therapeutics for SCLC. We thus queried gene-expression datasets from human normal lung tissue, naive SCLC, and chemo-resistant SCLC samples. Among the 48 NRs, RXRγ showed the highest upregulation in resistant tumors compared to naïve tumors and normal lung tissues. In contrast, the expression of RXRα and RXRβ exhibited minimal change in resistant tumors when compared to naïve tumors ( ) ( ). We next classified all single-cell transcriptome sequencing cells into 12 clusters according to their canonical markers and characterized the expression of RXRγ on the single cell level ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S1A). Results revealed that RXRγ exhibits unique cellular expression patterns compared to RXRα/β. Specifically, RXRγ was mainly expressed in epithelial cells, while RXRα/β was found in various cell types, including T cells, epithelial cells, macrophage, and fibroblasts (SI Appendix, Fig. S1B). Notably, the expression of RXRγ was significantly higher in epithelial cells from chemo-resistant tumors than in epithelial cells from naïve tumors ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S1C). Further clustering within the epithelial compartment identified that RXRγ was highly expressed in subtype cells of SCLC-N and SCLC-A (SI Appendix, Fig. S1 D and E). Analysis of publicly available bulk RNA-seq dataset (GSE1037) revealed that the expression of RXRγ was significantly higher in SCLC tumors than in normal lung tissues (SI Appendix, Fig. S1F). To verify the correlation between RXRγ expression and the transition of cancer cells from chemosensitivity to chemoresistance, a chemo-resistant trajectory was constructed based on the URD map for SCLC cells ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S1G). The trajectory started from the naïve tumor cells to chemo-resistant cells. Along the trajectory, the expression of RXRγ was progressively increased. Immunohistochemical analysis of human normal lung tissue, primary tumors, and resistant tumors revealed that RXRγ is overexpressed in more than 50% of the tumors, and that high levels of RXRγ protein are associated with tumor resistance ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S1H). RXRγ protein was detected readily by immunoblotting in patient’s SCLC tumors and cells, but not in the human normal lung tissues and bronchial epithelial cells (BEAS-2B) (SI Appendix, Fig. S1I). Moreover, analysis of published tumor datasets showed that high RXRγ mRNA is strongly associated with poor survival of SCLC patients ( ) ( ).
 Next, we examined whether elevated RXRγ expression alone is sufficient to stimulate the growth and chemoresistance of SCLC cells. Indeed, RXRγ overexpression significantly enhanced the survival, migration, and invasion of SCLC cells ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S1 J–N). Notably, the ectopic expression of RXRγ in SCLC cells significantly blocked the inhibitory effects of first-line chemotherapy drugs (Eto/DDP) on cell survival ( ). To assess whether RXRγ ectopic expression is sufficient to drive chemoresistance and metastasis in vivo, we further evaluated the effects of RXRγ on SCLC progression by established H146-derived xenograft models. We found that Eto/DDP treatment significantly inhibited tumor growth in the control group, whereas RXRγ overexpression in H146 cells led to chemoresistance, potently blocked the tumor-inhibitory effects of Eto/DDP ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S1O). Additionally, RXRγ overexpression strongly promoted the growth and metastasis of H146 xenograft tumors in mice (SI Appendix, Fig. S1 P–S). Together, these data demonstrate that RXRγ is overexpressed in chemo-resistant SCLC tumors and that its overexpression contributes to SCLC tumor chemoresistance and metastasis.

## RXRγ Inhibition Overcomes Chemoresistance in SCLC.
The findings described above prompted us to evaluate the effects of RXRγ inhibition on SCLC progression. Knockdown of RXRγ by either different siRNAs or shRNA markedly inhibited the growth of H69 cells and its chemoresistance-derivative H69AR cells ( ). Such strong growth inhibition was also observed in other SCLC-A and SCLC-N subtype cell models, including H128, H446, H1048, and H82 cells, while knockout of RXRα and RXRβ has no effect on cell growth (SI Appendix, Fig. S2 A–C). Similarly, HX-531, a potent antagonist of the retinoid X receptor (RXR), significantly inhibited the growth of wild-type cells ( ). Notably, HX-531 also exhibited similar inhibitory effects on the growth of RXRα/β knockout SCLC cells, and this inhibition was reversed by RXRγ knockdown ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S2D). These results suggest that HX-531 exerts its function in SCLC by inhibiting RXRγ activity. Moreover, both pharmacological and knockdown of RXRγ resulted in a poor survival of SCLC cells as measured by colony formation ( ) and caused pronounced apoptosis as demonstrated by the activation of caspase-3 and caspase-7 and the cleavage of poly (ADP-ribose) polymerase 1 (PARP1) (SI Appendix, Fig. S2 E–I). Given that 3D organoids may closely mimic clinical tumors in response to therapeutics, we treated organoids derived from both chemotherapy sensitive- and resistant-SCLC PDX tumors with HX531. We found that all organoids were highly sensitive to HX531 treatment ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S2 J and K). Consistently, knockdown of RXRγ significantly inhibited the growth of resistant patient-derived organoids as well (SI Appendix, Fig. S2 L and M).
 Next, we assessed the effects of RXRγ inhibition on SCLC tumor progression in vivo. Consistent with the in vitro results, RXRγ knockdown effectively blocked tumor formation and growth in H446-derived xenograft models ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S2N). Moreover, the administration of the antagonist HX531 significantly inhibited tumor growth in xenografts derived from both wild-type and RXRα/β knockout H128 cells by suppressing the activity of RXRγ ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S2 O–R). To examine whether RXRγ antagonists have effects on chemo-resistant SCLC tumors, we then generated xenografts by tail vein injection of chemo-resistant H69AR cells and treated mice with RXRγ antagonist. Bioluminescence imaging showed that HX531 significantly inhibited H69AR tumor growth ( ). Furthermore, meticulous assessment of tumor size across multiple organ sites and histological analysis disclosed a significant reduction of lung, brain, liver, and bone metastases in the HX531 treatment group (SI Appendix, Fig. S2 S–U). In line with the effects of RXRγ antagonist on metastasis, our results also revealed that both pharmacological and knockdown of RXRγ significantly inhibited migration and invasion of SCLC cells in vitro (SI Appendix, Fig. S2 V–Y). Taken together, these data suggest that RXRγ inhibition can be a strategy for effective treatment of advanced SCLC.

## RXRγ Orchestrates Cancer Stemness and Prometastatic Gene Programs via Increasing Chromatin Accessibility.
To define the molecular mechanism by which RXRγ drives SCLC progression, we performed transcriptional profiling of H128 cells treated with the antagonist HX531 and RXRγ siRNA individually. Gene ontology (GO) analysis of genes commonly downregulated by siRNA and HX531 revealed that these genes are enriched for factors involved in cell–cell junction, stem cell development, cell migration, cell adhesion, WNT signaling, and neuronal function ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S3A). Further examination by gene-set enrichment analysis (GSEA) revealed that genes involved in cell junction, stemness, and neuronal function were significantly altered by RXRγ inhibition ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S3B). In line with the effect of RXRγ inhibition on gene expression profiling, RXRγ overexpression significantly increased the expression of genes associated with cell–cell junction, stemness, and neuronal function (SI Appendix, Fig. S3 C and D). The genes commonly downregulated upon RXRγ inhibition and upregulated upon RXRγ overexpression included CSC markers (for example SOX2, SOX9, and HOXB3), neuronal functional genes (for example, NFIB, BOC, and SEMA6A), EMT markers (for example, Snail and Slug), adhesion molecules (for example, CADM3 and AMIGO2), and migration molecules (for example FOXA1, and CDH5) ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S3E). To validate the findings from RNA-seq, we performed qRT-PCR on SCLC cells treated with HX531 or RXRG siRNA. The results exhibited a significant decrease in expression of most pathway genes, including NFIB, SOX2, SOX9, and Snail ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S3F). Conversely, ectopic expression of RXRγ markedly elevated the expression of these genes ( ). Further confirmation through immunoblotting revealed consistent effects of both RXRγ inhibition or overexpression on the protein levels of key genes ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S3 G and H). Consistently, the expression of RXRγ mRNA in human SCLC tumors showed a positive correlation with the expression of stemness and metastasis genes such as NFIB, SOX2, SOX9, and Snail (SI Appendix, Fig. S3 I and J). It is well established that the processes of stemness, cell–cell junction, EMT, adhesion, and migration play crucial roles in chemoresistance and metastasis. Emerging studies have shown that introducing characteristics of a neuronal-like program into SCLC states enhances metastatic potential. Therefore, our gene expression profiling, conducted following gain and loss of function experiments, indicates that RXRγ regulates various tumor functions to promote SCLC tumor chemoresistance and metastasis.
 To understand the underlying mechanism of RXRγ controlling gene expression, we then examined the effects of RXRγ inhibition on chromatin accessibility through ATAC-seq assays, as well as local chromatin modifications using ChIP-seq assay. The results revealed that treatment with an RXRγ antagonist significantly reduced chromatin accessibility, along with a genome-wide decrease in histone 3 lysine 27 acetylation (H3K27ac, a well-established transcriptional activation-linked histone mark), compared to vehicle treatment ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S3K). This reduction in chromatin accessibility and H3K27ac occupancy are linked to the cancer stemness and prometastatic gene programs (SI Appendix, Fig. S3L), and was notably observed at the promoters and/or enhances of RXRγ key downstream genes, including NFIB, SOX2, SOX9, and Snail ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S3M), which is consistent with the effect of RXRγ inhibition on gene expression. Our ChIP assay further verified that RXRγ antagonist treatment significantly decreased the occupancy of H3K27ac at the promoter of RXRγ major downstream genes (SI Appendix, Fig. S3N). Consistently with the reduction of RXRγ downstream gene transcripts, Pol II occupancy at their promoters was significantly inhibited by RXRγ inhibition (SI Appendix, Fig. S3O). Together, these data indicate that RXRγ aberrantly activates cancer stemness and prometastatic gene programs by modulating H3K27 acetylation and chromatin accessibility in SCLC cells.
We next examined whether RXRγ, as a transcriptional factor, directly controls these gene transcription. ChIP-qPCR analysis demonstrated that RXRγ bound to the putative RXRE sites at promoters and/or enhances of genes including NFIB, SOX2, SOX9, and Snail in SCLC cells. When cells were treated with HX531, RXRγ binding was reduced, as compared to treatment with vehicle (SI Appendix, Fig. S3P). We then performed reporter-gene assays with promoters of NFIB and SOX2 and found that they were highly responsive to RXRγ-mediated transactivation, while RXRγ antagonist effectively attenuated RXRγ-dependent activation (SI Appendix, Fig. S3Q). Together, these findings suggest that in SCLC cells, RXRγ directly controls the expression of genes associated with stemness and metastasis via remodeling chromatin accessibility.

## RXRγ Drives Stemness-Associated Resistance to Chemotherapy in SCLC.
CSCs are a highly plastic state that allows cells to rapidly adapt to changing microenvironments, including developing resistance to chemotherapy. The significant association between RXRγ expression and stemness gene programs prompt us to assess the impact of RXRγ on the acquisition and maintenance of CSCs in SCLC. As shown in , RXRγ overexpression significantly increased the number and size of spheroids (SI Appendix, Fig. S4A), while both pharmacological inhibition and knockdown of RXRγ conversely decreased the number and size of spheroids in SCLC cells ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S4B). Moreover, RXRγ ectopic expression in SCLC cells significantly blocked the inhibition of first-line chemotherapy drugs (Eto/DDP) on sphere formation ( ). Additionally, Bexarotene, a selective retinoid X receptors (RXR) agonist, also significantly promoted cell growth and spheroid formation in wide type and RXRα/β knockout SCLC cells ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S4 C and D), and drove the SCLC tumor growth and increased the tumor weight in vivo ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S4 E and F). In line with the effect on cell self-renewal ability, RXRγ overexpression and Bexarotene increased the expression of stemness-associated genes ( ), including Nanog, ALDH1A1, and SOX2, while RXRγ inhibition led to a significant downregulation of stemness-associated proteins ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S4 G and H). To explore the effect that RXRγ maintains the phenotypes of CSCs in vivo, we further evaluated the effects of RXRγ on SCLC progression by establishing the tumor-initiating cell (TIC) models in immune-deficient nude mice. Using the models, we found that RXRγ overexpression in H146 cells increased the tumor-initiating cell frequency by nearly threefold compared with that in control cells (tumor-initiating cell frequency from ~1 of 1/7,575 to ~1 of 2,791 cells) ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S4I). In addition, RXRγ overexpression drove the tumor growth and increased the tumor weight in vivo ( ). As expected, the expression of stemness marker genes were also significantly increased by RXRγ overexpression ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S4 J and K).
 To determine whether the above results were the same in tumors of patients with SCLC, we first analyzed stemness activity in tumors of SCLC patients. Same as the expression of RXRγ, the stemness score, derived from the gene expression signatures of SCLC chemo-resistant tumors compared to naïve tumors, was progressively increased along with chemo-resistant trajectory (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 L–O). The signature of stemness activity in chemo-resistant tumors showed a strong positive correlation with RXRγ expression, but not in naïve tumors ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S4P). To validate correlation between RXRγ and stemness during the transition of SCLC tumors from naïve tumors to chemo-resistant, we recapitulated SCLC chemoresistance in vivo. We transplanted SCLC human patient-derived xenografts (PDX) into NOD-SCID mice subcutaneously and then treated these tumor-bearing mice with multiple cycles of chemotherapy (E/P) until the development of chemoresistance (SI Appendix, Fig. S4Q). To this end, three chemosensitive human patient-derived xenografts were used to successfully establish chemoresistant SCLC xenograft models (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 R and S). Upon drug exposure, the established chemoresistant models displayed much shorter survival than their parental chemosensitive counterparts (SI Appendix, Fig. S4T). In line with the results of scRNA-seq analysis, the expression of RXRγ and stemness related genes at both the mRNA and protein levels was significantly higher in chemo-resistant tumors than in their parental chemosensitive counterparts (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 U and V). Multiplex immunofluorescence (mIF) staining further showed that the high expression and colocalization of RXRγ and stemness related genes SOX2 and ALDH1A1 in chemo-resistant tumors (SI Appendix, Fig. S4W). These data collectively suggest that RXRγ is a crucial driver of tumor stemness during evolution from chemosensitivity to chemoresistance in SCLC.

## RXRγ Physically Interacting with LSD1 Promotes SCLC Cell Stemness and Chemoresistance.
Since RXRs can form homodimers or heterodimers with other nuclear receptors (NRs) and activate gene transcription through association with cofactors such as epigenetic regulators, we were interested in determining how RXRγ controls transcriptional profiling to promote SCLC cell stemness and chemoresistance. To identify the proteins that directly interact with RXRγ, we performed immunoprecipitation and mass spectrometry (IP-MS) analysis (SI Appendix, Fig. S5A and Dataset S1) with RXRγ antibody in H128 cells and SCLC PDX chemo-resistant tumors. Intriguingly, our results did not reveal any of the NRs involved in heterodimer formation with RXRγ in two SCLC models. Of the 113 transcription factors/regulators (commonly) identified within the list, only LSD1, a lysine specific demethylase, was reported to be associated with chemoresistance and stemness in SCLC (SI Appendix, Fig. S5A and Dataset S1) ( , ). In line with the effect of LSD1 inhibition reported previously ( , , ), we found that RXRγ knockdown similarly regulated the expression of genes involved in the antigen presentation machinery, nonneuroendocrine gene programs (such as proteasome), and stemness (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 B–D). This transcriptional overlap suggests that RXRγ and LSD1 may collaboratively regulate cellular functions. IF analysis reveals a prominent colocalization of RXRγ and LSD1 in the nucleus of SCLC cells ( ). Proximity ligation assays (PLA) further demonstrated that there is a strong interaction between RXRγ and LSD1 in the nucleus ( ). Reciprocal Co-IP experiments showed that endogenous RXRγ was indeed associated with LSD1 in SCLC cells ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S5E), and the association was significantly increased in chemo-resistant PDX tumors compared to that in its parental tumors ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S5F). Notably, we found that the interaction between RXRγ and LSD1 was strongly suppressed by RXRγ antagonists ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S5G). On the other hand, we did not detect any significant association between RXRα/β and LSD1 (SI Appendix, Fig. S5H). GST pull-down experiments with purified proteins demonstrated that RXRγ directly interacts with LSD1 ( ). To map the interaction domains, we transfected HEK 293 T cells with plasmids encoding full-length RXRγ and LSD1 or their truncated mutants. Co-IP analysis showed that the C-terminal region (LBD domain, 233-463 aa) of RXRγ interacted with the Tower domain (417-522 aa) of LSD1 ( ).
 We next analyzed the protein structural features of the interaction and found that the Tower domain of LSD1 contains an LXXLL motif, which is well-established motif in cofactor proteins that interact with NR. Our structure prediction using Alphafold ( , ) also strongly suggests that the C-terminal region (LBD domain) of RXRγ interacted with the LXXLL motif in Tower domain of LSD1. The superimposition suggests that residues 465-469, 465aa, 468aa, and 469aa from LSD1 and RXRγ form extensive hydrophobic interactions ( ). Indeed, the mutations of 465L, 468L, and 469L in LSD1 almost completely abrogated the interaction between RXRγ and LSD1 ( ).
We next evaluate the function of the RXRγ–LSD1 complex at the promoters of RXRγ target genes, our ChIP–re-ChIP assays using anti-RXRγ followed by anti-LSD1 antibodies also demonstrated that RXRγ and LSD1 simultaneously co-occupied common targets ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S5I). Employing SOX2 and NFIB promoter driven reporter-gene assay, our data also indicated that simultaneous expression of RXRγ and LSD1 resulted in a synergistic transactivation of target genes ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S5J). RXRγ antagonist significantly decreased the occupancy of LSD1 on the promoters of target genes and reduced their expression ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S5J). LSD1 specifically removes mono- and dimethyl groups on the H3K4 and H3K9, resulting in repressive or activating transcriptional histone marks. Notably, LSD1 forms a complex with NRs such as AR, ERα, ERRα, and removes repressive histone marks by demethylation of H3K9, thereby promoting the expression of their target genes ( ). To define the underlying mechanism of RXRγ–LSD1 complex controlling gene expression, we first performed ChIP-seq and ChIP-qPCR analysis of H3K4me2 and H3K9me2 in H128 cells treated with either the vehicle or HX531 ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S5K). As expected, our ChIP-seq data showed that HX531 treatment led to a substantial increase in the occupancy of H3K9me2 at the promoters of key downstream genes regulated by RXRγ ( ). Unexpectedly, we found that HX531 treatment reduced the occupancy of H3K4me2 at the promoters of target genes ( ). This reduction is not linked to the demethylase activity of LSD1; instead, it reflects an indirect effect of the inhibition. These findings are in line with recent reports indicating that inhibition of LSD1 may mediate gene expression independent of H3K4me2 status at sites bound by LSD1 ( , ). Thus, inhibition of RXRγ results in the downregulation of target gene expression, which is associated with an increase in H3K9me2 occupancy at the promoters of these target genes due to LSD1 inhibition. Collectively, these data indicate a crucial role of the RXRγ–LSD1 complex in the regulation of stemness and cancer progression.

## Interaction with LSD1 Promotes RXRγ Phase Separation and Alters the Transcriptome in SCLC Cells.
Liquid–liquid phase separation (LLPS) has emerged as a fundamental mechanism involved in transcriptional control ( ). Multiple transcriptional factors have been shown to activate genes via the phase separation capacity of their activation domain. We found that RXRγ contains two putative intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs) with high strength: IDR1 (average strength = 0.81, amino acids 1 to 132) and IDR2 (average strength = 0.79, amino acids 182 to 264) ( ), which are crucial for the generation of biomolecular condensates. We constructed EGFP-fusion IDR proteins and an IDR-depleted RXRγ fusion protein to measure droplet formation (SI Appendix, Fig. S6 A and B). As expected, we found that only the full length of RXRγ was capable of forming green aggregates in vivo, whereas the depletion of the IDRs domain resulted in a diffused formation of RXRγ ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S6C). Moreover, puncta, in live cells that expressed GFP-RXRγ fusion protein, was bleached with a 488 nm laser, the bleached puncta quickly reassembled ( ). The kinetic recovery of EGFP-RXRγ fluorescence indicated that the majority of RXRγ foci fluorescence recovered within 10 s ( ). Notably, fusion proteins of GFP-RXRγ-IDR1 and GFP-RXRγ-IDR2 formed green droplets in an in vitro setting with 20% PEG8000, but not the IDRs-depleted EGFP-RXRγ (RXRγ ΔIDRs) fusion proteins ( ). The turbidity assay conducted on the fusion proteins further validated this observation ( ). Moreover, the aggregation of green droplets formed by RXRγ-IDR1 and RXRγ-IDR2 was disrupted with an increase in NaCl concentration ( ). This was accompanied by a corresponding decrease in the turbidity of the IDR droplets ( ). Additionally, treatment with 1, 6-hexanediol significantly reduced the formation of EGFP-RXRγ puncta in live cells ( ). Taken together, these data suggest that RXRγ proteins possess the property of phase separation formation.
 We next examined whether LSD1 is pivotal to RXRγ LLPS. As shown in and SI Appendix, Fig. S6D, knockdown of LSD1 significantly inhibited the reassembly of EGFP-RXRγ fusion proteins in the nucleus of SCLC cells. Consistently, we purified LSD1 protein significantly increased the formation of EGFP-RXRγ-IDRs droplets, remarkably enhanced LLPS formation at lower concentrations, and resulted in an obvious delay in the dissolution of EGFP-RXRγ-IDRs LLPS when exposed to increasing concentrations of NaCl (SI Appendix, Fig. S6E). Notably, we observed more and larger nuclear puncta of LSD1-RXRγ in chemo-resistant SCLC tumors than in their parental chemo-sensitive counterparts ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S6F). These data indicate that the formation ability of RXRγ droplets is correlated with LSD1 in SCLC cells.
To validate the role of the LLPS of RXRγ in SCLC cellular stemness and chemoresistance, we ectopically expressed full-length RXRγ (FL) or RXRγ ΔIDRs in SCLC cells and found that only the FL RXRγ promotes cell proliferation and sphere formation, and drives chemoresistance ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S6 G–I). We then performed reporter-gene assays with promoter of SOX2, results showed that FL RXRγ but not RXRγ ΔIDRs activated SOX2 promoter, and LSD1 further increased FL RXRγ mediated transcriptional activity (SI Appendix, Fig. S6J). Consistently, only FL RXRγ but not RXRγ ΔIDRs increased the expression of stemness-associated genes SOX2, SOX9, and NFIB ( ). RXRγ is characterized as a multidomain protein, and the two IDRs are predominantly disordered and independent to the folding of the core α-helical structure of RXRγ as shown in the structural model of RXRγ predicted by Alphafold3 (SI Appendix, Fig. S6K) ( ). Thus, the observed reduction in SCLC stemness of RXRγ-IDR1 and RXRγ-IDR2 is likely due to the functional role of the IDRs rather than protein misfolding. Taken together, these results strongly indicate that the LLPS of RXRγ plays a crucial role in mediating SCLC chemoresistance and stemness.

## RXRγ Inhibition Sensitizes SCLC to Chemotherapy and Prolongs the Survival of Model Mice.
Chemotherapy (etoposide plus cisplatin or carboplatin (EP/CP)) remains the standard first-line treatment for SCLC3. However, its effectiveness is limited by drug resistance. Given the crucial role of RXRγ driving SCLC chemoresistance and stemness, we then examined whether RXRγ antagonists could improve the response of SCLC cells to chemotherapy. Indeed, cotreatment of chemo-resistant cells or PDX-derived organoids with RXRγ antagonist HX531 and EP resulted in a synergistic inhibitory effect on cell survival and sphere formation ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S7 A–C). We next evaluated the therapeutic efficacy of combining RXRγ antagonist with EP in multiple SCLC xenograft models. First, in two PDX models (EP sensitive PDX-LN140 and EP resistant PDX-p944520), administration of the antagonist HX531 alone significantly inhibited two PDX tumor growth, whereas EP alone only inhibited LN140 tumor growth but not the tumor of PDX-p944520 ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S7 D–G). Coadministration of the two drugs resulted in the persistent suppression of two PDX tumor growth, which indicates that the RXRγ antagonist can sensitize SCLC tumors to EP treatment. In line with the effects of combined treatment on tumor growth, immunostaining revealed that the combined treatment strongly induced tumor cell apoptosis and reduced their proliferation, which was demonstrated by increased levels of cleaved-Caspase3 and decreased levels of Ki-67 respectively (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 H–J). Importantly, neither the individual nor the combined treatments had any significant impact on the mice body weight or organs, and their behaviors (such as food and water consumption, grooming) during the treatment course, indicating that the RXRγ antagonist or the combination treatment approach is well tolerated by the treated mice (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 E and G).
 To further establish the therapeutic potential of the combinatorial effects on SCLC tumor metastasis, we administered H69AR cells through intravenous tail injection and then treated the mice with HX531 and Chemotherapy daily for 4 wk. Results showed that the combination treatment caused a dramatic inhibition of H69AR-derived tumor metastasis ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S7K). Moreover, examination of multiorgan-site metastasis and histological analysis revealed that the combined treatment potently suppressed metastasis on the brain, liver, kidney, lung, and bone (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 L–R). Notably, combination treatment significantly improved the overall survival of tumor-bearing mice (about twofold, if compared with the control mice) ( ). Since LSD1 inhibitors have entered clinical trials for the treatment of cancers, including SCLC ( ), we examined whether HX531 could enhance the tumor-suppressive effects of LSD1 inhibitors. Our results showed that the combined treatment of HX531 and LSD1 inhibitor ORY-1001 led to a synergistic tumor growth inhibition in H526 cell-derived SCLC xenograft tumors (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 S and T). To determine whether the effects of the combined treatment on SCLC tumor growth and metastasis was relevant to RXRγ mediated-stemness and LLPS, the protein levels of stemness markers and LLPS in tumors were analyzed. Interestingly, although treatment by the RXRγ antagonist alone significantly reduced the expression of SOX2, SOX9, NFIB, and SNAIL in the tumors, the combination treatment resulted in a stronger reduction of the four proteins than either of the single treatments (SI Appendix, Fig. S7U). Furthermore, either RXRγ antagonist alone or combined treatment significantly reduced puncta of LSD1-RXRγ in tumors (SI Appendix, Fig. S7V). Together, the results strongly suggest that RXRγ antagonists alone or in combination with chemotherapy can be a strategy for the effective treatment of advanced SCLC.

## Discussion
Chemotherapy remains the most efficacious first-line treatment for SCLC, but resistance emerges rapidly, leaving patients with limited treatment options. Our study demonstrates not only that RXRγ is a key driver of chemoresistance, but also that it provides a unique opportunity to explore an effective therapeutic intervention for advanced SCLC. We found that RXRγ is overexpressed in the chemo-resistant tumors of SCLC patients. Genetic gain and loss of functional study revealed that RXRγ promotes tumor stemness, enhancing chemoresistance and metastasis. Mechanistically, RXRγ directly stimulates the expression of cancer stemness genes and prometastatic genes through phase separation with LSD1, RXRγ antagonist potently disrupt the binding of RXRγ and LSD1 complex to the target gene locus and suppress their expression in vitro and in vivo. We also demonstrated in multiple SCLC preclinical models that RXRγ antagonist potently overcomes chemoresistance and suppresses metastasis. Notably, RXRγ antagonist promotes sustained SCLC response to chemotherapy and significantly improves the overall survival of SCLC tumor-bearing mice. Thus, our data identify RXRγ as an attractive therapeutic target in SCLC.
RXRγ, a NR containing a ligand-binding domain, represents a readily druggable target, and several antagonists have recently become available. Our data demonstrate that chemo-resistant SCLC undergoes transcriptomic reprogramming favoring tumor stemness and metastasis through RXRγ upregulation. RXRγ antagonist and knockdown effectively inhibited the expression of cancer stemness genes (for example SOX2 and SOX9) and prometastatic genes (for example NFIB and Snail), thereby mitigating or even eliminating the capability of chemoresistance, self-renewal, and metastasis in vitro and in vivo, while RXRγ overexpression exhibited the opposite effects. RXRγ antagonist potently inhibited the growth and metastasis of different SCLC xenograft tumors in mice, including cell derived-xenograft models and Patient-derived xenograft models. Notably, tumors that are resistant to chemotherapy (EP) are sensitive to RXRγ antagonist. Coadministration of antagonist and EP drugs caused a dramatic inhibition of tumor growth and metastasis, and significantly extended the overall survival of tumor-bearing mice (about twofold, if compared with the control mice). These data strongly suggest that RXRγ targeting can have broad clinical utility in SCLC. However, RXRγ has been demonstrated to act as a tumor suppressor in different tumor types. Increased expression of RXRγ has been linked to enhanced apoptosis in ovarian cancer ( ). In lung cancer, epigenetic silencing of RXRγ has been correlated with decreased overall survival in non–small cell lung cancer ( ). Therefore, further investigation is needed to uncover the exact function of RXRγ in specific cellular and tissue contexts.
RXRγ exhibits unique functions in SCLC that differ from RXRα and RXRβ. We found that the expression of RXRγ was significantly higher in chemo-resistant tumors than in normal tissue or sensitive tumors, but not the expression of RXRα and RXRβ. Consistently, silencing of either RXRα and/or RXRβ did not inhibit SCLC cell survival, self-renewal, and tumor growth, while RXRγ plays crucial roles as described above. RXRs activates gene transcription through association with cofactors in terms of their molecular functions ( , ). Our data show that RXRγ directly interacts with LSD1 and forms phase-separated droplets in the nucleus, promoting RXRγ-mediated gene transcription activity and thereby enhancing cancer stemness and chemoresistance. Additionally, we found that the N-terminal activation domain of RXRγ is crucial for the phase transition between RXRγ and LSD1. The use of RXRγ antagonists, LSD1 knockdown, and mutant LSD1 (disrupting RXRγ–LSD1 interactions) effectively disrupts the nuclear puncta of RXRγ and causes its diffusion, which in turn attenuates the expression of RXRγ-mediated genes and related biological functions. Moreover, the downregulation of RXRγ-mediated genes (such as SOX2, SOX9, NFIB, and Snail) following treatment with either an RXRγ antagonist or LSD1 knockdown does not appear to be mediated by changes in H3K4me2. Instead, we observed that their expression is associated with an increase in H3K9me2 and a decrease in H3K27ac at the gene loci upon treatment with the RXRγ antagonist and LSD1 knockdown. A lack of change in H3K4 methylation in response to LSD1 inhibition was also found in a recent study of SCLC ( ), accumulating evidence has demonstrated that LSD1 can also contribute to transcriptional activation by demethylating H3K9 ( ). For instance, LSD1 interacts with the NR AR and ER to lead to the transcriptional activation of their target genes. Beyond histone substrates, LSD1 also regulates the methylation dynamics of a number of cancer-relevant nonhistone proteins, including tumor suppressor p53 ( ), transcription factor E2F1 ( ), and DNA methyltransferase 1 (DNMT1) ( ), thereby modulating their function. Moreover, LSD1 might have additional biology functions independent of its demethylase activity ( ). Our data demonstrate that both LSD1 knockdown and RXRγ inhibition significantly reduce the survival and spheroid formation of LSD1 inhibitor-resistant SCLC cells, such as H1048 and H446 cells ( and SI Appendix, Figs. S2B and S8 A–D) ( ), indicating that LSD1 may also affect the function of RXRγ through its nonenzymatic activity in SCLC cells. It will be critical for future work to uncover the exact underlying mechanism by which RXRγ with LSD1 cooperate to regulate gene expression and promote SCLC progression. The functions of LSD1 in cancer cells are likely to be highly context dependent.
In summary, our study identified the key role of NR RXRγ and the mechanism by which RXRγ promotes the resistance to chemotherapy in SCLC. NRs are a privileged family of intracellular druggable targets, the druggable nature of NRs makes them good targets, many NR-targeting drugs are used routinely in clinics for the treatment of various diseases, such as antiandrogen for prostate cancer ( ). The development of selective RXRγ inhibitors for anti-SCLC therapy seems to be a very promising strategy, as RXRγ is highly expressed in a wide range of SCLC tumors, especially in the chemo-resistant tumors for which there is an unmet clinical need. Moreover, the RXRγ knockout mice were viable, fertile ( ), and RXRγ inhibitors were well tolerated by the treated mice, without overt toxicity, demonstrating that targeting RXRγ may represent a promising treatment option for SCLC.
