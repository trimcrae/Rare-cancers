## ABSTRACT
Skeletal muscle fibrosis is caused by excessive production or reduced degradation of extracellular matrix, leading to excessive accumulation of collagen. Obesity can trigger physiological and pathological changes in skeletal muscle, such as fibrosis, muscle atrophy, and impaired muscle regeneration. Nerve growth factor‐induced gene B (Nur77), a transcription factor encoded by orphan nuclear receptor 4A1 (NR4A1), a member of the immediate early gene nuclear receptor subfamily 4A group, participates in a variety of life activities. Here, we found that in the obese mouse model fed with a high‐fat diet, mice with Nur77 gene knockout would exacerbate the skeletal muscle fibrosis phenotype caused by obesity. It is worth noting that Nur77 can bind to the central downstream molecules Smad3, which regulates the synthesis of fibrotic proteins COL1A1 and COL3A1. The activity of the Smad3 protein is crucial for obesity‐related muscle fibrosis, suggesting that Nur77 may affect skeletal muscle fibrosis by regulating the activity of Smad3.
Keywords: Nur77, obesity, skeletal muscle fibrosis, smad3

Skeletal muscle fibrosis stems from abnormal extracellular matrix accumulation due to excessive production or reduced degradation of collagen. Obesity induces this fibrosis, along with muscle atrophy and impaired regeneration. Nur77, a transcription factor encoded by NR4A1, is involved in diverse life activities. In high‐fat diet–induced obese mice, Nur77 knockout exacerbates muscle fibrosis. Notably, Nur77 binds to Smad3, a key regulator of fibrotic proteins COL1A1 and COL3A1, implying Nur77 modulates obesity‐related muscle fibrosis via Smad3 activity.

## Introduction
Obesity has reached epidemic proportions globally, posing a severe burden on healthcare systems worldwide [ , ]. It is a major risk factor for numerous metabolic complications, most notably type 2 diabetes and insulin resistance [ , ]. Beyond these systemic conditions, obesity and high‐fat diet (HFD) feeding adversely affect skeletal muscle structure and function, leading to fibrosis, atrophy, and impaired regeneration [ , , ].
Skeletal muscle fibrosis, characterized by the excessive accumulation of extracellular matrix (ECM) components like collagen, is a key pathological feature in obesity [ , , ]. Studies have demonstrated significant increases in collagen content in the muscles of both HFD‐fed mice and obese human subjects [ , ]. This excessive ECM deposition reduces muscle elasticity, impairs contractile function, and hinders satellite cell–mediated regeneration. Crucially, muscle fibrosis has been directly linked to the development of insulin resistance in obesity, highlighting its clinical significance as a therapeutic target [ , ].
The transformation of fibroblasts into overactive myofibroblasts is a central event in fibrosis, driven by a complex network of signaling pathways. Among these, the transforming growth factor–beta (TGF‐β) pathway is a master regulator [ ]. TGF‐β binding leads to the phosphorylation of Smad2/3, which then complexes with Smad4 and translocates to the nucleus to promote the transcription of pro‐fibrotic genes, including collagen I and III [ , ]. The critical role of Smad3 in obesity‐induced muscle fibrosis is well established [ ], its phosphorylation is elevated in the muscles of obese individuals and Smad3 deficiency markedly reduces fibrotic deposition in mouse models [ , ].
Nur77 (NR4A1), an orphan nuclear receptor, is an emerging regulator of metabolism and fibrosis. It is expressed in skeletal muscle and plays a role in glucose uptake and lipid metabolism [ , ]. Notably, Nur77 expression is downregulated in the skeletal muscle of both HFD‐fed rodents and obese humans [ , , ], suggesting a potential protective role. While Nur77 has been implicated in mitigating cardiac fibrosis [ ] and promoting renal and pulmonary fibrosis in other contexts [ , ], its function in skeletal muscle fibrosis remains entirely unexplored.
Given the established role of Smad3 in driving obesity‐related muscle fibrosis and the emerging, context‐dependent functions of Nur77 in fibrotic pathways, we hypothesized that Nur77 might regulates skeletal muscle fibrosis. This study aims to investigate the role and mechanism of Nur77 in obesity‐induced skeletal muscle fibrosis, thereby identifying a potential novel therapeutic target.

## Methods
2.1.

## Animals
The Nur77−/− mice were purchased from The Jackson Laboratory in the United States and were housed at the Experimental Animal Center of China University of Medical Sciences. The housing environment was at SPF level, with a temperature of 23°C ± 2°C, humidity of 50%–60%, and a 12‐h dark/light cycle. The mice had free access to normal diet or high‐fat diet and water. All animal experiments were approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of China University of Medical Sciences (2024PS1487K).
Male wild‐type (WT) C57BL/6 mice were randomly allocated to their respective groups at weaning at postnatal day 21. From 4 weeks of age, the designated groups received either a normal diet or a high‐fat diet (HFD, Research Diets #D12492, providing 60% of calories from fat) and were maintained on these diets for a duration of 24 weeks.

2.2.

## Antibodies and Reagents
Rabbit anti‐Collagen I a1 (1:1000, Zenbio, R26615), Rabbit anti‐Collagen III a1 (1:1000, Proteintech, 22 734‐1‐AP), Rabbit anti‐Smad3 (1:1000, Cell Signaling Technology, 9523), Rabbit anti‐p‐Smad3 (1:1000, Cell Signaling Technology, 9520), Rabbit anti‐TGF‐β1 (1:1000, Abcam, ab142139), Rabbit anti‐Nur77 (Novusbio, JM59‐11), Rabbit anti‐pax7 (1:1000, Abcam ab211542), Rabbit anti‐myod (1:1000, SantaCrue sc‐32 758), Rabbit anti‐myog (1:1000, SantaCrue sc‐52 903), Rabbit anti‐GAPDH (1:1000, Cell Signaling Technology, 92 310), Rabbit anti‐AKT (1:1000, Cell Signaling Technology, 9272), Rabbit anti‐Phospho‐AKT (ser473) (1:1000, Cell Signaling Technology, 4060), Rabbit anti‐GSK‐3β (1:1000, Cell Signaling Technology, 12 456), Rabbit anti‐Phospho‐GSK‐3β (1:1000, Cell Signaling Technology,5558)，PA (palmitic acid)was purchased from Solarbio (IP0670，Beijing，China), SIS3 was purchased from MCE (HY‐13013).
Antibody binding was detected using HRP‐conjugated anti‐rabbit or mouse secondary antibody and visualized using DAB Peroxidase Substrate kit (DAB‐0031, MXB Biotechnologies). The images were acquired using a microscope (Nikon, DS‐Fi2).

2.3.

## Muscle Tissue Preparation
Morphological analysis was performed by administering pentobarbital sodium (50 mg/kg, i.p.) to anesthetize the animals, and then perfusing the excised gastrocnemius muscle tissue with 4% paraformaldehyde in 0.1 M phosphate‐buffered saline (PBS) for fixation. The excised gastrocnemius tissue was soaked in the fixative overnight. After gradient ethanol dehydration, the gastrocnemius muscle was embedded in paraffin and then sectioned. For biochemical and molecular biological analyses, the gastrocnemius and anterior tibialis muscles were dissected from mice that were euthanized by cervical dislocation and immediately immersed in cold PBS. Then, the dissected muscle tissues were rapidly frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at −80°C until use.

2.4.

## Cell Culture
HEK293T and C2C12 cells were purchased from Cell Bank in the Chinese Academy of Sciences Shanghai, NIH/3 T3 cell was purchased from Suzhou Starfish Biotechnology Co. Ltd. HEK293T, NIH/3T3 and C2C12 cells were cultured in high‐glucose Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium (DMEM) supplemented with fetal bovine serum (FBS) (10%, CLARK, Australia), penicillin (100 U), and streptomycin (100 μg/mL) in 5% CO2 at 37°C.

2.5.

## In Vitro GST Pull‐Down
Fusion proteins GST‐Nur77 were constructed by inserting the coding region of human APP into pGEX‐5X‐1. The bacterial expression constructs were transformed into BL21‐competent cells (Takara). Cells were induced to protein overexpression by addition of 1 mM IPTG (isopropylβ‐D‐1‐thiogalactopyranoside) at 30°C for 3 h, and then, they were resuspended in bacterial lysis buffer (50 mM Tris–HCl (pH 8.0), 20% glucose, 10% glycerol, 2 mM MgCl2), followed by ultrasonication. The proteins were purified with glutathione sepharose 4B according to the manufacturer's protocol, in which a Flag‐tagged protein synthesized by transcription and translation kit in vitro (Promega, P2221) was added, further incubating for 4 h at 4°C in binding buffer (20 mM Tris–HCl (pH 7.5), 50 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, 1% NP‐40). After washing three times with binding buffer, the proteins were eluted with 2× SDS sample buffer by boiling for 10 min. Western blot was used to detect bound proteins.

2.6.

## Immunocytochemistry
For immunocytochemistry analysis, HEK293 cells were grown on coverslips and were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 10 min at room temperature and subsequently blocked with 0.1% Triton X‐100/10% goat serum for 30 min at room temperature. Fixed cells were incubated overnight at 4°C with the corresponding primary antibodies: rabbit anti‐COL1A1 (Millipore, MAB348, 1:200 dilution), rabbit anti‐COL3A1 (CST, 3288, 1:100 dilution), mouse anti‐Rab7 (Santa Cruz, sc‐376 362, 1:100 dilution). Coverslips were then washed in phosphate‐buffered saline (PBS) and stained for 60 min with Alexa Fluor 488‐, Alexa Fluor 568‐, and Alexa Fluor 647‐conjugated secondary antibodies (Invitrogen; 1:400). Coverslips were washed again with PBS and mounted with DAPI (Sigma, 32 670). Imaging was performed on GE Deltavision OMX SR using a Plan Apo N 60 × 1.42 Oil. Image reconstruction and processing were performed using SoftWoRx 8.0.0 software.

2.7.

## Western Blot and IP Analyses
Firstly, the cells or tissues are lysed in modified lysis buffer (150 mM NaCl, 1 mM NaF, 50 mM Tris–HCl (pH 7.6), 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM Na3VO4, 1% NP‐40, 1 mM MgCl2, 1 mM DTT, 0.25% taurine sodium) on ice, and treated with a protein inhibitor mixture (Sigma) and 1 mM benzylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF). Then, BCA quantification (Pierce) is performed. The cell or tissue lysate is incubated with the corresponding antibody at 4°C for 3 h, and then incubated with A/G–Sepharose beads at 4°C overnight. Afterwards, the protein–antibody complex is washed three times with cold lysis buffer at 4°C, and 2× SDS sample buffer is added and boiled for 10 min to dissociate.

2.8.

## Immunofluorescence Staining
The cells were pre‐coated on a glass slide the previous day. The next day, they were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde at room temperature for 15 min, then punched with 0.1% Triton X‐100 for 20 min, and then blocked with 10% goat serum at room temperature for 60 min. The cells were incubated with rabbit anti‐Nur77 (1:1000, Sigma, 07131) or mouse anti‐Smad3 (1:1000, ZEN Bioscience, 200474) overnight at 4°C. They were washed three times in PBS, then incubated with Alexa 568–conjugated goat anti‐rabbit IgG (1:400, A‐11011, Thermo Fisher Scientific) or Alexa 488–conjugated goat anti‐mouse IgG (1:400, A‐11001, Thermo Fisher Scientific) for 1 h at room temperature. The final counterstaining with DAPI (Sigma, 32670) was performed for 3 min. After the final three washes in PBS, the mounting medium and cover slip were used for mounting, and the sample was air‐dried. Images were obtained using a fluorescence microscope (Nikon, Ni‐U, Japan). The average fluorescence intensity (Mean) was quantified using ImageJ software and expressed as relative integral density. The mean (mean gray value) = total density/area.

2.9.

## Flow Cytometry Analysis
Stimulate the stable overexpressed or knocked‐down Nur77 cell lines with PA, and determine the cell apoptosis status. Next, collect the cells using trypsin without EDTA, and stain 1 × 105 cells with the FITC Annexin V apoptosis detection kit I (BD Biosciences, 556547) according to the instructions. Analyze the apoptosis frequency using flow cytometry (BD Accuri). C6 Plus, BD Biosciences.

2.10.

## Quantification and Statistical Analysis
All data are presented as the mean ± standard error of the mean. Statistical analysis for comparisons between two groups were performed using Student's t‐test; for multiple groups, one‐way ANOVA combined with Tukey's post hoc test is used. N represents the number of animals or the number of replicate experiments. Data analysis is conducted using GraphPad Prism 7 (GraphPad Software). A p value < 0.05 is considered significant. The p value is expressed as p > 0.05 (ns, no statistical significance), and the average fluorescence intensity and protein expression levels are quantified using ImageJ software.

## Results
3.1.

## The Expression of Nur77 Protein in Obese Mice Decreased, and the Structure and Function of Skeletal Muscles Were Affected
In order to investigate the effect of diet on the skeletal muscle function of mice, we measured the body weight, fat content, grip strength, and movement ability of mice in the WT‐ND group and the WT‐HFD group. The results showed that compared with the WT‐ND group of mice, the WT‐HFD group of mice had increased body weight (Figure  ). The body composition analysis revealed that the percentage of fat content in the WT‐HFD group of mice increased as a proportion of the total body weight (Figure  ). The results of the grip strength and motor ability tests showed that the grip strength, maximum movement time and movement distance of the mice in the WT‐HFD group were significantly decreased (Figures  ). The above results indicate that obesity leads to a decline in skeletal muscle function. To further investigate the effects of obesity on the skeletal muscle tissue of mice, we measured the lean body mass and gastrocnemius muscle mass of mice in the WT‐ND group and the WT‐HFD group. We found that the percentage of lean body mass in the total body weight of mice in the WT‐HFD group was lower than that in the WT‐ND group (Figure  ), and the percentage of gastrocnemius muscle mass in body weight was also lower (Figures  ). The H&E staining results indicated that the cross‐sectional area of muscle fibers in the WT‐HFD group was significantly smaller than that in the WT‐ND group (Figure  ); the results of Periodic Acid‐Schiff (PAS) staining and Masson staining suggested that the amount of collagen deposited in the skeletal muscles of the WT‐HFD group was significantly higher than that of the WT‐ND group (Figures  ). From this, it can be seen that obesity leads to atrophy of skeletal muscle, accompanied by significant fibrosis. We calculated the distribution pattern and average cross‐sectional area of the muscle tissue fibers. The cross‐sectional area of the muscle fibers in Nur77 knockout mice was significantly reduced (Figures  ). As a key target for regulating fibrosis, Nur77 may also be involved in the occurrence and development of fibrosis in skeletal muscle induced by obesity. To verify the effect of obesity on the expression level of Nur77 protein in skeletal muscle, we conducted a Western blot analysis to measure the expression level of Nur77 protein. The results showed that, compared with the WT‐ND group, the expression level of Nur77 protein in the skeletal muscle of the WT‐HFD group was significantly lower (Figure  ).
 
3.2.

## Nur77 Knockout Exacerbates the Degree of Skeletal Muscle Fibrosis Induced by Obese Mice
Based on the previous results, we speculate that obesity‐induced myofiberization may be related to the decreased expression of Nur77. The genotypes of the mice were identified by agarose gel electrophoresis using the mouse tail DNA from WT and Nur77 knockout mice. The results showed that the WT mice had a band at 190 bp, while the Nur77 knockout mice had a band at 350 bp (Figure  ), thereby identifying the mouse genotypes. WT and Nur77−/− mice were respectively fed with ND and HFD for 12 weeks, and their body weight and body composition changes were measured. The results showed that the body weight of mice in the HFD group was significantly higher than that in the ND group (Figure  ); and compared with the WT‐ND group, the body weight of mice in the Nur77−/− (ND) group increased; however, compared with the WT‐HFD group, the body weight of mice in the Nur77−/− (HFD) group showed an upward trend, but there was no statistical difference (Figure  ). The body composition analysis revealed that, compared with the WT‐ND group, the fat content as a percentage of the total body weight in the Nur77−/− (ND) group of mice was significantly increased; and compared with the WT‐HFD group, the fat content as a percentage of the total body weight in the Nur77−/− (HFD) group of mice was also significantly increased (Figure  ). It indicates that the knockout of Nur77 accelerates the process of obesity. The results of grip strength and motor ability tests showed that compared with the WT‐ND group, the grip strength, maximum movement time and movement distance of mice in the Nur77−/− (ND) group were significantly decreased; compared with the WT‐HFD group, the grip strength, maximum movement time and movement distance of mice in the Nur77−/− (HFD) group were also significantly decreased (Figures  ). The above results indicate that the knockout of Nur77 exacerbates the skeletal muscle dysfunction in obese mice. The study found that skeletal muscle fibrosis leads to skeletal muscle atrophy and dysfunction. Therefore, in order to further elaborate on the extent of the effect of Nur77 knockout on obesity‐induced bone fibrosis, we measured the lean body mass and gastrocnemius muscle mass of each group of mice, and performed H&E staining, Sirius Red staining, and Masson staining on the gastrocnemius muscles of the mice. The results showed that compared with the WT‐ND group, the percentage of lean body mass in the total body weight of mice in the Nur77−/− (ND) group decreased, and the percentage of gastrocnemius muscle mass in body weight also significantly decreased (Figure  ). Compared with the WT‐HFD group, the percentage of lean body mass in the Nur77−/− (HFD) group decreased, and the percentage of gastrocnemius muscle mass in body weight also significantly decreased (Figures  ). The H & E staining results indicated that, compared with the WT‐ND group, the cross‐sectional area of muscle fibers in the Nur77−/− (ND) group was significantly reduced; and compared with the WT‐HFD group, the cross‐sectional area of muscle fibers in the Nur77−/− (HFD) group also decreased significantly (Figure  ). The results of the Sirius Red staining and Masson staining indicated that, compared with the WT‐ND group, the amount of collagen deposited in the skeletal muscles of Nur77−/− (ND) mice was significantly increased (Figure  ). Compared with the WT‐HFD group, the amount of collagen deposited in the skeletal muscles of the Nur77−/− (HFD) group also significantly increased (Figure  ). Skeletal muscle fibrosis is characterized by excessive accumulation of collagen. Therefore, we further conducted immunohistochemical staining for COL1A1 and COL3A1 in the gastrocnemius muscles of each group of mice, and used Western blot to detect the protein expression levels of COL1A1 and COL3A1. The results indicated that the knockout of Nur77 led to an increase in the expression of COL1A1 and COL3A1 in the skeletal muscle tissue of mice, and this increase was further enhanced with the high‐fat feeding of the mice; compared with the WT‐HFD group of mice, the expression of COL1A1 and COL3A1 in the skeletal muscle of the Nur77−/− (HFD) group also significantly increased (Figure  ). Meanwhile, Western blot was used to detect collagen and satellite cell‐related indicators in wild‐type and Nur77−/− mice, such as Pax7, MyoD, and MyoG. It was found that the knockout of Nur77 affected satellite cell renewal and led to an increase in the phosphorylation level of Smad2/3 (Figure  ). The above results indicate that the knockout of Nur77 exacerbates the fibrosis level and muscle atrophy in obese mice, and obesity‐induced myofiberization may be related to the decreased expression of Nur77.
 
3.3.

## Knocking Out Nur77 Leads to an Increase in the Phosphorylation Level of Smad3 in the Skeletal Muscle Tissue of Obese Mice
In order to determine whether Nur77 participates in the occurrence of skeletal muscle fibrosis by influencing the activation of Smad3, we used Western blot to detect the expression and phosphorylation levels of Smad3 protein in the gastrocnemius muscles of each group of mice. It was found that, compared with the WT‐ND group, the expression of P‐Smad3 protein in the Nur77−/‐ND group and the WT‐HFD group increased, while the total protein of Smad3 did not change much. Compared with the WT‐HFD group, the expression of P‐Smad3 protein in the Nur77−/‐HFD group also increased, while the total protein of Smad3 showed no significant change (Figure  ). The above results indicate that the knockout of Nur77 promotes the activation of Smad3 phosphorylation in the skeletal muscles of obese mice.
In cancer cells, the TGF‐β/Smad3 signaling pathway is often aberrantly activated and may be influenced by a distinct set of co‐factors, potentially uncoupling it from Nur77 regulation. In contrast, our findings in NIH/3 T3 fibroblasts and mouse skeletal muscle highlight a novel, context‐specific role for Nur77 in modulating Smad3 activity within the framework of obesity‐induced metabolic stress.
We treated NIH/3 T3 cells with 50 μM PA for 9 h to simulate the high‐fat environment in vivo. The Western blot results showed that the expression level of Nur77 protein decreased after PA stimulation; conversely, the expression levels of fibrosis indicators COL1A1 and FN proteins increased (Figure  ). This is consistent with the results of our previous animal experiments. Subsequently, we infected NIH/3 T3 cells with lentiviruses containing different Nur77‐specific interference sequences and Nur77 overexpression for 48 h. After screening with puromycin, we obtained stable cell lines and then collected the cell proteins to detect the expression level of Nur77 protein. The results showed that, compared with the control group, the level of Nur77 protein in the shNur77‐3 group decreased significantly. Therefore, this plasmid was used in the subsequent experiments. Furthermore, the expression level of Nur77 protein in the overexpression group was significantly higher than that in the control group, indicating that the stable transfection cell line was successfully constructed. We performed PA stimulation treatment on the knockdown or overexpression groups and their control groups, and detected the expression levels of fibrosis‐related proteins. We found that regardless of whether PA stimulation was given, the expression levels of COL1A1 and FN proteins significantly increased after Nur77 knockdown; conversely, overexpression of Nur77 inhibited the expression of COL1A1 protein (Figures  ). The above results indicate that Nur77 inhibits the expression of fibrosis‐related protein indicators in fibroblasts. In the Nur77 knockdown cell line, Nur77 was overexpressed and simultaneously exposed to PA stimulation. Through flow cytometry, the apoptosis of cells was detected. It was found that after Nur77 knockdown, apoptosis increased under PA stimulation, while when Nur77 was overexpressed, the apoptotic situation could be rescued (Figure  ).
 
3.4.

## Nur77 Inhibits the Phosphorylation of Smad3 and Activates the Regulation of Fibroblast Collagen Synthesis
In order to further investigate whether Nur77 regulates the phosphorylation level of Smad3 in fibroblasts and thereby affects the expression of fibrosis‐related indicator proteins, we constructed knockdown and overexpression stable cell lines. On this basis, 9 h of stimulation with 50 μM PA was given, and Western blot was used to detect the changes in the levels of Smad3 and p‐Smad3 proteins. The Western blot results showed that after NIH/3 T3 cells were stimulated with PA, the expression level of p‐Smad3 protein increased; moreover, regardless of whether PA stimulation was given or not, the expression level of p‐Smad3 protein also increased after Nur77 knockdown; conversely, overexpression of Nur77 would inhibit the expression of p‐Smad3 protein (Figures  ). It is worth noting that the expression levels of total Smad3 protein did not show any significant changes (Figures  ). We further confirmed the protein interaction between Nur77 and Smad3 in 293 cells using the Co‐IP experiment. However, whether this interaction affects the phosphorylation and activation of Smad3 still requires further investigation (Figures  ). We conducted cell immunofluorescence and found that Nur77 and Smad3 were co‐localized (Figure  ). At the same time, we performed an in vitro GST‐pulldown experiment, which confirmed that there was a direct binding between the two (Figure  ). We overexpressed Nur77 in satellite cells and examined their proliferation. The results showed that Nur77 could promote the proliferation of satellite cells (Figure  ). We administered different concentrations of growth factor TGF‐β and found that both collagen COL1A1 and COL3A1 increased (Figures  ). In order to further confirm whether Nur77 functions through Smad3, we administered the inhibitor of Smad3—SIS3. We found that Nur77 functions through Smad3 (Figures  ). However, when Nur77 agonist Csn‐B was administered simultaneously with TGF‐β, the levels of COL1A1 and COL3A1 decreased. Based on the changes in the protein levels of fibrosis‐related indicators, we speculate that Nur77 affects the phosphorylation and activation of Smad3, thereby regulating the expression of collagen‐related proteins in fibroblasts. The mechanism of action may be through binding to Smad3 to regulate the expression of downstream related fibrosis indicator proteins.

## Discussion
Skeletal muscle fibrosis indicates a significant imbalance in muscle homeostasis. Although this tissue exhibits a remarkable ability to adapt to physiological changes and regenerate after injury, this capacity can be overwhelmed [ ]. The excessive deposition of extracellular matrix (ECM) is a hallmark of various pathological conditions [ ]. For instance, collagen deposition increases in the skeletal muscle of both humans and rodents with insulin resistance [ , ]. Furthermore, hyperglycemia and diabetes can promote the synthesis of fibronectin, laminin, and type IV collagen. This process leads to the thickening of the basement membrane and contributes to the development of diabetes‐related microvascular complications. It is postulated that remodeling of the extracellular matrix (ECM) underpins the pathogenesis of obesity and metabolic dysfunction [ ]. In this study, we employed a 12‐week high‐fat diet regimen in wild‐type mice to examine skeletal muscle alterations. Consistent with the literature, the diet‐induced obese mice developed significant muscle atrophy and collagen deposition, concomitant with a decline in muscle function, relative to the normal diet group. These data indicate that obesity induces skeletal muscle fibrosis, thereby driving functional impairment. Previous research has predominantly focused on the role of skeletal muscle in treating and managing metabolic diseases, particularly type 2 diabetes (T2DM) [ , , ]. In contrast, investigations into the extracellular matrix (ECM) and its remodeling have been relatively scarce. Therefore, to advance our understanding of skeletal muscle fibrosis, future studies should prioritize the composition, structure, function, and regulatory mechanisms of the ECM, as well as its dynamic interplay with metabolic diseases such as obesity and diabetes [ , , , ]. The study found that the expression of Nur77 protein in the gastrocnemius muscle of obese mice was decreased. Nur77 plays a crucial role in maintaining energy homeostasis and glucose metabolism. In the hypothalamus, Nur77 participates in regulating the central control of leptin on food intake, and simultaneously expands the effect range of leptin on the liver and adipose tissues, thereby regulating lipid metabolism. The decrease in Nur77 levels in the hypothalamus will affect the sensitivity of obese mice to leptin [ , ]. Furthermore, Nur77 acts as a stress sensor in pancreatic β cells, negatively regulating insulin secretion in response to glucose stimulation [ ]. It also inhibits high glucose and high lipid‐induced endoplasmic reticulum stress and/or ROS production by down‐regulating the level of p‐JNK, thereby reducing β cell damage and apoptosis. In obesity and type 2 diabetes, the response of skeletal muscle to insulin is weakened in Nur77 [ ]. Moreover, the absence of Nur77 increases the susceptibility to diet‐induced obesity and insulin resistance. Furthermore, compared with ApoE−/− mice, Nur77−/−/ApoE−/− mice showed a more pronounced trend of myocardial thinning and rupture after myocardial infarction. This might be due to the absence of Nur77 in monocytes and macrophages, which promoted the pro‐inflammatory phenotype, thereby leading to a reduced myocardial repair ability, an increased scar area after myocardial infarction, and a significant decrease in collagen density. In the myocardial infarction (MI) model established by left anterior descending artery ligation, Nur77 downregulates the epithelial‐mesenchymal transition (EndMT) through inhibiting the NF‐κB‐dependent pathway, thereby suppressing cardiac fibrosis after myocardial infarction [ ]. In the kidney tissues of aged mice, the lack of Nur77 leads to an increase in the phosphorylation level of Smad2/3, exacerbating the renal fibrosis associated with aging [ ]. Although existing studies have demonstrated that Nur77 plays a crucial role in the occurrence and development of fibrosis in various tissues, these studies have not explored its potential function in the process of obesity‐related skeletal muscle fibrosis. In this study, by establishing a high‐fat diet model in Nur77−/− mice, it was found that the lack of Nur77 exacerbated obesity‐related skeletal muscle fibrosis and skeletal muscle dysfunction. Therefore, Nur77 may be involved in the occurrence and development of obesity‐related skeletal muscle fibrosis, providing a new research perspective for revealing the mechanism of obesity‐related skeletal muscle fibrosis, and having significant theoretical and practical research value [ , , , ].
A recent study by chen feng et al., utilizing the same Nur77‐KO mouse model, has comprehensively documented the pivotal role of Nur77 in regulating muscle atrophy, including the expression of markers such as MuRF1 and MAFbx [ ]. While their work elegantly delineates the involvement of Nur77 in protein turnover pathways, our present study unveils a distinct and crucial dimension of its function: the governance of the fibrotic response in skeletal muscle under obesogenic conditions [ ]. The amelioration of fibrosis in Nur77‐KO mice, as demonstrated here, cannot be fully explained by the attenuation of atrophy alone, suggesting that Nur77 exerts its effects through parallel, yet complementary, mechanisms. Therefore, our findings position Nur77 as a central regulator that integrates both atrophic and fibrotic pathways, highlighting its potential as a multifaceted therapeutic target for improving muscle quality in metabolic disease.
The main signal transduction pathways regulating muscle fibrosis are the Wnt/β‐catenin pathway and the TGF‐β/Smad pathway. Moreover, there is a co‐regulatory protein Smad2/3 in the process of signal transduction. Both of them can directly stimulate the transcription of fibrotic proteins by nuclear translocation of Smad2/3 [ ]. Previous studies have shown that in the hearts and aortas of obese diabetic mice, the TGF‐β/Smad3 pathway is activated, promoting the deposition of collagen in the myocardium and aorta, thereby mediating the occurrence of fibrosis. Furthermore, Smad3‐deficient mice are protected from the effects of diet‐induced obesity and diabetes. This study found that in the gastrocnemius muscle tissue of Nur77‐deficient mice, p‐Smad3 phosphorylation was activated, and the expression levels of fibrosis‐related proteins COL1A1 and COL3A1 were higher compared to those of the WT‐ND group of mice. This indicates that Nur77 may be involved in the occurrence of obesity‐induced skeletal muscle fibrosis by inhibiting the activation of p‐Smad3. The extracellular matrix (ECM) of skeletal muscle is produced by myofibroblasts located in the interstitial space of muscle fibers. Myofibroblasts can arise from fibroblasts, hematopoietic stem cells, vascular endothelial cells, and skeletal muscle satellite cells. Therefore, we chose NIH/3 T3 cells for the in vitro experiments and exposed them to PA to simulate the high‐fat environment in the body. The results showed that knockdown of Nur77 could promote the activation of p‐Smad3 and increase the expression of collagen‐related indicator proteins; conversely, overexpression of Nur77 inhibited the activation of p‐Smad3 and reduced the expression of collagen‐related indicator proteins. The in vitro experiments further confirmed the effect of Nur77 on p‐Smad3. Regardless of whether there was PA stimulation or not, knockdown of Nur77 could promote the activation of p‐Smad3 and increase the expression of collagen‐related indicator proteins; and vice versa. Furthermore, through the Co‐IP experiment, we discovered that Nur77 has a protein interaction with Smad3. However, the regulatory effect of this binding on the nuclear translocation activity of Smad3, the stability of p‐Smad3 protein, and fibrosis requires further investigation.
Our study delineates a crucial role for the Nur77‐Smad3 axis in regulating muscle fibrosis in the context of obesity. However, it is widely appreciated that obesity‐induced fibrosis is a multifactorial process, driven by a confluence of signals including chronic inflammation (e.g., TNF‐α, IL‐6, and NF‐κB pathway), oxidative stress, and dysregulated metabolic signaling (e.g., Akt/mTOR). Other potent fibrogenic pathways, such as canonical and non‐canonical TGF‐β signaling and Wnt/β‐catenin signaling, also play well‐established roles [ , ]. An important and emerging question is how these diverse signals are integrated to produce a coordinated fibrotic outcome [ ]. In this context, our finding that Nur77 governs Smad3 activation presents an intriguing possibility: Nur77 may function as a regulatory node within this broader network. As a stress‐sensitive transcription factor, Nur77 is known to be modulated by inflammatory cytokines and metabolic status. Therefore, we speculate that Nur77 could serve as a common conduit, through which various obesity‐related insults converge to amplify the pro‐fibrotic signal via Smad3. While the precise crosstalk between Nur77 and other pathways like NF‐κB or mTOR in muscle fibrosis awaits further investigation, our work establishes the Nur77‐Smad3 axis as a central component of a more complex regulatory framework, offering a new perspective for understanding the integration of metabolic and fibrotic signaling.
In summary, Nur77 mediates fibroblast regulation of obesity‐related skeletal muscle fibrosis. The possible mechanism is to inhibit the phosphorylation and activation of p‐Smad3, thereby suppressing the expression of downstream fibrosis‐related proteins.
