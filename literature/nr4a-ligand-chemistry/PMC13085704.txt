## Abstract

## Background
Orphan-Nuclear-Receptors belong to the Nuclear-Receptors superfamily of ligand-dependent transcription factors that act as sensors for hormones, vitamins and dietary lipids. Orphan-Nuclear-Receptors are differentially expressed in normal mammary and breast-cancer tissues. Unlike other Nuclear-Receptors, Orphan-Nuclear-Receptors lack known physiological ligands. Limited information is available on Orphan-Nuclear-Receptors function in breast-cancer.

## Methods
We performed a number of silencing studies based on the use of siRNAs targeting the Orphan-Nuclear-Receptors expressed in a panel of cell-lines, recapitulating breast-cancer heterogeneity. NR2F6 function was further validated by stable shRNA-mediated silencing in estrogen-receptor-positive (MCF7) and triple-negative (MDA-MB-231) cell-lines. This was followed by assays on proliferation, clonogenicity and motility in MCF7 and MDA-MB-231 cells as well as cytokine responses in MCF7 cells. Whole-genome RNA sequencing was used to define NR2F6-dependent transcriptional networks. Immunoprecipitation coupled with flow-injection high-resolution mass-spectrometry was performed to identify endogenous molecules binding NR2F6.

## Results
Significant levels of ten Orphan-Nuclear-Receptors were observed in most breast-cancer cell-lines and in mammary tumor tissues. Functional siRNA studies revealed that NR2F2 and NR4A2 silencing enhanced the proliferation of breast-cancer cells, indicating a potential tumor-suppressive role. In contrast, NR2F6 knockdown consistently reduced proliferation of breast-cancer cells, supporting an oncogenic role. NR2F6 is highly expressed in breast tumors as compared with their normal counterparts. Stable NR2F6 silencing decreased the growth and clonogenicity of MCF7 and MDA-MB-231 cell lines, corroborating the siRNA findings. In the mesenchymal and high motility MDA-MB-231 cell-line, NR2F6 silencing diminished directional cell migration. In the epithelial MCF7 cell-line, this Orphan-Nuclear-Receptor induced an antiproliferative response to immune cytokines. From a mechanistic point of view, NR2F6 knock-down up-regulated gene-networks related to cell-cell and cell–matrix interactions, while it down-regulated gene-networks involved in cell-cycle progression and proliferation. A mass-spectrometry–based ligand screening approach led to the identification of palmitoylethanolamide as the only endogenous molecule capable of binding NR2F6 with high-affinity.

## Conclusion
Our study identifies NR2F6 as an oncogenic driver of breast-cancer and a promising therapeutic target for the personalized treatment of this tumor. The discovery of palmitoylethanolamide as a physiological ligand of NR2F6 provides the molecular foundation for a rational design of NR2F6 antagonists/inhibitors to be evaluated for their therapeutic potential in breast-cancer.

## Background
Nuclear-Receptors are a family of 48 ligand-dependent transcription factors including Estrogen-Receptors and Retinoid-Receptors [ , ]. Each Steroid-Nuclear-Receptor regulates the transcription and translation of multiple genes and participate in different biological processes. The transcriptional activity of Nuclear-Receptors can be activated/repressed by direct binding of small organic molecules [ ]. Most Nuclear-Receptors bind to physiologically relevant endogenous ligands, like estradiol and retinoic-acid in the case of Estrogen-Receptors and Retinoid-Receptors [ – ]. However, 15 receptors (NR0B1, NR0B2, NR1D1, NR1D2, NR2C1, NR2C2, NR2E1, NR2E3, NR2F1, NR2F2, NR2F6, NR4A1, NR4A2, NR4A3 and NR6A1) are known as Orphan-Nuclear-Receptors (ONRs), because they lack an identified physiological ligand [ ].
Breast-cancer is a heterogeneous tumor type, which can be classified in the Basal-like, HER2-Enriched (HER2+), Luminal-A (LumA), Luminal-B (LumB) and Normal-like groups, according to the PAM50 gene-expression signature [ ]. Noticeably, LumA and LumB tumors are predominantly estrogen receptor-positive (ER+) tumors that differ in their aggressiveness and treatment response. LumA are typically HER2− and slower-growing tumors, which are characterized by a favorable prognosis. LumB tumors are more aggressive, have a higher proliferation rate (high Ki-67), and can be either HER2+ or HER2− [ ]. Despite the existence of various therapeutic options, breast-cancer personalized/stratified treatment requires innovative strategies. ONRs may represent novel therapeutic targets, as increasing evidence indicates that certain ONRs play a role in the development/progression of different tumor types [ , ]. With respect to this, it is noticeable that the crystal structure of the various ONRs is known or can be reconstructed with the available computational methodologies, which is a major advantage for the synthesis of specific ONR agonists/antagonists.
In the present study, we defined the levels of ONR mRNAs in a large panel of breast-cancer tissue-specimens and cell-lines recapitulating the heterogeneity of the disease. In addition, we used an siRNA-based silencing approach to define the relevance of ONRs for the growth/viability of breast-cancer cells. NR4A2- and NR2F2-silencing increased cell-growth, which is consistent with tumor-suppressive properties, while NR2F6-silencing reduced cell-growth, suggesting an oncogenic action. Given its significance as a potential therapeutic target, we focused on NR2F6 and performed a series of studies aimed at supporting its oncogenic action and at elucidating the underlying mechanisms. The RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq) data generated provided insights into the gene-networks involved in NR2F6 oncogenic action. Finally, we identified palmitoylethanolamide as a potential and endogenous NR2F6 ligand.

## Methods

## Cell cultures
MCF7, MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-453, MDA-MB-436, SKBR3, MDA-MB-157, T47D, CAMA1 and MB-157 cell-lines were from ATCC (American-Type-Culture-Collection), the Hs578T cell-line was from Sigma-Aldrich, while CAL120 and CAL85.1 cell-lines were from DSMZ (Deutsche-Sammlung-von-Mikroorganismen-und-Zellkulturen-GmbH). Cells were cultured in phenol-red-free DMEM/F-12 medium (Invitrogen-ThermoFisher-SCIENTIFIC) supplemented with glutamine (2 mM), penicillin (100 units/mL)/streptomycin (100 µg/ml) and 5% fetal-bovine-serum (Euroclone). IFNγ (Interferon-γ) and TNFα (Tumor-Necrosis-Factor-α) were from PeproTech-Proteins (ThermoFisher-SCIENTIFIC). Cell migration (wound-healing assay; [ , ]) and colony assays were performed, as detailed in Supplementary-Information.

## Western blots
Proteins were extracted using RIPA buffer 1X (Tris-HCl 50 mM, pH 7.4–8.0, NaCl 150, Triton X-100 1%, Sodium deoxycholate 0.5%, SDS 0.1%) and quantified with the Pierce™ BCA Protein Assay Kit (Invitrogen, ThermoFisher-SCIENTIFIC). Protein samples were subjected to SDS-PAGE and Western blot analyses using the following antibodies: anti-NR2F2 (Cell Signaling Technology, #6434), anti-NR2F6 (Proteintech, clone 2H2B8, #60117-2-PBS), anti-p65 Ser536 (NFKB) (Cell Signaling Technology, #3033), anti-p65 (NFKB) (Cell Signaling Technology, #8242), anti-STAT1 Tyr701 (Cell Signaling Technology, #9167), anti-IRF1 (Cell Signaling Technology, #8478), anti-RIP1 Ser166 (Cell Signaling Technology, #65746), anti-RIP1 (Cell Signaling Technology, #73271), anti-MLKL (Cell Signaling Technology, #26539), primary antibodies and appropriate fluorescence-tagged secondary antibodies [Cy3-conjugated AffiniPure goat anti-mouse IgG (H + L), #115-165-003 and Cy5-conjugated AffiniPure goat anti-rabbit IgG (H + L) #111-175-144, Jackson ImmunoResearch]. For the analysis of membrane-associated proteins, cells were lysed directly in 2X Laemmli sample buffer (Tris-HCl 125 mM, pH 6.8, SDS 4%, Glycerol 20%, Bromophenol blue 0.02%, β-mercaptoethanol 2%) to ensure efficient solubilization and recovery of proteins tightly bound to the plasma membrane.

## RealTime-Glo Annexin V apoptosis assay
MCF7 cells were seeded at a density of 50,000 cells/ml in 96-well plates. After 24 h, cells were treated with TNF-α and IFN-γ (40 ng/µl). Sixteen hours post-treatment, apoptosis and necrosis were assessed using the RealTime-Glo™ Annexin V Apoptosis and Necrosis Assay (Promega). Luminescence and fluorescence signals were recorded from 16 to 40 h after treatment with the GloMax® Discover Microplate Reader (Promega).

## Caspase-Glo 3/7 assay
MCF7 cells were seeded at a density of 50,000 cells/ml in 96-well plates. After 24 h, cells were treated with TNF-α and IFN-γ (40 ng/µl). Forty hours post-treatment, caspase-3 and − 7 activity was assessed using the Caspase-Glo® 3/7 Assay (Promega). Luminescence signals were recorded with the GloMax® Discover Microplate Reader from 30 min to 3 h after the addition of the assay reagent.

## siRNA and shRNA studies
We purchased ONR-targeting siRNAs from ThermoFisher-SCIENTIFIC. The siRNA sequences are available in Supplementary-Information. The control siRNA was also from ThermoFisher-SCIENTIFIC (Stealth-RNAi™-siRNA-Negative-Control-Med-GC, cat. No. 12935300). Reverse transfection in 96-well plates was performed using Lipofectamine-RNAiMAX (ThermoFisher-SCIENTIFIC), as detailed in Supplementary-Information. The effect of ONR silencing on cell proliferation was quantified as the percentage of cell growth in siRNA-treated cells relative to their respective controls at day 6, based on optical density (OD) values obtained from the sulforhodamine B (SRB) assay [ ]. For each ONR, 5 or 6 breast cancer cell lines were analyzed. To determine the overall impact of ONR silencing on breast cancer cell proliferation, the mean cell growth inhibition across the analyzed cell lines was calculated. To obtain stable NR2F6-silenced cell-lines, the shRNAs indicated in Supplementary-Information were cloned into the pGreenPuro (CMV) vector (System Biosciences, SBI). MCF7 and MDA-MB-231 cells were infected with lentiviral vectors harboring NR2F6-targeting and control shRNAs or an empty vector (System-Biosciences protocol). Cell populations were selected with puromycin (1 µg/ml).

## RNA-Sequencing and GSEA Enrichment-Map Analyses
RNA-Sequencing (RNA-seq) and GSEA Enrichment-Map Analyses were performed as detailed in Supplementary-Information. The sequencing results were deposited in the ArrayExpress database (accession-number: E-MTAB-14498).

## Flow Injection-High Resolution Mass Spectrometry Analysis (FIA-HRMS)
To conduct these studies, we transfected MCF7 cells with plasmids containing a FLAG-tagged versions of NR2F6 (NR2F6_FLAG) and the unrelated PLAAT4 control (PLAAT4_FLAG). Twenty-four hours post-transfection, cells were lysed in Tris-HCl pH 7.5 (50 mM) containing NaCl (150 mM) and Igepal (0.5%). Subsequently, we immunoprecipitated 3 independent extracts of the NR2F6_FLAG and PLAAT4_FLAG transfected cells with anti-FLAG coupled beads. NRF2F6-FLAG and PLAAT4-FLAG immunoprecipitation pellets were resuspended in 50 µl of cold methanol. Suspensions were deproteinised with the 96 well ISOLUTE® PPT + on Biotage® Extrahera™ (Biotage) and extracts were subjected to mass-spectrometry analysis. Untargeted metabolomics profiling was carried out using the Flow Injection Analysis High-resolution mass spectrometry (FIA-HRMS) strategy [ ]. Briefly, a portion of the NRF2F6-FLAG and PLAAT4-FLAG immunoprecipitation extracts (8 µL) was analyzed by an Orbitrap-QExactive-Mass-Spectrometer (ThermoFisher-SCIENTIFIC) equipped with an electrospray source operated in negative and positive modes. The EASY-FIA tool was used for the pre-processing of data [ ].

## LC-HRMS analysis
LC-HRMS analysis was performed on a QExactive Mass-Spectrometer (Thermo Scientific Bremen) operated in ElectroSpray positive mode (Full scan MS at 35,000 resolution) and in data dependent MS2 (17,500 resolution, 10 to 50 NCE collision energies), coupled with an Agilent 1200 High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) system. Chromatographic separation of organic extracts (8 µl) was performed with an Atlantis-T3-column (150 × 2.1 mm ID, 3 μm phase, Waters), using an isocratic system with a flow rate of 200 µL/min, a column temperature of 40 °C, a mobile phase of acetonitrile: formic acid 0.1% in water (85:15), for a total run time of 16 min. The exact mass of interest (m/z 300,2896) and the MS/MS fragmentation patterns were extracted from full-scan data, with a 6ppm window using Qual Browser Thermo Xcalibur v 4.0.27.19 (ThermoFisher-SCIENTIFIC) and validated against sphingosine and palmitoylethanolamide standards.

## Results

## ONR expression in mammary-tumor tissues and cell-lines
Using the TCGA RNA-seq data, we defined the levels of the 15 ONR mRNAs in breast-cancer tissues (Total) and normal mammary-glands (Normal-tissue) (Fig.  /box-plots). Moreover, we delineated the average levels of the ONR transcripts in the breast-cancer sub-types [Basal-like, Figure ONR mRNA expression in mammary tumors and breast cancer cell-lines.
 HER2+, LumA, LumB, HR+ (LumA+LumB) and Normal-like] identified with the PAM50 gene-expression signature contained in the TCGA dataset (Fig.  /box-plots). All tumor sub-types and normal mammary-glands showed high levels of 12 ONRs (NR1D1, NR1D2, NR2C1, NR2C2, NR2F1, NR2F2, NR2F6, NR4A1, NR4A2, NR4A3, NR6A1 and NR0B2). Relative to the neoplastic counterparts, mammary-glands displayed larger amounts of the NR1D1, NR1D2, NR2C1, NR2C2, NR2F1, NR4A1, NR4A2, NR4A3 and NR0B2 mRNAs (Fig.  /red-asterisks), while they showed similar levels of the NR6A1 transcript. The expression profile of the NR2F2 mRNA was the most variable. Indeed, mammary-glands, HR+ and Normal-like tumors presented with similar amounts of the NR2F2 transcript. In contrast, mammary-glands expressed lower NR2F2 levels than LumA tumors and an opposite trend was evident in Basal-like, HER2+ and LumB tumors. Finally, all tumor sub-types contained larger amounts of the NR2F6 transcript than their normal counterparts. As for the remainder 3 ONRs, NR0B1, NR2E1 and NR2E3 mRNAs were substantially unmeasurable in mammary-glands and breast-cancers.
We used the RNA-seq results available in the CCLE database to determine ONR levels in 51 cell-lines. According to the immune-histological profiles, these cell-lines clustered in 3 groups, HR+, HER2+ and Basal-like/Triple-Negative (Fig.  /column-diagrams) [ ]. Consistent with the high levels in mammary-tumor specimens, all cell-lines expressed substantial amounts of the NR1D1, NR1D2, NR2C1, NR2C2, NR2F2, NR2F6, NR4A1, NR4A2 and NR6A1 transcripts, regardless of the HR+, HER2+ or Basal-like phenotype. As observed in breast-cancer specimens, the entire panel of cell-lines showed low levels of NR0B1, NR2E1, NR2E3 and NR4A3. Contrary to the breast-cancer specimens, which expressed large amounts of the 2 transcripts, all cell-lines presented with undetectable/low levels of the NR0B2 and variable amounts of the NR2F1 transcripts.

## Silencing studies in breast-cancer cells
To evaluate whether ONRs played any role in the growth/viability of breast-cancer cells, we took a stealth-siRNA-based screening approach. Following challenge with specific ONR-targeting siRNAs, we determined the levels of the corresponding transcripts and the growth rate of silenced cells. In these studies, we silenced ERα (ESR1) in MCF7 cell-line as a technical control of our screening approach. Indeed, the ERα-targeted siRNAs considered provided a reproducible knock-down of the ERα mRNA in MCF7 cells and this resulted in an equally reproducible growth arrest (Fig.  a). In the case of ONRs, we employed 2 targeted siRNAs for each receptor (the sequence of the siRNAs used is available in the Supplementary Information file) and a control counterpart (siCTRL). In these studies, we transfected a maximum of 6 cell lines with each ONR. The cell-lines selected for each ONR, (Fig.  ; black-dots) were representative of the different breast cancer subtypes and expressed significant amounts of the ONR mRNA under evaluation. Given the low/undetectable amounts of the corresponding mRNAs in our cell-lines, we did not perform silencing experiments on NR0B1, NR0B2, NR2E1, NR2E3 and NR4A3 (Fig.  ). As far.
 as the remaining 10 ONRs, we were unable to decrease the expression levels of NR2C1, NR4A1 and NR6A1 in any of the cell-lines considered, as illustrated for 2 representative cases in Suppl. Figure 5), NR4A2 (Fig. 3a), NR2F2 (Fig. 3b) and NR2F6 (Fig. 4).
In the majority of the cell-lines considered, silencing of the NR1D1, NR1D2, NR2C2 and NR2F1 receptors did not reach the arbitrary threshold (> 30% increase/decrease of cell-growth relative to the non-silenced control cell-lines) that we used to define the average proliferative or anti-proliferative effects exerted by each ONR (Fig. 3b, grey areas) (see METHODS). Nevertheless, 
NR1D1 silencing with siNR1D1a produced a minor decrease in the growth of MCF7 (HR+) and SKBR3 (HER2+) cells, while siNR1D1b caused a minor increase in the growth of MDA-MB-157 (Basal-like) cells (Suppl. Conversely, siNR1D2b increased MDA-MB-157 and MDA-MB-436 growth leaving MCF7, MDA-MB-453 and MB157 proliferation-rate unaltered. The two siRNAs exert the same antiproliferative effect only in SKBR3 cells. As for NR2C2, siNR2C2a and siNR2C2b did not affect MB157 (Basal-like) proliferation-rates, while they reduced SKBR3 and MDA-MB-436 growth (Suppl. Figure 5). In MCF7 and MDA-MB-157 cells, only siNR2C2b was active in reducing cell proliferation. Finally, NR2F1 silencing by either siNR2F1a or siNR2F1b exerted slight or no effects on the growth of MCF7, MDA-MB-453, T47D (HR+) and Hs578T (Basal-like) cells, while it increased the proliferation of the MDA-MB-436 cell-line (Suppl. Figure 5). Overall, these data indicated that NR1D1, NR1D2, NR2C2 and NR2F1 played no consistent role in the growth of HR+, HER2+ and Basal-like cells.
In our cellular models, NR4A2, NR2F2 and NR2F6 were the ONRs whose silencing exerted the most significant effects on cell proliferation (Fig. 3b and c). In particular, NR4A2 and NR2F2 knock-down exerted a general proliferative action, whereas NR2F6 silencing caused a significant and reproducible arrest in the growth of all the selected cell-lines.
As for NR4A2, we considered the Basal-like, MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-436 and CAL85.1 cell-lines, the HR+, MCF7 and T47D cell-lines, as well as the HER2+, SKBR3 cell-line (see Fig. 1/black-dots). To silence NR4A2, we used the targeted siRNAs, siNR4A2a and siNR4A2b, as well as the siCTRL control. Unlike siCTRL, siNR4A2a and siNR4A2b down-regulated NR4A2 in all cell-lines (Fig. 3 a). In addition, the targeted siRNAs increased MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-436, CAL85.1 and T47D proliferation, while they had no effect on the growth of MCF7 and SKBR3 cells (Fig.  a). As for NR2F2, we transfected the Basal-like, MDA-MB-231, CAL120 and MDA-MB-157 cells, the HR+, MCF7 and T47D cells as well as the HER2+, MDA-MB-453 cells (see Fig. 1/black-dots) with siCTRL as well as the targeted siRNAs, siNR2F2a and siNR2F2b. In all cell-lines, siNR2F2a and siNR2F2b down-regulated the NR2F2 transcript and protein (Fig. 3b). This translated into an evident decrease in NR2F2 protein levels (Fig. 3b, middle-panels). Both siNR2F2a and siNR2F2b increased the growth of the MDA-MB-231, CAL120, T47D and MCF7 cell-lines (Fig.  b), whereas these siRNAs left the proliferation of MDA-MB-453 and MDA-MB-157 cells unaffected. Overall, our silencing data suggest that NR4A2 and NR2F2 expression in the neoplastic cell may play an onco-suppressive action in Basal-like and HR+ breast-cancer.
 In the case of NR2F6, whose expression was significantly higher in all breast-cancer sub-types than normal mammary glands (Fig.  ), we transfected the Basal-like, MB157, MDA-MB-231 and MDA-MB-157 cells, the HR+, MCF7 and CAMA-1 cells as well as the HER2+, SKBR3 cells with 2 targeted siRNAs (siNR2F6a/siNR2F6b) and the siCTRL control. Unlike siCTRL, siNR2F6a and siNR2F6b down-regulated NR2F6 mRNA in all cell-lines (Fig.  /left-panels). This translated into an evident decrease in NR2F6 protein levels (Fig.  /middle-panels). Significantly, both siNR2F6a and siNR2F6b reduced the proliferation/survival of MB157, MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-157, CAMA-1, MCF7 and SKBR3 cells (Fig.  /right-panels). Our data are consistent with the idea that NR2F6 expression in the neoplastic cell is endowed with oncogenic properties in all types of breast-cancer. With respect to the hypothesized oncogenic action of NR2F6, the data obtained from a cohort of untreated breast-cancer patients contained in a public platform [ ] support this idea. In fact, they indicate that breast-cancer patients characterized by high tumor expression levels of NR2F6 show a decreased overall survival (Suppl. Figure 6a), distant-metastasis-free survival (Suppl. Figure 6b) and relapse-free survival (Suppl. Figure 6c).
 

## Stable NR2F6 silencing in MCF7 and MDA-MB-231 cells
Given the results described in the previous section, we concentrated our attention on NR2F6, as the siRNA-based studies indicated that this was the most interesting ONR from a therapeutic standpoint. To extend the siRNA results, we generated MCF7 and MDA-MB-231 cells characterized by stable NR2F6-silencing with retroviral constructs containing 2 separate NR2F6-targeting shRNAs [shNR2F6(1)/shNR2F6(2)]. MCF7 and MDA-MB-231 cells infected with a non-targeting retroviral construct (shCTRL) were used as controls. Relative to the parental cell-line (no-infection) and the shCTRL counterpart, a substantial down-regulation of the NR2F6 transcript/protein in shNR2F6(1)/shNR2F6(2) infected MCF7 and MDA-MB-231 cells was evident (Fig.  a and d). Consistent with the siRNA results, shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) decreased the growth of MCF7 and MDA-MB-231 cells (Fig.  b and e). In addition, the growth inhibitory action of shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) was associated with a significant reduction in the clonogenic potential of MCF7 (Fig.  c) and MDA-MB-231 cells (Fig. 5 f). This last observation supported the idea that NR2F6 expression was involved not only in the control of breast-cancer cell proliferation, but also in the control of cell survival. In a further set of studies, we investigated whether NR2F6 influenced not only the growth/viability, but also the motility of neoplastic cells. In fact, motility is a parameter directly associated with the metastatic behavior of cancer cells. To this purpose, we defined the directional motility of shNR2F6(1)-, shNR2F6(2)-, and shCTRL-infected MCF7 (Fig. 5 g, top panel) and MDA-MB-231 (Fig.  g, bottom panel) cells, using the wound-healing assay. In the epithelial/low-motility.
 MCF7 cells, NR2F6-silencing exerted no significant effect on the wound-closure time. In the mesenchymal/high-motility MDA-MB-231 cells, NR2F6-silencing increased wound-closure time, which is consistent with an inhibition of directional motility.
As NR2F6 was reported to decrease anti-tumor immunity in certain conditions [ , ], we evaluated whether NR2F6-silencing reduced not only the growth of breast-cancer cells, but also their sensitivity to the cytotoxic effects of TNFα (Tumor-Necrosis-Factor-α) and IFNγ (Interferon-γ), two of the major immune-modulatory and anti-cancer cytokines released by tumor-associated immune cells [ , ]. To perform these studies, we used shNR2F6(1), shNR2F6(2) and shCTRL infected MCF7 cells, given the high sensitivity of the parental cell-line to TNFα and IFNγ. As indicated by the sulforhodamine assay, at the low concentrations used, TNFα caused a significant reduction in the growth of shCTRL infected MCF7 cells, while IFNγ was devoid of any anti-proliferative effect (Fig.  a). Nevertheless, the growth-inhibitory action of TNFα was enhanced by the combination of TNFα + IFNγ. Relative to shCTRL infected controls, shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) infected cells showed a higher level of growth inhibition following exposure to both TNFα and TNFα + IFNγ (Fig. a). This is consistent with NR2F6 oncogenic action. In shCTRL, shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) infected cells, the TNFα + IFNγ combination caused not only a growth inhibitory effect, but it also induced an apoptotic response, as indicated by the time-dependent increase in Annexin-V levels (Fig. b). Following normalization for the corresponding vehicle treated control values, Annexin-V induction by TNFα + IFNγ was higher in shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) than in shCTRL infected cells. At 40 h, the apoptotic response activated by TNFα + IFNγ was confirmed by an increase in caspase-3/7 activity, which, once again, was significantly enhanced in shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) infected cells relative to the shCTRL counterparts (Fig. c). This increase was likely to be due to caspase-7 activation, as MCF7 cells are devoid of caspase-3 [ ]. These findings indicate that NR2F6 depletion sensitized breast cancer cells to cytokine-induced apoptosis, suggesting that the ONR acts as a pro-survival factor under inflammatory conditions.
 To gain insights into the possible molecular mechanisms underlying NR2F6 modulation of the TNFα/IFNγ effects in MCF7 cells, we defined the levels and activation state of proteins, which are known to be regulated by the two cytokines. As for TNFα, the cytokine has been shown to promote cell survival through Ser536-phosphorylation of NFKB [ ]. In our setting, we observed an increase in NFKB phosphorylation following challenge with TNFα alone and the effect was enhanced by the TNFα + IFNγ combination (Fig. 7d). Relative to the shCTRL counterpart, shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) infected cells were characterized by a reduction of the TNFα and TNFα + IFNγ dependent increase in NFKB Ser536-phosphorylation (Fig. 6 d). On the basis of these results, it is possible to hypothesize that the reduction in NFKB phosphorylation observed upon NR2F6 silencing contributes to the increase in TNFα + IFNγ cell death, as illustrated in Fig. 7b and c. As for IFNγ, the corresponding pathway was activated in shCTRL infected cells by IFNγ and enhanced by TNFα + IFNγ, as indicated by an evident increase in STAT1 Tyr701-phosphorylation and IRF1 protein levels (Fig. 7e). Relative to the shCTRL infected cells, we observed a partial down-regulation of IFNγ signaling in shNR2F6(1) infected cells, which was more evident in the shNR2F6(2) infected counterpart, as illustrated by the results obtained with STAT1 phosphorylation and IRF1 protein levels (Fig. 7e, left). In lung epithelial cells, IFNγ has been shown to enhance the susceptibility to necroptosis by upregulating MLKL [ ]. For this reason, we determined the amounts of MLKL in our experimental conditions. In shCTRL infected cells, TNFα and IFNγ increased the levels of membrane associated MLKL and this effect was enhanced by TNFα + IFNγ (Fig. 7e, right). Relative to the shCTRL counterpart, shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) infected cells were characterized by an enhancement of the TNFα, IFNγ and TNFα + IFNγ dependent increase in MLKL. In shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) infected cells, these effects were accompanied by an increase in the TNFα + IFNγ dependent phosphorylation of RIPK1, a well-known necroptosis marker [ ]. Thus, NR2F6 silencing seemed to activate a TNFα/IFNγ-driven necroptotic-like response, which was set in motion by RIPK1 phosphorylation and culminated with MLKL translocation to the membrane.
Overall, the shRNA-based approach substantiated NR2F6 oncogenic properties in luminal MCF-7 and mesenchymal MDA-MB231 cells. Indeed, NR2F6-targeting shRNAs reduced the growth and clonogenic ability of both cell-lines as well as the sensitivity of MCF7 cells to anti-cancer immune cytokines. Finally, the effects of NR2F6-silencing on the motility of MDA-MB-231 cells supported the idea that the ONR favors the spread of breast-cancer cells.

## NR2F6 regulation of gene-expression in MCF7 cells
To obtain insights into the gene-networks and molecular mechanisms underlying the oncogenic action of NR2F6, we performed comparative whole-genome RNA-seq studies in shNR2F6(1), shNR2F6(2) and shCTRL infected MCF7 cells, (Suppl.Table  ; 3-replicates/experimental-group). The reliability of the RNA-seq data was validated for 3 up-regulated transcripts (CLIC3/COL5A1/L1CAM) and the NR2F6 down-regulated counterpart by RT-PCR analysis (Suppl. Figure 7).
 On the basis of the RNA-seq data, we conducted an initial pathway-analysis with the use of the GSEA Enrichment-Map platform. The pathway-analysis performed on the GSEA platform permitted the identification of differentially regulated CoPs (Clusters-of-Pathways) in shNR2F6(1)- and shNR2F6(2)-infected cells (Fig. 7 a). Relative to the shCTRL control, shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) up-regulated and down-regulated the 2 common “Cell-Cell-and-Cell-Extracellular- Matrix-Interaction” and “Cell-Cycle-and-Cell-Proliferation” CoPs, respectively. Modulation of these CoPs was consistent with the anti-motility and growth-inhibitory effects observed upon NR2F6-silencing.
To gather more detailed information on the gene-networks potentially involved in NR2F6 oncogenic action, we evaluated the gene-set enrichment analyses of the RNA-seq data with the.
HALLMARK (Fig.  b and Suppl.Table 2), KEGG (Fig.  c and Suppl.Table 2) and REACTOME (Fig.  d and Suppl.Table 2) platforms. The HALLMARK data demonstrated that shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) up-regulated and down-regulated 6 and 3 common gene-networks, respectively (Fig.  b). As for the up-regulated networks, induction of the “Apical-Junctions” and “EMT” networks suggested an involvement of NR2F6 in the control of tumor-cell-plasticity. Up-regulation of the “P53-pathway” may explain the impact of NR2F6 silencing on cell growth and cytokine response. As for the down-regulated gene-networks, the data suggested an involvement of NR2F6 in the passage from the G2 to the M phase of the cell-cycle via control of the c-MYC dependent pathway (Fig.  b).
 The results obtained on the KEGG platform demonstrated that shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) caused an up-regulation of the “Focal-Adhesion” and “Regulation-of-Actin-Cytoskeleton” gene-networks (Fig.  c). This suggested a participation of NR2F6 in the control of cell-adhesion via modulation of actin functional activity. Consistent with an involvement of DNA-damage-repair in NR2F6 proliferative effects, NR2F6-targeting shRNAs down-regulated the “Homologous-Recombination” gene-network. The potential role played by NR2F6 in DNA-damage-repair was supported by the REACTOME data demonstrating shNR2F6(1)- and shNR2F6(2)-dependent down-regulation of two gene-sets controlling this process (Fig.  d). In addition, the REACTOME analyses indicated that NR2F6-silencing up-regulated 6 gene-networks involved in collagen and extra-cellular matrix homeostasis, providing further clues as to the mechanisms underlying the observed effects of this ONR on the neoplastic cell clonogenic and motility properties. Finally, shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) infection resulted in the up-regulation of the REACTOME-networks involved in “Tissue-Development”, “Signaling-in-Cancer”, “pre-NOTCH-Processing-by-the-Golgi apparatus” and VEGF signaling (Fig.  d).
A stringent comparison of the RNA-seq data obtained following stable infection with the shNR2F6(1), shNR2F6(2) and shCTRL retroviral constructs led to the identification of single genes whose expression was directly or indirectly modulated by NR2F6 (Fig.  e). Relative to shCTRL, shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) caused a significant up-regulation of 114 and 184 genes, respectively. In addition, shNR2F6(1) infection resulted in the down-regulation of 68 genes and shNR2F6(2) caused the down-regulation of 115 genes. However, shNR2F6(1) and shNR2F6(2) up-regulated and down-regulated only 50 and 9 common genes in a similar manner (Fig. 7e, left heatmap).
Some of these 59 genes were members of 5 interacting gene/protein networks, as indicated by the results obtained with the STRING (Search-Tool-for-the-Retrieval-of-Interacting-Genes/Proteins) tool (Fig.  e). The largest STRING interactome consisted of 21 proteins controlling various cellular processes, including cell-proliferation, cell-adhesion and cell/cell-interactions (up-regulated: ABAT/ASS1/BCAT1/COL5A1/COL4A5/LAMB1/L1CAM/SPTBN2/NT5E/NRP1/DKK1/CD24/EPAS/TFF1/TFF3/CLDN1/MUC5B; down-regulated: UGCG1/SEMA6D/ESR1/ARMT1). CD24 and L1CAM up-regulation suggests that NR2F6 oncogenic action involves effects on the number/functionality of neoplastic stem-cells [ , ]. In addition, down-regulation of the estrogen-receptor, ESR1, indicates that the oncogenic action of NR2F6 in HR+ breast-cancer cells may involve activation of the estrogen-receptor pathway. Obviously, the anti-proliferative effect of NR2F6-silencing in triple-negative cells is unrelated to ERα signaling, as triple-negative breast cancer cells do not express detectable levels of estrogen receptors. Three up-regulated proteins (SUSD2/LGALS1/S100A11), which participate in cell-cycle control and cell-proliferation [ – ], define a second interactome (Fig.  e). Each of the last 3 STRING interactomes (Fig.  e) consists of 2 up-regulated members (COX4I1 and COX6B1; KCNK2 and KCNK15; SRPRA and SEC61B). Interestingly, SEC61B participate in the endocytosis process [ ], like 8 other proteins (RAB6A/TMED3/STARD3NL/RNF167/VPS4B/TEX2/UNC13D/ATP2A3), which fall outside any identified interactome (Fig.  e).
Most of the 59 common proteins modulated by NR2F6 knock-down did not fall in any interactome. Among these, WWC3, CLIC3, RMDN1, SH2D4A (up-regulated) and CGAS (down-regulated) proteins are of particular interest (Fig.  e). WWC3 is a modulator of the Hippo signaling pathway and it may reduce neoplastic cell proliferation [ ]. CLIC3 is a nuclear chloride-channel, which regulates diverse cellular processes including cell-growth [ ]. RMDN1 is involved in the attachment of mitotic-spindle microtubules to the kinetochore [ ]. SH2D4A is a protein-phosphatase-1 regulatory subunit, which inhibits esophageal-cancer progression through the FAK/PI3K/AKT signaling pathway [ ]. Finally, CGAS promotes cellular senescence and it is also involved in the inflammatory response to genome instability and double-stranded DNA breaks [ ]. A second set of remarkable NR2F6-regulated proteins, which do not fall in any interactome, consist of factors (RAB6A, TMED3, STARD3NL, RNF167, VPS4B, TEX2, UNC13D and ATP2A3) which are involved in the endosomal pathway [ ]. Interestingly, STARD3NL plays a role in endosomal cholesterol transport [ ], while TEX2 is a lipid transfer protein [ ], which suggests that NR2F6 may influence tumor cell-growth and motility via control of lipid or cholesterol homeostasis.

## Identification of potential NR2F6 endogenous ligands
Further investigations were aimed at the identification of endogenous NR2F6 ligands. To conduct these studies, we transfected MCF7 cells with plasmids containing a FLAG-tagged versions of NR2F6 (NR2F6_FLAG) and the unrelated PLAAT4 control (PLAAT4_FLAG) (Fig.  a). We immunoprecipitated 3 independent extracts of the NR2F6_FLAG and PLAAT4_FLAG transfected cells with anti-FLAG coupled beads (Fig.  b). As expected, the Western blots performed with anti-FLAG antibodies indicated that the beads captured NR2F6 only in NR2F6_FLAG transfected and over-expressing cells (Fig. 8b). Similarly, PLAAT4 was immunoprecipitated only in PLAAT4_FLAG transfected and over-expressing cells (Fig. b). We challenged the anti-FLAG coupled beads with methanol and the corresponding solvent extracts were subjected to Flow Injection-High Resolution Mass Spectrometry Analysis (FIA-HRMS) (Fig. 8c). EASY-FIA analysis of the FIA-HRMS data identified 1148 molecules, which were present in at least 2 replicates of the NR2F6_FLAG or PLAAT4_FLAG immune-precipitates (Fig. 8d/Suppl.Table 3). The molecules identified in all the 3 NR2F6 immunoprecipitation replicates and in no more than a single PLAAT4 replicate amounted to 13 (Table 1). Three (m/z values: 283.2632; 357.2028; 300.2896) of these 13 compounds were measurable only in NR2F6_FLAG methanol extracts (Table 1). The fragmentation spectrum of these 3 compounds permitted the identification of a single mass (300.2896) corresponding to either sphingosine or palmitoylethanolamide. The LC-MS/MS (Liquid-Chromatography-Tandem-Mass-Spectrometry) studies performed on our immune-precipitates with the sphingosine and palmitoylethanolamide standards indicated the presence of the latter molecule only (Fig. 8e). Thus, the data were consistent with the idea that palmitoylethanolamide was likely to be an endogenous NR2F6 ligand.
 To confirm palmitoylethanolamide interaction with the ONR, we conducted studies with the recombinant full-length NR2F6 protein (NR2F6; 63 kDa) and the corresponding recombinant ligand-binding-domain (NR2F6/LBD; 45 kDa), each carrying an 18 kDa amino-terminal tag-polypeptide (Suppl. Figure 8). We expressed the plasmid constructs containing NR2F6, NR2F6/LBD and the sole tag-polypeptide in E.coli (Suppl. Figure 8). To determine palmitoylethanolamide binding, the His-tagged recombinant proteins (NR2F6, NR2F6_LBD and the tag-polypeptide) were affinity-purified on a Ni-NTA Sepharose column. Following purification, each protein was incubated with 25 µM palmitoylethanolamide in Tris-HCl pH 7.5 (50 mM) containing NaCl (150 mM) and Igepal (0.5%) for 1 h at room temperature. We subjected the purified samples to LC- MS/MS analyses. Significant amounts of bound palmitoylethanolamide were measurable only in the NR2F6 and NR2F6/LBD samples (Suppl. Figure 8). This complementary approach validated the data obtained in MCF7 cells. In addition, the results generated with the NR2F6/LBD construct supported the idea that palmitoylethanolamide interacted with the NR2F6 ligand-binding-domain.

## Discussion
In the present study, we systematically investigate the expression of the 15 known ONRs [ ] in a large panel of breast-cancer cell-lines and tumor tissues reflecting the heterogeneity of this disease. We identify 10 ONRs, which are characterized by significant expression in our panel of breast-cancer cell-lines. The role played by these 10 ONRs in the control of breast-cancer cell proliferation and viability is evaluated with the use of an siRNA-based screening approach. Our screening data permitted the identification of 3 ONRs, i.e. NR2F2, NR4A2 and NR2F6, capable of regulating breast-cancer cell growth in a reproducible manner. While NR2F2 and NR4A2 are characterized by an anti-proliferative action, NR2F6 simulates neoplastic cell-growth.
Given its positive effect on breast-cancer cell proliferation and its potential as a target for the development of innovative therapeutic strategies in the personalized treatment of mammary tumors, we focused our attention on NR2F6. NR2F6 knock-down with our siRNAs inhibits the proliferation of all the breast-cancer cell-lines considered, independently of their HR+, HER2+ or Basal-like phenotype. Consistent with a putative oncogenic function, NR2F6 expression level is elevated in multiple breast-cancer cell-lines and it is higher in breast tumors than normal mammary tissues. Our data are in agreement with a study defining the expression levels of Nuclear-Receptors in normal and tumor mammary samples [ ]. In this study, the authors measured higher levels of the NR2F6 mRNA in ER+ and ER− tumor tissues relative to what they observed in the normal counterparts. Interestingly, NR2F6 is a member of a gene signature consisting of 5 Nuclear-Receptors, which permits the discrimination of tumor from normal tissues [ ].
Although large amounts of the NR2F6 mRNA have been reported in leukemia, colon-carcinoma, cervical-carcinoma, ovarian, lung and hepatocellular cancers, the role played by this ONR in the neoplastic cell remains largely undefined [ ]. In line with our data on the proliferation of breast-cancer cells, diverse studies support the idea that NR2F6 is a driver of cell growth/survival and chemotherapy resistance in various neoplastic diseases [ ]. For instance, in acute myelogenous leukemia, NR2F6 stimulates the clonal longevity and is a negative regulator of differentiation in hematopoietic and leukemia cells [ ]. In colon-cancer, NR2F6 knock-down reduces the growth of tumor xenografts [ ]. In ovarian cancer, the ONR confers cisplatin resistance by activating the NOTCH3 signaling pathway [ ]. In this type of tumor, NR2F6 expression correlates with poor overall survival and it is significantly up-regulated in chemo-resistant cells. In addition, NR2F6 over-expression promotes, while NR2F6 knock-down represses the cancer stem-cell phenotype [ ]. Finally, NR2F6 knock-down sensitizes ovarian cancer cells to cisplatin in vitro and in vivo [ ]. The above reported evidence indicating that NR2F6 sustains clonogenicity in both leukemia and ovarian cancer cells supports our observation that NR2F6 silencing reduces the clonogenic potential of MCF7 and MDA-MB-231 cells.
In breast-cancer, the only evidence that NR2F6 may play a role in the homeostasis of tumor cells comes from studies on docetaxel resistance. In a first study [ ], NR2F6 is found to be significantly down-regulated in surgical breast-cancer specimens obtained from docetaxel resistant patients. The finding is corroborated by the development of MCF7 and MDA-MB-231 cell-lines characterized by resistance to the taxane. Indeed, resistant cell lines express lower levels of the NR2F6 transcript relative to the sensitive counterparts and exposure to docetaxel induces the expression of the ONR. In this cellular context, NR2F6 is regulated by a leptin driven axis and NR2F6 expression is necessary for the sensitivity of breast-cancer cells to docetaxel. In a second study [ ], opposite results are presented. In fact, profiling of the Nuclear-Receptors in the NCI-60 panel of the National Cancer Institute demonstrate that cancer cell-lines expressing low levels of NR2F6 are characterized by significantly higher sensitivity to various microtubule-targeting drugs than cell-lines expressing high levels of this receptor.
NR2F6 is emerging as a T-lymphocyte/macrophage intrinsic immune checkpoint, which is capable of fine-tuning adaptive immunity. In different mouse cancer models, inactivation of the NR2F6 gene in T-cells increases the infiltration of CD4+ and CD8+ T-lymphocytes in the tumor microenvironment and modulates the functional activity of these immune cells [ ]. In addition, NR2F6 knock-out animals present with a compromised splenic myeloid compartment and a reduced IL-15 priming of NK-cells (Natural Killer cells), which results in defective maturation and effector responses [ ]. Finally, NR2F6 ablation represses the expression of the activating receptor NKp46, a key player in NK-cell mediated cytotoxicity during infection and tumor rejection. In all these mouse models, NR2F6-deficiency results in anti-tumor and anti-metastatic immune responses which cause a survival benefit [ , ]. More importantly, NR2F6 genetic ablation enhances the anti-tumor activity of immune checkpoint inhibitors targeting PD-L1 and CTLA-4 [ , ]. In the reported studies, the tumor immune-modulatory action of NR2F6 appears to be intrinsic to immune cells. However, more recent reports support a tumor-cell autonomous activity of NR2F6 in melanoma [ ]. Indeed, NR2F6 expression in melanoma cells strongly correlates with resistance to immune checkpoint inhibitors. Overall, the available scientific literature suggests that both tumor-intrinsic and tumor-extrinsic expression of the NR2F6 receptor contributes to the effects exerted by the ONR on anti-tumor immunity. In line with a tumor-intrinsic action of the ONR, we observed that NR2F6 influences the response of MCF7 cells to the combination of 2 crucial inflammatory cytokines like TNFα and IFNγ. Indeed, NR2F6 silencing increases the sensitivity of the cancer cells to the necroptotic-like action of TNFα + IFNγ. This suggests that NR2F6 tunes the cross-talk between cancer cells and the immune microenvironment.
Being a transcription factor belonging to the COUP (Chiken Ovalbumin Upstream Promoter) family, NR2F6 is supposed to act under the form of a homodimer or a heterodimer, which represses or activates target genes via recognition of a GGTCA consensus sequence located in the corresponding promoter regions [ ]. To gain insights into the target gene-networks underlying NR2F6 oncogenic activity in breast-cancer cells, we compared the whole-genome RNA-sequencing data obtained in control and NR2F6 silenced MCF7 cells. Our transcriptomic data indicate downregulation of only 9 of the 59 genes differentially expressed by NR2F6 silencing in MCF7 cells, which suggests that NR2F6 acts predominantly as a transcriptional repressor [ ]. Interestingly, the scientific literature indicates that NR2F6 exerts a repressive transcriptional activity in T-lymphocytes as well. In these cells, NR2F6 suppresses the transcription of the NFAT and AP-1 genes directly and this suppression causes an inhibition of IL-2 and IFNγ secretion in activated T-lymphocytes [ , ]. Several other Steroid-Nuclear-Receptors, such as GR, PPARs, VDR, RXRs and RARs are known to impair the ability of NFAT and/or AP-1 to transcribe target genes [ ] and this effect is believed to represent the basis of the anti-inflammatory action exerted by steroids. Another important aspect of our transcriptomic results relates to the observation that cell-cycle is among the main biological processes stimulated by NR2F6 silencing. Indeed, the down-regulated “E2F_targets” gene-set (HALLMARK platform), which contains cell-cycle related targets of the E2F transcription factors, is endowed with a high Normalized-Enrichment-Score (NES) in the NR2F6 silenced MCF7 clones (Fig.  b). This finding is in line with the inhibitory effect on cell-growth exerted by NR2F6 silencing. Among the other HALLMARK gene-sets modulated by NR2F6 silencing, the up-regulated “Apical_junction” and “Epithelial_Mesenchimal_Transition (EMT) ones stand out. These last data support the idea that NR2F6 silencing modulates epithelial plasticity in a complex and possibly opposing manner via the regulation of multiple gene networks.
It remains to be established whether the effects exerted by NR2F6 in breast-cancer and/or immune cells require the presence of an endogenous organic ligand, which may activate or repress the activity of this ONR. In the present study, we identify palmitoylethanolamide, as the only endogenous molecule present in MCF7 breast-cancer which is capable of binding NR2F6. Palmitoylethanolamide is an endogenous fatty-acid amide, known for its anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective properties [ ]. Future efforts should be aimed at establishing whether palmitoylethanolamide plays any role in NR2F6 oncogenic action.

## Conclusion
The oncogenic properties of NR2F6 indicate that the ONR represents a significant new target for the development of pharmacological antagonists/inhibitors to be used in the treatment of breast-cancer. The identification/synthesis of NR2F6 antagonists/inhibitors is likely to be facilitated by the results obtained in the present study indicating that palmitoylethanolamide is likely to be an endogenous and physiological ligand of this ONR.
