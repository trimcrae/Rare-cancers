## Abstract
This study aims to investigate the potential active ingredients, targets, and signaling pathways of Yangyin Quyu decoction (YYQYD) for the treatment of pulmonary tuberculosis (PTB), utilizing network pharmacology and molecular docking technology. In this study, we constructed an “ingredient-disease” target library utilizing TCMSP, UniProt, GeneCards, OMIM, and SangerBox. We developed a protein interaction network for YYQYD in the treatment of PTB using Cytoscape 3.10.2 software. Additionally, we employed SangerBox and Wei Sheng Xin to conduct Gene Ontology/Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes enrichment analysis and to build the “component-pathway-target” network. Furthermore, we utilized molecular docking technology to visualize the active ingredients of YYQYD alongside the core targets of PTB. In this study, we screened quercetin, γ-aminobutyric acid, luteolin, kaempferol, and formononetin, identifying TP53, STAT3, AKT1, JUN, and ESR1 as the key targets of the decoction. Enrichment analysis of Gene Ontology and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes pathways showed the mechanisms mainly involved multiple signaling pathways (e.g., pathways in cancer, P = 4.90 × 10−29; metabolic pathways, P = 1.15 × 10−5). Furthermore, molecular docking results indicated that the active components exhibited significant binding affinities with the core targets of PTB. Quercetin and kaempferol, both with a binding affinity of −8.5 kcal/mol for STAT3 and ESR1 respectively, demonstrated excellent binding capabilities. Luteolin also showed strong binding to STAT3 with a binding affinity of −8.2 kcal/mol. Formononetin exhibited certain binding capabilities across various targets, while γ-aminobutyric acid displayed moderate binding affinities, suggesting potential synergistic roles within the biological context. This study proposed that the intricate network pharmacological mechanisms underlying the efficacy of YYQYD in the treatment of PTB. This research provides a molecular foundation for the development of novel antimicrobial therapies and proposes a targeted intervention strategy that aligns with the modernization of traditional Chinese medicine.
Keywords: molecular docking, network pharmacology, pulmonary tuberculosis, Yangyin Quyu decoction

## 
1. Introduction
Pulmonary tuberculosis (PTB) is a chronic infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis (MTB),[ ] which continues to pose a significant public health challenge globally. According to the Global PTB Report 2024 published by the World Health Organization, the epidemiological situation of PTB remains critical despite the advancements made in PTB control efforts.[ ] MTB primarily affects the lungs.[ ] Its pathogenesis involves the evasion of bacterial clearance through the inhibition of macrophage activation and the fusion of phagolysosomes with lysosomes. This process induces a T helper cell type 1 (Th1)/ T helper cell type 2 (Th2) immune imbalance and leads to the formation of chronic granulomas.[ ] Additionally, MTB can disseminate hematogenously to extrapulmonary organs, such as lymph nodes, meninges, and kidneys.[ ] Currently, first-line treatment for PTB primarily relies on antibiotic drugs, including isoniazid, rifampicin and pyrazinamide. This treatment regimen typically lasts for 6 to 9 months, which often leads to poor patient compliance and increases the risk of developing drug-resistant strains.[ ] Consequently, there is an urgent need to explore novel anti-PTB drugs based on host immunotherapy.
Traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) has developed a comprehensive theoretical framework and extensive practical experience in combating infectious diseases over the past millennia.[ ] Its multi-target synergistic regulation, low toxicity and side effects, and holistic approach have underscored its unique value in the prevention and treatment of PTB.[ ] At the same time, TCM synergistically enhances the efficacy of anti-PTB treatment through multiple pathways. These include the regulation of host immune balance, the inhibition of the microenvironment that supports the survival of MTB, and the improvement of lung tissue damage.[ ] Several studies have demonstrated that the combination of Chinese and Western medicine in the treatment of secondary PTB is more effective than Western medicine alone, and it is beneficial in reducing the incidence of adverse reactions.[ ] Yangyin Quyu decoction (YYQYD), as a representative formula embodying the Chinese medicinal principle of “nourishing Yin and supporting correctness, dispelling blood stasis and dispersing knots” has been utilized clinically as an adjunctive treatment for various diseases, including PTB. This formula comprises 13 Chinese medicinal ingredients, such as ophiopogon japonicus, rehmannia glutinosa, stemona japonica and mahonia bealei among others. It is known for its efficacy in benefiting Qi, nourishing Yin, activating blood circulation, and removing blood stasis. Stemona japonica, a widely utilized TCM for alleviating cough and asthma, has demonstrated significant anti-MTB effects in contemporary research. Notably, its alkaloid components have been confirmed to exert a certain inhibitory effect on MTB.[ ] Similarly, Ophiopogon japonicus, another traditional Chinese medicinal material, is extensively employed in TCM for its properties of nourishing Yin and moistening the lungs.[ ] Recent studies have further elucidated the antibacterial potential of its extracts, particularly their inhibitory effects on Staphylococcus aureus and various fungi. Recent studies have demonstrated that the combination of YYQYD with thymosin α1 is both effective and safe for treating patients with PTB and diabetes mellitus.[ ] Meanwhile, Zhu et al found that the combination of YYQYD with a standardized treatment plan for PTB effectively promotes the healing of cavities, accelerates the absorption of foci, and improves lung function and symptoms associated with TCM.[ ] However, the precise regulatory network of this formula in the interactions between MTB and its host remains unclear. Additionally, the molecular mechanisms behind the synergistic effects of its various components call for deeper analysis through systems biology approaches.
Network pharmacology is an emerging interdisciplinary field grounded in the principles of systems biology, integrating pharmacology, bioinformatics, computer science, and other multidisciplinary knowledge.[ ] Its core premise is to analyze drug action mechanisms from a holistic and systemic perspective by constructing an interaction network encompassing “drug-chemical composition-gene-target-disease.”[ ] The essence of this study lies in the organic integration of the broad screening offered by network pharmacology and the deep validation provided by molecular docking, culminating in a systematic research framework of “overall prediction – key validation – initial exploration of mechanism” (Fig. ). This integration strategy yields significant synergistic effects. Specifically, network pharmacology delineates potential active ingredient groups, target networks, and regulatory pathways of YYQYD for PTB treatment at a macroscopic level, thereby guiding the direction and scope of the study. In contrast, molecular docking emphasizes the intricate details of microscopic interactions between key molecules, validating and enriching the predictive outcomes of network pharmacology. Together, these approaches complement and corroborate each other, offering a more comprehensive and reliable insight into the complex mechanisms underlying the action of YYQYD in PTB treatment.
 

## 
2. Materials and methods

## 
2.1. Acquisition of active ingredients and targets of YYQYD
The composition of YYQYD comprises thirteen TCMs: ophiopogon japonicus, rehmannia glutinosa, poria cocos, paeonia lactiflora, scrophularia ningpoensis, stemona japonica, fritillaria cirrhosa, paeonia suffruticosa, salvia miltiorrhiza, mahonia bealei, agrimonia pilosa, panax notoginseng and bletilla striata. Utilizing these TCMs as keywords, we conducted a search in the TCM Systems Pharmacology Database (TCMSP, version 2.3, accessed on June 17, 2025).[ ] The screening criteria were oral bioavailability > 30% and drug-likeness > 0.18[ ] to obtain the active ingredient database of YYQYD. These criteria stem from classical TCM-active component screening studies: (oral bioavailability) > 30% ensures effective oral absorption and efficacy,[ ] while (drug-likeness) > 0.18 reflects druggability based on drug-target interaction structures.[ ] Widely used in network pharmacology, these thresholds balance screening specificity and enrichment of candidate components with potential in vivo activity. Furthermore, potential targets of the effective components were retrieved from the TCMSP database, and gene names were verified using the UniProt database (https://www.uniprot.org, release 2025-03, accessed on June 17, 2025)[ ] to construct a comprehensive database of related targets for the active components of YYQYD.

## 
2.2. Acquisition of PTB disease targets and identification of relevant targets for the treatment of PTB with YYQYD
Searches were conducted in the GeneCards human genome annotation database (https://www.genecards.org/, version 5.24, accessed on June 18, 2025)[ ] and the Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man database (OMIM, https://www.omim.org/, updated June 2025, accessed on June 18, 2025)[ ] using “pulmonary tuberculosis” as the keyword. Duplicate entries were eliminated to construct a target database for PTB. The online platform Sangerbox (http://sangerbox.com/, latest version, accessed on June 18, 2025) was utilized to analyze and compare the predicted targets of YYQYD with those of PTB, extract the intersection, and generate a Venn diagram.

## 
2.3. Construction of protein–protein network diagrams
Import the intersecting targets, represented as gene symbols, into the STRING database (https://cn.string-db.org/, version 12.0, accessed on June 18, 2025) for PPI analysis.[ ] Select “Homo sapiens” as the species of interest. Subsequently, import the resulting data in TSV format into Cytoscape version 3.10.2 (accessed on June 19, 2025). Adjust the node size and color to reflect the degree value. Using node degree, betweenness centrality, and closeness centrality as the primary metrics, filter the nodes to identify core targets with values exceeding the median for at least 2 of these metrics.[ ]

## 
2.4. Gene Ontology and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes analysis
Utilizing the SangerBox online platform and Bioinformatics (https://www.bioinformatics.com.cn/, accessed on June 19, 2025), import the intersecting targets for KEGG and GO analysis. Subsequently, transfer the results to Bioinformatics for further evaluation. Ensure that the species is set to “Homo sapiens” and filter the therapeutic biological processes and related pathways with the condition of P < .05.

## 
2.5. TCM-active ingredient-target construction
Cytoscape is an open-source software project designed to integrate biological molecular interaction networks with high-throughput expression data and other molecular states into a unified conceptual framework.[ ] In this study, we imported TCMs, along with each active component obtained in step 1 and the intersecting targets identified in step 2, into Cytoscape version 3.10.2 to construct a network diagram illustrating the relationships between TCM, active components, and targets. Subsequently, we analyzed the network using Analyze and selected the top 4 active components with the highest degree values for further molecular docking studies.

## 
2.6. Molecular docking validation
To conduct this study, we will first download the molecular structures of the top 4 core targets with the highest degree values from the protein data bank in protein data bank format. Subsequently, we will retrieve the structural formulas of the top 4 active components with the highest degree values from the PubChem database (https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/, latest version, accessed on June 20, 2025). Using the CB-DOCK2 platform (https://cadd.labshare.cn/, online version, accessed on June 20, 2025), we will dock each of the 4 TCM components with the corresponding proteins. The results will be analyzed based on the binding energy values, where lower values indicate stronger affinity. Typically, a binding energy threshold of less than −5.0 kcal/mol is employed to assess good binding affinity.[ ]

## 
3. Results

## 
3.1. Screening of active ingredients and target prediction of YYQYD
A total of 13 TCMs included in the YYQYD were identified through the selected databases. The results, after merging and deduplication, are as follows: Ophiopogon japonicus has 20 active components and 227 target sites; Rehmannia glutinosa has 9 active components and 216 target sites; Poria cocos has 15 active components and 25 target sites; Paeonia lactiflora has 13 active components and 92 target sites; Scrophularia ningpoensis has 9 active components and 52 target sites; Stemona japonica has 32 active components and 104 target sites; Fritillaria cirrhosa has 13 active components and 41 target sites; Paeonia suffruticosa has 11 active components and 69 target sites; Salvia miltiorrhiza has 65 active components and 137 target sites; Mahonia bealei has 3 active components and 21 target sites; Agrimonia pilosa has 5 active components and 185 target sites; Panax notoginseng has 8 active components and 187 target sites; Bletilla striata has 9 active components and 28 target sites.

## 
3.2. Acquisition of TB disease targets and prediction of potential targets for TB treatment with YYQYD
Through a comprehensive search of the Genecards and OMIM databases for PTB disease targets, a total of 3335 unique disease targets were identified after merging and deduplication processes. By intersecting these 3335 PTB-related disease targets with the 566 action targets of the active components in YYQYD, we identified a total of 280 potential targets for PTB treatment (Fig. ).
 

## 
3.3. PPI analysis
The 280 intersecting genes were input into the STRING database for PPI analysis, and the resulting files were imported into Cytoscape version 3.10.2. This analysis identified 219 potential targets and 828 connecting lines representing target relationships. Data analysis was conducted using the cytoNCA plugin in Cytoscape 3.10.2, with sorting based on Betweenness values >6.688961, Degree values exceeding 8, and Closeness values above 0.6315789. A total of twenty-one core targets, which play a significant role in the PPI network, were identified: MAPK1, CCND1, RELA, FOS, CASP3, EGFR, IL1B, MYC, BRCA1, MAPK8, CDKN1A, STAT1, TP53, STAT3, AKT1, JUN, ESR1, IL-6, TNF, HSP90AA1, and BCL2. The node size and color were adjusted to reflect the degree value, thereby constructing a comprehensive PPI network (Fig. ). The top 5 protein targets in terms of degree value are: TP53, STAT3, AKT1, ESR1 and JUN.
 

## 
3.4. GO and KEGG analysis
The intersecting targets were imported into the SangerBox platform for KEGG and GO analyses. The GO Biological Processes primarily encompass responses to chemical substances, responses to organic substances, regulation of biomass, cellular responses to chemical stimuli, stress responses, system development, negative regulation of cellular processes, establishment of localization, transport, and regulation of responses to stimuli. The GO Cellular Components mainly involve the cytoplasm, extracellular region, endomembrane system, parts of the extracellular region, vesicles, extracellular spaces, nucleoplasm, parts of the plasma membrane, and mitochondria. The GO Molecular Functions primarily include catalytic activity, cation binding, enzyme binding, homodimer binding, small molecule binding, anion binding, signaling receptor binding, drug binding, nucleotide binding, and nucleoside phosphate binding (Fig. A). The KEGG pathway enrichment analysis results indicate that the top 5 signaling pathways are: pathways in cancer (P = 4.90 × 10−29), metabolic pathways (P = 1.15 × 10−5), human cytomegalovirus infection (P = 1.67 × 10−18), the PI3K-AKT signaling pathway (P = 8.21 × 10−12), and the AGE-RAGE signaling pathway in diabetic complications (P = 1.04 × 10−27) (Fig. B).
 

## 
3.5. TCM-active ingredient-target construction
The main active components and targets of YYQYD were imported into Cytoscape 3.10.2 to establish a drug-component-intersecting target network (Fig. ). The yellow squares in the center represent a total of 280 intersecting target proteins, while the outer circles denote the individual herbs and their active components in YYQYD. The size of each circle correlates with its significance in the YYQYD-PTB network. Upon analysis with Analyza network and sorting by Degree values, the top 5 compounds identified are quercetin, γ-aminobutyric acid(GABA), luteolin, kaempferol, and formononetin.
 

## 
3.6. Molecular docking validation
Molecular docking was conducted between 5 key components (quercetin, GABA, luteolin, kaempferol, and formononetin) and 5 core target proteins (TP53, STAT3, AKT1, JUN, and ESR1), resulting in 25 distinct combinations of docking results (Table ). The findings indicate that most key active components of YYQYD exhibit favorable binding affinities (binding energy < −5.0 kcal/mol) with the core targets, with the exception of GABA. Notably, quercetin demonstrated the strongest binding affinity with STAT3, while kaempferol exhibited the highest binding affinity with ESR1. The CB-DOCK2 platform was utilized to visualize the complexes that displayed relatively strong binding energies (Fig. ).
 

## 
4. Discussions
Although previous studies have explored the pharmacological activities of YYQYD, this study is the first to systematically elucidate its multi-component, multi-target, and multi-pathway mechanisms in treating PTB, utilizing network pharmacology and molecular docking techniques. This research fills a critical gap in understanding the anti-PTB molecular mechanisms of this prescription. Emphasizing a “holistic view,” network pharmacology transcends the limitations of TCM research models, which typically focus on “component separation and single target verification.”[ ] By constructing a “drug-disease-target” network, this study accurately predicts potential associations between core components, such as quercetin and GABA, and key targets, including TP53 and AKT1. This provides a modern scientific basis for explaining the TCM theory of YYQYD, which aims to “nourish yin and strengthen the body, remove blood stasis, and resolve masses.” Furthermore, it offers a new paradigm for researching the anti-PTB mechanisms of Chinese medicine compounds.
The active components screened in this study, including quercetin, luteolin, GABA, and formononetin, exhibit multiple synergistic effects in anti-PTB processes. As a typical flavonoid,[ ] quercetin shows significant inhibitory effects against multidrug-resistant Mycobacterium tuberculosis (MDR-TB). When combined with anti-PTB drugs (such as rifampicin), it produces a synergistic effect, significantly inhibiting the growth of MDR-TB.[ ] Sun et al found that quercetin can also regulate the NF-κB signaling pathway to reduce excessive release of pro-inflammatory factors (e.g., TNF-α, IL-6) in macrophages, alleviating immunopathological damage.[ ] Luteolin inhibits the expression of bacterial virulence factors, thereby weakening pathogenicity.[ ] It also downregulates the expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α), reducing inflammatory responses.[ ] These properties make it valuable in treating chronic inflammatory diseases, including TB-related inflammation. GABA, an important inhibitory neurotransmitter, primarily acts in the central nervous system. However, recent studies have shown that GABA also has extensive functions in the immune system and nonneural tissues, particularly in antibacterial, anti-inflammatory, and immune regulation. Studies have indicated that GABA enhances IFN-γ secretion in CD4⁺ T cells by regulating GABAA receptor-mediated Ca²⁺ influx, reversing Th1 cells functional exhaustion caused by bacterial infection.[ ] Kaempferol competitively inhibits excessive activation of the PI3K-AKT pathway, blocking M2 polarization of macrophages induced by tuberculous secretion of effector proteins, and restores normal bactericidal function of macrophages.[ ] In TB-related inflammation, kaempferol reduces pulmonary dysfunction by inhibiting MTB-induced inflammation, showing potential therapeutic value.[ ] Formononetin exhibits strong antibacterial activity against Staphylococcus aureus and Escherichia coli.[ ] Meanwhile, it significantly suppresses the production of nitric oxide, TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β in LPS-induced RAW264.7 macrophages, exerting anti-inflammatory effects.[ , ] Compared with previous single-component anti-PTB studies, this research places multiple components within the compound system of YYQYD, more realistically reflecting the synergistic effect mode of the prescription in vivo.
Molecular docking and KEGG pathway enrichment analyses indicate that core targets such as TP53, STAT3, AKT1, JUN, and ESR1, along with signaling pathways like PI3K-AKT and AGE-RAGE, play pivotal roles in the anti-PTB efficacy of YYQYD. TP53, a recognized tumor suppressor gene,[ ] holds particular significance in the context of anti-PTB treatment. It modulates the host immune response against MTB by regulating the expression of various immune-related genes. For instance, TP53 interacts with transcription factors such as IL-6 and JUN to orchestrate immune responses, thereby participating in processes related to extracellular region activity and protein binding.[ ] Furthermore, the high affinity between quercetin and STAT3 is a crucial mechanism underlying its diverse biological activities. Quercetin modulates the phosphorylation, nuclear translocation, and transcriptional activity of STAT3 through both direct and indirect interactions. Research has demonstrated that quercetin can inhibit STAT3 phosphorylation at specific sites, such as Tyr705 and Ser727, effectively obstructing its signal transduction pathways.[ – ] Quercetin indirectly inhibits STAT3 activation by suppressing the activity of upstream kinases, such as JAK2 and SRC.[ , ] Simultaneously, quercetin activates the STAT3 signaling pathway, leading to the up-regulation of antioxidant enzyme expression (e.g., MnSOD, GSH), which reduces oxidative stress damage and protects vital organs, including the heart and liver.[ ] AKT1, a serine/threonine kinase, plays a crucial role in cell survival, proliferation, and metabolism. In the context of tuberculosis, AKT1 influences the survival of MTB and host defense mechanisms by regulating autophagy and immune responses in macrophages. Research has shown that increased AKT1 activity inhibits autophagy, thereby promoting MTB survival within macrophages.[ ] AKT1 and JUN interact within specific signaling pathways to jointly regulate the host immune response to MTB. For instance, the JNK signaling pathway serves as a crucial effector downstream of AKT1; the activation of AKT1 suppresses macrophage apoptosis and inflammatory responses by inhibiting JNK signaling, thereby promoting MTB survival.[ ] Additionally, JUN activity is also modulated by AKT1. During MTB infection, JUN influences the expression of immune factors such as IL-12B by regulating HDAC1 expression.[ ] The enrichment of pathways such as “Pathways in Cancer” and “Metabolic Pathways,” along with other nontraditional anti-PTB pathways in the KEGG database, offers new insights into MTB survival strategies and the mechanisms underlying YYQYD. Research indicates that MTB can exploit host cell proliferation signals and metabolic pathways to evade the immune response.[ – ] For example, MTB infection can activate cell proliferation signaling pathways, including EGFR/AKT/mTOR, to enhance its survival and reproduction within host cells.[ – ] YYQYD disrupts these bacterial survival strategies by modulating these pathways. Specifically, kaempferol binds to ESR1 to regulate the AGE-RAGE signaling pathway, thereby interfering with MTB’s “nutritional hijacking”of host glycolytic metabolism and disrupting the bacterial energy supply. This approach presents novel strategies for treating drug-resistant PTB. Notably, although the binding energy of GABA to core proteins does not reach the empirical threshold of strong binding (<−5 kcal/mol), this value is not an absolute standard. As a small molecule, it may exert its functions through specific polar interactions. Moreover, in the compound prescription, it can synergize with other high-affinity components, participating in the overall pharmacodynamic effect through auxiliary binding or regulating target conformations, and its specific role requires further experimental verification. On the other hand, the enrichment of “Pathways in Cancer” establishes an unexpected yet significant connection between PTB and cancer-related signaling. Emerging evidence indicates that MTB infection shares overlapping regulatory networks with cancer progression, particularly in pathways that govern cell survival, immune evasion, and chronic inflammation. For example, MTB can exploit cancer-associated pathways, such as PI3K-AKT and JAK-STAT, to inhibit macrophage apoptosis and promote intracellular persistence – strategies that are analogous to those employed by cancer cells to evade immune surveillance.[ , ] Furthermore, chronic granulomatous inflammation induced by MTB triggers sustained activation of NF-κB and other pro-inflammatory pathways, which not only support bacterial survival but also foster a microenvironment conducive to DNA damage and abnormal cell proliferation, thus mirroring the inflammatory basis of certain cancers.[ , ] Therefore, YYQYD’s modulation of cancer-related pathways may disrupt MTB’s survival niche while alleviating the long-term inflammatory sequelae of PTB, underscoring a multifaceted therapeutic potential that bridges infectious disease and chronic pathology regulation.
This study acknowledges certain limitations. Currently, it relies solely on theoretical predictions derived from network pharmacology and molecular docking, with no supporting in vitro or in vivo experimental validation. Future research should first incorporate cell models and animal experiments to verify the anti-PTB potential and underlying mechanisms of the active components in YYQYD, as well as to evaluate their safety profiles. Only after such foundational experimental evidence is obtained can subsequent exploration of the prescription’s potential for clinical application in PTB management be pursued – with the long-term goal of potentially providing new insights for developing TCM-based strategies in addressing PTB (including drug-resistant strains), and ultimately laying groundwork for the future clinical translation of TCM compounds in the anti-PTB field.

## 
5. Conclusions
This study systematically explores the potential mechanisms of TCM compound YYQYD in treating PTB via network pharmacology and molecular docking. Results show YYQYD’s active components (quercetin, GABA, luteolin, kaempferol, formononetin) may synergistically act on core targets (TP53, STAT3, AKT1, JUN, ESR1) through their networks, though these findings are predictive and require further in-depth experiments to validate feasibility. Future research should validate key components’ efficacy, clarify core targets’ regulatory mechanisms, and promote clinical translation. This work provides a theoretical basis for YYQYD’s modern research and clinical use, and insights for developing TCM-based PTB therapeutics.
