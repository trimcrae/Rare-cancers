Overall, this study reveals regulatory mechanism of FOXL2 on chicken ovulation at transcriptomic level. Our findings lay a foundation for further identifying key nodes regulating ovulation.

## Introduction
Birds ovulate in a progressive manner, which is different from periodical ovulation of eutherian mammals. Generally, a single small yellow follicle will be recruited almost on daily basis to join the preovulatory hierarchy follicles waiting to be expulsed during the peak egg-laying period of hens. Once recruitment occurs, the follicle will undergo no apoptosis and mature rapidly, accompanied by yolk accumulation and follicle volume increase ( ). Normal ovarian ovulation depends on the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis ( ). The hypothalamus regulates gonadotropin secretion from the pituitary gland by releasing gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH). Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) and luteinizing hormone (LH; also known as ovulation-inducing hormone, OIH) released by the pituitary gland are transported by the blood to the ovaries to promote the development and maturation of the ovaries ( ). Prior to ovulation, the pituitary gland secretes large amount of LH to form LH peaks, and these peaks are sensed by follicular granulosa cells, in turn activating the release of downstream ovulation-related signaling molecules regulating oocyte meiosis and follicular rupture and expulsion ( ). In addition, FSH secreted by the pituitary gland promotes cell proliferation and differentiation and progesterone (P4) synthesis in follicular granulosa cells, and FSH synergizes with LH to drive the ovulation ( ). Immediately after ovulation, the residual post-ovulation follicles (POF) exhibit a strong ability to secrete steroid hormones, but this ability will gradually regress with the degradation of the POF structure ( ). After follicles are removed or ligated on the first day after ovulation, egg-laying of the hens is delayed for about 1–7 days, which implies that follicular regression affects ovulation in chicken ( ). The post-ovulation follicles have been reported to be degraded through apoptosis in chicken ( ; ; ). However, the effects of the complex ovulation-inducing factors on biological processes and regulatory gene networks as well as apoptosis occurrence mechanism after ovulation remain largely unclear.
Granulosa cells (GCs) dominate follicular development, and their rapid proliferation and differentiation are essential for maintaining normal follicular function. Once follicle is selected as the preovulatory hierarchy follicle, FSH receptor expression in GCs increases significantly in response to FSH stimulation, thus inducing P4 synthesis, ultimately promoting follicular maturation ( ; ). Signaling regulation of GCs tightly links the processes of oocyte maturation and ovulation. The aberrant gene regulation in GCs will prevent mammalian follicles from maturation and rupture ( ; ). Immediately before ovulation, LH peaks can activate downstream signaling pathways in GCs to induce ovulation ( ). Undoubtedly, GCs play a very crucial role in inducing ovulation, and GC apoptosis will result in a decrease in egg production rate ( ). However, few reports on the involvement of GCs in the regulation of ovulation in poultry are available.
Forkhead box L2 (FOXL2) is a transcription factor specifically expressed in GCs, and it is regarded as a molecular marker of early ovarian differentiation, affecting morphological transformation, proliferation, and differentiation of GCs, hence influencing follicular maturation ( ; ; ; ; ; ). Foxl2 has been reported to regulate cell cycle, differentiation, and senescence of GCs ( ). In chicken, silencing FOXL2 promotes the proliferation and cell cycle progression and inhibits apoptosis of pre-ovulatory granulosa cells (PO-GCs) ( ). Moreover, FOXL2 regulates actin cytoskeleton recombination of GCs, thus affecting granulosa cell expansion ( ). Overall, FOXL2 has a wide range of roles and is required for function maintenance of GCs. Notably, FOXL2 is highly expressed during follicular development in chicken, and its expression increases with ovulation moment approaching, suggesting that FOXL2 may be involved in the regulation of ovulation ( ). We hypothesized that FOXL2 may regulate the physiological processes of granulosa cells to affect follicular development and ovulation in chicken. Nevertheless, the potential effects of FOXL2 on ovulation has rarely been studied.
In this study, we explored the molecular mechanisms underlying ovulation triggering, ovulation, and post-ovulation follicular degeneration through transcriptome sequencing in chicken. Based on ovulation RNA-seq, the reported FOXL2 ChIP-seq, and FOXL2 RNA-seq data, we investigated the potential function of FOXL2 in the ovulation. This study provides an insight into the mechanism by which FOXL2 gene regulates ovulation in chicken at transcriptomic level.

## Materials and methods

## Animals
Forty-five thirty-week-old Lohmann Pink laying hens were raised at the experimental chicken farm of Huazhong Agricultural University (Wuhan city, China). They were caged individually in standard pens with ad libitum access to water and maintained under a cycle of 16 h light (05:00–21:00): 8 h darkness. Each chicken was offered about 100 g feed in 3 feedings per day and numbered with a metal ring attached to its leg. The egg-laying events were observed continuously for about 20 days, and the egg-laying time was accurately recorded. Based on the above-mentioned observations and records, the optimum sampling time was determined as 22 h before ovulation, 3 h before ovulation, and ovulation moment. Given observations of pre-sampling, 20 min after egg-laying was defined as the next ovulation time. A total 10 follicles were collected from each group for the subsequent study. The animal study protocol was approved by the Ethics Committee of Laboratory Animal Centre, Huazhong Agriculture University (ID Number 202412120014).

## Sample collection
Hens were sacrificed by cervical dislocation, and then ovaries were immediately collected and placed into a cooled sterilized phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) solution. The adherent tissue was gently removed from the follicle surface with sterile forceps. Notably, the granulosa layer and theca layer adhered together should be retained on follicles as completely as possible when the yolk was gently removed from the follicles using forceps. Subsequently, the obtained follicle samples were put into cryopreserved tubes, and these tubes were rapidly stored into liquid nitrogen (−196 °C) for later use. For POF collection, fresh POF was cut from the ovarian stroma with sterile surgical scissors and quickly stored into liquid nitrogen (−196 °C), followed by stripping the loose connective tissue for preservation. The follicle which was ruptured but was not expelled yet was defined as post-ovulatory follicle. The POF tissue was collected and promptly placed in 4 % paraformaldehyde fixative for histological analysis.

## RNA-seq
Total RNA was purified to obtain mRNA using the oligo (dT) magnetic beads with Poly (A) tail, followed by heat fragmentation and cDNA synthesis from the RNA template. A total of 1 μg RNA was used as input material for the RNA-seq library construction. Totally, 18 cDNA libraries were constructed using NEB Next UltraTM RNA Library prep Kit for Illumina (NEB, USA).

## Quantitative real-time PCR (qPCR)
The total RNA was extracted from follicle tissues using the TRIzol method (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA). The final concentration and purity (OD260/280) of extracted RNA were determined using a NanoDrop 2000 instrument (NanoDrop Technologies), and the integrity of RNA was evaluated through agar-gel electrophoresis. After removing gDNA, RNA was reverse transcribed into cDNA using the prime script RT Master Mix (RK20402, ABclonal, Wuhan, China). The qPCR was conducted in the CFX-384 system (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA) with 2 × Universal SYBR green Fast qPCR Mix (RK21203, ABclonal, Wuhan, China). The 10 µL PCR system contained 5 µL of 2 × SYBR Green qPCR Master Mix, 0.2 µL of forward and reverse primers (see ) (10 µM), 1 µL cDNA (approximate concentration of 100 ng/µL) and 3.6 µL RNase-free ddH2O. PCR procedures mainly included 40 cycles of denaturing at 95 °C for 5 min, annealing at 60 °C for 30 s, and extension at 72 °C for 20 s. Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH) was used as internal control mRNA, and all the qPCR data were obtained using 3 biological replicates, each with 4 technical replicates. Before qPCR, primer specificity was assessed through 1.5 % agarose gel electrophoresis, and after qPCR, it was validated by analyzing the melting curve. The relative expression of genes was calculated by the 2−ΔΔCT method ( ).

## Preparation of paraffin section
The follicle samples were fixed by 4 % paraformaldehyde, and then cut into cube of 1 cm3. Next, these cubes were dehydrated with the gradient alcohol, permeabilized with xylene, and embedded in paraffin. The paraffin-embedded tissue blocks were then carefully trimmed and cut into sections with a thickness of 5 μm using a pathological microtome. Finally, the tissue sections were sequentially deparaffinized, rehydrated, and stained with hematoxylin and eosin.

## Bioinformatics analysis
After library quality control, paired-end sequencing (PE150) was performed on an Illumina HiSeqX-Ten platform. The adapters and low-quality sequences were removed using Fastp software to obtain clean data. The clean reads were aligned to the Ensembl Gallus_gallus-6.0 (GRCg6a) reference genome using HISAT2 software (http://ccb.jhu.edu/software/hisat2/index.shtml). The successfully aligned reads were assembled using featureCounts (1.5.0.p3) software. FPKM (Fragments per kilobase of transcript per Million fragments mapped) was introduced as an indicator to measure gene expression level ( ). The principal component analysis (PCA) of samples was performed using the DESeq in R package. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified using the DESeq2 with the thresholds of |log2(FoldChange)| ≥1 and Padj <0.05 (adjusted P value) ( ). The time-series gene expression analysis was performed with k-means clustering in R package MaSigPro (v 3.12). Gene Ontology (GO) functional annotation and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway enrichment analysis were performed using clusterProfiler. The protein-protein interaction (PPI) of DEGs was predicted based on STRING database ( ).

## Statistical analysis
Bioinformatics analyses and visualization of partial data were conducted using R software (Version 4.2.0), while GraphPad Prism 8 was employed for other data visualization. Paired t-test was used to test significance differences in LH hormone levels in the blood of hens before or after ovulation. Multiple mean comparisons of the mRNA expression level of FOXL2 were performed using one-way ANOVA analysis followed by LSD post hoc test. Data were expressed as mean ± SD, and p < 0.05 was considered as statistically significant.

## Results

## Morphology of chicken follicles after ovulation
LH surge determines ovulation in chicken. In reference to previous literature ( ), LH hormone levels were determined in the blood of hens at 22 h before ovulation, 3 h before ovulation, and ovulation phase (20 min after egg-laying). The result showed LH surge at ovulation moment ( A). F1 (the largest follicle) at ovulation, F1 at 22 h before ovulation, F1 at 3 h before ovulation, POF (post-ovulation follicle at the moment of follicle rupture), POF at 2 h after ovulation, and POF at 21 h after ovulation were collected and named F1, H22-F1, H3-F1, POF, H2-POF, and H21-POF, respectively ( B). After follicle sections in POF, H2-POF, and H21-POF groups were stained with hematoxylin-eosin (HE), the morphology of granulosa cells was observed. The result showed that the structure of follicle granulosa layer was damaged to some extent at ovulation phase. Then granulosa layer of POF gradually shed off with extended ovulation time, and it completely disappeared from POF at 21 h after ovulation. In addition to the impairment of granular layer, the remaining part of POF exhibited loose structure with the next follicle gradually approaching ovulation ( C). 

## RNA-seq of follicles before and after ovulation
To further investigate the regulation mechanism of the chicken ovulation, RNA-Seq was performed on the follicles collected at 6 different time points. A total of 346 million clean reads were obtained after removing adaptor and low-quality reads, and clean reads of each sample ranged from 18 million to 22 million ( ). The results of PCA and Pearson's correlation analysis showed that three biological replicates for each sample were clustered together, with the Pearson's correlation coefficients being above 0.9, indicating that the RNA-Seq data were reliable and qualified for subsequent analyses ( A–B). 

## Functional analysis of top 1 % genes
In order to investigate the functions performed by follicles in chicken ovulation period, the top 1 % genes in each follicle sample group were subjected to GO functional annotation. As shown in A, biological processes related to extracellular matrix structure and peptide and amide biosynthesis such as “extracellular structure organization”, “translation”, “peptide biosynthetic process”, and “amide biosynthetic process” were significantly enriched with top 1 % genes in each follicle group, suggesting that these biological processes were required throughout the whole ovulation. Additionally, top 1 % genes in POF group were specifically annotated to biological process GO terms related to the regulation of endopeptidase and hydrolase activity, implying that the activity increase of these enzymes in follicles promoted ovulation. In addition, multiple genes related to collagen (such as COL6A1, COL8A1, and COL12A1) and integrins (such as ITGA8, ITGB1, and ITGA6) were annotated to extracellular matrix-related process GO terms such as “biological adhesion” and “cell adhesion” ( B). In brief, the extracellular matrix components changed dramatically before and after ovulation, highlighting their important roles in ovulation. 

## Functional analysis of high-expression genes in ovulation
Further, time-series analysis (TSA) was performed to investigate the gene expression patterns at different ovulation phases. The TSA results showed the genes were clustered into three clusters (Cluster1–3) ( ), of which Cluster1 genes were highly expressed in F1 and H22-F1 groups before ovulation; Cluster 2 genes were highly expressed in H3-F1 and POF groups; and Cluster 3 genes were highly expressed in H2-POF and H21-POF groups ( A). 
GO Biological Processes (GO BPs) and KEGG pathway enrichment analyses of 3 gene clusters were performed, followed by construction of a PPI network. As shown B, biological processes (BPs) enriched by Cluster3 genes differed significantly from those enriched by Cluster1 and 2 genes. Some Cluster1 and Cluster2 genes were collectively annotated into such BPs as “Tissue development”, “cellular component morphogenesis”, and “anatomical structure morphogenesis”, suggesting that there was significant tissue and cellular morphogenesis during the pre-ovulation and ovulation phases in chicken. PPI analysis of proteins encoded by these genes showed pairwise interaction among BCL2, IL6, and BDNF and among BMPR1B, BMP2, and BMP4 ( C). Besides these commonly annotated BPs, some neuronal projection morphogenesis-related or actin cytoskeleton organization-related BPs were specifically annotated by Cluster1 genes, such as “neural crest cell development”, “neuron projection morphogenesis”, “actin cytoskeleton organization”, and “actin filament-based process” ( B). Among the proteins encoded by these genes, the cross-interaction was found among 9 proteins (CALD1, TLN1, CTNNA2, ACTN1, CFL2, ACTC1, TPM2, ACTA1, and DSTN) and pairwise interaction was identified between SEMA3E and ARF6 ( D). Cluster2 genes were specifically enriched in cell metabolism-related BPs such as “pigment metabolic process” and “positive regulation of cellular metabolic process”. Totally, 20 proteins coded by these genes (such as CIART, TEF, CRY1, NR1D2, and CLOCK) exhibited cross-interaction, of which 3 pairwise interactions were observed among PITX2, ISL1, and GATA4 ( E). Cluster3 genes were specifically enriched in peptide biosynthesis-related and chromatin assembly-related BPs such as “peptide biosynthetic process”, “posttranscriptional regulation of gene expression”, “histone modification”, “chromatin assembly or disassembly”, and “cellular amide metabolic process” ( B). A total of 36 proteins (such as PRL15, RPLP1, EEF1A1, and RPS12) exhibited cross-interaction.
Additionally, KEGG pathway enrichment analysis showed that Cluster1 genes were significantly enriched in such signaling pathways as steroidogenesis and TGF-beta signaling pathway ( G). Cluster2 genes were significantly enriched in pathways such as MAPK signaling pathway, gap junction, p53 signaling pathway, and regulation of actin cytoskeleton ( H). Cluster3 genes were significantly enriched in tissue degradation-related pathways such as ribosome, RNA degradation, and autophagy ( I).

## Molecular mechanisms of the ovulatory response
To explore the mechanisms of ovulation in chicken, gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) was performed on the expressed genes in F1 group and POF group. The background genesets were obtained from GO and KEGG databases of Gallus gallus, and the heatmap of gene expression in F1 group and POF group was visualized in A. 
GSEA-GO results showed that biological functions such as regulation of meiosis, positive regulation of cell communication, T cell differentiation, blood vessel development, cellular component morphogenesis, and production of molecular mediator of immune response were significantly up-regulated in the POF, compared to the F1 ( B). Notably, 18 core genes (CATH1, CD36, CGAS, EXO1, IL13RA2, IL7R, KIT, MALT1, NR4A3, TGFBR2, ANXA1, DUSP10, MALT1, NFKBIZ, RASGRP1, SHH, SYK, and TGFBR2) were significantly enriched in the BPs such as “production of molecular mediator of immune response” and “positive regulation of T cell differentiation”. Four genes (FBXO43, MOS, MSX1, and WEE2) were involved in the regulation of meiotic cell cycle. Sixty-one genes (such as ADGRB3, ANXA1, APOLD1, and AQP1) were involved in the vascular development. Seventy-four genes (such as ADGRB3, ALCAM, ANXA1, ARC, and ATP8A2) were involved in cellular component morphogenesis. GSEA-KEGG analysis showed that neuroactive ligand-receptor interactions, NOD-like receptor signaling pathway, MAPK signaling pathway, P53 signaling pathway, and Toll-like receptor signaling pathway were significantly up-regulated ( C), whereas oxidative phosphorylation, ribosome, lysosome, VEGF signaling pathway, and steroid biosynthesis pathways were significantly down-regulated ( D). Furthermore, 29 core genes (such as COX7A2, ATP5A1Z, and NDUFA1) were enriched in oxidative phosphorylation pathway; 6 core genes (CYP51A1, DHCR7, FDFT1, MSMO1, NSDHL, and SQLE) in steroid biosynthesis pathway, and 12 core genes (CASR, CATH1, CATH3, DEFB4A, GABARAPL1, IL6, IL8L2, ITPR2, NFKBIA, PANX1, TNFAIP3, and TRPM6) in Toll-like receptor signaling pathway.
A total of 328 DEGs were screened in the comparison of POF vs. F1, including 165 up-regulated DEGs and 163 down-regulated DEGs ( E). The DEGs were screened with the thresholds of |log2FC|≥1 and Padj<0.05. The PPI analysis of these DEGs revealed that 2 proteins (COX7B and COX6C) enriched in oxidative phosphorylation pathway interacted mutually with an interaction score of 0.99. Five proteins (CYP51A1, FDFT1, MSMO1, NSDHL, and SQLE) involved in steroidogenesis interacted mutually, of which the interaction between CYP51A1 and SQLE was strong with an interaction score of 0.989. There was a strong interaction between the ribosomal proteins (RPS29, RPS11, RPL36AL, and RPL15) and the RNA stability-related protein (IMP3) ( F).

## Bioinformatics analysis reveals potential function of FOXL2 in chicken ovulation
RNA-seq analysis above indicates that the whole ovulation involves dynamic changes in the extracellular matrix (ECM). Notably, FOXL2, which serves as an upstream transcriptional regulator of ECM genes, plays critical roles before ovulation ( ). To investigate whether FOXL2 gene is involved in the ovulation process in egg-laying hens, the mRNA expression level of FOXL2 was detected in follicle GCs before and after ovulation. The qPCR results showed that the mRNA expression level of FOXL2 showed an upward trend in the F1 follicle before ovulation, and the highest expression was observed at 3 h before ovulation, followed by a rapid decline ( A). Further, to explore the role of FOXL2 in the ovulation, genes regulated by FOXL2 were screened from the gene pool related to ovulation. 
The intersection of FOXL2 target genes (obtained from ChIP-seq) and ovulation-related genes was defined as geneSet1, and the intersection of genes significantly down-regulated by FOXL2 overexpression and significantly up-regulated by FOXL2 silencing and ovulatory-related genes was defined as geneSet2 ( B-C). It should be noted that the FOXL2 target genes based on ChIP-seq and RNA-seq were derived from the previous literature ( ; ; ). Further, the GO functional enrichment and KEGG pathway analyses of geneSet1 and geneSet2 were performed. The results showed that the genes in geneSet1 were significantly enriched in transcriptional regulation-related BPs such as “regulation of transcription from RNA polymerase Ⅱ promoter” and “negative regulation of transcription by competitive promoter” ( D-E), and the genes in geneSet2 were significantly enriched in cell proliferation-related and cell division-related BPs such as “mitotic checkpoint”, “DNA replication”, and “cell differentiation”, as well as the pathways related to cell cycle and senescence ( F-G). Based on these results, we speculated that FOXL2 might affect the structure of the extracellular matrix, in turn affecting cell cycle progression, and ultimately affecting ovulation by regulating its target genes.
Next, PPI analysis of the proteins encoded by geneSet1 and geneSet2 was performed to construct PPI network ( H-I). The results showed that in PPI network, there were a total of 72 proteins encoded by geneSet1 under the screening criterion of confidence >0.9, of which AARS2, MTO1, and TADA2B were pairwise interacted ( H); and there were a total of 80 proteins encoded by geneSet2, of which CCNA2, a well-known cell cycle regulatory protein, interacted with FOXM1, SKP2, PBK, and CDCA3, respectively ( I).

## Functional enrichment analysis of FOXL2 candidate genes in ovulation
To investigate the roles of FOXL2-regulated genes at different ovulation phases, TSA of the 314 FOXL2-regulated genes from the union of geneSet1 and geneSet2 genes was performed. As shown in , the genes in follicle samples collected at different time points were clustered into three clusters (C1–3, all of them were regulated by FOXL2) by k-means clustering method according to gene expression level. C1 contained 60 genes, which were highly expressed at 22 h before ovulation; C2 contained 134 genes, which were highly expressed at 3 h before ovulation and at follicle rupture in ovulation (POF); and C3 contained 120 genes, which were highly expressed at 2 and 21 after ovulation ( A). 
Further, GO enrichment and KEGG pathway analyses of C1–3 were performed. The results showed that the genes in C1 were significantly enriched in BPs related to DNA replication and cell cycle, mainly involving genes such as RRM1, MCM3, MCM10, MCM5, DTL, PRIM2, MCM3, MCM10, and MCM5; the genes in C2 were significantly enriched in BPs related to cell division, mitotic spindle assembly checkpoint, and meiotic sister chromatid condensation, primarily involving genes such as NDC80, MAD2L1, BUB1B, BUB1, RAD52, RAD51, ANLN, NUSAP1, and BIRC5; and the genes in C3 were significantly enriched in BPs related to extracellular matrix organization and positive regulation of immune response and immune response, mainly involving genes such as POSTN, MMP16, NDNF, RECK, PTGER4, IL8L1, REL, and CCL19 ( B–D). Based on gene function analysis results, we speculated that FOXL2 affected cell cycle and chromatin of follicular GCs before chicken ovulation and during ovulation and the regression of the extracellular matrix of the granulosa layer and the degradation of other structures of follicles after ovulation. The genes in C1 were significantly enriched in DNA replication, cell cycle, and purine metabolism-related pathways; the genes in C2 were significantly enriched in oocyte meiosis-related and actin cytoskeleton regulation-related pathways; and the genes in C3 were significantly enriched in pathways related to calcium signaling pathway, cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction, and metabolic enzymes and cell senescence ( E).
PPI analysis of the proteins encoded by C1–3 genes was performed. The results showed that at interaction confidence of > 0.9, there were a total of 11 proteins encoded by C1 genes in PPI network ( F); a total of 47 proteins encoded by C2 genes in PPI network, of which RAD52, RAD51, and EXO1 interacted mutually, and the cell cycle protein CCNA2 interacted with FOXM1, CDCA3, DLGAP5, CENPE and CENPF, respectively, and there are pairwise interactions among FANCB, FANCC, and FANCF (members of the protein family associated with DNA repair) ( G); and a total of 40 proteins encoded by C3 in PPI network, of which NFKB1-MAP3K8, NFKB1-REL, and NFKB1-IL8L1 interaction pairs were related to the inflammatory response ( H).

## Discussion
The follicles undergo various hormonal modulations, and in follicles cell morphology exhibited significant change before and after ovulation. To investigate the gene regulatory network during follicular ovulation, the morphological observations and comparative transcriptome analyses of chicken follicles before and after ovulation were performed in this study. We found that follicular granulosa layer was damaged during ovulation and gradually disappeared after ovulation, with a complete vanishing at 21 h after ovulation. This finding also drew our attention to the roles of granulosa layer in follicular ovulation. Moreover, during follicular regression process after ovulation in teleost fish and avian species, the granulosa layer of follicles are subjected to a loss of basement membrane integrity, mitochondrion apoptosis, and significant increase in endoplasmic reticulum stress-related gene and protein expression level ( ; ), which supports our speculation.
During follicular development, the follicular basement membrane surrounds the granulosa cells since the primordial follicles, and it is remodeled with the morphological change of granulosa layer, enabling the expansion of the follicular granulosa layer ( ; ). It has been reported that local remodeling of the stroma allows new capillaries to reach the granulosa layer from the follicular endomembrane, thereby transporting oxygen and nutrients required for follicular development as well as LH hormones secreted by the pituitary gland to the ovaries ( ; ). As ovulation approached, the basal layer separating the GC layer from the oocytes is disrupted to varying degrees, and the ECM at cell layer is damaged, thus weakening the follicular wall, eventually forming a stigma (an opening) on the top of follicle ( ). Our result showed that the biological process of “cellular component morphogenesis” was significantly upregulated at the onset of ovulation, supporting the important role of follicular cell component morphogenesis in the ovulation. However, among the 74 genes involved in this BP, which genes play a key role in ovulation requires further study in the future. Additionally, the biological process of “blood vessel development” was significantly up-regulated during ovulation in chicken. This phenomenon may be driven by two mechanisms. On one hand, the weak zone of follicular vessel ruptures to release the yolk and oocytes to promote successful ovulation. On the other hand, vasoconstriction at the follicular tip and on the follicular surface drives follicular expulsion ( ; ).
Immune response is a protective response of tissues against various pathogens, cell damage, and external stimuli ( ). Actually, it was proposed in mammals as early as 1980 that ovulation resembled immune response ( ). Moreover, research showed that several inflammatory- and immune-related cytokines, such as IL-1β and IL-6, and multiple chCXCLi family chemokines were significantly increased in ovulating follicles, and follicles exhibited significant immune cell infiltration and a significant increase in lymphocytes after ovulation, indicating follicular regression may be an inflammatory event in chicken ( ). Our results showed that the BPs of T cell differentiation and production of molecular mediator of immune response were significantly up-regulated compared to the largest follicle to be expelled (F1). Eighteen core genes (such as CD36, IL13RA2, and IL7R) were enriched in these two BPs, which was consistent with the aforementioned findings. Inflammation mediates ovulation, but excessive immune response will disrupt the normal follicular development. Immune response genes such as IL8, IL1B, and NOS2 have been reported to be highly expressed in GCs of female patients suffering from polycystic ovarian syndrome ( ). The leukocyte consistent invasion events to follicles and short-term excessive invasion may disrupt the normal ovulation, and dysregulation of inflammatory mediators may destruct the regular collaboration of different ovarian fractions, potentially ultimately resulting in ovulation failure ( ). Notably, follicular regression has been identified as a programmed process that occurs through cell apoptosis ( ). It can be speculated that the inflammatory response and programmed cell apoptosis driving follicle regression are complementary in follicular regression.
Granulosa cells can provide ions, metabolites, and amino acids for oocyte growth and development via the extracellular matrix ( ; ). FOXL2, a core transcription factor specifically expressed in follicular granulosa layer, has been reported to regulate the collagen gene COL4A1 in chicken preovulatory GCs ( ), and regulate Col1a2, thereby affecting the ovarian reserve depletion and ovarian fibrosis in mouse ( ). Consistent with previous reports, our screened FOXL2-regulated ovulation-related genes were significantly enriched in extracellular matrix signaling pathways. FOXL2 has been reported to affect the proliferation and apoptosis of GCs by regulating genes related to proliferation and apoptosis, and meanwhile it can directly regulate genes related to ECM ( ; ). Combining previously reported RNA-seq and ChIP-seq data of FOXL2 with our ovulation data, we speculated that FOXL2 may be involved in regulating the cell cycle and chromatin of follicular GCs before ovulation and during ovulation, and might affect ECM structure of granulosa layer, thus influencing follicular regression after ovulation. This speculation was partially suggested by our observation that FOXL2-regulated MMP16 was highly expressed in POF and was significantly enriched in immune response-related BPs, supporting that FOXL2 could regulate the immune response, thereby affecting the regression of POF after ovulation in chicken.

## Conclusions
In conclusion, our comparative transcriptome analysis of pre-ovulation and post-ovulation follicles revealed that the oxidative phosphorylation, steroidogenesis, ribosome and lysosome functions were all down-regulated during chicken ovulation, while plasma membrane architecture and cellular structure and morphology underwent major alterations, and immune response mediators and T cells were activated in response to ovulation. RNA-seq data and ChIP-seq data of FOXL2, and ovulation-related RNA-seq data jointly indicated that FOXL2 could regulate the cell cycle and chromatin of GCs before ovulation and during ovulation, and affect ECM structure after ovulation. This study provides important information and lays a theoretical foundation for identifying key factors and regulatory nodes impacting ovulation in chicken.
