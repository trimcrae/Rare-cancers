## Abstract

## Background
Intestinal inflammation and malignancy represent two critical pathological states in the gut that severely impair patients’ quality of life. Understanding their molecular mechanisms holds significant therapeutic implications. Lactate plays a key role in cellular signaling and immune regulation. Lactylation, a modification mediated by lactate, plays a key role in epigenetic regulation. Targeting lactate metabolism and lactylation has emerged as a promising intervention strategy for intestinal diseases.

## Main Body
This review summarizes the basic framework of the lactate metabolic system and the biological functions of lactate and lactylation, with a focus on the core mechanisms of lactylation in intestinal inflammation and malignancy. Lactylation exerts a context-dependent “paradoxical modulation” role. In intestinal inflammation, as exemplified by inflammatory bowel disease, lactylation drives macrophage phenotypic conversion, mediates gut microbiota-host interactions, regulates fibrosis progression, and modulates intestinal inflammation and tissue repair. Colorectal cancer, a major form of intestinal malignancy, is promoted by lactylation through mechanisms including immunosuppression, malignant proliferation, drug resistance, and tumor metastasis. Finally, we discuss the basis of the paradoxical modulation role of lactylation and explore the therapeutic potential of targeting lactate metabolism and lactylation as novel treatment strategies.

## Conclusions
In summary, this review highlights lactylation as a central player in intestinal diseases, providing insights into the pathomechanisms of intestinal inflammation and colorectal cancer. Lactate metabolism and lactylation hold significant potential as therapeutic targets for intestinal inflammation and malignancy, providing a promising path for precise intervention strategies in intestinal diseases.

## Introduction
Intestinal inflammation is one of the core pathological processes in digestive system diseases. Among these, inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) is a typical chronic, recurrent gastrointestinal inflammatory disorder, including crohn’s disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC), whose global incidence and prevalence [ ]. IBD has become a significant public health burden, severely impairing patients’ quality of life [ – ]. The pathogenesis of IBD involves factors such as genetic susceptibility, abnormal intestinal immune responses, dysbiosis, and impaired intestinal mucosal barrier function [ , ]. Colorectal cancer(CRC), one of the most representative types of intestinal malignancy, ranks as the third leading cause of cancer mortality worldwide, with over 1.85 million new cases and 850,000 deaths annually [ ]. Approximately 20% of patients present with metastatic disease at initial diagnosis, while an additional 25% of locally advanced cases ultimately progress to tumor metastasis, representing a significant public health burden [ ]. Similar to IBD, the development of CRC is driven by a combination of mechanisms, including genetic mutations, immune microenvironment dysregulation, and host-microbiota interactions [ , ]. Notably, IBD, as a representative chronic intestinal inflammatory disease, is one of the most significant risk factors for CRC [ ]. Despite a decline in CRC incidence in European countries over the past 30 years, IBD patients still face a higher risk of developing CRC compared to the healthy population [ ]. Persistent intestinal inflammation in IBD can repeatedly damage mucosal tissues, with some patients progressing to CRC over time [ ]. Currently, clinical management of both IBD and CRC faces significant challenges: IBD lacks effective treatments and exhibits frequent recurrence [ ]; CRC patients often confront issues such as drug resistance to chemotherapy and radiotherapy, drug toxicity, and tumor metastasis [ ]. Therefore, a deeper understanding of the molecular mechanisms linking intestinal inflammation to carcinogenesis is urgently needed to advance the treatment of intestinal diseases.
Lactate, a key metabolite of glycolysis, has long been underestimated for its biological significance. Since the nineteenth century, lactate was largely regarded as a metabolic waste product [ ]. The discovery of the Warburg effect—revealing that cancer cells rely on glycolysis for energy even under aerobic conditions, producing large amounts of lactate—changed the perception of lactate’s biological significance [ ]. Subsequent research further demonstrated that lactate plays a crucial role in metabolic reprogramming, cell fate determination, and immune evasion by regulating intracellular signaling pathways [ – ]. In 2019, Zhao et al. reported a novel post-translational modification (PTM) of proteins—lactylation [ ]. Early studies focused on histone lactylation, confirming that lactylation modulates diverse cellular activities such as gene transcription and inflammatory responses by altering histone spatial structure [ , ]. For instance, B cell adaptor protein(BCAP) drives the transition of inflammatory macrophages toward a reparative phenotype by promoting histone lactylation [ ]. Under pathological conditions, lactylation participate in cellular metabolic reprogramming, immune homeostasis regulation, and cell fate determination by modulating gene expression and signaling pathways, there by contributing to the onset and progression of various intestinal diseases. Taking CRC as an example, aldolase, fructose-bisphosphate B(ALDOB) promotes lactate production and secretion by activating the PDK1 pathway. This, in turn, enhances CEACAM6 stability through lactylation modification, driving CRC metabolic reprogramming, proliferation, and chemotherapy resistance [ ]. These studies reveal the potential therapeutic value of targeting lactate metabolism and lactylation modification in treating intestinal inflammation and CRC. However, targeted intervention strategies for lactylation remain exploratory, and specific therapeutic agents are currently lacking [ – ]. This review aims to illustrate the mechanisms by which lactate and lactylation modification influence intestinal pathophysiological processes. Due to the current research focus on IBD and CRC, we emphasize the regulatory mechanisms of lactylation modification in these two conditions to reveal its role in intestinal disease progression. We further discuss the therapeutic potential of targeting the lactylation pathway, providing theoretical foundations for novel therapeutic strategies based on restoring lactylation homeostasis in intestinal diseases.

## Lactate metabolism and its biological effects

## Lactate homeostasis
Lactate homeostasis relies on the dynamic balance between production and clearance as a central metabolic product in the body’s metabolic network. Lactate is primarily generated through glycolysis, while clearance occurs via two main pathways: oxidation for energy production through the mitochondrial tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle and conversion into glucose via gluconeogenesis. This precise regulation of production and clearance collectively maintains the dynamic balance of lactate within the body.

## Lactate production
Glycolysis is the primary pathway for lactate production. Glucose enters cells via glucose transporters and undergoes a series of enzymatic reactions to yield two molecules of pyruvate, meanwhile producing two molecules of ATP and two molecules of NADH [ ]. Under aerobic conditions, pyruvate is transported into mitochondria via the pyruvate carrier. It is converted into acetyl-CoA by pyruvate dehydrogenase (PDH), subsequently entering the TCA cycle for complete oxidation into CO₂ and H₂O [ ]. Under hypoxic conditions, however, pyruvate is not metabolized by PDH. Instead, it is converted into lactate by lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) in the cytoplasm [ ]. Notably, cancer cells have an alternative pathway for lactate production: glutamine is converted to glutamate by glutaminase, then undergoes glutamate dehydrogenase to generate α-ketoglutarate, entering the TCA cycle. This ultimately converts to oxaloacetic acid and is converted into malic acid. After being transported out of the mitochondria, the latter is converted into pyruvate by cytoplasmic malate dehydrogenase and ultimately reduced back to lactate by LDH [ , ].

## Lactate clearance
Intracellular lactate clearance is primarily mediated by PDH, which catalyzes the conversion of pyruvate to acetyl-CoA within mitochondria, allowing it to enter the TCA cycle and reducing the conversion of pyruvate to lactate in the cytoplasm [ ]. The mitochondrial lactate oxidase complex (mLOC) system also synergizes the clearance of lactate. The mLOC system consists of mitochondrial LDH, monocarboxylate transporters (MCTs), the chaperone protein CD147, and cytochrome oxidase [ ]. Cytoplasmic lactate can be oxidized to pyruvate by LDH, transported into mitochondria via MCTs, and ultimately oxidized in the TCA cycle [ , ]. Extracellular lactate is mainly cleared via the Cori cycle: the liver and skeletal muscle can uptake lactate from the bloodstream and reconvert it into glucose through gluconeogenesis [ ].

## Lactic acid metabolism in tumors: the warburg effect
The core feature of tumor metabolic reprogramming is the Warburg effect. It is characterized by cancer cells preferentially using the cytoplasmic glycolytic pathway for energy production even under oxygen-sufficient conditions, accompanied by lactic acid fermentation [ ]. As an efficient energy acquisition mechanism for tumor cells, the Warburg effect promotes intracellular lactic acid accumulation while supplying energy. This lactic acid buildup significantly accelerates tumor growth [ , ]. Notably, the Warburg effect is not exclusive to tumor cells. Activated immune cells, such as macrophages, dendritic cells, and T cells, also undergo similar metabolic reprogramming during immune responses. This enhanced glycolytic activity provides carbon precursors to produce inflammatory mediator, thereby establishing a direct link between metabolic reprogramming and the regulation of subsequent inflammatory processes [ ]. The molecular mechanisms primarily include: Upregulation of key glycolytic enzymes; Accelerating glucose conversion to pyruvate [ ]; Co-activation of oncogenes and inactivation of tumor suppressors synergistically enhance glycolysis [ ]; Lactic acid generated by the Warburg effect is transported via MCT into the tumor microenvironment(TME), acidifying TME, suppressing immune cell function, and degrading the extracellular matrix. These effects promote tumor invasion and metastasis [ ]. Tumor-associated macrophages and fibroblasts can also increase lactic acid accumulation through the “reverse Warburg effect” [ ]. For instance, hypoxia-inducible factor HIF-1 accumulates under hypoxic conditions, promoting glucose uptake and lactate efflux by upregulating membrane transporters such as GLUT1 and MCT-4 [ , ]. Additionally, other pathways can increase the accumulation of glycolytic intermediates [ , ].
Driven by the Warburg effect, disrupted intracellular and TME lactate homeostasis promotes tumor progression through multiple regulatory networks [ , ]. The glycolysis-H3K18la-TTK/BUB1B positive feedback loop exacerbates pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma development [ ]; In human bladder cancer, CircXRN2 suppresses H3K18la-driven tumor progression by activating the Hippo signaling pathway [ ]; High glycolytic activity in CRC correlates with poor prognosis and chemotherapy resistance [ ]; ALDOB enhances lactate production by activating PDK1, thereby increasing CEACAM6 stability through lactylation to drive the Warburg effect and chemotherapy resistance [ ]; Hypoxia promotes β-catenin lactylation by enhancing glycolysis, activating Wnt signaling and maintaining stemness in CRC [ ].

## Biological effects of lactate

## Lactate participation in cellular energy metabolism
Organisms obtain energy from glucose via oxidative phosphorylation and glycolysis pathways, which begin with the breakdown of glucose into pyruvate [ ]. In the oxidative phosphorylation, pyruvate enters mitochondria to participate in the TCA cycle. Instead, it is directly reduced to lactate in glycolysis [ ]. Research confirms that lactate serves multiple functions: it acts as a precursor for gluconeogenesis to synthesize glucose for energy [ ]; maintains brain excitatory activity—including neuronal function and synaptic transmission—during glucose deprivation [ – ]; functions as a signaling molecule to induce cellular metabolic reprogramming [ , ]; and regulates lipid metabolism under pathological conditions [ , ].

## Lactate shuttle and lactate signaling
Functioning as a key link between glycolysis and oxidative metabolism, the lactate shuttle system is composed of proton-dependent MCTs and sodium-coupled monocarboxylate transporters (SMCTs) [ , , ]. Through stereospecific mechanisms, it mediates the transmembrane transport of metabolites such as lactate, pyruvate, and ketone bodies [ ]. Based on the direction of lactate transport, cells expressing MCT can be classified as “driver cells” and “receptor cells.” Cells with high glycolysis that export lactate via MCT are termed “driver cells,” while “receptor cells” are those that import lactate [ , ]. The former refers to cells with high glycolytic activity that export lactate via MCT4, while the latter import lactate via MCT1 [ ]. The expression of the MCT family exhibits significant tissue specificity: MCT1 is widely distributed in the intestine and muscle tissue to support energy metabolism; MCT1, 2, and 4 are overexpressed in malignant tumors; MCT3 is specifically expressed in retinal pigment epithelial cells [ – ].
Based on the lactate shuttle system, lactate modulates the immune microenvironment through the G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) family, thereby influencing disease progression. GPR81 (HCAR1) exhibits multifaceted pro-tumor functions in the TME, such as inducing pro-angiogenic factors, downregulating dendritic cell MHC-II molecules and type I interferon production, promoting programmed death ligand 1(PD-L1) expression to mediate immune escape, and enhancing chemotherapy resistance [ – ]. Notable, inhibiting GPR81 function restores CD8+ T cell anti-tumor activity. GPR132 is widely distributed in human immune tissues (e.g., macrophages) and the gut [ , ]. It mediates lactate-induced M2 polarization of macrophages, promoting cancer cell migration and invasion [ , ]. In CRC, downregulation of GPR132 attenuates lactate-induced suppression of NK cell function and enhances the CRC-killing function of CAR-NK cells [ ]. GPR37 promotes LDHA expression and lactate production via the Hippo pathway, enhancing H3K18-lactylated-dependent CXCL1/5 secretion to drive CRC liver metastasis [ ].

## Lactylation

## Functions of lactylation
Lactylation modulates immune cell function dynamically through epigenetic reprogramming, influencing inflammatory responses [ ]. In macrophages, BCAP drives aerobic glycolysis via TLR signaling, promoting lactate production and histone lactylation. This modification upregulates repair genes such as Arg1 and Klf4, facilitating the shift from M1 pro-inflammatory to M2 reparative phenotypes [ , ]. Probiotic-derived lactate alleviates intestinal inflammation by inhibiting NLRP3 inflammasome activation through increased H3K18la [ ]. In sepsis, lactate induces HMGB1 lactylation, disrupting endothelial barrier integrity via exosome secretion and exacerbating systemic inflammatory responses [ ]. In regulatory T cells (Tregs), lactate activates TGF-β signaling by inducing MOESIN lactylation, enhancing immunosuppressive functions [ ].
Through histone lactylation, lactate activates pro-fibrotic gene expression, playing a key role in the fibrotic process [ ]. In a bleomycin-induced pulmonary fibrosis model, alveolar macrophages taking up lactate showed a significant increase in H3K18la at the promoter regions of pro-fibrotic genes [ ]. In the process of hepatic fibrosis, activated hepatic stellate cells exhibit enhanced glycolysis, with H3K18la specifically enriched in the promoter regions of α-SMA and COL1A1, activating their transcription [ ]. In intestinal fibrosis, dual activation of GCGR/GLP1R effectively blocks epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) by inhibiting histone H3K9la, thereby alleviating fibrosis progression [ ].
In malignant tumor, lactylation modification downregulates tumor suppressor gene expression [ ] and drives CRC metastasis [ , ]. In TME, lactylation induces the polarization of tumor-associated macrophages and activates Tregs, jointly establishing an immunosuppressive environment that ultimately promotes tumor immune escape [ , , ].
In recent years, research on non-histone lactylation has significantly advanced. By regulating key pathways such as glycolysis, it influences cellular metabolic phenotypes, promotes lactate accumulation and lactylation [ – ], thereby modulating pathological processes including intestinal inflammation and malignancy [ – ].

## Lactylation regulation mechanisms
Lactate-mediated regulation occurs via both enzymatic and non-enzymatic pathways. The enzymatic pathway depends on lactyl-CoA formation, while the non-enzymatic pathway involves the metabolism of methylglyoxal (MGO), a glycolysis byproduct [ ]. Due to the chirality of carbon atoms in the human body, L-lactate is the predominant form of lactate [ ]. It mediates enzymatic lactylation, whereas D-lactylation is mainly mediated by the non-enzymatic pathway [ , ].

## Enzymatic regulatory pathways: “Writers”, “Erasers”, and “Readers”
The enzymatic regulation pathway of lactylation is mediated by “writers”, “erasers”, and “readers”. As shown in Fig. , when intracellular lactate accumulation exceeds a threshold (determined by cellular metabolic state and lactylation enzyme activity) [ , ], L-lactate is converted to lactyl-CoA, which transfers a lactyl group to lysine residues via CoA transferase [ , ]This system involves the directed transfer and clearance of lactyl groups, with its regulatory network exhibiting significant spatiotemporal specificity and pathology dependence [ , ].
 As a key “writer”, the classical acyltransferase p300/CREB-binding protein (CBP) has been demonstrated to possess histone lactyl-transferase activity in macrophages, playing a crucial catalytic role in mediating histone H3/H4 lactylation [ , – ]. The lactyl-transferase activity is regulated by GPR81 lactate signaling mediated [ ]. GCN5, KAT8, AARS1-2, and TIP60 have also been demonstrated to possess “writer” functions, suggesting a multi-enzyme interaction mechanism for lactylation modification [ , – ].
The histone deacetylase system(“erasers”) is mainly consists of silent information regulator proteins (SIRT1-3) and histone deacetylases (HDAC1-3) [ , ]. SIRT1-3 and HDAC1-3 reduce histone deacetylation levels, with HDAC3 being the most potent deacetylase [ ]. For example, SIRT3 specifically delactylates H4K16la to inhibit cancer cell cycle progression, which closely linked to the regulation of cyclin E2 activity [ , ]. However, HDAC inhibitor treatment in liver fibrosis reduces H3K18la levels paradoxically, indicating HDACs may exhibit atypical functions: pro-lactylating in certain pathological stages [ , ]. The recognition mechanisms of “erasers” require further elucidation.
“Readers” specifically recognize lactylation sites [ ]. Although preliminary studies have identified “readers”, their molecular mechanisms require further research [ ]. In CRC, NSUN2 and YBX1 function as the “writer” and “reader” of ENO1, enhancing lactate production via m5C-dependent pathways. Accumulated lactate further activates NSUN2 transcription through histone H3K18la modification and induces lactylation at its K356 site, improving its RNA-binding capacity. This establishes a “lactate accumulation-lactylation formation” positive feedback loop [ ]. Fig. illustrates several known writer, eraser, and reader enzymes involved in lactylation. Research on reader enzymes is essential for further understanding the epigenetic regulatory network, clarifying the relationship between epigenetics and chromatin, and understanding the maintenance mechanisms of epigenetic marks. This knowledge provides a theoretical foundation for targeting lactylation signaling, developing lactate metabolism inhibitors and epigenetic modulators, ultimately contributing to a better prognosis in intestinal inflammation and malignancy [ ].

## Non-enzymatic regulatory pathways
In the human body, L-lactate is main form of lactate. It is produced via the reduction of pyruvate catalyzed by LDH and participates in enzymatic lactylation [ ]. In contrast, in prokaryotes, D-lactate is the main form of lactate. While L-lactate metabolism occurs, it does not predominate [ ]. In humans, D-lactate is mainly generated by gut microbes through glucose metabolism. Following intestinal absorption into systemic circulation, it contributes to non-enzymatic lactylation and plays a key role in the progression of diseases associated with gut microbiota imbalance [ ]. As a glycosis byproduct, MGO reacts with glutathione catalyzed by glyoxalase 1 (GLO1) to form lactylglutathione (LGSH). LGSH induces D-lactylation modification in proteins by transferring a lactyl group onto lysine residues [ , , ]. This modification is significantly enriched in key glycolytic enzymes, implying its involvement in the self-regulation of the glycolytic [ ]. Knockout of glyoxalase 2 (GLO2) leads to LGSH accumulation and elevated lactylation levels, indicating that the glyoxalase system negatively regulates non-enzymatic lactylation. This provides a potential strategy for targeted intervention in metabolic diseases such as cancer [ ].

## Cross-talk between lactylation and other PTMs
Beyond the central role in PTM networks, histone lactylation also interact intricately with other PTMs, jointly modulating diverse pathological pathways [ ]. Lactate serves as a carbon source for histone acetylation, and lactate treatment or LDH inhibition affecting intracellular acetylation levels respectively [ ]. Histone lactylation and acetylation share key modifying enzymes, including “writers” (p300) and “erasers” (HDAC1-3). These modifications co-localize within the genome and exhibit competitive relationships [ , ]. In vascular smooth muscle cells, TNF-α induces Sox10 lactylation via PI3K/AKT signaling, with its phosphorylation-dependent mechanism driving vascular smooth muscle cell transdifferentiation [ ]. In melanoma, H3K18 lactylation downregulates PER1/TP53 mRNA levels through the m6A-YTHDF1 axis while enhancing Methyl transferase-like 3 (METTL3)-mediated m6A methylation of JAK1 mRNA [ , ]. Methyl-CpG-binding protein 2 (MECP2) lactylation regulates the MAPK signaling pathway by altering epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) phosphorylation levels [ ]. In prostate cancer, blocking lactate-mediated crosstalk between cancer cells and tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) achieves durable androgen deprivation therapy-independent tumor control in PTEN/p53-deficient aggressive variant prostate cancer [ ].

## Lactate, lactylation, and intestinal inflammation
As a key regulatory node in metabolism and epigenetics, lactylation plays a central role in the pathological progression and outcomes of IBD [ – ]. By bridging cellular metabolic reprogramming with epigenetic remodeling, lactylation participates in key pathological processes such as intestinal immune homeostasis disruption, microbiota-host interactions, and tissue fibrosis. These processes are closely associated with colitis-related tumorigenesis [ , ]. These findings provide new insights into IBD pathogenesis and reveal potential targeted intervention strategies. ## Intestinal inflammation and immune regulation
Lactylation promotes the phenotypic transition of macrophages from pro-inflammatory (M1) to reparative (M2) phenotypes, which plays a key role in resolving intestinal inflammation and restoring immune homeostasis in IBD [ , ]. Research indicates that this conversion is regulated by BCAP. BCAP enhances histone lactylation levels by promoting aerobic glycolysis and lactate production mediated by PI3K, ultimately driving the shift from proinflammatory to reparative macrophage phenotypes. BCAP deficiency leads to persistent intestinal inflammation and impaired tissue repair through sustained activation of FOXO1 and GSK3β. This process is accompanied by defective aerobic glycolysis, reduced lactate production, and decreased histone lactylation, ultimately inhibiting repair-related gene expression [ ]. Similarly, TAK-242 enhances histone lactylation by inhibiting TLR4 signaling, promoting M2 reparative macrophage differentiation, thereby improving mucosal healing in UC [ ]. Furthermore, engineered lactate-producing probiotic Saccharomyces cerevisiae modulates macrophage polarization and alleviates sodium dextran sulfate (DSS)-induced UC by enhancing macrophage MCT-mediated lactate uptake, inhibiting NLRP3 inflammasome and caspase-1 pathway activation, and upregulating histone H3K18 lactylation and H3K9 acetylation levels [ ]. The traditional Chinese medicine compound Gelgenqinlian Decoction alleviates UC by inhibiting lactate production and LDH activity, reducing pan-lysine lactylation levels and lactylation expression at specific sites (H3K18la, H3K23la, H4K8la, and H4K12la), thereby suppressing M1 macrophage polarization and proinflammatory factor release. Notably, this effect is reversible with exogenous lactate [ ]. Table illustrates the regulation mechanisms of macrophage phenotype transformation mediated by lactylation.
 Beyond regulating macrophage polarization, lactylation reshapes the IBD intestinal immune microenvironment by driving Th17/Tregs phenotypic conversion. In Th17 cells, lactate induces genome-wide histone H3K18 lactylation, triggering metabolic and epigenetic reprogramming that significantly reduces IL-17A production and upregulates Foxp3 expression. This ROS-dependent mechanism of IL-2 secretion reduction transforms the pro-inflammatory Th17 phenotype into immunoregulatory Tregs. Notably, high lactate concentrations inhibit Th17 pathogenicity in animal models [ ].
Furthermore, lactylation is closely associated with inflammation-related carcinogenesis. METTL3-mediated m6A methylation enhances Kcnk6 mRNA stability in a YTHDF2-dependent manner, while histone lactylation further activates the YTHDF2/Kcnk6 transcriptional axis, promoting NLRP3 inflammasome activation and colitis-associated tumor progression. In contrast, Kcnk6 deficiency restores intestinal homeostasis and suppresses malignancy [ ].
In conclusion, lactylation plays a protective role in the progression of intestinal inflammation within the gut immune microenvironment. Current studies have demonstrated that lactate can induce lactylation in certain intestinal immune cells, driving them from a pro-inflammatory to an anti-inflammatory phenotype, thereby reducing the release of inflammatory factors and promoting mucosal repair. Research targeting H3K18la has provided an intervention point for the anti-inflammatory effects of lactylation; however, the specific signaling pathways of lactylation remain unclear in most studies [ ]. Notably, exogenous lactate can reverse the protective effects mediated by low lactylation levels [ , ]. Future research should focus on the precise mechanisms of phenotypic switching regulated by lactylation in immune cells. Additionally, it is essential to clarify how lactate concentration influences immune cell phenotype transformation, particularly whether a clear concentration threshold exists that separates pro-repair low levels from pro-inflammatory high levels. This will clarify interaction between different immune cells and the intestinal lactate microenvironment.

## Gut microbiota
Microbiota-derived lactate and lactylation interact with the host immune system and epigenetic regulation through their metabolites, playing a key role in the pathological regulation of IBD [ , , ]. The degradation of 17β-estradiol by the human gut fungus Aspergillus niger RG13B1 involves methylation, acetylation, and lactylation reactions. Its metabolites suppress the expression of inflammatory genes and proteins by interacting with NF-κB p65 [ ]. In animal models, engineered Saccharomyces cerevisiae directly converts glucose into lactate via an efficient lactate biosynthesis pathway. This process alleviates colonic histological damage, enhances mucosal barrier function, and downregulates pro-inflammatory cytokine expression [ ]. On the whole, gut microbiota exert anti-inflammatory effects by mediating lactylation, provide a reparative foundation for mucosal healing in IBD. This highlights the potential of targeting this pathway for improving disease progression and prognosis. However, the regulatory mechanisms of microbial lactate dynamics, as well as the specific molecular targets that mediate host lactylation-dependent phenotypic changes, remain poorly understood and require further investigation [ ].

## Intestinal fibrosis
Intestinal fibrosis is a significant pathological outcome of chronic inflammation in IBD results from abnormal activation of fibroblasts, which is driven by lactylation and extracellular matrix remodeling, indicating that lactate metabolism plays a crucial regulatory role in fibrosis [ ]. Studies in IBD patients and animal models shows that lactate and lactylation accelerate intestinal fibrosis progression. The mechanism involves significantly downregulated expression of glucagon receptor (GCGR) and glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor (GLP1R) in the colons. This leads to lactate accumulation, which promotes EMT through histone H3K9 lactylation, ultimately promoting intestinal fibrosis. The dual receptor agonist peptide 1907B, by simultaneously activating the GCGR/GLP1R pathway, reduces H3K9 lactylation levels and improves fibrosis, suggesting its potential as an anti-fibrotic therapeutic strategy for IBD fibrosis [ ].

## Lactate, lactylation, and CRC
Beyond the previously described abnormal intestinal lactate metabolism and lactylation-mediated dynamic regulatory network, CRC progression involves several specific mechanisms: metabolic reprogramming mediated by lactate homeostasis disruption, dysregulation of TME immune homeostasis, and lactate-mediated regulation of cell proliferation and fate determination. These mechanisms collectively drive the malignant phenotype in CRC by integrating metabolic and epigenetic signals, forming key molecular hubs linking lactate metabolism to oncogene expression [ – ].

## Tumor lactate homeostasis dysregulation
Disrupted lactate homeostasis influences the activity of key enzymes, mediating lactylation and downstream signaling, creating a metabolic-epigenetic interaction network that collectively promotes malignant phenotype in CRC [ ]. Table shows the mechanisms linking lactate metabolic dysregulation to CRC progression. Lactate promotes metabolic gene rearrangements to enhance glucose uptake in tumor cells [ ]. Simultaneously, it inhibits glycolysis and promotes the TCA cycle, thereby reshaping tumor cell energy metabolism patterns [ , ]. Malic enzyme 2 (ME2) K352la enhances its enzymatic activity to promote CRC progression; conversely, SIRT3-mediated de-lactylation at this site inhibits tumor growth by disrupting redox homeostasis [ ]. PFKP K688 lactylation directly suppresses its enzyme activity, establishing negative feedback loop on lactate production [ ]. The downregulation of tsRNA-08614 inhibits EFHD2 transcriptional activity by suppressing ALDH1A3 expression, reducing glycolysis and decreasing H3K18 lactylation, thereby enhancing CRC cell sensitivity to oxaliplatin chemotherapy [ ]. H3K18 lactylation promotes glycolysis via AMP-activated protein kinase by enhancing transcription of the oncogene GOLPH3, thereby driving CRC progression and inducing radiation resistance [ ]. Interestingly, lactylation also contributes to non-genetic mechanisms of chemotherapy resistance in CRC. SMC4 depletion enhances glycolytic enzyme expression and increases lactate production. High lactate concentrations upregulate ABC transporter expression via histone lactylation, rendering tumor cells insensitive to chemotherapy. Meanwhile, the co-loss of SMC4 and PGAM1 inhibits cytokinesis, leading to polyploidy formation and reduced proliferation. These effects collectively establishing a non-genetic mechanism of chemotherapy resistance [ ]. In brief, lactate acts not only as a signaling molecule that reprograms tumor glycolysis to drive progression, but also facilitates lactylation to promote tumor proliferation and drug resistance. Future studies should focus on exploring the distribution and transport mechanisms of lactate within the tumor microenvironment, as well as identifying key molecules that regulate downstream lactylation. Such insights will help maintain lactate homeostasis in tumors, ultimately reducing the contribution of dysregulated lactylation to CRC progression [ ].
 

## TME regulation
In the TME of CRC, lactate-mediated epigenetic reprogramming significantly suppresses macrophage antitumor activity by establishing an immunosuppressive microenvironment [ , ]. Fig. shows how lactylation affects CRC progression by regulating macrophage and T cell function. Lactate in the TME inhibits macrophage RARγ transcription through H3K18 lactylation, activating the TRAF6–IL6–STAT3 axis to promote tumorigenesis [ ]. PCSK9 promotes tumor metastasis by regulating EMT and PI3K/AKT signaling, while inhibiting M2 macrophage polarization by reducing lactate levels [ ]. H3K18la in CRC cells upregulates METTL3 expression, which enhances METTL3-mediated RNA binding through the m6A-YTHDF1 axis to enhance JAK1–STAT3 signaling, and lactylation of the METTL3 zinc finger domain enhances its RNA-binding capacity, collectively reinforcing immunosuppressive functions [ ]. METTL1 mediates PKM mRNA m7G modification to promote PKM2 expression, increasing glycolysis and H3K9 lactylation, forming a self-activating loop and upregulating CD155 to induce immune escape [ ].
 Lactylation modifies and induces immune evasion by mediating cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) dysfunction in the TME [ ]. Lactylation of tumor cell PD-L1 impairs antitumor responses of CTL, and blocking PD-1/PD-L1 signaling restores the function of serine/glycine-deficient diet (−SG diet)-recruited CD8+ T cells [ ]. Mutant KRAS (KRASMUT) promotes transcription of circular RNA circATXN7 via lactylation [ ]. circATXN7 sequesters NF-κB p65 in the cytoplasm by binding to its p65 subunit and masking its nuclear localization signal motif, thereby enhancing tumor-specific CTL sensitivity to activation-induced cell death (AICD) and inducing immune escape [ , ]. Clinical study indicate that high circATXN7 levels in tumor-specific CTLs correlates with poor prognosis and immunotherapy resistance, while the ablation of circATXN7 enhances anti-PD1 efficacy [ , ]. Concurrently, tumor-associated microbiota in colorectal liver metastases enhance lactate production, inducing lactylation of retinoic acid-inducible gene 1 (RIG-1). This inhibits NF-κB recruitment to macrophage NLRP3 promoters, reducing NLRP3 transcription. Consequently, CD8+ T cell function is impaired, while Tregs’ immunosuppressive activity is amplified [ , ]. Notably, it is possible to target interventions such as circATXN7 knockout or RIG-I lactylation inhibition effectively to restore T cell antitumor function [ , ].
Lactylation drives tumor stroma remodeling by activating inflammatory pathways and inducing fibroblast phenotypic conversion, which are linked to poor clinical prognosis [ ]. METTL3-mediated Kcnk6 m6A modification activates NLRP3 inflammasomes, with histone lactylation as a critical regulatory step in this pathway [ ]. Single-cell transcriptomic analysis reveals lactylation participates in fibrosis by promoting fibroblast transformation into cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs), potentially linking to stromal remodeling observed in intestinal fibrosis. Lactylation-associated fibroblast subclusters such as FB_2_CALD1, FB_3_TPM4, and FB_4_AHNAK are associated with poor clinical outcomes [ ]. A lactylation gene signature, including CALB2, effectively predicts CRC prognosis and immunotherapy response [ ].
To sum up, lactylation promotes CRC progression by reshaping the TME into an immunosuppressive state, which represents a significant risk factor [ ]. Specifically, lactylation suppresses the anti-tumor activity of macrophages, impairs CTL dysfunction, and induces phenotypic transformation of fibroblasts. These effects establish an immunosuppressive TME that enhances immune evasion and leads to poor prognosis. PD-1/PD-L1, a core therapeutic target in gastrointestinal cancers, has shown initial potential in reversing lactylation-mediated immunosuppression to treat tumors such as hepatocellular carcinoma and CRC [ , ]. However, the TME is an interactive network involving immune cells, stromal cells, inflammatory factors, and other components. The lactylation-related crosstalk within this network remains poorly understood and requires systematic exploration [ ]. Therefore, the future studies should focus on elucidating the mechanisms of lactylation cross-talk among various cellular components in the TME. Investigating how lactylation modulates immunosuppression by targeting key regulators such as PD-1/PD-L1 and H3K9 will deepen our understanding of its role in CRC progression.

## Tumor-microbiota interactions
Metabolites from gut microbiota dysregulate immune cell function by modulating host epigenetic reprogramming, thereby influencing CRC progression [ , ]. Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) from Salmonella typhimurium SL1344 induces the long non-coding RNA LINC00152 expression in human colonic cells. This mechanism involves LPS-mediated histone lactylation in the LINC00152 promoter region, which reduces the binding of the repressor protein YY1, thereby promoting CRC invasion and migration [ ]. During the biotransformation of capsaicinoids, the human gut fungus Rhizopus oryzae R2701 primarily produces hydroxylated and lactylated metabolites [ , ]. Among these, the hydroxyl-lactylated metabolite significantly inhibits lysine-specific demethylase 1 (LSD1) activity, suggesting its potential as an LSD1 inhibitor in cancer therapy [ , ]. Notably, current research has focused on L-lactate-mediated enzymatic lactylation in humans, while D-lactylation derived from microbiota remains poorly understood [ ]. Clarifying D-lactylation is essential for understanding the lactylation regulation in gut microbiota and developing therapies targeting microbial lactylation homeostasis in CRC.

## Tumor cell proliferation and fate determination
Lactylation modifies cellular proliferation and fate decisions, influencing tumor drug resistance and driving CRC progression [ , ]. Fig. summarizes the role of lactylation in modulating the tumor microenvironment, thereby directing CRC cell fate. Hypoxia-induced β-catenin lactylation enhances protein stability and promotes cell proliferation by activating the Wnt signaling pathway [ ]. As a pan-lactotransferase, KAT8 catalyzes lactylation of eEF1A2 at K408, significantly increasing protein synthesis rates to meet the metabolic demands of rapidly growing tumors [ ]. ALDOB activates lactate production via PDK1, subsequently enhancing CEACAM6 stability through LDHB to drive cell proliferation and chemotherapy resistance [ ]. Notably, lactylation also regulates tumor cell ferroptosis phenotypes in CRC. HDAC1 K412la confers CRC ferroptosis resistance by maintaining FSP1 mRNA stability, while HDAC inhibitors reverse this modification to enhance treatment sensitivity [ ]. Histone H4K12 lactylation inhibits ferroptosis by upregulating GCLC, enhancing CRC stem cell chemotherapy resistance [ ]. PRMT5 K240 lactylation enhances CRC ferroptosis resistance via the ALKBH5/SLC7A11 axis, promoting tumor progression [ ].
Recent research reveals that lactylation plays a key role in CRC liver metastasis [ ](Table ). GPR37 enhances LDHA expression by activating the Hippo pathway, promoting glycolysis and H3K18 lactylation, and upregulating CXCL1/CXCL5 to induce neutrophil recruitment, significantly accelerating liver metastasis [ ]. Long noncoding RNA USP3–AS1 drives CRC liver metastasis by promoting H3K18 acetylation and CDC27 expression in CRC through the MYC-glycolysis regulatory axis [ ]. In CRC, Escherichia coli inhibits NF-κB recruitment to the Nlrp3 promoter in macrophages by inducing RIG-1 histone acetylation, thereby reducing its transcription. This affects the immunosuppressive activity of Tregs and the antitumor activity of CD8+ T cells, promoting liver metastasis [ ]. Escherichia coli promotes tumor invasion and migration via LPS by inducing LINC00152 promoter deacetylation to inhibit YY1 binding [ ].
In summary, lactylation contributes to poorer CRC prognosis by driving tumor cell proliferation, chemotherapy resistance, and distant metastasis. Future studies should focus on how lactylation regulates cell fate and to develop lactylation-targeted ferroptosis inducers. These efforts may offer novel strategies for managing CRC [ ].

## Unraveling the paradox: context dependent outcomes of lactylation
Lactate and lactylation exhibit a notable “paradoxical modulation” effect in the progression of intestinal inflammation and malignancy. In intestinal inflammation, they alleviate disease by modulating immune homeostasis, suppressing inflammatory cytokine release, and promoting mucosal repair; whereas in intestinal malignancy, they drive tumor progression by remodeling the metabolic and immune microenvironment, inhibiting cellular immunity, and inducing immune escape. This context dependent functional modulation is primarily mediated by differences in lactylation modification sites, immune cell phenotype regulation, and cytokine distribution character. Based on current research, we summarize the mechanisms underlying this context-dependent modulation below.
The context-dependence of specific lactylation site serves as the central role mediating the paradox. The same lactylation site shows different functions in inflammatory versus neoplastic disease contexts. Notably, lactylation sites including H3K9, H3K18, H4K8, and H4K12 exhibits the most pronounced context-dependent effects; the specific functional differences are detailed in Tables and . Overall, in the context of intestinal inflammation, lactylation at these sites can promote anti-inflammatory effect and mucosal repair by inducing M2 macrophage polarization, suppressing the release of intestinal inflammatory factors, and upregulating repair‑related gene expression [ , , – ]. In contrast, in intestinal malignancy, the context‑dependent modulation demonstrates opposing pro‑tumor effect: driving malignant progression by inhibiting tumor cell ferroptosis, inducing immune escape, enhancing chemotherapy resistance, and promoting hepatic metastasis [ , , , , , , ].
Lactylation also serves as a key role mediating the paradoxical modulation effects on immune cell phenotypes and cytokine profiles. In the intestinal inflammatory environment, lactylation promotes M2 macrophage polarization, reduces the release of proinflammatory cytokines such as IL-1, IL-6, and TNFα, and upregulates the expression of anti-inflammatory factors including IL10 and TGFβ. Furthermore, it contributes to the repair of the intestinal mucosal epithelium and maintains the homeostasis of the immune microenvironment [ , , , ]. Additionally, lactylation modulates the balance between Th17 and Treg cell subsets: suppresses the release of Th17 and enhances Treg-mediated immunosuppression [ ]. In the intestinal TME, lactylation not only induces M2 macrophage polarization but also inhibits the cytotoxicity of CTLs, impairing their ability to specifically eliminate tumor cells. Concurrently, it elevates anti-inflammatory cytokines such as IL10 and TGFβ while reducing proinflammatory cytokines like TNFα, collectively forming an immunosuppressive environment [ , , , , ]. Lactylation can also regulate the interaction between intestinal microbiota and the host, relieving fibrosis to suppress intestinal inflammation and promoting the invasion and metastasis of malignancy [ , ].
In a nutshell, lactylation contribute to an immunosuppressive environment in the gut [ ]. Although the changes in immune cell phenotypes and cytokine profiles regulated by lactylation share certain common trends, the pathogenic mechanisms of intestinal inflammation and malignancy are fundamentally distinct. The core pathological feature of intestinal inflammation is overactivation of the immune system, where immunosuppressive mechanisms such as M2 macrophage polarization are beneficial for better prognosis. In contrast, impaired immune surveillance is a key risk factor for malignant tumors. Therefore, in malignancy context, lactylation-mediated immunosuppression, such as inhibition of CTL function, diminishes the host anti-tumor immune response and promotes immune escape [ ]. Thus, the immunosuppressive effects driven by lactylation “paradoxical regulation” under different disease backgrounds, reflecting the context-dependent character of lactylation.

## Conclusion
Chronic intestinal inflammation, such as IBD, is a significant risk factor for CRC development. In recent years, lactate-mediated lactylation has emerged as a key node linking cellular metabolism and epigenetic regulation, playing a crucial role in the pathogenesis of intestinal diseases [ , , ]. Previous studies indicate that lactylation may act as a “double-edged sword” for human health and diseases. On one hand, it promotes neuronal differentiation, facilitates DNA repair, and suppresses inflammation; on the other hand, it may accelerate fibrosis and tumor growth [ ]. In the central nervous system, lactate plays a neuroprotective and (or) damaging role by affecting mitochondrial respiration, abnormal protein metabolism, cell death and neuroinflammation [ ]. This review elucidates the core functions of lactylation in IBD and CRC. In IBD, lactylation shows immunoregulatory and tissue repair properties. It drives the shift of macrophages from pro-inflammatory to reparative phenotypes, regulates the balance of Th17 and Treg cells, mediates host-microbiota interactions, influences fibrosis processes, and contributes to tissue repair. These actions collectively maintain intestinal immune homeostasis [ , , , , ]. In CRC, lactylation exhibits distinct pro-tumor properties by reshaping the tumor immune microenvironment, inducing immune suppression, and promoting immune evasion to support tumor growth. Furthermore, lactylation participates in regulating gut microbiota metabolism, driving malignant proliferation, invasion, and metastasis of tumor cells, and conferring resistance to chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and immunotherapy [ , , , , , , ]. In conclusion, lactylation has a “Paradoxical Modulation” effect on the progression of intestinal diseases: it maintains immune homeostasis and tissue repair, exerting a protective role in IBD, while becoming a key driver of tumor progression in CRC.
Despite the key role of lactylation in intestinal diseases, several limitations remain in current research. First, the complete regulatory pathway of lactylation modification remains incompletely elucidated. Existing studies remain fragmented, lacking systematic validation of the mechanism chain from lactate to lactylation modification, which subsequently induces changes in protein conformation and function, ultimately affecting downstream signaling pathways and biological phenotypes. Second, existing research primarily focuses on L-lactate-mediated enzymatic lactylation, with limited understanding of D-lactate derived from gut microbiota and non-enzymatic lactylation. The functional and mechanistic similarities and differences between these two pathways warrant further investigation [ ]. Third, while the role of lactylation in intestinal immune regulation has been relatively well-systematized in macrophages [ ], its functions in other immune cells (e.g., B cells, neutrophils) remain poorly understood [ , , ]. Similarly, microbiota-host interactions mediated by lactylation currently relies primarily on phenotypic associations, with its specific molecular mechanisms still unclear. Fourth, the causal relationship between the spatiotemporal dynamics of lactylation and its functions remains unclear. It is currently unknown whether lactylation levels and their distribution within or between cells, or across different organelles, undergo dynamic changes during different pathological stages of IBD or CRC. Fifth, lactate has a dual role as both a metabolic molecule and a signaling molecule, and its specific role in particular pathological circumstances requires further clarification. This ambiguity complicates discerning causality: does lactylation drive immune phenotype alterations, or do immune changes reshape lactylation homeostasis? Sixth, “Paradoxical Modulation” effect of lactylation in IBD and CRC necessitates careful weighing of benefits versus risks for targeted interventions. For instance, inhibiting pro-tumor lactylation might impair its protective role in maintaining intestinal immune homeostasis? Seventh, complex cross-talk exists between lactylation and other histone modifications means that targeting lactylation could trigger unpredictable epigenetic cascades, thereby limiting the development of highly specific and safe therapies for lactylation [ ].
Given the regulatory role of lactate and lactylation modification in intestinal diseases, coupled with the current limitations of research, this field presents broad prospects for future investigation [ ]. First, developing therapeutic strategies that precisely target key nodes of lactylation represents a crucial future direction. With the successive discovery of lactylation “writers” (e.g., p300/CBP), “erasers” (e.g., SIRT1-3, HDAC1-3), and “readers”, designing highly specific modulators holds promise for achieving precise intervention on specific lactylation modifications while minimizing off-target effects. For example, inhibiting the lactyl-transferase activity of p300/CBP can impair H4K12la levels in CRC stem cells, thereby enhancing tumor sensitivity to chemotherapeutic agents [ ]. Notably, p300/CBP serves as a shared enzyme for both lactylation and acetylation. Future studies should explore small molecule inhibitors that specifically bind to the lactyl-CoA binding domain of p300/CBP to avoid interfering with normal acetylation regulatory pathways.
Second, regulating intracellular and extracellular lactate homeostasis by targeting upstream lactate metabolic processes (e.g., using LDH inhibitors or MCT blockers) is expected to maintain lactylation homeostasis at its source [ – ], thereby slowing the malignant progression of CRC and enabling disease prevention. Preclinical studies have demonstrated that activating the LDH–H3K18la–Nur77 axis in small cell lung cancer models enhances tumor immune escape, whereas inhibiting lactylation can restore T-cell antitumor function, reduce tumor burden and improve survival [ ]. Combining LDH inhibitors with immune checkpoint blockade therapy promotes the activation of effector T cells, effectively controlling the progression of melanoma and colon cancer in mice [ ]. As mentioned earlier, lactylation exhibits a notable “paradoxical modulation” effect during IBD and CRC progression; therefore, systemic inhibit the target of lactate metabolism such as LDH and MCT may lead to severe toxic side effects. For example, lactylation enhances MYH9 stability, activates the FOXO3a/Bim death pathway, and induces intestinal vascular barrier injury [ ]. Restricted by the paradox, targeting lactylation in CRC treatment may risk excessive activation of intestinal inflammation. Similarly, suppressing intestinal inflammation via lactylation inhibition may increase the risk of CRC development. Previous studies have shown that nano-delivery technology holds promising potential for colorectal cancer and liver cancer [ , ]. Future research could focus on developing tumor microenvironment responsive nanocarriers to specifically deliver LDH and MCT inhibitors to CRC cells, thereby impairing the side effect of the paradoxical modulation.
Third, given that the gut microbiota is a significant source of lactate [ ], screening for immunomodulatory lactate-producing probiotic strains to regulate host lactate levels holds promise as a novel microbiotherapy by restoring intestinal immune homeostasis [ , ]. Emerging nanocarrier technology shows the potential for on-demand regulation of the gut microbiome while reducing side effects of the paradoxical modulation [ ]. For example, orally or intravenously administered irinotecan-loaded dextran nanoparticles can inhibit the proliferation of F. nucleatum, significantly enhancing the efficacy of first-line chemotherapy in CRC [ ]. Research in the future should develop gut microbes targeted delivery systems to reduce chemotherapy resistance and improve prognosis of CRC.
Moreover, compared with traditional PTMs, lactylation is more closely linked to cellular metabolism. This characteristic makes lactylation an effective “indicator” of cellular metabolic status, capable of reflecting physiological changes within cells [ ]. Studies have shown that lactylation may serve as a potential biomarker for the diagnosis and treatment of sepsis [ ]. Using proteomics to identify specific lactylation markers in serum, tissues, or feces has shown promise in providing laboratory evidence for assessing disease activity and enabling early diagnosis in IBD and CRC [ , ].
