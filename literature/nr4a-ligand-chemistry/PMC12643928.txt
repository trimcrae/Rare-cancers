## Abstract
The Foxp3⁺ regulatory T (Treg) cell repertoire carries age-dependent biases, with neonatal subsets enriched for highly self-reactive clones. However, the thymocyte features distinguishing neonatal from adult Treg selection remain unclear. Here, we show that neonatal double-positive mouse thymocytes, unlike their adult counterparts, fail to upregulate Zap70 during thymic selection, creating a calcium signaling bottleneck. This attenuated Zap70-dependent signaling limits negative selection, allowing highly self-reactive clones to evade deletion. Modulating Zap70 expression alters this balance; reducing Zap70 in adults rescues development of these clones, whereas increasing Zap70 in neonates enforces their deletion. Similarly, enhancing neonatal calcium signaling via increased LAT Y136-mediated PLCγ1 activation promotes clonal deletion. Analysis of pediatric human thymi reveals that ZAP70 expression remains low during the first year of life, aligning with the peak window for thymic Treg cell development. These findings suggest that age-dependent Zap70 expression regulates negative selection and thymic Treg cell development.
Subject terms: Central tolerance, CD4-positive T cells

Lo and colleagues report that double-positive thymocytes from neonates express less Zap70 and show reduced Ca2⁺/NFAT signaling compared to double-positive thymocytes from older thymi. This diminished Ca2⁺ signaling alters negative selection for self-reactive TCRs, resulting in a cell-intrinsic temporal window for regulatory T versus conventional T cell development in the thymus.

## Main
Early life necessitates rapid immune development while maintaining tolerance to benign stimuli. Neonatal T cell microrepertoires, featuring unique T cell antigen receptor (TCR) clonotypes compared to those in adults, help establish immune homeostasis via their effector functions and tissue localization – . Among these microrepertoires, thymic-derived Foxp3+ regulatory T (Treg) cells and CD8αα intraepithelial lymphocytes (IELs) contribute to peripheral and mucosal tolerance, respectively. A defining feature of neonatal Treg cells and CD8αα IEL precursors is their enrichment for self-reactive clones generated via agonist selection during a tightly controlled developmental window . While thymocyte-extrinsic factors (for example, antigen-processing machinery and thymic epithelial and antigen-presenting cells) are suggested to shape the conventional T (Tconv) cell and Treg cell repertoires – , the extent to which neonatal thymocytes use cell-intrinsic pathways to adjust negative selection and diversify into regulatory subsets remains unclear.
In the adult thymus, CD4⁺CD8⁺ double-positive (DP) thymocytes undergo positive selection by ligands for which they have very weak affinity, whereas strong TCR signals typically induce negative selection . Critically, ligands that drive positive selection and many that induce negative selection generally do not support mature T cell expansion or effector differentiation . These observations highlight two principles shaping the mature T cell repertoire: (1) thymocyte maturation is accompanied by a programmed reduction in TCR sensitivity, and (2) the TCR can precisely distinguish the strength and abundance of peptide bound to major histocompatibility complex (MHC) ligands, translating this information into distinct functional outcomes – . Thus, TCR sensitivity and function are progressively refined during T-lineage cell maturation. ζ-Associated protein of 70 kDa (Zap70), a cytoplasmic tyrosine kinase , is pivotal in this process, mediating TCR signaling and ensuring the generation of a repertoire that balances immune responsiveness with self-tolerance.
Zap70 expression begins after the double-negative (DN) 3 stage and is essential for supporting DP thymocytes through thymic selection – . Complete Zap70 deficiency causes severe immunodeficiency , and both hypermorphic and hypomorphic mutations are linked to autoimmunity, emphasizing the necessity of precise Zap70 regulation in maintaining tolerance. In mature T cells, Lck-mediated phosphorylation of the ζ-chain facilitates Zap70 binding, functioning as a signaling rheostat: elevated self-reactivity increases ζ-chain phosphorylation, thereby enhancing TCR sensitivity , , . After antigen recognition, Lck-activated Zap70 phosphorylates LAT and SLP76, assembling the signalosomes that trigger multiple pathways, including ERK and NFAT , . Interestingly, Zap70 phosphorylates LAT and SLP76 with varying efficiency . Phosphorylation at LAT Y136, which recruits PLCγ1, is relatively inefficient compared to phosphorylation at other sites that recruit GADS and GRB2. Consequently, ERK signaling can be induced by weaker stimuli, whereas PLCγ1-dependent calcium and NFAT activation demand stronger signal inputs , , . Thus, the LAT signalosome functions not just as a scaffold but as an active integrator of signal strength and ligand quality during TCR recognition of peptide bound to MHC , , , , . A critical yet unresolved question is whether these signaling thresholds are inherently fixed or can adapt with the host’s age. If LAT-dependent signaling is recalibrated in an age-dependent manner, this could profoundly shape neonate thymocyte fate, biasing the repertoire toward greater tolerance or increased self-reactivity.
Here, our findings reveal that thymic negative selection is limited during the neonatal stage, correlating with a modest reduction in Zap70 expression in both mouse and human thymocytes. Diminished neonatal Zap70 expression selectively dampens calcium signaling while maintaining ERK activation. Consequently, transcriptional activation is skewed: NFAT, IRF4, NR4A3 and EGR2 show reduced activation in neonatal DP thymocytes, whereas ERK-dependent factors, including NR4A1, Fos and EGR1, along with NF-κB p65, remain largely unaffected. Experimental modulation of Zap70 expression within physiological levels is sufficient to reprogram selection thresholds (Zap70 haploinsufficiency in adults recapitulates neonatal-like selection, and transgenic expression eliminates neonatal-biased clones). Thus, age-dependent Zap70 expression and its effects on calcium signaling serve as a developmental switch, allowing highly self-reactive thymocytes to escape clonal deletion and contribute to the neonatal Treg cell repertoire.

## Results

## Neonatal thymocytes resist deletion while favoring diversion
Although it is evident that some self-reactive T cell clones develop in the neonatal thymus, whether this arises solely from holes in self-peptide presentation or whether it also involves thymocyte-intrinsic mechanisms remains unresolved. To assess clonal deletion and clonal diversion across ages, we first quantified levels of cleaved caspase-3, a marker of apoptosis, in signaled (CD5int/hiTCRβint/hi) and non-signaled (CD5loTCRβlo) populations (Extended Data Fig. ). This approach allowed us to differentiate apoptosis caused by clonal deletion from that induced by death by neglect . In the adult thymus, the proportion of signaled thymocytes expressing cleaved caspase-3 was approximately twice that observed in neonates (Extended Data Fig. ). By contrast, no differences in the frequency of caspase-3 activation were observed in the non-signaled thymocyte populations of neonates and adults (Extended Data Fig. ).
 Age-dependent cleaved caspase-3 differences were most pronounced at the DP stage marked by high expression of CD69 and TCR (Fig. and Extended Data Fig. ). Following lineage commitment, similar reductions in neonates were observed in agonist-selected populations, including CD25+ CD4-single-positive (CD4SP) Treg cell precursors, Foxp3+ Treg cells and IEL precursors (Fig. and Extended Data Fig. ), suggesting that these alterations stem from strong TCR selecting signals. Consistent with alterations in TCR signal-induced differences in thymocyte apoptosis, neonates exhibited a marked increase in CD25+ Treg cell precursors (Extended Data Fig. ).
 To directly test the hypothesis that the neonatal thymus includes DP thymocytes with increased resistance to apoptosis, we stimulated neonatal and adult DP thymocytes expressing the B3K506 transgenic TCR (an I-Ab-restricted TCR with specificity for a triple-lysine-substituted Eα52–68 peptide (3K)) with a range of TCR ligands of varying strength: the strong agonist 3K, the partial agonist P-1A and the weak agonists P8A and P2A. Using culture with peptide-pulsed bone marrow-derived dendritic cells in vitro, we measured the frequency of thymocytes that upregulated CD69 expression without activating caspase-3 after 5 h. Stimulation over a range of ligand affinities and concentrations revealed that approximately twice as many neonatal DP thymocytes upregulated CD69 expression without initiating apoptosis in response to strong TCR signaling compared to adult thymocytes (Fig. ). This occurs despite a similar overall frequency of CD69 expression in neonatal and adult DP thymocytes (Extended Data Fig. ). These findings reveal that neonatal thymocytes are more resistant to caspase-3 activation in response to strong selection signals.
To explore the physiological impact of age-dependent changes in TCR signaling-induced apoptosis, we analyzed the deletion of Vβ5+, Vβ11+ and Vβ12+ clonotypes by endogenous superantigen in C57BL/6 × B10.D2 (F1) mice . Although C57BL/6 mice carry mouse mammary tumor viruses 8, 9 and 30, which render these Vβ-expressing T cells susceptible to negative selection, the natural absence of I-E (I-E⁻) prevents their deletion. However, crossing C57BL/6 mice with B10.D2 (I-E⁺) mice restores I-E expression and facilitates negative selection of Vβ5+, Vβ11+ and Vβ12+ thymocytes. Deletion is stronger in I-E+ adults than in neonates or I-E− controls, with the Vβ6+ subset unaffected (Fig. and Extended Data Fig. ). To evaluate how self-reactive thymocytes are diverted toward a regulatory fate versus eliminated through deletion, we measured Foxp3 induction and caspase-3 activation. Superantigen exposure promoted Treg cell development among Vβ5+, Vβ11+ and Vβ12+ CD4SP cells in both neonates and adults, but this diversion was more robust in neonates (Extended Data Fig. ). In adults, these cells more frequently activated caspase-3, consistent with a shift toward clonal deletion (Extended Data Fig. ). Together with prior studies , , , , these results support the hypothesis that neonatal negative selection is inherently constrained.

## Neonatal thymocytes exhibit attenuated calcium signaling
To investigate how TCR signaling contributes to age-dependent differences between apoptosis and clonal diversion, we used NR4A1 (Nur77)–enhanced green fluorescent protein (eGFP) and NR4A3 (Nor1)–Tocky fluorescent reporter mice, well-established transcriptional reporters of TCR signaling intensity . Ex vivo thymocytes showed progressively increasing expression of these transcription factors during development. However, when comparing neonatal thymocytes to their adult counterparts, distinct differences emerged: neonatal thymocytes displayed higher levels of NR4A1, whereas adult thymocytes preferentially induced NR4A3 (Fig. ). To confirm that these differences directly resulted from TCR signaling, we crossed NR4A1–eGFP and NR4A3–Tocky mice with B3K506 TCR transgenic mice. Ex vivo neonatal and adult B3K506 TCR transgenic DP thymocytes were cultured overnight with bone marrow-derived dendritic cells pulsed with varying concentrations of ligands with strong (3K), medium (P-1A) or weak (P8A and P2A) affinities. We found that neonatal thymocytes exhibited stronger induction of NR4A1 in response to all four ligands of varying affinity (Fig. ). By contrast, adult thymocytes preferentially upregulated NR4A3, particularly in response to the partial agonist P-1A and weaker ligands P8A and P2A (Fig. ). As these observations were made with transcriptional reporters, we next used direct antibody staining to more accurately assess real-time difference in endogenous protein expression. This analysis revealed that differences in NR4A1 and NR4A3 expression were detectable as early as 3 h after stimulation with either the cognate 3K peptide or the partial agonist P-1A (Extended Data Fig. ). These findings reveal age-dependent modulation of TCR signaling in thymocytes, suggesting that neonatal thymocytes escape negative selection owing to changes in TCR signal processing rather than diminished sensitivity.
To directly test whether neonatal and adult thymocytes propagate TCR signals differently following recognition of high-affinity ligands, TCR signaling was analyzed using ex vivo B3K506 DP thymocytes. Using 3K or P-1A peptide-loaded biotinylated I-Ab monomers followed by streptavidin (SA) cross-linking, we found that neonatal B3K506 DP thymocytes consistently displayed weaker calcium responses than their adult counterparts (Fig. ). Lower responses were observed for both calcium release from the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and calcium influx through store-operated calcium release-activated calcium (CRAC) channels in the cell membrane (Fig. ). Similarly, signaling events responsive to the activation of calcium signaling, including the nuclear translocation of NFAT and induction of IRF4 and EGR2 expression, showed comparable reductions in neonatal thymocytes following peptide 3K or P-1A stimulation in vitro (Fig. ).
 Despite impaired calcium signaling, neonatal thymocytes maintained robust activation of the ERK pathway, with levels comparable to or exceeding those of adults (Fig. ). ERK-induced transcription factors, including Fos and EGR1, were similarly expressed at equal or higher levels in neonates (Extended Data Fig. ). The NF-κB p65 pathway, which is induced by co-stimulatory signals, showed strong nuclear translocation in both neonatal and adult thymocytes (Extended Data Fig. ). CD69, an early activation marker partially regulated by ERK signaling, was initially more strongly induced in neonatal DP cells (Fig. and Extended Data Fig. ), but its expression matched adult levels after overnight culture (Fig. ). These findings highlight a specific diminution of calcium-dependent signaling and its downstream effectors, such as NFAT, in neonatal thymocytes, while other TCR signaling pathways including ERK and NF-κB, remain fully operational. The divergence in NR4A1 and NR4A3 expression also aligns with this pattern of signaling (Fig. and Extended Data Fig. ) . These results indicate a selective impairment in calcium signaling, contrasted by intact activation of other pathways in neonatal thymocytes.
 

## Augmented calcium responses eliminate neonate-biased T cells
To examine how distinct calcium signaling patterns influence neonatal T cell selection, we used LatG135D mice, in which a glycine residue preceding Y136 is replaced with aspartate . This single-amino-acid change enhances LAT Y136 phosphorylation, leading to increased PLCγ1 activation and augmented calcium signaling with little impact on ERK and NF-κB activation , . Using two in vivo models, we examined the role of calcium signaling in neonatal negative selection and its potential to influence the generation of the neonatal Treg cell repertoire.
First, to assess high-affinity TCR-mediated thymic negative selection, we analyzed Vβ5+, Vβ11+ and Vβ12+ thymocytes in LatG135D mice with or without I-E expression in superantigen models. On the C57BL/6 (I-E⁻) background, LAT-G135D expression did not alter TCR Vβ usage or overall TCRα and TCRβ repertoire diversity and characteristics (Extended Data Fig. ). However, in I-E+
LatG135D mice, neonatal thymocytes expressing Vβ5+, Vβ11+ and Vβ12+ underwent robust clonal deletion, reaching levels comparable to those in I-E+ wild-type adult mice (Fig. ). Superantigen-susceptible clones were blocked at the DP2–DP3 and mature 1–mature 2 (Extended Data Fig. ) transitions and showed increased cleaved caspase-3 expression and impaired development of Foxp3⁺ Treg cells (Fig. ). Changes in TCR Vβ usage owing to the expression of LAT-G135D appear to result from clonal elimination rather than differences in positive selection, as the superantigen-resistant Vβ6+ subsets remained at normal frequencies (Fig. ).
 To investigate the impact of increased calcium signaling on neonatal-biased Foxp3⁺ Treg cell development, we used YAe62β TCR transgenic (YAe62β) mice, in which the expression of a fixed TCRβ chain alongside a diverse TCRα repertoire enables the identification of I-Aᵇ-restricted Padi4-specific Treg cell clonotypes . These clonotypes preferentially differentiate into Foxp3⁺ Treg cells during the neonatal stage but are eliminated through negative selection in adults . We generated YAe62β LatG135D mice by crossing LatG135D mice with YAe62β and Foxp3–eGFP reporter mice. As expected, in wild-type mice, Padi4-specific Treg cells primarily developed during the neonatal stage, with their selection frequency decreasing tenfold in adulthood (Fig. ). Expression of LAT-G135D led to the loss of Padi4-specific thymic Treg cells during the neonatal stage (Fig. ); it exerted a milder yet similar effect on the Tconv repertoire, which has weaker affinity for this self-ligand (Fig. ). This deletion occurred during the transition to TCRhi DP3 thymocytes (Extended Data Fig. ), the same selection stage at which these clones are eliminated in adult wild-type mice . As a result of increased thymic deletion, significantly fewer Padi4-specific Treg cells were observed in the secondary lymphoid organs of YAe62β LatG135D mice (Fig. ) and in the bone marrow, where these cells are normally abundant (Extended Data Fig. ). LAT-G135D expression also resulted in fewer CD25+ Treg cell precursors among neonatal CD4SP cells, mirroring the wild-type adult profile (Extended Data Fig. ). These findings underscore the limited ability of neonatal thymocytes to engage the calcium pathway as a key signaling characteristic that influences negative selection, allowing the development of neonatal-biased Treg cells.

## Neonatal thymocytes express lower Zap70 levels
To explore the factors that constrain the ability of neonatal DP thymocytes to effectively activate calcium signaling, TCR proximal signaling components were analyzed. Among the key initiators of TCR signaling, Zap70 uniquely stood out with an RNA transcript and protein expression pattern that was specifically enriched in adults relative to neonates (Fig. ). Although adults demonstrated a progressive increase in Zap70 expression across developmental stages, neonates displayed a consistently lower expression pattern from the preselection DP1 stage to the ongoing and postselection (DP2 and DP3) stages of thymic selection (Fig. ). This age-dependent difference was not observed for the upstream kinase Lck (Extended Data Fig. ), the downstream adaptor Lat (Extended Data Fig. ) or the CD3 and ζ-chains to which it binds after activation (Extended Data Fig. ). Additionally, there was no compensatory upregulation of Syk (Fig. ), a kinase closely related to Zap70 that is unable to substitute for Zap70 function during DP stages . These findings highlight the unique age-specific expression patterns of Zap70 and its potential role in shaping TCR signaling in neonatal thymocytes.
 

## Zap70 expression levels dictate calcium signaling strength
To investigate a link between Zap70 expression and calcium signaling, we analyzed thymocytes isolated from wild-type, monoallelic Zap70 (Zap70Het) and Zap70 analog-sensitive (Zap70AS) mice. Zap70Het DP thymocytes show reduced Zap70 expression by approximately one-third in neonates and one-half in adults, relative to expression in wild-type biallelic mice (Fig. ). As a result, Zap70Het adult thymocytes exhibited expression levels comparable to those of wild-type neonatal thymocytes (Fig. ). By contrast, Zap70AS mice feature a bacterial artificial chromosome transgene that carries the full endogenous Zap70 genomic locus and its native promoters , allowing for physiologically relevant modulation of Zap70 expression via an additional allele-driven increase. In both neonatal and adult DP thymocytes, the presence of Zap70AS led to a progressive elevation of Zap70 expression throughout thymic selection (Fig. ). This increase effectively raised Zap70 levels in neonatal DP thymocytes to adult levels (Fig. ). Importantly, Zap70Het and Zap70AS thymi produced similar numbers of overall CD4SP cells (Extended Data Fig. ), supporting the feasibility of using these models to analyze changes in calcium signaling and T cell repertoire development.
To test if Zap70 expression levels influence calcium signaling, B3K506 TCR transgenic mice were bred onto Zap70Het and Zap70AS genetic backgrounds, and calcium responses were assessed in neonatal and adult DP thymocytes. Reduction of Zap70 expression in Zap70Het mice selectively limited calcium responses and NFAT nuclear translocation following TCR activation, whereas elevated Zap70 expression enhanced calcium flux and promoted NFAT nuclear translocation (Fig. ). However, these modest changes in Zap70 expression had little effect on ERK activation or NF-κB nuclear translocation (Extended Data Fig. ). To confirm that the enhanced calcium–NFAT responses were specifically mediated by the Zap70AS allele, we took advantage of the fact that this allele can be selectively inhibited by 3MB-PP1 without affecting the wild-type allele. Treatment with 3MB-PP1 acutely suppressed calcium flux and NFAT nuclear translocation in Zap70AS thymocytes, restoring both to wild-type levels (Fig. ). These findings demonstrate a direct link between age-dependent Zap70 expression and calcium–NFAT signaling in DP thymocytes.

## Constrained Zap70 promotes neonate-like Treg cell development
To examine the ability of neonate-level Zap70 expression to reshape adult T cell selection, we examined the development of superantigen-sensitive T cells and Padi4-specific Treg cells on a Zap70Het background. In the superantigen model, reducing Zap70 expression partially restored the development of T cells expressing superantigen-sensitive Vβ5, Vβ11 and Vβ12 TCRs in adults (Fig. ), but had no effect on superantigen-resistant Vβ6+ cells (Fig. ). Further analysis indicated that adult Zap70Het CD4SP cells preferentially differentiated into Treg cells rather than undergoing apoptosis, as evidenced by increased Foxp3 upregulation and reduced cleaved caspase-3 activation (Fig. ).
 Next, we used the Padi4-specific Treg cell model to evaluate whether similar effects on neonatal-biased T cell maturation occurred in YAe62β Zap70Het mice. Increased Padi4-specific Treg cell development was observed in the neonatal thymus of Zap70Het mice (Fig. ), consistent with previous findings that high-affinity clonotypes can still undergo deletion in neonatal thymi , which may be limited by partial reduction of Zap70. In adult mice, Zap70 haploinsufficiency rescued Padi4-specific Treg cell development to near-neonatal levels, as evidenced by a fivefold increase in frequency and a sevenfold increase in cell counts (Fig. ), with only a modest rise in Foxp3− CD4SP subsets (Extended Data Fig. ). The eightfold increase in Padi4-specific Treg cells in the spleen and threefold increase in the bone marrow indicate increased thymic export and peripheral seeding (Fig. and Extended Data Fig. ), reinforcing the link between Zap70 levels and Treg cell repertoire development. Analysis of the selection process at the DP stage revealed that the rescue of Padi4-specific cells under reduced Zap70 expression begins during the DP2-to-DP3 transition (Extended Data Fig. ). At this stage, monoallelic Zap70-expressing cells progressed to postselection stages at a tenfold higher rate than biallelic Zap70-expressing cells (Extended Data Fig. ). Likewise, on the C57BL/6 background, the CD25+ Treg cell precursors abundantly observed in wild-type neonates, which were diminished in wild-type adults, persisted in the Zap70Het adult thymus (Extended Data Fig. ). These findings support the hypothesis that reduced Zap70 expression can restore thymocyte populations that would otherwise be deleted during adulthood owing to agonist-level TCR signals.
 

## Elevated Zap70 promotes adult-like negative selection
To examine the sufficiency of adult-level Zap70 expression to reshape the neonatal Treg cell repertoire, Zap70AS mice were introduced onto the I-E+ background. Adult-like Zap70 expression in neonatal Zap70AS thymocytes amplified superantigen-mediated deletion of sensitive TCR Vβ subsets, recapitulating the adult-stage deletion pattern (Fig. ). This effect likely reflects dampened clonal diversion toward the Treg cell lineage and preferential activation of the caspase-3 apoptotic pathway over Foxp3 induction (Fig. ). Similarly, Padi4-specific Treg cell development was greatly reduced during the neonatal stage in mice carrying Zap70AS (Fig. ) with a more modest effect on Padi4-specific Tconv cells (Extended Data Fig. ). This led to a reduced population of Padi4-specific Treg cells in both the peripheral lymphoid tissues and the bone marrow (Fig. and Extended Data Fig. ). Analysis of developmental changes at the DP stage revealed that the loss of Padi4-specific Treg cells results from a more than sixfold reduction in Padi4-specific DP cells passing the first negative selection checkpoint (Extended Data Fig. ). Furthermore, although the total Treg cell population in the bone marrow remained stable, the selective loss of Padi4-specific Treg cells points to a possible thymic selection defect contributing to localized specificity gaps.
 The overall frequencies of Padi4-specific CD4SP Tconv cells in Zap70AS mice did not entirely phenocopy those of adult wild-type mice (Extended Data Fig. ). However, the thymocyte subset distribution across maturation stages in Zap70AS mice revealed a notable shift (Extended Data Fig. ). In wild-type mice, Padi4-specific CD4SP cells were distributed relatively evenly among immature and mature stages. However, Zap70AS mice showed a pronounced reduction of mature CD69−H-2Kb+ CD4SP cells, indicating a developmental block at the semimature stage. Consistent with this, mature Padi4-specific CD4SP thymocytes were reduced to adult wild-type levels during the neonatal stage on the Zap70AS background (Extended Data Fig. ), aligning with their frequencies in the peripheral T cell pool (Extended Data Fig. ). These data may reflect prior observations that presentation of the Padi4 epitope by thymic antigen-presenting cells is increased in an age-dependent manner . Additionally, increased Zap70 expression in polyclonal C57BL/6 neonates led to the loss of CD25+ Treg precursor cells (Extended Data Fig. ), further highlighting Zap70’s pivotal role in regulating neonatal Treg cell development. These data argue that age-dependent Zap70 expression is a thymic selection switch that controls the development of mouse neonatal and adult Treg cell repertoires.

## Zap70 expression levels control neonatal Treg cell development
To quantify overall differences in T cell repertoires, we performed TCR Vα2 deep sequencing on conventional CD4SP and Treg cells sorted from neonatal and adult YAe62β wild-type, LatG135D, Zap70Het and Zap70AS mice. The 1,000 most frequently observed clonotypes in each group were assessed for repertoire similarity to wild-type neonatal or adult Treg cell repertoires using Morisita–Horn index analysis. This analysis revealed that the neonatal wild-type Treg cell TCR repertoire closely resembled both neonatal and adult Treg cell repertoires in Zap70Het mice while being highly distinct from their adult counterparts and the neonatal and adult Treg cell repertoires that develop in LatG135D and Zap70AS mice (Fig. ). The LatG135D and Zap70AS adult Treg cell profiles were distinct from wild-type Treg cell profiles, perhaps owing to enhanced negative selection, whereas Zap70Het Treg cell repertoires demonstrated the greatest dissimilarity, likely a product of reduced negative selection (Fig. ). The Treg cell repertoires that develop in each of the different genotypes are also highly distinct from their corresponding Tconv cell repertoires (Extended Data Fig. ).
 Hierarchical clustering of 11,424 nonredundant clonotypes derived from the 1,000 most frequently observed clones among the sequencing datasets was performed to evaluate similarity and distance between T cell repertoires. This approach revealed that wild-type neonatal Treg cell repertoires most closely clustered with the neonatal and adult repertoires that developed in Zap70Het mice (Fig. and Extended Data Fig. ), whereas wild-type, LatG135D and Zap70AS adult Treg cells, as well as neonatal LatG135D and Zap70AS Treg cells, formed alternative clusters. Our data highlight that reduced Zap70 expression in adults promotes the emergence of Treg cell repertoires with neonatal-like features, whereas enhanced Zap70 expression in neonatal thymocytes limits the development of neonatal-biased clones.

## Lower ZAP70 marks age window for human agonist selection
To investigate whether ZAP70 expression is age dependent and linked to human T cell development, we analyzed thymic samples from pediatric individuals undergoing cardiac surgery ranging in age from 2 days to 18 years. Thymic cellularity correlated with organ volume, plateauing around infancy (Extended Data Fig. ). This cohort was then examined for ZAP70 expression levels in DP thymocytes at different ages. Although ZAP70 expression remained relatively stable between newborns and infants under 1 year of age, we observed a marked increase in ZAP70 expression in DP2 and DP3 thymocytes from individuals older than 1 year (Fig. ). When exploring Treg cell development, we found a distinct early wave in infants (Fig. and Extended Data Fig. ), coinciding with the period of low ZAP70 expression. Another agonist-selected subset, IEL precursors within the DN population, also showed a pronounced early expansion (Fig. and Extended Data Fig. ). Functional analyses revealed that thymocytes from infants exhibited impaired calcium responses but maintained comparable ERK activation (Fig. ), mirroring our observations in neonatal mice. These results suggest that ZAP70 expression in human thymocytes is age dependent and directly influences their ability to trigger calcium signaling. When ZAP70 expression is low, calcium signaling is more significantly impaired than ERK activation. This window of low ZAP70 expression overlaps with a peak in Treg cell development, indicating a potential role for ZAP70 in shaping early human thymocyte selection and repertoire formation.
 

## Discussion
Immune homeostasis in early life relies on the development of T cell microrepertoires (clonotypes with specialized effector functions and tissue localization). A defining feature of neonatal-biased repertoires is self-reactivity: in MHC class II-restricted thymocytes, this neonatal self-reactivity promotes Foxp3⁺ Treg cell commitment, whereas self-reactivity toward MHC class I facilitates the development of CD8αα IEL precursors. Neonatal Treg cells thus emerge as a distinct, highly self-reactive population that escapes negative selection during this window.
Despite that neonatal Treg cells are central to peripheral tolerance, the thymocyte-intrinsic mechanisms that coordinate with age-related changes in thymic architecture and self-antigen display remain unclear. We identify age-dependent changes in Zap70 expression that limit calcium signaling during neonatal thymocyte development. This reduced calcium signaling allows highly self-reactive MHC class II-restricted thymocytes to evade negative selection and instead be diverted into the Treg cell repertoire. A similar pattern occurs in the human thymus, where ZAP70 expression is low during infancy, coinciding with reduced calcium induction and peak generation of Treg cells and IEL precursors. Our findings reveal a thymic selection ‘switch’, governed by Zap70 levels, that regulates neonatal thymocyte development.
Several hypotheses have been proposed to explain how the neonatal thymus fosters highly self-reactive T cell clones. Because the neonatal window coincides with organismal development, age-dependent protein expression across tissues and antigen transfer to dendritic cell subsets influence thymic antigen presentation , , , . Antigen presentation machinery in thymic antigen-presenting cells , the composition of thymic dendritic cell subsets and the abundance of medullary thymic epithelial cell subsets and post-AIRE⁺ mimetic cells also undergo age-related changes , – . These thymocyte-extrinsic models suggest that neonatal T cells ‘avoid’ negative selection because tolerance-inducing self-antigens are either absent or presented at lower levels. However, the neonatal development of CD8αα IELs expressing ‘forbidden’ superantigen-sensitive TCR Vβ suggests that thymocyte-intrinsic mechanisms (particularly TCR signaling) also contribute , .
By directly analyzing TCR signaling and repertoire formation in neonatal and adult thymocytes, we demonstrated a clear association between reduced Zap70 expression, diminished calcium signaling and the selection of self-reactive, neonate-biased Treg cell clones. We observed that elevated Zap70 expression and consequent enhanced calcium signaling cull thymocyte Treg cell precursors and neonate-biased Treg cell clones. Conversely, experimentally reducing Zap70 expression by using haploinsufficient adult mice allowed for the development of Treg cell clones with neonatal-like features. These genetic manipulations largely modulate thymic selection outcomes independent of the host’s age, although some subtle differences between T cell repertoires remain.
We posit that this observation underscores the importance of cell-extrinsic, age-dependent factors in thymic selection and connects our results to prior studies highlighting developmental differences in antigen presentation. Several factors may explain the incomplete recapitulation of adult repertoires in the LatG135D neonatal setting. First, LAT-G135D dramatically enhances calcium responses, surpassing the levels in adult wild-type thymocytes , and thus likely represents an extreme case. Second, the neonatal thymic microenvironment differs from that of the adult , , , potentially limiting key self-antigens needed to shape an adult-like Treg cell repertoire. Third, lower thymocyte numbers and reduced interleukin-2 availability in neonates may alter clonal competition and influence Treg cell precursor and Treg cell selection.
Has evolution selected for a unique LAT polymorphism that supports the generation of age-dependent immunoregulatory T cell repertoires? Because Zap70 (and, to a lesser degree, LAT) are largely restricted to the T cell lineage, any evolutionary pressure on these genes likely reflects their role in T cell function. A twofold reduction in Zap70 expression selectively limits TCR-induced calcium signaling, likely due to Zap70’s reduced efficiency at phosphorylating LAT Y136 compared to other sites , , . The upstream glycine at LAT Y136 creates a suboptimal phosphorylation site, unlike the canonical negatively charged amino acid found upstream of most Zap70 targets on LAT or SLP76 (refs. , , ). Substituting glycine at position 135 with aspartic acid restores calcium signaling and subjects self-reactive clonotypes, which would normally be diverted into neonatal Treg cells, to negative selection. Thus, the neonate-to-adult thymic selection switch requires Zap70 and LAT to work in tandem, the former through expression level and the latter through a noncanonical binding site, together tuning calcium signaling in thymocytes.
That the thymus regulates calcium signaling to influence neonatal Treg cell clone development is perhaps not surprising. Foxp3⁺ Treg cells diverge from Tconv cells primarily at the CD4SP stage, but prospective Foxp3⁺ Treg cell clones must first navigate the DP stage, surviving both positive and negative selection , . Calcium signaling is a key determinant of thymocyte fate , , as strong calcium influx promotes apoptosis during negative selection, whereas positive selection is associated with weaker, more gradual calcium accumulation – . Consistent with these observations, STIM-dependent store-operated calcium entry is dispensable for Tconv cell development but is critical for the differentiation of Treg cells and other agonist selection populations – . By attenuating calcium signaling, our data suggest that some self-reactive DP thymocytes (normally eliminated in the adult thymus) are instead permitted to survive in the neonatal thymus, allowing them to develop into Treg cells.
A key unresolved question is how Zap70 expression is regulated across developmental stages and ages. One possibility is that fetal and adult thymic progenitors carry distinct epigenetic imprints that influence Zap70 expression . Neonatal T cells also express higher levels of CD5 (ref. ), a surface receptor associated with ubiquitin ligases that may regulate Zap70 expression through post-translational modification .
Our findings reveal that neonatal thymocytes fine-tune Zap70 expression to regulate TCR-mediated calcium signaling, enabling the survival of highly self-reactive T cell clonotypes that contribute to the neonatal Treg cell repertoire. By uncovering this role for Zap70, our work highlights an intrinsic ability of thymocytes to modify the activation threshold of specific TCR signaling branches. This age-dependent modulation of thymic selection provides a unique developmental window that shapes early immune homeostasis and tolerance.

## Methods

## Experimental model: mice
C57BL/6, B6.K, B10.D2, YAe62β Foxp3–eGFP, YAe62β Foxp3–eGFP Zap70Het, YAe62β Foxp3–eGFP Zap70AS, YAe62β Foxp3–eGFP LatG135D, B3K506 TCR transgenic (wild-type and all genetically modified strains) and Tcra-knockout mice were housed in the specific pathogen-free facilities at the University of Utah. C57BL/6, B3K506 TCR transgenic, NR4A1–eGFP and NR4A3–Tocky mice were housed in the specific pathogen-free facilities at the University of Massachusetts Chan Medical School. Mice were treated according to protocols that were approved by the University of Utah veterinary committees (to W.-L. L.) or by University of Massachusetts Chan Medical School (to E.S.H.), and all protocols were in accordance with National Institutes of Health (NIH) guidelines. Neonatal mice (postnatal days 10–14) and adult mice (6–8 weeks old) of both sexes were used for all experimental procedures.

## Experimental model: human thymi
Thymic samples were obtained as discarded tissues from pediatric cardiothoracic surgeries at the University of Utah Primary Children’s Hospital, with age recorded. Samples were placed in 50-ml tubes with 20 ml of RPMI 1640, volume measured, and transferred to 100-mm culture dishes for imaging. Thymi were minced into pieces (≤5 mm) and mechanically dissociated using a 20-ml syringe plunger. The resulting suspension was filtered through a 100-μm strainer and collected. This process was repeated with fresh medium until 50 ml of suspension was obtained. Cells were centrifuged (300g, 7 min), resuspended in 40 ml of RPMI and filtered again. Total thymocyte count was determined using a hemocytometer. The pellet was resuspended in fetal bovine serum (FBS; Omega Scientific) based on age (~9 ml for <2 years and ~15 ml for ≥2 years). After final filtration, an equal volume of freezing medium (20% DMSO in FBS) was added dropwise. Aliquots were cryopreserved at –80 °C before transfer to liquid nitrogen storage.

## Generation of recombinant biotinylated monomers and tetramers
Recombinant I-Ab molecules were produced in insect cells as previously described . Peptides were encoded at the N terminus of the I-Ab β-chain, followed by a Gly-Ser linker (3K peptide (FEAQKAKANKAVD), P-1A (FAAQKAKANKAVD), P2A (FEAAKAKANKAVD) and P8A (FEAQKAKANAAVD)). Monomeric I-Ab molecules were biotinylated with recombinant BirA (Avidity) and mixed at 1:8 molar ratios, respectively, with SA–PE (PJRS25-1, Agilent).

## Tetramer staining assay
Thymocytes were collected and prepared as a single-cell suspension and then resuspended in PBS with 1% FBS and 1 mM EDTA. To prevent TCR downregulation during tetramer staining, cells were treated with 50 nM dasatinib (Sigma-Aldrich) for 30 min at 37 °C. Cells were then stained with 1 μl (~3 μg) of PE-conjugated Padi4/I-Ab tetramer at 37 °C for 2 h. Surface marker antibodies were added in the final 30 min, with anti-B220 included as a dump gate. After two washes, cells were kept on ice before acquisition on an LSRFortessa or LSRFortessa X-20 (BD Biosciences). For neonatal spleen and bone marrow samples, whole tissues were used for tetramer staining. For adult spleens and bone marrow, CD4 negative selection magnetic beads were used to enrich the CD4 population.

## Analysis of thymic clonal deletion versus clonal diversion
Thymic samples were prepared and initially subjected to surface staining at 4 °C for 30 min to characterize individual subsets, including CD4, CD8, CD3, TCRβ, CD5, CD69, H-2Kb, PD-1 and CD122. Thymocytes were then fixed with freshly prepared 4% paraformaldehyde (electron microscopy grade; Electron Microscopy Sciences) and washed twice with permeabilization buffer from a Foxp3/Transcription Factor Staining Buffer Set (Thermo Fisher). Next, thymocytes were stained with anti-cleaved caspase-3 (Asp 175) at a 1:400 dilution and anti-Foxp3 at a 1:200 dilution for 30 min at room temperature. Samples are analyzed on an LSRFortessa or LSRFortessa X-20 (BD Biosciences).

## Thymocyte activation assay
Thymocytes were isolated from wild-type Rag1−/− B3K506 TCR transgenic mice or B3K506 TCR transgenic mice coexpressing NR4A1–eGFP or NR4A3–Tocky. Thereafter, 1 × 106 thymocytes were cocultured with 1 × 105 bone marrow-derived dendritic cells for 18 h in 200 μl of S-MEM in a 96-well plate at 37 °C. Following coculture, thymocytes were surface stained for 30 min at 25 °C and cooled to 4 °C for flow cytometry acquisition. For the induction of activated caspase-3, thymocytes were similarly cultured for 5 h. Cells were then cell surface stained for 20 min at 25 °C, fixed/permeabilized (BD Cytofix/Cytoperm) for 20 min at 4 °C, stained for cleaved caspase-3 for 1 h at 25 °C and cooled to 4 °C for flow cytometry acquisition.

## Intracellular staining of transcription factors or signaling proteins
Thymi were collected and rapidly processed into a single-cell suspension in RPMI medium containing 2% FBS (Omega Scientific). Peptide-pulsed Tcra-knockout splenocytes were preincubated at 37 °C for at least 30 min at the desired concentration before coculture. A total of 1 × 106 thymocytes per well were then cocultured with 5 × 105 peptide-pulsed splenocytes per well at 37 °C for the indicated duration. At the desired time points, stimulation was terminated by transferring the cells to a V-bottom plate on ice. Once all time points were collected, cells were fixed with freshly prepared 4% paraformaldehyde (electron microscopy grade; Electron Microscopy Sciences) and washed twice with 1× permeabilization buffer from a Foxp3/Transcription Factor Staining Buffer Set (Thermo Fisher). Antibodies specific for the indicated proteins were then used at a 1:100 dilution, except for NR4A3, NFAT and IRF4, which were used at a 1:400 dilution, for 30 min at room temperature in 1× permeabilization buffer (Thermo Fisher). Samples were analyzed using an LSRFortessa or LSRFortessa X-20 (BD Biosciences).

## Flow cytometry-based analysis of transcription factor nuclear translocation
The flow cytometry-based nuclei isolation and staining protocol was adapted from a previous publication . Briefly, thymocytes were stimulated with peptide-pulsed Tcra-knockout splenocytes for the indicated duration. After stimulation, cells were collected, spun down at 300g at 4 °C and immediately resuspended in 250 μl of ice-cold PBS containing 320 mM sucrose, 10 mM HEPES (pH 7.4; Life Technologies), 8 mM MgCl2, 1× Roche EDTA-free cOmplete Protease Inhibitor (Millipore Sigma) and 0.1% (vol/vol) Triton X-100 (Millipore Sigma). The suspension was incubated on ice for 15 min, followed by centrifugation at 2,000g at 4 °C for 10 min. The pellet was then washed twice with 250 μl of ice-cold PBS containing 320 mM sucrose, 10 mM HEPES (pH 7.4), 8 mM MgCl2 and 1× Roche EDTA-free cOmplete Protease Inhibitor, with centrifugation at 2,000g at 4 °C for each wash. After the final wash, pellets were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde (electron microscopy grade; Electron Microscopy Sciences) and incubated on ice for 30 min for fixation, followed by two additional washes and resuspension in 1× PBS with 2% FBS. Nuclei were pelleted by centrifugation at 1,000g and kept at 4 °C until flow cytometry analysis. Samples were analyzed using an LSRFortessa or LSRFortessa X-20 (BD Biosciences).

## RNA flow cytometry
Thymocytes were stained using a live–dead marker (Thermo Fisher) and subjected to cell surface staining for developmental subset characterization. Cells were then fixed and permeabilized using a PrimeFlow RNA Assay kit (Thermo Fisher), blocked with FCR blocking reagent (Biolegend, clone 93) and stained with the following PrimeFlow probe sets (Thermo Fisher): Zap70 AF647 (type 1; VB1-3032514-PF), Lck AF647 (type 1; VB1-3030567-PF) and β-actin (type 4; VB4-10432-PF) as a control. Cells were acquired on a BD LSRFortessa.

## Analysis of intracellular calcium
Mouse thymocytes were prepared and stained with antibodies to CD4 and CD8 to allow subset identification. The cells were then labeled with 1 μM of the calcium indicator dye Indo-1 (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and 0.02% Pluronic F-127 (Thermo Fisher Scientific) in RPMI medium supplemented with 5% FBS at 37 °C for 30 min. After incubation, cells were washed twice with PBS and labeled with a 1:100 dilution of biotinylated peptide-loaded monomers (3K or P-1A; NIH Tetramer Core) on ice for 30 min. Flow cytometry-based calcium assays were then performed. Indo-1 fluorescence was recorded for 30 s to establish a baseline, after which SA (10 μg ml−1; Jackson Immunoresearch) was added at the 30-s mark to induce TCR cross-linking. Calcium responses were continuously recorded for a total duration of 5 to 6 min, as specified in individual experiments. Human thymocytes were prepared using the same protocol, except they were not prelabeled with biotinylated human anti-CD3. Instead, anti-CD3 was added after baseline fluorescence recording and immediately before SA cross-linking. All experiments were conducted using an LSRFortessa flow cytometer.

## p-ERK activation assay
Tcra-knockout splenocytes were pulsed with titrated peptide concentrations in U-bottom 96-well plates and incubated at 37 °C for at least 30 min. Thymocytes were barcoded by labeling each clone with different concentrations of CellTrace Violet (Thermo Fisher Scientific) in PBS at 37 °C for 20 min in the dark. After labeling, complete medium was added for 5 min at 37 °C, followed by washing with ice-cold PBS. Cells were resuspended in PBS at 2.5 × 106 cells per ml and kept on ice. Barcoded thymocytes were combined and added to each well containing peptide-pulsed Tcra-knockout splenocytes. Throughout the process, plates remained on ice. Cells were mixed, briefly centrifuged (300g, 4 °C, 30 s) and transferred to a 37 °C water bath for 5 min to initiate stimulation. For anti-CD3 stimulation, cells were treated with biotinylated CD3 followed by SA cross-linking. Stimulation was terminated by adding 4% formaldehyde (final concentration: 2%), and plates were incubated at room temperature for 30 min for fixation. Cells were washed with FACS buffer (2% FBS and 1 mM EDTA in PBS), permeabilized in 90% ice-cold methanol overnight at –20 °C, washed, rested in FACS buffer at room temperature for 30 min and stained with anti-p-ERK (Cell Signaling Technology) for analysis on an LSRFortessa (BD Biosciences).

## TCR sequencing of polyclonal LatG135D mice
Bulk TCR sequencing libraries of CD4SP and CD8SP thymocytes isolated from adult B6 and B6 LatG135D mice by FACS were generated using SMARTer mouse TCRα/TCRβ Profiling kits (Takara). Libraries were sequenced on a MiSeq with paired-end 300-base pair (bp) reads (Illumina) at the University of California San Diego Institute for Genomic Medicine. Sequencing libraries were aligned and analyzed using MiXCR v4.1.1 (ref. ) and VDJtools v1.2.1 (ref. ).

## Clonotype analysis of neonatal and adult conventional CD4+ and Treg cell populations
Splenic CD4+ (YAe62β CD4+Foxp3–GFP−) and Treg (YAe62β CD4+Foxp3–GFP+) cell populations were isolated from 2-week-old (neonatal) and 8-week-old (adult) mice on a C57BL/6, Zap70Het and LatG135D genetic background at the University of Utah. Cells were sorted to 98% purity using a BD FACSAria II (University of Utah Flow core), resuspended in 300–500 μl TRIzol and shipped to the laboratory of E.S.H. TCRα library preparation was performed as previously described . Briefly, sorted T cells were resuspended in 1 ml of TRIzol (Life Technologies), and total RNA was extracted according to the manufacturer’s protocol, using 20 μg ml−1 RNase-free glycogen (Life Technologies) as a carrier. cDNA was generated from up to 2 μg of RNA by priming with 10 μM oligo(dT) (Promega) and extended with the Omniscript RT kit (Qiagen), following the manufacturer’s instructions. PCR amplification of Tcra was performed using a two-step, nested primer approach. In the first step, 20-cycle PCR reactions were performed with Vα2-specific (5′-CCCTGGGGAAGGCCCTGCTCTCCTGATA-3′) and TCR Cα-specific (5′-GGTACACAGCAGGTTCTGGGTTCTGGATG-3′) primers. One-tenth of the primary PCR reaction was then used as the template for a second 20-cycle PCR with barcoded primers containing the Illumina PE primer and P5/P7 regions. The resulting ~300-bp fragment was gel purified (Gene Clean II, MP Biomedicals) and sequenced on an Illumina MiSeq (single-end, 250 bp). Clonotype counts for Vα2 were determined using TCRklass (version 0.7.2) . TCRα sequencing was performed at the University of Massachusetts Deep Sequencing Core Facility. The TCR repertoire similarity within each population was quantified using the Morisita–Horn similarity index, which ranges from 0 (minimal similarity) to 1 (maximal similarity). Morisita–Horn indices were calculated with abundance data from the 1,000 most frequent clonotypes in each sample, using EstimateS (version 9.1.0). For hierarchical cluster analysis, a dataset of average clonotype abundances in counts per million for neonatal and adult CD4+ and Treg cell populations was used, and hierarchical clustering was performed in MATLAB (v25.1.0.2943329) using the clustergram function. The dataset contained 11,424 nonredundant clonotypes derived from the 1,000 most frequent clones among the sequencing datasets.

## Quantification and statistical analysis
Statistical analysis was conducted on technical replicates or biologically independent mice for each experiment. All experiments described in this study were performed at least twice, with the exact number of independent experiments yielding similar results indicated in the figure captions. Data collection and analysis were not performed blind to the conditions of the experiments. Statistical analyses were performed using nonparametric, two-tailed Mann–Whitney U-tests or two-way ANOVA test as indicated in each figure. Flow cytometry data acquisition was conducted using BD FACSDiva version 8.0.1, and data analysis was performed with FlowJo version 9.9.3, 10.8.1 or 10.10.0. GraphPad Prism 10 (GraphPad Software) was used for statistical analysis and data visualization. Bar graphs display means with overlaid scatter dots or error bars (indicating standard deviation) to illustrate data distribution, as specified in each figure caption. P values are reported as exact values or as P < 0.0001, with statistical significance determined using a 95% confidence level. Sample sizes were not predetermined using statistical methods but were comparable to those reported in previous publications. The data met the assumptions of the statistical tests used, and individual data points are shown in figures where applicable. Although data distributions were assumed to be normal, this was not formally tested. No randomization was applied in the experiments, but age-matched animals were assigned based on genotype.
