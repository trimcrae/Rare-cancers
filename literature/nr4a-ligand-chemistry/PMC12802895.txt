## Abstract
In nucleic acid-binding proteins, short linear motifs (SLiMs)—an important subclass of intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs)—offer diverse opportunities for therapeutic intervention, yet their structural and functional roles remain largely elusive. Away from the active site of cancer immunotherapy target exonuclease TREX1, a novel modulation site formed by the intrinsically disordered α7–α8 loop is discovered by X-ray crystallography with newly identified inhibitors. Despite that the structure of α7–α8 loop upon binding-coupled disordered-to-ordered transition is inhibitor specific, a pattern of multivertex clamping is consistently observed. Mechanistically, the fuzzy TREX1–inhibitor interactions elucidated by structural analysis and molecular dynamics simulations reveal an ensemble of chemical-scale amphiphilic units for inhibitor moieties to anchor to. Functional assays confirm that our newly identified inhibitors disrupt the DNA binding and immunosuppressive activity of TREX1, establishing α7–α8 loop as a druggable SLiM. This work provides a first collection of atomic details for small-molecule inhibition involving a DNA-binding SLiM, and the mechanistic principles uncovered here may be generalized to targeting IDRs in cancer immunotherapy.

## Introduction
The prevalence of intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs) in nearly 50% of eukaryotic proteins indicate that the specific, multivalent, but usually not very strong interactions they mediate are functionally indispensable [ – ]. An important subclass of IDRs is short linear motifs (SLiMs) that directly mediate binding events, including protein–protein and protein–DNA interactions, with typically 3–12 residues in length [ , , ]. However, experimental data and mechanistic knowledge of DNA-binding SLiMs remain limited, hindering the development of classification criteria and predictive models. Given the essential roles of genome maintenance, transcriptional control, and stress responses, there is an urgent need for detailed characterization of DNA-binding IDRs and SLiMs. Furthermore, their functional diversity enriches the mechanistic underpinning of drug development since a broad spectrum of specific, pathogenic interactions could be opted to counteract disease progression [ – ]. Comparing to catalytic centers or canonical sites, SLiMs and IDRs tend to display very different evolutionary patterns showing distinctive sequence signatures even in closely related family members [ , – ]. Small-molecule inhibition focusing on intrinsically flexible protein regions thus draws significant attention as higher safety could be achieved by introducing less off-target effects on housekeeping homologs [ – ]. However, the complicated structural dynamics, including binding-coupled disordered-to-ordered transitions and fuzzy interactions with multiple conformations, present significant challenges to rational drug design and high-throughput screening [ , ]. The establishment of chemical principles currently lacks high-resolution X-ray structures that elucidate SLiM-involved protein sites and their binding with inhibitors or important substrates such as DNA.
Our target is the most abundant 3′-5′ exonuclease in mammalian cells, three prime repair exonuclease 1 (TREX1). The protomer of this dimeric exonuclease is 314 a.a. in both human and mouse (hTREX1 and mTREX1). The active-site motif of TREX1, composed of Asp, Glu, Asp, Asp, and His residues, is highly conserved within the DEDDh nuclease family [ – ]. With the functional activity of binding and degrading single-stranded DNA (ssDNA), double-stranded DNA (dsDNA), single-stranded RNA (ssRNA), and DNA–RNA hybrids, TREX1 plays central roles in DNA repair and immune regulation [ ]. Although TREX1 inhibitors hold great potentials in biomedical applications [ – ], such as enhancing the efficacy of cancer immunotherapy [ – ], radiotherapy [ ], and CRISPR-based genome editing [ ], high structural similarity with housekeeping DEDDh homologs raises the risk of off-target effects [ , ].
A distinctive feature separating TREX1 from the other DEDDh members is the α7–α8 loop (residues 167–178), which is not in human housekeeping exonucleases such as 3′hExo or ISG20 nor in bacterial enzymes like RNase T, ExoI, or ExoX. The crystal structures of apo mTREX1(PDB ID: 3MXJ), nucleotide-bound mTREX1 [ ] and IDR prediction tools PrDOS [ ] and PONDR® (https://pondr.com/) indicate that α7–α8 loop is intrinsically disordered. In the X-ray structures of TREX1 complexed with DNA [ ], this IDR was resolved to lie in the substrate-binding groove, suggesting that α7–α8 loop could be a DNA-binding SLiM that regulates the interactions with nucleic acid substrates. Interestingly, α7–α8 loop is recognized by the eukaryotic linear motif resource (http://elm.eu.org/) [ ] as a potential SLiM despite that its functional activity in DNA binding is elusive. Furthermore, structural information regarding whether α7–α8 loop could be targeted for inhibition is lacking. To date, the few crystal structures of TREX1 with inhibitors (PDB IDs: 7U8Y, 8VL7, 9AVA, 9O0W, 9O0X, and 9O0Y) only inform active site binding with limited details regarding the inhibition efficiency and mechanisms.
By solving the X-ray structures of mTREX1 in complexation with a series of newly found inhibitors, a novel modulation site defined by α7–α8 loop is identified in this work. All inhibitors explored here share a 4-hydroxynaphthalene-2-sulfonic acid core that anchors to the modulation site, while other moieties differing in size, polarity, and scaffold induce α7–α8 loop to adopt specific structures. At the modulation site, the well-defined but inhibitor-dependent conformations of α7–α8 loop reveal a consistent pattern termed here as multivertex clamping. All-atom molecular dynamics (MD) simulations initiating from our inhibitor-bound X-ray structures unravel the fuzzy interactions mediated by α7–α8 loop. In particular, the chemical-scale amphiphilicity due to the proximity of polar and ring groups in inhibitor moieties is shown here as a key feature for mediating robust interactions with the IDR. Inhibitor-coupled biochemical and cell-based assays are also used to confirm that α7–α8 loop is indeed an DNA-binding SLiM that mediates substrate binding and recognition. By resolving the atomic details of small-molecule inhibition at the modulation site formed by a DNA-binding SLiM, our findings uncover important chemical principles for the cancer immunotherapy of targeting TREX1. These mechanistic insights may also be extended to the drug development efforts focusing on other SLiMs or IDRs that are involved in nucleic acid binding.

## Materials and methods

## Protein expression and purification
The trex1 gene (1–242 and 11–242 a.a.) from Mus musculus (UniProt: Q91XB0, Gene ID: 22040) and Homo sapiens (UniProt: Q9NSU2, Gene ID: 11277) was inserted into the expression vector, pET28a, as described [ , ]. The truncated mTREX1 (1–242 a.a.) and hTREX1 (11–242 a.a.) were used in the biochemical assays. The truncated mTREX1 (11–242 a.a.) was used for crystallization experiments. The expression vector was introduced into Escherichia coli BL21-CodonPlus(DE3)-RIPL strain and cultured in Lysogeny broth (LB) medium supplemented with 50 μg/ml kanamycin, 35 μg/ml chloramphenicol, and 25 μg/ml streptomycin. The cells were cultured until they reached an OD.600 of 0.6–0.8, then induced with 1 mM isopropyl-β-D-thiogalactopyranoside at 18°C for 20 h. The harvested cells were dissolved in 50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 8.0, and 300 mM NaCl and underwent high-pressure homogenization using a Desktop NanoLyzer N2 homogenizer (Gogene Corp. Taiwan). The cell lysate was centrifuged at 4°C, 13,000 rpm for 30 min to gain the supernatant. TREX1 was purified sequentially from the supernatant using a HiTrap TALON crude affinity 5 ml column (Cytiva), followed by a HiTrap Heparin HP 5 ml column (Cytiva), and finally a HiLoad 16/60 Superdex 75 prep grade column (GE Healthcare). The purified TREX1 in 50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.0, and 300 mM NaCl was concentrated to 11–35 mg/ml and stored at −20°C.

## Inhibitor sources
The inhibitors used in this study are from the Developmental Therapeutics Program (DTP) database of the National Cancer Institute (NCI), a part of the National Institutes of Health in the United States (https://www.cancer.gov/policies/copyright-reuse). All inhibitors were dissolved in 100% dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) and stored at −20°C, and they were thawed at room temperature before use. PCMPS (C367750, Toronto Research Chemicals) was dissolved in DEPC as a positive control since it broadly inhibits TREX1 and other DEDDh exonucleases [ , ].

## Nuclease activity assays
Inhibitors were mixed with TREX1 at room temperature for 10–15 min. The TREX1 or TREX1–inhibitor mixtures were mixed with 5′- Fluorescein amidites (FAM)-labeled ssDNA (ssDNA 20mer 0.5 µM; 5′-FAM-ACTGGACAAATACTCCGAGG-3′) or polymerase chain reaction (PCR) product (730 bp 6 ng/ul; as described as [ ]) in 20 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.0, 120 mM NaCl, and 10% DMSO at room temperature for 30 min. The FAM-labeled ssDNA and PCR product substrates were used in the other biochemical assays in this study, such as binding assays. For the FAM-labeled ssDNA substrate, after adding 2× TBE–urea sample dye (G-Biosciences, USA) and 95°C for 5 min, the mixtures were applied to 20% Tris-Borate-EDTA (TBE)–urea denature PAGE. The results of FAM-labeled nucleic acid digestion patterns were detected by the blue light-emitting diode (LED) and Electron Multiplying Charge-Coupled Device (EMCCD). For the PCR product substrates, the reactions were stopped by introducing about 4 mg/ml protease K into the mixture at 37°C for 30 min, then separated on a 1% agarose gel and visualized under ultraviolet (UV) light.
The inhibition IC50 (half maximal inhibitory concentration) was determined through activity analysis using the PCR product as the substrate. ImageJ 1.54g software was employed as the analytical tool to quantify DNA digestion patterns, and the GraphPad Prism 8.0.2 software model was used to analyze three independent tests. The quantified values from ImageJ were plotted against the corresponding inhibitor concentrations using the software model ([Inhibitor] vs. response – Variable slope): Y = Bottom + (Top-Bottom)/(1+(IC50/X)^HillSlope), to obtain the IC50 value. HillSlope describes the steepness of the family of curves, while Top and Bottom represent plateaus in the units of the Y axis.

## Intrinsic tryptophan fluorescence (ITF)
A 300 μl reaction mixture containing 20 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.0, 120 mM NaCl, 2 mM MgCl2, 10% DMSO, 2 μM protein, and the specific inhibitor was incubated at room temperature for 10 min. The emission spectrum was measured with 1-nm bandwidth resolution at 290 and 500 nm wavelengths by FluoroMax-4 Plus (Horiba Instruments Inc.) with a 1 ml quartz cuvette. Spectra were plotted, and the fluorescence intensity collected at 340–350 nm was used for analysis using GraphPad Prism 8.0.2 software. The dissociation constant (Kd) was calculated using the Specific binding with the Hill slope model: Y = Bmax × Xh/(Kdh + Xh), where X represents the inhibitor concentration, Bmax denotes the maximum specific binding, and h represents the Hill slope. Three measurements were taken for each sample and averaged. The Kd between TREX1 and NSC37203 or NSC37204 could not be measured due to precipitation problems or interference signals.

## Cell cultures and treatments
HEK293, HEK293T, Huh7, MDA-MB-231, and MCF-7 cells were grown at 37°C in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS). HCC1395 cells were grown at 37°C in Roswell Park Memorial Institute 1640 medium with 10% FBS. TREX1 inhibitors were reconstituted in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) at 20 mM in stock and stored at −20°C. TREX1 inhibitor stocks were diluted in cell culture medium for cell treatment experiments to a final concentration of 10 μM unless specified. Cells were treated in the culture medium with TREX1 inhibitors for 48 h of incubation at 37°C and collected for RNA or protein analyses. Tunicamycin (Sigma–Aldrich, T7765-1MG) was reconstituted in DMSO to a final concentration of 1 mg/ml in stock and stored at 4°C, which was diluted to 4 μg/ml in cell culture medium for cell culture use.

## UV-irradiation of live cells
Cell cultures were washed with and maintained in phosphate buffered saline (PBS) prior to the exposure to UVC in a CL-1000 UV crosslinker at the indicated Ultraviolet (UV) doses. After UV exposure, cells were reconstituted in a regular cell culture medium at 37°C for post-UV-irradiation incubation.

## DNA plasmids transfection
DNA transfection was performed according to the manufacturer’s instructions using Mirus TransIT-X2 Transfection Reagent. Cells were seeded in 6-well plates 18–24 h prior to transfection. A total of 1 µg of plasmid DNA was transfected into each well of a 6-well plate of HCC1395 cells by mixing 4 µl of TransIT-X2 reagent and 250 µl of Opti-MEM. After 15 min of incubation, the mixture was evenly distributed dropwise into the 6-well plate cells and incubated at 37°C for 5 h. After 5 h of incubation, Opti-MEM mixture was changed back to the regular culture medium. Cells were harvested 48–72 h after transfection.

## Cell counting kit-8 (cck-8) assay
Cells were seeded in 96-well plates and incubated to reach an 80% confluency. Cells were treated with serially-diluted TREX1 inhibitors and then incubated for 24 or 48 h at 37°C. Then, 10 µl of CCK-8 solution was added to each well of the 96-well plate for 1 h at 37°C in the incubator. The absorbance at 450 nm was measured using a VERSA max microplate reader.

## Quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction (qPCR)
The reverse transcription was performed according to the manufacturer’s instructions using the iScript (cDNA) synthesis kit (Bio-Rad, 1708891) with random hexamer from 500 ng of RNA extracted by TRIzol reagent. The target cDNA and the internal control, GAPDH, were quantified by real-time PCR using SYBR Green (ABI, 4367659) on a Step One Real-Time PCR System (ABI) according to the manufacturer’s manual. Each sample was assayed in technical triplicate. Each 10 µl reaction complex contained 5 µl 2× SYBR Green Mix, 0.5 µl of each 10 µM primer working solution (forward and reverse), 1 µl cDNA template, and 3 µl ddH2O. The samples were run in the real-time PCR program as follows: 50°C for 2 min, 95°C for 10 min, followed by 40 cycles of 95°C for 15 s and 60°C for 1 min. All data presented were relative quantification, and the relative expression of each target gene was normalized by the internal control (GAPDH).

## Immunoblotting assay
Harvested cell pellets were lysed in Triton X-100 lysis buffer (20 mM Tris, 137 mM NaCl, 1.5 mM MaCl2, 1 mM ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA), and 1% Triton X-100) or Radioimmunoprecipitation assay (RIPA) lysis buffer [50 mM Tris, 150 mM NaCl, 5 mM EDTA, 1% NP-40, 0.1% sodium dodecyl sulphate (SDS), and 12 mM sodium deoxycholate] containing protease inhibitor at 4°C for 20 min. The Bradford assay was used to determine protein concentration. Each protein sample was mixed with 4× SDS sample buffer [240 mM Tris–HCl (pH 6.8), 5% β-mercaptoethanol, 8% SDS. 0.4% bromophenol blue, 40% glycerol]. The samples were separated in 12% sodium dodecyl sulphate–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis and transferred to polyvinylidene fluoride membrane (PVDF) membrane. The PVDF membrane was blocked in 5% nonfat milk (wt/vol) in PBST (0.1% Tween-20 in PBS) at 4°C for 2 h. Primary antibodies were used at their optimal working concentrations according to the manufacturer’s instructions in PBST at 4°C overnight. The dilution factor of each primary antibodies in PBST are as following: rabbit anti-TREX1(D8E2O) at 1:1000; rabbit anti-cGAS (D1D3G) at 1:1000; rabbit anti-STING (D2P2F) at 1:1000; mouse anti-FLAG-M2 at 1:5000; mouse anti-α-tubulin at 1:10 000. The membrane was washed and then incubated with diluted secondary antibody (horseradish peroxidase-linked donkey anti-rabbit IgG antibody or anti-mouse IgG antibody) at 1:5000 in PBST containing 5% milk for 1 h at room temperature. Finally, the membrane was incubated with SuperSignal West Pico Maximum Sensitivity Substrate and the signal was detected by chemiluminescence.

## Crystallization and soaking experiments
The truncated mTREX1 (11–242 a.a.) was concentrated at 11–35 mg/ml for crystallization. mTREX1 protein was mixed with 2 mM inhibitor for 10 min, and co-crystallization was done using the vapor diffusion method at 20°C. The crystallization conditions are shown in and . Three conditions of apo-mTREX1 crystals were crystallized by the vapor diffusion method at 20°C ( ), then used in soaking experiments by adding 2 mM inhibitors to obtain mTREX1–inhibitor complex structures—the soaking durations ranging from 19 h to 37 days ( ). Before data collection, all crystals were cryoprotected using Paraton-N (Hampton Research, USA).

## Structure determination and data collection
All diffraction data were collected at TPS05A, TPS07A, and TLS15A in NSRRC, Taiwan, and were processed using HKL2000. The phase problem was solved via the molecular replacement method using Balbes from online-CCP4 (https://ccp4online.ccp4.ac.uk/ccp4online/), and model building and refinement were conducted using Coot 0.9.6 and Phenix 1.19.2–4158. Ligand structure building utilized the eLBOW program from Phenix 1.19.2–4158. Table  and list all data collection and refinement statistics. Diffraction structure factors and structural coordinates were deposited in the RCSB Protein Data Bank.
 

## Electrophoretic mobility shift assay
Inhibitors were mixed with TREX1 at room temperature for 10–15 min. The TREX1 or TREX1-inhibitor mixtures were mixed with 5′- FAM-labeled dsDNA (0.5 µM) or PCR product (24 ng/μl) in 20 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.0, 120 mM NaCl, 10% DMSO, and 20 mM EDTA on ice for 15–20 min. Following incubation, the reaction mixture was mixed with 6 × loading dye (0.35% orange G and 20% Ficoll) and loaded onto an 10% TBE native gel or 0.5% agarose gel. DNA-binding patterns were visualized by FAM or via SYBR® Safe DNA Gel Stain (Invitrogen™) staining under blue LED or UV light.

## Fluorescence anisotropy
The half-maximal inhibitory concentration IC50 of inhibitors against TREX1 DNA binding ability was determined using fluorescence anisotropy measurements as previously described [ ]. Briefly, the measurements were performed by incubating 20 nM mTREX1 or hTREX1 with 5 nM 5′-FAM-labeled ssDNA and varying concentrations of the inhibitors for 15 min at room temperature in a buffer system containing 20 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.0, 120 mM NaCl, 20 mM EDTA, and 10% DMSO. The fluorescence anisotropy signals were measured from three independent trials and analyzed using GraphPad Prism 8.0.2 software. The dose-dependent binding curves were fitted using the built-in “[Inhibitor] vs. response – Variable slope” model in GraphPad Prism, following the equation Y = Bottom + (Top-Bottom)/(1+(IC50/X)^HillSlope), to determine the IC50 values that represent the inhibitory potency against substrate binding. The IC50 values for NSC37203 and NSC37204 could not be determined due to compound precipitation and signal interference, respectively.

## All-atom MD simulation
The all-atom models of apo-mTREX1, mTREX1–37203, mTREX1–37204, and mTREX1–37215 simulations were developed using the X-ray structures resolved in this work. The CHARMM software [ ] was employed for patching missing atoms, and the CHARMM36 all-atom protein force field [ ] was used to describe the molecular interaction energetics. The molecular mechanics force field of NSC37203 and NSC37204 were obtained by the CHARMM General Force Field (CGenFF) program [ , ]. Since the CGenff of NSC37215 included terms of nonzero penalty scores, the fftk tooltkit [ ] implemented in VMD [ ] was employed to obtain these force field parameters following the CHARMM parametrization protocol. Each simulation system of mTREX1 was solvated in a dedecahetran box of TIP3P water at the 1.5 mM ionic strength with at least 1.0 nm between any protein atom and the periodic boundary. The GROMACS software [ ] was used to conduct the MD simulations in explicit solvent. The 1.2 nm cut-off was used for van der Waals and short-range electrostatic interactions with a switching function effective at 1.0 nm. After 10 ns of heating and equilibration, the production run was conducted at 300 K and 1.103 bar for 2 μs. The sampled configurations were saved every 1 ps for analysis. For each simulation model, consistency of the molecular interaction patterns of mTREX1 with the inhibitor is examined by performing multiple independent runs of 2 μs (mTREX1–37204 and mTREX1–37215) or extending the duration of MD simulation (mTREX1–37203).

## Results

## Identification of new TREX1 inhibitors
To identify new TREX1 inhibitors, we started from the inhibitors previously reported for other DEDDh nucleases [ , ]. The inhibition efficiencies of their derivatives and additional compounds screened through docking experiments were evaluated by in vitro inhibitor-coupled nuclease activity assays on human and mouse TREX1 (hTREX1 and mTREX1) against ssDNA and dsDNA substrates ( ). P-chloromercuriphenyl sulfonate (PCMPS), a universal DEDDh exonuclease inhibitor, was used as a positive control [ , ]. New TREX1 inhibitors identified through this approach using the NCI compound library include derivatives of pontacyl violet 6R (PV6R) [ ], NSC37203, NSC37204, and NSC37215, and NSC292217 and NSC637699 from in silico docking. The results show that NSC37203, NSC37204, NSC37215, and NSC292217 inhibit hTREX1 effectively, while NSC637699 exhibits weak inhibition (Fig.  ). For mTREX1, the inhibition efficiencies of NSC37203 and NSC637699 are similar to those of targeting hTREX1 while NSC37204, NSC37215, and NSC292217 display relatively weaker inhibition (Fig.  ). Furthermore, the half-maximal inhibitory concentrations (IC50) of these compounds were determined by quantifying the remaining dsDNA in inhibitor-coupled nuclease activity assays (Fig. and  , and   and ). The results demonstrate that NSC37203 and NSC37204 are the most potent TREX1 inhibitors, while NSC37215 and NSC292217 have comparable efficiencies.
 Next, the Kd between inhibitors and TREX1 were measured using intrinsic tryptophan fluorescence (ITF). The Kd values were successfully determined for NSC37215 and NSC292217, whereas the measurements for NSC37203 and NSC37204 were hindered due to precipitation or signal interference. Notably, NSC37215 exhibits a lower Kd with hTREX1 than that with mTREX1 (Fig.  and  , and ), and the stronger interaction with hTREX1 corresponds to higher inhibition efficiency (Fig. - ). In contrast, although NSC292217 has similar Kd values for binding hTREX1 and mTREX1, its inhibition efficiencies toward the two homologues are different. This inhibitory behavior of NSC292217 could be related to the lower thermostability of hTREX1 [ ].

## Inhibitors bind mTREX1 at a novel modulation site by multivertex clamping
We first crystallized and determined three apo-mTREX1 structures under various conditions ( ). All apo structures of mTREX1 are dimeric with a largely disordered α7–α8 loop between α7 and α8. By soaking inhibitors into the apo-mTREX1 crystals and by co-crystallizing mTREX1 with different inhibitors, a total of three mTREX1–NSC37203 complex structures, three mTREX1–NSC37204 complex structures, and five mTREX1–NSC37215 complex structures were determined. Details of experimental conditions as well as the statistics of data collection and refinement are reported in Table  , Supplementary text, and – .
In all of the complexed crystal structures, only one of the mTREX1 monomers binds an inhibitor molecule. Instead of the active site, all of the tested inhibitors bind mTREX1 at a novel modulation site formed by α8 (180–188 a.a) and α7–α8 loop (Fig.  and  ). The α7–α8 loop is fully resolved in the bound protomer but remains unstructured in the other, indicating inhibitor binding-coupled disordered-to-ordered transition. Binding at the newly discovered modulation site is consistently observed when using different strategies to determine the complex structures of mTREX1 with NSC37203, NSC37204 (Fig. 2A and B, Supplementary Figs 6 and 7, and Supplementary Tables 5 and 6), and NSC37215, despite that NSC37215 shows two inhibitor conformations, NSC37215-1 and NSC37215-2 (Fig.  ,  and , and and ). Binding at the modulation site thus persists through the various crystallization conditions explored for each inhibitor (Fig.  and ).
 Comparison of the chemical structures of the newly identified inhibitors NSC37203, NSC37204 and NSC37215 with previously reported compounds targeting TREX1, including in vivo-validated inhibitors [ , ] and those in the complexed structures with TREX1 (PDB IDs: 7U8Y, 8VL7, 9AVA, 9O0W, 9O0X, and 9O0Y), shows that our inhibitors possess different chemical scaffolds. The resolved structures of previously reported TREX1 inhibitors all illustrate binding at the active site of TREX1. In contrast, NSC37203, NSC37204, and NSC37215 bind TREX1 at a distinct region formed by the disordered α7–α8 loop. Therefore, the compounds identified here constitute a new class of TREX1 inhibitors that can bind the modulation site.
Inhibitor binding at the modulation site of α7–α8 loop is observed to follow a pattern that is deduced here as multivertex clamping. A vertex consistently observed in all complexed structures of mTREX1 is Trp188 in α8 that interacts with the bound inhibitor through an indirect hydrogen bond mediated by a water molecule and stacks the inhibitor by a protruding structure (Fig. and  , and  – ). Another vertex is contributed by α7–α8 loop that involves Lys175 or Arg174 forming hydrogen bonding and stacking interactions with the inhibitor (Fig.  and  , and  ). In binding NSC37203 that has a smaller size among the new inhibitors studied here, Lys175 of the α7–α8 loop adopts a “standing” conformation, while in binding NSC37204 and NSC37215 of larger sizes, Arg174 becomes the anchoring residue (Fig.  and  , and  ). Our X-ray structures thus illustrate that inhibitors of different sizes can induce alternative IDR conformations for binding mTREX1 (Fig.  ). In fact, α7–α8 loop also contributes a third vertex for the inhibitors to clamp on, albeit the participating residues exhibit more variations ( – ) as compared to the other two vertices discussed above.

## α7–α8 loop exhibits different conformations in binding inhibitors or substrates
As detailed in Supplementary Text, analysis of forty-seven crystal structures in PDB further demonstrates that α7–α8 loop of mTREX1 and hTREX1 is indeed an IDR. In the X-ray structures of mTREX1–DNA complexes (PDB IDs 5YWS and 5YWU) [ ], in which the substrate is bound at the active site, are the rare cases in which α7–α8 loop was resolved, indicating a substrate binding-coupled disordered-to-ordered transition. The mTREX1–DNA structures indicate that the 172–176 a.a. region of α7–α8 loop is involved in DNA binding at the active site, in which Arg174 and Lys175 display consistent orientations (Fig. ). An intriguing question is whether the IDR assumes distinct structural states when binding different partners.
 At the modulation site, the mTREX1–inhibitor complex structures resolved here show that α7–α8 loop adopts conformations different from those in binding DNA (Figs and  ). Arg174 that contacts DNA shifts from the substrate-bound position to interact with NSC37203 while Lys175 forms a steric epiphysis to stack and hydrogen bond the inhibitor in the mTREX1–NSC37203 complex (Fig. and  ). The X-ray structures of mTREX1–NSC37204 and mTREX1–NSC37215 complexes also illustrate that the inhibitor-bound conformations of α7–α8 loop are different from that in the DNA-bound form (Fig.  and  ). Inhibitor binding at the modulation site may thus disrupt DNA binding and inhibit the nuclease activity of mTREX1.
Together, the crystal structures of mTREX1–inhibitor complexes resolved here and mTREX1–DNA structures [ ] show that as an IDR, α7–α8 loop undergoes disordered-to-ordered transition upon binding to substrate or inhibitors (Figs and  ). However, α7–α8 loop is stabilized to distinct structures by interacting with DNA and inhibitors at different sites. Even at the modulation site uncovered here, each inhibitor induces α7–α8 loop to adopt a specific conformation (Fig.  and ). The context-dependent structure upon disordered-to-ordered transition illustrates the fuzzy interactions mediated by α7–α8 loop.

## Fuzzy interactions of mTREX1 with inhibitors in aqueous solution
To investigate the inhibitor-mediated structural dynamics at the modulation site, the complex structures determined here were employed to initiate all-atom MD simulations in explicit solvent with the all-atom MD of apo-mTREX1 as a reference for comparison. The Cα root-of-mean-squared-fluctuation (RMSF) in Å over the 2 μs production run of apo-mTREX1 indicates that, as an IDR, α7–α8 loop is indeed highly flexible (Fig. ). In the inhibitor-bound trajectories of mTREX1 with NSC37215, NSC37204, and NSC37203, the Cα RMSF of α7–α8 loop drops by half (Fig.  ). This result is consistent with the results of X-ray structures that inhibitor binding stabilizes the intrinsically disordered α7–α8 loop.
 The all-atom MD simulations also illustrate multivertex clamping as in the complexed crystal structures of mTREX1, but fuzzy interactions with broader participation of other protein residues are identified. By conducting multiple independent production runs or extended simulation to ensure consistent behaviors, inhibitors are found to interact with mTREX1 at three sites. Site A is the modulation site discovered by the mTREX1–inhibitor structures resolved here. Site B is around the substrate-binding groove in the mTREX1–DNA structures [ ]. Site C is the modulation site in the other protomer of the mTREX1–inhibitor structures. To delineate the specific interactions, only the inhibitor atoms exhibiting significant hydrogen bonds or contacts with mTREX1 for more than 10% of a trajectory are considered.
For the case of mTREX1 complexed with NSC37215, a representative example is trajectory mTREX1–NSC37215A, in which NSC37215 stayed in the modulation site (Site A) formed by α7–α8 loop and α8 during the 2 μs production run (Fig.  and  , and ). Around Trp188 in α8 that is a clamping vertex identified in X-ray structures (cf. Fig.  and  ), His53 in the α2-β3 loop connecting α2 and β3 is also found in the MD simulation to hydrogen bond the sulfonate group from a different side ( ) while the naphthalene ring hosting SO3− makes contacts with the side chains of His53, Pro54, and Trp188 (Fig.  and ). Nearby the Arg174 vertex in α7–α8 loop discussed earlier, the MD trajectory indicates the participation of Ser169 in α7–α8 loop and Arg185 of α8 in hydrogen bonding the amine groups of NSC37215 ( and and ). Next to these polar anchors, the MD trajectory shows Pro168, Ile182, and Leu186 in α7–α8 loop and α8 making hydrophobic contacts with the benzene rings of the inhibitor ( ).
A general mechanism emerges from the multivertex clamping of mTREX1 in X-ray structures and MD simulation is thus that the polar and hydrophobic ring groups in the various moieties of the inhibitor form closely-spaced hydrogen bonding and stacking interactions with the specific protein environment at a vertex (Fig.  and ). Despite the fuzzy interactions due to the structural dynamics of IDR, robustness in such a pattern of inhibitor binding is tested by initiating multiple independent MD runs from the initial structure. Out of the five trajectories with at least 2 μs of production run in each, NSC37215 entering Site A in the inter-helical pocket of α7–α8 loop is observed in three, which reveal similar residues in clamping the inhibitor as in the mTREX1–NSC37215A trajectory (Fig.  ). In one case (mTREX1–NSC37215C), NSC37215 left the original protomer and entered the corresponding binding pocket of α7–α8 loop in the other protomer (Site C). The specific interactions of NSC37215 in the IDR-involved modulation site are thus robust in an aqueous solution under thermal fluctuations (Fig.  and ).
In the other two trajectories, NSC37215 is observed to exit the original pocket and enter a different site. The alternative binding site is on the α6 side of α7–α8 loop and is referred to as Site B (Fig.  and based on the mTREX1–NSC37215B trajectory), which is similar to the substrate-binding position in the mTREX1–DNA structures discussed earlier [ ]. At Site B, a key vertex is Arg128 in the β4–α6 loop that makes hydrogen bonds with the inhibitor while the nearby Tyr129 and Pro132 of α6 (129–140 a.a.) contribute stacking interactions ( ). At another vertex, Arg224 and Lys230 in the N-terminal segment mediate hydrogen bonding while Val229 and Tyr233 form hydrophobic contacts with NSC37215 ( ). At Site B, the LPS cluster (Lue24–Pro25–Ser26 in α1) that was shown to modulate the substrate recognition and nuclease activity of mTREX1 [ ] also constitutes a vertex for inhibitor binding with the hydrogen bonding provided by Ser26 and the hydrophobic stacking offered by Pro25 together with Pro29 in α1–β2 loop ( ). Therefore, all-atom MD simulations illustrate that the substrate-binding groove (Site B) of mTREX1 can also mediate multivertex clamping to bind NSC37215.
In the all-atom MD simulations of NSC37204 and NSC37203, multivertex clamping at both Site A and Site B are also observed ( , and – ), for which the details are reported in Supplementary Text. Interestingly, the specific interactions at different sites can be corresponded with the residue RMSF profiles as discussed in Supplementary Text with Fig.  and  , and  . Since the vertices of both binding sites involve loop regions, this result shows that inhibitor-mediated modulation on loop flexibility depends on the context of the binding pocket. Although each inhibitor exhibits distinctive details in the multivertex clamping with mTREX1, the major anchoring residues at both Site A and Site B are consistently observed in the all-atom MD simulations and in X-ray structures (Figs and  , – , and – ), revealing robustness of the specific though fuzzy interactions.

## Chemical-scale amphiphilicity for anchoring to IDR-involved sites
Based on the analysis of X-ray structures and all-atom MD simulations, the different cases of molecular binding involving an IDR uncovers a common theme—at a vertex of inhibitor clamping, the polar and nonpolar groups in an inhibitor moiety form closely-spaced hydrogen bonding and stacking interactions with the protein residues. For example, the 4-hydroxynaphthalene-2-sulfonic acid moiety common in NSC37215, NSC37204, and NSC37203 mediates both hydrogen-bonding and hydrophobic interactions with mTREX1 via amphiphilic residues such as Trp188 and Arg174/Lys175 or with several nearby residues of different side-chain polarities (Figs and  , and – ). The multiple choices for the interactions at a vertex highlight the fuzzy interactions emergent from the flexible structural dynamics. With the involvement of an IDR, the formation of a network of specific hydrogen bonds is difficult without a well-defined conformation. In this case, the chemical-scale amphiphilicity of an inhibitor moiety such as 4-hydroxynaphthalene-2-sulfonic acid, which is based on the chemical structures and sizes of its polar and ring groups, appears to be a key property for mediating robust interactions with the IDR. As illustrated in Fig.  and based on the trajectories of mTREX1 with NSC37215, the other moieties of the inhibitor also mediate closely-spaced hydrogen bonding and stacking interactions at both Site A and Site B. This result and those of the other inhibitors discussed earlier indicate that the mTREX1 structure exhibits multiple amphiphilic units at the chemical scale, many of which involve residues in flexible regions such as α7–α8 loop, β4–α6 loop, and the LPS cluster. The important contributors of inhibitor binding, including Pro168, Arg174, Lys175, Arg185, Arg128, and the LPS cluster discussed earlier (Fig. and – ), can also be found in the closely related homologue hTREX1. Such recurring chemical-scale amphiphilic units in proteins are coined here as amphicons. The multivertex clamping at the IDR-involved binding sites identified in this work is thus the matching of inhibitor moieties with mTREX1 amphicons in the presence of complicated structural dynamics.
 

## Inhibitors targeting the IDR-involved modulation site impede substrate binding
The modulation site in the pocket of α8 and α7–α8 loop (MD simulation Site A) includes key residues such as Arg174 that are essential to binding DNA [ ]. In substrate-binding groove (MD simulation Site B), mutations of Arg128 in β4–α6 loop were shown to reduce the binding affinity of mTREX1 with DNA and are linked to the autoimmune disease systemic lupus erythematosus [ , ]. Interestingly, β4–α6 loop provides a vertex along with those in β4, α6, and LPS cluster to clamp the inhibitor (Figs and  and  , and  ). This result highlights that the inhibitor-clamping vertices identified in our X-ray structures and MD simulations offer mechanistic insight for biochemical and clinical data.
 Since α7–α8 loop is located at the interface between the modulation site and the substrate-binding groove (Figs  and  ), the DNA binding of mTREX1 is expected to be affected by the new inhibitors identified in this work. To test this proposition, electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) and fluorescence anisotropy measurements were performed with short (20 bp) and long (730 bp) dsDNA using PCMPS as a positive control [ ]. The EMSA results confirm that NSC37203, NSC37204, NSC37215, and NSC292217 all inhibit mTREX1 in binding DNA (Fig.  and ). NSC37203 exhibits the most significant inhibition on binding, which is consistent with its ranking in reducing the nuclease activity of mTREX1 (Fig.  ). In contrast, NSC637699 that does not lower nuclease activity shows no effect on the substrate binding of mTREX1. Similar results are also observed with hTREX1 ( ). The IC50 of NSC37215 in the inhibition of binding DNA was measured by fluorescence anisotropy as 3.522 ± 0.159 μM for hTREX1 and 1.889 ± 0.078 μM for mTREX1 (Fig.  and ), while the values of other inhibitors are unavailable due to precipitation or self-fluorescent signals. Together, these results show that NSC37203, NSC37204, and NSC37215 inhibit the nuclease activity of TREX1 by interfering with DNA binding.

## Inhibitors modulating the IDR exhibit efficacy in cellular models
Blocking immunosilencing mediated by the nuclease activity of hTREX1 has been shown to enhance type I IFN expression through the cGAS-STING pathway [ – , , ]. To test whether the inhibitors that target the modulation site and disrupt DNA binding could specifically inhibit the nuclease activity of hTREX1 in cells, MCF-7 (breast) and Calu-3 (lung) cancer cells—both expressing hTREX1, cGAS, and STING—were used as models with their IFN mRNA levels as functional readouts [ , ] (Fig.  , , and Supplementary Text). To evaluate inhibitor specificity at the cellular levels, HCC1395 cells that lack endogenous hTREX1 were transfected with wild-type or D18N-mutant hTREX1 as negative controls (Fig. and ). All inhibitors identified here display low cytotoxicity, except NSC637699, with >80% viability across the three cell lines ( ).
 To confirm the functionality of the DNA-sensing pathway, MCF-7, Calu-3, and hTREX1-deficient HCC1395 cells were UV-irradiated to induce cytosolic DNA accumulation for IFNB1 expression [ , ] ( and Supplementary Text). In MCF-7 and Calu-3 cells, IFNB1 mRNA increased up to 0.4 mJ/cm² UV but plateaued at higher doses (Fig. ). In contrast, HCC1395 cells showed a dose-dependent IFNB1 response, confirming that hTREX1 expression under high UV-irradiation suppresses IFNB1 induction, consistent with previous findings [ ]. In UV-irradiated MCF-7 cells, NSC37215 was shown to effectively reduce the nuclease activity of hTREX1 (Fig.  ), whereas NSC37203 and NSC37204 could not be evaluated in this assay due to solubility issues. In HCC1395 cells, exogenous expression of active hTREX1 (but not the inactive D18N mutant) lowered IFNB1 mRNA, and NSC37215 specifically reversed this suppression and increased IFNB1 levels, whereas NSC292217 also raised IFNB1 but through a nonspecific mechanism. (Fig.  and  ,  , and Supplementary Text), confirming the in vivo specificity of NSC37215. This result, together with the X-ray structures determined here and the substrate binding measurements based on in vitro biochemical essays, establishes the intrinsically disordered α7–α8 loop as a DNA-binding SLiM that plays important roles in the functional activity of TREX1. The other details of cellular experiments are reported in Supplementary Text.

## Discussion
For the multifunctional nuclease studied here, TREX1 is a key factor of immune evasion in cancer since it degrades cytosolic DNA and thereby suppresses cGAS-STING activation and type I interferon (IFN-I) signaling [ – ]. The critical participation of TREX1 in DNA repair and in CRISPR-Cas9-mediated homologous recombination [ ] further expands its therapeutic value as a drug target. Whether the flexible segment of TREX1 could be categorized as a SLiM, and if so, whether they could be targeted for small-molecule inhibition, are thus key questions closely related to cancer treatments in terms of immunotherapy, chemoresistance, and drug safety. However, active-site inhibition is concerned with selectivity and side effects since TREX1 belongs to the DEDDh exonuclease family in which the catalytic residues are conserved across numerous family members.
The X-ray structures of mTREX1 bound with the newly identified inhibitors illustrate that the intrinsically disordered α7–α8 loop (residues 167–178) forms a novel modulation site. Remarkably, the inhibitor-stabilized conformations of α7–α8 loop are different from its structure in binding DNA. Biochemical assays confirm that the new inhibitors identified here indeed obstruct the DNA binding of TREX1. The impact of our inhibitors on the nuclease activity of TREX1 in cancer cells is also validated. The intrinsically disordered α7–α8 loop is thus established here as a DNA-binding SLiM. In the category of DNA-binding proteins, the collection of mTREX1 structures resolved here is a previously unavailable example of atomic details for an IDR-involved modulation site and the inhibitor-bound poses thereat.
The information extracted from our crystal structures and MD simulations also reveals chemical principles for the inhibitor development involving SLiM or IDR in general. Discovery of the new binding pocket in mTREX1 illustrates that a manner in which a SLiM offers specificity in drug development is formation of modulation site. The interactions mediated by pontacyl violet 6R (PV6R) derivatives—including NSC37203, NSC37204, and NSC37215—at the modulation site in mTREX1 are fuzzy, since the stabilized structure of α7–α8 loop is inhibitor dependent. Using all-atom MD simulations initiating from our mTREX1–inhibitor structures further captures how does a disordered loop adopt alternative conformations to adapt to the binding with different inhibitors. Despite the fuzziness due to context-dependent disordered-to-ordered transition, the chemical mechanism of IDR’s druggability can be deduced by putting together the atomic details of X-ray structures and MD simulations. Together, they provide the rarely observed structural details of fuzzy inhibitor modulation in proteins. For the multivertex clamping at the modulation site of mTREX1, we show that the α7–α8 loop and α8 contribute vertices with various levels of fuzziness.
Importantly, the fuzzy interactions with inhibitors are identified to arise from an ensemble of chemical-scale amphiphilic units in mTREX1 that are termed here as amphicons. The SLiM-inhibitor interactions thus differ from the conventional view of drug binding that typically involves on a network of specific hydrogen bonding. The structural analysis presented earlier shows the specific IDR conformations stabilized by different inhibitors. Our results thus illustrate that inhibitor moieties matching with protein amphicons can guide the disordered segments into ordered states while retaining specificity. With SLiMs and IDRs increasingly recognized as druggable regions in nucleic acid-binding proteins, these findings suggest that a generalizable paradigm could be established for targeting disease-relevant IDRs such as in BRD4 and p53 proteins [ , , ]. An instrumental step is obtaining detailed structural information as captured by the combination of X-ray crystallography and MD simulations conducted here. Another important implication is that the safety concerns of active-site inhibition within the DEDDh nuclease family could be avoided by targeting the modulation site, which opens new avenues for next-generation TREX1 inhibitors toward enhanced cancer immunotherapy and lowered therapy resistance.
