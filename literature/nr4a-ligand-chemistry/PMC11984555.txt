LiuWenhua LiGaoxiang https://orcid.org/0000-0002-6039-9805ShiJianfeng Pharmacological inhibitors of platelets have become the foundation in the clinical treatment of thrombo-ischemic diseases. These antiplatelet drugs show variable pharmacological responses between individuals. For example, response variability of aspirin has been reported in up to 30% of patients, and high on-treatment platelet reactivity of clopidogrel and prasugrel has been reported in 10% to 45% and 5% to 10% of patients, respectively. Therefore, understanding the molecular mechanism that regulates platelet activation and developing new antiplatelet drugs against thrombotic diseases are of great clinical importance.
Nuclear receptors, which include 48 members in humans, are a large family of ligand-regulated transcription factors that play critical roles in development, physiology, and pharmacology. , Nuclear receptors have become primary targets for drug discovery in many therapeutic areas. The agonists or antagonists of nuclear receptors account for ≈13.5% of all Food and Drug Administration-approved drugs. Although platelets are anucleate cells, we recently reported that the circadian nuclear receptor Rev-erbα is involved in the regulation of platelet activation and thrombus formation. 
NR4A1 (nuclear receptor subfamily 4 group A member 1; also known as TR3 in humans, Nur77 in mice, and NGFI-B in rats), along with NR4A2/Nurr1 and NR4A3/Nor1, constitutes the NR4A. NR4A1 is classified as an orphan nuclear receptor without identified physiological ligands, but a variety of compounds were found to bind to NR4A1 at sites other than the putative ligand-binding pocket and alter its function. , NR4A1 not only serves as a transcription factor regulating the expression of downstream genes, but also influences the biological functions of other proteins through nongenomic mechanisms. – NR4A1 has pleiotropic regulatory effects. It influences the development and function of T cells in the immune system, – acts as either a tumor suppressor or promoter depending on the context of cancer, plays an integral role in neuronal homeostasis and neuroprotection, attenuates fibrosis by acting as an inhibitor of TGF-β signaling in fibroblasts, and regulates metabolism and energy balance in the liver, muscle, and adipose tissue. Importantly, NR4A1 has recently emerged as a key player in cardiovascular pathophysiology. – However, the expression profile of NR4A subfamily members in platelets and their biological functions in platelet aggregation and activation have never been reported.
In this study, we report our discovery that nuclear receptor NR4A1 is expressed in platelets and acts as a negative regulator of platelet activation and thrombus formation. Mechanically, NR4A1 hinders platelet function through a nongenomic CAP1/AC/PKA (adenylyl cyclase-associated protein 1/adenylyl cyclase/protein kinase A) signaling pathway. Importantly, NR4A1 is upregulated in platelets from patients with hypercholesterolemia, and NR4A1-specific agonists inhibit hypercholesterolemia-induced platelet hyperreactivity.

## Results

## NR4A Subfamily of Nuclear Receptor NR4A1 Is Present in Human and Mouse Platelets
To our knowledge, the expression of the NR4A subfamily of nuclear receptors in platelets has never been reported. To detect the expression of NR4A subfamily members in platelets, we performed quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction and immunoblotting on total mRNA and protein, respectively, obtained from human platelets. The human lung, kidney, and brain samples served as positive controls. We detected NR4A1 mRNA in human platelets (Figure A). Immunoblotting confirmed the presence of an NR4A1 protein band at ≈78 kDa in positive controls as well as in platelets (Figure B). No significant differences were observed in the mRNA and protein levels of human platelet NR4A1 between males and females (Figure S1A and S1B). Nor did we observe significant differences in the mRNA and protein levels of platelet NR4A1 between young and aged humans (Figure S1C and S1D). The specificity of the antibody was validated through its immunogenic peptide (Ag29513) in human platelets (Figure C). To determine whether NR4A1 is localized on the platelet surface, we conducted flow cytometry experiments. In addition to the antibody targeting the C-terminal (amino acids 299–598) used in immunoblotting, we also use an antibody targeting the N-terminal (amino acids 1–269). Compared with control IgG, both antibodies did not show a significant increase in fluorescence intensity without permeabilization treatment, but there was a notable increase in fluorescence intensity after permeabilization in human platelets (Figure D). These findings suggest that NR4A1 is not localized on the human platelet surface. The localization of several nuclear receptors can be altered in response to physiological stimuli, and the biological functions of NR4A1 are associated with its subcellular location in various cell types. Thus, we further analyzed the intracellular localization of NR4A1 in human platelets by in situ immunofluorescence microscopy. In resting human platelets, NR4A1 was found to be dispersed throughout the platelet cytoplasm (Figure E). In response to various physiological platelet agonists (ie, U46619, a thromboxane A2 receptor agonist), we did not observe obvious changes in the NR4A1 localization in human platelets (Figure E).
 Similarly, mRNA (Figure F) and protein (Figure G) of NR4A1 were detected in mouse platelets. Consistent with findings in humans, the mRNA and protein levels of platelet NR4A1 showed no significant differences between male and female mice (Figure S1E and S1F). Additionally, compared with young mouse platelets, the mRNA and protein levels of NR4A1 were unchanged in aged mouse platelets (Figure S1G and S1H). However, NR4A1 protein was not detected in platelets obtained from NR4A1-deficient mice (Figure H). Consistent with human platelets, the results of flow cytometry experiments suggest that NR4A1 is not localized on the mouse platelet surface (Figure I). In situ immunofluorescence confirmed that NR4A1 was dispersed throughout the platelet cytoplasm in both resting and activated mouse platelets (Figure J).
Additionally, we also detected the mRNA and protein expression of NR4A2 in human platelets (Figure S2A and S2C). However, the mRNA and protein expression of NR4A3 was not detected in human platelets (Figure S2B and S2C). Consistent with the findings in human platelets, NR4A2 was detected, while NR4A3 was not detected in mouse platelets (Figure S2D through S2F).

## Expression of NR4A1 Is Upregulated in the Setting of Hypercholesterolemia
Platelets exhibit hyperreactivity under conditions of hyperlipidemia. – Previous studies have reported that NR4A1 expression is upregulated in macrophage and endothelial cell lines stimulated with oxLDL (oxidized low-density lipoprotein), as well as in primary hepatocytes and cardiac myocytes isolated from mice fed a high-fat diet (HFD). Thus, we further evaluated the expression of NR4A1 and NR4A2 in platelets in the setting of hypercholesterolemia (Table S1). Compared with platelets from healthy subjects, the protein and mRNA levels of NR4A1 were significantly increased in platelets from patients with hypercholesterolemia, whereas NR4A2 expression remained unchanged (Figure A through F). To recapitulate the findings in humans, ApoE−/− mice were fed an HFD for 8 weeks to mimic hypercholesterolemic patients. The protein and mRNA levels of platelet NR4A1 were significantly upregulated in ApoE−/− mice fed an HFD, compared with those in ApoE−/− mice fed a control diet, whereas the protein and mRNA levels of NR4A2 in mouse platelets were not affected by hypercholesterolemia (Figure G through L). Moreover, we found that NR4A1 predominantly resided in the cytoplasm of mouse megakaryocytes, precursors of platelets, as demonstrated by in situ immunofluorescence (Figure A). Protein and mRNA levels of NR4A1 in megakaryocytes isolated from ApoE−/− mice fed an HFD were significantly upregulated compared with those from ApoE−/− mice fed a control diet (Figure B and C).
 NR4A1 acts as an intracellular signaling protein and an immediate-early response gene in response to various stimuli. To explore the impact of oxLDL on NR4A1 expression in platelets, we treated human and mouse platelets with oxLDL in vitro for 24 hours. In alignment with prior research, oxLDL treatments augmented the formation of platelet extracellular vesicles postagonist stimulation (Figure S3A and S3F). However, the protein expression of NR4A1 was not significantly changed by oxLDL treatment in human and mouse platelets (Figure S4). Platelet activation markers, including aggregation response, P-selectin exposure, and αIIbβ3 activation, were also not significantly altered by in vitro oxLDL treatment for 24 hours in human (Figure S3B through S3E) and mouse (Figure S3G through S3J) platelets. Platelets, anucleate blood cells, are shed from the cytoplasm of megakaryocytes. We then investigated the effect of oxLDL on NR4A1 expression in both primary mouse megakaryocytes and Meg-01 cells (a human megakaryoblast cell line). In contrast to platelets, the protein and mRNA levels of NR4A1 in both primary mouse megakaryocytes (Figure D and E) and Meg-01 cells Figure S5B and S5C) were significantly increased after treatment with oxLDL for 24 hours in vitro. These data suggest that the increased NR4A1 expression in platelets may originate from megakaryocytes in the setting of hypercholesterolemia.
Hypercholesterolemia induces the overproduction of intracellular reactive oxygen species. To investigate whether reactive oxygen species is responsible for the upregulation of NR4A1 in the setting of hypercholesterolemia, 2 reactive oxygen species inhibitors, N-acetyl cysteine and edaravone, were used. Preincubation with 10 mmol/L N-acetyl cysteine or 100 μmol/L edaravone abrogated the upregulation of NR4A1 in response to oxLDL in both primary mouse megakaryocytes (Figure F and G) and Meg-01 cells (Figure S5D and S5E). Moreover, upon H2O2 treatment, NR4A1 expression rapidly increased in both primary mouse megakaryocytes (Figure H and I) and Meg-01 cells (Figure S5F and S5G). These data suggest that oxLDL upregulates the NR4A1 expression in megakaryocytes in a reactive oxygen species-dependent manner.

## NR4A1 Deficiency Shortens Bleeding Time
To determine whether platelet-expressed NR4A1 mediates platelet activation or acts as an antiplatelet signal, global NR4A1 knockout (Nr4a1−/−) mice were first used. Nr4a1+/+ mice were used as controls. Nr4a1−/− mice were fertile and healthy without visible abnormalities, spontaneous bleeding, or thrombosis. Compared with Nr4a1+/+ littermates, Nr4a1−/− mice exhibited significantly shortened tail bleeding time (Figure S6A) and smaller ratios of bleeding time ≥15 minutes (Figure S6B) after tail transection. There was no difference in mean platelet volume, platelet counts, prothrombin time, activated partial thromboplastin time, red blood cell counts, hemoglobin, or white blood cell counts between Nr4a1−/− mice and Nr4a1+/+ littermates (Figure S6C through S6I). These results suggest that the enhanced hemostasis induced by NR4A1 deficiency is not mediated by coagulation factors or blood cell counts.
To further investigate the role of platelet-expressed NR4A1, we generated platelet-specific NR4A1-deficient (Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+) mice. The insertion of the LoxP sequence (Figure S7A) and deficiency of NR4A1 protein and mRNA (Figure S7B and S7C) in platelets were confirmed. Although NR4A2 is also expressed in human and mouse platelets, we did not observe significant changes in the expression of NR4A2 between Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− and Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ platelets (Figure S7E through S7G). Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ and Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− mice did not differ in platelet counts (Figure S7H), mean platelet volume (Figure S7I), the expression levels of the major platelet membrane proteins (CD41 and GPVI [glycoprotein VI]; Figure S7J and S7K), and the platelet microstructures (α-granules and dense granules; Figure S7L). Consistently, Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ mice exhibited a shortened bleeding time (Figure A) and smaller ratios of bleeding time ≥15 minutes (Figure B), compared with Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− littermates. These results suggest that NR4A1 deficiency in platelets enhances hemostasis without altering thrombopoiesis.
 

## NR4A1 Deficiency Enhances Thrombus Propensity
Then, we evaluated the effects of NR4A1 deficiency on thrombus tendency, measured as the time to occlusion in the FeCl3-induced carotid artery thrombosis assay using the laser speckle imaging technique. The mean time to occlusion in Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ mice was significantly shorter than that in Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− controls (Figure C). The thrombus cross-sectional area of the FeCl3-injured carotid artery segment at 5 minutes after FeCl3 treatment in Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ mice was significantly larger than that in Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− controls (Figure D). Next, a collagen/epinephrine-induced thromboembolism assay in the lung was performed in the absence of endothelial influence. In this assay, the parts of the lung with thrombi kept a pink color because thrombi impeded the penetration of Evan blue dye. The pink parts were larger in Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ mice than in Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− controls (Figure E). Moreover, the area of pulmonary vasculature thrombi in the pulmonary sections of Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ mice was larger than that in the pulmonary sections of Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− controls (Figure F).
In AMI, microvascular microthrombi obstruction in the peri-infarct tissue contributes to myocardial infarct expansion and a continued decline in heart function. , Thus, we further evaluated the effects of NR4A1 deficiency in an AMI model, which was established by ligation of the left anterior descending coronary artery. , Then, microthrombi obstruction, infarct area, and cardiac function were evaluated 7 days after ligation, as described previously. , Histological data consistently showed that more microthrombi were found in the peripheral infarct zone of Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ mice than that of Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− controls (Figure A). Furthermore, Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ mice exhibited increased infarct area (Figure B) and exacerbated cardiac dysfunction (Figure C), compared with Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− controls. These results indicate that NR4A1 acts as a negative regulator of thrombus formation in various models.
 

## NR4A1 Deficiency Enhances Platelet Aggregation and Multiple Aspects of Platelet Activation

## Platelet Aggregation and Dense Granule Release
The antithrombotic effects of NR4A1 were further studied by analyzing the platelet functions of Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ and Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− mice in vitro. Platelet aggregation, the process by which platelets cluster together at sites of vascular injury, has long been recognized as crucial for hemostatic plug formation and thrombosis. The release of secretory granules (dense granules and α-granules) amplifies platelet activation. Therefore, we evaluated platelet aggregation and ATP release (one of the dense granule constituents) using a Lumi-Aggregometer. ATP release was monitored by adding luciferase/luciferin reagents 5 minutes after the aggregation measurement began. Washed platelets from Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ mice and their Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− controls were stimulated with various physiological agonists. Compared with that of Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− platelets, the aggregation response and ATP release of Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ platelets were significantly increased upon stimulation with low concentrations of thrombin (Figure A and B), collagen (Figure C and D), U46619 (Figure E and F), or ADP (Figure G).
 

## α-Granules Release
P-selectin, a glycoprotein, is located within the α-granule membrane of resting platelets and is translocated to the plasma membrane, accompanied by the release of α-granules in activated platelets. Compared with that of Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− platelets, P-selectin exposure on the plasma membrane of Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ platelets was significantly increased upon stimulation with low concentrations of thrombin (Figure H) or ADP (Figure J).

## αIIbβ3 Activation
Upon agonist stimulation, the transduction of inside-out signals leads integrin αIIbβ3 to switch from a low- to high-affinity state for fibrinogen and other ligands. Ligand binding causes integrin clustering and subsequently promotes outside-in signaling, which initiates and amplifies a range of cellular events to drive essential platelet functions such as aggregation, spreading, clot retraction, and thrombus consolidation. In response to low concentrations of thrombin or ADP, the integrin αIIbβ3 activation of Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ platelets measured by JON/A (specifically binds to activated mouse αIIbβ3) was significantly higher than that of Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− platelets (Figure I and K).

## Platelet Spreading
Platelet spreading is an early phase outside-in signaling event downstream of integrin αIIbβ3 activation. Compared with Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− platelets, Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ platelets exhibited significantly enlarged spreading areas on fibrinogen (Figure L).

## Clot Retraction
Clot retraction manifests at a late stage to ensure the consolidation of the thrombus, thereby aiding in wound healing. Compared with Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− platelets, Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ platelets exhibited significantly accelerated clot retraction measured by washed mouse platelets mixed with human platelet-poor plasma (Figure M). Collectively, these data suggest that NR4A1 deficiency increases platelet aggregation and activation.

## Pharmacological NR4A1 Activation Decreases Platelet Aggregation and Multiple Aspects of Platelet Activation
We further tested the effects of pharmacological NR4A1 activation in both mouse and human platelets. The octaketide cytosporone B (Csn-B), which is isolated from the endophytic fungus Dothiorella sp. HTF3, is the first natural specific agonist identified for NR4A1 and binds to full-length NR4A1 with a 0.74 μmol/L dissociation constant (Kd). Using molecular docking analysis, we confirmed the binding affinity of Csn-B with monomer full-length NR4A1, whose structures were predicted by AlphaFold2. Docking scores of Csn-B with human and mouse NR4A1 were −6.178 and −6.087 kcal/mol, respectively (Figure S8). According to the 2-dimensional interaction map, human NR4A1 complexed with Csn-B, with an H-bond at Asp514 and hydrophobic interactions at Phe103, Phe106, Leu556, Tyr109, and Leu509 (Figure S8A). Taken together, the molecular docking confirmed the stable interaction between Csn-B and NR4A1.
Next, washed mouse platelets of wild type were treated with Csn-B for 15 minutes before platelet activation tests. Csn-B concentration-dependently inhibited aggregation (Figure A; Figure S9A) and ATP release (Figure B; Figure S9B) induced by thrombin, collagen, U46619, or ADP in mouse platelets. Csn-B inhibited procoagulant activity in mouse platelets activated by thrombin while showing no influence on procoagulant activity in resting mouse platelets (Figure S9C). Furthermore, Csn-B inhibited thrombin or ADP-induced P-selectin exposure and integrin αIIbβ3 activation measured by JON/A-binding in mouse platelets, while showing no influence on P-selectin exposure and integrin αIIbβ3 activation in resting platelets (Figure C and D; Figure S9D and S9E). Additionally, Csn-B inhibited clot retraction in mouse platelets (Figure E). To verify the specificity of Csn-B, we investigated its effect on Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ platelets. Csn-B lost its antiplatelet action in Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ platelets (Figure S10A through S10D) and antithrombotic effects in Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ mice in both models of carotid artery thrombosis (Figure S10E) and lung thromboembolism (Figure S10F). These data suggest that the effects of Csn-B are mainly mediated by NR4A1 in platelets. To further investigate the platelet-specific action of Csn-B in AMI, we used a platelet depletion/reconstitution model. The platelets of wild-type mice were depleted and then transfused with platelets pretreated with vehicle or Csn-B. Platelet counts before and after depletion, as well as after reconstitution, showed successful platelet depletion/reconstitution (Figure S11A). Consistently, less microthrombus area was found in the peripheral infarct zone of mice receiving Csn-B-treated platelets than that of mice receiving vehicle-treated platelets (Figure S11B). Moreover, mice receiving Csn-B-treated platelets exhibited decreased infarct areas (Figure S11C) and ameliorated cardiac dysfunction (Figure S11D), compared with mice receiving vehicle-treated platelets.
 Then, we investigated the effects of Csn-B on human platelets for potential clinical application. Csn-B concentration-dependently inhibited agonist-induced aggregation (Figure F; Figure S9F) and ATP release (Figure G; Figure S9G) in human platelets. Csn-B inhibited procoagulant activity in human platelets activated by thrombin while showing no influence on procoagulant activity in resting human platelets (Figure S9H). Furthermore, Csn-B inhibited thrombin or ADP-induced P-selectin exposure and integrin αIIbβ3 activation (measured by PAC-1, which specifically binds to activated human αIIbβ3) in human platelets, while showing no influence on P-selectin exposure and integrin αIIbβ3 activation in resting platelets (Figure H and I; Figure S9I and S9J). In addition, Csn-B inhibited clot retraction in human platelets (Figure J). Together, these results suggest that pharmacological activation of NR4A1 inhibits the aggregation and activation of human and mouse platelets.

## Pharmacological NR4A1 Activation Abrogates Platelet Hyperreactivity Induced by Hyperlipidemia
As expected, hypercholesterolemia significantly increased the aggregation responses of human platelets induced by thrombin or collagen (Figure S12A). Similarly, platelet aggregation responses were significantly increased in hypercholesterolemic mice (Figure S13A). Moreover, the mean time to occlusion in ApoE−/− mice fed an HFD was significantly shorter than that in those fed a control diet (Figure S13F), and the area of pulmonary vasculature thrombi in the pulmonary sections of ApoE−/− mice fed an HFD was significantly larger than that of those fed a control diet (Figure S13G).
We then determined the effects of the NR4A1 agonist Csn-B on hypercholesterolemic platelets. Csn-B significantly inhibited aggregation, ATP release, P-selectin exposure, and integrin αIIbβ3 activation of platelets from patients with hypercholesterolemia to the levels of platelets from healthy controls (Figure S12B through S12E). Csn-B also inhibited the activation of platelets from hypercholesterolemic mice to the degree of platelets from control mice (Figure S13B through S13E). Moreover, Csn-B significantly inhibited carotid arterial occlusive thrombosis and pulmonary vasculature thrombosis in hypercholesterolemic mice to the degree of those from control mice (Figure S13F and S13G).

## NR4A1 Regulates the CAP1-Mediated AC/PKA Signaling Pathway in Platelets
Transcription could not happen in platelets, because platelets, which are shed from the cytoplasm of megakaryocytes, are anucleate blood cells. Accordingly, we investigated the nongenomic pathway to further elucidate the potential molecular mechanisms underlying the function of NR4A1 in platelet activation. The NR4A1-interacting proteins were characterized using immunoprecipitation followed by mass spectrometry, which confirmed the presence of NR4A1. Based on the results of mass spectrometry (Supplemental Material), CAP1 was identified as a potential candidate for further analysis. The results of the coimmunoprecipitation revealed that NR4A1 co-immunoprecipitated with CAP1 in both human and mouse platelets (Figure A and B). Moreover, the NR4A1 agonist Csn-B enhanced the interaction of NR4A1 and CAP1 (Figure C). Because CAP1 is known to play a role in AC activation, , the finding that NR4A1 binds to CAP1 suggests that NR4A1 might regulate AC. As expected, Csn-B significantly increased 3′,5′-cAMP levels in platelets activated by ADP (Figure D), epinephrine (Figure F), or other platelet agonists (Figure S15A). ADP inhibited the prostaglandin I2 (PGI2)-mediated cAMP formation, and Csn-B attenuated the inhibitory action of ADP on PGI2-mediated cAMP formation (Figure H). Subsequently, Csn-B significantly enhanced the phosphorylation of VASP (vasodilator-stimulated phosphoprotein) at Ser157 (Figure E, G, and I; Figure S15B), a classical substrate of PKA in platelets. , However, the cAMP levels (Figure J; Figure S15C) and the phosphorylation of VASP (Figure K; Figure S15D) in Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre+ platelets were significantly downregulated compared with those in Nr4a1fl/flPF4Cre− platelets in the presence of a low concentration of PGI2 or platelet agonists. Csn-B lost its inhibition ability on platelet function, including aggregation, ATP release, P-selectin exposure, and integrin αIIbβ3 activation, and lost its enhancement on cAMP levels and phosphorylation of VASP in Cap1−/− platelets (Figure S16). Taken together, these results suggest that NR4A1 attenuates platelet activation via a nongenomic CAP1/AC/PKA pathway.
 

## Discussion
In the present study, we identified a novel role of the orphan nuclear receptor NR4A1 in platelets. The novel contributions included the following: we detected NR4A1 in both human and mouse platelets. Platelet-specific NR4A1 knockout promoted platelet activation and enhanced thrombus formation in multiple thrombosis models, while pharmacological activation of NR4A1 by specific agonists inhibited the activation of both human and mouse platelets. Mechanistically, NR4A1 hindered platelet function through a nongenomic CAP1/AC/PKA signaling pathway. Importantly, we found that NR4A1 was upregulated in platelets in the setting of hypercholesterolemia, and NR4A1-specific agonists inhibited hyperreactivity of platelets from hypercholesterolemic patients. Collectively, the current study provides the first evidence that NR4A1 negatively regulates platelet activation and thrombus formation through a nongenomic pathway.
Nuclear receptors are multifunctional intracellular proteins that regulate diverse cellular biological processes , and are a privileged family of druggable targets regulated by various natural and synthetic ligands. Interestingly, although platelets are anucleate cells, several members of the nuclear receptor superfamily have been found to be present in human platelets and regulate platelet functions. Previous pharmacological experiments with selective nuclear receptor modulators indicated that liver X receptor, farnesoid X receptor, glucocorticoid receptor, , retinoic acid receptor, and retinoid X receptor , negatively regulate platelet activation, while androgen receptor positively regulates platelet activation. However, these experiments could not rule out the off-target effects of nuclear receptor agonists/antagonists. In a recent study using genetic (ie, global or platelet-specific knockout) mouse models, we demonstrated that the circadian nuclear receptor Rev-erbα potentiated platelet activation and thrombus formation. However, among the 48 nuclear receptors discovered in humans, the role of the majority of them in platelet remains unknown.
NR4A subfamily members are expressed in various types of cells and tissues and participate in a variety of important biological processes. Recent studies have indicated that NR4A1 plays a protective role in ischemic diseases, including AMI , and ischemic stroke. Moreover, NR4A1 upregulates thrombomodulin expression in endothelial cells to inhibit thrombus formation. However, the expression profile and biological function of NR4A1 in platelets have never been investigated. Here, we reported our new findings that NR4A1 was present in human and mouse platelets. NR4A1 deficiency significantly increased platelet aggregation and multiple aspects of platelet activation, reinforced the ability of platelets to form thrombi in various thrombosis models, and resulted in aggravated microvascular microthrombi obstruction and infarct expansion in a model of AMI. Of clinical relevance, pharmacological activation of NR4A1 by the specific agonist Csn-B exhibited significant antiplatelet actions in human platelets. These lines of evidence convincingly pointed out a previously unidentified role of NR4A1 as a negative regulator in platelet activation and thrombus formation. Since the phenotypes of NR4A1 deficiency in mouse platelet activation and thrombus formation are relatively moderate, and NR4A2 is also expressed in both human and mouse platelets, further investigation is required to determine whether these 2 isoforms exhibit functional redundancy.
Platelets exhibit hyperreactivity under conditions of hyperlipidemia, which may contribute to an increased risk of atherothrombotic disease. – Consistent with previous reports, we observed a prothrombotic state induced by an HFD and platelet hyperreactivity provoked by hypercholesterolemia. NR4A1 is well known to rapidly respond to a variety of stimuli. We detected an increased expression of NR4A1 in platelets in both hyperlipidemic mice and patients with hypercholesterolemia, and the NR4A1 agonists significantly inhibited the activation of hypercholesterolemic platelets to the levels of control platelets. NR4A1 has been reported to have anti-atherosclerotic effects, – coupled with its platelet-targeted antithrombotic activity, there exists a potentially new paradigm of treatment that can target a range of pathophysiological conditions associated with atherothrombotic disease.
NR4A1 functions through both transcriptional activity and nongenomic actions, depending on the stimuli and its cellular localization. However, transcription could not happen in platelets because platelets are anucleate blood cells. Thus, we investigated the nongenomic effect of NR4A1 in platelets to elucidate the underlying mechanism. Based on the results of mass spectrometry, CAP1 was identified as a candidate target protein for NR4A1 functions in platelets. Coimmunoprecipitation analyses confirmed that NR4A1 interacted with CAP1 in both mouse and human platelets. CAP1 is known to bind and activate AC in mammalian cells. , The conversion of ATP into cAMP by AC, the activation of PKA by cAMP, and the phosphorylation of VASP by PKA play a key role in the inhibition of platelet function. , Here, deficiency of NR4A1 significantly decreased cAMP levels and phosphorylation of VASP, while pharmacological activation of NR4A1 significantly increased cAMP levels and phosphorylation of VASP in resting and agonist-activated platelets. These data suggest NR4A1 regulates platelet functions through a nongenomic CAP1/AC/PKA signaling pathway.

## Limitation
Since platelets are anucleate blood cells shed from the cytoplasm of megakaryocytes, , the current approach to accomplishing platelet-specific deletion depends on the specific deletion of their precursor megakaryocytes. In the current PF4-Cre transgenic platelet/megakaryocyte-specific knockout mouse models, megakaryocyte NR4A1 deficiency may contribute to the observed thrombosis phenotypes through transcriptional activity. Thus, to determine the contribution of platelet NR4A1 to the thrombosis phenotypes, we further performed experiments using a platelet depletion/reconstitution model (Figure S17). Additionally, we performed pharmacological experiments using several small-molecule compounds (ie, reactive oxygen species inhibitors N-acetyl cysteine and edaravone, and the NR4A1 agonist Csn-B) in the present study. Close attention should be given to the specificity of these compounds and their possible off-target effects.

## Clinical Implications of This Study
The current experimental findings are scientifically and clinically important. The identification of nuclear receptor NR4A1 as a novel functional protein in platelets may broaden our understanding of the pleiotropic functions of NR4A1. Our observations, along with the recent findings about the important roles of several nuclear receptors in platelets, highlight a potential regulatory cross-talk among these nuclear receptors to maintain the delicate homeostatic balance of hemorrhage and coagulation. Most importantly, our work indicates that NR4A1 is a new potential target for antiplatelet treatment and introduces the NR4A1 agonists as potent antiplatelet agents to prevent and treat platelet hyperreactivity and thrombosis-based cardiovascular diseases.
