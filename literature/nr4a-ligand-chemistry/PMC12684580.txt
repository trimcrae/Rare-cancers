## ENZYMATIC LACTYLATION
Lactylation involves the covalent attachment of lactyl groups to proteins through a multistep enzymatic pathway. Initially, specific catalytic proteins facilitate the conversion of lactate into lactyl‐CoA. This activated intermediate then donates lactyl groups to acceptor proteins, a reaction mediated by enzymatic modifiers referred to as “writers.” Several histone acetyltransferases, including E1A‐binding protein p300 (p300), CREB‐binding protein (CBP), general control non‐depressible 5 (GCN5), Tat‐interactive protein 60 (TIP60), and lysine acetyltransferase 7 (KAT7) and KAT8, have been identified as key mediators of this posttranslational modification. Lactylation is reversible, with “eraser” proteins (primarily histone deacetylases [HDACs] and sirtuins [SIRTs]), responsible for its removal. , , However, the precise mechanisms by which lactate contributes to lactyl‐CoA synthesis and regulates protein lactylation remain unclear. Recently, a novel class of lactylation writer proteins, known as alanyl‐tRNA synthetases, was identified. This group includes aminoacyl‐tRNA synthetase 1 (AARS1) , and AARS2. Due to the structural similarity between lactate and alanine, AARS1 can effectively bind lactate molecules. Through an adenosine triphosphate–dependent mechanism, AARS1 catalyzes the formation of lactyl‐adenosine monophosphate from lactate. The resulting lactyl‐adenosine monophosphate intermediate subsequently donates lactyl groups to specific protein targets, facilitating posttranslational modification. AARS1 functions predominantly in the cytoplasm, whereas AARS2 is primarily localized to the mitochondria. With increasing research on lactylation, additional lactylation‐related enzymes are being identified. These enzymes serve as direct substrates for lactylation. Recently, Professor Yingming Zhao from the University of Chicago identified that succinyl‐CoA synthetase GTPSCS can function as a lactyl‐CoA synthetase in the nucleus, regulating histone lactylation and glioblastoma progression. Researchers have also demonstrated that ACSS2 collaborates with GCN5, where ACSS2 converts lactate into lactyl‐CoA, and GCN5 subsequently catalyzes the lactylation of histone H3. This discovery reveals a novel mechanism through which histone lactylation contributes to tumor immune evasion. However, the efficiency of these reactions and functional differences among these enzymes remain largely unexplored. Further lactylation‐related enzymes are yet to be identified.
Research on Kla‐specific “reader” proteins is relatively limited. Some studies have reported that Brahma‐related gene 1 (BRG1) functions as a histone H3 lysine 18 lactylation (H3K18la) reader during induced pluripotent stem cell reprogramming. This is the first evidence of BRG1 binding to H3K18la, highlighting its role as a Kla reader protein. In addition, double PHD fingers 2 (DPF2) has been identified as a histone H3K14la reader that regulates gene transcription and promotes cervical cancer progression. However, studies investigating the role of lactylation in CVDs remain scarce.

## NONENZYMATIC LACTYLATION
Nonenzymatic lactylation is associated with D‐lactate production. Methylglyoxal, a byproduct of glycolysis, can form lactoylglutathione, which serves as a direct substrate for nonenzymatic lactylation. Because D‐lactylation is a nonenzymatic process involving lactoylglutathione, it occurs only when the glyoxalase system is impaired. Studies have shown that lactate modification can originate from methylglyoxal‐derived glycolytic products. This reactive metabolite rapidly interacts with glutathione via glyoxalase 1 (GLO1) to generate GLO2, which is subsequently hydrolyzed into glutathione and D‐lactate by GLO2. Through a nonenzymatic reaction, lactoylglutathione can transfer its acyl group to lysine residues in proteins, leading to the formation of lactate‐modified lysine. 

## CROSSTALK BETWEEN LACTYLATION AND ACETYLATION
An increasing number of studies have shown that lysine acylation exhibits similarities in substrate specificity, enzymatic regulation, biological functions, and disease associations. The regulation of lysine acylation is governed by a dynamic balance between enzymes that add acyl groups (writers) and those that remove them (erasers).
Acetylation was one of the earliest identified lysine acylation modifications, a characteristic it shares with lactylation. Both modifications utilize similar writer and eraser enzymes, yet they have distinct functional roles in cellular processes. In addition, both modifications rely on acyl‐CoA as a substrate. Acetylation is catalyzed by adenosine triphosphate–citrate lyase, whereas the mechanisms underlying lactyl‐CoA generation remain poorly understood. Beyond enzymatic regulation by acyltransferases, lysine acylation can also occur through a nonenzymatic, pH‐dependent mechanism, where higher pH levels promote increased acetylation. , Studies have demonstrated competitive interactions between acetylation and lactylation in hepatic stellate cells, where elevated lactate levels enhance lactylation while suppressing acetylation—an effect referred to as the “lactate clock.” Kinetic analyses using U‐13C6‐glucose have further shown that lactylation reaches a steady state within 6 hours, whereas acetylation requires 24 hours to do so. Current evidence suggests that lactylation is generally less competitive than acetylation. However, the precise mechanisms underlying the interplay and competition between these modifications remain unclear.

## LACTYLATION AND ACETYLATION SHARE THE SAME MODIFICATION SITES
Lactylation and acetylation primarily occur at lysine residues in proteins. In histones, these modifications often target the same sites, such as lysine 18 (H3K18), lysine 27 (H3K27), and lysine 23 (H3K23) on histone H3, suggesting a potential interplay between these 2 modifications. For example, a CUT&Tag experiment on circulating monocytes revealed a correlation between lactylation (H3K18la) and acetylation (H3K18ac) at histone H3K18 following MI. Specifically, 566 genes (14% of those marked by H3K18la) exhibited a substantial increase in both H3K18la and H3K18ac levels. However, in the majority of genes marked by H3K18la, H3K18ac levels remained largely unchanged. This finding indicates that while these modifications can co‐occur at certain loci, their regulation may be independent in most cases. In a mouse model of myocardial hypertrophy induced by transverse aortic constriction, significant changes in histone lactylation were observed. Pan‐Kla expression and H3K18la levels increased significantly, whereas histone acetylation levels remained largely unchanged. No significant differences were detected in pan‐Kac, H3K18ac, or H4K5ac between transverse aortic constriction and control groups. These findings suggest that lactylation and acetylation exhibit distinct regulatory dynamics in myocardial hypertrophy, potentially indicating functional divergence in their roles. 

## LACTYLATION AND ACETYLATION SHARE THE SAME MODIFYING ENZYMES
The substrate preferences of writer and eraser enzymes for lactylation and acetylation provide valuable insights into the relationship between these modifications. Among histone acetyltransferases, p300 is the most versatile, catalyzing a range of acyl modifications, including propionylation, butyrylation, lactylation, and crotonylation. , , , Studies have demonstrated that p300 and CBP exhibit distinct substrate specificities for acetylation. For instance, p300 displays ≈10‐fold higher specificity than CBP when acetylating histone H3 at lysine 14 (H3K14), lysine 18 (H3K18), and lysine 23 (H3K23). However, CBP preferentially acetylates H3K18 when using a histone tetramer as a substrate. The mechanisms governing the preference of p300 and CBP for lactylation over acetylation remain unclear. Zhang et al identified p300 as the enzyme responsible for α‐myosin heavy chain (α‐MHC) lysine 1897 lactylation (K1897la) and demonstrated that this modification affects the interaction of α‐MHC with titin. Reduced α‐MHC K1897la exacerbates HF, highlighting its functional significance. Moreover, p300 has been implicated as a lactylation transferase in several CVDs, including MI, myocardial I/R, atherosclerosis, and vascular calcification. , , , CBP, another lactylation transferase, plays a critical role in lactate‐driven endothelial to mesenchymal transition (EndoMT) following MI. CBP interacts with Snail1, modulating both its acetylation and lactylation, which promotes its nuclear translocation and activation of the transforming growth factor β (TGF‐β) signaling pathway. This mechanism influences EndoMT and contributes to myocardial fibrosis and cardiac dysfunction post‐MI. Thus, CBP represents a potential therapeutic target for these conditions. In addition, GCN5 functions as a lactyltransferase, facilitating H3K18 lactylation, which is crucial for regulating monocyte histone lactylation and the expression of downstream repair genes. 

Studies have also identified HDACs as key erasers of acyl modifications. HDAC1–3, for instance, regulate various acylation processes, contributing to the overall modification landscape of proteins. Crosstalk, coordination, or competition among acetylation, lactylation, and crotonylation further complicates this regulatory network. HDACs exhibit clear preferences for specific modification types and sites. Recent studies have shown that inhibition of class I HDACs enhances H3K18 acetylation in hepatic stellate cells while reducing lactylation at the same site. Specifically, HDAC3 inhibition increases histone H4 lysine 12 lactylation (H4K12la), which promotes senescence‐associated secretory phenotype transcription, exacerbating vascular smooth muscle cell senescence and atherosclerosis. RNA interference targeting HDAC1 significantly increases H4K5 lactylation, whereas HDAC2 knockdown does not affect lactylation at this site, suggesting site‐specific regulatory roles among HDACs. SIRT1, a member of the sirtuin family, mediates NAD‐dependent deacetylation of histones and nonhistone proteins and interacts with α‐MHC in myocardial tissues. Overexpression of SIRT1 or treatment with a SIRT1 activator reduces α‐MHC lactylation and has a significant impact on the modification of the α‐MHC K1897 site. 

## EFFECTS OF LACTYLATION ON PROTEINS
Lactylation can be classified into 2 categories based on the type of protein it modifies: histone lactylation and nonhistone lactylation. Each category exerts distinct effects on proteins. Histone lactylation primarily influences gene transcription by altering chromatin structure and accessibility, whereas nonhistone lactylation affects protein stability, localization, and function. ## HISTONE LACTYLATION REGULATES GENE TRANSCRIPTION
The basic structural unit of chromatin is the nucleosome, which consists of DNA segments coiled around histone proteins. These histone proteins, as essential chromatin constituents, undergo diverse chemical modifications that significantly influence epigenetic mechanisms. Emerging evidence suggests that histone lactylation primarily stimulates chromatin activity and promotes the expression of associated genes. Based on their structural characteristics and molecular properties, histone proteins are categorized into 5 principal groups: H1, H2A, H2B, H3, and H4. Recent studies have identified lactylation modifications in 4 of these variants: H1, H2B, H3, and H4. , , , 

In CVDs, histone lactylation plays a crucial role in disease initiation and progression. Lactylation at multiple histone sites has been implicated in CVD treatment. For example, following MI, lactylation at histone H3K18 in monocytes increases rapidly, activating repair genes distant from the injury site. This early gene activation promotes cardiac repair and improves function. Currently, H3K18 lactylation is the most extensively studied modification and is associated with various cardiovascular conditions, including MI, myocardial I/R injury, atherosclerosis, myocardial hypertrophy, and vascular calcification. In addition, H3K9 lactylation has been relatively well studied, particularly in relation to myocardial I/R injury and vascular calcification. Other histone lactylation sites have also been identified. For instance, histone H4K12 lactylation in vascular smooth muscle cells induces senescence and promotes atherosclerosis. During myocardial I/R injury, HSPA12A in cardiomyocytes regulates the expression of SMAD‐specific E3 ubiquitin protein ligase 1 (SMURF1), which stabilizes hypoxia‐inducible factor 1α (HIF‐1α) protein levels. Furthermore, H3K56 lactylation may interact with these proteins to regulate cardiomyocyte metabolism and survival. 

## NONHISTONE LACTYLATION HAS DIVERSE EFFECTS ON PROTEINS
Nonhistone lactylation alters protein function by inducing steric hindrance, modifying protein conformation, and neutralizing charges. These modifications affect protein–protein interactions, stability, subcellular localization, and enzyme activity. In CVDs, nonhistone lactylation influences protein function through 4 primary mechanisms.

## Modulating Protein Stability
Lactylation can either enhance or reduce protein stability, , depending on the context. For example, glycolysis inhibition reduces lactylation of LIM domain‐containing protein 1 (LCP1), leading to its increased degradation and ultimately mitigating cerebral ischemia progression. In another study, an I/R cell model using H9c2 cells subjected to hypoxia/reoxygenation demonstrated that LDHA promotes lactylation at the K245 site of NLR family pyrin domain containing 3 (NLRP3), increasing its protein stability. During myocardial I/R injury, K351 of Serpina3k (SA3K) protein undergoes lactylation modification (SA3K–K351la). This modification has an important impact on the stability of the SA3K protein. L–lactate can promote protein stability and inhibit its degradation by enhancing SA3K–K351la. 

## Regulating Protease Catalytic Activity
Lactylation at enzyme active sites can influence catalytic activity. For example, in cardiomyocytes subjected to oxygen–glucose deprivation/reoxygenation, malate dehydrogenase 2 (MDH2), a key mitochondrial enzyme, catalyzes the reversible conversion of malate to oxaloacetate. Dysfunctional MDH2 leads to the accumulation of tricarboxylic acid cycle intermediates, compromising cellular energy supply. Lactylation at K241 of MDH2 may modulate its activity, thereby affecting tricarboxylic acid cycle homeostasis. 

## Modifying Protein‐Molecule Interactions
Lactylation can modulate interactions between proteins and other biological macromolecules. In HF‐affected tissues, lactate levels significantly decrease. Specifically, K1897la , of α‐MHC enhances its interaction with Titin, mitigating HF severity. In radiation‐induced heart disease, the interaction between prolyl 4‐hydroxylase subunit beta (P4HB) and prostaglandin‐endoperoxide synthase 2 (PTGS2) in cardiomyocytes regulates inflammatory responses. Lactylation at the K311 site enhances P4HB–PTGS2 binding, whereas a K311R mutation significantly disrupts this interaction. 

## Influencing Protein Function
Posttranslational lactylation modifications play a crucial role in transcriptional regulation. A notable example is Snail1, a transcriptional activator that undergoes lactylation via interaction with the CBP/p300 complex. This modification facilitates EndoMT in postinfarction cardiac tissue. Moreover, as a TGF‐β–responsive transcription factor, lactylated Snail1 enhances TGF‐β/Smad2 signaling under hypoxic or ischemic conditions. Beyond transcriptional regulation, protein lactylation modulates other functions. For instance, myocardial I/R injury induces FOXO1 lactylation, enabling it to mediate myocardial injury by regulating NLRP3 production. Lactylation also influences protein distribution and conformation in diseases beyond cardiovascular conditions. In macrophages, lactylation of PKM2 at K62 promotes its transition from a tetramer to a dimer. This structural change enhances pyruvate kinase activity and reduces its nuclear localization. 

## RESEARCH PROGRESS ON LACTYLATION IN CVDS
The heart is a metabolically active organ with numerous mitochondria, continuously pumping blood to deliver oxygen and nutrients throughout the body. Lactate plays a critical role in cardiac energy metabolism, and its production increases under hypoxic conditions or metabolic disturbances. These fluctuations in intracellular lactate levels influence lactylation. The following sections summarize the current mechanistic research and clinical translation status of lactylation modification in various CVDs (Table  and Figure  ). Elucidating the relationship among lactylation, cardiac metabolism, and disease pathogenesis may facilitate the development of novel therapeutic strategies.
 

## LACTYLATION AND MI

## Regulation of MI Repair Gene Expression
Wang et al reported that within 4 to 24 hours after MI, lactylation and H3K18la modifications in monocytes and macrophages increase sharply. Additional studies have demonstrated that H3K18la facilitates the activation of repair‐associated genes, such as Lrg1, Vegf‐a, and IL‐10, thereby improving myocardial function in mouse models. These findings indicate that monocytes undergo a metabolic shift from oxidative phosphorylation to glycolysis following myocardial injury, influencing both lactylation and gene expression. Furthermore, the study identified a regulatory mechanism in which GCN5 mediates H3K18la via the IL‐1β/IL‐1R1 pathway, providing potential therapeutic targets for modulating early repair gene activation after MI. 

## Role in EndoMT

MI contributes to increased cardiac fibrosis and dysfunction, driven by key factors such as TGF‐β signaling and Snail1 expression. Epigenetic modifications also play a crucial role in regulating gene expression during cardiac fibrosis. Fan et al demonstrated that treatment with the glycolysis inhibitor 2‐deoxy‐D‐glucose (2‐DG) significantly improved cardiac function, reduced myocardial fibrosis, and inhibited EndoMT in affected mice, suggesting that targeting glycolysis may be an effective strategy to mitigate post‐MI fibrosis and dysfunction. Their study further revealed that lactate activates the TGF‐β/Smad2 pathway, increasing Snail1 lactylation and enhancing its interaction with CBP/p300. In vivo experiments confirmed that Snail1 downregulation improved cardiac function and inhibited EndoMT in MI models, underscoring the role of Snail1 lactylation in promoting EndoMT via the TGF‐β/Smad2 axis. 

## Influence on Cardiomyocyte Proliferation
Some studies have reported that post‐MI metabolic shifts mediated by LDHA promote cardiomyocyte proliferation. This occurs through a reduction in reactive oxygen species levels and the induction of an anti‐inflammatory M2 macrophage phenotype, facilitating tissue repair. These findings suggest that LDHA may be a promising target for enhancing myocardial regeneration after MI. 

## LACTYLATION AND I/R INJURY
Myocardial I/R injury is a significant cause of poor outcomes following blood flow restoration after a heart attack. The molecular chaperone HSPA12A plays a crucial protective role in cardiac muscle cells during oxygen deprivation and the subsequent reoxygenation events. Through its interaction with SMURF1, this heat shock protein maintains the stability of HIF‐1α, which is a key transcriptional regulator. This stabilization process leads to upregulation of genes involved in glycolytic metabolism. Such molecular interactions support continued aerobic glycolysis throughout the reperfusion phase, preserve histone lactylation modifications, and enhance myocardial cell viability, ultimately mitigating the damage caused by I/R events in cardiac tissue. In addition, it has been observed that exercise can decrease myocardial lactylation, whereas I/R increases it. Lactylation promotes the expression of YTH N6‐methyladenosine RNA‐binding protein 2 (YTHDF2) in cardiomyocytes. YTHDF2 affects cardiomyocyte size and apoptosis in an RNA N6‐methyladenosine (m6A)–independent manner by regulating GTPase‐activating protein‐binding protein 1 (G3BP1). Modulating the lactylation/YTHDF2/G3BP1 axis may be a promising strategy for treating heart disease. LDHA stabilizes NLRP3 expression by promoting lactylation at the K245 site. The upregulation of NLRP3 can negate the protective effect of LDHA knockdown on hypoxia/reoxygenation injury, suggesting that NLRP3 is a potential therapeutic target for ischemic heart disease. Some studies have found that L–lactic acid promotes protein stability by enhancing SA3K–K351la. Knocking out SA3K exacerbates cardiac injury, while its overexpression alleviates the injury, and this protective effect depends on K351 lactylation. Mechanistically, SA3K secreted by fibroblasts protects cardiomyocytes through paracrine, inhibits the Wnt pathway and activates the reperfusion injury salvage kinase (RISK) and survivor activating factor enhancement (SAFE) pathways, which provides a new direction for the treatment of myocardial I/R injury. Research on lactylation modification in ischemic heart disease has primarily utilized mouse models (eg, bone marrow monocytes and cardiac macrophages), while the characteristics and dynamic changes in lactylation modification in human patients with MI remain unclear.

## LACTYLATION AND HF
Lactate and lactylation play important roles in HF regulation. High levels of lactate in the blood can predict poor outcomes in patients with HF. When lactylation levels decrease in HF, heart muscle scarring and heart function problems worsen. Stopping a protein called MCT4 can reduce heart muscle cells in patients with HF, indicating that lactate inside the cells can protect the heart. Further, in a zebrafish model of acute HF, lactate‐based therapy attenuated inflammation and cardiac edema. Recent research has shown that a specific lactylation site on Titin (K1897la) is important for Titin to bind to the α‐MHC and that lactylation at K1897 of titin facilitates its interaction with α‐MHC. This binding helps to maintain the structure and function of the heart. Damage to the heart muscle due to high blood pressure occurs because there is less Titin K1897la. This breaks the Titin‐α–MHC complex and can lead to HF. Conversely, supplementing sodium lactate and treating with the MCT4 (modulating intracellular lactate levels by controlling its export out of cells) inhibitor VB124 can significantly increase the level of Titin K1897la, reduce myocardial damage, and improve HF. This study comprehensively elaborates on the core mechanism of end‐organ damage in hypertension and proposes new protective strategies. Does intracellular lactate exert similar cardioprotective effects in human patients? Although some clinical data are available, current research on the role of lactylation in HF remains limited due to small patient sample sizes, with most experimental evidence still confined to animal models (eg, mice) and cellular studies.

## LACTYLATION AND HYPERTENSION
Pulmonary hypertension (PH) is a chronic and progressive CVD that is associated with a high mortality rate. It is primarily characterized by pulmonary vascular remodeling, endothelial cell dysfunction, apoptosis, and inflammation. These pathological changes lead to increased resistance in pulmonary blood vessels, resulting in elevated pressure within the pulmonary arteries. This can ultimately lead to right‐sided HF and even death. In cells derived from the endothelium and smooth muscle of pulmonary arteries in patients and animal models of PH, there is a significant metabolic shift from oxidative phosphorylation to glycolysis, which results in more lactate. Researchers are interested in the causes of this shift in glycolysis. When there is insufficient oxygen, harmful molecules called reactive oxygen species accumulate in mitochondria. These molecules prevent a protein called HIF‐1α from being changed in a way that would normally turn off glycolysis. Instead, this protein turns on other proteins (pyruvate dehydrogenase kinase 1 [PDK1] and PDK2) that prevent another protein (PDH‐E1α) from working. This chain reaction causes cells to switch to glycolysis. Lactate, which is produced by this switch, causes a change called Kla on proteins, especially histones H3K18 and H4K5. This change helps smooth muscle cells in the pulmonary arteries to grow and change blood vessels, which is detrimental for PH. Using a drug that stops lactate dehydrogenase can reverse this process. 

Lactate also affects another type of change in RNA, called m6A, through Kla. This changes how RNA is produced and used in cells. These changes affect how cells grow, use energy, and interact with the immune system, which can influence PH. Kla may help us find new ways to treat PH by changing how cells look, how new blood vessels grow, and how cells use energy. However, more research is needed, both in the laboratory and in clinical trials, to determine whether these treatments work and are safe. Specifically, studies focusing on rodent models and in vitro experiments as well as large‐scale clinical trials are needed. Further, targeted therapy strategies for lactylation modification or m6A modification have not yet entered the clinical stage, warranting further studies.

## LACTYLATION AND ATHEROSCLEROSIS
Atherosclerosis is a major contributor to cardiovascular morbidity and represents a significant clinical challenge. Although physical activity has been widely recognized as cardioprotective, the molecular pathways mediating these beneficial effects remain poorly characterized. In a recent investigation using an atherosclerotic mouse model, Wang et al demonstrated that exercise‐generated lactate exerts antiatherogenic effects. Their findings revealed that this protective mechanism involves lactylation modification at the K271 site of methyl‐CpG‐binding protein 2 (Mecp2) in endothelial cells lining the blood vessels.
Researchers have discovered a novel mechanism involving Mecp2 lactylation at the K271 residue (Mecp2 K271la), which plays a crucial role in the progression of atherosclerosis. This study revealed that this specific posttranslational modification significantly reduces epiregulin expression levels. The molecular mechanism involves the regulation of epidermal growth factor receptor phosphorylation patterns, which subsequently affects the mitogen‐activated protein kinase signaling pathway. This cascade of events induces substantial modifications in endothelial cell function, particularly in the synthesis of several important molecules including vascular cell adhesion molecule 1, intercellular adhesion molecule 1, monocyte chemoattractant protein‐1, interleukin 1β (IL‐1β), IL‐6, and endothelial nitric oxide synthase. Balanced control of these molecular components appears to be essential for both stabilization and reduction of atherosclerotic plaques. These groundbreaking results highlight the potential of targeting lactate metabolism–induced lactylation as an innovative treatment strategy for atherosclerosis, offering new hope for CVD management. Regarding histone lactylation modification, H3K18la drives the occurrence of EndoMT and atherosclerosis. The histone acetyltransferase p300 forms a complex with the histone chaperone anti‐silencing function 1A (ASF1A), specifically mediating the enrichment of H3K18la at the promoter region of Snail family transcriptional repressor 1 (SNAI1), a key transcription factor for EndoMT, thereby activating its expression. In addition, it was found that tumor necrosis factor receptor‐associated protein 1 (TRAP1) drives vascular smooth muscle cell senescence and promotes atherosclerosis by upregulating glycolysis, leading to lactate accumulation. Elevated lactate levels enhance H4K12la via suppression of the histone deacetylase HDAC3, which, in turn, activates senescence‐associated secretory phenotype and exacerbates atherosclerosis. Human data from these studies only come from peripheral blood mononuclear cells, and not arterial plaque tissue (difficult to obtain because of ethical constraints). However, patient‐derived peripheral blood mononuclear cells cannot fully represent intraplaque macrophages, and thus arterial surgery waste specimens or organoid models can be tried in the future.

## LACTYLATION AND VASCULAR CALCIFICATION
Emerging evidence reveals that vascular calcification is closely linked to histone lactylation through 2 interconnected metabolic‐epigenetic pathways. In calcified vascular regions, upregulated nuclear receptor subfamily 4 group A member 3 (NR4A3) binds to promoters of glycolytic genes (ALDOA and PFKL), enhancing their transcription to promote glycolysis and lactate production. The resulting lactate drives histone lactylation, particularly H3K18la, which directly accelerates vascular calcification. Notably, inhibiting NR4A3 reduces H3K18la levels and mitigates calcification, suggesting that targeting the NR4A3–glycolysis–lactylation axis could be a therapeutic strategy. In addition, independent studies show that overexpression of TRAP1 alters energy metabolism by boosting glycolysis and lactate accumulation while suppressing the deacetylase HDAC3, leading to increased H4K12la formation. This lactylation‐mediated histone remodeling activates senescence‐associated secretory phenotype, promoting vascular smooth muscle cell senescence and atherosclerosis. Together, these findings highlight how metabolic reprogramming (via NR4A3 and TRAP1) and subsequent histone lactylation (H3K18la/H4K12la) converge to drive vascular calcification and atherosclerosis, offering new insights into epigenetic regulation of vascular pathology.

## POTENTIAL MECHANISMS LINKING LACTYLATION TO CVD: IMPLICATIONS FOR FUTURE THERAPEUTIC RESEARCH
Lactylation plays a key role in the control of many normal and disease‐related processes. The impact of proteins varies, leading to diverse results. Lactylation could be a new way to treat heart and blood vessel diseases. Emerging evidence suggests that lactylation may contribute to cardiovascular pathophysiology through diverse mechanisms, though its therapeutic potential remains largely exploratory. Studies have shown that lactylation is regulated by metabolic factors, and current strategies to modulate lactylation can be categorized as direct (targeting lactylation enzymes) or indirect (targeting upstream metabolic pathways) (Figure  ). Below, we synthesize mechanistic insights from preclinical studies, highlighting gaps that warrant further investigation before clinical translation.
 

## DIRECTLY TARGETING LACTYLATION ENZYMES
The amount of lactylation inside cells can be changed by targeting enzymes that add lactyl groups (transferases) or enzymes that remove lactyl groups (such as sirtuin or SIRT enzymes).

## Targeting Transferases
Preclinical studies have identified p300 and AARS1 as key enzymes involved in lactylation regulation, though their therapeutic targeting remains mechanistically complex. The p300 inhibitors include C646 , and A485. For instance, the p300 inhibitor C646 was shown to suppress H3K56la and abolish HSPA12A‐mediated cardiomyocyte protection, indicating a functional role of p300‐dependent lactylation in cardioprotection. , Conversely, pharmacological activation of p300 by agents such as the cholera toxin B subunit increased lactylation at α‐MHC K1897, thereby enhancing its interaction with Titin. Independently, β‐alanine was found to inhibit AARS1‐mediated lactylation by competitively disrupting lactate binding to the enzyme. , However, current evidence is primarily derived from preclinical models, and the specificity of these interventions for lactylation remains unclear. Further research is needed to dissect the mechanistic links between enzyme inhibition, lactylation dynamics, and physiological outcomes.

## Targeting De‐Transferases
Eraser enzymes can remove protein lactylation. The identified eraser enzymes are histone deacetylases (HDACs and SIRTs). Trichostatin A and suberoylanilide hydroxamic acid, inhibitors of class I and II HDACs, may reduce MI areas and improve cardiac function. Trichostatin A and suberoylanilide hydroxamic acid are nonspecific inhibitors that cannot precisely antagonize a certain subtype and may inhibit the activities of all class I and II HDACs. SIRT1 activators (such as SRT1720) and inhibitors (such as EX527) can affect α‐MHC lactylation. However, the effects of histone deacetylase inhibitors on lactylation may be complicated. This is because these inhibitors can also increase acetylation, a modification that competes with lactylation.

## INDIRECT ACTION‐TARGETING UPSTREAM METABOLIC PATHWAYS
Lactate is the source of lactyl‐CoA and facilitates its lactylation. Lactate production inside cells is a complex process that involves numerous steps and is assisted by various enzymes. Therefore, if we can control how much lactate is produced or how it moves around inside cells, lactylation can be controlled.

## Affecting Lactate Transport
Modulating lactate transport through MCT inhibitors has emerged as a potential strategy to influence protein lactylation in various cardiovascular contexts. For instance, AZD‐3965, an MCT1 inhibitor, was shown to block lactate uptake in macrophages, thereby suppressing histone lactylation and associated gene expression. Similarly, VB124, an MCT4 inhibitor, increased lactate levels in the heart and enhanced lactylation of α‐MHC at K1897, which promoted its binding to Titin. In addition, the pan‐MCT inhibitor α‐cyano‐4‐hydroxycinnamate (CHC) attenuated lactate‐induced endothelial dysfunction. Collectively, these studies indicate that metabolic targeting via MCT inhibition can modulate lactylation processes; however, the clinical translation of such approaches remains challenging due to the pleiotropic effects of lactate manipulation.

## Affecting Enzymes Related to Lactate Production
Glycolysis‐derived lactate serves as a critical substrate for histone lactylation—exemplified by modifications such as H3K18la—which, in turn, influences inflammatory responses and the progression of CVDs. Targeting glycolytic enzymes with pharmacological agents represents a potential strategy to modulate lactate levels and downstream lactylation, though the outcomes are often context‐dependent and mechanistically complex. For instance, LDH inhibitors including oxamic acid and FX‐11 effectively reduce lactate accumulation and H3K18la levels. , , , However, in mouse models of MI, FX‐11 not only decreased lactylation in monocytes and macrophages but also unexpectedly exacerbated inflammation and impaired cardiac function. , , Other inhibitors targeting key glycolytic nodes—such as sodium dichloroacetate (a PKM inhibitor), 2‐DG (a hexokinase blocker), and shikonin (a PKM2 inhibitor)—also suppress lactate production and lactylation, with shikonin additionally downregulating YTHDF2 expression in cardiomyocytes. , , , , , , Intriguingly, rotenone, an inhibitor of mitochondrial complex I, paradoxically elevated both lactate and H3K18la, promoting an anti‐inflammatory response via mediators such as IL‐10. Further expanding the mechanistic landscape, the targeted degradation of hexokinase 2 (HK2) using proteolysis‐targeting chimera HK2 Degrader‐1 attenuated glycolytic flux and reduced atherosclerosis in mice. Additional compounds such as dexmedetomidine and aloe emodin conferred cardioprotection by modulating specific lactylation events: dexmedetomidine reduced lactylation of MDH2 to alleviate myocardial I/R injury, while aloe emodin inhibited P4HB lactylation, mitigating mitochondrial reactive oxygen species damage. , Natural products also exhibit relevant activity, eg, A2‐4‐2‐4 (a lactate‐rich extract from Trapa taiwanensis Nakai), inhibited cardiac hypertrophy through suppression of tnnt2, and andrographolide reduced both lactate synthesis and p300 activity, thereby preventing aortic valve calcification in ApoE−/− mice. , Collectively, these findings underscore the therapeutic potential of targeting lactate metabolism and lactylation in CVDs, yet also highlight profound complexities in their physiological and pathological roles.

## SYNTHESIS AND TRANSLATIONAL OUTLOOK
Current evidence positions lactylation as a plausible, yet still emerging, contributor to CVD mechanisms. Significant challenges remain, including the limited specificity of existing modulators (eg, p300 or MCT inhibitors) for lactylation, pronounced context‐dependence across cell types and disease stages, and the absence of lactylation‐targeting drugs in clinical trials for CVD. Moving forward, priority must be given to developing lactylation‐specific molecular probes, implementing systems‐level approaches to elucidate lactylation networks, and rigorously establishing mechanistic causality before any therapeutic exploration is pursued.

## DISCUSSION
Lactylation, a recently identified protein, is modifiable postsynthesis and affects protein actions and gene regulation. Therefore, it plays an important role in the development of heart and blood vessel diseases (CVDs). Lactate metabolism and lactylation provide new ideas for exploring innovative therapeutic targets for CVDs. However, clinical translation still faces numerous challenges, mainly including those listed below.

## Spatiotemporal Complexity of Lactylation in Disease
Lactylation modification is temporally dynamic, and the rapid accumulation of lactate (such as via the Warburg effect or hypoxia) in acute lesions (such as I/R injury and acute infection) may drive the transient increase in lactylation modification and participate in acute inflammatory responses or cellular stress protection. In chronic lesions (eg, tumors, fibrosis, metabolic diseases), the long‐term lactate microenvironment may lead to the continuous accumulation of lactylation modifications, but the modification patterns may differ between different tissues or cell types. Due to differences in the metabolic microenvironment and the regulation or cross‐modification of modifiers and de‐modifiers, there is spatial heterogeneity, such as different cell types of different metabolism and utilization of lactate, resulting in different targets and functions of lactylation modification. , , Lactylation can occur in histones (intranucleus), metabolic enzymes (cytosol), or mitochondrial proteins, and their spatial distribution affects their function; the modification profiles of high‐lactate tissues (eg, tumors and ischemic myocardium) and low‐lactate tissues were significantly different. To address the complexity of lactylation in cardiovascular biology, a cell type–specific multiomics approach (eg, single‐cell RNA sequencing and spatial metabolomics) should be employed to map lactylation targets across cardiomyocytes, fibroblasts, and immune cells. Functional proteomics (eg, lactylome profiling) can identify nonhistone lactylation sites, while genetically engineered organoids and disease models (eg, MI with timed lactylation modulation) can dissect context‐dependent roles. Integrating immune‐metabolic axis analysis (eg, cytokine‐lactylation crosstalk) and network modeling will unravel the dynamic regulation in pathological remodeling, bridging mechanistic gaps toward therapeutic discovery. 

## Interactions Between Epigenetic Modifications
First, the relationship between histone lactylation and other epigenetic modifications (such as acetylation) is complex. How these changes affect each other, cooperate or compete in the occurrence and development of CVDs, and jointly regulate gene expression remains unclear. Second, little is known about enzymes that specifically add or remove lactylation from proteins. We also do not understand what controls whether an enzyme acts as a writer (adding lactylation) or an eraser (removing lactylation). In addition, little research has been conducted on proteins that recognize and bind to the lactylated sites (reader proteins). Currently, our knowledge of how these different modifications work together is limited. More detailed research is needed to determine how they help control what cells do. Resolving these knowledge gaps requires a multiomics approach (eg, ChIP sequencing, RNA sequencing, and metabolomics) to dissect the crosstalk between lactylation and other modifications (eg, acetylation) in gene regulation. CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats)–based screening, proteomics, and structural biology can identify lactylation writers, erasers, and readers, while engineered enzymes (eg, optogenetic tools) and disease models (eg, ApoE−/− mice) can functionally validate their roles in CVD. Integrating clinical data and computational predictions may reveal therapeutic targets, ultimately enabling precision modulation of lactylation in CVD. 

## Limitations of Preclinical Models
Although some achievements have been made in basic research, animal models cannot fully simulate the complexity of CVDs in humans. Human samples provide molecular signatures closer to the real pathology. However, most studies rely on small sample cohorts or animal models, lacking large‐scale clinical validation. In addition, most human studies only provide evidence of correlation and do not perform functional experiments (such as gene knockout or intervention). It is necessary to accurately assess the lactylation level in cardiovascular patients, develop effective intervention measures, ensure their safety and effectiveness, and integrate lactylation‐related treatment strategies into clinical practice. Future research should focus on clarifying the status of lactylation‐modifying enzymes in the cardiovascular system, constructing regulatory networks, identifying biomarkers to help diagnose CVDs, and combining multiomics technologies to improve diagnostic accuracy. The development of drugs that can regulate the activity of lactylation‐modifying enzymes is a novel therapeutic strategy. Based on the understanding of lactylation modification mechanisms, novel drugs that can specifically regulate lactylation modification will be developed, including small‐molecule compounds and biological agents, to improve cardiovascular function by regulating lactylation modification. Relevant clinical trials will be conducted to verify the safety and effectiveness of treatment methods targeting lactylation modification in patients with CVD, promoting the transformation from basic research to clinical application, and bringing new breakthroughs to the treatment of CVDs.
