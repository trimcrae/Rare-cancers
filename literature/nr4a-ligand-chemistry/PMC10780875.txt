## INTRODUCTION
L-Tyrosine (Tyr), L-phenylalanine (Phe), and L-tryptophan (Trp) are aromatic amino acids (AAAs) that are essential not only for protein biosynthesis in all living cells but also for the production of numerous primary and specialized metabolites ( , ). In plants, AAAs are predominantly synthesized inside the plastid before being translocated into the cytosol for further biosynthesis of vital compounds for plant growth ( – ). Early work demonstrated that Phe and Tyr are synthesized via two alternative pathways: the arogenate pathway and the phenylpyruvate/4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate (PPY/HPP) pathway ( ) ( , ). Many plants use the arogenate pathway, in which prephenate is converted to arogenate by prephenate aminotransferase, followed by decarboxylation or dehydration to Phe and Tyr by arogenate dehydratases or dehydrogenases (known as ADT and ADH), respectively ( ) ( – ). Decades of research have broadly defined the core AAA biosynthetic pathways within plastids ( ). However, recent investigations have shed light on the presence of cytosolic enzymes responsible for the production of Phe and Tyr, highlighting the existence of a PPY/HPP pathway for Phe and potentially for Tyr biosynthesis in the cytosol ( ). For instance, cytosolic chorismate mutase 2, a cytosol-localized ADT3 enzyme with prephenate dehydratase (PDT) activity, and a cytosolic PPY aminotransferase constitute the cytosolic PPY pathway in petunia (Petunia hybrida) ( , ). Similarly, genes encoding enzymes involved in a cytosolic HPP pathway for Tyr formation have been identified in petunia and legumes ( , ). In Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana), the cytosolic enzyme ADT6 exhibits PDT activity, whereas two cytosolic Tyr aminotransferases (Tyr-ATs) display a preference for transaminating HPP to Tyr in vitro ( , ).
 The biosynthesis of AAAs in plastids is tightly regulated through feedback mechanisms, either by the pathway end products or via feedback mediated by downstream metabolites. For example, 3-deoxy-d-arabino-heptulosonate 7-phosphate synthases (DHSs), which catalyze the first committed reaction of the shikimate pathway, are strongly inhibited by the intermediates of shikimate or phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, such as chorismate and caffeate, respectively ( ). The committed enzymes of AAA biosynthesis—ADT, ADH, and anthranilate synthase—are inhibited by their respective final products, Phe, Tyr, and Trp ( ,
 – ). This type of regulation is crucial to ensure sufficient production of AAAs, as the demand for AAAs and their downstream metabolites can vary drastically and rapidly in response to specific developmental or environmental conditions ( – ). However, the cytosolic enzymes involved in AAA biosynthesis appear to be insensitive to feedback regulation ( , ). The cytosolic pathway has been documented to compensate for the production of AAAs in situations where the arogenate route is disrupted ( ). This compensation mechanism potentially plays a role in AAA biosynthesis, particularly when there is an increased demand for their derivatives, such as during plant responses to biotic and abiotic stresses ( , ). To date, our understanding of the regulation of the cytosolic AAA biosynthetic pathway and the coordination of AAA biosynthesis in plastids and the cytosol is limited.
To elucidate the components involved in AAA biosynthesis and metabolism, we conducted a suppressor screen in the arogenate dehydrogenase 2 (adh2) mutant background, which lacks one of the two ADH genes for Tyr biosynthesis in plastids in Arabidopsis ( , ). The severe growth defect observed in the adh2 mutant, attributed to its limited availability of Tyr ( ), renders it an invaluable tool for mutant screening. We used this phenotype as an effective proxy for compensatory mechanisms in AAA biosynthesis. In this work, we describe the identification of REVERSAL OF SAV3 PHENOTYPE 1 (VAS1), a cytosolic aromatic aminotransferase that was previously known for its role in catalyzing the conversion of indole-3-pyruvic acid (3-IPA) to Trp ( , ). VAS1 uses the ethylene biosynthetic intermediate methionine as an amino donor and the auxin biosynthetic intermediate 3-IPA as an amino acceptor to produce Trp and 2-oxo-4-methylthiobutyric acid ( ). The loss of VAS1 function simultaneously increases the levels of auxin and the ethylene precursor 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylate, revealing a key role for VAS1 in coordinating the production of two classical plant hormones ( ). Here, we report a function for VAS1 that uses AAAs as amino donors to produce 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine ( ). The proposed biosynthetic pathways for the two 3-carboxy AAAs occur through the isochorismate pathway in the cytosol, which are separate from the general pathways of aromatic biosynthesis and represent a distinct pathway developed in certain plant species. Furthermore, we reveal a dual function of VAS1 in the production of AAAs, suggesting the possibility that cytosolic pathways contribute to the synthesis of AAAs in Arabidopsis. These discoveries elucidate the fine-tuning of AAA formation by VAS1, revealing potential targets for metabolic engineering highlighting subtleties that will inform rational design strategies for synthesizing 3-carboxy AAAs.

## RESULTS

## Suppressor screening of adh2 identified a recessive loss-of-function point mutation in VAS1
To identify components involved in the biosynthesis or metabolism of AAAs in plants, we conducted a genetic screen to look for suppressors of the Arabidopsis adh2 mutant. The adh2 mutant displays a severe stunted growth phenotype and accumulates significantly lower levels of Tyr, Phe, and Trp than the wild-type Columbia-0 (Col-0) (fig. S1, A to C). We focused here on one of these suppressors of adh2 (soa) mutants, soa1, which partially restored the growth of adh2 and elevated the levels of Tyr, Phe, and Trp over those seen in adh2 (fig. S1, A to C). When we backcrossed the soa1 mutant to adh2, we observed a stunted phenotype similar to adh2 for the soa1 × adh2 F1 progeny (fig. S1D). In the F2 generation, the frequency of soa1-like F2 plants conformed to a Mendelian segregation (3:1 ratio, wild type to mutant), indicating that soa1 is a single recessive mutation. Whole-genome sequencing of soa1-like F2 progenies revealed that soa1 carries a single nucleotide C-to-T mutation in codon 520 (CCT to TCT) in the sixth exon of At1g80360, resulting in a change from proline to serine at residue 174 (fig. S2A). At1g80360 was previously identified as VAS1 and encodes a cytosolic aminotransferase that uses 3-IPA to produce Trp ( ) ( ). The overexpression of VAS1 in the soa1 background fully rescued the loss of VAS1 function and resulted in plants with a similar dwarf phenotype as the adh2 mutant (fig. S3). The overexpression of VAS1 in the adh2 background exacerbated the dwarf phenotype of adh2 (fig. S4). These observations indicate that soa1 is a loss-of-function mutation of VAS1.
VAS1 is a pyridoxal phosphate (PLP)–dependent aminotransferase ( ). The mutation of VAS1 identified in soa1 occurred in a well-conserved amino acid residue located near a predicted PLP binding site (fig. S5). Relative VAS1 transcript levels were comparable among Col-0, the soa1 mutant, and adh2 (fig. S1E). We introduced the mutation identified in soa1 into the VAS1 sequence, yielding the VAS1P174S construct, for generating transgenic lines overexpressing (OE) VAS1P174S in both the Col-0 and adh2 backgrounds. Unlike the diffuse distribution of VAS1 in both backgrounds, we observed punctuate structures in the cytoplasm for VAS1P174S (fig. S6A). We also attempted to produce the mutant protein VAS1P174S in Escherichia coli cells; unfortunately, we failed to obtain any recombinant protein, although we succeeded in producing and purifying intact VAS1 (fig. S6B). We crossed the soa1 mutant with Col-0 plant to remove the adh2 mutation and obtain the VAS1P174S mutant (vas1-2) in the Col-0 background (fig. S2A). We determined the abundance of endogenous VAS1 in Col-0, adh2, soa1, and vas1-2 plants using an anti-VAS1 antibody. Immunoblot analysis indicated that endogenous VAS1 protein accumulates to similar levels in Col-0 and the adh2 mutant. By contrast, we detected no VAS1 protein in the soa1 or vas1-2 mutant (fig. S6C). These findings strongly suggest that the point mutation in VAS1 led to the abnormal expression of VAS1 protein.
We obtained a homozygous transferred DNA (T-DNA) insertion mutant (vas1-1, SALK_207250) in the eighth exon of VAS1, which fully knocks out the VAS1 gene (fig. S2, A and B). The immunoblot analysis also showed that the VAS1 protein was undetectable in the vas1-1 mutant (fig. S6C). When we crossed adh2 to vas1-1, we determined that the disruption of VAS1 in the adh2 background partially rescues the stunted growth phenotype of adh2 ( ), as well as that of its leaves, flowers, and siliques (fig. S2, C to H). In contrast to the adh2 mutant, the vas1-1 adh2 double mutant exhibited elevated levels of Phe, Tyr, and Trp ( ), suggesting a crucial involvement for VAS1 in the utilization of AAAs. These findings provide strong genetic evidence that mutations of VAS1 suppress the adh2 phenotype, supporting the notion that VAS1 likely plays an important role in AAA metabolism within the cytoplasm.
 

## VAS1 uses AAAs as amino donors to produce 3-carboxy AAAs
As previously reported, VAS1 uses 3-IPA as an amino acceptor and methionine (Met) as an amino donor to produce Trp in vitro ( ). However, knocking out VAS1 in adh2 resulted in elevated levels of AAAs (Phe, Tyr, and Trp) ( ). This observation supported the hypothesis that VAS1 likely uses AAAs as substrates to produce other metabolites in vivo. We thus conducted a nontargeted metabolome analysis of 3-week-old Col-0 and vas1 plants by ultrahigh performance liquid chromatography–quadrupole time-of-flight mass spectrometry (UPLC-QTOF-MS) analysis. We obtained a list of 33 metabolite features exhibiting at least a twofold higher abundance (nominal P < 0.01) in Col-0 compared to the vas1-1 mutant ( ). We also subjected the dataset to partial least squares discriminant analysis (PLS-DA), from which we extracted potentially influential features to conduct a regression coefficient plot. Among the top 20 features with the highest variable importance for projection values, two features were more abundant in Col-0 (labeled as a and b, ) and showed positive values in the regression coefficient plots (indicated by red colors; fig. S7). These analyses led to the identification of compounds a and b as two potential in vivo products of VAS1. Tandem MS (MS/MS) analysis revealed that compound a has a mass/charge ratio (m/z) value of 210.0761 in positive ionization mode; the MS/MS fragments indicated that it might be the carboxy-substituted phenylalanine ( ). Comparison of the chromatograph characteristics between compound a and an authentic standard for 3-carboxyphenylalanine revealed that the two chemicals share identical retention time, accurate mass, and MS/MS fragments ( and fig. S8A). We conclude that compound a is 3-carboxyphenylalanine. We similarly determined that compound b, which had a m/z value of 226.0688 in positive ionization mode ( ), is 3-carboxytyrosine based on its MS/MS fragments and comparison to a chemically synthesized standard (fig. S8, C to E).
 Functional VAS1 decreased AAA contents of adh2 compared to those of the vas1-1 adh2 double mutant ( ), whereas the loss of VAS1 function resulted in much lower levels of 3-carboxy AAAs (3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine) relative to Col-0 ( ). Therefore, we speculated that VAS1 may use AAAs as amino donors, thus yielding 3-carboxy AAAs ( ). To test this hypothesis, we chemically synthesized 3-carboxyphenylpyruvate (3CPPY) and 3-carboxy-4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate (3CHPP), the corresponding keto acids of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine, respectively [MS/MS and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectra shown in fig. S9]. We purified recombinant VAS1 in E. coli and incubated the purified protein with 3CPPY, the cofactor PLP, and AAAs as the amino donor. We observed the in vitro formation of 3-carboxyphenylalanine with retention time and MS/MS spectra identical to those of compound a (derived from Col-0 plants) and the authentic standard of 3-carboxyphenylalanine ( and fig. S8B). When we incubated the purified protein with 3CHPP as amino acceptor, we observed a signal for 3-carboxytyrosine in the extracted ion chromatography. The retention time and MS/MS fragments of this signal matched those of compound b derived from Col-0 plants and a chemically synthesized standard ( and fig. S8F). When we mixed plant-derived compounds and commercial or chemically synthesized standards, we obtained a co-elution pattern identical to the individual components for both 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine (fig. S8, G and H). These results provided strong evidence that compounds a and b, the products of VAS1 in Arabidopsis, are 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine, respectively.
In all the above VAS1-catalyzed reactions, Phe, Tyr, and Trp served as amino donors for the conversion of 3-carboxy aromatic keto acids to 3-carboxy AAAs ( ). We observed that the most preferred amino donor for the 3-carboxyphenylalanine-forming and 3-carboxytyrosine-forming in vitro reactions was Tyr ( ). We quantitatively analyzed the accumulation of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine in plants. Targeted metabolite profiling showed that loss of VAS1 function both in the Col-0 and in the adh2 background all results in a substantial decrease of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine levels ( ). By contrast, the content of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine showed a two- to threefold increase in VAS1-OE plants compared to the wild type ( ), while AAA levels decreased in the OE lines (fig. S10). From these observations, we propose that VAS1 functions as a 3CPPY/3CHPP:AAA aminotransferase, using AAAs as amino donors to produce 3-carboxy AAAs both in vitro and in vivo.

## The reverse enzymatic activity of VAS1 in AAA biosynthesis is inhibited by 3-carboxyphenylalanine
Our above results revealed that VAS1, a 3-carboxy AAA-forming aminotransferase, catalyzes transamination reactions between 3-carboxy keto acids and aromatic keto acids. Aminotransferases generally catalyze reversible reactions ( , ). We thus tested the reverse enzymatic activity of VAS1 by evaluating whether it could use PPY, HPP, or 3-IPA as a keto acid substrate ( , and fig. S11). The reverse enzymatic activity was functional for recombinant VAS1, as it catalyzed the formation of Phe, Tyr, or Trp in the presence of amino donors such as Met and several other amino acids ( , and fig. S11). However, 3-carboxyphenylalanine exhibited poor amino donor capabilities for VAS1 in an AAA-forming in vitro reaction ( , and fig. S11), suggesting that VAS1 hardly produces AAAs by catalyzing the conversion of aromatic keto acids to 3-carboxy aromatic keto acids. VAS1 exhibited lower apparent Michaelis-Menten constant (Km) values toward both 3CPPY and 3CHPP compared to PPY, HPP, and 3-IPA when the same amino donor was used (table S1). For example, we determined the Km values of 3CPPY and 3CHPP by VAS1 to be 2.1 and 12.7 μM, respectively, when using Tyr as the amino donor, whereas those of PPY and 3-IPA were 44.9 and 418.6 μM, respectively (table S1). The catalytic efficiencies (kcat/Km) of VAS1 toward aromatic keto acids were much lower than those for 3-carboxy aromatic keto acids (table S1). For instance, the kcat/Km values for 3CPPY and 3CHPP in the presence of Tyr were 43.9 and 7.74 μM−1 min−1, while those of PPY and 3-IPA were 2.27 and 0.24 μM−1 min−1, respectively (table S1). These observations indicate that VAS1 prefers to use 3-carboxy keto acid substrates, using AAAs as the amino donors, for the biosynthesis of 3-carboxy AAAs.
 To evaluate whether the activity of VAS1 in AAA biosynthesis can also be observed in plant tissues, we prepared total protein extracts from vas1 mutants and from VAS1-OE lines. We subjected these extracts to VAS1 enzymatic assays by adding varying concentrations of PPY, HPP, or 3-IPA as keto acids (fig. S12). The addition of PPY to the protein extracts led to an increase in Phe content, with higher accumulation of Phe with increasing concentration of PPY (fig. S12A). Notably, Phe contents measured from the vas1 mutant extracts were substantially lower than that from the Col-0 extracts, whereas it was much higher in the extracts prepared from the VAS1 OE lines (fig. S12A). We obtained similar results when using HPP or 3-IPA as keto acid substrates (fig. S12, B and C), suggesting that VAS1 can potentially use PPY, HPP, and 3-IPA as physiological keto acid substrates to form AAAs. Consistent with the observation from the in vitro enzyme assay, VAS1 activity toward 3CPPY and 3CHPP in the crude protein extract was also much higher than that for PPY, HPP, or 3-IPA (fig. S12, D and E), indicating the preference of VAS1 for 3-carboxy keto acid substrates in vivo. Notably, we observed a notable drop in the levels of Phe, Tyr, and Trp when we included 3-carboxyphenylalanine as a potential amino donor in the enzymatic reaction, regardless of the presence of a keto acid substrate (fig. S13A). Consistent with this observation, the addition of 3-carboxyphenylalanine also resulted in a notable inhibition of recombinant VAS1 enzymatic activity for the formation of AAAs in vitro (fig. S13B). These observations suggested that 3-carboxyphenylalanine may inhibit the biosynthesis of AAAs both in vitro and in vivo.
The activity of VAS1 in the formation of Phe, Tyr, and Trp differed in terms of 3-carboxyphenylalanine feedback inhibition ( ). VAS1 activity for the formation of Tyr was highly sensitive to 3-carboxyphenylalanine, with a half maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) of 0.28 mM, whereas that for Phe and Trp was less sensitive to 3-carboxyphenylalanine, with IC50 of 0.55 and 0.86 mM, respectively ( ). We further examined the biological consequences of 3-carboxyphenylalanine in vivo in Arabidopsis seedlings. Accordingly, we treated 1-week-old Col-0 plants on growth medium containing 3-carboxyphenylalanine and collected the resulting seedlings after 7 days. The presence of 3-carboxyphenylalanine led to a strong decrease in Phe levels compared to control seedlings grown in the absence of 3-carboxyphenylalanine ( ). Similarly, the presence of 3-carboxyphenylalanine led to a substantial decrease in Tyr and Trp levels in vivo ( ). Thus, 3-carboxyphenylalanine functioned as an inhibitor of AAA biosynthesis in Arabidopsis. The above results indicate that VAS1 can catalyze the formation of both AAAs and 3-carboxy AAAs. The latter, such as 3-carboxyphenylalanine, can exert an inhibitory effect on VAS1 enzymatic activity during the formation of AAAs.

## 3-Carboxy AAAs are derived from the isochorismate pathway but are present in only specific species
3-Carboxy AAAs are metabolites that have been insufficiently investigated in terms of their biosynthesis pathway and functional roles ( – ). To follow their biosynthetic route in planta, we performed isotopic tracing experiments with 13C6-labeled Phe or 13C6-labeled Tyr. In neither case did we detect labeled 3-carboxyphenylalanine or 3-carboxytyrosine (fig. S14). This observation led us to speculate that neither 3-carboxyphenylalanine nor 3-carboxytyrosine is derived from other pathways rather than from the direct modification of Phe or Tyr in plants. 3-Carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine were first identified from land plants in the 1960s and were later suggested to be derived from chorismate through a pathway involving isochorismate ( , ). In Arabidopsis, chorismate is converted to isochorismate through the action of isochorismate synthase (ICS), which diverts the metabolic flux from AAA biosynthesis ( , ). Subsequently, isochorismate is exported from plastids to the cytosol, where it participates in the biosynthesis of the plant hormone salicylic acid (SA) ( ) ( , ). To investigate the potential involvement of ICS in the biosynthesis of 3-carboxy AAAs, we used the ics1 ics2 double mutant to block the isochorismate pathway. Notably, we detected no 3-carboxyphenylalanine or 3-carboxytyrosine in the ics1 ics2 mutant ( ). These findings provided genetic evidence that the biosynthesis of 3-carboxy AAAs occurs through the isochorismate pathway ( ). We further performed isotopic tracing with chemically synthesized 13C6-labeled 3-carboxyphenylalanine (MS/MS spectra displayed in fig. S15). We detected three potential metabolites derived from 3-carboxyphenylalanine as being labeled (table S2). When 3-carboxyphenylalanine was administered in parallel, we identified an additional 38 potential metabolites labeled with 13C6-3-carboxyphenylalanine (table S2), suggesting the existence of numerous derivatives from 3-carboxyphenylalanine when this pathway is active. These observations uncover an unexplored AAA metabolic pathway producing 3-carboxy AAAs and their derivatives via the activity of VAS1.
To explore the phylogenetic distribution of the 3-carboxy AAA biosynthesis pathway, we selected 34 representative species of bacteria, algae, bryophytes, lycophytes, pteridophytes, monocots, and dicots (table S3). We looked for the proteins with the highest sequence identity with Arabidopsis VAS1 and Arabidopsis ICS1 in these species in the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) protein database using the Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST) (table S3). Phylogenetic analysis suggested that VAS1 and ICS1 homologs can each be divided into differently related classes among eudicots, monocots, and other species ( ). A prokaryotic origin of ICS1 was supported by the presence of ICS genes in the chloroplast genomes of algae and other plant species that use the ICS pathway to synthesize SA ( ), indicating a widespread ICS pathway in nature ( ). AAAs and SA were widespread among these species ( and fig. S16). However, most of the species tested here, for example, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, a bacterium that synthesizes SA using the ICS pathway ( ), did not accumulate a detectable amount of 3-carboxy AAAs ( ). We only detected 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine in specific species, such as Brassica rapa, Brassica oleracea, and radish (Raphanus sativus) from Brassicaceae ( and fig. S16). We chose several representative aminotransferases from Brassica napus (BnAT), tomato (Solanum lycopersicum, SlAT), maize (Zea mays, ZmAT), and soybean (Glycine max, GmAT). In vitro enzymatic assays revealed that all these aminotransferases produce AAAs and 3-carboxy AAAs (fig. S17). These results suggest that the absence of 3-carboxy AAAs in these species may be attributed to the lack of substrates required for their biosynthesis. Moreover, the VAS1-mediated 3-carboxy AAA biosynthesis pathway is only present and active in specific plant species.
 

## DISCUSSION
AAAs are required for protein biosynthesis and the formation of numerous aromatic primary and secondary metabolites ( , ). Arabidopsis contains two plastic ADH enzymes encoded by ADH1 and ADH2 to decarboxylate arogenate into Tyr ( ). ADH2 is critical for maintaining amino acid homeostasis and controlling plant development, as it is strongly feedback inhibited by its product Tyr ( ). Genetic screening has recently been conducted to identify suppressors of adh2, revealing multiple dominant mutations in DHS enzymes targeting the entry step of the shikimate pathway in plastids. These mutations help enhance the production of AAAs and improve CO2 assimilation ( ). In our screen for suppressors of adh2, we identified VAS1, a cytosolic aromatic aminotransferase, whose loss of function partially rescued the growth defects of adh2 ( ). VAS1 was previously identified in a screen for suppressors of tryptophan aminotransferase of Arabidopsis 1 (taa1), whose encoded enzymes convert the auxin biosynthetic intermediate 3-IPA to Trp in Arabidopsis ( ). Another study identified this gene as INDOLE SEVERE SENSITIVE (ISS1) due to its involvement in the indole-dependent overproduction of indole-3-acetic acid ( ). Both studies suggested that VAS1 contributes to Trp biosynthesis. Our work indicates that VAS1 participates in the catabolism of not only Trp but also of Phe and Tyr, because the disruption of VAS1 partially rescued the defect in AAA biosynthesis deficiency observed in adh2 ( ). Our data demonstrate that VAS1 uses AAAs as amino donors to catalyze the formation of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine in vitro and in vivo ( ). When VAS1 is disrupted, AAAs that would have been consumed are “saved,” so that the phenotype of reduced AAAs in adh2 is partially rescued.
Aminotransferases often can catalyze reactions in both directions, indicating their capability to facilitate reversible reactions ( , ). For instance, tryptophan aminotransferase of arabidopsis 1 (TAA1) converts Trp to 3-IPA but also exhibits the reversible activity of a Trp aminotransferase, converting 3-IPA back to Trp ( ). The capability of TAA1 to catalyze the reverse reaction ensures the precise biosynthesis of 3-IPA, thus optimizing the homeostasis of auxin ( ). VAS1 has also been demonstrated to catalyze 3-IPA to Trp ( ). However, it is unknown why plants have two different aminotransferases to catalyze the reaction from 3-IPA to Trp. Our study demonstrated that VAS1 is a 3CPPY/3CHPP:AAAs aminotransferase, consuming AAAs to form 3-carboxy AAAs ( ). VAS1 also functions as an AAA-forming enzyme when amino acids such as Met were used as amino donors. However, the reverse enzymatic activity of VAS1 is limited when 3-carboxyphenylalanine serves as the substrate ( and fig. S10), enabling the irreversible production of 3-carboxy AAAs. Non-AAAs are among the amino donors used by VAS1 to produce AAAs ( and fig. S10), indicating that the VAS1-mediated reactions are involved in the recycling of certain amino acids.
The idea that plastids are the exclusive site of AAA biosynthesis has been challenged by the identification of enzymes involved in the cytosolic PPY/HPP pathway for Phe and Tyr biosynthesis ( , ). The recently reported metabolic cross-talk between cytosolic Phe and auxin biosynthesis highlights the biological importance of cytosolic-derived AAAs ( ). Because the large arrays of AAA-derived compounds are essential for plant growth and development, stringent regulation of AAA metabolism and biosynthesis from plastids and cytosol is critical ( ). VAS1 efficiently catalyzes AAA metabolism to produce 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine in an irreversible reaction ( and fig. S10). However, VAS1 can also recycle other amino acids to produce AAAs in the cytosol. The Km values of VAS1 toward both 3CPPY and 3CHPP were substantially lower than those for PPY, HPP, and 3-IPA when the same amino donor was used (table S1). These distinct Km values ensure the preference of VAS1 for catalyzing the formation of 3-carboxy AAAs and the efficient metabolism of AAAs to prevent their excessive accumulation. At the same time, the products of these reactions inhibit cytosolic AAA biosynthesis catalyzed by VAS1, maintaining a balanced metabolic flux. The substrate specificity of VAS1 reflects a fine-tuned mechanism that ensures the maintenance of AAA homeostasis in response to fluctuations in AAA biosynthesis under various conditions.
We elucidated the de novo biosynthesis pathway for 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine in the cytosol ( ), which branches off from the highly conserved ICS-mediated SA biosynthesis pathway found in the plant kingdom as well as in bacteria ( , ). Unlike the widely distributed plant hormone SA, the biosynthesis pathways of 3-carboxy-substituted AAAs appear to have evolved after the emergence of dicots. We detected 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine only in certain plant species from the Brassicaceae family ( ). The reasons behind the unique VAS1-mediated mechanisms that control the levels of AAAs in these specific plant species remain unclear. We did not identify any metabolites that could be assigned to 3-carboxytryptophan, suggesting that the biosynthesis pathway of 3-carboxytryptophan might not exist in most of the species tested in this study. Notably, 3-carboxy AAAs might serve as precursors to numerous unexplored metabolites in Arabidopsis. We identified at least three potential metabolites derived from 3-carboxyphenylalanine in this study. Moreover, when 3-carboxyphenylalanine was administered in parallel with 13C6-3-carboxyphenylalanine, we detected 41 potential labeled metabolites (table S2). These findings strongly indicate the existence of a diverse array of derivatives originating from 3-carboxyphenylalanine when this metabolic pathway is active.
The biosynthesis pathways of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine represent a previously unknown route for AAA metabolism. Our study provides opportunities for metabolic engineering of natural products using 3-carboxy AAAs as precursors, not through direct structural modification of AAAs, but rather the metabolism of AAAs acts as an amino donor in these reactions ( ). Overall, our findings shed light on the complex and fine-tuned regulation of AAA biosynthesis and metabolism in plants, expanding our understanding beyond the conventional plastid biosynthesis pathway. Further research is required to investigate the significance of the evolution of the ICS-dependent biosynthesis pathway for 3-carboxy AAAs, which could provide valuable insights into the evolutionary history and functional significance of AAA regulation.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

## Chemicals
3-IPA, HPP, PPY, SA, Tyr, Phe, Trp, and other amino acids used in this study were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA). An authentic standard for 3-carboxyphenylalanine was purchased from Shanghai BidePharmatech Co. Ltd. (https://bidepharmatech.com/). Ring-13C-labeled Phe (13C6-Phe) and 13C6-Tyr were purchased from Cambridge Isotope Laboratories (Andover, MA, USA). 3CPPY and 3CHPP were chemically synthesized by Z.Z. (Peking Union Medical College, Chinese Academy of Medical Science) using the protocols that were previously described ( – ). 3-Carboxytyrosine and 13C6-3-carboxyphenylalanine were chemically synthesized by Tronnal Biomedicine (http://tronnal.com/). All synthesized chemicals used in this study were confirmed by carbon-13 NMR spectrometry, proton NMR (HNMR), and MS analyses. MS/MS and NMR spectra for these chemicals are shown in figs. S8, S9, and S15.

## Plant materials and growth conditions
The Arabidopsis (A. thaliana) T-DNA insertion mutants adh2 (SALK_001765, in At1g15710) and vas1-1 (SALK_207250, in At1g80360) are in the Col-0 background. The adh2 mutant was provided by Y. Qi (Tsinghua University, China). The vas1 mutant was purchased from AraShare (http://arashare.cn). The ics1 ics2 double mutant was described in our previous work ( ). For screening of adh2 suppressors, ~150,000 adh2 mutant seeds were mutagenized using 0.2% (v/v) ethyl methane sulfonate for ~16 hours with shaking. Mutagenized seeds were sown for two generations to obtain M2 seeds. Seedlings whose phenotype was similar to wild type were selected as putative adh2 suppressors. After verifying the phenotype in the next generation, one of the putative adh2 suppressors, soa1, was chosen for characterization. In this study, Arabidopsis seeds were sown on half-strength Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium (Caisson), supplemented with 1.5% (w/v) sucrose and solidified with 0.4% (w/v) Phytagel (Sigma-Aldrich). The plates were stored at 4°C in darkness for 3 days and then transferred to a growth chamber under long-day conditions with a 16-hour light/8-hour dark photoperiod at 22°C. The T-DNA insertion lines for VAS1 were screened by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) using specific primers (listed in table S4), and PCR products were sequenced to determine the exact insertion points. For the generation of VAS1 OE lines, the 35S:VAS1-cYFP and 35S:VAS1P174S-cYFP (VAS1 carrying a proline-to-serine mutation at residue 174) constructs were generated using the ClonExpress II One Step Cloning Kit (catalog no. C112, Vazyme). The full-length coding DNA sequences (CDS) of VAS1 and VAS1P174S (without stop codon) were individually amplified using cDNA prepared from total RNA extracted from Col-0 and soa1 plants, respectively, as template. The fragments were cloned into a modified pCAMBIA1300 vector carrying a C-terminal of yellow fluorescent protein (cYFP) tag. The 35S:VAS1-cYFP and 35S:VAS1P174S-cYFP constructs were transformed into Agrobacterium (Agrobacterium tumefaciens) strain GV3101; the resulting Agrobacteria cultures were used to transform 5- to 6-week-old Col-0 or adh2 plants using a modified floral dip method ( ). Primary (T1) transformants were selected on half-strength MS containing hygromycin (30 μg/ml) and timentin (100 μg/ml). Independent T1 seedlings with a positive YFP fluorescence signal were selected, and homozygous T3 progeny were obtained for experiments.

## Reverse transcription PCR and quantitative reverse transcription PCR assays
Total RNA was extracted from 3-week-old Arabidopsis seedlings or 6-week-old Arabidopsis flowers using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Total RNA (2 μg) was reverse-transcribed by Superscript Reverse Transcriptase III (Invitrogen) and used for PCR or quantitative PCR (qPCR). PCR was performed with 2× EasyTaq PCR SuperMix (TransGen) using TUBULIN 5 as control. qPCR was performed with SYBR Premix Ex Taq (Takara) using TUBULIN 5 as an internal control. The average levels of transcripts were calculated from three biological replicates, and each biological replicate was tested with three technical replicates. The primers are listed in table S4.

## Immunoblotting
Arabidopsis seedlings were ground in liquid nitrogen, and the crude proteins were extracted with 100 mM tris-HCl (pH 6.8), 200 mM dithiothreitol (DTT), 4% (w/v) SDS, and 20% (v/v) glycerol. The proteins were separated by 10% (w/v) SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis before being transferred to membrane (Millipore) and immunoblotted with anti-Arabidopsis VAS1 antibody that was developed from the amino acids of Arabidopsis VAS1 protein (AboRo), anti–green fluorescent protein (Roche), and anti-ACTIN (ABclonal) antibodies. Immunodetection was conducted with a SuperSignal West Pico PLUS Chemiluminescent Substrate (Thermo Fisher Scientific).

## Protein purification
The full-length coding sequences of VAS1 and other aminotransferase genes from different species were individually amplified and cloned into the pMAL-c2x vector with maltose-binding protein (MBP) tag. Primers for the constructs are listed in table S4. Vectors (MBP tag) were transformed in E. coli strain BL21 (DE3). The cells were grown in 1 liter of LB liquid culture at 37°C until the optical density of 600 nm reached approximately 0.8, and then isopropyl-β-d-thiogalactoside (0.5 mM) was added to induce recombinant protein production. Cells were grown at 16°C for 20 hours and harvested by centrifugation at 5000 rpm for 30 min at 4°C. Cell pellets were resuspended in column buffer [20 mM tris-HCl (pH 7.4), 200 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, and 1 mM DTT] and lysed by sonication. The recombinant VAS1-MBP was then purified from the supernatant using amylose resin (New England Biolabs).

## Nontargeted metabolome analysis
For nontargeted metabolomics analysis, 21-day-old Arabidopsis plants from the wild type (Col-0) and the vas1-1 mutant were extracted with 80% (v/v) methanol, and the supernatant was collected and filtered through a 0.22-μm Millipore membrane before analysis. UPLC-QTOF-MS analysis was performed on an Agilent 1290 LC system equipped with a Q-TOF 6546 mass spectrometer (Agilent Technologies) as described in our previous work with slight modifications ( ). The mobile phase consisted of a linear gradient of 0.1% (v/v) aqueous formic acid (A) and acetonitrile (B): 0 to 8.0 min, 5 to 45% B (v/v); 8.0 to 15.0 min, 45 to 95% B (v/v); 15.0 to 20 min, 95% B (v/v); 20.0 to 25.0 min, 5% B (v/v). The flow rate was 0.3 ml/min, and the injected volume was 5 μl. The electrospray ionization (ESI) source parameters were set according to our previously developed method ( ). All data were recorded and processed using Agilent MassHunter Workstation software (version B.08.00), and Profinder (version 10.0) was used to extract data. Data analysis was conducted using a web-based tool (www.metaboanalyst.ca). Data were normalized by median centering, and significantly altered metabolites were defined as those with absolute log2 fold change > 1 and P < 0.01 for vas1-1. PLS-DA was performed to discern differences between Col-0 and mutant plants.

## Isotopic labeling experiments
Isotopic labeling experiments and analysis were performed as described in our previous work with slight modifications ( ). For the in vivo labeling experiments performed with 13C6-3-carboxyphenylalanine, Arabidopsis Col-0 seeds were sown on half-strength MS medium containing 100 μM 13C6-3-carboxyphenylalanine. Experiments using 50% dimethyl sulfoxide solvent (mock) and unlabeled 3-carboxyphenylalanine (natural-abundance sample) were conducted in parallel. After 7 days, seedlings were harvested for metabolite analysis.

## Accurate mass high-resolution MS/MS analysis
The chemical structures of 3CPPY, 3CHPP, 3-carboxyphenylalanine, and 3-carboxytyrosine were elucidated by high-resolution accurate mass MS/MS analysis with UPLC-QTOF-MS. For data analysis, Mass Hunter Qualitative Analysis Software B.08.00 was used.

## In vitro VAS1 enzymatic assays
Enzymatic assays of VAS1 and other aminotransferases from different species were conducted as previously described with modifications ( ). Reactions were performed in 100-μl reactions containing 2 mM amino donor (e.g., Phe, Tyr, or Trp), 1 mM 3CPPY or 3CHPP, 0.2 mM PLP, and 1 μg of purified VAS1 recombinant protein in a reaction buffer consisting of 50 mM K2HPO4/KH2PO4 (pH 8.5). Steady-state kinetic assays for VAS1 were performed in 100-μl reactions containing variable amounts of amino acceptor (1 to 1000 μM) and 100 μM keto acids. All reactions were incubated at 37°C for 1 hour with shaking at 800 rpm and subsequently stopped by the addition of 100 μl of methanol. The reaction product was placed in a freezer at −80°C for 2 hours to precipitate the protein. The samples were then filtered through a 0.22-μm Millipore membrane before analysis. All reactions were conducted in triplicate.

## Preparation of crude protein extracts from Arabidopsis and enzymatic assays
The extraction of crude proteins from Arabidopsis was conducted as previously described with modifications ( ). One-week-old Arabidopsis leaves were harvested and ground to a fine powder in a mortar and pestle in liquid nitrogen. More than 5 g of powder was resuspended in 20 ml of extraction buffer [20 mM Hepes (pH 7.6), 1 mM DTT, and 0.1% (v/v) β-mercaptoethanol] and filtered through Miracloth. Samples were centrifuged at 10,000g for 10 min and subsequently at 5000g for 60 min at 4°C. The resulting supernatant was concentrated by centrifugation with ultracentrifugal filters until the solution volume was less than 1 ml. The concentrated solution was stored at 4°C for enzymatic assays. The VAS1 assay was performed at 37°C for 1 hour in an assay mixture containing 50 μl of enzyme extract, 48 μl of 100 mM tris-HCl (pH 8.8), 0.1 μl of 200 mM PLP, 1 μl of 100 mM amino donor, and 1 μl of 100 mM keto acids. The reaction was conducted in triplicate and terminated by adding 100 μl of methanol before analysis. Separations of products from in vitro and in vivo enzymatic assays were performed using an Agilent ZORBAX Eclipse Plus C18 column (2.1 mm by 50 mm, 1.8 μm) operated at 25°C. The product of the VAS1 reaction was monitored on an Agilent 1290 ultrahigh performance liquid chromatography coupled to a 6470 triple quadrupole mass spectrometer (UPLC-QQQ-MS/MS) (Agilent Technology, Santa Clara, CA, USA) and analyzed using the following conditions as described in the next section.

## Metabolite extraction and analysis
Amino acids in plants were extracted from 10 mg (dry weight) of 3-week-old Arabidopsis seedlings with 1 ml of 25 mM HCl in 50% (v/v) methanol containing 10 μl of internal standard solution (cell free amino acid mixture-13C,15N, Sigma-Aldrich) ( ). Samples were shaken at 1500 rpm for 40 min at 4°C, and extracts were centrifuged at 12,000 rpm for 10 min. The supernatants were subsequently filtered using a 0.22-μm nylon membrane, and 200 μl of the extraction supernatant per sample was transferred into autosampler vials. UPLC-QQQ-MS/MS analysis was conducted for the detection of amino acids. Separations were performed using an Agilent hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC) column (100 mm by 3.0 mm, 2.7 μm) operated at 25°C. The flow rate of the mobile phase was 0.5 ml/min. Eluent A was 20 mM ammonium formate in water (pH 3), and eluent B was 20 mM aqueous ammonium formate (pH 3) in 9:1 (v/v) acetonitrile/water. The separation gradient was 100% B at 0 min, 70% (v/v) B at 11.5 min, and 100% B at 12 min. The injection volume of each sample was 2 μl. The optimum operating ESI conditions were as follows: gas temperature, 330°C; gas flow rate, 13 liters/min; nebulizer pressure, 35 psi; sheath gas temperature, 390°C; and sheath gas flow, 12 liters/min. The capillary voltages were optimized to 1500 V in positive mode with equal nozzle voltages (0 V). The LC electrospray ionization multiple reaction monitoring (LC-ESI-MRM) method was used for the quantification of amino acids; detailed conditions are provided in table S5.
The extraction of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine in plants was performed as described above for the nontargeted metabolomics analysis. Separations were performed using an Agilent ZORBAX Eclipse Plus C18 column (2.1 mm by 100 mm, 1.8 μm) operated at 25°C. The flow rate of the mobile phase was 0.3 ml/min. Eluent A was 0.1% (v/v) formic acid in water, and Eluent B was acetonitrile. The separation gradient was 5% (v/v) B at 0 to 1 min, 60% (v/v) B at 6 min, and 5% (v/v) B at 9 min. The injection volume of each sample was 2 μl. The LC-ESI-MRM method was used for the quantification; detailed conditions are provided in table S5. The linear range for quantified compounds was established to be 0.001 to 5 or 10 μM per injection with a correlation coefficient of 0.9998 to 0.9999 (table S5).

## Multiple sequence alignment of VAS1 homologs and phylogenetic analysis
The VAS1 sequence was used as a query for a BLASTp search (https://blast.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/), and 467 sequences were selected with more than 50% overall sequence identity and more than 90% sequence coverage with VAS1. Multiple alignment using fast fourier transform (MAFFT) version 7 ( ) was used to generate a multiple sequence alignment for the VAS1 homologs. On the basis of the result of multiple sequence alignment, four proline residues at the PLP binding site were used to generate sequence logos by WebLogo 3 ( ). Sample source information for the various species used in is listed in table S3. The sequences of VAS1, ICS1, and their orthologs in different plant species were extracted from NCBI; their NCBI accession numbers are summarized in table S3. The evolutionary history was inferred using the maximum likelihood (ML) method, and the analyses were conducted in MEGA11 ( ).

## Statistical analysis
Statistical tests were performed in GraphPad Prism 8.0 software and SPSS software. Error bars indicate means ± SD and different letters in the figures indicate the result analysis of variance (ANOVA) testing followed by a Tukey’s post hoc test (P < 0.05).
