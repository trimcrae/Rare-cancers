## Abstract
The molecular recognition process between proteins is the foundation of complex biological functions, driven by residue-level interactions between regulatory and functional domains. Therefore, change in network is the root cause of normal physiology to pathophysiology. Since the network can only be traced through structural data, such insights are essential. However, identifying the critical structural and conformational determinants facilitating signalling cascades remains a major challenge for protein-protein interactions (PPIs) based therapeutic interventions. This challenge is further compounded by the absence of structural data, which makes deciphering the intricate web of PPIs even more difficult. Structural insights are paramount, as PPIs are inherently flexible, exploring a dynamic conformational space characterized by low-energy states interconnected by high-energy transition paths. Autophagy is a cellular process heavily reliant on PPIs, and researchers from academia and industry are targeting them for therapeutic intervention due to their beneficial role in the modulation of multiple diseases, including cancer, neurodegenerative and metabolic diseases. In autophagy pathway, Beclin 1 is a pivotal protein in the signalling cascade. However, targeting Beclin 1 for therapeutic purposes and understanding its role in the signalling cascades remain challenging, primarily due to the lack of structural insights into the mechanisms governing its interactions with its regulatory partners. To overcome these challenges, we integrate AlphaFold predicted models with experimentally resolved PDB structures to construct a comprehensive, domain wise and residue level map of Beclin 1 interactome capturing both structured and unstructured regions, identifying critical interaction interfaces, and uncovering pivotal determinants for Beclin 1 specific therapeutic interventions.
Keywords: Autophagy; Beclin 1; PPI, MD simulations; Disorder-to-order transition; Diseases; Therapeutics

## Introduction
Protein-protein interactions (PPIs) play a vital role in various biological cascades, including the modulation of autophagy pathways . While PPIs provide opportunities for therapeutic intervention by enabling specific interactions with protein partners, they also present significant challenges. Applying the PPI-based approach to autophagy-related pathways holds great potential for therapeutic development; however, a lack of mechanistic and structural understanding remains a major limitation, restricting our ability to explore their atomic-level interactions and recognition processes , . This structural ambiguity complicates the identification of druggable pockets for designing small molecules targeting these interactions, rendering them undruggable . The challenge is even greater in cases where the structure of the interacting regions is available, as PPIs are inherently dynamic and transient. The presence of unstructured regions further complicates the process, making it a challenging research problem , . Despite these difficulties, modulating biological phenomena through PPIs offers broad therapeutic applications due to the potential for selective pharmacological effects. Further exploration of PPIs is essential for developing controlled therapies, as both positive and negative regulation of pathways can lead to toxicity. For example, while autophagy inducers can enhance cellular clearance and restore homeostasis offering therapeutic benefits in conditions such as neurodegenerative diseases, infections, and metabolic disorders excessive or dysregulated autophagy may become maladaptive, contributing to pathological cell death in certain contexts, including cardiac disorders and cancer , . This duality underscores the importance of context-specific modulation of autophagy, as its effects can range from protective to detrimental depending on the intensity, duration, and cellular environment in which it is activated. Autophagy is a highly conserved cellular process that maintains homeostasis, ensures quality control, and facilitates stress adaptation in eukaryotic cells . The process is initiated by the formation of a pre-autophagosomal structure (PAS), followed by membrane nucleation, elongation, and eventual maturation into a double-membraned autophagosome. This autophagosome then fuses with a lysosome, forming an autolysosome where the enclosed contents are degraded and recycled back into the cytoplasm ( ,A) , , , . Being a complex cellular event, autophagy requires multiple protein partners to coordinate its successful execution. Currently, 40 autophagy-related proteins (ATGs) have been identified and characterized, of which 16 form the core machinery required for autophagosome biogenesis , . These include the ULK1 complex, ATG9A trafficking components, and the ATG12 conjugation system, each playing distinct roles at different stages of autophagosome formation and maturation ( ,A). Apart from ATGs, phosphatidylinositol 3-kinases (PI3-kinases) also play a crucial role in the mammalian autophagy pathway. The catalytic subunit structure of PI3-kinases classifies them into three types. Class I and class II PI3-kinases are primarily involved in signal transduction in response to various environmental stimuli , whereas class III PI3-kinases regulate intracellular membrane trafficking . In mammals, class III PI3-kinases exist in two complexes: complex I, which is essential for autophagosome formation, and complex II, which is required for autophagosome maturation. Complex I consists of VPS34, VPS15, Beclin 1, and Atg14L, whereas in complex II, UV irradiation resistance-associated gene (UVRAG) replaces Atg14L . In 1998, a team of Japanese scientists discovered a novel autophagy-related gene, ATG6, in yeast, which was found to regulate autophagy through protein complex formation. Later, Professor Beth Levine and her team identified Beclin 1 as the mammalian homolog of ATG6, demonstrating its similar function , , . Beclin 1 exists as an inactive homodimer, associated with Bcl-2, a negative regulator of Beclin 1 . Under cellular stress, Bcl-2 dissociates from the Beclin 1 homodimer through multiple phosphorylation events. Simultaneously, the Beclin 1 homodimer itself dissociates, leading to the formation of Beclin 1-VPS34-VPS15 core complexes which play a crucial role in autophagy and membrane trafficking, influencing various physiological and pathological processes . Beclin 1 activity increases under cellular stress and decreases during the cell cycle, underscoring its vital role in programmed cell survival . Beyond its role in autophagy regulation, Beclin 1 directs autophagic proteins to pre-autophagosomal structures (PAS) and modulates the activity of the lipid kinase VPS34. These functions are mediated through interactions with various protein partners, including Atg14L, UVRAG, Bif-1, Rubicon, Ambra1, HMGB1, nPIST, VMP1, SLAM, IP3R, PINK, survivin, USP19, and PACER, all of which contribute to autophagy regulation , . These interactions occur at specific protein-protein interfaces, making molecular recognition of Beclin 1's interacting partners essential for understanding its function. Identifying these critical interaction sites is key to deciphering the Beclin 1 interactome in a PPI-driven manner. Despite advancements in PPI mapping and structural elucidation, major challenges persist. The absence of well-curated atomic-level data on interaction interface sites, their dynamics, and allosteric sites, coupled with limited knowledge of ligand-binding pockets in key autophagy proteins, hinders targeted modulation of Beclin 1 . These limitations prevent precise control over Beclin 1's activity, making it difficult to selectively regulate autophagy. Addressing these challenges is crucial for realizing the therapeutic potential of autophagy modulation via PPIs , , . To overcome these barriers, here we employs an integrated strategy that combines AlphaFold predicted full length models of Beclin 1 interactors with experimentally resolved structures. This approach enables residue level mapping of Beclin 1 interaction interfaces across its all domain, bridging gaps in structural coverage and enhancing mechanistic understanding of Beclin 1 mediated autophagy regulation. As a major regulator of autophagy, Beclin 1 remains a prime target for developing strategies to fine-tune autophagy in various disease contexts. However, a deeper understanding of its interacting partners, binding interfaces, and key residues responsible for complex formation is essential. Here, we curate these critical determinants and provide a structure-function overview of Beclin 1 interactors, offering insights that can guide the design of targeted therapeutics.

## Beclin 1 is a central regulator implicated in multiple pathological processes
Dysregulation of Beclin 1 is implicated in various diseases due to its critical role in maintaining cellular homeostasis , , . In various cancers, it acts as a haploinsufficient tumour suppressor due to its monoallelic deletion , . Beyond cancer, Beclin 1 is also pivotal in liver diseases, neurodegenerative disorders, kidney diseases, inflammatory bowel disease, diabetes mellitus, cardiovascular conditions, and infectious diseases , , , , , , . Pathogens such as viruses and bacteria exploit Beclin 1-mediated autophagy to enhance their survival and replication ( ,B) , . Below, we discuss some of the major diseases in which Beclin 1 involvement is highly evident.
2.1.

## Role of Beclin 1 in cancer
Beclin 1 acts as a tumour suppressor through its involvement in autophagy-mediated cell death and the regulation of cellular senescence , . Studies have shown that monoallelic deletions and epigenetic silencing of Beclin 1 are common in various cancers, including breast, ovarian, and prostate cancers, promoting tumorigenesis and metastasis , , , . The downregulation of Beclin 1 in cancer cells often correlates with poor prognosis and resistance to therapy, underscoring its clinical significance , .
Mechanistically, Beclin 1 interacts with Bcl-2 family proteins, such as Bcl-2 and Bcl-XL, to regulate autophagy and apoptosis balance . Disruption of these interactions using targeted therapies has shown promise in preclinical models by restoring autophagic flux and sensitizing cancer cells to chemotherapy-induced cell death , . Moreover, recent advancements in Beclin 1 mimetics and peptide regulators highlight potential therapeutic avenues for targeting autophagy dysregulation in cancer treatment.

2.2.

## Neurodegenerative diseases and Beclin 1
In neurodegenerative disorders, including alzheimer's disease, huntington's disease, macado joseph's disease and parkinson's disease, impaired autophagy due to Beclin 1 dysfunction contributes to the accumulation of misfolded proteins and neuronal cell death , , , , . Research suggests that enhancing Beclin 1 expression or activity can alleviate neurotoxic protein aggregation and improve neuronal survival in experimental models , .
Furthermore, microRNA-mediated regulation of Beclin 1 expression adds another layer of complexity to its role in neuroprotection and disease pathogenesis . For instance, miR-30a and miR-376b have been implicated in modulating Beclin 1 levels in alzheimer's disease models, highlighting the intricate regulatory networks governing autophagy in the brain . Future studies exploring these mechanisms could lead to novel therapeutic strategies aimed at restoring autophagy flux and mitigating neurodegenerative pathology.

2.3.

## Cardiovascular implications of Beclin 1
In cardiovascular diseases, Beclin 1 mediated autophagy plays a critical role in maintaining cardiac homeostasis and responding to stress conditions, such as ischemia/reperfusion injury and cardiomyopathies . Beclin 1 facilitates the selective removal of damaged organelles, particularly through mitophagy, thereby preserving cellular function and preventing apoptosis in cardiac cells , . Dysregulation of Beclin 1 signalling pathways, including interactions with AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) and mTOR, contributes to cardiac dysfunction and adverse remodelling in various cardiovascular disorders .
Preclinical studies have demonstrated that enhancing Beclin 1-mediated autophagy can protect against myocardial infarction and heart failure by reducing oxidative stress and preserving mitochondrial integrity . Therapeutic strategies aimed at modulating Beclin 1 expression or activity, such as gene therapy or small molecule agonists, hold promise for mitigating cardiovascular disease progression and improving clinical outcomes , . However, further research is needed to elucidate the specific roles of Beclin 1 in different cardiac pathologies and optimize targeted interventions.

2.4.

## Beclin 1 in infectious diseases
The role of Beclin 1 extends in infectious diseases, where it influences host-pathogen interactions and immune responses. Pathogenic viruses and bacteria, including HIV, herpesvirus, influenza, HCMV, rabies, epstein-barr virus, flaviviruses and hepatitis viruses, coxiella burnetii, acinetobacter baumannii, mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb) exploit or antagonize Beclin 1-mediated autophagy to facilitate infection and evade host immune surveillance , , , , , , . Understanding these complex interactions is crucial for developing antiviral or antibacterial strategies that target Beclin 1 and its associated pathways to enhance innate immunity and restrict viral propagation. Detailed mechanisms of Beclin 1 pathogen interactions, including structural insights into direct binding partners and domain specific modulation, are further elaborated in section .
With appropriate modulation, Beclin 1 holds immense promise as a broad-spectrum therapeutic target, capable of transforming treatments across multiple disease domains as mentioned in ,B.

## Why knowing Beclin 1 pairing is important to unveil its PPI relationship
Mapping the precise interactome of Beclin 1 presents significant challenges. Developing effective drugs that target disease-causing proteins or modulate their interactions is a cornerstone of modern therapeutics . However, identifying optimal interacting protein pairs from millions of potential candidates remains a major bottleneck in drug discovery, as such modulation must minimize disruptions to key biological pathways .
The human proteome, with over 20,000 to several million proteins, generates millions of possible interaction combinations . On average, each protein interacts with 15 to 17 partners to perform its specific roles however, this number can vary significantly, as some proteins have over 100 interactions . Identifying the correct interacting pairs is critical for targeted therapies . Moreover, determining the affinity sites between two interacting partners requires detailed mining of 3D structural data . However experimental methods such as X-ray crystallography, nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy, and cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) each have inherent limitations when studying the structures of new targets and their interactors . Traditional wet lab methods are resource demanding, time consuming, and typically yield low success rates . These limitations underscore the unmet need to revolutionize the target identification phase in research and development, particularly the need to identify key interaction sites responsible for molecular recognition, which is essential for facilitating the development of therapeutic applications.
Understanding Beclin 1's interactions is especially complex due to several factors. First, the limited availability of crystallographic data restricts the exploration of its structural characteristics and interaction interfaces. Second, structural modelling tools like AlphaFold struggle to accurately capture the diverse conformational states involved in Beclin 1's interactions with both positive and negative regulators , . Third, Beclin 1's association with membranes adds further complexity to understanding its functional roles and dynamics , . Fourth, the presence of highly disordered regions and the absence of well-defined binding sites of its interactors make identifying interaction hotspots challenging . Lastly, less computational studies, such as molecular dynamics (MD) and enhanced sampling techniques limits understanding of Beclin 1's conformational sampling, which is crucial for investigating its dynamic behaviour and interaction mechanisms . Despite these challenges, targeting Beclin 1 to maintain cellular homeostasis under pathophysiological conditions by modulating autophagy has emerged as a promising therapeutic strategy due to its diverse roles across multiple disorders . Both academic and industrial research efforts are actively focused on this pathway, as dysregulation of Beclin 1 is implicated in various diseases due to its critical role in cellular homeostasis , . Companies like Ability Pharma, Biophagy, Caraway etc (detailed discussion below) are exploring autophagy to restore normal cellular functions under pathological conditions.

## Structural elucidation of the multiple domains of Beclin 1
The structural organisations of Beclin 1 encompass several functional domains (as demonstrated in ), each contributing to its diverse functions in autophagy regulation as they bind different set of interaction at same or different surfaces. Understanding the structural basis of Beclin 1 provides insights into its protein-protein interactions and potential sites for therapeutic intervention.
 Beclin 1 was first identified as a 450 amino acid long Bcl-2 interacting protein . The structural architecture of Beclin 1 reveals that it has three major functional domains (1) N-terminal intrinsic disorder (IDR) region (residue 1 to 150) this region contains multiple phosphorylation sites to control the biological activity of Beclin 1 , . (2) a long helical coiled-coil domain to interact with protein partners like Atg14L and UVRAG (residue 174 to 266) and (3) a beta-alpha repeated autophagy-specific domain (BARA) to maintain the lipid kinase activity by interacting with membranes (residue 266 to 450) . Aforesaid major functional domains can be further classified into three subdomains, residue 107 to 127 of IDR specifically known as Bcl-2 homology-3 (BH3) domain required for interactions with Bcl-2 and Bcl-XL proteins for the down-regulation of Beclin 1 mediated autophagy , , , , a flexible helical region lie in between IDR and CC domain (residues 141-171) coined as Flexible Helix Domain (FHD) due to its tendency to adopt helical conformations , and the highly conserved overlapping region of CC domain and BARA domain amongst homologs of Beclin 1 termed as Evolutionarily Conserved Domain (ECD) . The full-length structure of Beclin 1 is yet to be crystallised due to high conformational flexibility, although some cryo-EM reconstructions enlighten that complexes containing Beclin 1, VPS34, VPS15 and either Atg14 or UVRAG have a V-shaped structural arrangement and reveal the molecular structure of each protein within the complex , , though the N-terminal IDR of Beclin 1 still unanswered.
Beclin 1, exhibits a dynamic balance between structured and unstructured regions, contributing to its functional versatility. A defining feature of Beclin 1 is its association with cellular membranes, which is crucial for the formation and expansion of autophagosomes , . The FFW motif (F359, F360, W361), located in a flexible loop (residues 354-363) within the BARA domain plays a central role in membrane interaction, these residues interact with phospholipid bilayers via hydrophobic interactions and stabilize Beclin 1 at autophagy initiating membranes, such as the endoplasmic reticulum and mitochondria-associated membranes (MAMs) , . Mutations in these residues severely impair Beclin 1's membrane localization and autophagy, which highlights their functional importance in autophagy regulation . Additionally, there are many locations in this domain (BARA) that mediate interactions with numerous Beclin 1 regulatory proteins. For example, the Evolutionarily Conserved Domain (ECD) of Beclin 1 contains residues 267 to 284 that are essential for interaction with both PACER (positive regulator) and Rubicon (negative regulator) . The binding of these oppositely functioning regulators at the same site significantly influences Beclin 1's structural conformation, ensuring the correct assembly and function of the autophagy-initiation complex .
Beyond these regions, Beclin 1 contains an intrinsically disordered region (IDR) that accommodates several post-translational modification sites , . It also features a BH3 domain and a coiled-coil domain (as mentioned above) . The BH3 domain serves as a crucial convergence point between autophagy and apoptosis by interacting with Bcl-2 family proteins whereas the coiled-coil domain plays a significant role in mediating interactions with other regulators of Beclin 1 contributing to the formation of two distinct phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase complexes . Together, these structural features support the dynamic regulatory processes of Beclin 1, modulating its function and localization .
Furthermore, the surface topology of Beclin 1 particularly its BARA domain, exhibits multiple pockets that could be leveraged for drug discovery research. For instance, the GAPR-1 binding site (residues 267 to 284) and residues next to the aromatic finger motif undergo conformational changes in specific conditions that form deep clefts, those may be used as a promising druggable site , . Interestingly, Beclin 1's interaction with membranes through the FFW motif may cause structural alterations that reveal or restructure these grooves increasing their accessibility . Targeting these grooves or residues by virtual screening (VS) or peptidomimetics offers tremendous therapeutic potential.
4.1.

## Beclin 1 modulation via PPI
Beclin 1 modulates autophagy through protein-protein interactions (PPIs), which facilitate molecular recognition and communication processes at complex interface sites ( ). Modulating Beclin 1 via these PPIs holds significant therapeutic promise due to its role in various diseases. The Beclin 1-VPS34-VPS15 complex induces autophagy by recruiting essential components, recycling cellular elements, and ensuring membrane trafficking , . Beclin 1's interactions with anti-apoptotic proteins like Bcl-2 and Bcl-XL finely balance the crosstalk between autophagy and apoptosis , . Additional interactors such as Atg14L, UVRAG, and Ambra1 play crucial roles in autophagosome initiation, maturation, and localization (discussed below). Exploring these interactions provides insights into autophagy's role in cellular homeostasis and disease . However, the scarcity of high-resolution structural information about its interactors (both positive and negative) and the determinants responsible for its functional states presents major challenges. To address this, we summarize the structural and residue-level interactome of Beclin 1 to aid its characterization, enhance mechanistic understanding, and support therapeutic interventions to provide insights into autophagy's role in cellular homeostasis and disease.
 4.1.1.

## Domain specific positive regulators of Beclin 1

BH3 domain mediated interaction

Ambra1: Ambra1 (Activating Molecule in Beclin 1-Regulated Autophagy Protein 1) is a large protein made up of 1298 amino acids (UniProt id-Q9C0C7), with most of its structure being disordered (for structural information look ). It plays a key role in autophagy by binding to the BH3 domain of Beclin 1, which helps displace inhibitory proteins like Bcl-2 and Bcl-XL, thereby triggering autophagy , . This interaction not only enables Beclin 1 to relocate to the ER membrane an essential step for forming autophagosomes but also stabilizes its interaction with VPS34, ensuring the proper assembly of the autophagy initiation complex . Ambra1 also works as part of an E3 ubiquitin ligase complex with RBX1/CUL4, where it mediates K63-linked ubiquitination of Beclin 1, enhancing autophagy during times of starvation , . Beyond autophagy, Ambra1 plays a vital role in nervous system development . Studies in mice show that when Ambra1 is deficient, embryos develop severe neural tube defects. This deficiency impairs autophagy, causes an accumulation of ubiquitinated proteins, disrupts cell proliferation, and leads to excessive cell death . When Ambra1 levels drop, autophagy induced by rapamycin or nutrient deprivation is significantly reduced, while higher levels of Ambra1 enhance both basal and rapamycin-triggered autophagy. Recent findings have identified DLC1, a component of the dynein motor complex, as a negative regulator of Ambra1 through yeast two-hybrid assays. This regulation highlights Ambra1's pivotal role in ensuring Beclin 1's translocation to the ER during autophagy, further linking cytoskeletal dynamics to autophagosome formation . These discoveries shed light on the intricate mechanisms that connect Ambra1's functions to cellular homeostasis and development .
 
Coiled-coil domain (CCD) mediated interaction

UVRAG: UVRAG (UV radiation resistance-associated gene protein) is a 699 amino acid (UniProt id-Q9P2Y5) protein that positively regulates Beclin 1 by binding to its coiled-coil domain (CCD) (for structural information look ) . It plays a crucial role in autophagy by promoting autophagosome formation through Beclin 1 complex activation and supporting autophagosome maturation by recruiting fusion machinery to late endosomes in mammals , . UVRAG's direct interaction with Beclin 1, disrupts the Beclin 1 dimer interface to form a stable homodimer . However, some studies indicate that UVRAG overexpression does not affect Beclin 1 self-interaction . Additionally, the loss of Beclin 1 or VPS34 reduces UVRAG levels during starvation-induced autophagy. Interestingly, UVRAG and Atg5/Atg16L1 do not colocalize during heightened autophagy, nor do UVRAG punctae associate with LC3 under starvation conditions, suggesting UVRAG has non-autophagic roles. Instead, UVRAG predominantly resides in Rab9-positive late endosomes and partially associates with Rab5 and Rab7 , , . Structurally, the Beclin 1-UVRAG complex forms a parallel heterodimeric coiled-coil assembly , . This interaction is stabilized by “perfect” leucine zippers at the heterodimer interface, including five critical pairs: Leu178(Beclin 1)-Leu232(UVRAG), Leu185(Beclin 1)-Leu239(UVRAG), Leu192(Beclin 1)-Leu246(UVRAG), Leu196(Beclin 1)-Leu250(UVRAG) and Leu210(Beclin 1)-Leu264(UVRAG) , , also it is observed that Beclin 1 residue Arg203 forms a salt bridge with UVRAG residue Glu260, neutralizing destabilizing charges at the hydrophobic interface by retaining hydrophobic interactions and gain additional stabilizing features, the Beclin 1-UVRAG heterodimer exhibits significantly greater stability than the Beclin 1 homodimer .
 Atg14L: Atg14L, also known as Barkor , is a critical protein involved in autophagy, consisting of 492 amino acids (UniProt id-Q6ZNE5). It contains two coiled-coil domains (CCDs) essential for interacting with the CCD regions of Beclin 1. Atg14L is a human homologue of yeast Atg14 (18% sequence identity and 32% sequence similarity) . In mammals, Atg14L localizes to the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and plays a vital role in autophagosome formation . During autophagy, Atg14L colocalizes with Atg5 and Atg16L1 on isolation membranes. It forms distinct and mutually exclusive complexes with Beclin 1 and VPS34, with the Atg14L-containing complex primarily driving autophagosome nucleation and expansion . Beclin 1 is crucial for stabilizing Atg14L (or vies-versa) within this complex, and its loss, significantly reduces Atg14L levels, indicating potential non-autophagic roles or altered stability of these proteins in the absence of the complex , .
Due to the limited crystallographic studies (for structural information look ), the precise binding zone and mode of interaction between Atg14L and Beclin 1 remain unresolved. However, recent studies suggest that the metastable homodimeric coiled-coil domain (CCD) of Beclin 1 transitions into a stable heterodimer upon interaction with Atg14L, mediated by specific hydrophobic residues on Beclin 1, including Leu184, Leu194, Val208, Leu212, Leu222, Tyr233, Phe236, and Val250, and corresponding residues on Atg14L, such as Leu109, Ile120, Leu123, Ile127, Leu151, and Ile165 . Mutations in these residues impair the interaction and disrupt autophagy . Additionally, some charged residues of Beclin 1 (Glu189, Arg203, and Glu224) also are critical for Beclin 1-Atg14L interaction mutating these charged residues to hydrophobic leucine significantly reduces or abolishes the interaction .
 Both Atg14L and UVRAG possess CCDs that facilitate oligomeric assemblies with Beclin 1, enhancing complex stability and function . However, despite its critical role, the crystal structure of the Beclin 1-Atg14L complex remains unavailable, emphasizing the need for further structural studies.
HMGB1: The High Mobility Group Box 1 (HMGB1) protein is involved in nuclear functions and extracellular signalling, with critical roles in autophagy regulation , , . Structurally ( ), it consists of 215 amino acids (UniProt id-P09429) and is divided into three domains: the A-box (9-79 aa), B-box (89-162 aa), and the acidic C-terminus (186-215 aa) . The A-box and B-box are positively charged DNA-binding domains, while the acidic C-terminal tail is negatively charged, mediating HMGB1-DNA interactions and regulating chromosomal dynamics , , . Functionally, the B-box drives inflammatory responses, whereas the A-box counteracts this effect , . Additionally, the N-terminus (6-12 aa) is crucial for heparin binding, which modulates HMGB1's receptor affinity and reduces its proinflammatory activity , . It has three cysteine residues at positions 23, 45 (A-box), and 106 (B-box), which play pivotal roles in its function and localization . The Cys23/Cys45 disulfide bond is essential for Beclin 1 binding and autophagy induction, while oxidation of Cys106 is critical for cytoplasmic translocation and sustaining autophagy , , . Mutational studies reveal that C106S mutants enhance cytoplasmic HMGB1 levels and Beclin 1 binding, promoting autophagy on the other hand, C23S and C45S mutants fail to bind Beclin 1 or disrupt its interaction with Bcl-2, thereby inhibiting autophagy . Under cellular stress, HMGB1 translocates from the nucleus to the cytoplasm in a redox-dependent manner, playing a vital role in autophagic induction during nutrient starvation or rapamycin treatment, this translocation facilitates ERK1/2-mediated Bcl-2 phosphorylation, causing the dissociation of the Beclin 1-Bcl-2 complex , . HMGB1-deficient cells exhibit reduced autophagic flux, evidenced by decreased LC3 puncta and LC3-II levels, along with increased apoptosis . HMGB1's role extends to its interaction with receptors like RAGE, where reducible HMGB1 promotes autophagy and enhances tumour cell resistance to chemotherapy . In contrast, oxidized HMGB1 induces apoptosis through mitochondrial pathways, highlighting its dual role in regulating cell survival and death . Elevated HMGB1 levels in cancer correlate with enhanced survival of malignant cells under metabolic stress and oxidative injury . Furthermore, HMGB1 links autophagy with inflammation, apoptosis, and necrosis . Its cytoplasmic localization during autophagy drives protective responses, while its release during necrosis serves as a DAMP signal, activating immune responses , . This intricate regulation highlights HMGB1's importance in cellular homeostasis, stress response, and disease, making it a key therapeutic target. Bif-1: Bif-1, also known as Endophilin B1 or SH3GLB1, is a 365-amino acid ( ) cytosolic protein that belongs to the Endophilin B family (UniProt id-Q9Y371) . Structurally, it comprises an N-terminal N-BAR (Bin-Amphiphysin-Rvs) domain, which binds membranes and induces curvature, and a C-terminal SH3 (Src-homology 3) domain that mediates protein-protein interactions . Unlike the well-characterized Endophilin A family, which plays roles in endosome formation, the Endophilin B family, including Bif-1, is less understood . Bif-1 was initially identified as a Bax-binding protein and is implicated in membrane dynamics and vesicle formation, localizing to intracellular organelles such as the Golgi apparatus and mitochondria , , , . Functionally, Bif-1 plays a pivotal role in autophagy. It interacts with Beclin 1 through UVRAG to activate the PI3KC3 lipid kinase complex, promoting autophagosome biogenesis , , . Loss of Bif-1 significantly suppresses PI3KC3 activation, reducing autophagosome formation, as evidenced by lower LC3-II levels and diminished GFP-LC3 puncta , , . Furthermore, Bif-1-deficient cells show impaired early-stage autophagosome formation and spontaneous tumorigenesis, highlighting its role as a tumour suppressor . Immunofluorescence studies reveal that nutrient deprivation drives Bif-1 from the cytosol to punctate structures alongside Atg5 and LC3, suggesting involvement in phagophore elongation , . The SH3 domain of Bif-1 is essential for UVRAG binding, bridging interactions with Beclin 1, reinforcing its role in autophagy regulation , , .
 nPIST: nPIST (neuronal isoform of PDZ protein interacting specifically with TC10) is a multifunctional protein originally identified through yeast two-hybrid studies as a binding partner of the glutamate receptor D2 (GluD2) and the autophagy regulating protein of Beclin 1 , . It plays a critical role in linking membrane receptors, such as GluD2, to autophagy pathways . nPIST binds GluD2 via its C-terminal PDZ domain and associates with Beclin 1 through its N-terminal coiled-coil domain (CCD), forming a physical linkage between GluD2 and Beclin 1 . This interaction enables nPIST to regulate Beclin 1's autophagy-inducing ability . The full-length nPIST protein, comprising 463 amino acids (UniProt id-Q9HD26), features an N-terminal coiled-coil region and a C-terminal PDZ domain . As there is no fully solved crystal information available till now ( ) some structural modelling studies have revealed that the coiled-coil domain is highly helical, while the PDZ domain is more disordered, providing flexibility to the protein a WH2 domain, located within the coiled-coil region (residues 230-250), was identified based on sequence conservation and structural alignment and it has a tendency to fold exactly same as seen in various structures co-crystallized with G-actin , . Also, some docking studies showed that this WH2 domain binds to G-actin in a manner similar to VopL-actin complexes, with residues such as Trp238, Gln240, Leu241, and Ile245 identified as critical binding hotspots , . These findings suggest nPIST may influence both cytoskeletal dynamics and Beclin 1-mediated autophagy, offering new insights into autophagy regulation.
 RUBCNL/PACER: RUBCNL/PACER (RUN and cysteine-rich domain-containing Beclin 1 interacting protein-like) is a 662 amino acid long protein (UniProt id-Q9H714) belonging to the RUBCN/Rubicon family . It is characterized by a conserved RUBCN homolog (RH) domain and is pivotal in regulating autophagy , , . Despite its importance, no crystal structure of PACER is currently available (For structural information look ). Predictions from AlphaFold (AF-Q9H714-F1) suggest that PACER predominantly comprises disordered regions , . PACER plays a central role in autophagosome maturation, autolysosome formation, and cellular homeostasis , . PACER is directly phosphorylated by the mTOR complex 1 (mTORC1) at Ser157 under nutrient-rich conditions, which inhibits autophagosome maturation, and dephosphorylation of PACER promotes autolysosome formation. This phosphorylation-acetylation switch is a critical mechanism regulating PACER's autophagic functions , . It interacts directly with Beclin 1, via Beclin 1 coiled-coil domain (CCD) . PACER also associates with UVRAG, VPS34, and p150, forming a critical part of the UVRAG-Beclin 1-PI3KC3 subcomplex, while showing no interaction with Atg14, hNRBF2, or Rubicon . PACER utilizes a conserved 196-235 amino acid motif to stabilize the UVRAG-Beclin 1 complex . Under autophagic stress, PACER's interaction with the PI3KC3 complex is enhanced, displacing Rubicon and promoting PI3KC3 activity. This displacement increases phosphatidylinositol 3-phosphate (PtdIns3P) levels, essential for autophagosome biogenesis and maturation , . PACER's unique structural properties and its role in autophagy regulation highlight its importance in maintaining cellular homeostasis , , . PACER is a promising therapeutic target for diseases associated with autophagy dysregulation, including liver diseases, neurodegeneration, and inflammatory disorders. Its deficiency is linked to neurodegenerative conditions like amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), while its overexpression enhances autophagic responses in certain inflammatory contexts, studies in ALS mouse models have shown that PACER overexpression can exacerbate neuroinflammation and accelerate disease progression, suggesting a need for cautious, context specific modulation of this regulator .
 
Evolutionary conserved domain (ECD)

VPS34: VPS34 (UniProt id-Q8NEB9), initially identified in yeast as part of the PI3K complex, is essential for intracellular protein trafficking and autophagy through its phosphorylation of PtdIns to PtdIns3P , , . Human VPS34 comprises an N-terminal C2 domain (residues 1-255) for membrane binding, a middle helical domain (293-530), and a C-terminal catalytic kinase domain (533-887) , , , . The C2 domain facilitates interactions with membranes and binds Beclin 1 , . Beclin 1 interacts with VPS34 through its residues 244-337 (for structural information look ), critical for autophagy, and this interaction stabilizes the PI3K complex . A structural study on Drosophila melanogaster VPS34 reveals its HELCAT domain, including an activation loop (key residues Lys833, Pro832, Tyr826; human homologs Lys771, Pro770, Tyr764) for recognizing PtdIns, a P-loop for ATP binding, and a catalytic loop containing the conserved DRH motif (D805-R806-H807 in drosophila; D743-R744-H745 in humans) for phosphoryl transfer . Mutations in these residues, including K771A in the activation loop and D743A or H745A in the catalytic loop, drastically reduce activity . The C-terminal helix (residues His879-Trp885) also influences activity, with mutations like H879A or W885A impairing ATPase function in the presence of vesicles while increasing basal activity in their absence . The helix regulates auto-inhibition and activation depending on membrane association. VPS34's ATP binding pocket, constrained by an inward-curling P-loop and aromatic residues Phe612 (P-loop) and Phe684 (hinge region), restricts binding of typical PI3K inhibitors , .
 VPS15: Human VPS15 (UniProt id-Q99570) is a multi-domain protein integral to phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase complex III (PI3KC3) functionality . As per previous articles, Beclin 1 ECD recruits the VPS34 subunit and Beclin 1 binding to it and with regulatory subunit VPS15/PIK3R4 forms a complex that is required to generate a lipid phosphatidylinositol 3 phosphate (PtdIns3P) leading to the formation of a membrane anchoring complex . Protein VPS15 basically comprises an N-terminal serine/threonine kinase domain (residues 11-262), a C-terminal WD-40 domain (residues 1000-1300), and an intermediate HEAT repeat-containing domain (residues 400-700) linking the kinase and WD-40 domains ( ) , , . VPS15 is myristoylated at its N-terminal sequence (residues 1-6), which anchors it to target membranes, including endosomal and autophagosomal membranes , . This membrane association facilitates VPS34 translocation and activation for PI3P synthesis, essential for autophagy and endosomal biogenesis . The WD-40 domain functions as a scaffold, binding Rab5 to direct VPS34-VPS15 to early endosomes and interacting with Rab7 and PtdIns3P phosphatases (MTM1/MTM2) on late endosomes , , . Notably, the N-myristate is sequestered in the N-lobe of the kinase domain, stabilizing interactions that repress VPS34's catalytic activity. GTP binding to VPS15's unique R103 gatekeeper residue structurally reinforces this inhibition by reorienting key residues like D166 in the DFG motif . This configuration aligns GTP's three phosphates with Mg2+, deviating from active kinases where phosphotransfer occurs , . The myristoyl switch mechanism in VPS15 contrasts ARF GTPases, where GTP stabilizes myristate burial, suppressing membrane binding , , . Such insights elucidate VPS15's multifaceted regulatory mechanisms, advancing understanding of autophagy modulation and offering potential therapeutic targets for pharmacological activation.
 
Other positive regulators:

Vacuole membrane protein 1 (VMP1), a 406-amino-acid-long ER transmembrane protein (UniProt id-Q96GC9) without a resolved crystal structure, is a vital autophagy regulator and a marker of autophagosomal membranes. Its hydrophilic C-terminal Atg domain is indispensable for interacting with Beclin 1's BH3 domain and inducing autophagy, as mutations in this region abolish LC3 recruitment and disrupt Beclin 1 interactions , , . VMP1 is upregulated by starvation and rapamycin, with its overexpression promoting LC3-I to LC3-II conversion and autophagosome formation , . Tumour protein 53-induced nuclear protein 2 (TP53INP2) further enhances autophagy by translocating to autophagosomal structures and interacting with VMP1 and LC3, though the role of Beclin 1 in mediating these interactions remains unclear . Similarly, SLAM (SLAMF1 or CD150), a 335-amino-acid microbial sensor (UniProt id-Q13291), interacts with Beclin 1 to facilitate immune defence by driving autophagy and phagosome-mediated bacterial killing, with SLAM deficiency impairing NOX2 complex activity . PTEN-induced putative kinase 1 (PINK1) (UniProt id-Q9BXM7), a 581-amino-acid mitochondrial serine/threonine kinase, protects cells from mitochondrial stress by regulating mitophagy . Its full-length structure interacts with Beclin 1 to enhance autophagy, it also observed that an N (residue 1 to 111) and C (residue 497 to 581) terminal truncated PINK1 protein lost its interaction with Beclin 1 which indicates it might be interacting with Beclin 1 through its both terminal . Lastly, Survivin (BIRC5), a 142-amino-acid anti-apoptotic protein (UniProt id-O15392), modulates the interplay between apoptosis and autophagy through Beclin 1-mediated degradation, influencing glioma cell sensitivity to TRAIL-induced apoptosis . These proteins collectively underscore the central role of Beclin 1 in autophagy regulation and its intricate crosstalk with apoptosis and immune responses .

## Domain specific negative regulators of Beclin 1

BH3 domain mediated interaction

Bcl-2 and Bcl-XL: Beclin 1 interacts with anti-apoptotic proteins of the Bcl-2 family, including Bcl-2 and Bcl-XL . This interaction serves as a key convergence point between autophagy and apoptosis, two fundamental cellular processes (for structural information look ). Beclin 1 binds to Bcl-2 and Bcl-XL through its BH3 domain, forming an amphipathic helix that occupies the conserved hydrophobic groove of these proteins , , . Unlike pro-apoptotic BH3-only proteins, Beclin 1 has a lower binding affinity for Bcl-2, which may explain its limited influence on apoptosis . Beclin 1 and Bcl-2 interaction limiting Beclin 1's availability to induce autophagy by forming complexes such as Class III PI3K complexes C1 and C2 . This interaction downregulates autophagy and plays a role in maintaining cellular homeostasis . Various other factors, including competitive binding by other proteins and post-translational modifications, modulate this interaction, thereby influencing autophagy levels. Proteins such as VMP-1, ARF, and AMBRA can displace Beclin 1 from Bcl-2 to promote autophagy , , . On the other hand, proteins like NAF-1, IP3R, and RTN3 enhance the interaction between Beclin 1 and Bcl-2 and suppressing autophagy , , . This dynamic interplay between Beclin 1 and Bcl-2 affects processes like cardiac health, neurodegeneration, and cancer, disruption of this interaction has shown promising results in reducing amyloid accumulation, tumorigenesis, and cardiac pathologies , . So targeting the Beclin 1-Bcl-2 interaction site may offer potential therapeutic avenues for conditions such as cancer and cardiac dysfunction, emphasizing the need for precise modulation based on disease context.
 Mcl-1: Induced myeloid leukemia cell differentiation protein Mcl-1 a 350 amino acid long (uniprot id-Q07820) anti-apoptotic Bcl-2 family homolog protein ( ), known to balance between autophagy and apoptosis during cellular stress . Mcl-1 regulate apoptosis through its interaction with pro-apoptotic proteins like BAX while also modulating autophagy via Beclin 1 . Localized to mitochondria, Mcl-1 interacts with Beclin 1 through its BH3 domain (residue 209 to 223) to inhibit autophagosome formation this interaction plays a crucial role in maintaining cellular homeostasis , . Deletion or degradation of Mcl-1, triggered by stressors like nutrient deprivation, activates autophagy in post-mitotic neurons. Conversely, stress-induced degradation of Mcl-1 relieves its inhibitory effect on both apoptosis and autophagy, with additional signalling pathways determining the predominance of one pathway over the other , . Mcl-1 degradation is mediated by ubiquitin ligases like Mule and β-TRCP, often in response to nutrient starvation or mTOR inhibition , . This positions Mcl-1 as an upstream regulator sensitive to cellular stress, coordinating cell survival or death based on the context. Notably, its mitochondrial localization contrasts with Bcl-2's association with Beclin 1 at the ER, highlighting distinct regulatory mechanisms for autophagosome formation across organelles , . Given their roles in stress responses, Beclin 1 and Mcl-1 are critical in pathological conditions such as cancer and neurodegenerative diseases.
 
Coiled-coil domain (CCD)

While the coiled-coil domain (CCD) of Beclin 1 is classically known for mediating interactions with positive regulators such as UVRAG and Atg14L, it also serves as a regulatory hub targeted by negative modulators via post-translational modifications. For instance, proteins like EGFR and AKT inhibit Beclin 1-mediated autophagy through phosphorylation events that involve the CCD. These inhibitory interactions are discussed in detail in the post translational modifications section (Post-Translational Modifications of Beclin 1: Multiple Strategies for Autophagy Regulation) , , , .

Rubicon: Rubicon (RUN domain protein as Beclin 1-interacting and cysteine-rich containing) is a novel negative regulator of autophagy (uniprot id-Q92622) that directly binds Beclin 1 (Residues 267-284) , , , . It is a 972 amino acid protein ( ) with a molecular mass of 108,622 Da . Structurally, the Rubicon consists of an N-terminal RUN domain (residues 48-189), a serine-rich region (residues 204-447), a coiled-coil domain (residues 509-551), and a cysteine-rich domain (residues 881-932) at the C-terminus . In some previous studies it was mentioned that Rubicon interacts with Beclin 1 through its RUN domain but later on, it is identified that residue 471-672 of Rubicon responsible for Beclin 1 binding , . Rubicon localizes to late endosomes and lysosomes but not autophagosomes and inhibits autophagy by preventing autophagosome maturation , . Overexpression of Rubicon reduces PI3KC3/VPS34 activity, impairs the acidification of LC3-associated vacuoles, and leads to the accumulation of abnormal endosomal structures positive for lysosomal markers like lysosomal-associated membrane protein 1 (LAMP-1) and multivesicular body markers such as lysobisphosphatidic acid . Immunoprecipitation studies demonstrate that Rubicon forms a distinct Beclin 1 complex with UVRAG, VPS34, and VPS15, but not Atg14L it is also observed that Rubicon becomes highly unstable in UVRAG-knockdown cells, which highlights UVRAG's role in stabilizing the Rubicon complex . Functionally, Rubicon inhibits autophagy by blocking the membrane targeting ability of Beclin 1's BARA domain, which is critical for PI3KC3 activity . Knockdown of Rubicon enhances autophagosome formation, even under nutrient-rich conditions, and facilitates autophagosome maturation and endocytosis , . Based on ongoing studies we can say that Rubicon is a potent negative regulator of autophagy, directly interacting with Beclin 1. Future research should focus on clarifying its precise mechanisms, particularly its role in endocytic trafficking and the interplay with other autophagy regulators, to uncover potential therapeutic strategies targeting autophagy.
 GAPR-1: Golgi-associated plant pathogenesis-related protein 1 (GAPR-1), also known as GLIPR2, has been identified as a negative regulator of human Beclin 1 . GAPR-1 belongs to the Cysteine-rich secretory proteins, Antigen 5, and Pathogenesis-related (CAP) protein superfamily . It is a small peripheral membrane protein with 154 amino acids (UniProt id-Q9H4G4) localized to lipid-rich microdomains of the Golgi apparatus (for structural information look ) . The binding of GAPR-1 to Beclin 1 retains it at the cytosolic leaflet of the Golgi complex, thereby downregulating autophagy . GAPR-1's role as a negative regulator of autophagy is primarily due to its interaction with Beclin 1, researchers confirmed a direct interaction between GAPR-1 and Beclin 1, pinpointing the interaction site to the amino acids 267-284 of Beclin 1 and a five-amino-acid region (His54, Glu86, Gly102, His 103, Asn138) on GAPR-1 , . Disruption of the Beclin 1 and GAPR-1 interaction using a GAPR-1 interaction site of Beclin 1 derived peptide (residues 267-284, Tat-Beclin 1) relocates Beclin 1 from the Golgi to the cytosol and activates autophagy . Crosslink studies indicate that GAPR-1 has a strong propensity to form homodimers in vivo and in vitro, with stimuli such as negatively charged lipids, IP6, heparin, and metal ions promoting oligomerization and amyloid-like fibril formation , . Some studies also indicate that GAPR-1 and Beclin 1 may have amyloidogenic properties, showing that these proteins form protein condensates under certain conditions without leading to toxicity . This contrasts with pathological amyloid proteins that typically induce cell damage . Moreover, these findings indicate that amyloidogenic oligomeric interactions between GAPR-1 and Beclin 1 have a functional role in autophagy, emphasizing the physiological significance of their amyloidogenic behaviour . These insights are crucial for identifying potential therapeutic targets and strategies to modulate autophagy in various diseases, including viral infections where Beclin 1 plays a pivotal role.
 GRB-2: Recent studies have identified Growth Factor Receptor-Bound Protein 2 (GRB-2), a 217 amino acid long adaptor protein ( ) primarily associated with HER2 and other tyrosine kinases (UniProt id-P62993), as a novel interactor of Beclin 1 , , . Studies identify that GRB-2 co-immunoprecipitated with Beclin 1 in multiple breast cancer cell lines and was shown to regulate autophagy by modulating the activity of the Class III PI3K complex . Researchers also revealed that GRB-2 knockdown impaired tumour growth, this suppression of tumorigenesis correlated with altered autophagic activity, suggesting a mechanistic link between GRB-2-mediated autophagy regulation and cancer progression , . Further studies also demonstrated that GRB-2 depletion in HeLa cells significantly increased autophagic flux by reducing the levels of the autophagic substrate p62, increased LC3 lipidation, and enhanced autophagosome formation under both basal and stress conditions . A protein-protein docking study followed by mutational analysis revealed that key residues in GRB-2, including Tyr52, Arg86, Glu87, Ser88 and Tyr209 are crucial for its interaction with Beclin 1 and its inhibitory effect on autophagy , , . These findings underscore GRB-2's dual role in autophagy and cancer, where it acts as a modulator of VPS34 and impacts tumorigenesis, identification of GRB-2 as a Beclin 1-binding protein opens new avenues for understanding autophagy regulation and its implications in cancer therapy .
 NLRP4: NLRP4 is a 994 amino acid long protein (UniProt id-Q96MN2), a member of the NOD-like receptor (NLR) family, that negatively regulates autophagy by interacting with Beclin 1 (for structural information look ). The NACHT domain (Residue 149-472) of NLRP4 physically binds the ECD of Beclin 1, inhibiting autophagic initiation , . During bacterial infections, NLRP4 plays a significant role in modulating autophagy . Knockdown of NLRP4 increases the formation of GcAVs (large GAS-containing autophagosomes) linked to Rab7 activation, highlighting its role in the elimination of invasive GAS bacteria . Furthermore, NLRP4 accumulates on bacteria containing phagosomes early in infection, aligning its dynamics with bacterial recognition pathways . Other NLRs, such as NOD1 and NOD2, also induce autophagy via Atg16L1 recruitment to bacterial phagosomes, linking pathogen sensing to autophagy , . This interplay underscores NLRP4's dual role in regulating autophagy and inflammasome activation, as it associates with Beclin 1-mediated pathways and potentially influences inflammatory responses , , .
 
Other negative regulators

IP3R: Beclin 1, interacts intricately with the inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate receptor (IP3R), a 2758-amino-acid-long membrane glycoprotein and calcium channel (UniProt id-Q14643), plays dual roles in apoptosis and autophagy, revealing a complex regulatory mechanism at the interface of calcium signalling and autophagy initiation . IP3R, located on the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), modulates Ca2+ release into the cytosol, influencing various cellular processes. Interestingly, IP3R also interacts with Beclin 1 through an indirect link mediated by Bcl-2, forming a regulatory triad , , . Studies have shown that IP3R act as an agonists to inhibit autophagy, whereas antagonists, like xestospongin B, induce it , . Xestospongin B disrupts the Beclin 1-IP3R interaction, a process accentuated during nutrient starvation-induced autophagy, emphasizing IP3R's role as an autophagy inhibitor , .

## Post-translational modifications of Beclin 1: multiple strategies for autophagy regulation
Beclin 1 activity is intricately regulated by PTMs, including phosphorylation, ubiquitination, and acetylation, which modulate its stability, localization, and interaction with binding partners. Deciphering the role of PTMs in Beclin 1 regulation offers a nuanced understanding of autophagy dynamics and potential therapeutic targets.
Phosphorylation: Phosphorylation stands as the most explored post-translational modification impacting autophagy, entailing the reversible attachment of phosphate groups onto serine, threonine, or tyrosine residues within substrate proteins . Such alterations in protein conformation wield the potential to influence accessibility for interactions and enzymatic activities, with several pathways and proteins being central to this regulation. One primary pathway involves the mTORC1 and ULK1 complex. When amino acids are depleted, mTORC1 gets inactivated, which removes its inhibitory effect on the ULK1 complex , . This allows ULK1 to phosphorylate Beclin 1 on Ser15, activating the Atg14L-containing VPS34 complexes necessary for autophagy initiation. Atg14L serves as an adaptor, recruiting ULK1 to Beclin 1 at the phagophore by binding both Beclin 1 and Atg13. If Atg14L fails to localize to the phagophore, ULK1 cannot phosphorylate Beclin 1 effectively, hindering autophagy , . AMBRA1 is another protein that gets phosphorylated by ULK1, which is essential for recruiting Beclin 1 to the phagophore. Additionally, during glucose depletion, AMPK phosphorylates ULK1, stimulating autophagy. AMPK also directly phosphorylates Beclin 1 on Ser90 and Ser93, affecting PI3-kinase activity. This dual regulation by AMPK involves inhibitory phosphorylation on VPS34 unless Atg14L is present, which then stimulates Beclin 1 activation , .
Several other phosphorylation sites on Beclin 1 play significant roles under different conditions. For instance, Ser90 is phosphorylated by various kinases such as MAPKAPK2/3, DAPK3, and CamKII under amino acid and serum starvation, and ionomycin treatment , . This phosphorylation competes with Bcl-2 binding, a crucial step for autophagy activation. Similarly, Ser30 is phosphorylated by PGK1 during glutamine deprivation and hypoxia in cancer cells, enhancing PI3-kinase activity. Ser295 and Ser234 are phosphorylated by AKT in nutrient-rich conditions, which sequesters Beclin 1 to inhibit autophagy , . Phosphorylation at Thr119 by DAPK1 or ROCK1 prevents Beclin 1 from interacting with Bcl-2, facilitating autophagy initiation. Conversely, phosphorylation of Beclin 1 at Thr108 by MST1 in cardiomyocytes stabilizes its interaction with Bcl-2, inhibiting autophagy and potentially inducing apoptosis . Growth factor receptors like EGFR and HER2 can phosphorylate Beclin 1, leading to autophagy suppression by increasing its interaction with negative regulators like RUBICON and Bcl-2. Tyrosine phosphorylation sites such as Tyr229, Tyr233, and Tyr352 are pivotal in modulating Beclin 1's interaction with these regulators , , . Stress responses also influence autophagy through phosphorylation. Kinases like JNK and p38MAPK phosphorylate Bcl-2, causing it to dissociate from Beclin 1 and promoting autophagy. Additionally, AMPK phosphorylates Beclin 1 at Thr388 during glucose deprivation, reducing its complex formation with Bcl-2, which enhances autophagy .
Phosphorylation is a key regulatory mechanism in autophagy, influencing various stages from initiation to maturation. The interaction between different kinases and phosphorylation sites on Beclin 1 orchestrates the autophagic response to various cellular conditions and stresses.
Ubiquitination and deubiquitination: Ubiquitination is a process that involves the attachment of ubiquitin molecules to a protein, influencing various cellular functions beyond merely marking the protein for degradation . This modification can alter a protein's localization, composition, or activity within multiprotein complexes , , . Beclin 1, a protein involved in autophagy, undergoes ubiquitination through the actions of E1, E2, and E3 enzymes . NEDD4 is an E3 ubiquitin ligase that attaches Lys11 and Lys63 linked polyubiquitination chain to Beclin 1 at BARA domain (residues 349-352), leading to Beclin 1's degradation . Interestingly, c-FLIP, a scaffold protein without enzymatic activity, can bind to Beclin 1 and block the action of NEDD4, thereby preventing Beclin 1 degradation . Another E3 ligase, Cul3, targets Beclin 1 for degradation via Lys48 linked ubiquitination at residues Lys53, Lys185, and Lys270, which inhibits autophagy . Conversely, TRIM50 ubiquitinates Beclin 1 by Lys63 linked ubiquitination, promoting the formation of the PI3KC3 complex and enhancing autophagy , . TRAF6, another E3 ligase, also attaches lys63 linked ubiquitination at residues Lys117, playing a vital role in TLR4-induced autophagy induction . However, Ala20, a deubiquitinating enzyme, can remove ubiquitin from Beclin 1, thus inhibiting autophagy . The phosphorylation of Beclin 1 by CAMKII at Ser90 increases TRAF6-mediated ubiquitination, which activates autophagy in specific cell types . NEK2 stabilizes Beclin 1 via its interaction with USP7, facilitating autophagy . Furthermore, Beclin 1 interacts with USP11, which stabilizes Beclin 1, increasing its expression and activating autophagy . USP15, another deubiquitinating enzyme, regulates the TRAF6 and Beclin 1 interaction by removing ubiquitin, thereby reducing autophagy and limiting lung cancer progression . Ambra1 and TRIM50 also act as E3 ligases that induce Lys63-linked ubiquitination of Beclin 1, promoting autophagy under conditions like nutrient deprivation. WASH protein competes with ubiquitination processes, binding to Beclin 1 and inhibiting its ubiquitination, which suppresses autophagy. Solute carrier family proteins can block the ubiquitin-dependent degradation of Beclin 1, stimulating autophagy as a result it helps in inhibiting breast cancer cell proliferation . Lysine-specific demethylase 2A has been shown to promote Beclin 1 degradation through ubiquitination, thereby inhibiting autophagy . On the other hand, deubiquitinating enzymes like USP14, USP9X, USP10, and USP13 can remove ubiquitin from Beclin 1, regulating autophagy. For example, USP14 negatively controls Lys63-linked ubiquitination of Beclin 1, thus modulating autophagy. Knockdown of USP14 in certain cancer cells increases cell migration and invasion, suggesting its role in inhibiting cancer progression by maintaining Beclin 1 ubiquitination , . USP19 deubiquitinates Beclin 1 at Lys437, reverses K11-linked ubiquitination, which serves as a signal for Beclin 1 degradation. Overexpression of USP19 promotes autophagic flux, highlighting its role as a guardian for Beclin 1 stabilization and autophagosome turnover . USP24 interacts endogenously with Beclin 1 and delays its degradation by reducing K48-linked ubiquitination. Overexpression of USP24 suppresses hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) proliferation, an effect partially reversed by Beclin 1 silencing . USP5 activation, mediated by Kras, deubiquitinates Beclin 1, stabilizing it and promoting autophagy, which contributes to lung tumour growth. Overall, the balance between ubiquitination and deubiquitination of Beclin 1 is crucial in regulating autophagy .
Other PTMs: Similar to ubiquitylation, ISGylation is a reversible process where the ubiquitin-like modifier ISG15 (interferon-stimulated gene 15 protein) is conjugated to a substrate protein. ISG15 expression, induced by Type I interferons, facilitates the ISGylation of Beclin 1 at specific lysine residues (Lys117, Lys263, Lys265, and Lys266) , . This modification competes with Beclin 1's Lys63-linked polyubiquitination, resulting in the inhibition of PI3KC3 activity and autophagy flux . HERC5, an E3 ligase, interacts with Beclin 1 to catalyze its ISGylation, while USP18 removes ISG15 from Beclin 1, thereby positively regulating autophagy . These mechanisms reveal a novel link between immunity and autophagy through ISGylation and deISGylation of Beclin 1.
SUMOylation, another post-translational modification, involves attaching a SUMO (small ubiquitin-like modifier) to a lysine residue on target proteins, influencing various pathways. PIAS3, a SUMO E3 ligase, enhances SUMO3 conjugation at Lys380 on Beclin 1 during cellular starvation, promoting autophagy by facilitating PI3KC3 complex formation. Conversely, SENP3-mediated deSUMOylation impairs PI3KC3 complex formation, inhibiting autophagy in various carcinoma cell lines , , .
Lysine acetylation, a reversible process, alters protein structure and function, affecting numerous cellular processes. Beclin 1 is acetylated by PCAF at Lys430 and Lys437, promoting its interaction with Rubicon, which inhibits autophagosome maturation and autophagy , . SIRT1 deacetylates Beclin 1, reducing its interaction with Rubicon and enhancing autophagosome maturation. Mutations at these acetylation sites in MCF7 xenografts result in increased autophagosome maturation .
Beclin 1 is also regulated by proteolytic cleavage mediated by caspase and calpain, linking autophagy and apoptosis. Caspase cleavage of Beclin 1 produces fragments that enhance apoptosis, while ABHD5 competes with Caspase-3 to prevent Beclin 1 cleavage. Calpain cleavage of Beclin 1 at a specific site inhibits autophagy, and such cleavage has been observed in various cellular contexts, including oxidative stress and retinal ischemic injury , .
Collectively, these post-translational modifications have intricate regulatory mechanisms for Beclin 1, influencing autophagy. Further research is needed to elucidate how these modifications interact and affect Beclin 1's role in different cellular and pathological contexts.

## Pathogen-derived Beclin 1 interaction partners
Due to Beclin 1's importance in the autophagy pathway, it is frequently targeted by pathogens that exploit or inhibit the host's autophagic machinery for their survival and replication. Numerous viral and bacterial pathogens have evolved mechanisms to interact with Beclin 1, manipulating autophagy to suit their needs.
 Among viruses, γherpesvirus-derived vBcl-2 proteins, such as those from Kaposi's sarcoma-associated herpesvirus (KSHV) and murine γHV68 (M11), directly bind to Beclin 1's BH3 domain and suppress autophagy . Studies showed that the M11 protein of γHV68 exhibits a significantly higher affinity for Beclin 1 compared to other vBcl-2 homologs, resulting in stronger inhibition of autophagy , , . Similarly, the herpes simplex virus (HSV) protein ICP34.5 interacts with Beclin 1 to block PKR-dependent macro-autophagy induction, a mechanism that limits neurovirulence in HSV-1 infections . Epstein-Barr virus also down-regulates autophagy through its vBcl-2 homolog protein BHRF1 in a non-canonical manner . In the case of HIV-1 and influenza A virus, the Nef and M2 proteins, respectively, interfere with autophagosome maturation by directly interacting with Beclin 1 , . Hepatitis C virus (HCV) also modulates Beclin 1-mediated autophagy, its nonstructural protein NS5A induces autophagy by enhancing the promoter activity of Beclin 1, leading to increased expression of the protein. Other HCV proteins have also been implicated in autophagy induction , , . Rabies virus uses Beclin 1 to induce incomplete autophagy through pathways involving CASP2, AMPK, AKT/mTOR, and MAPK signalling, enabling the virus to evade cellular degradation mechanisms , . Flaviviruses like Zika and Dengue viruses exploit a unique mechanism involving Aedes aegypti's saliva-specific protein AaVA-1, which binds to LRPPRC to release Beclin 1, thereby promoting autophagy and aiding viral replication . Hepatitis B virus (HBV) has been shown to induce autophagy through its X protein (HBx), which transactivates the Beclin 1 promoter and dissociates the Beclin 1-Bcl-2 complex via DAPK activation . ROS and JNK signalling further mediate this interaction, emphasizing Beclin 1's centrality in HBV-induced autophagy , . The viral protein TRS1 of Human Cytomegalovirus (HCMV) serves as a negative regulator of autophagy, functioning independently of its established role in modulating the kinase PKR, potentially through its interaction with the autophagy-associated protein Beclin 1 , .
Pathogenic bacteria also manipulate Beclin 1 to exploit autophagy. Coxiella burnetii, the causative agent of Q fever, survives within acidic phagolysosomes by subverting the Beclin 1 and Bcl-2 interaction, preventing apoptosis and establishing infection , . Similarly, Acinetobacter baumannii infection increases Beclin 1 expression through the AMPK/ERK/mTOR signalling pathway, enhancing autophagy to support its survival . In tuberculosis, Beclin 1 plays a pivotal role in combating Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb). However, during infection, the upregulated miR-144 suppresses DRAM2, impairing Beclin 1-mediated autophagosome maturation and facilitating Mtb survival .
Pathogens frequently target the Beclin 1-VPS34 complex, disrupting its formation or stabilizing autophagosomes to manipulate autophagic pathways. Collectively, these findings underscore the importance of Beclin 1 in host-pathogen interactions and highlight its potential as a therapeutic target for treating infectious diseases.

## Beclin 1 mediated therapeutic interventions
Several therapeutic strategies have been explored to target Beclin 1 across different disease contexts. Tat-Beclin 1, a peptide derived from an important region of Beclin 1 (Residue 267-284), has been shown to restrict pathogenic infections and facilitate the entry of lentiviral vectors. It has demonstrated efficacy both in vitro and in vivo, particularly in modulating Beclin 1 interactions in HIV treatment , . Additionally, it plays a role in inhibiting tumorigenesis by disrupting the HER2 Beclin 1 interaction in HER2 positive breast cancer cell lines such as BT-474 cells, BT-474-VH2 cells, SK-BR3 cells, and MDA-MB-361, thereby inducing a strong autophagic flux , .
Another peptide Tat-SP4, designed from the coiled-coil domain (CCD) of Beclin 1, enhances interaction with Atg14L and UVRAG, promoting autophagy and endolysosomal degradation . This peptide has shown anti-tumour effects by activating Beclin 1's functions in degrading oncogenic receptors such as EGFR , . The optimization of Tat-SP4 (also known as i7-01s-31) through staple scanning and sequence permutation has improved its binding affinity to Beclin 1, making it a promising candidate in therapeutic contexts beyond cancer , . Another therapeutic approach involves Tat-D3S2 peptide, which disrupts the Beclin 1-DIRAS3 interaction, inhibiting autophagy in ovarian cancer cells under amino acid deprivation. This peptide holds the potential for targeting dormant ovarian tumours .
For small-molecule strategies, AG-690/12088124 was identified through virtual screening as a candidate activating Beclin 1 by binding to its ECD, it induces autophagic cell death and apoptosis in breast cancer cell lines, suggesting it could be a lead compound for further development beyond cancer therapy . Similarly, Piceatannol, a Beclin 1 targeting small molecule, directly interacts with the ECD and enhances the Beclin 1-UVRAG interaction by reducing Beclin 1 phosphorylation at Ser295. This molecule shows promise for combination therapy with the mTOR inhibitor everolimus for treating gastric cancer, suggesting a broader therapeutic scope . On the other hand small molecule SW063058, SW076956 and BRD1991 reported to inhibit the interaction between Beclin 1 and Bcl-2 family proteins and increase autophagic flux, which can be Serve as lead templates for developing potent and selective Beclin 1-Bcl-2 inhibitors that may be clinically useful for various diseases as autophagy inducer .
Therapeutic strategies targeting Beclin 1, through peptides or small molecules, demonstrate its broader potential across various diseases. These approaches aim to modulate Beclin 1's interactions and functions, enhancing autophagy and maintaining cellular homeostasis in diverse pathological conditions. However, none of these molecules have yet received FDA approval. Advancing the development of more stable, potent, and selective Beclin 1 targeted agents is essential to improve their clinical efficacy and expand their therapeutic applications.

## Advances in structure-guided drug discovery
7.1.

## Unveiling the potential of protein-protein interactions
Proteins are integral to nearly every biological function, interacting with other proteins and molecules to form the complex human interactome network , . Disruptions in these protein-protein interactions (PPIs) due to anomalies or any external factors can cause various pathophysiological conditions. PPIs are some of the most intriguing yet challenging targets in drug discovery.
In modern drug discovery, both ligand-based and structure-based approaches (LB/SBDD) play critical roles, leading to significant advancements in refining small molecule leads and discovering new drugs , , . Success in these projects often hinges on understanding the nature of PPI interfaces and the druggability of associated binding pockets , which previously was difficult to study due to the limited availability of protein structures but now has expanded significantly due to techniques like X-ray crystallography, NMR spectroscopy, and cryo-EM. These detailed atomic-level structures of both unbound proteins (APOs) and protein-ligand or protein complexes (HOLOs) form a solid foundation for structure-guided drug discovery, aiding in identifying potential hits, designing new compounds, and optimizing lead molecules . Druggability refers to the potential of a drug-like compound to modulate or inhibit interactions between proteins . Unfortunately, about 60% of drug discovery projects fail due to the undruggability of target binding sites, which are often large, shallow, and lacking distinct concave pockets , . Molecular dynamics simulations fill this gap by capturing the dynamic changes in proteins, such as conformational shifts, co-factor binding, post-translational modifications, mutations, and allosteric effects. Therefore, compiling comprehensive structural and ligand data is essential for accurately identifying key targets and enhancing drug discovery efforts . Over the last decade, numerous small molecule ligands have been developed to bind directly to PPI interfaces, proving that certain PPIs can accommodate small molecule inhibitors . For example, the Bcl-2 inhibitor Venetoclax (ABT-199), the first PPI drug to receive FDA approval, is now widely used to treat chronic lymphocytic leukaemia . This progress demonstrates that some previously undruggable PPI interfaces possess hydrophobic grooves that can be targeted as hot spots for small molecule inhibitor design, opening new avenues in drug discovery .

7.2.

## AlphaFold and the revolution in computational structure prediction and protein-protein interaction (PPI) studies
The development of AlphaFold has significantly advanced the field of protein structure prediction, providing a powerful complement to conventional computational approaches in drug discovery, particularly for studying protein-protein interactions (PPIs) . This breakthrough has enabled researchers to overcome longstanding challenges in structural biology, such as resolving the structures of proteins that are difficult to crystallize or are not prone to experimental methods like X-ray crystallography, NMR or cryo-EM. Since accurate protein structures are essential for understanding PPIs and developing PPI-based therapies, AlphaFold's capabilities have become pivotal in drug discovery targeting protein complexes .
AlphaFold's mechanism is based on a combination of deep learning architectures and advanced neural network frameworks . AlphaFold utilizes multiple sequence alignments (MSAs) and structural templates to extract evolutionary and structural information about a target protein, which is crucial for understanding how proteins interact in the context of PPIs . Key components of its mechanism include: (1) Input Data Processing: AlphaFold analyzes the input protein sequence and identifies homologous sequences using MSAs, which provide insights into evolutionary constraints and conserved residues; (2) Evoformer Architecture: This module processes MSAs and pairwise residue relationships, capturing long-range dependencies and interactions, iteratively refining information to build a detailed representation of inter-residue distances and angles, which is essential for understanding protein interfaces in PPIs; (3) Structure Module: The refined representations are passed to the Structure Module, which generates 3D atomic coordinates for the protein, predicting backbone and side-chain conformations while ensuring physical plausibility; (4) End-to-End Optimization: AlphaFold employs an end to end training approach, optimizing its model by minimizing the differences between predicted and experimentally determined structures in a large training dataset; (5) Confidence Scoring: The model provides a confidence metric, the predicted Local Distance Difference Test (pLDDT) score, indicating the reliability of the predicted structure at each residue level . Despite its remarkable strengths in predicting static structures, AlphaFold struggles with modelling dynamic conformational changes, allosteric effects, and protein-ligand interactions critical aspects of PPIs , . Additionally, its performance in predicting multi-chain protein complexes remains less reliable compared to experimental methods or molecular dynamics simulations . These limitations highlight the need for integrating AlphaFold predictions with conventional computational tools, such as homology modelling, molecular docking and MD simulations, to provide a more comprehensive understanding of PPI dynamics and facilitate PPI-targeted drug discovery. Ultimately, AlphaFold's breakthrough offers vast potential for advancing structure-guided drug discovery, especially when combined with traditional techniques to study protein-protein interactions and dynamic processes.
Structure-guided drug discovery combines multiple computational approaches to identify and optimize small molecules targeting protein-protein interactions (PPIs). outlines the step-by-step workflow, beginning with protein structure prediction (AlphaFold, homology modelling) and PPI characterization (docking, molecular dynamics simulations) to ligand discovery (AI/ML-based virtual screening) followed by experimental validation (in-vitro, in-vivo assays). Conventional techniques like MD simulations refine dynamic PPI interfaces, AI accelerates hit identification, enhancing druggability and reducing time constraints. By seamlessly bridging computational predictions with real-world therapeutic development, this approach streamlines the transition from hit identification to clinical trials, improving drug discovery efficiency.

## Autophagy modulators: a promising avenue for broad-spectrum therapeutics in academia and pharmaceuticals
Autophagy, as a core biological process responsible for degradation and recycling, has gained substantial attention in both academic research and the pharmaceutical industry due to its central role in maintaining cellular integrity and influencing disease mechanisms , . Beclin 1, a key regulator of autophagy, has emerged as a critical target for therapeutic intervention due to its multifaceted role in cellular homeostasis, disease progression, and immune responses. Advances in understanding its regulation including genetic and epigenetic control mechanisms, protein-protein interactions, and modulation by microRNAs have opened new avenues for developing targeted therapies , , . The development of Beclin 1 mimetic peptides and small molecule modulators has shown promise in tailoring autophagy based treatments to specific disease contexts . Clinical efforts are currently exploring the safety and efficacy of these modulators in cancer, neurodegenerative diseases, cardiovascular disorders, and infections . However, autophagy modulation must be approached cautiously, as its effects are highly context-dependent . For instance, in cancer, elevated autophagy may promote tumor cell survival under stress, complicating treatment outcomes . BH3 mimetics such as ABT-737 disrupt interaction between Beclin 1 and Bcl-2 to activate autophagy, yet this can also lead to treatment resistance . Thus, a deeper understanding of Beclin 1's interaction network and disease-specific autophagy dynamics is essential for designing effective and safe therapeutic strategies , , , . In response to these complexities, pharmaceutical companies are now increasingly pursuing autophagy based drug discovery, targeting not only misfolded proteins but also large signalling complexes and damaged organelles through lysosomal pathways (see ) , . Unlike traditional single target therapies, interventions that modulate protein-protein interactions (PPIs) within the autophagy network are emerging as highly selective and effective strategies , , . These PPI-targeted approaches enable fine-tuned modulation of autophagy to either promote or inhibit the process, with implications for treating oncology, inflammation, neurodegeneration, metabolic disorders, and infections. For example, autophagy activation using agents like rapamycin has been effective in clearing toxic aggregates in neurodegenerative diseases , while Tat-Beclin 1, another autophagy enhancer, shows promise in treating infectious diseases . Conversely, inhibitors such as chloroquine and hydroxychloroquine are being tested to impair tumor-supportive autophagy in cancer . In the clinical biopharmaceutical field, companies such as AbilityPharma are leveraging autophagy to develop innovative treatments for cancer. AbilityPharma, a clinical-stage phase 2b biopharmaceutical company, has made notable progress with ibrilatazar (ABTL0812), an oral drug shown to enhance chemotherapy efficacy by 40% in patients with endometrial cancer. This breakthrough, based on the phase 1/2 ENDOLUNG clinical trial, underscores ibrilatazar's novel mechanism of action, which induces autophagy through ER-stress induction and inhibition of the PI3K/Akt/mTOR pathway vital for cellular function and implicated in cancer progression. In the neurodegenerative disease space, Samsara Therapeutics has emerged as a leader, focusing on autophagy activation to treat diseases such as ALS, Parkinson's Disease, and Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease. Their therapeutic pipeline includes SAM001, SAM0021, and SAM005. Similarly, Casma Therapeutics is reprogramming autophagy with its innovative programs and technologies, which enhances the lysosomal degradation of disease targets beyond the capacity of the ubiquitin-proteasome system. This platform holds promise for treating a range of diseases, from neurodegeneration to oncology, by reprogramming autophagy to form autophagosomes specifically around critical disease targets.
 These advancements in autophagy-based therapies are poised to have a transformative impact on disease treatment. As academic research continues to elucidate the mechanisms of autophagy, and pharmaceutical innovation accelerates, the development of advanced drug discovery platforms, such as high-throughput screening and artificial intelligence, will continue to progress. By leveraging interdisciplinary collaboration and targeting PPIs, autophagy modulators are positioned to revolutionize disease management across various therapeutic areas. The growing understanding of autophagy's role in immunity, inflammation, and ageing has broadened its therapeutic potential, opening new avenues for treating autoimmune disorders, cardiovascular diseases, and age-related conditions .

## Conclusion and future directions
Other than academia, now a series of various pharmaceutical companies are focusing on drug discovery and the development of therapeutics targeting novel cellular degradation approaches based on the autophagy pathway to explore new areas that will profoundly impact the therapeutic landscape, either as standalone treatments or in combination with existing front-line therapies of respective diseases, however, we are aware that none of the therapeutics have been FDA-approved to date.
Since autophagy is a conserved cellular process that contributes to overall cellular homeostasis, it addresses larger and more complex disease targets, such as organelles, protein aggregates, and large signalling complexes, directing these targets to the lysosome for elimination. Selective pharmacological modulation of autophagy is considered a valuable therapeutic approach to treat diverse human diseases; however, to achieve this the major requirment to crack the hurdles of PPIs, recognition sites which remain a tough nut to crack. Moreover, the discovery of small-molecule modulators has been greatly impeded due to insufficient structural details of targets (such as Beclin 1) with or without its interactors and their structural dynamics and functional determinants. Deciphering the hot-spots by targeting PPIs within the intrinsic autophagy regulatory network can serve as a valuable strategy for developing autophagy-based therapeutics. Targeting PPIs among autophagy regulators emerges as the most promising approach to modulate autophagy, rather than focusing on single targets. PPI based selective modulation of disease targets through autophagy can arrest or reverse disease progression across multiple therapeutic areas. Among autophagy regulators, Beclin 1 plays a pivotal role in stabilizing other members of autophagy complexes at various stages. Beclin 1-dependent autophagy modulation holds significant therapeutic potential due to its critical function in the autophagy process, from initiation to maturation. However, the functional consequences of modulating Beclin 1 are highly context-dependent, particularly given that autophagy plays divergent roles across different tissues and disease states. For example, excessive or prolonged autophagy activation may promote cell death in neurons, while insufficient autophagy can accelerate tumorigenesis in certain cancers. Conversely, in other contexts, autophagy may support cancer cell survival under stress conditions, raising the concern that indiscriminate enhancement of Beclin 1 function could lead to unintended oncogenic effects. Therefore, future therapeutic strategies must carefully consider cell-type specificity, disease context, and the dynamic regulation of Beclin 1 to avoid adverse outcomes. Moreover, directly targeting Beclin 1 is less effective, as it serves as a key node in this pathway. Its complete inhibition or hyperactivation may lead to off-target effects, making it a less viable strategy. Instead, a more effective approach involves designing peptides and/or small molecules that specifically target its interactors through PPIs. This strategy enables precise autophagy modulation, particularly under pathophysiological conditions, by focusing on positive and negative regulators as well as post-translational modifications (PTMs). Binding of these regulators at different domains of Beclin 1 drives diverse biological functions, ( ). These regulator-specific modulations provide a rationale for targeting them and leveraging knowledge of their interacting interface sites with Beclin 1. However, without structural information, targeting PPIs is significantly more challenging, as pinpointing the most likely interaction site is critical. Here, computational techniques such as protein-protein docking along with biological insights address these challenges significantly.
 Another bottleneck associated with target and their interactors is conformational changes as they are not static single atomic arrangements; they are flexible and explore a continuous dynamic space, often described as a set of low-energy states connected by higher-energy transition paths. Current experimental techniques provide a good picture of the most stable conformations but offer little information about the transition paths or intermediate states. Computational techniques such as molecular dynamics (MD) simulations can be employed to characterize protein dynamics by sampling their conformational space of structured as well as unstructured parts. However, the likelihood of capturing a specific conformation in a simulation is inversely proportional to its energy, making the discovery of transition states a rare event an essential factor to incorporate when designing modulators. Advanced computational approaches, including conventional and accelerated MD simulations, deep learning, and neural networks trained with discrete sets of structures derived from molecular simulations, can aid in generating diverse sets of protein conformations (clusters). Incorporating these methods, along with breakthroughs in AI/ML tools and innovations like AlphaFold, can provide valuable structural insights into the Beclin 1 interactome, its complex states with positive and negative regulators, and its inherent conformational flexibility. Another step needs to characterize is the transition between unstructured to structured and vice versa. We believe that this promising approach to target Beclin 1 regulated pathways may hold significant potential towards mechanistic understanding and for the discovery of therapeutics.
