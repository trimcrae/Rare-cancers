Reduced oxygen tension activates a conserved hypoxic signaling network centered on hypoxia-inducible factors (HIFs), which coordinate transcriptional and post-transcriptional programs that promote metabolic adaptation, angiogenesis, erythropoiesis, and redox balance. While HIF signaling is essential for development and tissue homeostasis, its dysregulation contributes to disease-associated dysfunction in cancer, ischemic disease, and chronic inflammatory disorders. Accordingly, therapeutic strategies aimed at modulating this pathway have gained considerable interest. This review critically examines current strategies aimed at suppressing HIF signaling, with a particular emphasis on approaches that reduce HIF-α protein abundance, inhibit transcriptional activity, or interfere with HIF complex formation. We discuss RNA-based therapies, small-molecule modulators of HIF stability, and noncanonical HIF inhibitors - including topoisomerase inhibitors, proteasome inhibitors, and metabolic modulators - that exert their effects independently of classical oxygen-dependent degradation. Emerging evidence indicates that many chemically diverse HIF inhibitors converge on translation-centered mechanisms, including inhibition of mTOR signaling, activation of eIF2α-dependent stress responses, and disruption of hypoxia-specific translational control. These pathways represent a dominant and previously underappreciated layer of HIF regulation. By contrasting stability-, translation-, and transcription-centered strategies, we highlight key therapeutic constraints imposed by oxygen availability, redox balance, and HIF isoform specificity. The clinical success of HIF-2α–specific allosteric inhibitors underscores the importance of exploiting unique structural vulnerabilities, whereas the lack of analogous pockets in HIF-1α necessitates alternative approaches. We propose that durable suppression of hypoxia-driven pathology will likely require integration of isoform-specific targeting with translational control mechanisms that decouple HIF signaling from generalized cytotoxic stress.

## Introduction
Oxygen is essential for aerobic life, serving as the final electron acceptor in mitochondrial respiration while simultaneously posing risk through the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS). To sustain oxygen homeostasis, aerobic organisms deploy tightly coordinated physiological, metabolic, and molecular mechanisms. Nevertheless, both physiological and pathological states can transiently disrupt the equilibrium between oxygen supply and demand, resulting in tissue and cellular hypoxia. At the cellular level, insufficient oxygen availability activates a conserved signaling network known as the adaptive response to hypoxia [ , ] (see ). 
This response features extensive transcriptional reprogramming aimed at preserving cellular integrity under low-oxygen conditions. Central to this response is a glycolysis-dominated metabolic shift accompanied by suppression of mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation [ , , , , , , ]. In parallel, hypoxia induces angiogenesis and erythropoietin production, thereby improving tissue oxygen delivery and limiting redox imbalance during reoxygenation [ , , , ]. When hypoxia is prolonged or adaptive capacity is exceeded, the failure to restore oxygen homeostasis triggers programmed cell death pathways [ , , ].
Aberrant regulation of hypoxic signaling is mechanistically associated with disease development across a range of conditions, including ischemic heart disease, stroke, cancer, and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease [ ]. As a result, modulation of hypoxia-adaptive pathways has emerged as an important therapeutic objective. Enhancing oxygen adaptation is beneficial following ischemic injury, such as stroke or myocardial infarction [ , ]. In contrast, suppression of hypoxia-driven angiogenesis and metabolic plasticity is desirable in pathological settings such as solid tumors [ , , ] and pathological neovascular eye conditions, encompassing age-related macular degeneration, glaucoma, and diabetic retinopathy [ , , , , , ].
Hypoxia signaling pathways that are maladaptive in disease contexts are nevertheless essential during embryonic development and tissue morphogenesis [ , ]. Despite the complexity and tissue specificity of hypoxic responses, hypoxia-inducible factors (HIFs) form the central transcriptional axis governing cellular adaptation to oxygen deprivation. Under limited oxygen supply, HIF-α subunits escape oxygen-dependent degradation, heterodimerize with HIF-β, and activate transcriptional programs that coordinate metabolic, vascular, and redox adaptation to hypoxic stress [ , , , ]. Accordingly, HIF signaling constitutes a central node linking oxygen sensing, metabolism, and redox homeostasis.
Therapeutic strategies targeting hypoxia pathways therefore fall into two broad categories: HIF signaling activation as a means to sustain tissue integrity and regenerative responses, or the inhibition of HIF-dependent transcription in diseases where hypoxia drives pathology. Pharmacological activation of HIF pathways is predominantly achieved using hypoxia mimetics [ ] that inhibit oxygen-dependent HIF degradation; several such agents are now approved for clinical use (reviewed in Refs. [ , , , ]). This review integrates recent mechanistic and translational advances in strategies aimed at attenuating HIF signaling. Current approaches seek to suppress HIF activity by reducing HIF-α protein levels, preventing heterodimer formation, or disrupting HIF–DNA interactions [ , , ]. Despite extensive preclinical development, only one selective HIF inhibitor - targeting HIF-2α (PT2977/pt2799, belzutifan; WELIREG™) - has reached clinical approval to date, first in the United States in 2021 and subsequently in the European Union in 2025 [ ]. Notwithstanding this progress, substantial challenges remain, including tissue specificity, on-target toxicity, and long-term safety. Here, we critically evaluate current HIF-inhibitory strategies and discuss their translational potential in the context of redox biology and human disease.
1.1

## HIFs as part of cellular oxygen-sensing mechanisms
Although atmospheric oxygen is ∼21%, most human tissues operate at substantially lower oxygen tensions (2–13%), a range referred to as physioxia [ ]. Intracellular oxygen concentrations are lower still (∼1.3–2.5%), while mitochondrial oxygen tension frequently falls below ∼1.3% [ ]. As a consequence, even modest perturbations in oxygen availability rapidly affect mitochondrial metabolism and critically influence cell survival. The elucidation of molecular mechanisms underlying cellular oxygen sensing was recognized by the 2019 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine (William G. Kaelin Jr., Sir Peter J. Ratcliffe, Gregg L. Semenza) for the elucidation of HIFs and the canonical pathways governing their oxygen-dependent regulation.
Oxygen sensing is mediated primarily by prolyl hydroxylase domain-containing proteins (PHDs), iron- and ascorbate-dependent enzymes in the 2-oxoglutarate (2-OG)–dependent dioxygenase family. In humans, three PHD homologs have been identified: EGLN2 (PHD1), EGLN1 (PHD2), and EGLN3 (PHD3). These enzymes are essential for repressing HIF signaling in an oxygen-dependent manner [ , ]. Under normoxia, PHDs hydroxylate conserved proline residues within HIF-α subunits, increasing their affinity for the von Hippel–Lindau (pVHL) E3 ubiquitin ligase complex, which promotes polyubiquitination and 26S proteasomal degradation [ , , , , ]. In contrast, HIF-β subunits are constitutively expressed in an oxygen-independent manner; thus, degradation of HIF-α prevents the formation of transcriptionally active HIF heterodimers [ ].
PHD activity is highly sensitive to oxygen availability, exhibiting an apparent Km for oxygen close to atmospheric pO2 and substantially higher than oxygen levels found in most tissues [ , ]. Consequently, even minor reductions in cellular oxygen tension inhibit PHD activity, resulting in stabilization and accumulation of HIF-α. Stabilized HIF-α translocates to the nucleus, heterodimerizes with HIF-β, and binds hypoxia-responsive elements within target genes, and initiates the adaptive transcriptional programs [ ]. Restoration of oxygen reactivates PHDs and rapidly terminates HIF signaling through renewed HIF-α degradation.
Another oxygen-dependent hydroxylase - factor inhibiting HIF-1 (FIH-1; HIF1AN) - adds a further regulatory layer. FIH-1 functions at lower oxygen tensions than PHDs and hydroxylates a single asparagine residue within the C-terminal transactivation domain of HIF-1α, consequently limiting engagement of transcriptional co-activator complexes [ , ]. This mechanism attenuates HIF transcriptional activity during early reoxygenation, acting as a buffer that limits the excessive metabolic activation and oxidative stress during the transition from hypoxia to normoxia. Given that HIF signaling promotes a glycolytic shift during hypoxia, FIH-1–mediated repression facilitates efficient metabolic readjustment upon oxygen restoration. 1.2

## Activation and termination of hypoxia signaling
Hypoxic signaling critically depends on the formation of heterodimeric HIF-α/β complexes. HIFs are classified as members of basic helix–loop–helix (bHLH)–Per–ARNT–Sim (PAS) family of transcription factors. Both α and β subunits contain an N-terminal bHLH domain mediating DNA binding and two PAS domains (PAS-A and PAS-B) required for dimerization [ ]. HIF-β partners are ARNT family proteins (ARNT and ARNT2); other class II bHLH–PAS proteins such as ARNTL/BMAL1 participate in related signaling networks but are not canonical HIF β partners. HIF-α subunits contain an oxygen-dependent degradation (ODD) domain targeted by PHDs and transcriptional activation domains (TADs) that drive gene expression [ ]. Accordingly, therapeutic strategies aimed at enhancing HIF signaling predominantly target PHDs or the VHL-dependent degradation pathway. Proline hydroxylation sites differ among HIF isoforms: HIF-1α (P402, P564), HIF-2α (P405, P531), and HIF-3α (P492) [ , ]. Both PHD and HIF isoforms display substrate selectivity, with PHD2 functioning as the principal regulator of HIF-1α stability and PHD3 preferentially modulating HIF-2α [ , , , ]. PHD2 is the most abundant hydroxylase under basal conditions, and FIH-1 also exhibits higher activity toward HIF-1α [ , ].
Although HIF-1α and HIF-2α share ∼50% sequence identity, they differ within their N-terminal transactivation domains, conferring distinct transcriptional outputs. HIF-3α differs structurally in that it lacks a C-terminal transactivation domain and undergoes extensive alternative splicing, resulting in multiple isoforms with diverse regulatory functions [ , , ]. Efficient nuclear translocation of HIF-α subunits is mediated by two nuclear localization motifs located within the N- and C-terminal regions [ ]. ## Transcriptional and post-transcriptional consequences of HIF activation
Although any HIF-α subunit can in principle heterodimerize with any HIF-β partner, only HIF-1α and HIF-1β (ARNT) are ubiquitously expressed. In contrast, HIF-2α, HIF-3α, ARNT2, and ARNTL exhibit pronounced cell type– and tissue-specific expression patterns [ , , ]. While HIF-2α and HIF-3α are detectable across multiple organs, their expression is restricted to defined tissues [ , ]. This diversity in HIF complex composition contributes to cell type–specific hypoxic transcriptional responses [ , , , , , , , ]. Although many hypoxia-exposed tissues rely on HIF-1 for the initial response, numerous cell types engage HIF-2 and HIF-3 to modulate or partially replace HIF-1 activity during prolonged hypoxia [ , ]. HIF-1 and HIF-2 regulate overlapping yet distinct gene sets, whereas the physiological contribution of HIF-3 remains poorly resolved [ , ].
In most tissues, hypoxic signaling follows a characteristic temporal pattern in which HIF-1 drives the early response and is subsequently replaced or supplemented by HIF-2 - and in some contexts HIF-3 - during prolonged hypoxia. This “HIF switch” supports long-term adaptation while limiting oxidative stress and metabolic imbalance [ , , , ]. Endothelial cells and many cancers exploit this switch, underscoring its relevance for pathological hypoxia and disease progression [ ]. Thus, hypoxia-induced transcriptional reprogramming is governed not solely by HIF-1 activity, but also by coordinated interactions with HIF-2 and HIF-3 ( ). 
Within the nucleus, transcriptionally active HIF heterodimers bind hypoxia-responsive elements (HREs) containing an asymmetric E-box core motif (RCGTG, where R = A or G) [ , ]. HIFs recruit transcriptional co-activators and chromatin-associated factors that facilitate stable DNA binding and transcriptional activation [ ]. While the majority of HREs can be recognized by both HIF-1 and HIF-2, differences within the N-terminal transactivation domains confer isoform-specific binding preferences [ ]. Distinct HRE sequences have been identified for HIF-1 and HIF-2 [ , , ], whereas no consensus motif unique to HIF-3 has been identified to date. In addition, HRE-selective gene activation is influenced by chromatin accessibility and genomic context, including distance from transcription start sites. Most functional HREs reside within open chromatin [ , , ] and HIF-1 preferentially binds promoter-proximal regions, whereas HIF-2 more frequently occupies distal enhancers. Promoter-proximal HIF-1 binding correlates with a higher transcript abundance [ , , , ].
Accordingly, HIF-driven transcriptional reprogramming reflects a combination of HRE sequence preference, isoform-specific DNA binding, tissue-restricted cofactors [ , , ], and epigenetic features such as enhancer–promoter interactions and chromatin accessibility [ , , , , ]. Many of these parameters are cell type–specific and dynamically altered by hypoxia, emphasizing the need for systematic, multi-cell-type analyses to define physiologically relevant HIF target networks [ ].

1.4

## HIF-regulated genes
Although numerous genes are proposed to be HIF-regulated, isoform-specific regulation is often highly context dependent. HIF-1 supports rapid cellular adjustment to reduced oxygen availability through coordinated metabolic reprogramming and enhancement of oxygen supply [ ]. Canonical HIF-1 targets include phosphofructokinase (PFK) and lactate dehydrogenase A (LDHA), which enhance glycolytic flux [ ]; monocarboxylate transporter 4 (MCT4) and carbonic anhydrase 9 (CA9), which prevent intracellular acidification; and angiogenic and cytoprotective genes such as endothelial nitric oxide synthase (NOS3) and heme oxygenase-1 (HMOX1) [ , , , , , ].
In specific respiratory cell populations - including bronchial epithelial club cells and carotid body cells - HIF-1α may fail to accumulate during hypoxia, whereas HIF-2α alone sustains hypoxic adaptation [ , , ]. In these cells, HIF-2α abundance is tightly regulated [ , ], suggesting specialized oxygen-sensing requirements and enhanced protection against ROS. Such deviations from canonical HIF signaling highlight gaps in our understanding of HIF-1 function in the conducting airways and warrant further investigation [ ].
In primary human endothelial cells, our analyses uncovered over 150 hypoxia-responsive genes involved in metabolic control and angiogenic regulation erythropoiesis, autophagy, and pro-survival signaling [ ]. Genes governed by overlapping HIF-1- and HIF-2-dependent regulatory inputs exhibited the strongest induction during hypoxia [ ], including classical targets such as VEGFA and GLUT1 [ , ]. These shared targets showed no differential enrichment of HIF-1 versus HIF-2 HREs, consistent with noncompetitive binding to common regulatory elements [ , , , ]. Genes engaged during early hypoxia retained transcriptional activity during prolonged exposure despite HIF-1 motif enrichment at their promoters, while genes responding selectively to prolonged hypoxia showed a bias toward HIF-2–associated regulatory elements. Whereas genes induced exclusively during prolonged hypoxia were enriched for HIF-2 motifs. This supports the concept that HIF-1 and HIF-2 operate on largely distinct regulatory timelines rather than competing for identical HREs [ ].

1.5

## HIF-2 and HIF-3 specific functions
Distinct functional outputs of HIF-2 are frequently linked to erythropoiesis and iron homeostasis. Translation of EPAS1 (HIF-2α) mRNA is regulated by an iron-responsive element in its 5′ UTR, coupling HIF-2 activity to iron availability [ , , ]. Erythropoietin (EPO) is often considered a prototypical HIF-2 target [ , ], although regulation by all three HIF isoforms has also been reported [ ]. Consistent with this, all HIFs contribute to transcriptional programs maintaining iron metabolism [ , , ]. Additional HIF-2 targets include matrix metalloproteinases (MMP2 and MMP13), the stem cell factor OCT-3/4 [ ], antioxidant enzymes (SOD1 and SOD2) [ ], and pro-apoptotic regulators [ ]. In human microvascular endothelial cells subjected to prolonged hypoxia, HIF-2α knockdown promotes endothelial sprouting [ , ], an effect attributed to unique C-terminal features of HIF-2α rather than differential HRE recognition [ ]. Notwithstanding these distinctions, the global architecture of HIF-1- and HIF-2-dependent target gene networks remains largely conserved across different cell types [ , , , ].
The contribution of HIF-3 to hypoxic signaling remains poorly resolved and has been subject to debate, owing in part to tissue-specific expression of numerous short and long HIF-3α isoforms produced through alternative splicing [ , ]. Early studies suggested that truncated variants lacking transactivation domains act as dominant-negative regulators of HIF-1 and HIF-2 [ , , , ]. Under conditions of limiting HIF-β availability, overexpression of HIF-3α can promote cytoplasmic aggregation with HIF-1α or HIF-2α, preventing nuclear localization [ ]. Because many HIF-3α isoforms are themselves induced by HIF-1 and HIF-2 [ ], these findings led to a proposed negative-feedback model in which HIF-3α attenuates HIF-dependent transcription [ , , ].
However, subsequent studies - including ours - demonstrated that knockdown of endogenous HIF-3α variants reduces expression of hypoxia-responsive genes, whereas overexpression of long HIF-3α isoforms enhances HRE-driven transcription when HIF-β is not limiting [ , , , , ]. These observations challenge the view that HIF-3α functions solely as a negative regulator. Indeed, HIF-3 shares transcriptional targets with HIF-1 and HIF-2, including EPO, GLUT1, ANGPTL4, REDD1 (DDIT4), LC3C, and SQRDL [ , , , ]. In zebrafish, cooperative activity of HIF-3 and HIF-1 governs pathways related to nutrient metabolism, apoptotic and proteolytic processes, and p53-and PPAR-linked signaling, whereas HIF-3 alone regulates distinct gene sets connected to nitrogen and methane metabolism as well as Jak–STAT and NOD-like receptor signaling [ , ]. Our data indicate that HIF-3α2 predominates among HIF-3 isoforms in human umbilical vein endothelial cells. This isoform accumulates progressively under prolonged hypoxia in primary human endothelial cells and promotes cell death through DDIT4-mediated inhibition of mTOR signaling [ , , , , , , , ]. Thus, gradual HIF-3α accumulation may serve as a molecular timer enabling apoptosis when adaptive responses fail.
Collectively, these findings indicate that coordinated, isoform-specific regulation of HIF-1, HIF-2, and HIF-3 is essential for appropriate hypoxic adaptation and, when necessary, activation of cell-death programs. This concept is further substantiated by contexts in which HIF-1 and HIF-2 exert opposing effects - for example, in clear-cell renal carcinoma, where HIF-1 inhibits c-Myc activity while HIF-2 enhances it [ ], and in endothelial cells, where HIF-1 promotes eNOS activity whereas HIF-2 suppresses it [ ]. The latter may reflect a protective role for HIF-2 in limiting ROS and preventing eNOS uncoupling [ ]. Together, these observations underscore the necessity of balanced HIF isoform activity and suggest that optimal hypoxic responses often require coordinated action of multiple HIF family members.

1.6

## The role of miRNAs
Beyond direct HRE-driven transcriptional control, HIFs also orchestrate post-transcriptional adaptation through regulation of microRNAs (miRNAs) [ ]. While transcriptional induction is essential to establish hypoxia-responsive gene programs, it cannot rapidly remodel the pre-existing transcriptome and proteome. In contrast, miRNAs provide temporal flexibility by promoting transcript degradation or inhibiting translation, thereby fine-tuning protein output during hypoxic stress [ , , , , ]. Hypoxia can also alter miRNA function independently of transcription, as specific miRNAs and their targets are selectively recruited into RNA-induced silencing complexes (RISCs) without changes in miRNA biogenesis [ ]. Hypoxia-induced changes in the miRNA landscape - often HIF-dependent - thus add a regulatory layer that supports rapid adaptation.
Numerous miRNAs are transcriptionally induced by HIF-1, including miR-210, miR-146a, miR-145, miR-382, miR-191, miR-363, miR-421, miR-204, miR-30a, miR-21, miR-687, miR-155, miR-429, and miR-19a [ , , , , , , , , , , , , , , , ]. Some hypoxia-regulated miRNAs, such as miR-210-3p, are controlled by both HIF-1 and HIF-2 [ ], underscoring partial redundancy at the transcriptional level. In human umbilical vein endothelial cells, we showed that most HIF-1–dependent miRNAs are also regulated by HIF-2, whereas prolonged hypoxia selectively engages HIF-2 to control a distinct set (e.g., miR-503, miR-543, miR-450b-5p, miR-374a-3p, miR-26a-2-3p, miR-10a-3p, miR-7-5p, miR-495-3p, miR-424-3p, miR-342-5p) [ ]. These miRNAs modulate discrete aspects of endothelial adaptation, highlighting isoform-dependent specialization at the post-transcriptional level.
Notably, differences in miRNA regulation between HIF isoforms are more pronounced than those observed for protein-coding genes. This supports a model in which the transition from HIF-1–dominant to HIF-2–dominant signaling during prolonged hypoxia is mediated primarily at the post-transcriptional level. We propose that in endothelial cells this “HIF switch” is largely executed through HIF-controlled miRNA networks, enabling rapid rewiring of cellular responses under conditions in which global transcription and translation are progressively impaired [ , ]. This mechanism would allow cells to adapt efficiently to sustained hypoxia while preparing for reoxygenation, minimizing the redox imbalance and proteotoxic stress. Although this hypothesis requires broader validation, it offers a conceptual framework for hypoxic adaptation beyond transcriptional control.
From a therapeutic perspective, HIF-dependent miRNA regulation has important implications for strategies aimed at inhibiting hypoxic signaling. Small-molecule HIF inhibitors primarily target HIF-α stability, dimerization, or DNA binding, yet may only indirectly or incompletely affect downstream miRNA-mediated circuits. Moreover, isoform-selective HIF inhibition - particularly HIF-2α targeting - may preferentially disrupt miRNA networks engaged during prolonged hypoxia, whereas HIF-1–regulated miRNAs may remain active during early phases. These considerations suggest that miRNA signatures may serve both as biomarkers of effective HIF inhibition and as potential combinatorial targets to enhance specificity or overcome adaptive resistance.

1.7

## Oxygen-independent regulation of HIF-α abundance and activity
Although hypoxia-inducible factors are classically regulated through oxygen-dependent prolyl hydroxylation and pVHL-mediated proteasomal degradation, it is now clear that HIF-α abundance and transcriptional competence can be regulated independently of oxygen availability. Such non-canonical control enables HIF signaling under normoxic conditions, giving rise to “pseudohypoxia”, a phenomenon frequently observed in cancer, inflammatory disorders, and cardiovascular pathology [ , , , ]. Oxygen-independent HIF regulation operates at multiple levels, including transcription, translation, post-translational modification, protein–protein interactions, subcellular localization, and proteostasis [ , , ]. Through integration of inflammatory, mitogenic, metabolic, and redox-dependent signals, these pathways effectively decouple HIF activity from oxygen tension per se.
HIF-1α can be transcriptionally upregulated under normoxic conditions through redox-sensitive inflammatory and oncogenic signaling, with NF-κB acting as a central regulatory hub. NF-κB directly engages functional κB elements within the HIF1A promoter, driving HIF1A mRNA and protein expression independently of hypoxia, a mechanism demonstrated across macrophages, vascular smooth-muscle cells, endothelial and renal epithelial cells, and multiple cancer models, where cytokines such as TNF-α and IL-1β elevate HIF-1α expression at normal oxygen tension [ , , , ]. This transcriptional activation is ROS-dependent, with mitochondria-derived ROS providing rapid redox signals that couple metabolic activity to NF-κB activation, while NOX4-generated ROS establish a more sustained, transcription-permissive redox environment, particularly in vascular and malignant cells [ , , , , ]. Functionally, NF-κB-driven HIF-1α accumulation is sufficient to induce hypoxia-responsive gene programs under normoxia, thereby establishing a redox-mediated pseudohypoxic phenotype [ ]. Importantly, NF-κB also governs the repressive arm of the hypoxic response: the majority of genes downregulated during hypoxia require NF-κB for their suppression, and a conserved subset of hypoxia-regulated genes depends on NF-κB across diverse cell types [ ]. In this context, NF-κB is essential for hypoxia-associated ROS generation and for regulation of gene networks controlling oxidative phosphorylation, underscoring its dual role as both an activator and repressor of hypoxic transcriptional programs and positioning NF-κB as a key integrator of redox signaling, metabolic remodeling, and HIF-dependent gene regulation.
Additional transcriptional regulators operate in parallel to reinforce HIF1A expression within this redox–signaling framework. The GC-box-binding factor SP1, whose DNA-binding and transactivation properties are sensitive to oxidative modification, promotes HIF1A transcription in response to insulin and ROS, particularly in metabolic and neoplastic contexts [ , , ]. Similarly, FoxK1, whose nuclear localization is regulated by PI3K/AKT/GSK3β signaling downstream of growth-factor- and ROS-dependent inputs, enhances HIF1A transcription via mTORC1-linked mechanisms [ ]. The proto-oncogene c-MYC further amplifies this program by facilitating chromatin remodeling and transcriptional elongation while cooperating with HIF-1 and HIF-2 to support redox-coupled metabolic reprogramming [ , , , ]. Collectively, these transcriptional events establish a redox- and growth-factor-driven increase in HIF-1α availability, which functionally primes HIF signaling for subsequent regulation at the translational level by PI3K–AKT–mTOR pathways.
Beyond transcription, HIF-1α levels are further shaped by oxygen-independent translational control that primes the protein pool for downstream proteostatic regulation. Redox- and growth-factor-dependent activation of PI3K–AKT enhances mTORC1 signaling, promoting 4E-BP1 phosphorylation and p70S6K activity, thereby increasing cap-dependent translation of HIF1A mRNA under normoxia [ , , ]. In parallel, a functional internal ribosome entry site within HIF1A enables cap-independent translation during metabolic or oxidative stress, sustaining HIF-1α synthesis when canonical initiation is constrained [ ]. Concomitantly, ERK- and p38-MAPK signaling integrates inflammatory and redox cues to enhance HIF-1α nuclear retention and transcriptional competence [ , ]. Together, these pathways expand the pool of HIF-α available for subsequent oxygen-independent stabilization or degradation, linking translational control directly to chaperone- and ubiquitin-mediated proteostasis.
Cellular metabolism constitutes a major oxygen-independent determinant of HIF-α stability by modulating the activity of PHDs, which require α-ketoglutarate (αKG), Fe2+, and O2 as cofactors. Accordingly, accumulation of tricarboxylic-acid-cycle intermediates such as succinate and fumarate, most often due to SDH or FH loss-of-function, competitively inhibits PHDs and stabilizes HIF-α during normoxia [ , ]. A similar effect is observed with 2-hydroxyglutarate, generated by mutant IDH enzymes or LDH-dependent reactions, which interferes with αKG-dependent dioxygenases and promotes HIF-α persistence [ , , ]. In parallel, lactate accumulation, a hallmark of aerobic glycolysis, reinforces HIF-1α stabilization by altering cellular redox balance and suppressing PHD activity, in part through ROS-dependent mechanisms [ , , ]. Perturbations in amino-acid metabolism exert comparable effects, as reduced intracellular αKG resulting from altered glutamate or alanine transaminase flux impairs PHD function and stabilizes HIF-1α independently of oxygen availability [ ].
Beyond metabolic control, oxygen-independent proteostatic mechanisms further shape HIF signaling fidelity. The E3 ubiquitin ligase HIF-associated factor (HAF) selectively targets HIF-1α, but not HIF-2α, for degradation independently of prolyl hydroxylation, thereby contributing to isoform-specific switching during cancer progression [ ]. Conversely, multiple deubiquitinases, including USP20, USP14, USP8, UCHL1, and TRIM44, counteract ubiquitination and stabilize HIF-α, particularly in oncogenic settings, while USP33-mediated deubiquitination preferentially enhances HIF-2α stability, adding another layer of oxygen-independent specificity [ , , ]. Chaperone systems integrate with these ubiquitin circuits: HSP90 preserves HIF-1α folding and stability, whereas competition with RACK1 promotes ubiquitin-dependent degradation independently of pVHL, a balance that can be shifted pharmacologically [ , ].
Finally, post-translational modifications fine-tune HIF-α activity downstream of protein abundance. Context-dependent phosphorylation by ERK1/2, CDK1, ATM, or PKA enhances nuclear retention and transactivation, while GSK3-dependent phosphorylation favors degradation [ , , , , , ]. Likewise, SUMOylation mediated by SUMO1 or CBX4 often augments transcriptional competence, and recruitment of p300/CBP serves as a decisive gate for HIF-1 activity. MAPK- and STAT3-dependent signaling promotes co-activator engagement, whereas FIH-1 restrains transactivation through asparaginyl hydroxylation independently of PHDs, with additional modulation by SIRT1, EAF2, and MUC1 [ , , , , , , , , , , ].

1.8

## Reactive oxygen species as central mediators of pseudohypoxic HIF signaling
The importance of redox signaling in oxygen sensing and HIF regulation became evident with the demonstration that mitochondrial-derived ROS are required for hypoxia-induced transcription, rather than representing secondary consequences of oxygen deprivation. In a seminal study, Chandel and colleagues showed that inhibition of mitochondrial ROS production abolishes hypoxia-driven gene activation, establishing ROS as active signaling intermediates in HIF regulation [ ]. This work provided a mechanistic basis for viewing hypoxic signaling as a redox-modulated process rather than a direct readout of oxygen availability. Subsequent studies revealed that moderate reductions in oxygen tension increase mitochondrial superoxide and hydrogen peroxide generation, particularly at respiratory complexes I and III, and that these ROS inhibit prolyl hydroxylase (PHD) activity, leading to HIF-α stabilization at oxygen levels well above anoxia. Importantly, this regulation is graded and redox-window–dependent, explaining how HIF signaling can be engaged under mild hypoxia or even near-normoxic conditions in metabolically active or oncogenically transformed cells, rather than exhibiting a binary oxygen response [ , , , ].
Beyond mitochondria, NADPH oxidases (NOXs) have emerged as major contributors to redox-dependent HIF control. Work by Görlach, Kietzmann, and colleagues demonstrated that NOX-derived ROS modulate HIF transcriptional activity and target gene expression, directly linking redox signaling to hypoxia-responsive genetic programs [ , ]. Notably, NOX4-generated ROS were shown to stabilize HIF-2α under normoxic and mildly hypoxic conditions, providing early evidence for isoform-selective HIF regulation independent of profound oxygen limitation [ ]. These findings anticipated later observations that HIF-2α preferentially persists during chronic inflammation, tumor growth, and sustained oxidative signaling, where redox tone outweighs oxygen tension [ ].
This framework was further refined by studies identifying ROS-dependent modulation of PHD2 activity under mild hypoxia, in which oxidative modification alters hydroxylase function without complete oxygen withdrawal [ , ]. Together, these data provide a mechanistic explanation for pseudohypoxia and support the view that HIF stabilization frequently reflects redox-controlled tuning of oxygen-sensing enzymes within a permissive oxygen range, rather than absolute hypoxia. At the chemical level, iron-dependent redox reactions add another layer of regulation. Fenton chemistry and hydroxyl radical formation influence both ROS propagation and the availability of catalytically active Fe2+ required for PHD function. Perturbations in iron redox cycling (driven by oxidative stress, chelation, or metabolic remodeling), therefore intersect directly with HIF hydroxylation chemistry, linking mitochondrial metabolism, oxidative reactions, and enzymatic HIF control into a unified redox framework [ ].
Importantly, redox-modulated HIF regulation operates in intact organisms, not only in cell culture. Studies by Fandrey and colleagues demonstrated redox-sensitive regulation of erythropoietin production and HIF activity in physiological settings [ ], while developmental analyses by Adelman, Maltepe, Simon, and co-workers established that HIF signaling is indispensable for embryonic oxygen adaptation and vascular development [ , , ]. These findings position HIF as a systemic oxygen sensor embedded within redox-regulated physiological programs.
Finally, framing HIF regulation in redox terms helps reconcile long-standing paradoxes in hypoxia-targeted pharmacology and chemoresistance. Interventions that alter mitochondrial function, translation, or proteostasis may either suppress or enhance HIF signaling depending on the prevailing redox–oxygen context, a phenomenon not predictable from oxygen availability alone. This principle was articulated in early analyses by Acker and by Wartenberg and colleagues, linking redox-based oxygen sensing to adaptive tumor behavior and therapeutic resistance [ , ].

1.9

## HIF signaling and cancer and metabolic diseases
Hypoxia-inducible factors form the core regulatory axis governing cellular responses to hypoxic and oxidative stress, integrating redox signals with metabolic, vascular, and immune responses through genome-wide transcriptional reprogramming [ , , , ]. Although HIF-1α and HIF-2α are not classical oncogenes, extensive genetic, experimental, and clinical evidence supports a critical role for aberrant HIF activation in cancer and other hypoxia-associated diseases [ , , ]. Consequently, preventing pathological HIF signaling has emerged as a compelling target for therapeutic intervention.
A clear causal link between HIF dysregulation and tumorigenesis is illustrated by loss of function mutations in von Hippel–Lindau (VHL), which impair proteasomal degradation of hydroxylated HIF α subunits and result in constitutive HIF activation despite normoxia [ , , , ]. Germline VHL mutations predispose to hereditary cancers, while somatic VHL inactivation occurs in most sporadic cases (ccRCC) [ ]. Restoration of functional VHL suppresses tumorigenicity in VHL deficient renal cancer cells, whereas re expression of HIF 2α restores malignant growth, demonstrating a direct oncogenic role for sustained HIF signaling [ , ].
Beyond VHL disease, deficiencies in mitochondrial Krebs cycle enzymes, including succinate dehydrogenase and fumarate hydratase, promote accumulation of metabolites that inhibit PHDs, consequently facilitating HIF-α accumulation [ , ]. These metabolic perturbations, which are tightly linked to altered cellular redox balance, result in pseudohypoxic signaling and increased cancer susceptibility [ , ]. In animal models, fumarate hydratase inactivation induces a precancerous phenotype associated with chronic HIF pathway activation, highlighting the intersection of mitochondrial metabolism, redox homeostasis, and HIF regulation [ , ].
Importantly, dysregulated HIF signaling also contributes to the pathogenesis of metabolic diseases, including diabetes mellitus and its complications [ , ]. Chronic hyperglycaemia is associated with is associated with heightened reactive oxygen species levels originating from mitochondrial and cytosolic compartment, which enables HIF-1α accumulation by interfering with prolyl hydroxylase activity through oxygen-independent mechanisms [ , , ]. In insulin responsive tissues, sustained HIF-1 activation promotes a shift toward glycolytic metabolism, alters mitochondrial function, and contributes to insulin resistance by disrupting normal oxidative metabolism and redox balance [ , ].
In pancreatic β cells, excessive HIF-1α signaling - driven by hypoxia, glucotoxicity, or oxidative stress - impairs mitochondrial metabolism and ATP production, thereby compromising glucose stimulated insulin secretion [ , , ]. Moreover, HIF dependent metabolic reprogramming and redox dysregulation exacerbate endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress, linking hypoxic signaling to activation of the unfolded protein response (UPR), particularly through the IRE1α–XBP1 axis [ , , , ]. Sustained ER stress and maladaptive IRE1α signaling amplify inflammatory and apoptotic pathways in diabetic states [ , ]. In parallel, pathological HIF activation plays a key role in diabetic complications, including nephropathy, retinopathy, and impaired wound healing, largely by driving aberrant angiogenesis, fibrosis, and vascular dysfunction in hypoxic and inflamed tissues [ , , ]. These findings suggest that preventing chronic, maladaptive HIF activation may restore metabolic flexibility and improve tissue function in diabetes and its associated vascular complications.
Clinically, chronic elevation of HIF-1α is frequently detected in solid malignancies and is associated with angiogenic remodeling, aggressive disease progression, diminished therapeutic response, and poor clinical outcomes [ , ]. From a mechanistic standpoint, HIF activation drives tumor progression by coordinating transcriptional programs that promote glycolytic metabolism, angiogenic factor production, cell survival, proliferation, immune evasion, and maintenance of cancer stem like properties [ , , ]. These functions are intimately coupled to redox regulation, as HIFs both sense and remodel intracellular ROS levels through effects on mitochondrial activity, antioxidant pathways, and metabolic flux [ , ]. Notably, tumor hypoxia also induces ER stress, and cooperative signaling between HIFs and UPR pathways, encompassing IRE1α dependent XBP1 splicing and regulated IRE1 dependent decay (RIDD), that support tumor cell adaptation, angiogenic signaling, and immune suppression within the tumor microenvironment [ , , ].

1.10

## HIF regulation of oxidative stress and metabolism
Beyond cancer and metabolic disease, pathological HIF activation contributes to diverse conditions characterized by chronic hypoxia, oxidative stress, and inflammation [ ]. In pulmonary hypertension, both HIF-1α and HIF-2α orchestrate maladaptive vascular remodeling, inflammatory cell recruitment, and metabolic reprogramming reflecting cell-type–dependent regulatory programs, processes that are accompanied by ER stress and UPR activation in vascular cells [ , , ]. Similarly, in ischemic retinal disorders, excessive HIF signaling drives pathological neovascularization and vascular leakage, while ER stress and IRE1α signaling further modulate angiogenic and inflammatory responses, leading to progressive tissue damage and loss of function [ , ].
HIFs additionally regulate immune cell metabolism and function, particularly in myeloid populations [ , ]. HIF dependent glycolysis is essential for macrophage polarization toward pro inflammatory phenotypes, and hypoxia-induced HIF activation promotes macrophage survival during inflammatory stress [ , ]. ER stress pathways intersect with HIF signaling in these cells, with IRE1α XBP1 activity shaping cytokine production, metabolic rewiring, and immune function [ , ]. While these responses can be protective in acute settings, sustained or spatially dysregulated HIF and ER stress signaling may contribute to chronic inflammation, immune dysfunction, and metabolic imbalance in the tumor microenvironment as well as in metabolic diseases such as diabetes [ , ].
Despite the strong rationale for targeting HIF signaling, direct pharmacological inhibition remains challenging. As discussed in the following section, many existing compounds modulate HIF activity indirectly by affecting upstream signaling pathways, mitochondrial ROS production, protein synthesis, or transcriptional co-activator interactions, raising concerns regarding specificity and off-target effects.
Together, these findings position HIFs at the crossroads of hypoxia, redox biology, metabolism, ER stress, and disease pathogenesis, underscoring both the therapeutic potential and the complexity of selectively targeting HIF signaling.

## Strategies to prevent HIF signaling: reducing levels of HIF-α subunits
Multiple experimental studies provide evidence that reducing or eliminating HIF-α subunit expression can provide therapeutic benefit , particularly in cancer models [ , ] (see ). However, many compounds capable of lowering HIF-α protein levels do so only indirectly, often through pleiotropic mechanisms that compromise specificity and result in significant toxicity. These limitations have historically constrained clinical translation (see ). 
For many years, hypoxia-inducible factors were considered “undruggable” transcription factors, largely because they lack an obvious ligand-binding domain analogous to those found in steroid hormone receptors [ ]. This view changed in 2009, when crystallographic studies revealed a large internal cavity within the PAS-B domain of HIF-2α capable of binding small molecules and modulating HIF-2α:ARNT heterodimerization in vitro [ ]. This landmark discovery enabled rational screening and structure-based design of HIF-2–selective inhibitors, culminating in the development of PT2977 (belzutifan; WELIREG™).
Belzutifan became the first direct HIF inhibitor approved for clinical use (FDA 2021; EU conditional approval 2025) in von Hippel–Lindau disease–associated renal cell carcinoma, central nervous system hemangioblastomas, and pancreatic neuroendocrine tumors without an immediate surgical indication [ ]. Despite this success, the druggable PAS-B pocket exploited by belzutifan is not conserved in HIF-1α [ ], necessitating fundamentally different strategies for selective HIF-1α inhibition.
More broadly, consideration of HIF isoform switching and dynamic hypoxic signaling suggests that indirect or transient suppression of HIF-α accumulation often proves effective only in tightly controlled in vitro settings [ , , , ]. These approaches rarely translate into sustained in vivo efficacy. Consequently, strategies that directly suppress HIF-α expression at the mRNA or translational level have attracted significant interest.
2.1

## RNA-based approaches targeting HIF-α expression
Genetic and pharmacological studies using inducible RNA interference against HIF-1α or HIF-2α demonstrated potent inhibition of tumor growth in animal models [ , ]. Based on these findings, RNA-directed therapies - such as small interfering RNAs and antisense oligonucleotides - emerged as attractive strategies for isoform-specific suppression of HIF-α expression, despite ongoing challenges related to delivery, stability, and distribution [ ].
The most advanced example of this approach is EZN-2968 (also known as RO7070179), a synthetic antisense oligonucleotide targeting HIF1A mRNA. EZN-2968 is built on locked nucleic acid (LNA) chemistry, in which a methylene bridge between the 2′-oxygen and 4′-carbon of the ribose ring locks the sugar into a rigid conformation. This structural feature enhances base-pairing affinity, nuclease resistance, and in vivo stability relative to conventional oligonucleotides [ , , ].
EZN-2968 binds specifically to HIF1A mRNA, promoting transcript degradation and inhibiting translation [ , ]. In vitro, it potently suppresses HIF-1α protein accumulation with IC50 values in the low-nanomolar range (1–5 nM) under both normoxic and hypoxic conditions. Due to a three-nucleotide mismatch, its activity toward EPAS1 (HIF-2α) mRNA is minimal [ ]. In preclinical studies, EZN-2968 effectively attenuated transcription of HIF-1 target genes and inhibited hypoxia-driven angiogenesis in multiple cancer models, including prostate cancer, glioblastoma, and DU145 xenografts [ ].
Encouraged by these results, EZN-2968 entered phase I clinical trials in lymphoma and advanced solid tumors with liver metastases (NCT01120288). In these studies, EZN-2968 reduced HIF1A expression in 4 of 6 evaluable patients based on paired biopsies, with concomitant suppression of HIF-1 target genes in two cases and was generally well tolerated [ , ]. However, subsequent evaluation in a Phase I study of hepatocellular carcinoma did not confirm these effects, and the trial was discontinued early due to failure to achieve its primary endpoint of HIF1A suppression after one treatment cycle [ ]. Additional completed trials (NCT00466583; NCT02564614) have not published results, and no ongoing clinical development is currently reported.
Although EZN-2968 demonstrated target specificity, its limited clinical impact may reflect intrinsic biological constraints. HIF1A mRNA is inherently unstable under hypoxic conditions, with an estimated half-life in human endothelial cells of approximately 1.6 h [ ], which likely restricts the efficacy of antisense strategies unless the tumors harbor mutations that elevate transcript abundance or stability. Moreover, solid tumors undergo continuous oscillations between normoxia and varying degrees of hypoxia - conditions that are difficult to model experimentally and further complicate sustained transcript suppression [ ].
Nonetheless, these studies suggest that an analogous LNA-based strategy targeting HIF-2α may yield improved outcomes. EPAS1 mRNA is generally more stable [ , ] and associated with prolonged hypoxia in many solid tumors, where HIF-2 activity often correlates with adverse prognosis. Similarly, although HIF-3α represents a potential target, extensive transcript heterogeneity caused by alternative splicing may limit feasibility. In principle, targeting the shared β subunit (ARNT) could suppress all HIF complexes; however, such a strategy would lack isoform specificity and might compromise widespread physiological hypoxia responses, and remains unexplored.

2.2

## Small-molecule inhibitors reducing HIF-α accumulation
Among small-molecule compounds reported to suppress HIF-1α accumulation, PX-478 (S-2-amino-3-[4′-N,N-bis(chloroethyl)amino]phenyl propionic acid N-oxide dihydrochloride) has been studied most extensively. PX-478 demonstrates effective passage across the blood–brain barrier and reduces HIF-1α levels through multiple mechanisms, including inhibition of HIF1A transcription, suppression of translation, and interference with HIF-1α deubiquitination [ ].
Mechanistic interrogation indicates that PX-478 leaves HIF-1α protein stability largely unaltered, while inhibiting translational output through a nonspecific pathway that does not trigger the classical integrated stress response. This effect preferentially impacts hypoxia-associated proteins, including HIF-1α, and leads to reduced nuclear localization and diminished transcription of angiogenic target genes. Consequently, antitumor activity appears to be mediated primarily by anti-angiogenic effects rather than cytotoxicity [ ].
PX-478 suppresses HIF-1α accumulation in multiple human cancer cell lines with IC50 values between 10 and 50 μM [ ]. In vivo, PX-478 limits tumor growth in xenograft models, potentiates radiosensitivity, and enhances treatment efficacy when used in combination with chemotherapeutic agents such as gemcitabine in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma [ , , ]. A Phase I dose-escalation trial in advanced solid tumors (NCT00522652) demonstrated acceptable tolerability and dose-dependent suppression of HIF-1α in peripheral blood mononuclear cells [ ]. However, the absence of a clearly defined, selective mechanism of action remains a major limitation to further development.

2.3

## Pleiotropic inhibitors and topoisomerase-dependent HIF suppression
Several chemically unrelated compounds - including celastrol, cardiac glycosides, and topoisomerase inhibitors - also reduce HIF-α protein levels and transcriptional activity.
Celastrol and derivatives. Pleiotropic inhibitory effects on HIF-1α accumulation and transcriptional activity - including reduced HIF1A mRNA levels - have been reported for celastrol, a natural pentacyclic triterpenoid [ ]. One study proposed that celastrol decreases both HIF1A transcript abundance and HIF-1α protein stability by disrupting its interaction with the molecular chaperone Hsp90, thereby limiting nuclear HIF complex formation and transcriptional output [ ]. However, these conclusions were derived primarily from steady-state in vitro analyses, and neither HIF1A mRNA nor HIF-1α protein stability was directly measured. Conversely, an independent investigation indicated that celastrol inhibits HIF-1α at the translational level without affecting transcript levels [ ], whereas its influence on HIF-2α has yet to be explored.
Celastrol exerts pleiotropic effects across multiple biological processes, including inhibition of mitochondrial lipid peroxidation, suppression of TNF-α–induced NF-κB signaling, inhibition of topoisomerase II (IC50 ≈ 7.4 μM), and interference with Hsp90 function [ ]. This polypharmacology confers a high risk of off-target effects and toxicity, reflected in a narrow therapeutic window [ ]. Its ability to inhibit topoisomerase II may underlie, at least in part, its indirect effects on HIF-1α levels, complicating mechanistic interpretation and clinical translation.
Nevertheless, celastrol served as a scaffold for medicinal chemistry optimization. Notably, compound C6, generated by modification of the C-29 carboxyl group, exhibits improved HIF-1α inhibitory potency (IC50 ≈ 50 nM; ∼5-fold stronger than celastrol) and substantially reduced cytotoxicity (∼22-fold lower) [ ]. Despite this encouraging preclinical efficacy, C6 has not yet entered clinical development.

2.4

## Cardiac glycosides and translational repression of HIF-α
A high-throughput screen of the Hopkins Drug Library [ ] identified 20 compounds that significantly reduced HIF-1 transcriptional activity, 11 of which were cardiac glycosides, including digoxin, ouabain, proscillaridin A, and digitoxin. In HepB cells, these agents reduced both HIF-1α and HIF-2α protein levels without altering HIF1A mRNA, indicating post-transcriptional regulation [ ].
Mechanistically, digoxin inhibits global translation in an mTOR-independent manner, with disproportionate suppression of HIF-1α synthesis. Although cardiac glycosides can inhibit topoisomerases I and II, genetic silencing of these enzymes did not recapitulate HIF-1α suppression, suggesting an indirect mechanism [ ].
In vivo, digoxin inhibits tumor growth and suppresses lung metastasis in orthotopic breast cancer models [ , ]. Clinically, digoxin has been evaluated across multiple cancers, frequently as part of drug-repurposing strategies. A recently completed study (NCT03928210) reported reduced circulating tumor cell cluster size, a known predictor of poor prognosis, with transcriptomic data linking this effect to diminished HIF activity [ ]. However, other trials - including a Phase II study assessing HIF-1 expression after two weeks of therapy (NCT01763931) - have not yet reported outcomes, highlighting persistent uncertainties.

2.5

## Topoisomerase inhibitors as noncanonical HIF modulators
Topoisomerase inhibitors have emerged as unexpected, noncanonical modulators of HIF activity [ ], opening an attractive, though mechanistically challenging - avenue for drug repurposing aimed at suppressing hypoxia-driven transcription. Topoisomerase I and II enzymes regulate DNA topology by catalyzing transient cleavage and religation of DNA strands during the normal cell cycle, thereby relieving torsional stress generated during replication and transcription. They also regulate basal DNA supercoiling, thereby facilitating protein–DNA interactions and safeguarding genomic stability [ ]. Pharmacological inhibition of topoisomerases stabilizes DNA–topoisomerase cleavage complexes, resulting in the accumulation of unrepaired single- and double-strand DNA breaks, stalled replication forks, replication stress, and highly genotoxic lesions that ultimately activate apoptosis and cell death pathways [ ]. These properties underpin the clinical utility of topoisomerase inhibitors as both antimicrobial and anticancer agents. Representative examples include doxorubicin (an anthracycline and topoisomerase II inhibitor [ ]), etoposide (topoisomerase II inhibitor [ ]), ciprofloxacin (a fluoroquinolone targeting topoisomerase II [ ]), and irinotecan (a camptothecin-derived topoisomerase I inhibitor [ ]).
Intriguingly, numerous independent studies have demonstrated that both topoisomerase I and II inhibitors - including camptothecin, topotecan, EZN-2208 (an active metabolite of irinotecan), MFTZ-1, and NSC-644221 - robustly suppress HIF-1–dependent transcription [ , , ]. This inhibition is frequently accompanied by decreased accumulation of HIF-1α or HIF-2α protein, despite unchanged HIF1A transcript levels [ , , ], indicating a post-transcriptional mode of regulation that is distinct from canonical oxygen-dependent degradation or transcriptional control of the HIF1A gene. Accumulating evidence now supports the conclusion that inhibition of HIF signaling by these agents occurs in a topoisomerase I- or II-dependent manner [ , ] but is largely independent of their canonical cytotoxic effects [ ]. Importantly, DNA replication is not required for HIF inhibition: pharmacological blockade of replication abolishes camptothecin-induced cytotoxicity yet does not reverse suppression of HIF-1 activity [ ]. This mechanistic uncoupling is particularly striking given that HIF-1α itself negatively regulates DNA replication by suppressing origin firing, reducing DNA synthesis, and inducing cell-cycle arrest in multiple cell types [ ].
Support for the concept of repurposing topoisomerase inhibitors as HIF modulators emerged from high-throughput screening of the NCI Diversity Set - comprising approximately 2000 structurally diverse small molecules - which identified several camptothecin analogs as selective suppressors of HIF-1–dependent transcription [ ]. Among these was topotecan (TPT; NSC-609699), a clinically approved topoisomerase I inhibitor, further stimulating interest in exploiting established anticancer agents as regulators of hypoxia signaling rather than purely as cytotoxic drugs. Despite incomplete understanding of the underlying molecular mechanism, current evidence supports the conclusion that topoisomerase inhibitors regulate HIF mainly at the level of protein synthesis. In hypoxia-exposed U251 human glioma cells, topotecan does not affect HIF1A mRNA abundance but instead reduces the rate of HIF-1α protein synthesis, independently of the PI3K–Akt–mTOR pathway [ ]. Importantly, topotecan does not inhibit global protein synthesis or the accumulation of unrelated proteins, arguing against nonspecific translational toxicity and supporting a selective mechanism of HIF-α regulation.
Accumulating evidence points to the presence of an internal ribosome entry site (IRES) within HIF1A mRNA, allowing efficient translation to be maintained under hypoxic conditions [ ]. One plausible hypothesis is that topotecan interferes with IRES-dependent translational control of HIF-1α, thereby selectively limiting HIF-1α protein synthesis during hypoxia. Nevertheless, it remains to be determined whether other IRES-containing mRNAs are similarly affected or whether this effect exhibits transcript specificity. This interpretation warrants caution, as topotecan has also been reported to inhibit global protein SUMOylation [ ]. Because SUMOylation enhances both the stability and transcriptional activity of HIF-1α [ ], altered SUMO conjugation may contribute to reduced HIF-1α protein levels. Consequently, the relative contributions of impaired translation versus post-translational destabilization of HIF-1α remain unresolved and require further mechanistic clarification.
Further evidence indicates that hypoxia-induced HIF-1α accumulation is reduced by both topotecan and the topoisomerase II inhibitor etoposide in a manner dependent on dose and exposure time. Notably, daily administration at low concentrations was more effective than a single high-dose treatment, suggesting a schedule-dependent component to HIF inhibition. These conditions were associated with diminished HIF-1α abundance alongside increased proteasomal degradation and attenuated Akt phosphorylation, indicating that HIF stability is modulated through signaling-dependent mechanisms [ ].
Mechanistic heterogeneity among topoisomerase inhibitors is further highlighted by studies of 3EZ,20Ac-ingenol, a catalytic topoisomerase I inhibitor. Under hypoxic conditions, this compound induced p53 phosphorylation and promoted HIF-1α degradation in Panc-1 pancreatic cancer cells, whereas no such effects were observed in Jeko-1 hematological cancer cells, underscoring cell-type specificity in HIF regulation [ ].
Divergent effects are also observed among topoisomerase II inhibitors. Mitoxantrone, a clinically used anticancer agent, markedly reduced hypoxia-induced HIF-1α accumulation in a dose- and time-dependent manner, whereas doxorubicin and etoposide failed to do so. Mitoxantrone-mediated suppression of HIF-1α occurred independently of transcriptional repression and enhanced degradation but was instead linked to translational inhibition in an mTOR-independent manner [ ]. Intriguingly, mitoxantrone increased polysome-associated HIF1A mRNA while simultaneously reducing HIF-1α protein synthesis, suggesting inhibition at the level of translation elongation. Given mitoxantrone's ability to intercalate into GC-rich nucleic acid sequences and stabilize secondary RNA structures, direct interaction with HIF1A mRNA has been proposed [ ].
Additional complexity arises from studies of acridine-based compounds. Acriflavine was initially proposed to suppress HIF signaling by preventing HIF-α dimerization with HIF-1β; subsequent work attributed this effect primarily to its topoisomerase I inhibitory activity [ ]. Importantly, not all acridine-class topoisomerase inhibitors suppress HIF signaling. For example, compound C-1305 lacks HIF inhibitory activity [ , ] and may even enhance tumor vascularization and angiogenesis [ ], indicating functional divergence within this chemical class.
An alternative post-transcriptional mechanism suggests that camptothecin induces HIF1A-targeting microRNAs, resulting in miRNA-mediated translational repression of HIF-1α. However, this model remains insufficiently validated, as the supporting data largely rely on hypoxia-mimetic conditions and reporter assays, without in-depth biochemical characterization of miRNA–HIF1A interactions [ ].
Finally, an elegant DNA-centric model has been proposed based on the sequence specificity of topoisomerase I cleavage. The consensus hypoxia response element (5′-RCGTG-3′) contains the dinucleotide 5′-TG, which is frequently observed at camptothecin-stabilized TopI cleavage sites. Camptothecin-mediated stabilization of TopI cleavage complexes at or near HREs may therefore interfere with HIF-1 binding to its target DNA sequences [ ]. Although HREs are not canonical TopI cleavage sites, they may become functional targets in the absence of higher-affinity sequences [ ]. Accordingly, this model rationalizes the selective suppression of only a fraction of the HIF transcriptional program and suggests that gene-specific sensitivity to topoisomerase inhibitors may depend on local DNA sequence context.
Consistent with preclinical observations, inhibitors of topoisomerases I and II suppress HIF-1α accumulation and reduce HIF-2α protein levels in xenograft mouse models [ , ]. In murine models of chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL), EZN-2208 - a water-soluble polyethylene glycol conjugate of SN-38, the active metabolite of irinotecan was reported to enhance the therapeutic efficacy of fludarabine, providing early in vivo support for combinatorial strategies targeting hypoxia-associated pathways [ ]. Early clinical evidence supporting HIF modulation has also emerged. A Phase I pilot trial of oral topotecan demonstrated reduced HIF-1α expression in vivo, establishing proof-of-concept that topoisomerase inhibition can attenuate H-mediated signaling in human tumors [ ]. In addition, a Phase I trial evaluating topotecan in combination with the proteasome inhibitor bortezomib in advanced solid tumors (NCT00388089) included plasma measurements of hypoxia-inducible proteins as a secondary endpoint [ ]. The study is listed as completed, although corresponding results have not been reported to date.
EZN-2208 has since been investigated in multiple early-phase clinical studies, including six Phase I and Phase II trials encompassing a range of solid malignancies, such as metastatic breast cancer (NCT01036113) and metastatic colorectal cancer (NCT00931840). Across these studies, EZN-2208 was generally well tolerated. In patients with metastatic triple-negative breast cancer, objective response and clinical benefit rates of 22.5% and 36.7%, respectively, were reported [ ]. In contrast, in advanced colorectal cancer, combination therapy with EZN-2208 and cetuximab did not confer a survival advantage compared with irinotecan plus cetuximab, indicating limited efficacy in this setting [ ]. Importantly, none of these trials included direct assessment of HIF signaling or HIF-α protein levels in patient samples, precluding conclusions regarding on-target hypoxia pathway inhibition.
Additional clinical experience has been gained with CRLX101, a nanoparticle-based formulation composed of camptothecin conjugated to linear cyclodextrin-polyethylene glycol polymers [ , ]. In a Phase II trial involving patients with ovarian cancer, CRLX101 demonstrated acceptable tolerability when administered either as a single agent or in combination with bevacizumab. As monotherapy, CRLX101 achieved an overall response rate of 11%, which increased to 18% when combined with bevacizumab [ ]. However, as with prior studies of topoisomerase inhibitors, these trials did not evaluate HIF-1 or HIF-2 protein levels or transcriptional activity in tumor or plasma samples, leaving unresolved whether clinical responses correlated with hypoxia pathway suppression.
Notably, existing clinical trials of topoisomerase inhibitors have predominantly focused on combination strategies designed to maximize cytotoxic efficacy, rather than explicitly interrogating their ability to inhibit HIF signaling. Accordingly, treatment regimens were optimized to achieve high peak drug concentrations over short periods, prioritizing tumor cell killing while minimizing systemic toxicity. Under such conditions, any inhibition of HIF-1 signaling is likely to be transient, and thus of uncertain biological or therapeutic significance. In contrast, in vitro studies employing prolonged low-dose exposure suggest that durable suppression of HIF-1α accumulation requires sustained target engagement. These findings indicate that the temporal dynamics of topoisomerase inhibition may critically influence HIF modulation and therapeutic outcome [ ].
Taken together, these findings emphasize the need to explore alternative dosing paradigms that enable sustained, non-cytotoxic inhibition of topoisomerase activity while preserving suppression of HIF signaling. Whether prolonged or low-intensity exposure to these agents can achieve durable HIF inhibition in the absence of overt toxicity will be a key determinant of their potential utility as modulators of hypoxia-driven transcriptional programs.
Importantly, further work is required to delineate HIF-specific inhibitory mechanisms from the broader pharmacological effects of topoisomerase inhibitors. This distinction is essential, as a strategy predicated on HIF repression would be undermined if the same molecular pathway were responsible for both HIF inhibition and cytotoxicity. In such a case, a separable therapeutic window would be unlikely, limiting the feasibility of dissociating hypoxia signaling modulation from genotoxic stress. Collectively, this data support a model in which topoisomerase inhibitors influence HIF activity predominantly through translation-centered mechanisms that are mechanistically distinct from their canonical DNA damage–associated effects. This underappreciated intersection between DNA topology control, translational regulation, and hypoxia-responsive signaling suggests that modulation of HIF may represent a previously unrecognized property of certain DNA-interacting agents. Nevertheless, rigorous mechanistic resolution of HIF-specific effects versus generalized stress responses remains critical for evaluating whether selective, non-cytotoxic targeting of hypoxia signaling can be achieved within this drug class.

2.6

## Decreasing HIF-α stability
An alternative strategy to attenuate HIF signaling involves destabilization of the HIF-α subunit. Several small molecules have been reported to attenuate HIF-1α protein accumulation through mechanisms that enhance degradation or interfere with stabilizing pathways. Decursin, a natural coumarin derivative, was shown to reduce HIF-1α protein levels and HIF-1–dependent transcriptional activity in hypoxia-exposed human cancer cells. This outcome was associated with increased ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation of HIF-1α, resulting in attenuated hypoxia-induced transcriptional activity [ ].
In a separate high-throughput screening effort, the small molecule 32-134D was identified as suppressing HIF-α accumulation in hepatocellular carcinoma cell lines. Treatment with this compound reduced HIF-1α and, to a lesser extent, HIF-2α protein levels without altering HIF1A or EPAS1 mRNA expression. Proteasome inhibition abrogated this effect, suggesting enhanced proteasomal turnover of HIF-α subunits, although direct measurements of protein half-life were not performed [ ]. Using the same screening strategy, the authors subsequently identified mebendazole (MBZ), an FDA-approved antiparasitic agent, as a compound capable of reducing HIF-1α, HIF-2α, and HIF-1β protein levels independently of transcript abundance [ ]. Whether these effects reflect enhanced degradation or dysregulated translation remains unresolved.
Paradoxically, bortezomib, a clinically approved proteasome inhibitor used in multiple myeloma, has been repeatedly associated with suppression of HIF-1 signaling, despite the expectation that proteasome inhibition would stabilize HIF-α subunits [ , , ]. Accumulating evidence indicates that this counterintuitive effect arises from proteasome-independent mechanisms that primarily target HIF-1α synthesis, activation, and subcellular localization rather than protein degradation [ , ]. Research reports support a model in which bortezomib attenuates hypoxia-induced HIF-1α accumulation by targeting the Akt/mTOR/p70S6K/S6 ribosomal protein axis, a signaling pathway that facilitates HIF-1α translation [ ]. This is consistent with earlier work identifying translational repression of HIF-1α as a key effect of bortezomib [ ]. In addition to Akt/mTOR-dependent control, translational repression of HIF-1α by bortezomib also involves phosphorylation of eukaryotic initiation factor-2α (eIF2α), a well-established mediator of stress-induced translational arrest [ ]. Phosphorylation of eIF2α has been shown to contribute to bortezomib-mediated repression of HIF-1α synthesis, strengthening the interpretation that bortezomib exerts its effects largely through inhibition of HIF-1α protein synthesis. Collectively, these data indicate that bortezomib suppresses HIF-1α at the level of protein synthesis through convergent attenuation of Akt/mTOR signaling and activation of stress-responsive translational control mechanisms.
Furthermore, bortezomib treatment resulted in impaired phosphorylation of p44/42 MAPK (ERK1/2), which manifested as a substantial decrease in nuclear HIF-1α coupled with redistribution of the protein toward the cytoplasmic fraction under hypoxic conditions [ ]. Importantly, HIF-1 transcriptional activity was suppressed not only by bortezomib but also by the MAPK‐pathway inhibitor PD98059, even though total HIF-1α protein abundance remained unaffected. This demonstrates that MAPK signaling regulates HIF-1α activity primarily through control of its subcellular localization, rather than through modulation of protein abundance [ ].
Previous studies across multiple tumor types including breast cancer, plasma cell leukemia, or multiple myeloma have reported variable effects of bortezomib on MAPK signaling [ , , ], underscoring the context-dependent nature of this interaction. Notably, MAPK-dependent regulation of HIF-1α has consistently been reported to occur independently of changes in overall HIF-1α protein, emphasizing the importance of subcellular trafficking as a dominant regulatory layer. These findings provide a coherent mechanistic explanation for prior reports in which bortezomib attenuated HIF-1 transcriptional activity even in settings where HIF-1α protein accumulation was observed. In this context, previously proposed mechanisms involving impaired recruitment of the transcriptional co-activator p300 - whether through factor inhibiting HIF (FIH)-dependent or FIH-independent pathways - may be secondary or even dispensable when nuclear accumulation of HIF-1α is itself strongly inhibited [ , , ].
Collectively, these data indicate that bortezomib suppresses HIF-1 signaling primarily through translational repression and MAPK-dependent control of nuclear localization, rather than through effects on HIF-1α protein stability. These mechanisms reconcile prior discrepancies in the literature and establish subcellular trafficking and translational control as dominant determinants of HIF-1α activity under conditions of proteasome inhibition [ ]. Clinically, several hundred active or recruiting trials are currently evaluating bortezomib alone or in combination with other anticancer agents. Of particular relevance to hypoxia-targeted therapeutic strategies, one clinical trial (NCT01522872) is assessing the hypoxia-activated prodrug in combination with bortezomib and additional agents in relapsed or refractory multiple myeloma. As a secondary endpoint, this study evaluates the relationship between bone marrow hypoxia and therapeutic response, providing a clinical framework to explore hypoxia-dependent treatment effects [ ].
HIF-1α stability can also be indirectly modulated through disruption of its interaction with molecular chaperones. Heat shock protein 90 (HSP90) stabilizes HIF-1α under hypoxia and in tumors, and pharmacological inhibition of HSP90 promotes HIF-1α destabilization [ ]. Ganetespib (STA-9090), a small-molecule HSP90 inhibitor, selectively decreases HIF-1α protein levels and suppresses tumor growth in vitro and in mouse models [ , , ]. Although ganetespib has been tested in multiple clinical trials, modulation of intratumoral HIF activity has not been systematically reported, and trials specifically incorporating HIF endpoints remain limited [ , ]. Geldanamycin was assigned similar mechanism of Hsp90-related HIF-1α levels reduction [ ].
Epigenetic modifiers represent another class of agents capable of destabilizing HIF-α. Vorinostat (suberoylanilide hydroxamic acid), a pan-histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor, exerts broad suppressive effects on HIF signaling, resulting in robust reductions in HIF-1α protein abundance [ , ]. Clinically, vorinostat is an FDA-approved therapy for cutaneous T-cell lymphoma [ ]. Mechanistically, vorinostat acts at multiple regulatory levels: it inhibits HIF-1α translation, impairs nuclear accumulation, and promotes ubiquitination-dependent proteasomal degradation, collectively suppressing HIF-1 transcriptional activity under hypoxia [ , ]. Vorinostat similarly reduces HIF-2α protein levels, indicating a general role for HDAC activity in maintaining HIF-α stability and function [ ]. While clinical responses observed in combination studies are compatible with suppression of HIF-dependent angiogenic pathways [ ], direct assessment of HIF modulation in patient tumors has not been reported.
Redox-regulated pathways also contribute to HIF-α stability. PX-12, an inhibitor of thioredoxin-1 (Trx-1), has been shown to reduce HIF-1α protein levels along with VEGF expression in cancer cell lines or xenograft models [ , , ]. Mechanistic studies suggest that PX-12 promotes HIF-1α ubiquitination through induction of spermidine/spermine N1-acetyltransferase 1, independently of Trx-1 inhibition [ ]. However, clinical evaluation of PX-12 failed to demonstrate beneficial effects on VEGF suppression or tumor progression [ , ] and no active clinical trials are currently ongoing.
Metabolic modulation represents an additional approach to destabilize HIF-1α. 3-oxoglutarate (DE-3-oxo), an analog of 2-oxoglutarate that is PHDs cofactor, was able to reduce HIF-1α protein levels in normoxic cancer cells independent of PHD -driven proteasomal degradation [ ]. The compound, however, was not effective under hypoxia (1% O2), and showed some effect under severe hypoxia (0.1%). It has been proposed that DE-3-oxo increases the level of cellular acetoacetate, a ketone body. Acetoacetate can be converted into acetyl-CoA and enter the Krebs cycle to be oxidized in the mitochondria. An abundance of intracellular acetyl-CoA might inhibit glycolysis and lead to the so-called Randle cycle, a glucose-fatty acid cycle, which is then accompanied by decreased levels of HIF-1α to slow down glycolysis. Support for this model comes from studies demonstrating that acetoacetate constrains cancer cell growth by restricting reliance on glucose as a metabolic fuel [ ], while ketone body supplementation in pancreatic cancer systems has been linked to dampened glycolytic activity, enhanced apoptotic signaling, and reduced cachexia [ , ]. An alternative hypothesis proposes that acetyl-CoA functions as a metabolic signaling molecule influencing HIF-1α abundance [ ]. However, this study needs further evaluation if the DE-3-oxo could actually work in physiologically relevant hypoxic conditions that will actually represent mild hypoxia (1% O2) in their studies, where notably no HIF inhibitory effects were observed.
Furthermore, limitation of mitochondrial respiratory activity in hypoxic cells has been established to promote intracellular oxygen redistribution from mitochondria to the cytosol, thereby restoring prolyl hydroxylase activity and accelerating HIF-1α degradation [ , , , ]. Consistent with this mechanism, both pharmacological inhibition of mitochondrial respiration and mitochondrial dysfunction have been reported to destabilize HIF-1α under hypoxia [ , ]. Although oxygen redistribution has been proposed as a driver of the transition from HIF-1- to HIF-2-dependent transcriptional programs, this model also suggests that inhibitors of oxidative phosphorylation OXPHOS could be exploited to suppress hypoxia-induced HIF-1α accumulation or accelerate the onset of the HIF switch. Indeed, treatment with mitochondrial inhibitors - including rotenone, oligomycin, the complex I inhibitor IACS-010759, mitochondrial uncouplers, and clinically approved agents such as atovaquone and metformin, has been associated with reduced HIF-1α protein levels under hypoxia [ , , , , , , ]. In contrast, other studies indicate that mitochondrial reactive ROS production is needed for HIF-1α stabilization, even in the absence of fully functional oxidative phosphorylation [ , ]. Specifically, mitochondrial ROS contribute to HIF-1α accumulation independently of respiratory activity, highlighting the paradoxical role of mitochondria in regulating hypoxic signaling [ , ]. Overall, these findings support the conclusion that the impact of mitochondrial inhibition on HIF-1α stability is highly context dependent and influenced by the balance between cytosolic oxygen availability and ROS-mediated signaling.
Importantly, the efficacy of this strategy is expected to be strongly oxygen dependent. Under severe hypoxia or anoxia, cytosolic oxygen levels may remain insufficient to restore prolyl hydroxylase activity even following mitochondrial inhibition, thereby limiting HIF-1α destabilization [ ]. Moreover, sustained intracellular oxygen redistribution has been reported to favor prolonged stabilization of HIF-2α [ ], raising the possibility that this approach may be suboptimal in tumors that have undergone a HIF-1 to HIF-2 switch or that rely predominantly on HIF-2–driven transcriptional programs. As monotherapies, inhibitors of OXPHOS generally display limited efficacy, as they preferentially induce cell death in cancer cell subsets that are highly dependent on mitochondrial respiration and lack the metabolic flexibility to switch to alternative energy-producing pathways. Nevertheless, OXPHOS inhibitors remain attractive components of combination therapeutic strategies, where they have been shown to enhance antitumor activity and sensitize tumors to chemotherapy and radiotherapy [ ]. Thus, while mitochondrial inhibition represents a compelling strategy to modulate hypoxia-associated signaling, its therapeutic utility is likely constrained by oxygen availability, mitochondrial ROS dynamics, and tumor-specific reliance on distinct HIF isoforms.
Furthermore, a pilot screen of the National Cancer Institute (NCI) Diversity Set (2000 compounds) identified two novel small-molecule hits, NSC-134754 and NSC-643735, that differentially inhibit HIF-1 transcriptional activity and HIF-1α protein accumulation in response to hypoxia. Notably, NSC-134754 suppresses both hypoxia-driven and growth factor–dependent mechanisms regulating HIF-1α expression, suggesting convergence on shared regulatory nodes controlling HIF-1α stability or translation [ ]. However, the precise molecular mechanisms underlying these effects, as well as their clinical relevance, remain to be defined and warrant further investigation.
Finally, additional small-molecule classes, including 2,2-dimethylbenzopyran derivatives [ , ] and chalcones [ ], have been reported to suppress HIF signaling. A recently developed chalcone incorporating a dimethylbenzopyran moiety (compound 5d) reduced HIF-1α expression and HIF-dependent transcriptional activity in hypoxic Hep3B cells and in xenograft models at micromolar concentrations [ ]. Although preclinical studies suggest favorable tolerability, the molecular details governing HIF-1α inhibition by this compound remain unresolved. Collectively, all these discussed above studies highlight the mechanistic diversity and context dependence of HIF-α levels regulation, underscoring the need to distinguish direct effects on protein stability from indirect modulation arising from transcriptional, translational, metabolic, and redox-sensitive pathways.

## Inhibitors of HIF transcriptional activity
A second major strategy to suppress hypoxia signaling is the direct inhibition of HIF transcriptional activity. This can be achieved at multiple regulatory levels, including disruption of HIF-α/HIF-β heterodimerization, inhibition of nuclear accumulation, interference with recruitment of essential transcriptional co-activators such as p300/CBP, and direct blockade of HIF binding to hypoxia-response elements (HREs) within target gene promoters (see and ).
One of the earliest compounds reported to directly interfere with HIF–DNA interactions is echinomycin, a bis-intercalator peptide quinoxaline produced by Streptomyces species [ ]. Echinomycin preferentially intercalates into DNA at sites containing the central 5′-CG-3′ dinucleotide, a critical recognition element within the HIF consensus HRE sequence (5′-ACGT-3′), thereby preventing binding of both HIF-1α and HIF-2α to their cognate response elements [ , ]. However, this sequence preference is not unique to HIF target genes. Echinomycin also binds DNA motifs recognized by other transcription factors, such as AP-1, NF-κB, or members of the Myc/Max family, whose E-box binding site (5′-CACGTG-3′) overlaps substantially with the HIF HRE core [ , ]. As a result, despite its potent ability to block HIF–DNA interactions, echinomycin exhibits limited specificity and a high propensity for off-target transcriptional effects. Consistent with these limitations, clinical trials conducted in the 1990s across multiple tumor types revealed substantial toxicity, most commonly gastrointestinal, with minimal antitumor efficacy [ , , ]. Consequently, echinomycin is not currently under active clinical investigation.
More selective strategies to prevent HIF–DNA binding have employed synthetic DNA-binding ligands. Hairpin polyamides and polyamides–acridine conjugates have been shown to inhibit protein–DNA complexes in a sequence-dependent manner [ , ]. A pyrrole–imidazole polyamide specifically formulated to target the HRE within the VEGF promoter binds the sequence 5′-WTWCGW-3′ (where W = A or T) and effectively blocks HIF-1 binding to this site in vitro [ ]. Treatment with this compound reduced VEGF mRNA and protein levels in cultured HeLa cells in a dose-dependent and sequence-specific manner. Genome-wide expression analyses further revealed coordinated downregulation of multiple hypoxia-inducible genes, supporting targeted disruption of HIF-dependent transcriptional programs [ ].
Several widely used chemotherapeutic agents have also been reported to inhibit HIF activity through effects on DNA binding. Anthracyclines, including doxorubicin, daunorubicin, epirubicin, and idarubicin, disrupt binding of HIF-1α and HIF-2α to DNA [ , ]. In tumor-bearing mice, chronic administration of anthracyclines at low doses suppresses HIF-1-dependend gene expression and angiogenesis [ ]. However, tumor cells can partially compensate for this inhibition by inducing HIF-1α expression. In normoxic cancer cell lines, doxorubicin-induced HIF-1α accumulation was shown to depend on STAT1 activity, leading to increase in inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) and nitric oxide production [ ]. Pharmacological or genetic inhibition of STAT1 or nitric oxide synthesis was sufficient to attenuate this compensatory HIF-1α accumulation, highlighting a redox-linked feedback mechanism that counteracts HIF transcriptional inhibition [ ].
Another class of compounds suppresses HIF transcriptional activity by disrupting interactions with transcriptional co-activators. The fungal metabolite chetomin destabilizes the CH1 domain of p300, a co-activator critical for optimal transcriptional activity of both HIF-1 and HIF-2 [ , ]. Disruption of HIF–p300 interactions by chetomin results in marked repression of HIF-dependent transcription in vitro and in murine xenograft models [ , , ]. Additional studies suggest that chetomin may also interfere with HSP90 binding to HIF-1α, thereby reducing HIF-1α accumulation independently of p300 disruption [ ]. These observations indicate that chetomin may act through multiple, partially overlapping mechanisms, warranting further mechanistic clarification.
A related but mechanistically complex agent is 3-(5′-hydroxymethyl-2′-furyl)-1-benzylindazole (YC-1), which interferes with HIF transcriptional activity, potentially by disrupting p300 recruitment [ , , ]. Notably, the timing of YC-1 administration strongly influences therapeutic outcome. Administration post radiotherapy reduces radiation-induced HIF-1 activation and attenuates tumor expansion in mouse xenograft models of cervical cancer, whereas pretreatment exacerbates hypoxia and diminishes radiotherapy efficacy [ ]. In addition, YC-1 is known to interact with several non-HIF targets, complicating attribution of its biological effects solely to HIF inhibition [ , ]. Additional small-molecule inhibitors of HIF signaling have been proposed, including benzo[d]isoxazole derivatives [ ] and cardenolides [ , ]. While these compounds suppress HIF-dependent reporter activity in vitro, detailed biochemical validation of their effects on HIF-α abundance, stability, and translational control is limited, underscoring the need for a more rigorous mechanistic evaluation.
Although the compounds discussed above are commonly classified as inhibitors of HIF transcriptional activity, emerging evidence indicates that many of these agents also intersect with upstream translational control mechanisms that govern HIF-α abundance or competence. Importantly, disruption of HIF–DNA binding or co-activator recruitment frequently coincides with activation of stress-responsive signaling pathways that converge on translational repression, including inhibition of mTOR-dependent protein synthesis and induction of eIF2α phosphorylation. These effects are especially relevant under hypoxic conditions, where global translational remodeling represents a dominant adaptive response. Thus, transcriptional inhibition and translational repression of HIF are not necessarily mechanistically independent, but often reflect coordinated responses to metabolic, redox, and proteotoxic stress. This convergence suggests that effective suppression of hypoxia signaling may arise not from isolated blockade of HIF–DNA interactions, but from broader perturbation of the translational and signaling networks that sustain HIF function under stress.
In contrast to the modest clinical efficacy observed with transcriptional inhibitors targeting HIF-1, therapeutic strategies that specifically disrupt HIF-2–dependent transcription - most notably through selective interference with HIF-2α/HIF-1β heterodimerization - have shown pronounced clinical success. This approach culminated in the approval of the HIF-2α antagonist PT2977 (belzutifan; WELIREG™), which received FDA approval in 2021 for the treatment of von Hippel–Lindau (VHL) disease–associated renal cell carcinoma, central nervous system hemangioblastomas, and pancreatic neuroendocrine tumors that do not require immediate surgical intervention, followed by conditional approval in the European Union in 2025 [ ].
Belzutifan and its predecessor PT2385 bind to a hydrophobic cavity within the PAS-B domain of HIF-2α, allosterically preventing heterodimerization with HIF-1β and formation of a transcriptionally active complex [ ]. In preclinical models, PT2385 suppressed HIF-2 signaling in ccRCC cell lines as well as xenografts, inducing marked tumor regression without cardiovascular toxicity characteristic of VEGF-pathway inhibition [ ]. Clinical evaluation revealed on-target inhibition of HIF-2α activity in both tumor and normal tissues, reflected by reduced erythropoietin levels and treatment-associated anemia that was predominantly low-grade and manageable [ ].
Second-generation HIF-2α antagonists designed to achieve improved pharmacokinetic performance, including PT2977 (MK-6482), demonstrated robust clinical activity in Phase I–III trials, with durable responses observed across ccRCC, CNS hemangioblastomas, and pancreatic neuroendocrine tumors [ , , , , ]. Continued efforts are now focused on developing next-generation HIF-2α inhibitors with enhanced selectivity and potency, such as YQ-0629 and casdatifan (AB521), both of which have shown promising preclinical and early clinical activity [ , , ].

## Off-target and safety considerations of HIF inhibition
Chronic inhibition of HIF signaling produces a characteristic, largely mechanism-based safety profile. The most frequent on-target toxicity with HIF-2 inhibitors (PT2385; belzutifan) is anemia from suppression of renal/hepatic erythropoietin. Anemia occurred commonly in early trials and, during prolonged belzutifan therapy, occasionally required transfusion or erythropoiesis-stimulating agents, hence the need for routine hematological monitoring [ , , ]. Hypoxia has also been reported, more often in early, heavily pre-treated cohorts—potentially reflecting impaired carotid-body ventilatory responses, a recognized HIF-2 function; incidence fell in later trials with healthier participants [ , ]. Notably, the cardiovascular toxicity (hypertension, thromboembolism, cardiomyopathy) typical of direct VEGF blockade is not prominent with belzutifan, suggesting a favorable risk profile for HIF-2 inhibition, possibly owing to partial rather than complete VEGF pathway suppression, though the mechanism remains to be defined. In contrast, HIF-1α antisense inhibition (RO7070179/EZN-2968) has shown dose-dependent, reversible hepatotoxicity (ALT/AST elevations) in advanced hepatocellular carcinoma, likely exacerbated by limited hepatic reserve, and long-term safety data remain sparse [ ]. Overall, adverse events with HIF-targeted agents are mechanistically informative and context-dependent, supporting patient selection, biomarker-guided monitoring, and agent-specific risk mitigation during prolonged therapy [ ].

## Conclusions
Collectively, pharmacological and genetic strategies aimed at suppressing HIF signaling demonstrate that regulation of HIF-α abundance, synthesis, stability, and transcriptional competence is highly interconnected rather than mechanistically discrete. Approaches that reduce HIF-α protein levels - whether by destabilizing the protein, inhibiting translation, or suppressing mRNA expression - frequently converge on stress-adaptive and redox-sensitive pathways that globally reprogram cellular translation under hypoxic conditions. In contrast, direct antagonists of HIF transcriptional activity bypass control of HIF-α abundance and instead interfere with DNA binding, heterodimerization, or co-activator recruitment; however, these approaches have often been limited by poor specificity, context-dependent compensation, and off-target transcriptional effects.
Importantly, accumulating evidence indicates that many agents nominally classified as inhibitors of HIF stability or transcription exert substantial effects via control of translational output. Translation-centered repression of HIF-α - mediated by inhibition of mTOR-dependent signaling, activation of eIF2α-dependent stress responses, or disruption of hypoxia-specific translation mechanisms such as internal ribosome entry - has emerged as a recurrent and mechanistically dominant mode of HIF regulation across multiple, chemically unrelated drug classes. Within this framework, reduced HIF transcriptional output frequently reflects impaired synthesis or nuclear availability of HIF-α rather than exclusive blockade of DNA binding or co-activator engagement.
These mechanistic distinctions carry important therapeutic implications. Strategies centered on HIF-α destabilization are inherently subject to regulation by ambient oxygen levels, mitochondrial activity, or intracellular redox balance, and therefore may be effective only within restricted redox–oxygen windows in which moderate levels of reactive oxygen species favor adaptive HIF signaling, whereas deviations in either direction diminish efficacy. As a result, indirect approaches that perturb oxygen sensing, metabolism, or proteostasis often yield variable or transient suppression of HIF activity in vivo. Direct inhibition of HIF transcriptional activity has achieved clear clinical success primarily when exploiting unique structural vulnerabilities, as exemplified by PAS-B–directed allosteric inhibitors of HIF-2α. However, this strategy cannot currently be applied to HIF-1α, which lacks an analogous druggable structural pocket. In contrast, translation-centered strategies operate at an early regulatory node governing both protein stabilization and transcriptional responses and are tightly integrated with cellular stress-adaptive programs, potentially offering a more durable means of suppressing hypoxia-driven gene expression under fluctuating microenvironmental conditions.
Notably, the majority of mechanistic and translational advances in HIF targeting have emerged from studies in cancer, particularly solid tumors, where hypoxia, metabolic stress, and redox imbalance are defining microenvironmental features. Cancer has therefore served as the primary experimental and clinical context in which HIF regulation has been dissected, druggability assessed, and therapeutic vulnerabilities identified. While these efforts have yielded important clinical successes, they also shape the prevailing conceptual framework of HIF biology, which is often extrapolated to other disease settings. Extending insights gained from oncology to non-malignant hypoxia-associated diseases will require careful consideration of tissue-specific HIF functions, adaptive stress responses, and therapeutic tolerance.
Taken together, these observations argue that effective and selective suppression of hypoxia signaling is unlikely to be achieved through isolated perturbation of a single regulatory layer. Instead, durable inhibition of HIF activity - particularly in solid tumors characterized by dynamic oxygen and metabolic gradients - will likely require therapeutic strategies that either exploit isoform-specific structural features or engage translational control mechanisms capable of uncoupling hypoxia signaling from generalized cytotoxic and redox-adaptive stress responses. Within this conceptual framework, translational regulation emerges as a unifying axis linking HIF-α synthesis, stability, and transcriptional competence, while redox biology defines the physiological boundaries that ultimately determine therapeutic vulnerability.
