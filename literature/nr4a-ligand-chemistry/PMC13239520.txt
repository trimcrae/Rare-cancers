Nevertheless, the majority of advanced-stage patients ultimately experience chemotherapy-resistant tumor regrowth. Overcoming chemoresistance therefore remains a critical challenge in the management of OC.
As the most prevalent subtype of OC, high-grade serous carcinoma (HGSC) exhibits extensive genomic instability and copy number alterations, primarily associated with TP53 mutations. Cancer cell intrinsic mechanisms of chemoresistance, namely tumor-specific genomic and epigenomic alterations and transcriptional programs, have been recognized as key contributors to treatment failure. Primary resistance has been associated with copy number amplifications of CCNE1 and KRAS. Moreover, therapy-induced acquired resistance involves reversion mutations in BRCA1/2 and demethylation of the BRCA1 promoter. However, these alterations are observed only in a subset of patients. In addition, reports indicating minimal genomic differences between primary and recurrent tumors suggest that chemoresistance in HGSC is not solely determined by cancer cell-intrinsic genomic or epigenomic features, but rather represents a multifactorial phenotype shaped by additional mechanisms.
In contrast, cancer cell-extrinsic mechanisms of chemoresistance have been proposed, such as those arising from indirect modulation of cancer cells or the formation of physical barriers by components of the tumor microenvironment (TME), including vasculature, immune cells, and fibroblasts. Distinct from other solid malignancies, HGSC mainly spreads intraperitoneally and establishes a unique TME in the omentum and peritoneum, which are its common metastatic sites. In HGSC, primary chemoresistance has been associated with factors such as increased stromal content and the presence of regulatory T cells. 
The intraperitoneal TME is not static but undergoes dynamic remodeling during chemotherapy. In HGSC, previous studies have reported bidirectional immunological modifications of the intraperitoneal TME following chemotherapy. Chemotherapy augments CD8+ T-cell cytotoxicity while concurrently promoting exhaustion and expanding regulatory T cells. In the myeloid compartment, chemotherapy induces immunosuppression yet also amplifies inflammatory signaling through inflammasome activation. However, it remains largely unexplored how these chemotherapy-induced alterations in the intraperitoneal TME influence the response to ongoing chemotherapy. In particular, experimental evidence demonstrating the role of a specific inflammatory pathway using orthogonal approaches is lacking.
In this study, we demonstrate that chemotherapy-induced, interleukin 1 beta (IL1β)-dependent neutrophil accumulation in tumors drives chemoresistance in HGSC using patient samples, clinically relevant murine models, genetic deletion of both ligand and receptor, single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq), flow cytometry, and confocal imaging. This observation reveals a tumor-extrinsic mechanism whereby chemotherapy-induced inflammatory remodeling of the intraperitoneal TME impairs tumor sensitivity to chemotherapy. Our study sheds light on one aspect of the multifactorial nature of chemoresistance in HGSC and suggests the potential to overcome chemoresistance by targeting therapy-induced inflammation.

## Materials and methods

## Bulk RNA sequencing analysis of human OC samples before-chemotherapy and after-chemotherapy
Gene expression data from pre-chemotherapy and post-chemotherapy OC tissue samples derived from the same patients were obtained from the following datasets: Javellana et al., 2022 , GSE109934, GSE181597, and GSE201600. Among these, the dataset from Javellana et al consists of RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data, while the remaining three are based on targeted gene expression panels. For the RNA-seq dataset from Javellana et al, paired differential expression was performed with the limma R package (RRID: SCR_001905) on log2-transformed values, and genes meeting false discovery rate <0.05 and ≥1.5-fold change were analyzed in ingenuity pathway analysis (IPA) (QIAGEN; Upstream Regulators). Inflammasome activity was quantified by single-sample Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (ssGSEA, GenePattern V.3.5.0; RRID: SCR_003201) with MSigDB REACTOME_INFLAMMASOMES and THE_NLRP3_INFLAMMASOME gene sets. We further analyzed gene expressions of IL1B and TNF in GSE109934, GSE181597, and GSE201600. In GSE181597, IL1B expression was additionally stratified and compared by chemotherapy response score (CRS) classification. CXCL1, CXCL2, CXCL3, CXCL5, CXCL6, CXCL8, CSF2, and CSF3—cytokines implicated in neutrophil migration and differentiation—were assessed in relevant datasets, except GSE109934, which did not contain chemokine expression data.

## Single-cell RNA sequencing analysis of human OC samples before-chemotherapy and after-chemotherapy
The treatment-naïve scRNA-seq dataset generated by Vázquez-García et al was retrieved from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GSE180661). An scRNA-seq dataset from post-chemotherapy patients was retrieved from the study by Zhang et al (GSE165897). The Seurat package V.4.3.0 (RRID: SCR_016341) in R software V.4.3.1 and the scanpy package V.1.9.8 (RRID: SCR_018139) in Python V.3.10 (RRID: SCR_008394) were used for downstream processing. Dimensionality reduction was performed using principal component analysis and Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) following the original protocol, with cell-type annotations from the original article. The R package scCustomize V.1.1.1 (RRID: SCR_024675) was used to generate density and joint plots.

## KM plotter
The Kaplan-Meier (KM) plotter (https://kmplot.com/analysis/), an open-access The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA)-based database comprising bulk RNA-seq data from 374 patients with OC, was used to assess the correlation between IL1B expression and overall survival (OS) or progression-free survival (PFS).

## Cell lines
KPCA (KRASG12VTrp53R172HCcne1OEAkt2OE), BPCA (Brd4OETrp53R172HCcne1OEAkt2OE) and BPPNM (Brca1−/−Trp53R172HPten−/−Nf1−/−MycOE) cells were generously gifted by Dr Robert Weinberg at the Whitehead Institute. These cell lines were cultured using the same reagents and under the same conditions as reported in the original study. Cells were passaged no more than 15 times prior to injection into mouse peritoneal cavities. Cells were routinely checked for Mycoplasma.

## Animal models
C57BL/6J (WT, RRID: IMSR_JAX:000664), C57BL/6J-Il1bem2Lutzy/Mmjax (IL1β KO, RRID: MMRRC_068082-JAX), and B6.129S7-Il1r1tm1Imx/J (IL1R1 KO, RRID: IMSR_JAX:003245) mice were purchased from Jackson Laboratories or Mutant Mouse Resource & Research Centers (MMRRC). Mice were kept on a 12 hours light-dark cycle and had access to food and water ad libitum. All animal procedures were performed in accordance with the Wistar Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee under protocol 201536.

## Tumor implantation, treatment, and evaluation
For KPCA tumors, 1×106 KPCA cells were injected i.p. into wild-type (WT), IL1β knockout (KO), or interleukin 1 receptor type 1 (IL1R1) KO mice in 200 µL of a 1:1 matrigel:phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) mix (Matrigel Matrix Basement Membrane, #354234, Corning). At day 7, mice were treated with 10 mg/kg carboplatin (C2538, Sigma) and 1 mg/kg paclitaxel (NSC 125973, Selleck) or vehicle control once a week for 2 weeks. At day 21, mice were imaged by in vivo imaging system (IVIS). Necropsy and flow cytometric analyses of tumor-infiltrating immune cells were performed on days 10, 17, and 22. For neutrophil depletion studies, the mixture of 200 µg of anti-Ly6G (L280, clone 1A8, Leinco Tech, RRID: AB_2737551) and 50 µg of anti-rat kappa light chain (I-2027, Clone MAR 18.5, Leinco Tech) monoclonal antibodies were injected i.p. in WT or IL1β KO KPCA tumor-bearing mice together with chemotherapy at day 7 and then three times a week for 2 weeks. For BPCA tumors, 3×106 BPCA cells were injected i.p. into WT or IL1β KO mice in 200 µL of a 1:1 matrigel:PBS mix. Considering the timing at which peritoneal dissemination becomes established, mice were treated with 10 mg/kg carboplatin and 1 mg/kg paclitaxel or vehicle control at day 35 and day 49. On day 64, mice were applied for necropsy analysis. For BPPNM tumors, 1×106 BPPNM cells were injected i.p. into WT mice in 200 µL of a 1:1 matrigel:PBS mix. Mice were treated with 10 mg/kg carboplatin and 1 mg/kg paclitaxel or vehicle control at day 14 and day 21. Necropsy and flow cytometric analyses of tumor-infiltrating immune cells were performed on days 17, 24.

## In vivo bioluminescent imaging
In vivo bioluminescence imaging was performed on an IVIS 50 (PerkinElmer; Living Image 4.3.1), with exposures of 1 s to 1 min, binning 2–8, field of view 12.5 cm, f/stop 1, and open filter. D-Luciferin (150 mg/kg in PBS, eLUCNA-1G, Gold Biotechnology) was injected into the mice i.p. and imaged 10 min later.

## Mesentery metastasis scoring
Mesentery metastasis score was calculated as previously reported. Briefly, all mesenteric tumor nodules were counted, and each mouse score was determined as follows:
0: no tumor was detected.
1: number of tumor nodules is less than 10.
2: number of nodules is 10–30.
3: number of nodules is over 30.

## Flow cytometry
Tissue dissociation was performed as previously described. Cells were stained with primary conjugated antibodies at indicated dilutions ( ). Intracellular staining was carried out using True-Nuclear Transcription Factor Buffer Set (#424401, BioLegend). Mesenchymal populations were defined within CD45− non-immune populations as follows: fibroblast (Podoplanin+PDGFRα+CD31−), mesothelial cells (podoplanin+PDGFRα−CD31−) and endothelial cells (podoplanin−PDGFRα−CD31+). For interferon (IFN)-γ and tumor necrosis factor (TNF) staining, single cells were incubated for 4 hours with Cell Activation Cocktail (with Brefeldin A) (#423303, BioLegend) prior to staining. Samples were analyzed on a BD FACSymphony A3 Cell Analyzer using FlowJo software (RRID: SCR_008520).

## In vitro chemotherapy treatment of BMDMs and neutrophils
Bone marrow cells were isolated from the femurs and tibiae of 6–10 weeks old female C57BL/6 mice. Bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs) were generated as previously described. Neutrophils were enriched using a biotin-based positive selection kit (#17683, STEMCELL Technologies) together with a biotin-conjugated anti-Ly6G antibody (#127603, BioLegend, RRID: AB_1186105). BMDMs were seeded at 1.5×105 cells per well in 200 µL of medium containing 20 ng/mL macrophage colony-stimulating factor (M-CSF) in 96-well plates. Neutrophils were seeded at 2×105 cells per well in 200 µL of medium containing 10 ng/mL granulocyte macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) in 96-well plates. Vehicle control, carboplatin (20 µM), or paclitaxel (200 nM) was added to each well. After 48 hours of culture, cells were harvested and analyzed for intracellular IL1β expression by flow cytometry. 

## Cell viability and drug sensitivity assays in the presence or absence of IL1β
On day 0, KPCA cells were seeded at a density of 2,000 cells per well in a 96-well clear flat-bottom plate. On day 1, recombinant mouse IL1β (401-ML-005, R&D Systems) was added at final concentrations of 0, 0.1, 1, or 10 ng/mL. On day 4, cell viability was assessed using Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK-8; HY-K0301, MedChemExpress). For determination of the half maximal Inhibitory Concentration (IC50) values of carboplatin and paclitaxel, the culture medium was replaced on day 1 with fresh medium containing carboplatin (final concentrations: 0.1, 10, 50, 100, 200, or 500 µM) or paclitaxel (final concentrations: 0.01, 0.1, 1, 2, 10, or 1,000 nM), with or without recombinant mouse IL1β at final concentrations of 0, 0.1, 1, or 10 ng/mL. On day 4, cell viability was assessed using CCK-8.

## Single-cell sequencing and analysis of mouse tumors
From omental tumors (n=3 mice/group), non-B cells were sorted and pooled. Samples of each group were then loaded onto a 10x Genomics Chromium Next GEM Single Cell 3′ V.3.1 cartridge to generate droplets. Libraries were prepared per 10x instructions, QC’d (Bioanalyzer 2100, High Sensitivity DNA) and quantified (KAPA qPCR), then sequenced on an Illumina NextSeq 2000 (P3 100-cycle; paired-end: R1 28 bp, i7 8 bp, R2 90 bp) (RRID: SCR_023614).
Base call files were converted to FASTQ with Cell Ranger (V.9.0.0, RRID: SCR_017344). Reads were aligned via STAR (V.2.7.9a, RRID: SCR_004463) to the mm10 reference (10x refdata-gex-mm10-2020-A) and unique molecular identifier counts were generated. Count matrices were imported into R and processed with Seurat (V.5.3.0). Data were normalized, scaled, and analyzed on highly variable genes by principal component analysis, embedded by UMAP, and clustered with the Louvain algorithm. Cell types were annotated with SingleR (RRID: SCR_023120) using ImmGenData (2024–02-26) and celldex (V.1.18.0). Differential expression used Seurat’s Wilcoxon rank-sum test with Bonferroni correction. Visualizations (UMAPs, heatmaps) were generated in R (ggplot2, RRID: SCR_014601).

## Immunofluorescence
Details of the antibodies and their concentrations are provided in . Mouse omentum was fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde. The 10 μm thickness tissue slides were blocked with 3% bovine serum albumin and 1% Triton in PBS and incubated with myeloperoxidase (MPO) and citrullinated histone H3 (cHH3) primary antibodies overnight. The tissues were washed and incubated with respective secondary antibodies. After washing, incubate specimens in 300 nM 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) solution. Confocal images were collected using a Leica SP8 microscope (RRID: SCR_018169).
3 µm thick formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded (FFPE) sections of human omentum were stained using a sequential double staining protocol. After deparaffinization/rehydration, sections underwent sequential tyramide signal amplification (TSA)-immunofluorescence. MPO was labeled with primary antibody, horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-polymer secondary, and TSA-Fluorescein Green (SAT701001EA, Akoya Biosciences); residual antibodies/HRP were inactivated by pH-9 antigen retrieval (#415201, Nichirei Biosciences). cHH3 was then detected using the same scheme with TSA-Cy5 Red (SAT705A001EA, Akoya Biosciences). DAPI was used for nuclear counterstain.

## Preparation of NET-conditioned media
Neutrophils were isolated from the bone marrow as described above. Isolated neutrophils were resuspended in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) with 4% fetal bovine serum (FBS) at a concentration of 2×10⁵ cells/well (in 500 µL per well) and plated in 24-well plates. Neutrophil extracellular trap (NET) formation was induced by treating cells with 20 nM phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate (P1585, Sigma). Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) was used to make respective control media. In some conditions, the PAD4 inhibitor GSK484 (SML1658, Sigma) was added at a final concentration of 10 µM. After overnight incubation, supernatants (NET-conditioned media) were collected by centrifugation.

## Cell viability assay with chemotherapeutic agents in NETs-conditioned media
KPCA cells were seeded at 2,000 cells/well in a 96-well clear flat-bottom plate. Next day, media was replaced with 100 µL of conditioned media from neutrophil cultures. Chemotherapeutic agents were added to designated wells: carboplatin (final concentration 20 µM) and paclitaxel (final concentration 10 nM), respectively. Cells were incubated for 48 hours at 37°C with 5% CO₂. After 48 hours, 30 µg of D-Luciferin was added to each well. Luminescence imaging and quantification were performed to assess cell viability.

## RNA isolation and RT-qPCR of Cxcl2
NIH-3T3 fibroblast cell line was cultured in DMEM with 10% FBS, alone, with recombinant IL1β (1 ng/mL, 211–11 B-10UG, PeproTech), αIL1β (1 µg/mL, I-437, Leinco), αIL1R1 (1 µg/mL, BE0256, Bio X Cell), a TAK1 inhibitor (5Z-7-oxozeaenol, 500 nM, MedChemExpress, Cat#HY-12686), or an NFκB inhibitor (BMS-245541, 0.2 µM, MedChemExpress, Cat#HY-10519) for 22 hours. Total RNA was extracted using TRIzol (15596018, Invitrogen). RNA content and purity were measured using a nanodrop. Complementary DNA was synthesized from normalized RNA numbers using PrimeScript RT Master Mix (RR036A, Takara) and a thermal cycler (A37029, Applied Biosystems). For reverse transcription quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR), reactions were prepared using TB Green Premix Ex Taq II FAST qPCR (RR830B, Takara) and ran on the QuantStudio 3 Real-Time PCR System (A28132, Applied Biosystems). C-X-C motif chemokine ligand 2 (Cxcl2) primers (F: 5’-CCA ACC ACC AGG CTA CAG G-3’, R: 5’-GCG TCA CAC TCA AGC TCT G-3’) were used to look at the target gene and 18S primers (F: 5’-CTT AGA GGG ACA AGT GGC G-3’, R: 5’-ACG CTG AGC CAG TCA GTG TA-3’) were used as the housekeeping gene.
WT mice bearing KPCA tumors received an i.p. injection of recombinant IL1β (400 ng) on day 7 after tumor inoculation. 4 hours later, omental tumors were harvested and processed for RNA isolation, followed by RT-qPCR as described above. To compare Cxcl2 expression between WT and IL1β KO mice under chemotherapy-naive conditions, KPCA omental tumors were harvested on day 21 after tumor inoculation and analyzed by RT-qPCR as described above.

## Tumor immune estimation resource
We assessed the association between IL1B, IL1R1 and CXCL2 messenger RNA expression and immune infiltration using the Tumor IMmune Estimation Resource (TIMER) 2.0 web platform http://timer.cistrome.org/. Neutrophil abundance was inferred with the TIMER deconvolution algorithm on TCGA data. Spearman correlation coefficients between each gene expression and inferred neutrophil levels were computed adjusting for tumor purity.

## Human samples
Paired FFPE omental tumor samples from 16 patients with HGSC at Kyoto University were collected before and after chemotherapy. Pretreatment specimens were obtained at diagnostic laparotomy/laparoscopy; post-treatment specimens were collected at interval debulking surgery after carboplatin-taxane chemotherapy. From each block, 3 µm sections were prepared; adjacent sections underwent H&E and immunofluorescence staining. Neutrophils and NETs were quantified as described below. Peripheral-blood neutrophil counts were also compared between baseline (before chemotherapy) and immediately before interval debulking surgery (after chemotherapy). This analysis was approved by the Kyoto University Graduate School and Faculty of Medicine Ethics Committee (reference number G531) and conforms to the Declaration of Helsinki. Informed consent was obtained from all participants via an opt-in approach or an opt-out approach.

## Neutrophil quantification using HoVer-NeXt
For nuclear identification and cell type classification on H&E-stained slides, we employed HoVer-NeXt, a state-of-the-art artificial intelligence (AI) model. HoVer-NeXt was trained on a modified version of the Lizard dataset and performed seven-class classification of the following cell types: (1) epithelial cells, (2) connective tissue cells, (3) lymphocytes, (4) plasma cells, (5) neutrophils, (6) eosinophils, and (7) mitotic figures. The whole-slide image was used as the region of interest, and default settings and parameters were applied for analysis. In the non-cancerous state, epithelial cells are absent in the omentum; therefore, epithelial cells identified within omental tumors are considered synonymous with cancer cells.

## NETs quantification using QuPath
For quantitative analysis of fluorescently labeled cells using multiplex immunofluorescence, QuPath (V.0.6.0, RRID: SCR_018257) was used. The whole-slide image was used as the region of interest. Cell counts for DAPI, fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC) (MPO), and Cy5 (cHH3) channels were obtained using the Cell Detection module, with default settings and thresholds applied throughout the analysis. NETs were defined by the colocalization of MPO and cHH3. For each slide, the ratio of NETs to DAPI-positive cells was quantified.

## Statistics
Statistical analyses were performed in GraphPad Prism (RRID: SCR_002798). The specific statistical tests applied—Student’s t-test, paired t-test, or one-way analysis of variance followed by Tukey’s post hoc multiple comparisons—are detailed in the figure legends. For mouse experiments, mice were randomly allocated to control and experimental groups. The investigator was not blinded to the group allocation. A priori power analysis was not performed. Results were considered significant at p<0.05. Results are presented as mean±SEM.

## Results

## IL1β pathway is upregulated in post-chemotherapy human OC tumors
To investigate how chemotherapy alters the TME, we analyzed multiple publicly available bulk RNA-expression datasets from patient-matched pre-chemotherapy and post-chemotherapy HGSC samples. First, we performed IPA focusing on cytokine regulators, which revealed activation of inflammatory pathways, including TNF, IL1β, and interleukin-6, as top hits in post-chemotherapy tumor samples ( ). Consistently, ssGSEA demonstrated upregulation of the inflammasome pathway, a key facilitator for IL1β secretion, following chemotherapy ( ). Using other independent datasets, we next compared the gene expression levels of TNF and IL1B. Although TNF, the top upregulated cytokine in the IPA, showed no significant change between pre-chemotherapy and post-chemotherapy samples ( ), IL1B, the second most upregulated cytokine, exhibited increased expression following chemotherapy ( ). Furthermore, in the GSE181597 dataset, stratification by chemotherapy response revealed that IL1B expression remained unchanged in chemotherapy-sensitive cases with a CRS3 but was notably elevated after treatment in less sensitive cases with scores of 1 or 2 (CRS1 and CRS2) ( ).
 To identify the cellular source of IL1β in HGSC, we analyzed scRNA-seq data from untreated and post-chemotherapy tumor samples. Interestingly, IL1B expression was predominantly observed in myeloid cells, regardless of chemotherapy exposure (red arrows in ). Finally, survival analysis of the TCGA ovarian serous cystadenocarcinoma (TCGA-OV) cohort demonstrated that high IL1B expression in pretreatment tumor samples was associated with shorter OS and PFS among chemotherapy-treated patients with HGSC ( ). Collectively, these findings suggest that chemotherapy-induced upregulation of IL1B may play a role in reduced treatment sensitivity in HGSC.

## IL1β from myeloid cells contributes to OC chemoresistance
We next sought to validate these findings using a murine OC model. To this end, we employed KPCA, a homologous recombination (HR)-proficient OC cell line derived from mouse fallopian tube epithelial cells that harbors genetic alterations relevant to human HGSC, including CCNE1 amplification. An intraperitoneal dissemination model was established using KPCA cells, followed by chemotherapy with carboplatin and paclitaxel ( ). KPCA tumors exhibited resistance to chemotherapy ( ). Flow cytometry analysis of IL1β expression across cell populations within omental tumors on day 22 revealed that, consistent with the aforementioned human data ( ), IL1β was predominantly expressed by myeloid cells, that is, monocytes, macrophages, and neutrophils ( ).
 Next, we sought to investigate the initial cellular source of chemotherapy-induced IL1β expression, we treated BMDMs and neutrophils with chemotherapy in vitro. In neutrophils, IL1β expression was unchanged compared with control treatment, whereas in BMDMs, intracellular IL1β expression was increased following treatment with either carboplatin or paclitaxel, suggesting that macrophages are the initial source of IL1β ( ).
To investigate the functional role of myeloid cell-derived IL1β in chemotherapy treatment, we compared chemotherapy responses between WT and IL1β KO mice. While WT mice showed minimal response to chemotherapy, IL1β KO mice exhibited a trend toward reduced bioluminescence signals and significant reductions in omental tumor burden and mesenteric dissemination following treatment ( ). Notably, in the absence of chemotherapy, tumor growth of KPCA cells did not differ between WT and IL1β KO mice. A consistent result for omental tumor burden was observed using another cyclin E1 (CCNE1)-overexpressed, HR-proficient cell line, BPCA ( ). These results collectively suggest that IL1β derived from myeloid cells contributes to chemotherapy resistance in OC.

## IL1β recruits neutrophil and impairs chemotherapy response
To determine whether IL1β affects chemotherapy sensitivity through a direct or indirect mechanism, we first examined the direct action of IL1β on tumor cells. IL1β did not affect cell viability in the absence of chemotherapy ( ). We next assessed drug sensitivity in the presence or absence of IL1β. The results showed that the IC50 values for carboplatin and paclitaxel remained largely unchanged regardless of IL1β concentrations, indicating that IL1β does not directly alter the chemosensitivity of OC cells ( ).
 To investigate the indirect effects of IL1β on chemosensitivity, we examined differences in the TME with or without chemotherapy in WT and IL1β KO mice. Flow cytometric analysis of immune cell populations in KPCA-derived omental tumors on day 22 revealed that neutrophil infiltration increased following chemotherapy in WT mice, whereas this increase was absent in IL1β KO mice ( ). The proportions of CD8+ T cells, CD4+ T cells, monocytes, and macrophages remained unchanged ( ). Similarly, in BPCA tumors, omental lesions from IL1β KO mice exhibited significantly fewer neutrophils after chemotherapy compared with those from WT mice ( ). In the KPCA model, no significant differences were observed in the proportions of CD8+ T cells with activation, exhaustion, or memory phenotypes ( ). We also assessed earlier time points and found that chemotherapy induced a modest increase in neutrophil infiltration 3 days after the first dose, followed by a marked increase 3 days after the second dose ( ). These findings suggest that IL1β contributes to the chemotherapy-induced recruitment of neutrophils.
We next examined whether the IL1β-driven increase in neutrophils contributes to chemotherapy resistance. Indeed, neutrophil depletion under chemotherapy led to a reduction in omental tumor size and mesenteric dissemination compared with control-treated mice in WT mice. In contrast, no such differences were observed in IL1β KO mice ( ). Interestingly, no increase in neutrophil infiltration was observed in the HR-deficient, chemosensitive BPPNM model ( ). These findings suggest that chemotherapy-induced neutrophil accumulation contributes to IL1β-mediated chemoresistance.

## T-cell suppression and NETs may contribute to the IL1β-neutrophil-induced chemoresistance
To investigate the mechanism by which chemotherapy-induced neutrophil accumulation contributes to chemoresistance, we performed scRNA-seq of omental tumors from chemotherapy-treated WT and IL1β KO mice. Focusing on T cells and natural killer cells, key components of antitumor immunity, we identified 10 distinct clusters ( , ). In IL1β KO mice, there was a marked reduction in cluster 0, which corresponds to stressed CD8+ T cells ( ), and a dramatic enrichment of cluster 2, characterized by an effector CD8+ phenotype expressing Ifng, Tnf, and low levels of Tox ( ), and genes encoding proteins that reflect recent antigen experience, such as Nr4a2, Nr4a3, and Tnfsf11 ( ). These findings suggest that CD8+ T cells shift toward a more effector-like transcriptional state in the absence of IL1β. In contrast, comparative transcriptomic analysis of neutrophils between the two groups revealed fewer differences than observed in T cells ( ). Additionally, we confirmed that Il1b was exclusively expressed in myeloid cells, that is, neutrophils and macrophages in omentum tumors in our model ( ).
 We next investigated the role of NETs. NETs are extracellular DNA-protein networks released during a form of neutrophil cell death and have been reported to be induced following chemotherapy and to promote tumor progression in breast cancer. As NETs cannot be identified through scRNA-seq, we evaluated their presence by imaging of omentum tumor sections. In WT mice, chemotherapy induced both neutrophil infiltration and prominent NET formation, as evidenced by the presence of cHH3. These effects were abrogated in IL1β KO mice, indicating that IL1β is required for chemotherapy-induced NET formation ( ). To determine whether NETs influence chemotherapy sensitivity, we conducted in vitro assays using NET-containing conditioned supernatant. The cytotoxicity of carboplatin and paclitaxel was reduced in the presence of NETs, whereas pharmacologic inhibition of NETs reversed this resistance ( ). These findings suggest that IL1β-driven neutrophils contribute to chemoresistance during chemotherapy through both suppression of T cell-mediated antitumor immunity and promotion of NET formation.

## IL1β induces neutrophil recruitment via CXCL2 upregulation after chemotherapy
We next investigated the mechanism by which IL1β promotes neutrophil recruitment following chemotherapy in OC tissues. Among neutrophil-related chemokines and growth factors, comparison of pre-chemotherapy and post-chemotherapy samples across publicly available datasets revealed that CXCL2 was consistently upregulated after treatment in all datasets examined ( ).
 To identify the cellular targets of IL1β, we analyzed the expression of its receptor, IL1R1, using the same human post-chemotherapy scRNA-seq dataset as previously described ( ). IL1R1 was found to be highly expressed in mesenchymal cell populations, particularly fibroblasts and mesothelial cells ( ). CXCL2 was also found to be highly expressed in both mesenchymal and macrophage populations ( ). Interestingly, co-expression of IL1R1 and CXCL2 was mainly observed in fibroblast populations ( ). High IL1R1 expression in mesenchymal cells, particularly fibroblasts, was further confirmed by flow cytometry of omental tumors from the murine KPCA model ( ).
In vitro, IL1β increased Cxcl2 expression in immortalized murine fibroblasts (NIH3T3 cells), and this IL1β-induced upregulation of Cxcl2 was abolished by inhibition of IL1β or IL1R1, as well as by inhibition of key downstream mediators of IL1R1 signaling, including TAK1 and NFκB ( ), consistent with previous studies. In vivo, IL1β treatment induced Cxcl2 expression in the omental tumors ( ). Although Cxcl2 expression did not differ between tumors from WT and IL1β KO mice without chemotherapy ( ), omental fibroblasts from IL1β KO mice exhibited significantly lower Cxcl2 expression after chemotherapy than those from WT controls ( ).
TIMER analysis of the TCGA-OV cohort revealed that higher expression levels of IL1B, IL1R1, and CXCL2 significantly correlated with increased estimated neutrophil infiltration ( ). These results suggest that IL1β acts on IL1R1-expressing mesenchymal cells to induce CXCL2, thereby promoting neutrophil recruitment in response to chemotherapy.

## IL1R1 KO mice exhibit chemo-sensitivity
As IL1R1 is predominantly expressed in host-derived mesenchymal cells and IL1β does not affect chemosensitivity in OC cells in vitro, we next investigated whether host IL1R1 KO could phenocopy IL1β KO by abrogating chemotherapy-induced neutrophil recruitment and the associated chemoresistance. Indeed, IL1R1 KO mice exhibited reduced omental tumor burden and decreased mesenteric metastasis following chemotherapy compared with WT controls ( ). Consistent with these findings, the number of neutrophils was lower in IL1R1 KO mice after chemotherapy than in WT mice, whereas the proportions of other immune cell populations remained unchanged ( ). Moreover, functional analysis of T cells revealed enhanced IFN-γ and TNF production in CD8+ T cells isolated from the omentum of IL1R1 KO mice ( ). Collectively, these results support the involvement of the IL1β–IL1R1-neutrophil axis in promoting chemotherapy resistance in OC.
 

## Neutrophils and NETs are increased in human post-chemo OC samples
Finally, we compared neutrophils and NETs in human OC tissues before and after chemotherapy. Paired omental tumor samples were obtained from 16 patients who underwent initial diagnostic omental biopsy followed by interval debulking surgery with omentectomy, with residual tumor present in the omentum at the time of surgery. To quantify each cellular population within the tumors, we employed the pathology AI platform HoVer-NeXt for cell-type annotation on H&E-stained slides ( , ). The region of interest encompassed the entire slide area. At higher magnification, cells labeled as neutrophils appeared smaller than tumor cells and exhibited segmented nuclei ( ). The proportion of cancer cells among total cells did not change with chemotherapy ( ). In contrast, the proportion of neutrophils among total cells and relative to tumor cells increased after chemotherapy ( ). Notably, circulating neutrophil counts decreased following chemotherapy ( ). Next, we evaluated NETs using multiplex immunofluorescence staining for cHH3 and MPO ( ). A trend toward an increased proportion of NETs among total cells was observed following chemotherapy ( ). Collectively, these findings suggest that chemotherapy induces infiltration of neutrophils and NET formation in human OC.
 

## Discussion
We identified chemotherapy-induced inflammatory remodeling of the TME, specifically the role of myeloid cell-derived IL1β, as a contributor to chemoresistance in HGSC. Following chemotherapy, IL1β promotes neutrophil recruitment through CXCL2 induction in mesenchymal cells, particularly fibroblasts, leading to NET formation and suppression of T cell-mediated antitumor immunity, thereby facilitating chemoresistance ( ). These phenotypes were markedly attenuated in mice deficient in either IL1β or its receptor IL1R1, resulting in improved sensitivity to chemotherapy.
Chemotherapy induces alterations not only in cancer cells but also within the surrounding TME. In breast and prostate cancers, chemotherapy-induced inflammatory remodeling of the TME has been demonstrated to modulate therapeutic responsiveness. In OC, chemotherapy-induced immunological modifications of the intraperitoneal TME have been reported ; however, there is little experimental evidence of how such remodeling influences the response to ongoing chemotherapy. To our knowledge, the present study provides the first experimental demonstration that a specific therapy-induced inflammatory pathway can extrinsically drive concomitant chemoresistance in OC.
IL1β is a proinflammatory cytokine known to promote tumor initiation and progression. However, in the present study, we observed no difference in tumor growth between untreated IL1β KO and WT mice. IL1β contributes to in vivo tumor progression only in the context of chemotherapy in our models (CCNE1 and Akt2 overexpressed) ( , ). This finding contrasts with observations in other malignancies such as breast cancer, pancreatic cancer, and glioblastoma, where host IL1β knockout or IL1β blockade alone has been sufficient to suppress tumor growth. These cancer type-specific discrepancies indicate that the function of IL1β is context-dependent.
To determine whether IL1β directly modulates chemotherapy response, we tested OC cells in vitro and found no significant change in their sensitivity in the presence of IL1β ( ). This differs from previous studies in other cancers, which showed that IL1β inhibits etoposide-induced apoptosis in pancreatic cancer and promotes carboplatin resistance via ZEB1 activation in colorectal cancer. In contrast, our study demonstrates that IL1β promotes chemoresistance indirectly, through host IL1β–IL1R1 signaling that induces neutrophil recruitment ( and 5A–5E). A similar mechanism has been described in breast cancer, where chemotherapy-induced IL1β expression contributes to resistance via neutrophil and NET formation, highlighting the significance of IL1β-mediated indirect effects on treatment sensitivity.
Among the mechanisms by which neutrophils promote tumor progression, suppression of T-cell function has been well documented in OC and other malignancies. In the present study, tumors from IL1β KO or IL1R1 KO mice failed to exhibit chemotherapy-induced neutrophil recruitment and instead showed changes consistent with increased T-cell immune activation compared with tumors from WT mice ( and ). These findings suggest that suppression of T-cell immunity by neutrophils under chemotherapy may be associated with IL1β-mediated treatment resistance. Interestingly, we observed that chemotherapy enhances neutrophil infiltration and NET formation in both human and murine HGSC tumors, and that NETs contribute to chemoresistance ( ). Chemoresistance mediated by NETs has been attributed to multiple mechanisms, including the stimulation of transforming growth factor (TGF)-β signaling that drives epithelial-mesenchymal transition, and the internalization of NET-derived DNA by tumor cells, which impairs drug-DNA interactions within the nucleus. 
In OC, immune checkpoint inhibitors have shown limited clinical efficacy, and their combination with chemotherapy has also failed to demonstrate significant therapeutic benefit. Consequently, no immunological strategies have yet been established to overcome treatment resistance in OC. The lack of additional benefit from the anti-IL1β antibody canakinumab in combination with chemotherapy in lung cancer is disappointing. However, the absence of IL1B upregulation following chemotherapy in lung cancer, along with the tissue-specific nature of IL1β activity, does not preclude the potential efficacy of IL1β inhibition in OC. Furthermore, with regard to NET inhibition, a first-in-human phase I clinical trial of the humanized monoclonal antibody CIT-013 (designed to target citrullinated histones H2A and H4) has been reported for the treatment of autoimmune diseases. These novel agents targeting NETs may offer a promising therapeutic strategy to overcome chemotherapy resistance, warranting further investigation.
This study has several limitations. First, the effects of chemotherapy were evaluated only under limited experimental conditions, and thus it remains unclear how tumor growth and immune cell infiltration may vary according to the timing of treatment initiation. Second, it is well established that the HR status of tumor cells directly affects chemotherapy sensitivity; therefore, the in vivo chemoresistance of the HR-proficient tumors is unlikely to be explained solely by neutrophils. Third, the murine models used do not fully capture the intratumoral heterogeneity of human HGSC, which is genetically unstable and characterized by the presence of subclones harboring distinct genotypes. The murine models employed in this study were generated using clonal cell lines with uniform genetic alterations and therefore do not fully recapitulate the heterogeneous TME of human HGSC. Although bulk RNA-seq of human HGSC samples demonstrated upregulation of IL1B following chemotherapy, this study does not address how IL1B expression, neutrophil infiltration, or neutrophil function may vary across spatially distinct subclones within the tumor. Future studies employing spatial transcriptomics or genomics will be essential to delineate how intratumoral heterogeneity and local immune landscapes evolve in response to chemotherapy in HGSC.
In conclusion, we demonstrate that chemotherapy-induced IL1β-dependent neutrophil infiltration contributes to chemoresistance in OC, uncovering a tumor-extrinsic mechanism by which therapy-induced inflammation undermines therapeutic efficacy. Targeting the IL1β-neutrophil-NETs axis may represent a promising strategy to overcome chemoresistance in OC.
