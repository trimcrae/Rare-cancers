## Abstract
This study investigated ERS-related gene expressions in CESC, identifying two molecular subtypes, P1 and P2, and constructing a precise prognostic model based on these subtypes. TCGA's whole-genome expression profiles were used to recognize these subtypes through the ConsensusClusterPlus method, further refining prognostic models with univariate and Lasso Cox regression analyses validated by the GSE39001 dataset. The study analyzed the expression distribution of ERS marker genes within T cell subgroups using scRNA-seq data (GSE168652), highlighting T cell diversity. The critical role of the CCL3 gene in prognostic models was examined explicitly in CD8 + T cells from healthy individuals and CESC patients. Elevated CCL3 levels were observed in patients' CD8 + T cells compared to healthy controls. Functional experiments involving CCL3 knockdown and overexpression in HeLa and SiHa CESC cell lines were conducted to investigate its impact on cell proliferation, migration, and invasion. These findings were subsequently validated in a nude mouse model. The results demonstrated that suppressing CCL3 inhibited cell proliferation, migration, and invasion significantly, while its overexpression promoted these processes. In the mouse model, CCL3 silencing reduced tumor growth and decreased Ki-67 labeling within the tumor tissues, indicating the therapeutic potential of targeting CCL3 in CESC treatment, possibly through CD8 + T cell regulation. This study contributes new prognostic assessment tools and personalized treatment options for CESC patients, paving the way for more targeted therapies in CESC by discovering the CCL3 gene, presenting significant clinical implications.

## Introduction
Cervical squamous cell carcinoma (CESC) represents a significant threat to global women's health, with 604,000 new cases diagnosed worldwide in 2020 (Fowler et al. ; Liu et al. ; Zhang et al. ; Wang et al. ). In the United States alone, approximately 13,800 individuals were diagnosed with CESC in 2021, leading to 4,290 deaths (Zhu et al. ; Warburton et al. ). While traditional treatments such as surgery, radiotherapy, and chemotherapy are effective in the early stages of cervical cancer, the five-year survival rate for patients with advanced, recurrent, and metastatic cervical cancer remains below 20% (Tang et al. ; Volkova et al. ; Tewari et al. ; Mileshkin et al. ). This situation underscores the need to explore new CESC prognostic markers and immunotherapy targets (Berek et al. , ; Berek et al. , ; Liao et al. ).
The endoplasmic reticulum (ER) plays a critical role in cellular physiology as the primary site of protein synthesis, folding, and modification (Ren et al. ; Ajoolabady et al. ; Krshnan et al. ). Various internal and external factors can compromise the ER's protein-folding capacity, triggering endoplasmic reticulum stress (ERS) characterized by the accumulation of unfolded or misfolded proteins (Park et al. ; Wang et al. ). A strong ERS response has been observed in several primary human cancers, including brain, breast, lung, and prostate (Raines and Huang ; Wang et al. ; Yang et al. ). Cancer cells enhance their tolerance to ERS, increasing their capabilities for survival, metastasis, angiogenesis, and immune suppression (Raines and Huang ; Xue et al. ; Mandula et al. ; Wei et al. ). Many stress factors within the tumor microenvironment (TME) disrupt the correct folding of ER proteins in stromal and malignant cells (Li et al. , , , ), revealing potential pathways to devise strategies to control or prevent drug resistance by understanding the TME in CESC (Wang et al. , ; Goenka et al. ; Massagué and Ganesh ).
Identifying molecular subtypes and constructing prognostic models are crucial for understanding the pathological mechanisms of CESC and guiding personalized treatment strategies (Yu et al. ; Hu et al. ). By analyzing whole-genome expression profiles, researchers can reveal various molecular subtypes of CESC, which play a significant role in predicting disease progression, treatment response, and patient prognosis (Lin et al. ; Osman et al. ; Rodney et al. ). The rapid development of bioinformatics and genomics technologies now allows for identifying tumor molecular characteristics and creating molecular marker-based prognostic models, offering more precise treatment and management plans for cervical cancer patients.
This study aims to delve into the expression patterns of endoplasmic reticulum stress-related genes (ERSRGs) in CESC and to explore molecular subtyping in CESC patients based on these patterns. Our goal is to establish prognostic models among different subtypes to assess the prognosis of CESC patients. By integrating single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data, we aim to validate the association between CESC and its immune microenvironment (IME) and the clinical value of this relationship. Additionally, this research offers new directions for diagnosing and treating CESC by analyzing transcriptional and post-transcriptional network regulation, potential targeted drugs, and pathways related to the prognostic models. This study promises to provide powerful molecular tools for the prognosis assessment and personalized treatment of CESC but also may uncover new molecularly targeted treatment pathways, having significant scientific and clinical implications for improving the survival rate and quality of life of cervical cancer patients.

## Materials and methods

## Data collection and preprocessing
We systematically collected a comprehensive dataset on CESC from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA, https://portal.gdc.cancer.gov/) database. This dataset includes, but is not limited to, count-based expression profiles, fragments per kilobase million (FPKM) of exon model per million mapped fragments, somatic mutation data, and copy number variation (CNV) data from the Affymetrix SNP 6.0 array, covering 307 samples. Among these, 304 are CESC patient samples, and 3 are healthy control samples. These datasets provide rich gene expression information and include vital clinical data, with survival status available for 219 samples (alive) and 72 samples (deceased), laying the groundwork for subsequent survival analysis and model construction.
To further validate our findings and the generalizability of our prognostic models, we selected four independent external validation datasets from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/) database (Wolf et al. ): GSE7803, GSE63514, GSE9750, and GSE39001. These datasets, originating from different sequencing platforms, include 38 samples from the GPL96 platform (10 standard and 28 patient samples) for GSE7803, 128 samples from the GPL570 platform (24 standard and 104 patient samples) for GSE63514, 66 samples from the GPL96 platform (24 standard and 42 patient samples) for GSE9750, and 54 samples (15 deceased and 39 alive status samples) from the GPL201 and GPL6244 platforms for GSE39001 (Table  ), used for validating prognostic models. The selection and collection of these samples were based on their relevance to cervical cancer and the commonality of their human cervical tissue origin, ensuring consistency and comparability of research data. By downloading and preprocessing these large datasets, we established a solid data foundation for unveiling molecular subtypes of CESC, constructing prognostic models, and assessing the models' reliability and applicability.

 

## scRNA-seq data download and preprocessing
Initially, we downloaded the single-cell sequencing dataset GSE168652 from the GEO database (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo) (Table  ). Generated on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 sequencing platform, this dataset is tailored for CESC research and includes 13,104 cells from CESC patients and 11,394 cells from healthy controls, ensuring the study's breadth and depth.
To ensure data quality, we employed the Seurat R package for preprocessing steps on the downloaded single-cell data. We first removed low-quality cells with few unique molecular identifiers (UMIs) (< 200) and a high proportion of mitochondrial gene expression (> 10%), ensuring that the cells analyzed had sufficient informational content and lower rates of death or damage. Subsequently, we normalized the remaining high-quality cell data to mitigate the impact of technical variations, such as sequencing depth, on the analysis. We also identified highly variable genes, which exhibit substantial variation in expression across different cells. These genes are crucial for distinguishing between various cellular states and types.

## Identification and differential expression analysis of ERSRGs
Initially, we utilized the keyword "Endoplasmic Reticulum Stress" in the GeneCards database (https://www.genecards.org) (Li et al. , , , ) to identify a comprehensive set of 8,668 ERSRGs. This step was based on an extensive literature review and database search, and we aimed to compile a broad collection of ERSRGs (Table ).
Subsequently, we analyzed these ERSRGs for CNV using Affymetrix SNP 6.0 array data from the TCGA database. Employing GISTIC 2.0 software (https://cloud.genepattern.org) (Zhou et al. ) with a default confidence level of 0.9, we identified gene deletions or amplifications and their GISTIC scores. Furthermore, somatic mutation data for CESC samples were downloaded from the TCGA database and analyzed in-depth using the maftools R package to assess the somatic mutations in ERSRGs. With maftools (Cui et al. ), we visualized the GISTIC scores of ERSRGs across different chromosomes, thereby evaluating CNV.
Building on the identification and analysis of ERSRGs, we further examined the expression differences of these genes between CESC patient samples and healthy control samples. Using the DESeq2 R package (Khozyainova et al. ), we performed differential expression analysis between CESC patients and standard samples, setting the significance threshold at |log2 fold change (FC)|> 1 and adjusted P-value < 0.05. We identified upregulated and downregulated differentially expressed genes (DEGs) and utilized the ggplot2 R package to create a volcano plot, visually presenting the distribution of DEGs. Additionally, we employed the RCircos R package to plot the localization of the top 10 most significantly different ERSRGs on chromosomes (Mondin et al. ) based on gene location information downloaded from the GENCODE database (https://www.gencodegenes.org) (Li et al. , , , ).

## Unsupervised clustering and identification of molecular subtypes in CESC
To explore CESC's molecular heterogeneity, we employed unsupervised clustering methods to identify distinct molecular subtypes. By conducting a detailed analysis of CESC expression profile data from TCGA, we aimed to uncover the biological characteristics differentiating these molecular subtypes and their associations with patient prognosis.
Initially, we utilized the ConsensusClusterPlus package in R for hierarchical clustering of FPKM data derived from TCGA-CESC (https://portal.gdc.cancer.gov/). Based on the global patterns in expression data, this approach grouped samples into distinct molecular subtypes by calculating similarities between samples.
To visually present the clustering results and their biological significance, we conducted dimensionality reduction analysis using uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP), a sophisticated technique that maintains the structural characteristics of high-dimensional data in a lower-dimensional space, based on topological data analysis and manifold learning principles. By applying the umap map package in R to transform high-dimensional expression data into two or three-dimensional representations and visualizing these using the ggplot2 package, we facilitated an intuitive understanding of the similarities and differences between samples and the delineation among molecular subtypes.
Upon successfully identifying the molecular subtypes of CESC, we explored their functional characteristics. We employed the clusterProfiler package in R for GO and KEGG enrichment analysis to identify the roles of differential genes in biological processes (BP) (Li et al. , , , ), with a significance threshold set at p < 0.05. Additionally, we downloaded the reference gene set "c5.go.v7.4.entrez" from the MSigDB database and conducted enrichment and differential analysis using the GSVA and limma packages in R, setting p < 0.05 and |log2FC|> 0.2 as the criteria for significance. Significant pathways were visualized using the pheatmap package in R, further enhancing our understanding of the biological differences among ERS subtypes.

## Expression characteristics and genomic association of KRT31 and IGF2BP1 in CESC
In this study, we downloaded and processed RNAseq data from the TCGA-CESC project, focusing on TPM and FPKM format data, and obtained corresponding clinical information. Standard tissue samples were removed to ensure data standardization, and the expression data were log2-transformed (log2(value + 1)). We then performed a correlation analysis using Spearman's rank correlation coefficient to explore the relationships between variables. The results were visualized using the ggplot2 package (version 3.3.6) in R (version 4.2.1) by generating scatter plots that included regression lines to illustrate correlation trends. Additionally, we conducted heatmap visualization using the ComplexHeatmap package (version 2.13.1) to display the expression patterns of KRT31 and IGF2BP1 genes across different samples (Gu et al. ). During the heatmap generation, rows were normalized, and both rows and columns were clustered using Euclidean distance.

## Immune microenvironment (IME) Analysis
To investigate the role of the IME across different ERS subtypes in CESC, this study employed the CIBERSORTx tool (Chen et al. ) and utilized the LM22 signature to assess and compare the infiltration levels of immune cells among various ERS subtypes. CIBERSORTx, an advanced bioinformatics tool, estimates the relative abundance of different immune cell types in samples based on gene expression data. In this study, we focused on quantifying 22 types of immune cells, including T cells, B cells, natural killer (NK) cells, macrophages, etc., which play crucial roles in the tumor IME.
By integrating FPKM data from TCGA-CESC with CIBERSORTx analysis, we calculated the abundance of these immune cells in each sample to accurately reflect the levels of immune cell infiltration across different ERS subtypes. To investigate differences in immune status between prognostic groups, we used ESTIMATE scores derived from expression data to assess the tumor's stromal and immune cell presence (Daoud and Mayo ). For a more intuitive presentation of the differences in immune cell infiltration levels among ERS subtypes, we employed the ggpubr R package to create box plots. These box plots provide a visual means to compare the abundance of each type of immune cell across subtypes, revealing potential associations between ERS subtypes and levels of immune cell infiltration.
Furthermore, to explore the relationship between the overall survival (OS) of patients with different ERS subtypes and the level of immune cell infiltration, we conducted survival analysis using the survival and survminer R packages. By comparing survival curves of patients across subtypes, we assessed the impact of immune cell infiltration levels on patient prognosis, offering valuable biomarkers and therapeutic targets for future clinical decision-making.

## Construction and validation of prognostic models
In this study, to construct a model that accurately predicts the prognosis of CESC patients, we first used univariate Cox regression to screen for ERSRGs that are significantly associated with prognosis and survival, with a selection criterion of p < 0.05. Here, HR > 1 indicates that high expression of the gene is associated with a higher risk of death (poor prognosis), while HR < 1 indicates that high expression correlates with a lower risk of death (favorable prognosis). This step was conducted using the R package survival. Next, to refine the gene set for the prognostic model, we applied Lasso Cox regression using the R package glmnet, selecting the optimal λ through cross-validation and retaining variables with non-zero coefficients, which were considered features significantly associated with survival.
After determining the gene set significantly associated with prognosis, we constructed the prognostic model with these genes and calculated the optimal cut-off point using the surv_cutpoint function in the R package survminer, classifying CESC patients into a High Score Group (HSG) and Low Score Group (LSG) (Wang et al. ). This cut-off point is based on patients' gene expression patterns and aims to maximize prognostic differences between the two groups. To evaluate the accuracy and reliability of the prognostic models, we conducted a Kaplan–Meier survival analysis and compared the OS times between HSG and LSG patients using the log-rank test. Additionally, we plotted the model's receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves for 3-year and 5-year periods using the time-dependent receiver operating characteristic (timeROC) package and calculated the area under the curve (AUC) to quantify the model's predictive performance. To further validate the model's predictive accuracy, we also validated independent GEO datasets, applying the surv_cutpoint function for grouping and assessing predictive capability through survival analysis and ROC curves.

## Cox regression analysis of prognostic models with clinical features and drug sensitivity analysis
Additionally, to explore associations between the prognostic model and molecular characteristics, we performed univariate and multivariate Cox regression analysis of the prognostic model with patients’ clinical characteristics (e.g., age and TNM stage). Specifically, the prognostic model was validated using internal TCGA-CESC and external GSE39001 datasets. In univariate Cox regression analysis, each variable (such as age, TNM stage, and ERS grouping) was independently included to calculate its HR and p-value with OS, identifying features significantly associated with survival. For multivariate Cox regression, we used stepwise regression with Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) as the optimization standard, starting with potentially significant variables from univariate analysis and iteratively selecting variables until the model reached its optimal form. The final multivariate model included prognostic scores and selected clinical characteristics, allowing assessment of each variable’s independent predictive power after controlling for other factors, with HR and p-values indicating statistical significance.
Moreover, leveraging TCGA-CESC's FPKM expression profiles and prognosis group information, we predicted the sensitivity to four common anticancer drugs (axitinib, erlotinib, imatinib, and metformin) using pRRophetic. Box plots for drug sensitivity based on prognosis grouping were created using the ggplot2 and ggpubr R packages. Drug sensitivity differences between groups were compared using the Wilcoxon rank-sum test. We downloaded drug-target pathway relationships from the Therapeutic Target Database (TTD) (https://db.idrblab.net/ttd) (Bhattacharjee et al. ) and extracted drug-pathway relationships relevant to these four drugs. The results were imported into Cytoscape for processing and network diagram creation.

## Transcriptional and post-transcriptional regulatory network analysis
In this study, we conducted in-depth transcriptional and post-transcriptional regulatory network analyses to comprehensively understand the regulatory mechanisms of genes within our prognostic models. It involved identifying DNA motifs near transcription start sites and exploring interactions between transcription factors (TFs), microRNAs (miRNAs), and their target genes.
Initially, we utilized the RcisTarget package in R to identify DNA motifs expressed around the transcription start sites of genes relevant to our prognostic models. By analyzing these motifs, we identified potential TFs and inferred their possible impact on gene expression. We focused mainly on motifs with a normalized enrichment score (NES) greater than 3, deemed highly relevant to gene expression regulation. Importing identified TF-mRNA relationships into Cytoscape, we constructed and visualized a transcriptional regulatory network, facilitating an intuitive understanding of how TFs influence gene expression within the prognostic models.
Next, we employed the multiMiR package in R to analyze relationships between miRNAs and their target genes. multiMiR, a comprehensive tool, predicts interactions between miRNAs and their target genes. In this step, we retained miRNA-mRNA pairs with prediction scores above 90, and these relationships were imported into Cytoscape for network mapping and analysis (Elias et al. ; Hosseinpour et al. ). Furthermore, we visualized the predicted sources of miRNAs using the UpSetR package, offering a more comprehensive insight into the complex relationships between miRNA and gene expression regulation (Durkalec-Michalski et al. ).
Lastly, to explore post-transcriptional regulatory mechanisms thoroughly, we downloaded RNA-binding protein (RBP) target gene pairs related to genes in our prognostic models from the ENCORI database (https://rna.sysu.edu.cn/encori) (Zhou et al. ). Analyzing these pairs revealed how RBPs influence gene stability and translation efficiency through interactions with mRNA, affecting cellular functions and disease progression. Importing these RBP-mRNA pairs into Cytoscape, we depicted the post-transcriptional regulatory network, offering new perspectives on the complexity of gene regulation within our prognostic models.

## Single-cell data quality control and clustering analysis
Quality control, normalization, and clustering analysis of the single-cell dataset GSE168652 were conducted to deeply understand the cellular heterogeneity within CESC. Cells were annotated by type using the SingleR package. This study meticulously processed scRNA-seq data from GSE168652 through quality control and clustering analysis using the Seurat package, ensuring the accuracy and reliability of the analysis.
Quality control measures were implemented for each cell in the dataset, including removing cells with fewer than 200 UMIs or more than 10% mitochondrial gene expression (Hao et al. ). This step aimed to exclude low-quality data potentially resulting from technical issues, thus ensuring the data quality for subsequent analysis. We normalized the data using the LogNormalize method via the NormalizeData function to eliminate variations in sequencing depth across cells. Following normalization, high variability genes exhibiting significant expression differences across cells were selected using the FindVariableFeatures function, laying the groundwork for dimension reduction and clustering analysis. Principal component analysis (PCA) was employed for feature extraction, selecting the top 30 principal components as recommended in the literature. PCA analysis helped reduce data dimensions while preserving primary variance information within the data (Li et al. ). Using the FindClusters function from the Seurat package and applying the Louvain algorithm, we performed unsupervised clustering to identify distinct cell populations. Subsequently, the UMAP technique was used to visualize the clustering results in two dimensions, intuitively showcasing the distribution of different cell populations.
To accurately annotate the identified cell populations, the SingleR package was utilized, referencing the Human Primary Cell Atlas Data, Cell Taxonomy database, and marker genes information provided in the original literature (Jiang et al. ). By comparing cell-specific expression patterns, we successfully associated cell clustering results with specific cell types, such as endothelial cells, epithelial cells, monocytes, smooth muscle cells, and T cells.

## T cell subgroup analysis
Utilizing the single-cell dataset GSE168652, we re-clustered the T cell populations using the Seurat package in R. High variability genes were identified with the FindVariableFeatures function using the "vst" method, retaining the top 2000 highly variable genes. The ScaleData function was employed for data scaling. Subsequently, PCA was applied to the highly variable genes for feature extraction, selecting the top 30 principal components and a resolution of 0.3. The FindAllMarkers function was used to identify marker genes for each cell cluster, setting an adjusted p-value of < 0.05 and |log2FC|> 1 as significance thresholds. Cell types were annotated using cell classification databases. Marker genes for CD8 + T cells included CD8A, GNLY, GZMA, and GZMB; for CD4 + T cells, CD4 + , IL7R, FOXP3, and TNFRSF4 were identified. The intersection of ERSRGs from the GeneCards database and marker genes of various cell clusters were visualized using the UMAP method.
The accuracy of the single-cell results was validated by inferring and calculating the CD8 + T cell content in each sample based on the bulk dataset GSE63514 and the LM22 background gene set provided by CIBERSORTx. Box plots for cancer and standard samples were generated using R's ggplot2 and ggpubr packages. The accuracy of the single-cell results was further validated from multiple angles using bulk datasets GSE7803 and GSE9750 and the LM22 background gene set from CIBERSORTx to infer and calculate CD8 + T cell content. Pearson correlation between ERS marker genes and CD8 + T immune cell content was calculated, and correlation heatmaps were visualized using ggplot2.
Enrichment analysis was conducted on the single-cell dataset GSE168652 and the reference gene set "c5.go.v7.4.entrez" downloaded from the MSigDB database (https://www.gsea-msigdb.org/gsea/msigdb) (Vasilopoulou et al. ) using the GSVA package in R, with differential analysis performed by the limma package. Significant differential pathways were defined by |log2FC|> 0.2 and p < 0.05, and bar charts were visualized using ggplot2.
Single-cell regulatory network inference and clustering (SCENIC) is a method for inferring regulator activity based on co-expression and motif analysis. Co-expression modules were constructed using the single-cell dataset GSE168652 and the SCENIC package in R to calculate cell regulatory activity scores. Differential analysis of regulatory activity scores in CD4 + and CD8 + T cells was then performed using the limma package, with an adjusted p < 0.05 considered statistically significant for regulators. Visualization was achieved through UMAP plots and heatmaps using the pheatmap package in R.

## Pseudotime trajectory analysis
Utilizing the Monocle R package for pseudotime trajectory analysis of T cells, we constructed developmental trajectories to explore their dynamic changes throughout the progression of CESC (Xu et al. ). By selecting highly variable genes and applying dimension reduction with the DDRTree method, we successfully established trajectories of cell states. The orderCells function was used to sequence cells, illustrating the transition process of T cells from an initial to a mature state. This analysis revealed the temporal dynamics of T cells in the immune response to cervical cancer and provided valuable insights into how they influence disease progression at different stages.

## Transcriptional regulatory analysis of T cell ERS marker genes
To further investigate the developmental process of T cells from a dynamic perspective, we conducted a pseudotime trajectory analysis using the Monocle package (Xu et al. ) based on the single-cell dataset GSE168652. Highly variable genes were selected as feature genes, and data dimensionality was reduced using the reduceDimension function with the DDRTree method. Cell developmental trajectories were established using the order of cell function. The pseudotime inference results were visualized from both cellular state and pseudotime aspects. Based on trajectory inference, Monocle automatically categorized cell states primarily based on branches and bifurcation points. Monocle calculated a pseudotime value for each cell based on changes in gene expression, arranging each cell along the developmental trajectory according to their pseudotime values, thereby revealing the cell development process. Consequently, the trajectory was colored based on pseudotime values, visually displaying the cell development process.

## Drug target analysis for T cell ERS markers
To identify drugs associated with T cell ERS marker genes, drug-target relationship pairs were downloaded from the TTD and cross-referenced with T cell ERS marker genes to obtain drug-mRNA pairs. The results were then processed and visualized in Cytoscape to construct network diagrams. Further, drug-pathway relationship pairs related to these drugs, also obtained from TTD, were extracted for drugs associated with T cell ERS marker genes. These pairs were imported into Cytoscape, and the MCODE plugin (Liang et al. ) was utilized with criteria set to a degree cutoff of 2, a maximum depth of 100, a node score cutoff of 0.2, and a K-Core of 2 to filter the highest scoring modules, identifying key hub pathways. Additionally, drug-miRNA relationship pairs associated with these drugs were extracted from the SM2miR database (http://www.jianglab.cn/SM2miR) (You et al. ). These pairs were imported into Cytoscape, and key subnetworks were identified using the cytoHubba plugin (Ma et al. ) based on the top 8 nodes by degree.

## Cell culture
Human cervical cancer cell lines, HeLa (catalog number: CRM-CCL-2, acquired from ATCC) and SiHa (catalog number: HTB-35), were cultured in Dulbecco's modified eagle medium (catalog number: 11965084, Gibco), enriched with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) (catalog number: 10099–141, Gibco) and 1% antibiotics (catalog number: V900929, Sigma-Aldrich, USA). This medium provided the necessary nutrients for cell growth and protection against bacterial contamination. Cells were incubated at a constant temperature of 37 °C with 5% CO2 in a cell culture incubator until approximately 70% confluency was achieved, ensuring cells were in optimal condition before transfection. Once the appropriate confluency was reached, cells were prepared for subsequent genetic manipulation experiments.

## Cell transfection and primer design
Before cell transfection, shRNA primers targeting the CCL3 gene were meticulously designed based on the gene's sequence, utilizing online bioinformatics tools such as NCBI BLAST and RNAi design software to select specific target regions. It ensures the shRNA can specifically silence the CCL3 gene without affecting the expression of other genes. The designed shRNA sequences, sh-1 (5' ACCTCGGTTAGACCACATCAATCTTTTTCAAGAGAAAAGATTGATGTGGTCTAACCTT 3') and sh-2 (5' ACCTCGAAGAGAACTGAGATAAGAGATTCAAGAGATCTCTTATCTCAGTTCTCTTCTT 3') target different coding regions of the CCL3 gene to enhance silencing efficiency and minimize false-positive outcomes. These primers were synthesized by Sigma-Aldrich, USA, and cloned into vectors suitable for cell transfection. In knockdown experiments, scrambled shRNA was used as the control.
For CCL3 gene overexpression, the full-length cDNA sequence of CCL3 was cloned into the expression vector pCMV-CCL3. This vector, containing a CMV immediate-early promoter, can efficiently express the target gene in mammalian cells. We constructed the CCL3 overexpression vector (pCMV-CCL3, AOGMA, China) and used the corresponding empty vector (pCMV-Vector, product number: AGM-P1419, AOGMA, China) as a negative control to counteract non-specific effects in overexpression experiments.
Lipofectamine 3000 (Invitrogen, USA, product number: L3000-008) was used for cell transfection, a highly efficient reagent capable of safely delivering shRNA or overexpression vectors into HeLa and SiHa cells. The transfection process followed the manufacturer's standard protocol: DNA was first mixed with Lipofectamine 3000 to form complexes and then added to cells pre-cultured to 70% confluency. Cells were used for subsequent EdU proliferation experiments or other relevant assays 48 h post-transfection.

## Peripheral blood mononuclear Cell (PBMC) isolation
PBMCs were isolated using the Ficoll-Paque PLUS density gradient centrifugation method, employing the Ficoll-Paque PLUS density gradient medium (GE Healthcare, USA, catalog number: 17–1440-03). Fresh peripheral blood samples were collected from healthy volunteers and cervical cancer patients using EDTA-containing blood collection tubes (BD Biosciences, USA, catalog number 367861) to prevent coagulation. The collected blood was diluted at a 1:1 ratio with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) (Gibco, USA, catalog number: 10010–023), then gently layered over Ficoll-Paque PLUS medium in centrifuge tubes. Centrifugation was performed at 400 g for 30 min at room temperature, without brake. Post-centrifugation, the PBMC layer, located between the plasma and Ficoll layers, was carefully aspirated and transferred to new centrifuge tubes for at least two washes with PBS (300 g for 10 min each). Finally, PBMCs were resuspended in RPMI 1640 medium (Gibco, USA, catalog number: 11875–093) supplemented with 10% FBS (Gibco, USA, catalog number: 10099–141) and 1% penicillin–streptomycin solution (Gibco, USA, catalog number: 15140–122). Cell count and viability were assessed using a hemocytometer or an automated cell counter in preparation for subsequent experiments. This procedure was repeated three times to ensure data reliability.
All operations were conducted under aseptic conditions to prevent sample contamination and appropriately handle all biohazard materials. This study strictly adhered to medical ethical principles, with written informed consent obtained from all participants. The relevant ethics committee approved the research protocol (Ethics number: 2024-KS-024–01). The privacy and confidentiality of all participants were respected, and all data was processed anonymously. The sample collection procedure was designed to minimize inconvenience and risk to participants. We ensured that all experimental practices complied with national and international guidelines on biosafety and bioethics.

## Isolation of CD8 + T cells
CD8 + T cells were isolated from PBMCs collected from healthy volunteers and cervical cancer patients using the magnetic-activated cell sorting (MACS) system and anti-CD8 microbeads provided by Miltenyi Biotec (catalog number 130–045–201). Initially, the extracted PBMCs were resuspended in PBS buffer containing 0.5% bovine serum albumin (BSA) and 2 mM EDTA, followed by the addition of anti-CD8 microbeads and incubation at 4 °C for 15 min to allow binding of the beads to the CD8 molecules on the cell surface. After incubation, the cell suspension was passed through a MACS separation column (catalog number: 130–042–201) fitted in a separator (catalog number: 130–042–401) to capture the labeled CD8 + T cells magnetically. The CD8 + T cells were then released and collected by adding MACS buffer and gently pushing through with a plastic plunger. The quantity and viability of the isolated CD8 + T cells were assessed in preparation for further experiments. This process was carried out under sterile conditions, following the manufacturer's protocol to optimize cell separation outcomes. The experiment was repeated at least three times to ensure the reliability and reproducibility of the results.

## Flow cytometry
In this study, flow cytometry was employed for the specific labeling and separation of CD8 + T cells, with precise analysis of CCL3 (chemokine) expression levels within this cell population by setting appropriate gates. Intracellular staining was performed using a specific antibody against CCL3 (CCL3/MIP-1 alpha Antibody Clone 24,006, catalog number: MAB270, R&D Systems), followed by the calculation of the percentage of CCL3 positive cells and mean fluorescence intensity (MFI) using flow cytometry data analysis software.

## Western blot analysis
The efficiency of CCL3 gene overexpression and silencing in HeLa and SiHa cell lines was evaluated through experiments involving cell culture, genetic manipulation, protein extraction, concentration determination, and Western blot analysis. Cells were cultured in Dulbecco's modified eagle medium supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% antibiotics until approximately 70% confluency was reached. ShRNA or overexpression vectors were transformed into HeLa and SiHa cells using Lipofectamine 3000. Forty-eight hours post-transfection, proteins were extracted and quantified using the Pierce™ BCA protein assay kit (catalog number: 23225, Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) to ensure equal protein loading in the Western blot. Protein separation was conducted using the Bio-Rad Mini-PROTEAN Tetra system and SDS-PAGE, followed by protein transfer to PVDF membranes (Millipore, catalog number: IPVH00010, USA). Membranes were blocked with 5% non-fat milk in TBST, incubated overnight with a CCL3-specific primary antibody (Abcam, catalog number: ab259372), and then detected with HRP-conjugated secondary antibody goat anti-rabbit IgG H&L (Abcam, catalog number: ab150077). Signal development was performed using ECL Western blot detection reagents (GE Healthcare, USA, catalog number: RPN2106), and protein expression levels were quantitatively analyzed using imaging analysis software.

## EdU assay
EdU (5-ethynyl-2'-deoxyuridine) staining was conducted using the Click-iT EdU Alexa Fluor 488 imaging kit (Invitrogen, USA, catalog number: C10329) following the manufacturer's instructions. After a 4-h incubation, cells were fixed and stained. Images were captured using a fluorescence microscope (Nikon, Japan), and the percentage of EdU-positive cells was calculated using ImageJ software (NIH, USA) to assess cell proliferation rates. This procedure was repeated three times to ensure the reliability of the results.

## Transwell migration and invasion assays
Migration assays were conducted using Transwell filters without a matrix gel coating (8 μm pore size, Corning, USA, catalog number: 3422). For invasion assays, the upper chamber membranes of the Transwell were pre-coated with Matrigel (BD Biosciences, USA, catalog number 354230) to simulate the extracellular matrix. Cells were seeded at a density of 1 × 105 cells/well, with DMEM containing 10% FBS added to the lower chamber as a chemoattractant. After 24 h of incubation, cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde and stained with 0.1% crystal violet, followed by counting under a light microscope. The number of migrated and invaded cells was assessed by averaging the counts from five random fields.

## Nude mouse tumorigenicity assay
Following the 4Rs principle in animal experiments (Reduction, Refinement, Replacement, Responsibility), we chose 6 mice per group to ensure statistical validity while minimizing the number of experimental animals. Female BALB/c nude mice aged 4–6 weeks (n = 6 per group) were obtained from Slack Experimental Animal Co., Ltd., Shanghai, and housed under humane and ethical standards. A random number table method was used to assign mice to predefined groups with equal probability, with gender stratified and body weight balanced to within 20% of the average weight. Mice were injected with HeLa cell suspensions treated with either CCL3 silencing or overexpression (1 × 106 cells/100 μL PBS) into the flank. During outcome assessment, blinding was implemented to reduce bias. Tumor length (L) and width (W) were measured every three days using a digital caliper, with tumor volume (V) calculated as V = (L × W2)/2. The experiment lasted 4 weeks, after which tumors were collected and weighed. This study strictly adhered to ethical guidelines, ensuring that all experimental procedures complied with relevant ethics regulations, particularly following the Animal Welfare Act and the Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals to ensure proper care and minimize unnecessary suffering. The Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) approved the experimental protocol, with approval number [HB2005007; 20,240,304–04]. All animal experiments were conducted under strictly controlled conditions to ensure animal welfare, with measures to minimize animal discomfort.

## Histological analysis
Tumor tissues harvested from nude mice were fixed in 4% formaldehyde, subsequently embedded in paraffin, and sectioned (4 μm thick). Sections were stained with H&E and immunohistochemically stained for Ki-67 (Ki-67 antibody, Abcam, UK, catalog number ab15580) to assess tissue morphology and cell proliferation. Images were captured using a light microscope (Nikon, Japan).

## Statistical analysis
In this study, all statistical analyses were conducted using the R programming language, incorporating a variety of statistical tests and model evaluation methods to ensure the accuracy and reliability of the research findings. Specific methodologies included the independent sample t-test for comparing mean differences between two groups, with the Wilcoxon rank-sum test applied for non-normally distributed data. The Kaplan–Meier survival analysis method was utilized to evaluate the survival outcomes of patients with CESC, and differences in survival among patients with different molecular subtypes were compared using the log-rank test. Additionally, Cox regression analysis was employed to investigate the impact of ERSRGs on patient prognosis and construct prognostic models. All statistical tests were two-sided, with a significance level set at P < 0.05 to determine the significance of the results. For quantitative data from the EdU proliferation experiment and Ki-67 immunohistochemical staining, one-way ANOVA was applied to ascertain statistically significant differences between treatment groups, followed by Tukey's HSD test for multiple comparisons upon detecting significance. Non-parametric Wilcoxon rank-sum tests were used for data distributions that did not meet normality. Differences between groups in the Transwell migration and invasion assays were assessed using independent sample t-tests. For tumor growth data in the nude mouse tumorigenicity assays, repeated measures ANOVA was utilized to analyze tumor volume changes over different time points. The students' t-test was applied to statistically compare tumor weights at the experiment's conclusion.

## Genomic variations, expression differences, and chromosomal localization of ERSRGs in CESC
In order to study the key genes and pathways in cervical squamous cell carcinoma and reveal their potential roles in disease development, we extensively investigated the genomic variations, expression differences, and chromosomal localization of endoplasmic reticulum stress-related genes (ERSRGs) in cervical squamous cell carcinoma. By visualizing the distribution of GISTIC scores for different genes across various chromosomes, the results revealed distinct patterns of amplification or deletion of ERSRGs on different chromosomes. Specifically, significant gene deletions of ERSRGs were observed on chromosome 2, whereas notable gene amplifications were found on chromosomes 3 and 11 (Fig.  A). Utilizing somatic mutation data from the TCGA-CESC dataset, we observed that the most frequent mutation types were missense mutations and single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), with C > T being the predominant SNP type. The top ten mutated genes were TTN, PIK3CA, MUC16, KMT2C, KMT2D, SYNE1, FLG, EP300, DMD, and FBXW7 (Fig.  B). An intersection operation with ERSGRs, based on gene expression data from TCGA-CESC, identified 1908 differentially expressed ERSGRs between normal and cervical cancer samples, including 1160 upregulated and 748 downregulated genes (Fig.  C), with the threshold set to |log2 FC|> 1 and adjusted P < 0.05. Furthermore, the chromosomal locations of the top ten genes (including both upregulated and downregulated genes) by |log2 FC| were mapped (Fig.  D), revealing genes like TERT and HAPLN1 on chromosome 5, CALML5 on chromosome 10, ATP12A and ZIC2 on chromosome 13, KRT31 and IGF2BP1 on chromosome 17, DSG1 on chromosome 18, and CASP14 and LGALS7 on chromosome 19. It was noted that some genes, such as KRT31 and IGF2BP1, shared identical chromosomal positions, suggesting similar expression characteristics at the transcriptomic level and a close genomic association. KRT31 and IGF2BP1 exhibit a significant expression correlation in cervical squamous cell carcinoma samples (Pearson correlation coefficient r = −0.461, p < 0.001), indicating that they share similar expression characteristics at the transcriptome level. Additionally, indicating that they share similar expression characteristics at the transcriptome level (Fig.  E). Additionally, expression profile analysis revealed that these two genes display similar expression patterns across different molecular subtypes, further supporting their close association at the genomic level(Fig.  F). These analyses highlight the significance of ERSGRs in the development of cervical cancer and provide crucial insights into their specific roles in the disease process.

## Expression differences, functional enrichment, and immune infiltration characteristics of ERS subtypes in CESC
After identifying the key roles of prognostic ERSRGs in cervical squamous cell carcinoma, we conducted further in-depth exploration and analysis.  We conducted GSVA on samples categorized by ERS subtypes to calculate the enrichment scores of pathways within the samples. ## Impact of CCL3 gene regulation on the biological properties of cervical cancer cells
Following bioinformatics analysis, we collected peripheral blood from healthy individuals and patients with cervical squamous carcinoma, extracting CD8 + T cells to assess the expression of the ERS marker gene, CCL3. CD8 + T cells are a crucial component of the adaptive immune system, playing a vital role in the immune defense against pathogens such as viruses, bacteria, and tumors (Zhang and Bevan ). Flow cytometry revealed a significant upregulation of CCL3 in CD8 + T cells from patients with cervical squamous carcinoma (Fig.  A). In cervical cancer cell lines HeLa and SiHa, we manipulated CCL3 expression through silencing (using shRNA primers sh-1 and sh-2) and overexpression (employing a CCL3 overexpression vector) (Fig.  B). The EdU proliferation assay demonstrated that silencing CCL3 reduced the percentage of EdU-positive cells markedly, indicating suppressed cell proliferation, while overexpressing CCL3 had the opposite effect, significantly increasing the percentage of EdU-positive cells, thereby enhancing cell proliferation capabilities (Fig.  C). Transwell migration and invasion assays showed that compared to the sh-NC control group, the CCL3-silenced group exhibited significantly fewer migrating and invading cells, whereas the CCL3-overexpressed group displayed increased cell migration and invasion (Fig.  D). Tumor formation experiments in nude mice indicated that tumors formed by CCL3-silenced HeLa cells were significantly smaller in volume and weight than in the sh-NC group, and the tumor growth rate decreased (Fig.  E). Lastly, histological analysis revealed a milder degree of disorganization in tumor tissue structure in the CCL3-silenced group, with a reduced proportion of Ki-67-labeled proliferating cells (Fig.  F). The result indicates that silencing the CCL3 gene leads to reduced cell proliferation, directly demonstrating the positive correlation between CCL3 and Ki-67. These experimental outcomes suggest that CCL3 acts as a potential regulator of cervical cancer cell proliferation and invasion capabilities, likely through the modulation of CD8 + T cell expression.

These insights not only shed light on the heterogeneity and complexity of T cells in cervical cancer but also provide crucial information for further exploration of T cell functions and their interaction with the tumor IME in cervical cancer.

## Analysis of the transcriptional regulatory network of ERS marker genes in cervical cancer T cells and potential drug targeting
To identify the transcriptional regulatory network and potential drug targets of ER stress marker genes in T cells in cervical cancer, we constructed a TF regulatory network based on T cell ER stress marker genes by predicting TF-mRNA relationships using the R package RcisTarget and visualized the network with Cytoscape (Fig.  A). Visualization revealed one-to-many and one-to-one regulatory relationships between TFs and mRNAs, such as HSF4-BGN, HSF1-BGN, SRF-ACTR3, and SRF-CD69.
 Further, using the R package multiMiR, we predicted miRNA-mRNA relationships to build a post-transcriptional regulatory network for T cell ERS marker genes, which was also visualized with Cytoscape (Figure ). This visualization highlighted one-to-one and one-to-many regulatory relationships between miRNAs and mRNAs, including interactions like PBX1 with miR-450b-5p, miR-129-5p, and others. Most miRNAs identified came from the miRDB database (Fig.  B).
Drug-target relationship pairs were downloaded from the TTD, and a drug-mRNA regulatory network related to T cell ERS marker genes was created and visualized with Cytoscape. This network showed that each target could be affected by multiple drugs, with shared targets among them, such as Eicosapentaenoic acid/docosahexaenoic acid and Naproxen (Fig.  C).
Therapeutic target database information was utilized to map drug-target pathways relevant to T cell ERS marker genes. A drug-target pathway regulation network was constructed, highlighting the interaction between specific drugs and pathways. Utilizing the Cytoscape plugin MCODE, the most significant modules within this network were identified, demonstrating that each drug could influence multiple pathways with notable overlap in areas such as Alseroxylon, Valbenazine Tosylate, Dopaminergic synapse, Synaptic vessel cycle, and Cocaine addiction (Fig.  D)—further analysis involved downloading drug-targeted miRNA pairs from the SM2miR database to construct a drug-targeted miRNA regulation network. Key subnetworks were extracted using the cytoHubba plugin in Cytoscape, showing that drugs like Fluorouracil and Diethylstilbestrol affect multiple miRNAs, including hsa-miR-15b and hsa-miR-195, indicating potential drug interactions (Fig.  E).

## Discussion
Our study utilized unsupervised clustering analysis based on the expression profiles of 308 prognosis-related ERSRGs to reveal the diverse expression patterns of ERS in CESC patients. Previous studies in oral squamous cell carcinoma (OSCC) employed weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA) to successfully identify key modules and hub genes (Zhang et al. ). Similarly, our study identified two significant ERS-related subgroups, further underscoring the importance of ERS genes in cancer progression. Unlike prior studies, we not only identified two distinct ERS-related subgroups but also found that patients in the P1 subgroup had significantly higher levels of CD8 + T cells, suggesting that increased immune infiltration may contribute to a better prognosis in this group. This finding is consistent with previous research on the role of CD8 + T cells in anti-tumor immunity (Peng et al. ; Guan et al. , ; Cillo et al. ; Fujiwara et al. ; Que et al. ), emphasizing the potential role of ERS in modulating the IME.
Utilizing the TCGA-CESC dataset, we developed a prognostic model of ERS subtypes comprising 32 genes. The roles of essential genes such as AQP3, BMP6, BMP7, GABRP, and GAPT in the immune infiltration process have been documented (Luo et al. ; Shi et al. , ; Yang et al. ); however, their specific functions in CESC remain underexplored. The prognostic model was validated using the GSE39001 dataset, and its predictive accuracy was assessed through timeROC analysis, offering a robust predictive tool for the 3-year and 5-year prognosis of cervical cancer patients.
This study explored transcriptional and post-transcriptional regulatory patterns associated with prognostic models in CESC by predicting TF-mRNA, RBP-mRNA, and miRNA-mRNA interactions. For instance, BRSK1 was upregulated in cervical cancer patients treated with cisplatin, and knocking down BRSK1 enhanced sensitivity to cisplatin treatment. The upregulation of U2AF2, an RNA-binding protein of BRSK1, in tumor tissues was significantly correlated with metastasis, tumor staging, and poor prognosis, suggesting that U2AF2 may promote tumor progression by modulating the pre-mRNA splicing and 3' end processing of BRSK1.
Utilizing scRNA-seq datasets, we delved into the heterogeneity of T cells, uncovering four T cell subgroups consistent with previous findings (Zheng et al. ), reinforcing the role of ERS in regulating the IME. Moreover, distinct regulators showed high activity in CD4 + and CD8 + T cells, findings echoed in lung adenocarcinoma (Guan et al. , ; Zheng et al. ; Song et al. ; Sureshchandra et al. ), warranting further investigation in CESC.
Research has shown that CCL3 enhances tumor rejection via NK cells and promotes CD8 + T cell infiltration, while IFNγ facilitates the recruitment of CD103 + dendritic cells (Allen et al. ). Our results indicate that six ER stress marker genes (CCL3, GZMB, GZMA, IFNG, KLRC1, and PRF1) exhibit high expression in CESC samples and CD8 + T cells (Table  , Fig.  E). There were significant changes in immune cell infiltration levels, including CD8 + T cells, between ERS subtypes (Fig.  E), and data from large datasets GSE7803 and GSE9750 confirmed that these genes, including CCL3, are positively correlated with CD8 + T cell infiltration levels (Fig.  G). We hypothesize that this effect may be mediated by NK cells enhancing tumor rejection, as suggested by inflammatory responses in NK cell immune infiltration levels (Fig.  E).
This research establishes molecular subtypes and prognostic models based on ERS-related genes in CESC, offering new insights for diagnosis and treatment. Identifying molecular subtypes and constructing prognostic models provide a scientific basis for personalized treatment strategies for CESC patients, enabling physicians to devise more precise treatments based on molecular characteristics. Our results indicate a positive correlation between the expression of the CCL3 gene and Ki-67, which may be associated with increased tumor cell proliferation. This finding is consistent with previous studies (Hartmann et al. ). Our discoveries could serve as potential targets for future therapies, opening new avenues to improve the survival and quality of life for CESC patients. These findings highlight the significance of ERS in tumor development and immune regulation, laying a theoretical foundation for developing novel treatment strategies, particularly in immunotherapy.
Despite the significant progress made in uncovering the molecular mechanisms and prognostic assessment of CESC, this study has certain limitations. First, the analysis primarily relies on retrospective data from public databases, which may introduce selection bias, limiting the generalizability of the results. Second, although the prognostic model was constructed and preliminarily validated using bioinformatics methods, further validation of the model and the functions and mechanisms of key genes (such as CCL3) is needed in additional clinical samples and experimental models. For example, using pathway analysis with inhibitors or gene knockdown could provide valuable insights into how CCL3 regulates cell proliferation and invasion through CD8 + T cells. We plan to conduct future experiments using CCL3-targeted inhibitors and gene knockdown techniques to analyze specific pathways and confirm its regulatory role in CD8 + T cells. Furthermore, this study did not account for all possible factors affecting CESC prognosis, such as patient lifestyle and genetic background, which also play crucial roles in disease impact.
Future research should address these limitations by employing prospective designs and larger clinical sample sizes to validate the findings of this study. In particular, the specific roles and mechanisms of essential genes like CCL3 in CESC development demand deeper exploration through more comprehensive molecular biology experiments. Additionally, the effectiveness and feasibility of the prognostic models based on ERS-related genes for clinical application warrant further clinical investigation. Although our findings reveal the potential role of CCL3 as a regulatory factor in cervical cancer cell proliferation and invasion, the underlying mechanism may involve regulating CD8 + T cell expression. However, if drugs target CCL3, CD8 + T cells could be suppressed, which may lead to a decrease in the immune system's overall function. It could pose a challenge by compromising the body's immune response. Looking ahead, discovering new therapeutic drugs targeting ERS-related pathways and integrating these treatment strategies with existing modalities (e.g., surgery, radiotherapy, chemotherapy, and immunotherapy) to enhance the treatment outcomes and quality of life for CESC patients will be an important direction for future research.

## Conclusion
In this study, we thoroughly investigated the functionality and prognostic significance of ERSRGs in CESC, revealing the complexity of ERSRGs in terms of genomic variations, expression differences, and chromosomal localizations. By conducting a comprehensive analysis of the TCGA-CESC dataset, we successfully identified and validated a group of molecular marker genes closely related to the prognosis of CESC under ERS conditions and constructed an accurate prognostic assessment model based on these genes. Notably, we discovered that the CCL3 gene plays a pivotal role in the proliferation, migration, and invasion processes of cervical cancer cells, significantly affecting the biological properties of these cells and tumor growth capability in a nude mouse model.
Single-cell transcriptomic analysis further revealed the heterogeneity of cervical cancer cells and the expression distribution of genes within the ERS prognostic model, providing new insights into the cellular heterogeneity and IME of cervical cancer. Moreover, we analyzed the correlation between prognostic subtypes and sensitivity to anticancer drugs, finding significant differences in drug sensitivity among patients with different prognostic subtypes of cervical cancer, offering potential targeted strategies for personalized treatment.
In summary, our study not only elucidated the critical role of ERSRGs in the development of CESC but also, through the construction of a prognostic assessment model based on ERSRGs, provided new molecular targets and strategies for the prognosis assessment and treatment of cervical cancer. The discovery of the CCL3 gene and its associated regulatory network offers valuable resources for targeted treatment and drug development for cervical cancer. Future research could further explore the specific mechanisms of these genes in the progression of cervical cancer and how these findings can be effectively utilized to improve clinical management and treatment outcomes for patients with cervical cancer.
