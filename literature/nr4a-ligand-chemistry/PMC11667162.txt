## Abstract
Despite the remarkable bioactivities exhibited by monascin against several tumors, its therapeutic targets remain unexplored. In this study, our objective is to identify therapeutic targets based on the antitumor activity of monascin against lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) A549 cells. The compound monascin was derived from extracts of Monascus purpureus-fermented rice. Its chemical structure was determined using spectroscopic methods, while the physicochemical properties of monascin were investigated through thermal behavior analysis. Herein, antitumor studies indicated that monascin significantly inhibited the viability of A549 cells, with an IC50 value of 2.05 μM; RNA-sequencing revealed 12 up-regulated and 29 down-regulated genes as differentially expressed genes (DEGs) for A549 cells in response to monascin; RT-qPCR validation supported the plausibility of the RNA-sequencing results. Moreover, utilizing data from the Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) database, univariate and multivariate Cox regression analyses provided evidence supporting GPR37 and FAM83A as potential candidate genes for predicting disease progression in LUAD patients. Additionally, the LASSO Cox regression and nomogram analyses confirmed that FAM83A and GPR37 genes had the potential to predict overall survival for LUAD patients. Finally, molecular docking studies suggested that monascin could interact with both GPR37 and FAM83A proteins through the hydrogen bonding and hydrophobic interactions; Western blotting assays indicated that monascin inhibited the expression levels of both GPR37 and FAM83A proteins. Overall, the clinical prognosis of GPR37 and FAM83A highlights the rationale for targeting these specific genes with monascin. Additionally, there findings provide compelling evidence for the polypharmacological properties of monascin against LUAD.
Keywords: Monascin, RNA-sequencing, Antitumor, Lung adenocarcinoma, GPR37, FAM83A

## Introduction
Lung cancer is a major contributor to the morbidity and mortality associated with cancer, posing a global health threat worldwide( ). Among all subtypes of lung cancer, lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) stands out as the most common subtype( ). Over the past two decades( ), numerous researchers have focused on identifying novel targeted therapeutic drugs that have shown promising results in reducing the mortality rates among some LUAD patients. For example, selective epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) inhibitors like erlotinib and gefitinib have demonstrated clinical feasibility in treatment for LUAD( , ). However, the 5-year survival rate of LUAD patients remains alarmingly low, especially in cases of metastatic LUAD( ). The main reasons behind this phenomenon are the metabolic adaptability of tumor cells and immune suppression in the tumor microenvironment, which frequently result in resistance to targeted therapies ( , ). Therefore, it is crucial to explore novel targeted therapeutic agents for LUAD in order to combat current drug resistance and improve patient survival.
The Monascus-fermented products show great potential as a source for exploring targeted agents, as they have been proven to possess significant pharmacological effects against tumors in animal models ( , , ). The fungus Monascus has a long history of producing dietary supplements( , ), some of which are currently being investigated as potential ingredients for tumor prevention ( ). Monascin, an azaphilonoid pigment found in rice fermented by Monascus purpureu (known as red rice or Hong-Qu)( ), has garnered significant attention due to its diverse range of biological activities, including its ability to inhibit tumor growth ( , ), reduce inflammation ( , ), and modulate the immune system( ). Furthermore, monascin specifically targets several disease-associated inflammatory factors such as NF-κB, IL-6, IL-1β, TNF-α, and COX2( ) that contribute to tumor angiogenesis and metastasis.
Despite the advancements made in the development of monascin for tumor treatment, the identification of its therapeutic target remains a significant bottleneck in early-stage drug discovery( , ). High-throughput genome sequencing has emerged as a valuable approach for exploring potential targets for tumors through analysis of differentially expressed genes (DEGs)( ). By utilizing DEG data, several target genes associated with LUAD can be identified at the cellular level( ). Intrigued by the challenges associated with identifying therapeutic targets, we performed mRNA-sequencing analysis on LUAD A549 cells treated with monascin. However, it is important to note that the results of this in vitro assay may provide a comprehensive list of DEGs for monascin but not all of them can be directly extrapolated to humans ( ).
The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) data are widely recognized as the largest and most commonly used public resource in human cancer research. This invaluable resource provides comprehensive genomic sequencing and copy number variation datasets representing over 11,000 individuals across more than 30 different types of cancer( ). Increasing evidence suggests that gene signatures at the mRNA level hold significant potential for predicting tumor prognosis( ). The comprehensive exploration of tumor gene signatures from TCGA has demonstrated its efficacy as a proficient approach in identifying therapeutic targets, thereby providing valuable guidance for the discovery of novel targeted agents ( ). By visualizing gene expression data alongside clinical features, researchers can gain insights into the impact of genomic alterations on phenotypes. Additionally, integrating Kaplan–Meier survival analysis based on prognostic gene signatures ( ) facilitates the identification of therapeutic targets.
To further our ongoing investigation into the potential targets of monascin in LUAD, we will employ high-throughput RNA-sequencing and real-time quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) analysis to identify DEGs in A549 cells treated with monascin. Subsequently, utilizing LUAD data, both univariate and multivariate analyses will be conducted to pinpoint two candidate genes. Additionally, molecular docking studies will be undertaken to gain valuable insights into the potential therapeutic targets in LUAD, which will then be validated through Western blotting.

## Materials and methods
2.1.

## Chemicals and reagents
The celecoxib (batch number orb322937) was purchased from Biorbyt (Wuhan, China). Deionized water used throughout the study was purified on a Milli-Q system (Millipore, Bedford, USA). All other reagents were of analytical grade and were obtained from Sinopharm Beijing Chemical Reagent Co. Ltd., China.

2.2.

## Isolation of monascin
The crude pigments extracted from rice fermented with Monascus purpureus were separated using ethanol and purified through silica gel column chromatography (200–300 mesh, Qingdao Ocean Chemical Co., Ltd, China)( ). The yield of the extracted monascin was approximately 0.008%. Monascin (batch number 2020060, purity greater than 98%) was obtained through the recrystallization method ( ).

2.3.

## High performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) for purity analysis
The purity analysis of monascin was conducted using a Waters ACQUITY TUV system equipped with an Agilent C18 column (250 × 4.6 mm, inner diameter 5 μm). The separation was achieved by employing the following elution gradient and mobile phase composition: 0–20.0 min, 60 – 90 % B; 20.0–20.1 min, 90 – 60 % B; and finally, from 20.1 to 25.0 min, maintaining a constant concentration of mobile phase B at 60 % (where mobile phase A refers to water and mobile phase B refers to acetonitrile), all operated at a flow rate of 1.0 mL/min. The detection wavelength was set at a specific vale of λ = 220 nm for monitoring purposes during the analysis process. Each injection consisted of an accurately measured volume of sample solution equaling to 5.0 μL.

2.4.

## Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) experiments
The NMR experiments were performed on a Bruker AVANCE III HD 400 NMR spectrometer (Bruker, Switzerland). 1H and 13C NMR resonance frequencies were set at 400 and 100 MHz, respectively. Chemical shifts are reported in parts per million (δ, ppm) relative to the signal of tetramethylsilane (TMS), which was used as an internal reference. Peak multiplicity in the 1H NMR spectra is denoted by s (singlet), d (doublet), t (triplet), m (multiplet), and dd (doublet).

2.5.

## Fourier transform infrared spectrum (FT-IR) spectroscopy
The FT-IR spectra were recorded using KBr particles on a Bruker ALPHA II spectrometer. The acquisition range was 4000–400 cm−1 with 10 scans at a resolution of 4 cm−1.

2.6.

## Thermogravimetric (TGA) and differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) analyses
Thermogravimetric (TGA) and differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) analyses were conducted using a TGA/DSC1 instrument (Mettler-Toledo, Germany). The sample, weighing precisely 6.7 mg, was placed in a sealed aluminum crucible with a pinhole of 40 μL capacity. It was then heated from 30 to 300 °C at a constant ramp rate of 10 °C/min under a dry nitrogen flow of 30 mL/min. The obtained TGA/DSC data were recorded and analyzed using STARe SW 14.0 software.

2.7.

## Cell lines and cultures
The human LUAD cell line (A549) and the human breast cancer cell line (MCF-7) were obtained from Procell Life Science & Technology Co. Ltd (Wuhan, China). Cells were cultured in RPMI1640 (Gibco, Thermo Fisher Scientific) supplemented with 10% heat-inactivated fetal bovine serum (Gibco, Thermo Fisher Scientific). Cultures were maintained in a humidified incubator at 37 °C with 5% CO2. Stock solutions of monascin (1000 μg/mL) were prepared using dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO, Sigma-Aldrich), which was then diluted with the cell culture medium to achieve desired concentrations (final DMSO concentration ≤ 0.1%).

2.8.

## Cell viability assay
The cell viability of monascin and celecoxib was assessed using the standard colorimetric assay of EnoGeneCell™ Counting Kit-8 (Vazyme Biotech, Nanjing, China). Briefly, A549 and MCF-7 cells were seeded at a density of 1 × 104 cells per well on a plate with 96 wells and incubated for 24h at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere containing 5% CO2. Subsequently, an equal volume of additional medium containing either the test compound or DMSO control was added to achieve the desired final concentration. The treated cells were further incubated for 48h. Cell viability was determined by comparing the optical density (OD) at 450 nm between test compound-treated cells and DMSO control-treated cells using an MK3 spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). The cell inhibition rate was calculated according to equation( ): cell inhibition rate = [(control well OD − experimental well OD)/control well OD] × 100%. Data analysis was performed using Phoenix WinNonlin software (version 8.1, Certara, L.P., USA) [I = Imax × Conc/(IC50 + Conc), where I represents the cellular inhibition rate; Imax represents the maximum inhibition rate; Conc represents the test compound concentration; IC50 represents the concentration at half of the maximum inhibition rate].

2.9.

## mRNA-sequencing
The A549 cells were seeded at a density of 1 × 105 cells/well on 6-well plates and incubated overnight for attachment. Subsequently, the cells were treated with either DMSO control or monascin at a concentration of 3.69 μM for 24 or 48h before RNA isolation. Total cellular RNA was extracted using TRIzol reagent (TIANGEN, China) following the manufacturer's protocol. Biomarker Technologies (Beijing, China) performed mRNA-sequencing analysis was performed on an Illumina HiSeqTM 4000 platform. Cutadapt software (version 1.10) was used to filter reads containing aptamer contamination, poly-N sequences and poor-quality bases from the raw data. The filtered data were mapped to the Homo sapiens reference genome using a combination of Bowtie2 (version 2.3), STAR (version 2.7) and Cufflink (version 2.2) programs. The reference genome and gene annotation files were downloaded from Ensembl (https://asia.ensembl.org)( ). Pairs of clean reads were aligned to the reference genome (GRCh38.p13) using STAR software (version 2.7)( ). After obtaining the final transcriptome, mRNA abundance was analyzed by StringTie software (version 2.1), quantified as fragments per kilobase per million mappings (FPKM), which takes into account sequencing depth and gene length in read counts estimation for gene expression levels assessment. The DESeq2 R package (version 1.24) provided statistical analysis for differential gene expression between DMSO control and monascin-treated samples. DEGs between sample pairs were identified based on adjusted P (Padj) values < 0.001 and absolute log2 fold change (FC) ≥ 1( ). Volcano plots were generated using bioinformatics online tool (https://www.bioinformatics.com.cn). Heat maps and Venn diagrams were created utilizing Cloud Platform online tool (https://www.ehbio.com/ImageGP).

2.10.

## Real-time quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) analysis
The A549 cells were treated with either DMSO control or monascin at concentrations of 0.78, 3.13 and 12.50 μM for 48 h before RNA isolation. Total mRNA isolation from the cells was performed using TRIzol as previously described in the mRNA-sequencing process. One microgram of extracted RNA was reverse-transcribed into cDNA using the HiScript® III RT SuperMix kit (Transgen, China) following the manufacturer's instructions. A total of 19 genes were selected based on RT-qPCR analysis and gene-specific PCR primers were synthesized according to the sequences provided in . Gene expression quantification was carried out using an ABI PRISM 7500 fast instrument (Applied Biosystems, USA) under the following reaction conditions: initial denaturation at 95 °C for 10 min followed by amplification consisting of 40 cycles at 95 °C for 10 s, and then annealing at 60 °C for 30 s. Relative gene expression levels were normalized to β-actin using the 2−ΔΔCt method.
 
2.11.

## LUAD patient-related survival data
The LUAD patient-related survival data were obtained from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA)-based UCSC Xena web data mining platform (https://xenabrowser.net/datapages/)( ). The survival data for 490 patients with LUAD included overall survival time, survival status, age, gender, pathological stage (T, N and M), tumor stage, and gene expression level. Univariate, multivariate and Lasso Cox regression analyses were performed on a bioinformatics online web-based data mining platform (http://vip.sangerbox.com)( ) to explore the correlation between each variable and overall patient survival. Variables were considered statistically significant at a cut-off of P < 0.05. Kaplan–Meier (KM) curves were applied to evaluate the predictive value of each variable. Patients were categorized into high-risk and low-risk groups based on the median value of each variable expression( ).

2.12.

## Molecular docking
The potential interaction pattern between monascin and gene-encoded proteins was investigated through molecular docking studies using the LeDock program (https://www.lephar.com/)( ). X-ray crystal structures were obtained from the Research Collaboration for Structural Bioinformatics (RCSB) Protein Data Bank (PDB, https://www.rcsb.org/)( ), while homology model structures of the target proteins were downloaded from the fully automated Protein Structure Homology Model Server (https://swissmodel.expasy.org)( ). CavityPlus (https://repharma.pku.edu.cn/cavityplus)( ) was employed to detect protein cavities and identify potential binding sites on the protein surface based on ligandness and druggability scores. The optimal conformation was determined through clustering analysis with a root mean square deviation (RMSD) threshold of 1.0 Å, and graphical analysis focused on selecting the lowest energy conformation out of 20 docking results. LigPlot+ (version 2.2)( ) was used to generate two-dimensional ligand-target interaction maps.

2.13.

## Western blot assay
Proteintech (Wuhan Sanying Biotechnology Co., LTD, Hubei, China) supplied the GPR37 polyclonal antibody (#14820–1-AP, 1:1000) and FAM83A polyclonal antibody (#20618–1-AP, 1:1000). ABclonal Technologies (Wuhan, Hubei, China) provided the β-Actin Rabbit monoclonal antibody High Dilution (#AC026, 1:4000), horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG H&L (#AS014, 1:4000), and HRP-conjugated goat anti-mouse IgG H&L (#AS003, 1:4000). Merck Millipore Chemical Technology (Shanghai, China) supplied the polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) membrane with a pore size of 0.45 um (#IPVH00010).
The proteins were extracted from A549 cells after treating them with either DMSO control or monascin at concentrations of 0.78, 3.13, and 12.50 μM for 48 h using a RIPA lysis buffer (#MA0151, Dalian meilune Biotechnology Co., LTD) containing the phosphatase inhibitor Phynomethanesulfonyl fluoride (100 mM) (#MA0001, Dalian meilune Biotechnology Co., LTD). The protein concentration was determined using a BCA protein determination kit. Subsequently, the proteins were separated by 4 % − 12 % sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (FuturePAGETM, Nanjing ACE Biotechnology Co., LTD). They were then transferred onto a PVDF membrane and incubated with 5 % albumin bovine (#A600669, Guanzhou Saiguo Biotech Co., LTD) for 2.5h at room temperature. The primary antibody was applied to the PVDF membrane overnight at 4 °C, followed by the secondary antibodies for 2h. Finally, the bands were visualized using a Bio-red Gel imaging system, and their optical density was analyzed using Image J software.

2.14.

## Statistical analysis
Data are presented as means ± standard deviation (SD). The student's t-test was used to analyze differences between experimental groups; levels of statistical significance are denoted as * and # P < 0.05; ** and ## P < 0.01. Apart from these assays, specific values are used to express levels of significance in other data analysis.

3.3.

## Thermal behavior
The TGA/DSC curves for monascin are presented in b. The initial weight loss of 1.12% observed in TGA curve between 0 and 200 °C can be attributed to water evaporation, while the subsequent weight loss of 7.83% before reaching 300 °C may indicate molecular binding of monascin. Additionally, the DSC curve exhibits an endothermic peak at approximately 149.6 °C (− 65.70 J/g), corresponding to monascin's melting point( ). There findings further confirm the high purity of the prepared monascin sample.

3.4.

## Inhibition of cell viability
Monascin has been reported to exhibit antitumor activity through its anti-inflammatory and immunomodulatory effects( ). In this study, we aim to further investigate the inhibitory potential of monascin on A549 and MCF-7 cells. As celecoxib has previously demonstrated inhibition of A549 and MCF-7 cells( ), it was selected as a positive control. illustrates the inhibitory effects of both celecoxib and monascin on tumor cells. As depicted in a and c, the Imax values for celecoxib at 129.17 μM (50 μg/mL) against MCF-7 and A549 cells were found to be 62.0% and 63.9%, respectively; with corresponding IC50 values of 1.66 μM for MCF-7 cells and 2.23 μM for A549 cells. On the other hand, b and d demonstrate that monascin at a concentration of 139.50 μM (50 μg/mL) exhibited an Imax value of approximately 82.3% against MCF-7 ( b) as well as an Imax value of around 79.7% against A549 cells; with respective IC50 values measured at 1.55 μM for MCF-7 cells and 2.05 μM for A549 cells. These results obtained from our in vitro viability assay confirmed that monascin had the inhibitory effects on both MCF-7 and A549 cell lines, suggesting that it exhibited comparable potency to celecoxib. Furthermore, this assay provided evidence supporting the potential development of monascin as a promising chemoprotection agent for patients with lung cancer( , ).

3.6.

## RT-qPCR validation
To validate the reliability of mRNA-sequencing results, we randomly selected 5 up-regulated and 14 down-regulated genes for quantification using the RT-qPCR method ( ). Comparative analysis revealed statistically significant alterations in the relative expression levels of NPNT, CRISPLD1, OGDHL, PTCHD4, CDK14 and OR51E2 genes upon treatment with monascin at concentrations of 0.78, 3.13 and 12.50 μM compared to DMSO control-treated genes. Similarly, both 3.13 and 12.50 μM concentrations of monascin resulted in statistically significant changes in the relative expression levels of MAGEA6, EREG, FAP, FAM83A and MME genes. Notably, only at concentrations of either 3.13 or 12.50 μM did monascin cause notable changes in the relative expression levels of GPR37, TMTC1, SNCA, METTL7A, HSPA6 and ADH4 genes. Additionally, a concentration-dependent effect was observed for SLX1B and AQP7 genes upon monascin treatment; however, no statistical significance was found regarding their expression alterations. Overall, RT-qPCR validation supported the plausibility of the mRNA-sequencing results ( ) obtained from this study, despite some quantitative discrepancies in relative expression levels.
 
3.7.

## Identification of candidate genes for monascin in LUAD tissues
To identify candidate genes for monascin in human LUAD tissues, we collected TCGA dataset to evaluate the differential gene expression between tumor and normal tissues ( ). Initially, we compared the DEGs ( and ) obtained from A549 cells with those in TCGA dataset, revealing that 16 genes were significantly differentially expressed in LUAD tissues ( a). In comparison to normal lung tissues, SLX1B, ATF3, MME, NPNT, TMTC1, METTL7A, KRT4, SNCA, and CDK14 were significantly down-regulated in tumor tissues (P < 0.0001), while HSPA6, HMGA2, GPR37, FAP, FAM83A, MAGEA6, and OGDHL displayed substantial up-regulation in tumor tissues (P < 0.0001). Among these up-regulation DEGs, they represent potential genes for the development of natural products or new chemical entity with antitumor properties. Consequently, HMGA2, GPR37, FAP, FAM83A, MAGEA6, and OGDHL were exclusively found to be down-regulated genes upon treatment of A549 cells with monascin ( a and ).
 Univariate and multivariate Cox regression analyses were performed on the 16 significant DEGs ( b and c), revealing that FAM83A [P < 8.7e-6, Hazard ratio (1.25, 95% CI: 1.13–1.38)] and GPR37 [P < 0.02, Hazard ratio (1.16, 95% CI: 1.02–1.33)], HMGA2 [P < 0.05, Hazard ratio (1.14, 95% CI: 1.00–1.31)] exhibited potential as risk genes for disease progression in LUAD patients. To further validate their association with disease risk, patients were stratified into low and high expression level groups based on the median value of gene expression levels ( d to 5f). Kaplan-Meier survival curves demonstrated that LUAD patients with low expression levels of FAM83A [ d, P = 2.3e-3, Hazard ratio (1.58, 95% CI: 1.17–2.13)] and GPR37 [ e, P = 2.7e-3, Hazard ratio (1.57, 95% CI: 1.17–2.11)] had significantly longer overall survival compared to those with high expression levels; this finding is consistent with previous findings( , ). However, HMGA2 gene [ e, P = 0.15, Hazard ratio (1.24, 95% CI: 0.93–1.67)] did not exhibit a significant difference in overall survival between the low and high expression level groups; this observation aligns with findings reported in human colorectal cancer( ) and ovarian cancer( ) studies. The expression of both FAM83A and GPR37 genes demonstrated strong prognostic implications for overall survival in LUAD patients. Therefore, these two genes, FAM83A and GPR37 genes, hold great potential as promising candidate genes for monascin.

3.8.

## Prognosis analysis of candidate genes
To investigate the potential of candidate genes as independent risk predictors for LUAD patients, we initially conducted regression analyses based on overall survival to explore the interdependence between key clinical features and candidate genes ( ). The key clinical features consisted of Tumor_stage (grade 1 to 9), Pathlogical_T (grade 1 to 9), Pathlogical_N (grade 1 to 5), Pathlogical_M (grade 1 to 5), age (33 to 88 years), and gender (grade 0 and 1). The candidate genes under consideration were FAM83A and GPR37. Subsequently, univariate and multivariate Cox regression analyses were performed on these eight indicators ( a and b), revealing that Tumor stage showed significant association with patient prognosis (P < 3.4e-4 in a and P < 4.2e-9 in b). Moreover, the expression levels of GPR37 gene also demonstrated a strong correlation with patient outcomes (P < 2.0e-3 in a and P < 1.0e-3 in b). Similarly, FAM83A gene expression was found to be an independent risk predictor for LUAD patients (P < 2.5e-3 in a and P < 2.7e-3 in b).
 The least absolute shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO) Cox regression analysis was performed to investigate the impact of Tumor stage, FAM83A and GPR37 gene expression on LUAD prognosis ( c to e). A prognostic model was established using data from 290 patients, and the risk score was calculated as follows: Risk score = 0.1877 × Tumor_stage + 0.1638 × FAM83A gene expression + 0.1937 × GPR37 gene expression. According to this formula, the risk score for each patient was calculated, and subsequently, they were divided into high-risk and low-risk groups based on the median risk score ( e), confirming that a combination of a two-gene signature with Tumor stage has potential in predict overall survival for patients [P < 2.4e-10, Hazard ratio (2.64, 95% CI: 1.93–3.62)]. The high-risk group exhibited significantly reduced overall survival compared to the low-risk group, with respective median survival times of 950 and 2261 days for high-risk and low-risk groups ( e). We analyzed the distribution of risk scores, survival status, and three risk features ( f), which revealed a significant decrease in patient survival with increasing risk scores; patients in the high-risk group had notably worse outcomes than those in the low-risk group. More importantly, Tumor stage, FAM83A and GPR37 genes demonstrated an up-regulated expression trend with increasing risk scores ( f).
A nomogram was constructed incorporating a two-gene signature along with Tumor stage to further predict the outcome of LUAD patients ( g). When Tumor stage (P < 4.2e-9), FAM83A (P < 1.0e-3) and GPR37 (P < 2.7e-3) genes were expressed at their average levels, they contributed 41.00, 40.37 and 17.92 points respectively towards the overall score calculation. With a total score of 99.29, it is evident that two-gene signature holds significant prognostic potential for LUAD patients as it contributes the most to overall survival (58.29 points). Hence, it can be concluded that the combination of FAM83A and GPR37 in a two-gene signature exhibits promising prognostic value for LUAD patients.

3.9.

## Molecular docking for FAM83A and GPR37 proteins
The potential of both FAM83A and GPR37 proteins as multiple targets of monascin will be further confirmed through molecular docking analysis. illustrates the detailed docking interactions between monascin and these two proteins. Initially, we conducted molecular docking to identify the key amino acid residues in GPR37 that interacted with monascin ( a and b). The obtained docking results were compared to those of hyocrellin B, which has been previously reported as the bioactive agent of GPR37( ). Interestingly, some of the binding sites of monascin ( a) overlapped with those hyocrellin B, supporting a similar binding pattern between monascin and hypocrellin B ( b). Similar to hyocrellin B, monascin can form hydrogen bonds with Asn512 residue and engage in hydrophobic interactions with Glu508, Tyr446, Leu443, Arg442, Ile421, Thr515, Arg401, Glu397, Glu341, and Ser537 residues with GPR37′s structure. Additionally, monascin also established hydrophobic interactions with Leu345, Asn531 and Gln535 residues. Subsequently, molecular docking was employed to investigate the key amino acid residues in FAM83A that interacted with monascin, in comparison with erlotinib as bioactive agent of FAM83A. The overlapping binding sites of monascin and erlotinib within the structure of FAM83A protein( ) ( c) revealed their identical binding patterns ( d). Monascin exhibited hydrogen bonding interactions with Lys243 and Ser537 residues, as well as hydrophobic interactions involving Phe282, Glu242, Phe244 and Gly255 residues for its interaction with FAM83A. In addition to these shared interactions, monascin specifically engaged with FAM83A through the hydrogen bonding interactions involving Arg408 and Thr407 residues, along with hydrophobic interactions involving Ser256 and Pro406 residues.

## Effects of monascin on the expression levels of both GPR37 and FAM83A proteins
To further investigate the impact of monascin on both GPR37 and FAM83A, A549 cells were co-incubated with monascin. The concentration-dependent analysis of GPR37 and FAM83A was conducted using a Western blot assay. As depicted in a, monascin significantly suppressed GPR37 at both concentrations of 3.13 and 12.50 μM, leading to a notable deduction in the ratio of GPR37 to β-Actin by 38.15% (3.13 μM-group vs. DMSO-group, P < 0.05) and 40.35% (12.50 μM-group vs. DMSO-group, P < 0.05). Likewise, as shown in b, consistent with previous mRNA expression results, treatment of A549 cells with monascin at both concentrations of 3.13 and 12.50 μM resulted in lower expression levels of FAM83A compared to DMSO-treated cells (3.13 μM-group vs. DMSO-group, P < 0.01; 12.50 μM-group vs. DMSO-group, P < 0.01). These findings indicated that that both GPR37 and FAM83A proteins were potential targets for monascin in LUAD.

## Discussion
The metastasis of LUAD cells to distant tissue sites poses a significant clinical challenge, leading to high mortality rates and a substantial proportion of patients experiencing therapeutic failure ( ). Approximately 70% of diagnosed LUAD patients are at an advanced stage characterized by both local and distant metastasis( , ). Among these patients, they have a median overall survival time of about 8.0 months (95% CI: 6.1 – 9.9) ( ). Undoubtedly, the metabolic adaptability of tumor cells has been proposed as one of the most significant processes in tumor metastasis ( ). Moreover, the tumor microenvironment employs immune suppression to facilitate immune evasion, proliferation, and metastasis of tumor cells ( ). Hence, it is crucial to explore novel targeted therapeutic agents for mitigating LUAD metastasis. To the best of our knowledge, this study is the first to identify LUAD-associated targets for monascin from extracts derived from Monascus purpureus-fermented rice.
In China, Monascus purpureus is the predominant species used for the production of Monascus-fermented red rice( ). Throughout history, ancient Chinese employed Monascus-fermented red rice as a natural food coloring agent( , ). Recently, extracts derived from Monascus-fermented red rice have been investigated as potential chemotherapy agents for treating tumors due to their pigments such as ankaflavin, monascin, and rubropunctatin( , ). Among these pigments, monascin exhibits certain antitumor and anti-inflammatory properties( ). Consequently, several recent studies have focused on investigating the antitumor effects of Monascus metabolites. The fermented red rice contains a mixture of six major substances including orange pigments (monascorubrin and rubropunctatin), red pigments (monascorubramine and rubropunctamine), as well as yellow pigments (ankaflavin and monascin)( , ). However, there are limited reports available regarding the biological targets of these pigments against tumors. Kung-Yen et al.( ) demonstrated that monascin prevented breast cancer metastasis while Ruei-Lan et al.( ) indicated that red mold rice mitigated oral carcinogenesis in Hamsters. Here we have demonstrated that monascin, one of the major isolated pigments in our study, has the potential to act as a targeted therapeutic agent for GPR37 and FAM83A. This compound effectively suppresses the proliferation of LUAD A549 cells in vitro.
In the mRNA-sequencing study, the significant DEGs of A549 cells in response to monascin treatment ( ) have the potential to serve as predictive, prognostic, and therapeutic biomarkers for cancer patients. For instance, alcohol dehydrogenase 4 (ADH4), a member of the ADH family, exhibits high expression levels and may serve as a more reliable prognostic biomarker for patients with non-small cell lung cancer( ). Heat shock protein family A member 6 (HSPA6), belonging to the HSP70 family, may act as a cytoprotective factor associated with cancer( ). Membrane metallo-endopeptidase (MME), a key zinc-dependent metallopeptidase in natriuretic peptide and kinin systems, is strongly linked to poor overall survival in patients with non-small cell lung cancer( ). Fibroblast-activated protein alpha (FAP) is highly expressed in stromal fibroblasts and promotes the proliferation of breast cancer cells( ). Aquaporin 7 (AQP7) is considered a novel regulator of breast cancer and predicts overall patient survival( ). Methyltransferase-like enzyme 7A (METTL7A), located within the inner retina membrane, has potential as a therapeutic biomarker for breast cancer( ). However, it is important to acknowledge that these significant genomic alterations observed in A549 cells cannot be directly extrapolated as predictive, prognostic, and therapeutic effects for LUAD patients in clinical settings. Therefore, an approach is required to identify DEGs found at the cellular level as potential target genes of monascin for LUAD patients.
The field of bioinformatics is widely recognized for its role in storing, analyzing, and utilizing information from multiomics data, which holds immense potential for unraveling complex biological systems( ). The bioinformatics approach integrates various statistical methods, such as univariate and multivariate Cox analyses, LASSO Cox regression analysis, and Kaplan-Meier survival analysis, to identify signature genes ( ). Despite significant changes in gene expression of ADH4 (Padjust < 0.0001), HSPA6 (Padjust < 0.0001), MME (Padjust < 0.0001), AQP7 (Padjust < 0.0001), and METTL7A (Padjust < 0.0001) observed upon monascin treatment in A549 cells ( ), it is worth noting that neither ADH4 nor AQP7 were down-regulated DEGs, and METTL7A was not an up-regulated DEG in LUAD patients ( a); subsequent univariate and multivariate Cox regression analyses revealed that neither HSPA6 (P = 0.82) nor MME (P = 0.57) were associated with an increased risk in LUAD patients ( b). Conversely, both FAM83A and GPR37 genes have emerged as promising candidate genes influenced by monascin treatment. This is supported by their highly significant levels observed in both A549 cells (Padjust < 0.0001 for both FAM83A and GPR37 genes in ) and LUAD patients (P < 0.0001 for both FAM83A and GPR37 genes in a). Furthermore, it should be noted that the overexpression of both FAM83A and GPR37 genes is associated with an unfavorable prognosis for LUAD patients( , , ).
Identification of signature genes with significantly increased expression in LUAD cells is crucial for overcoming resistance to current targeted therapeutic drugs. The upregulation of FAM83A gene has been observed in various cancer specimens( , ), indicating its potential role as a key regulator in the EGFR pathway and its contribution to the development of resistance against EGFR-tyrosine kinase inhibitors ( ). Overexpression of the FAM83A gene has been associated with early lymph node metastasis and poor overall survival in patients with LUAD( , ). Similarly, the expression of GPR37 exhibited a significant increase in specimens of human colorectal cancer liver metastasis, which was correlated with an unfavorable prognosis( ). Knockdown of the GPR37 gene hindered neutrophil recruitment within the tumor microenvironment of colorectal cancer liver metastasis, negatively affecting tumorigenesis and suppressing tumor cell proliferation in patients with colorectal cancer( ). Additionally, GPR37 was validated as a prognostic biomarker playing a key role in tumor progression and its upregulation is associated with a poor prognosis for LUAD patients( ). Clearly, both the FAM83A and GPR37 genes are involved in multiple regulatory feedback loops that govern tumor metastasis. Therefore, it can be observed that monascin targets both the signature genes of FAM83A and GPR37, potentially altering LUAD metastasis.
The current study has several limitations that need to be acknowledged. First and foremost, our investigation primarily focused on data from LUAD, so caution should be exercised when applying our findings to other types of cancer. Secondly, it is crucial to externally validate the prognostic effects of dual gene signatures in conjunction with a single clinical feature using additional independent cohorts. Lastly, due to the relatively low bioavailability ( ), we did not conduct any in vivo experiments in this study.

5.

## Conclusions
This study establishes the foundation for future optimization of the monascin scaffold in order to enhance binding potency and improve drug-like properties as a potential chemoprevention agent. Moreover, the identification of novel targets will facilitate the development of new agents to overcome the resistance observed with current targeted therapies for LUAD treatment. Both FAM83A and GPR37 proteins are considered promising targets for prediction, prognosis and treatment of LUAD. Undoubtedly, the identification of these potential targets will greatly facilitate future research on the antitumor mechanisms of monascin, thereby paving the way for developing targeted therapies and optimizing its chemical structure.
