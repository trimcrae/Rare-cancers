## Abstract

## Abstract
Microcin B17 (MccB17) is a ribosomally synthesized and post-translationally modified peptide (RiPP) with antibacterial activity, natively produced by Escherichia coli. Despite its unique gyrase-inhibiting activity, therapeutic development has been limited, primarily due to its low solubility and narrow spectrum of activity. Here, we employed a genome mining approach to identify and functionally characterize novel MccB17-like biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) from uncharacterized environmental Pseudomonas isolates. Four BGCs sharing substantial sequence similarity were cloned and heterologously expressed in E. coli. Regardless of their similarity, the clusters triggered congener-dependent growth retardation and cell elongation, despite lacking detectable antibacterial activity in overlay assays. Transcription and production of modified MccB17-like peptides or their unmodified precursors were confirmed by reverse transcription-quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) and liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC–MS), respectively. Transcriptome profiling indicated differential induction of stress responses and metabolic shifts in host cells, with limited overlap among the different BGCs. Our findings suggest that structurally similar MccB17 congeners can elicit distinct physiological responses. This study expands the known range of MccB17-like RiPPs and highlights the importance of functionally exploring RiPP diversity beyond known antimicrobial activity, even among congeners.

## Key points

• In silico identification of uncharacterized MccB17 congeners from Pseudomonas spp.

• Functional analysis of novel variants reveals phenotypic effects on expression host

• Transcriptomics reveals congener-specific effects despite similarity to E. coli MccB17

## Introduction
Peptides are increasingly recognized as promising therapeutic agents due to their high specificity, efficacy and safety, with broad applications in cardiovascular, gastrointestinal and infectious diseases, as well as the treatment of cancer and diabetes, and vaccine development (Wang et al. , ). Among these, class I bacteriocins represent a diverse group of ribosomally synthesized and post-translationally modified peptides (RiPPs) with antimicrobial activity. Produced by both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, they exhibit a wide diversity of structures and functions, generating considerable interest in their potential as novel antibiotics (Cavera et al. ; Chikindas et al. ). One notable group of bacteriocins are the microcins—small (< 10 kDa) antibacterial peptides produced predominantly by Gram-negative bacteria, especially by members of the Enterobacteriaceae family (Asensio et al. ; Parker & Davies ). Microcins are highly potent against Gram-negative bacteria such as Escherichia, Salmonella, Enterobacter, Klebsiella, Citrobacter and Shigella, with minimal inhibitory concentrations (MICs) in the nanomolar to micromolar range (Duquesne et al. ; Telhig et al. ). Importantly, they also show potential to inhibit the growth of multi-drug resistant (MDR) bacteria, making them promising candidates in the search for antibiotics targeting MDR pathogens (Telhig et al. ). However, only about 15 types of microcins have been characterized to date, leaving their diversity and functions largely underexplored.
Microcins are classified into two major classes based on size and post-translational modifications (PTMs). Class I microcins are smaller and undergo extensive modification. They include more complex biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) with low sequence conservation. Despite a limited number of successful efforts to detect variants in the past, the complexity and limited homology of these gene clusters have made bioinformatic discovery challenging (Maksimov et al. ; Parker & Davies ). In contrast, class II microcins are characterized by disulfide bonds or minimal modifications and show higher sequence conservation, facilitating identification through bioinformatic analysis (Cole et al. ; Parker & Davies ).
A well-studied example of class I microcins is microcin B17 (MccB17), a member of the thiazole/oxazole-modified microcins (TOMMs) family and linear azole-containing peptide (LAP) subclass of RiPPs (Collin & Maxwell ). This class is characterized by thiazole and oxazole heterocycles derived from cysteine and serine residues in the precursor peptide (Yorgey et al. ). It is produced by E. coli strains harboring a plasmid encoding the mcb operon. This operon includes mcbA, encoding the precursor peptide, mcbBCD, encoding the synthetase complex responsible for the post-translational modifications (Li et al. ; Milne et al. ), mcbEF, encoding the efflux pump and mcbG, an immunity gene that protects the producer cell from the action of MccB17 (Garrido et al. ). MccB17 is known to inhibit DNA gyrase in a similarly, though not identically, to quinolones, causing double-stranded DNA breaks and replication inhibition, ultimately resulting in cell death (Vizán et al. ).
Despite its promising and unique mode of action, clinical development of MccB17 has been relatively limited due to its narrow activity spectrum and its dependence on non-essential protein transporters for uptake, which facilitates the emergence of transport-based resistance (Collin & Maxwell ). Nevertheless, natural or engineered variants of antibiotics may exhibit improved properties and provide valuable insights for antibiotic development (Wang et al. ). For instance, the colistin congener macolacin was shown to be effective against extensively drug-resistant strains with colistin resistance. Likewise, a previously characterized MccB17-like molecule from Pseudomonas syringae demonstrated a broader activity spectrum (Metelev et al. ). Bioinformatic analysis of Pseudomonas genomes suggests an even higher structural divergence of uncharacterized congeners (Ghequire & De Mot ).
In this study, we aimed to identify and characterize novel MccB17-like BGCs using a genome mining approach. We identified four novel BGCs predicted to produce MccB17 congeners in four environmental Pseudomonas isolates. Inspection of the precursor peptide sequences indicated a post-translational modification pattern distinct from that of characterized MccB17 peptides from E. coli and P. syringae, suggesting a potential for discovering congeners with unique properties. The BGCs were cloned and heterologously expressed in E. coli. Difficulties in transformation of plasmids carrying the BGCs indicated a conditionally toxic effect dependent on the background of the heterologous expression host, despite no apparent signs of antimicrobial activity in growth inhibition assays. Additionally, expression of the BGCs induced cell elongation and growth retardation of the host. RNA-seq analysis indicated differential induction of stress responses and metabolic shifts in host cells, with limited overlap among the different BGCs. We discuss how future research on the functional characterization of novel congeners of known antimicrobials could be a valuable but underexplored approach to combating antibiotic resistance.

## Materials and methods

## Bacterial strains and growth conditions
An overview of the strains and plasmids used in this work is shown in Table  . MccB17-like BGCs were identified in Pseudomonas putida BW12S1 and RW4S1, and Pseudomonas fluorescens PGSB8075 and SWRI140_87202, kindly provided by Professor R. De Mot (KU Leuven). Other bacterial strains used were E. coli TOP10F’ and E. coli MG1655 as heterologous expression hosts. E. coli TOP10 carrying pCID909 and E. coli TOP10F’ carrying an empty cloning vector pUCP18 were used as positive and negative controls. Antimicrobial activity was assessed against E. coli XL1-Blue, E. coli MG1655, E. coli MG1655 sfiA::lacZ and E. coli BL21(DE3).

 

## Bioinformatic approach
We leveraged our in-house collection of 446 sequenced environmental Pseudomonas isolates, which had been previously assembled and annotated using Prokka (Seemann ). To identify MccB17-like BGCs, we initially screened the draft genomes using antiSMASH version 6.0.0 (Blin et al. ) using default bacterial settings with relaxed stringency and Prodigal gene calling, along with three comparison modules, i.e. –cb-general, –cb-subclusters, and –cb-knownclusters, to provide extended ClusterBlast and known-cluster similarity analysis. In addition, we also developed a custom genome mining pipeline specifically tailored to detect MccB17-related BGCs, based on a combination of local BLAST searches and gene neighborhood analysis. As a query, we used the full E. coli MccB17 biosynthetic operon, comprising the genes mcbA, mcbB, mcbC, mcbD, mcbE, mcbF, and mcbG. Protein BLAST databases were generated for each genome using makeblastdb from the BLAST + suite. PSI-BLAST (Position-Specific Iterated BLAST) was performed using the MccB17 query proteins against the annotated proteomes of each isolate (Altschul et al. , ).
To identify candidate BGCs, both the genomic proximity and orientation of BLAST hits were evaluated. Clusters were defined as regions containing two or more distinct query-matching genes located within a ± 5 gene window and oriented in the same transcriptional direction. Functional annotations obtained from Prokka were used to assess the likely role of each gene in the cluster. Only gene clusters containing multiple biosynthetic components and showing similarity to the canonical mcb operon architecture were retained for further analysis. Manual curation was performed to eliminate redundant or spurious hits and to confirm the presence of core biosynthetic and transport-associated genes.

## Cloning
Selected gene clusters were cloned under the control of an inducible Plac promoter. Fragments of 5035 bp (P. fluorescens PGSB8075), 4987 bp (P. fluorescens SWRI140_87202), 5005 bp (P. putida BW12S1) and 5033 bp (P. putida RW4S1), containing the mcbABCDEF variant genes and the putative native promoter (Moreno et al. ), were synthesized de novo by Elegen (CA, USA) (sequences in Supplementary Information 1). The fragments were cloned into the blunt-end pJET 1.2 cloning vector using the CloneJet PCR Cloning Kit (ThermoFisher) and validated by Sanger sequencing using the pJET 1.2 Forward (5’-CGACTCACTATAGGGAGAGCGGC-3’) and Reverse (5’-AAGAACATCGATTTTCCATGGCAG-3’) sequencing primers. The gene cluster fragments were then excised from the pJET1.2 constructs using the restriction enzymes XmaI and SbfI (New England Biolabs, MA, USA) following the manufacturer’s protocols. The same restriction enzymes were used for the linearization of the target plasmid pUCP18. The purified fragments and linearized vector were assembled using either restriction ligation (ThermoFisher) or Gibson Assembly (New England Biolabs, MA, USA). The resulting plasmids were transformed into the heterologous expression host E. coli TOP10F’ and verified by whole plasmid sequencing (Eurofins Genomics, Ebersberg, Germany). Verified plasmids were transformed into E. coli MG1655.

## RNA extraction and RT-qPCR
Starting from an overnight culture grown without antibiotic selection, mid-exponential phase cultures were prepared as follows. The overnight culture was diluted 1:100 into fresh LB medium supplemented with Ap100 and IPTG. The diluted culture was incubated at 37 °C until OD600 ~ 0.5.
Total RNA was extracted from 0.5 mL of mid-exponential phase cultures using the RNeasy Plus Mini Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). Residual DNA was removed with TURBO DNase (ThermoFisher, MA, USA) and reverse transcription was performed using M-MuLV reverse transcriptase, according to the manufacturer’s instructions (New England Biolabs, MA, USA). cDNA concentrations were measured using Qubit (ThermoFisher, MA, USA) and normalized to 7.5 ng/µL.
For RT-qPCR, 2 µL of cDNA was mixed with 7.5 µL Power SYBR Green Master Mix (ThermoFisher, MA, USA), 0.3 µL of Forward and Reverse Primer (25 µM) and 4.9 µL of nuclease-free water. Primers were designed to target the first and last gene and intergenic regions of each BGC (sequences in Supplementary Information 4-Table S4). RT-qPCR was carried out on a StepOnePlus Real-Time PCR system (ThermoFisher, MA, USA) with the following reaction conditions: 95 °C for 15 min; 45 cycles of 95 °C for 15 s, 60 °C for 1 min; final extension at 60 °C for 5 min; followed by melting curve analysis. StepOne Software v2.3 (ThermoFisher, MA, USA) and Graphpad Prism 10 were used for analysis and visualization of the data.

## Overlay assay to test for antimicrobial activity
MccB17 congeners were assessed for antimicrobial activity against the target strains E. coli XL1-Blue, E. coli MG1655, E. coli MG1655 sfiA::lacZ and E. coli BL21(DE3) using an overlay assay. Activity of the native P. putida and P. fluorescens strains was also tested. E. coli TOP10F’ and P. aeruginosa PAO1 were included as negative control. To prepare the samples, overnight cultures were centrifuged for 5 min at 2,000 × g. The supernatant was heat-treated for 5 min at 95 °C, while the resulting cell pellet was resuspended in 100 µL 1 × PBS and lysed with 0.1 mm silica beads using the Precellys 24 Touch Homogenizer (Bertin Technologies, France) under the following conditions: 5 cycles at 6.5 m/s for 45 s with 1 min cooling. To remove the beads, the homogenized cell lysate was centrifuged for 10 min at 20,000 × g, 4 °C. Using the Apricot S1 with 96-core (SPT Labtech, Melbourn, UK), 2 µL of overnight culture, supernatant and cell lysate were spotted onto LB agar plates. Target strains were grown overnight and diluted 1:1000 in 0.8% soft agar, then overlaid onto the LB agar plates. The resulting plates were incubated overnight at 37 °C and inspected for antimicrobial activity by measuring zones of growth inhibition the next day.

## Peptide precipitation and LC–ESI–MS
Bacterial cells were cultured overnight in M9 minimal medium, supplemented with 0.2% glucose. Protein expression was induced with 1 mM IPTG. For cell lysis, 50 mL of bacterial cells were subjected to the Branson Digital Sonifier 250 (Branson Ultrasonics Corp., CT, USA) using eight cycles of 30 s pulses at 40% amplitude, with a 30 s cooling interval between cycles. The lysate was centrifuged at 4,000 × g for 20 min, 4 °C, to pellet and remove cellular debris. The supernatant was diluted with 100% trichloroacetic acid (TCA), to achieve a final concentration of 20% TCA. The mixture was incubated on ice for 1 h to precipitate proteins. Precipitated proteins were pelleted by centrifugation at 4000 × g for 20 min, 4 °C. The resulting pellet was washed three times with an ice-cold solution of 0.01 mM HCl in 90% acetone, air-dried and resuspended in 200 µL of Milli-Q (MQ) water. A 10 µL aliquot of the supernatant was loaded onto an ACE C4-300 reverse phase column (Advanced Chromatography Technologies, Aberdeen, UK; 100 × 2.1 mm). Mobile phases consisted of water (A) and acetonitrile (B), each supplemented with 0.1% formic acid. The gradient 0–2.5 min 5% B, 2.5–14 min 5–100% B, 14–19 min 100% B, 19–20.4 min 100–5% B, 20.4–25 min 5% B, was applied at a flow rate of 0.200 mL/min. The Bruker Impact II ESI-Q-TOF mass spectrometer (Bruker, MA, USA) was operated in positive ion mode with a scan range of 50–3000 m/z. Source conditions were: end plate offset at − 500 V; capillary at − 4500 V; nebulizer gas (N2) at 1.6 bar; dry gas (N2) at 8 L/min; dry temperature at 180 °C. Ion transfer conditions were: ion funnel RF at 200 Vpp; multiple RF at 200 Vpp; quadrupole low mass at 55 m/z; collision energy at 5.0 eV; collision RF at 600 Vpp; ion cooler RF at 50–350 Vpp; transfer time at 121 µs; pre-pulse storage time at 1 µs. Calibration was performed with 1 mM sodium formate through a loop injection of 20 μL at the start of each run. Individual peaks were detected between 8.7 min and 10 min and analyzed using Bruker Compass DataAnalysis (Bruker, MA, USA). Identification of the MccB17 positive control (Supplementary Information 4 – Fig. S1) was used to validate the protocol.

## Microscopic imaging
Microscopic analysis was performed using a Nikon Ti-E inverted microscope equipped with a Qi2 CMOS camera. Overnight cultures of the heterologous expression hosts were spotted onto pads of 10 mM MgSO4 with 2% w/v agarose supplemented with 1 mM IPTG. To determine the length of the bacterial cells, image analysis on the brightfield images was performed using Fiji (ImageJ) (Abramoffet al. ; Schindelin et al. ).

## Growth effect
To evaluate the effect of the BGCs on the growth of the heterologous host, overnight bacterial cultures were diluted 1:100 into 198 µL fresh LB supplemented with 1 mM IPTG. The cultures were incubated at 37 °C and monitored for growth over 16 h using the BioSpa 8 Automated Incubator (BioTek Instruments, Inc., VT, USA). OD595 was measured every 10 min, with shaking performed shortly before each measurement. Growth curves were analyzed using Graphpad Prism software version 10. A threshold of OD595 = 0.05 was set to determine the time point corresponding to the growth delay (i.e. lag phase). Carrying capacity matches the maximally measured OD595 value over 16 h.

## RNA-seq
Bacterial cultures were grown until OD600 ~ 0.6–0.8 after 1:100 dilution of the overnight cultures. Aliquots of 0.5 mL were incubated for 15 min with RNAprotect Bacteria Reagent (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). Cells were pelleted by centrifugation for 2 min at maximum speed. The supernatant was removed and samples were stored at −80 °C until sent for RNA sequencing by Azenta Life Sciences (MA, USA).
Raw paired-end RNA-seq reads were quality-checked using FastQC v0.11.9. Adapter and low-quality bases were trimmed with Trimmomatic v0.39 (SLIDINGWINDOW:4:20, MINLEN:50) (Bolger et al. ). Trimmed reads were aligned to strain-specific reference genomes using HISAT2 v2.2.0 with default parameters (Kim et al. ). SAM files were converted to BAM files using SAMtools v1.10 (Li et al. ), and gene-level read counts were obtained using featureCounts v2.0.0 (Liao et al. ). Differential expression between strains carrying the BGC-containing plasmid and the empty vector control was determined in Python using PyDESeq2 v0.5.1 (Muzellec et al. ). Functional enrichment (Gene Ontology) analysis of significantly differentially expressed genes (|2|> log2foldchange; p < 0.05) and visualization was carried out using the enrichGO function from ClusterProfiler v4.14.6 (Wu et al. ) and the ggplot2 package (Wickham ) in RStudio, hereby taking into consideration up- and downregulation of the identified pathways. Heatmaps were generated in Python using seaborn v0.13.2 (Waskom ). The principal component analysis (PCA) was performed using sklearn v.1.5.1.(Pedregosa et al. ) in Python. The top 10% of genes with the largest Euclidean distances from the center were selected for clear visualization, using a threshold corresponding to the 90th percentile of the distance distribution. The PCA plot was visualized using seaborn v0.13.2.

## Statistical analysis
Statistical analysis was performed in Graphpad Prism software version 10 (Boston, MA, USA). A non-parametric Kruskal–Wallis test was used to analyze cell length data. A one-way ANOVA test was used to analyze the growth effect of the BGCs on the heterologous host. Both tests were followed by a Dunnett’s post hoc test for multiple comparisons between the gene clusters and the negative control.

## Results

## Bioinformatic analysis detects MccB17-like variants in an in-house Pseudomonas collection
While MccB17 is a well-characterized DNA gyrase inhibitor produced by E. coli, little﻿ sequence diversity has been described for MccB17-like gene clusters (Metelev et al. ). However, previous bioinformatic analysis has revealed Pseudomonas spp. as potential MccB17-like producers (Ghequire & De Mot ). Hence we set out to mine our in-house collection of Pseudomonas spp. to uncover novel MccB17 congeners, aiming to expand our understanding of their sequence diversity and potential functional variation. A first screening of 446 Pseudomonas spp. genomes with antiSMASH v6 (Blin et al. ) returned no BGCs of the MccB17 MiBIG class. To improve sensitivity, we developed a custom bioinformatics pipeline based on BLASTp and local gene neighbourhood analysis. Using the complete MccB17 BGC (mcbA–G) as a query, the workflow identified putative clusters that were subsequently filtered according to explicit curation criteria. These criteria—integrated directly into the computational pipeline— included: (i) the presence of at least two mcbA–F homologues co-localized within a 5 kb window; (ii) BLASTp hits meeting predefined similarity thresholds (E-value ≤ 1 × 10⁻1 and bit score ≥ 0); (iii) an McbA precursor peptide of appropriate size and with expected sequence features; (iv) the presence of core modifying enzymes (mcbB–F) and associated transport genes within the same operon; and (v) a coherent ORF orientation consistent with operon architecture, which followed directly from the neighbourhood extraction step and was not limited to manual inspection. This approach identified eight unique novel candidate MccB17-like clusters in Pseudomonas strains isolated from diverse rhizosphere environments: BW12S1 (banana, Sri Lanka), RW4S1 (rice, Sri Lanka), SWRI140_87202 (wheat, Iran), SWRI23_87216 (wheat, Iran), SWRI47_87223 (wheat, Iran), PGSB8075 (maize, Belgium), PGSB8007 (maize, Belgium), and OE46_2_87194 (maize, Belgium), none of which had been detected by antiSMASH (Supplementary Information 2 and 3). Four gene clusters were selected based on their origin and sequence variation. The four Pseudomonas BGCs originating from P. fluorescens PGSB8075 (PG-MccB17), P. putida BW12S1 (BW-MccB17), P. putida RW4S1 (RW-MccB17) and P. fluorescens SWRI140_87202 (SW-MccB17) all encoded homologs of mcbA–mcbF, with functional annotations suggesting conserved biosynthetic roles: mcbB (putative zinc metalloprotein), mcbC (SagB/ThcOx-family dehydrogenase), mcbD (YcaO-like cyclodehydratase), mcbE (ABC transporter permease), and mcbF (ATP-binding cassette domain-containing protein). However, no mcbG (immunity gene) homologs were identified in the vicinity of these BGCs, nor elsewhere in the genome assemblies, suggesting alternative self-immunity mechanisms or gene loss (Fig.  a). In contrast, homologs of the tldD and tldE housekeeping genes, required for post-translational maturation of MccB17 (Collin & Maxwell ) were consistently detected. Although similarities are observed in the protein sequences of the MccB17-like gene clusters, clear variations can be detected. These include differences in the expected cyclisation pattern, derived from dipeptide and tripeptide motifs related with peptide cyclisation (Ghequire & De Mot ; Roy et al. ) as well as differences in protein sequence (Fig.  b-c). Despite this, all clusters encoded McbA homologs featuring the conserved central glycine triplet (GGG motif), which is essential for species specificity and activity against Pseudomonas (Metelev et al. )(Fig.  b).
 

## Potential conditional toxicity of MccB17 congener
To perform functional characterization in a commonly used wildtype strain, the gene clusters were cloned into an expression vector and transformed into E. coli MG1655 for heterologous expression. For all BGCs, the transformation efficiency was consistently lower compared to the empty vector control, and transformation of E. coli with PG-MccB17 and BW-MccB17 failed, suggesting a possible toxic effect toward the host (Fig.  a). Likewise, difficulties during transformation were observed for pCID909, which carries the canonical E. coli mcb operon. In contrast, E. coli TOP10F’, a strain optimized for high-efficiency cloning, was successfully transformed with all gene clusters and was therefore selected as a heterologous host for further functional characterization of the BGCs.
 

## RT-qPCR and LC–MS confirm expression of BGCs
Heterologous expression of BGCs in E. coli is often inefficient due to differences in codon usage, promoter incompatibility and the requirement for accessory genes. Confirming effective transcription is a critical first step before assessing product formation. To this end, reverse transcription-quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) was performed on total RNA extracted from the host strains harboring each BGC. Primer sets targeted mcbA and mcbF, respectively the first and last gene of each gene cluster, as well as the intergenic regions between mcbA-mcbB and mcbB-mcbC to confirm contiguous transcription across the cluster (Supplementary Information 4-Table S4). The Ct-values are similar or lower compared to gyrA and below 35, indicating robust transcription of the entire operon at similar or higher expression levels compared to the essential housekeeping gene (Fig.  b). The negative controls correspond with the genomic DNA of the heterologous expression host in the absence of the BGCs.
Next, intracellular peptide extracts of the host strains were assessed for the presence of MccB17 congeners using UHPLC-ESI-Q-TOF–MS to confirm translation. No shift of 20 Da, indicative of a single oxazole/thiazole cyclisation reaction (Kelleher et al. ), was observed for any of the BGCs. Only for PG-MccB17, BW-MccB17 and RW-MccB17, deconvoluted mass spectra revealed m/z values corresponding to the mass of the unmodified precursor peptides from which four (PG-MccB17) and two (BW-MccB17 and RW-MccB17) hydrogen atoms are missing. This corresponds to mass shifts of −2 Da, reflecting the formation of disulfide bridges and thereby confirming the absence of oxazole/thiazole heterocycles (Fig.  c). UHPLC-ESI-Q-TOF–MS analysis of SW-MccB17 did not indicate the presence of unmodified precursor peptides and is therefore not included in the figure. The absence of a modified peptide might be explained by non-functional tailoring peptides in the heterologous host. Although RT-qPCR demonstrates transcription of the entire operon, this does not ensure correct translation, folding, or activity of the tailoring machinery, which is essential for cyclization of the precursor peptide. Nevertheless, RT-qPCR, together with LC–MS, confirms the successful heterologous expression and translation of the precursor peptide at both mRNA and protein levels.

## MccB17 congeners show no detectable antimicrobial activity
Given the reported antimicrobial properties of E. coli MccB17, the antimicrobia﻿l activity of the Pseudomonas congeners was assessed following the confirmation of their expression. Overnight culture, (heated) supernatant and cell extract of the heterologous expression hosts containing the BGCs, the native Pseudomonas producer strains, the positive control expressing E. coli MccB17 and the negative controls E. coli TOP10F’ and P. aeruginosa PAO1, were tested in an overlay assay against the target strains E. coli XL1-Blue, E. coli MG1655, E. coli MG1655 sfiA::lacZ and E. coli BL21(DE3). Zones of growth inhibition could only be distinguished for the positive control of the supernatant samples against known sensitive target strains E. coli BL21 (DE3) and E. coli XL1-Blue (Supplementary Information 4-Fig.S2). For the spotted overnight cultures, read-out of the positive control is masked by bacterial proliferation. In addition, the four Pseudomonas BGCs did not produce any inhibition zones, suggesting either an absence of antibacterial activity against the tested target strains in these growth conditions, or improper or insufficient compound production, maturation or export. Since the effect of the BGCs can also manifest in alternative ways, their impact on the physiology and morphology of the heterologous hosts was systematically evaluated.

## BGC expression results in growth retardation and morphological aberrations
Growth dynamics are often affected in bacterial cells under stress. Considering no antibacterial activity was observed upon expression of the four BGCs, growth curves of the heterologous expression hosts were analyzed and evaluated. A first observation shows a growth delay of 39 min (BW-MccB17), 59 min (RW-MccB17), 1 h 43 min (SW-MccB17) and 2.5 h (PG-MccB17) for the four gene clusters. The carrying capacity is reduced by 11%, (BW-MccB17), 13% (RW-MccB17) and 17% (SW-MccB17), except for PG-MccB17, which shows no difference (Fig.  a). Furthermore, significant reductions were observed in the area under the curve (resp. 18%, 22%, 24% and 31%) and in maximal growth rate (µmax) (resp. 11%, 25%, 33% and 36%) in the presence of the gene clusters, indicating a reduced fitness and cell viability, again suggesting severe stress or damage to the cells (Fig.  b-c; Supplementary Information 4-Fig.S3b-c). Since MccB17 is known to induce stress by triggering the SOS response in bacterial cells, leading to cell filamentation, we examined whether similar effects occurred in the expression hosts (Garrido et al. ). Microscopic imaging of overnight cultures revealed that the presence of the BGCs induced cell elongation of the heterologous expression host compared to the bacteria carrying the empty vector control (Fig.  d.; Supplementary Information 4-Fig.S3a). Significant differences compared to the empty vector control were observed only for three BGCs, with the most pronounced elongation for the gene cluster from P. fluorescens PGSB8075 (73%), followed by P. putida BW12S1 (26%) and P. putida RW4S1 (20%). While these results suggest a bacterial stress response, a more comprehensive understanding of the effect is required to identify the specific underlying pathways responsible for these observations.
 

## Global gene expression profiling shows distinct alterations in host physiology
Analyzing transcriptomic data can help identify the regulatory pathways affected by antibiotics and thereby provide insight into their mode of action (Heo et al. ; Matern et al. ; O’Rourke et al. ). To better understand the reduced growth dynamics and elongated cell phenotypes, transcriptome profiles of the heterologous expression host E. coli TOP10F’ with the four BGCs were compared to those of the empty vector control. A total of 320 genes were significantly up- or downregulated for the four BGCs compared to the negative control. Only 10 (3%) of those could be found in the expression profiles of all four BGCs. 84 genes (26%), 37 genes (12%), 4 genes (1%) and 40 genes (13%) were uniquely expressed by the heterologous expression hosts of the PG-MccB17, SW-MccB17, BW-MccB17 and RW-MccB17 gene clusters. Approximately half of the genes were detected in the expression profiles when either two or three BGCs were present, with the majority of the significantly expressed genes being observed for the PG-MccB17, RW-MccB17 and SW-MccB17 gene cluster simultaneously (Fig.  a). A PCA analysis showed the clustering patterns of the genes that were significantly up- or downregulated (Supplementary Information 4-Fig. S4). Although the genes primarily grouped according to their respective gene clusters, no clear patterns were observed that would suggest specific pathways were affected.
 The observed genes were linked with the corresponding pathways by performing a Gene Ontology (GO)-enrichment analysis in R (Fig.  b). Each gene cluster displays its own unique set of pathways, yet the enrichment pattern is the most similar between RW-MccB17 and SW-MccB17, followed by PG-MccB17. The pattern for BW-MccB17 is the most distinct, indicating the strongest differential expression compared to the negative control. Despite their similarity to MccB17 and each other, these results confirm that variants of a well-known gene cluster can elicit distinct responses and are still relevant targets in natural product discovery.

## Discussion
Despite challenges in druggability, MccB17 remains of major interest in antibacterial drug development. Understanding its mode of action, biosynthetic machinery, transport mechanisms and resistance pathways could greatly aid in the design of novel antibiotics (Collin & Maxwell ). As an example, variations in the protein sequence or chemical structure could target pathogens that are resistant to the original compound and thereby expand the spectrum of sensitive pathogens (Metelev et al. ; Wang et al. , ). Motivated by earlier observations of MccB17-like gene clusters (Metelev et al. ), we therefore hypothesized that the MccB17 gene cluster variants, despite their similarity to the known cluster, could produce molecules with distinct functions and improved properties.
By using a custom genome mining-based approach, this study identified novel MccB17 congeners in eight environmental Pseudomonas isolates, of which four were selected based on their origin and protein sequence. Multiple sequence alignment of the BGCs revealed higher diversity in these gene clusters compared to those earlier identified in E. coli and P. syringae, particularly in the modification site patterns flanking the conserved central glycine triplet. Furthermore, none of the newly identified congeners contained the immunity gene mcbG. These observations corroborate earlier findings from Ghequire & De Mot ( ) and Metelev et al. ( ), and demonstrate their significance in Pseudomonas spp. The general absence of mcbG might be explained by sufficient expression of mcbEF, which encodes the export machinery, resulting in protection against the produced molecule. Earlier mode-of-action studies could only be executed in an E. coli expression host (Metelev et al. ), indicating a tighter regulation or specific environmental conditions for the BGCs to be expressed in the original Pseudomonas producer strains. A yet unknown defense mechanism may therefore be responsible for protection against the produced compounds in the native host strains.
Functional characterization of the four BGCs suggested a possible toxic effect of PG-MccB17, BW-MccB17, and E. coli MccB17 when transferred into the wild-type strain E. coli MG1655. This strain contains an active SOS response and restriction system, making it an interesting host for studying the mode of action of the putative toxins derived from the Pseudomonas BGCs. However, as E. coli MG1655 is potentially more sensitive to the metabolic burden and stress caused by the relatively large plasmid inserts and produced compounds, recA and endA mutations in E. coli TOP10F’ prevent plasmid degradation and increase plasmid stability and transformation efficiency (ThermoFisher, ). Engineered for molecular cloning and better equipped to tolerate such plasmids, this strain was unlikely to be affected by the production and modification of the peptides and selected as heterologous expression host. Furthermore, together with the observed cell elongation and growth retardation in the heterologous expression hosts, the inability to transform two of the four gene clusters into E. coli MG1655 suggests a toxic effect on the host strain. Cell elongation induced by antibacterial agents is often associated with the inhibition of cell division, as seen for MccB17, which triggers the SOS response by causing DNA damage, leading to cell filamentation (Herrero & Moreno ; Vizán et al. ). In addition, transcriptomic analysis reveals the up- and downregulation of various pathways, indicating a metabolic stress response and subsequent rewiring. However, considered together, these data support distinct physiological responses to distinct secondary metabolites, albeit with shared phenotypic features.
Despite these observations, no antibacterial activity was observed against E. coli MG1655 in growth inhibition assays, nor against other strains that are known to be (hyper)sensitive to E. coli MccB17 (Metelev et al. ). Considering that the BGCs were identified in Pseudomonas isolates and their often limited spectrum of activity, testing antimicrobial activity against more closely related species might therefore be more relevant (Duquesne et al. ). In addition, the effective microcin concentration under the specific experimental conditions of the current study may be too low; often, large fermentation volumes and dedicated extraction procedures are employed to increase the concentration of biologically active secondary metabolites. Alternatively, incomplete modification of the peptide could affect the antibacterial activity; the eight heterocycles in E. coli MccB17 are essential for its inhibitory effect on bacteria (Roy et al. ). Although RT-qPCR illustrates successful transcription of the mcb operons, LC–MS only confirms the production of unmodified, premature peptides. This is supported by −2 Da shifts in the spectra for PG-MccB17, BW-MccB17 and RW-MccB17, illustrating disulfide bond formations and indicating unsuccessful oxazole/thiazole formation. However, additional verification using β-mercaptoethanol is required. Hampered synthesis of the biosynthetic proteins, including the exporter McbEF, is a possible explanation for the incomplete compound and absence of exogenous antibacterial properties.
In conclusion, this work aimed to provide an initial understanding of the modes of action related to the identified MccB17-like molecules. An overall negative phenotypic effect of the BGCs on the heterologous expression host indicates a toxic effect, but a more detailed and thorough analysis will be required to fully understand the actual effect of the BGCs. Nevertheless, the findings in this work suggest that the exploration and functional characterization of novel congeners of known antimicrobials may be a valuable, yet underexplored path to uncover the full potential of these compounds in our fight against antibiotic resistance.
