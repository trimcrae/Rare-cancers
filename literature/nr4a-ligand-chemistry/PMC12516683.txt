## Abstract
The ligand-activated transcription factors of the NR4A
family are
implicated as promising drug targets with neuroprotective and anticancer
potential and attract strong attention in drug discovery. Several
NR4A modulators have been described, but a validated set of direct
ligands for biological studies is lacking. Here, we profiled the reported
and commercially available agonists and inverse agonists under uniform
conditions in several orthogonal test systems to establish a highly
annotated tool and gain comprehensive insights into the NR4A modulator
characteristics. This comparative profiling revealed a lack of on-target
binding and modulation for several putative NR4A ligands and validated
a set of chemically diverse compounds as direct NR4A modulators for
chemogenomics-based target identification studies. Prospective applications
unveiled roles of NR4A receptors in endoplasmic reticulum stress and
adipocyte differentiation, demonstrating suitability of the set to
link the orphan targets with phenotypic effects.

## Introduction
The ligand-activated transcription factors
Nur77 (NR4A1), Nurr1
(NR4A2), and NOR1 (NR4A3) form the NR4A family of nuclear receptors
(NRs), which translate ligand signals into transcriptional responses
and thus enable pharmacological control of gene expression. They are attracting remarkable attention in early
drug discovery and medical research as data from patients, animal
models, and in vitro phenotypic studies ascribe their
modulation therapeutic potential in diverse indications ranging from
neurodegeneration and cancer to inflammation and metabolic dysfunction.
−

The NR4A receptors are widely expressed with overall low tissue
specificity. NR4A2 shows the highest protein
expression levels among the three receptors across various tissues,
including the brain. NR4A3 displays high protein levels only in the
thyroid gland and kidney, and NR4A1 exhibits high protein expression
in the adrenal gland, bronchi, and testis. The receptors share the
archetypal domain structure of NRs (reviewed in refs 
−

) including a DNA binding domain (DBD) recognizing
specific DNA response elements and a ligand binding domain (LBD) for
ligand-dependent activation or inhibition. The LBD recruits coregulatory
proteins to the DNA bound receptor in a ligand-dependent fashion to
activate or block transcriptional activity ( a) wherein the C-terminal helix 12 containing
the ligand-dependent activation function (AF2) has a central role.
It can adopt various conformations and positions regulating the recruitment
and release of coregulators. Typically, helix 12 is unordered in apo-state
and agonist binding induces a stabilized active conformation of the
helix bound to the core of the LBD. However, partly due to two salt
bridges between helix 4 (Glu440 (NR4A1/2), Glu471 (NR4A3)) and helix
12 (Lys590 (NR4A1/2), Lys618 (NR4A3)) as well as helix 11 (Lys577
(NR4A1/2), Lys605 (NR4A3)) and helix 12 (Asp589 (NR4A1/2), Asp617
(NR4A3)) present in NR4A receptors, the AF2 is ordered and bound to
the LBD in an active position also in absence of a ligand ( b), resulting in
substantial constitutive transcriptional inducer activity. Moreover, the NR4A receptors lack the canonical
hydrophobic cavity serving as (orthosteric) ligand binding pocket
in the LBD of most NRs ( c). Although the LBD of NR4A adopts a canonical fold resembling
that of many other NRs, the core region is blocked by bulky hydrophobic
residues preventing the formation of a ligand binding cavity and thus
contributing to stabilization of the LBDs in an autoactivated conformation. Nevertheless, the NR4A activity can be modulated
by ligands. NR4A1 is best characterized in terms of ligand binding
and structural biology, and four putative ligand binding regions have
been identified on the surface of its LBD in crystal structures ( d,f).
−

 For NR4A2 and NR4A3, molecular insights in ligand binding are very
limited, which impedes modulator design. It may be speculated that
similar binding epitopes exist in NR4A2/3 as observed in NR4A1, but
biochemical/biophysical evidence is scarce. To date, cocrystal structures for NR4A2 have only been solved with
5,6-dihydroxyindole (DHI) and prostaglandin
A1 (PGA1), which are each covalently
bound to Cys566 behind helix 12 ( e, site A), and X-ray structures of NR4A3
are not available.
 Preclinical findings
−
,,
 indicate that pharmacological
modulation of NR4A receptors holds
potential to address urgent unmet medical needs but extensive target
validation will be required to eventually translate the promising
therapeutic hypotheses into new medicines. Therefore, high-quality
and highly annotated chemical tools are needed to advance NR4A receptors
as targets, but in contrast to the well-studied NR1 and NR3 families, chemical
tools for NR4 cannot yet be selected from a large pool of high-quality
ligands. Moreover, several of the few published ligands for this protein
family lack validation and sufficient characterization for application
in functional and phenotypic studies, which also puts observations
made with these incompletely profiled tools in cellular and animal
studies into question.
To close this gap and identify a set
of NR4A ligands applicable
to target identification and validation studies, we have evaluated
NR4A modulators described in the literature for their suitability
to probe NR4A-mediated biology. Comparative profiling in uniform and
orthogonal cellular and cell-free in vitro test systems
revealed significant deviations from published activities for several
examples up to a complete lack of NR4A binding and modulation. From
the scarce remaining collection of confirmed ligands, we have assembled
a set of eight validated direct NR4A modulators for reliable in vitro studies on NR4A receptor effects in chemogenomics
(CG)
,,
 and related
applications. Therein, we focused on commercially available tools
to promote broad and unrestricted use. This recommended and highly
annotated set comprises five NR4A agonists and three inverse agonists
with strong chemical diversity, adding further orthogonality. Proof-of-concept
applications in phenotypic in vitro settings corroborated
the set as a robust tool to explore NR4A-mediated biology and revealed
roles in protection from endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress and adipocyte
differentiation.

## Results

## NR4A Modulators
Despite increasing interest in NR4A
receptors, the collection of NR4A ligands in scientific literature
is still scarce and ChEMBL35 (released
December 2024) contains bioactivity data on NR4A receptors for only
653 compounds ( g–i). For comparison, more than 8900 compound/bioactivity
pairs (>6800 active compounds) are available for the extensively
studied
peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors (PPARs, NR1C). Among the
653 compounds tested for NR4A modulation, 344 are reported as active
(≤100 μM), 212 have an EC50, IC50, or K
d value ≤10 μM, and
only 48 have an annotated potency ≤1 μM. The 344 active
compounds represent 159 unique Murcko scaffolds, indicating that different ligand chemotypes were discovered
but only few compound series have been systematically studied for
structure–activity relationships (SAR). NR4A3 is strongly under-represented,
with only six compounds annotated as NOR1 ligands. However, most of
the reported NR4A modulators have not been tested on all three receptors,
which distorts this picture, and observations with more comprehensively
tested NR4A modulators suggest that all three receptors bind very
similar ligands. These results underscore that NR4A receptors are
highly understudied in terms of ligand discovery and that more efforts
are needed to develop high-quality NR4A modulators.
The collection
of NR4A ligands reported in the literature contains several chemotypes
that do not qualify as chemical tools for several reasons. Unsaturated
fatty acids
,
 and prostaglandins
,,
 were identified as potential
endogenous NR4A ligands providing important insights into the mechanisms
and biological roles of the receptor family, but their physicochemical
characteristics, chemical and metabolic instability, lack of specificity
for NR4A, and interaction with multiple lipid-binding proteins and
lipid membranes hinder their application as chemical tools. Similarly,
the dopamine metabolite 5,6-dihydroxyindole (DHI) is a natural NR4A2 ligand and has enabled crucial advance
in structural understanding
−

 of this receptor but is highly
reactive and lacks sufficient potency and selectivity to be used as
a tool. Additionally, the scientific literature contains a number
of putative NR4A receptor modulators
,
 containing
PAINS
,
 motifs with incomplete or flawed characterization
data. Their proposed activity on NR4A is questionable and their chemical
reactivity as well as the lack of evidence for direct binding prohibit
further consideration as tools.

Still, progress has been made in recent years in developing potent
and well-characterized NR4A modulators. As NR4A receptors display
constitutive activity due to their autoactivated conformation, their
activity can be modulated by ligands in a bidirectional fashion
,,,
 and both agonists (enhancing NR4A-dependent transcriptional activity)
and inverse agonists (reducing the receptors’ activity) have
been identified. Despite mostly not reaching chemical probe quality,
,
 these compounds could enable confident target identification and
validation studies for the NR4A receptors when used as a set of well-characterized
modulators with orthogonal chemical diversity following the concept
of chemogenomics (CG).
,
 Moreover, a considerable number
of reported NR4A modulators are commercially available, enabling broad
use in the community ( , overview in Table S1). For their
comparative profiling and validation, we employed uniform Gal4-hybrid-based
and full-length receptor reporter gene assays for all NR4A receptors
to determine cellular NR4A modulation ( , Figures S1–S5, and Tables S4–S6). Selectivity screening was performed
in the Gal4-hybrid setting for a representative panel of NRs outside
the NR4A family ( ). Additionally, isothermal titration calorimetry
(ITC) and differential scanning fluorimetry (DSF) served as cell-free
validations of direct NR4A binding ( and Figures S1d–S4d and S6). Therein, we evaluated all compounds for binding to
NR4A2 as the most prominent receptor of the family and additionally
tested the interaction with NR4A1 for compounds that were initially
reported as ligands of this receptor. Additionally, all compounds
were evaluated for purity (HPLC) and identity (MS or NMR; see Supporting Information), kinetic solubility (Table S2), and in a multiplex toxicity assay
( , Figures S1c–S4c, and Table S11) monitoring
confluence, and phenotypic features characteristic for cell health
(metabolic activity, apoptosis, necrosis) using orthogonal reagents
(WST-8) and dyes (NucView Caspase-3 Dye and Nuc-Fix Red) to confirm
suitability of the chemical tools for application in cellular settings.
 

## Validation and Comparative Profiling of NR4A Modulators
Cytosporone B (CsnB, 1; and Figure S1a) was one of the first NR4A modulators reported in literature and
was identified as a potent NR4A1 agonist (cellular EC50(NR4A1) = 0.115 nM; K
d (NR4A1) = 0.852
μM) in an in vitro screening of natural products. A cocrystal structure of Nur77 in complex with 1 (PDB: 6KZ5) supported direct binding to a pocket located at the receptor’s
dimer interface ( d, site D). Initial SAR studies suggested
the alkyl ester function as a key pharmacophore for Nur77 activation, but any potency increasing modifications also
enhanced the already extensive lipophilicity of 1 (logP
= 7.33). TMPA (2), PDNPA, and THPN (3) are
notable synthetic CsnB derivatives that have been used for biological
studies. TMPA (2) was claimed as a neutral antagonist
of NR4A1 with no (intrinsic) activity in a transactivation assay at
0.5 μM. A cocrystal structure revealed
binding of 2 at two sites on the NR4A1 LBD surface, one
located between helices 4, 11, and 12 ( d, site A) and one in a cavity close to helices
1, 5, and 8 ( d, site B). PDNPA was reported to bind to site A ( d) of the NR4A1 LBD and to suppress LPS-induced NFκB reporter
activity (IC50 = 1.6 μM) in a NR4A1-dependent manner.
Direct NR4A modulation by PDNPA was not evaluated. THPN (3) has been investigated for Nur77-dependent anticancer activities
,
 and later described as a NR4A1 agonist/modulator.
,
 A cocrystal structure indicated THPN (3) binding to
another solvent-accessible pocket on the NR4A1 LBD surface between
helices 5, 7, 8, 9, and 10 ( d, site C).
,
 Overall, the molecular
characterization of 1–3 and PDNPA
as NR4A ligands is incomplete, and although cocrystal structures may
suggest these compounds as validated direct binders, several open
questions remain. Independent studies failed to confirm binding of 2 to the NR4A2 LBD via NMR and
the proposed binding of the chemically similar compounds (1–3 and PDNPA) to three distinct sites in cocrystal
structures of the NR4A1 LBD is surprising. Closer inspection of these
X-ray structures reveals very weak to nonexistent electron densities
for the ligands ( f). For validation or devalidation, we thus profiled 1–3 for NR4A modulation and binding. Gal4-hybrid
reporter gene assays and full-length reporter gene assays using the
NR4A monomer response element NBRE revealed no effects of CsnB (1) and TMPA (2) on Nur77, Nurr1, or NOR1 activity
at a high nontoxic concentration ( and Figure S1b,c). THPN (3) exhibited moderate pan-NR4A activation in
the hybrid settings but no conclusive activity in the full-length
receptors. Similarly, ITC showed binding of 3, no binding
of 1, and possibly weak binding of 2 to
the NR4A1 and NR4A2 LBDs, while DSF supported potential binding of
all three compounds to the NR4A2 LBD (Figures S1d and S6). Based on these results, 1 and 2 cannot be considered direct NR4A modulators and are not
suitable as tools to explore NR4A biology. Observations made in biological
experiments with these compounds should not be considered as NR4A-mediated
effects. THPN (3) likely exhibits direct NR4A1–3
activation based on our data and may be an early chemical tool to
study NR4A receptors.
Two further natural products, celastrol
(4) and isoalantolactone (5), have been
proposed as NR4A1 modulators with inverse agonist activity.
,
 SPR supported binding of celastrol (4) to the NR4A1
LBD (K
d = 0.29 μM), while direct
interaction of isoalantolactone (5) with the NR4A1 LBD
remains to be confirmed. Comparative profiling in our systems ( and Figures S1b–d, S6, and S7) revealed pronounced
cytotoxicity and no detectable NR4A modulation at nontoxic concentration
by celastrol (4), preventing conclusive characterization
in a cellular setting. DSF was not suitable to assess binding of 4 to the NR4A2 LBD due to strong quenching effects, and while
ITC failed to confirm a direct interaction with the NR4A2 LBD, it
indicated possible weak binding to the NR4A1 LBD. Isoalantolactone
(5) turned out to be inactive in NR4A reporter gene assays,
and both DSF and ITC did not support binding. Moreover, the natural
product turned out to be rather unstable (Figure S8) and degraded too fast for application in cellular settings.
Resveratrol, flavonoids,
,
 and 12-deacetyl-12-epi-scalaradial have also been reported as putative NR4A1 modulators, but
these natural products exhibit well-known pleiotropic and nonspecific
effects and contain PAINS elements.
,
 Convincing
evidence for direct interaction with NR4A receptors has not been provided,
and therefore, we have not considered these compounds further.
The endogenous NR4A ligand 5,6-dihydroxyindole (DHI) disqualifies as a chemical tool but has enabled
important progress in NR4A ligand development. It is a reactive metabolite
endogenously formed from dopamine and binds covalently to Cys566 of
the NR4A2 LBD located in a noncanonical pocket behind helix 12. The metabolite was reported to induce weak NR4A2
activation at ≥10 μM. Evaluation
of fragment-like DHI analogues for NR4A2 binding and modulation revealed
enhanced NR4A2 affinity and activation for 5-haloindols, among which
5-chloroindol emerged as a valuable DHI mimetic (K
d = 15 μM, cellular NR4A2 activation ≥5 μM). Binding and cellular activity of 5-chloroindol
are well-validated, but the compound’s potency is too low for
a use as a chemical tool. Further progress was made based on virtual
screening of 14,421 5-chloroindol derivatives for binding to the solvent-exposed
DHI binding site in the NR4A2 LBD yielding the DHI-derived NR4A agonist
5o (cellular EC50(NR4A2) = 3 μM, K
d(NR4A2) = 0.5 μM). 5o demonstrated druggability of the DHI binding epitope and opened
an avenue to more potent ligands by addressing surface regions, but
the compound contains a potentially reactive motif and lacks sufficient
potency. Further optimization yielded the highly potent NR4A ligand
37 (ME43, 6), which has qualified as a chemical probe.
,

6 activates the NR4A receptors with nanomolar potencies
and displayed uniform effects on all studied NR4A dimers and response
elements ( and Figures S1b–d and S5). Its binding was
evident in ITC (K
d 0.12 μM) and DSF ( and Figure S6). The latter
revealed thermal destabilization by the ligand likely resulting from
agonist-induced dissociation of the strong NR4A2 homodimer, which
has been proposed as an important component of the receptor’s
activation mechanism.
,

Novel NR4A modulators have
also been identified based on the natural
ligand PGA1. Prostaglandins evolve from enzymatic cyclooxygenase (COX)
activity, and hence, COX inhibitors have been evaluated as potential
NR4A ligands. Activity screening of chemically
diverse nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) revealed oxaprozin
(7) and parecoxib (8) as inverse agonists
on all three NR4A receptors. Valdecoxib (9), lornoxicam
(10), and mofezolac displayed inverse agonism with preference
for NR4A2 (9) and NR4A1/3 (10 and mofezolac),
respectively. The NSAIDs exhibited moderate potency and are not specific
for NR4A, but 7–10 may qualify as
early inverse agonist chemical tools. Oxaprozin (7) has been further evaluated for its SAR
as inverse Nurr1 agonist resulting in the more selective descendant
compound 53 (11).
11 blocks NR4A activity very efficiently (up to 6% remaining
activity, 37% at 50 μM) and lacks
retinoid X receptor (RXR) agonism and COX inhibition of 7, thus improving the chemical tool characteristics. Although the
moderate potency of 11 requires further optimization,
the compound is a useful NR4A modulator for CG. Comparative profiling
of 7–11 revealed consistent inhibition
of Nur77, Nurr1, and NOR1 activity in Gal4-hybrid settings and NBRE-based
full-length receptor reporter gene assays ( and Figure S2b). Cytotoxicity at their effective concentrations was low ( and Figure S2c), supporting the use as a tool in
a cellular context. ITC and DSF supported binding of 7–11 to the Nurr1 LBD with low micromolar affinities
according to ITC (1.0–3.5 μM, Figure S2d), providing orthogonal evidence for NR4A-mediated effects.
7–9 and 11 were thus considered suitable as chemical tools for NR4A
for CG. Due to their lower potency, efficacy, and similar chemical
scaffolds, 10 and mofezolac were not evaluated further.
Drug fragment screening discovered methyl 1H-indole-3-carboxylate
as another inverse NR4A agonist scaffold (IC50(NR4A2) =
48 μM).
,
 Systematic optimization and structural
extension revealed a preference for substituents in 5-position and
yielded 12 (IC50(NR4A2) = 2.3 μM) and 13 (IC50(NR4A2) = 3.8 μM) as improved inverse
NR4A2 agonists. Despite similar potencies, 13 exhibited substantially stronger NR4A2 inhibition, and
both compounds displayed a slight preference over NR4A1/3.
13 counteracted NR4A2 homodimerization
and displaced NCoR-1/-2 from the receptor but had no effect on heterodimerization
and binding of NCoA6. The chemotype of 12/13 comprises a methyl ester, and the corresponding
carboxylic acids were found to be markedly less active. Attempts to replace this labile motif have not
been successful yet. Thus, 12 and 13 are
only suitable as tools for in vitro application but
valuably extend the inverse NR4A agonist collection with a chemically
orthogonal and slightly more potent scaffold. Considering its higher
efficacy, we prioritized 13 and comparative profiling
revealed robust NR4A inhibition in the cellular assays ( and Figure S2b), lack of toxicity at effective concentration (Figure S2c), and binding to the NR4A2 LBD in
ITC (Figure S2d) supporting its use as
a chemical tool for NR4A.
Screening of approved drugs has been a very fruitful source of
NR4A modulators. The antimalarials amodiaquine (AQ, 14) and chloroquine (CQ, 15) were the first synthetic
ligands shown to bind and activate NR4A2. Later studies have validated binding to the NR4A2 LBD and demonstrated modulation of all three NR4A
receptors with similar potencies.
14 and 15 were instrumental as early tools to
promote research on NR4A2 as an attractive target in neurodegenerative
diseases,
,−

 but both compounds are
highly unselective and exhibit substantial nonspecific effects in
cellular settings.
,,
 Additionally, 14 has PAINS character and fluorescent
properties, rendering its use in many test systems problematic. Therefore,
several studies have explored the SAR of 14/15 to overcome their limitations and to obtain NR4A modulators with
improved potency. Fragmentation studies have shown that the 7-chloroquinoline-4-amine
shared by 14 and 15 alone is sufficient
to induce weak NR4A activation, and systematic optimization yielded
8-chloro-2-methylquinolin-4-amine as a fragment-like NR4A agonist
(cellular EC50(NR4A2) = 17 μM). Additionally, efforts to overcome the unfavorable 4-aminophenol
motif of amodiaquine (14) have succeeded in identifying
5-(4-chlorophenyl)­furan-2-carboxamide and (5-(4-chlorophenyl)­furan-2-yl)­methanamine
residues as valuable replacement. Scaffold
hopping, fragment growing, and fusion of the SAR knowledge on the
chemotype resulted in 16 (EC50(NR4A2) = 0.09
μM) as highly optimized NR4A agonist derived from the AQ scaffold.
16 was validated in several orthogonal
settings, and comprehensive profiling supported high chemical tool
quality. In midbrain organoids derived from LRRK2 mutant iPSC, treatment
with 16 rescued Nurr1-regulated tyrosine hydroxylase
(TH) expression. Another remarkable optimization campaign of AQ (14) as NR4A ligand testing more than 500 derivatives discovered
4A7C-301 (17) as a NR4A2 agonist exhibiting beneficial
effects in Parkinson’s disease in vitro models. While 17 exhibited high affinity
to NR4A2 in a radioligand binding assay (IC50 = 50 nM),
cellular activation in a reporter gene assay was only observed at
concentrations ≥10 μM. Interestingly, 17 was reported to exhibit a pronounced preference for NR4A2
over NR4A1 and NR4A3, which is a very
rare feature of NR4A ligands. 16 and 17 thus
emerge as the most promising chemical tools for NR4A receptors derived
from AQ. Additionally, the wide previous use of 14 and 15 in many studies addressing Nurr1 supported further evaluation
for comparison. Comprehensive profiling of 14–17 revealed rather diverse activity profiles ( and Figure S3). AQ (14) mediated the strongest reporter activation
in the cellular assays of all NR4A ligands evaluated in this study
(Figure S3b). CQ (15) also
displayed strong efficacy, but both compounds exhibited weak potency
and their effective concentrations (30–100 μM) in the
cellular assays coincided with cytotoxicity (Figure S3b,c). Additionally, substantial nonspecific effects on transcriptional
activity have been observed for antimalarials of the chloroquinoline-type,
,
 which likely contribute to the observed strong reporter activation.
This was also evident from the effects of 14 and 15, which showed similarly high activity on the ligand-independent
transcriptional inducer Gal4-VP16
,
 as observed
for Gal4-NR4A (Figure S7). The optimized
descendant 16 was nontoxic, exhibited NR4A activation
at significantly lower concentrations (0.3–1 μM), and
had minimal impact on Gal4-VP16 activity (Figure S7). Efficacy of 16 was lower compared to 14 and 15, and, as reported previously,
16 only activated NurRE and DR5
reporters but not the monomer response element NBRE. Profiling of
4A7C-301 (17) revealed moderate reporter activation in
all Gal4-NR4A hybrid assays and all NBRE-based settings at 10 μM
and markedly higher effects at 20 μM, indicating a dose–response
with an unusually steep slope. The cytotoxicity assay revealed a similar
profile with mild toxic effects of 17 at 10 μM
that substantially increased at 20 and 30 μM. Nevertheless,
Gal4-VP16 activity was not markedly affected by 17 at
up to 20 μM, suggesting the presence of NR4A-mediated effects.
Orthogonal validation of binding to the NR4A2 LBD by ITC (Figure S3d) revealed micromolar affinities for 14, 15, and 17, and a strong nanomolar
affinity for 16, while no
binding was evident for 14, 15, and 17 from DSF (Figure S6).
AQ (14) and CQ (15) have a prominent
role in NR4A research as the first synthetic NR4A2 modulators. Both
compounds have been extensively used in early studies, and broad data
on likely NR4A-mediated phenotypic effects are available. Despite
confirmed direct NR4A modulation, it must be noted that these compounds
display limited potency, affinity, and selectivity and contain PAINS
elements contradicting their application as chemical tools. Any further
use of 14 and 15 to study NR4A biology must
consider these limitations. The optimized analogue 16 emerges as an improved AQ-derived tool for NR4A with higher potency,
affinity, and selectivity, while 17 shares some limitations
of 14 and 15.
Members of the statin
drug class have also been identified as NR4A
modulators. Based on a fragment screening
hit, fluvastatin (18) was discovered as a NR4A2 activator
(cellular EC50 = 1.9 μM) and follow-up experiments
revealed NR4A2 modulation by all drug-approved statins except pravastatin
(19). Interestingly, 19 is also the only statin for which no neuroprotective effects
have been demonstrated, suggesting a potential connection between
this activity and NR4A2 modulation. Pitavastatin (20)
emerged as most potent NR4A2 agonist of the drug class (cellular EC50 = 0.12 μM), whereas the most widely used representative
simvastatin (21) displayed intermediate potency (cellular
EC50 = 5.1 μM). Simvastatin
effects on inflammatory pathways, apoptosis, cell cycle regulation,
and energy homeostasis in cellular studies involving NR4A2 knockdown
indicated that NR4A2 modulation may contribute to its neuroprotective
activity. Statins have been validated
for direct (HTRF assays) and cellular target engagement and appear
suitable as CG candidates despite their ″main″ target
activity on HMG-CoA reductase. Comparative profiling of 18, 20, and 21 as representatives of the
class revealed consistent activation of all NR4A receptors at nontoxic
concentrations in the Gal4-hybrid and full-length reporter gene assays
( and Figure S3b,c). Based on stronger efficacy, orthogonally
confirmed binding (HTRF, ITC, and DSF
(Figures S3d and S6)), and more available
NR4A modulation data, we recommend the use of fluvastatin (18) and simvastatin (21) as chemical tools, but NR4A activation
and binding were also confirmed for pitavastatin (Figure S3d), which can hence be considered for an extended
set. Pravastatin (19) displayed no NR4A activation or
binding in any test system (Figures S3b and S6) and may thus serve as a negative control compound for statins in
the context of NR4A.
The dihydroorotate dehydrogenase (DHODH)
inhibitor vidofludimus
(22), which is in late-stage clinical development for
the treatment of multiple sclerosis, has recently been found to exhibit
substantial NR4A agonism. Its potency
on NR4A2 (cellular EC50 = 0.4 μM) exceeded DHODH
inhibition (IC50 = 0.61 μM), while NR4A1 (cellular
EC50 = 3.1 μM) and NR4A3 (cellular EC50 = 2.9 μM) agonism was an order of magnitude weaker. With these
characteristics and strong NR4A activation efficacy, 22 emerged as an attractive chemical tool and as lead to develop optimized
NR4A agonists. Other DHODH inhibitors (farudostat, MEDS433, RP7214,
and AG-636) did not affect Nurr1 activity supporting a direct effect
of 22, which was also evident in ITC (K
d = 0.7 μM). Structural
optimization of 22 has yielded compound 29 as a more potent NR4A agonist with less DHODH inhibitory activity. Nevertheless, the commercially available 22 is an attractive CG compound candidate for NR4A and emerged
as one of the strongest activators in comparative cellular profiling
and with confirmed binding in ITC and DSF ( and Figures S3b,d and S6).
Further NR4A modulators reported in literature and
emerging as
potential tools for a CG set include imidazopyridine derivatives related
to SR24237 and SA00025, bis­(indolyl)­methane
derivatives related to C-DIM12 (23),
−

 isoxazolo-pyridinones related
to IP7e (24), and the Nur77
modulators 10g (25)
,
 and 26. SR24237, SA00025, and related
compounds have been independently evaluated for NR4A binding and modulation
in previous studies
,
 and devalidated as direct NR4A
ligands. Direct NR4A modulation has also been questioned for C-DIM12
(23) derivatives and IP7e (24), which exhibited
no binding to NR4A2 in NMR experiments. In our hands, C-DIM12 (23) failed to modulate any NR4A

(25) was obtained in a docking-guided hybridization strategy
and reported to exhibit antiproliferative activity in liver cancer
cell lines.

## Selectivity Profiling of Selected NR4A Modulators
Initial
evaluation validated 3, 6–9, 11, 13–18, 20–22, and 26 as direct NR4A
ligands potentially suitable to form a CG library. As a key feature
of chemical tools, we next evaluated the selectivity of these candidate
compounds over related nuclear receptors in cellular assays with the
same uniform Gal4-hybrid format as for NR4A ( and Tables S7–S10). We selected a representative panel
,
 of 13 nonsteroid
hormone nuclear receptors covering the NR1, NR2, and NR5 families.
The validated NR4A ligands displayed generally favorable selectivity.
Notable off-target activities at the recommended concentration for
NR4A modulation were detected on peroxisome proliferator-activated
receptor γ (PPARγ; 7 and 18),
RXRα (7), reverse ErbA α (revERBα; 3, 8, and 22), retinoic acid receptor-related
orphan receptor γ (RORγ; 6, 7, 9, 11, 20–22, and 26), hepatocyte nuclear factor 4α (HNF4α; 7–9, 14, 15, 18, 20–22, and 26), and liver receptor homologue-1 (LRH1; 7–9, 13, 15, 20, and 26). Despite this incomplete selectivity of several NR4A modulators,
the compounds comprised nonoverlapping off-target effects supporting
their application in CG in which the use of several orthogonal compounds
compensates lower selectivity.

## Comparison of NR4A Modulators and CG Set Composition
Sixteen validated, direct NR4A modulators emerged from the comprehensive
profiling as chemical tool candidates. However, potency, affinity,
efficacy, and selectivity varied substantially in this collection,
advocating the selection of preferred (core set) and secondary (extended
set) tools based on community-agreed criteria (Table S3), the respective best activity profiles, and chemical
diversity.
Among the inverse agonists 7–9 and 11 potency was in a similar (low) range
with no main distinction criterion, but 8 and 11 displayed better selectivity than 7 and 9 and were hence prioritized for the preferred CG set. Additionally, 13 offered slightly higher potency than 8 and 11, and it added further chemical diversity to the preferred
inverse agonist collection. Among the confirmed NR4A agonists, 6, 16, and 22 could be clearly prioritized
for the preferred set based on their submicromolar potencies and binding
affinities, favorable selectivity, and low toxicity. Additionally, 18 and 21 offered sufficient potency/affinity
and selectivity to be included in the core set to enhance chemical
diversity. Pairwise Tanimoto similarity computed on extended connectivity
fingerprints (radius = 2, ECFP4) confirmed
the high chemical diversity of the core set (0.15 ± 0.04 pairwise
similarity, 0.16 ± 0.07 pairwise scaffold similarity (mean ±
SD); Figure S9). The remaining validated
NR4A modulators would either not improve the chemical diversity of
the core set or display limitations such as low potency (15 and 26) and unspecific effects (14 and 15), but may be a valuable extension in some applications.
The NR4A ligands 6, 13, 16, 18, 21, and 22 in the core
set comply with the community-agreed criteria for CG compounds (Table S3) and appear highly suitable for application
in phenotypic in vitro models. 8 and 11 deviate from this high quality, but their inclusion is
needed to represent inverse agonism sufficiently in the set. The favorable
profiles of both compounds, despite the high concentrations required
for effects on NR4A, support this compromise.

## Application of NR4A Modulators for Chemo­geno­mics
The core set of eight NR4A modulators (6, 8, 11, 13, 16, 18, 21, and 22) emerges as a highly annotated
chemical tool for CG. To further explore the set’s suitability
for this purpose and possibly reveal new roles of NR4A receptors,
we applied it to functional and phenotypic in vitro models for proof of concept ( ). For a comprehensive comparison and to obtain uniform
data of all validated NR4A modulators, we also included the extended
set in these experiments.
 Based on conflicting reports on the role of NR4A
receptors in endoplasmic
reticulum (ER) stress,
−

 we probed the impact of the CG set on tunicamycin-induced activity
of the activating transcription factor 6 (ATF6), which senses ER stress. An ATF6 reporter cell line revealed strong and
consistent effects of NR4A modulators on the ATF6 activity ( b). Except for the
NR4A1 preferential agonist 26, all NR4A agonists contained
in the core set and extended set mediated a pronounced reduction in
ATF6 activity with several compounds almost completely abolishing
the effect of tunicamycin. Inverse NR4A agonists had only a weak impact
and displayed no consistent trend. These results support an involvement
of NR4A receptors in ER stress regulation or resolution and indicate
that their activation could protect from ER stress. The lack of effect
for 26 may suggest that NR4A2/3 rather than NR4A1 mediated
the ER stress reduction but as 26 is the only NR4A1 preferential
compound in the collection, further experiments will be required to
confirm this hypothesis.
While NR4A receptors have been mainly
studied in and linked to
neuroprotection
,
 and cancer,
,
 some lines of evidence also point to their regulatory involvement
in metabolic balance. Based on a proposed
role of NR4A receptors in obesity and fat loss we studied the effects of the CG set on adipocyte differentiation
and lipid storage ( c) using adipocyte-derived stem cells (ASCs). These cells
differentiate into mature adipocytes and accumulate large amounts
of fat under cyclic treatment with insulin, isobutyl-methylxanthine,
dexamethasone, and the PPARγ agonist pioglitazone. All three NR4A receptors are expressed in ASC
cells,
,
 and, interestingly, are upregulated during
differentiation pointing to a role in the differentiation process.
NR4A agonists did not affect adipocyte differentiation or fat storage
in this complex phenotypic setting. Inverse NR4A agonists, in contrast,
increased the fat content of adipocytes measured after 18 days of
differentiation and treatment with varying degrees of efficacy. 8 mediated the strongest fat deposition and resembled pioglitazone;
compounds 13 and 7 had an intermediate effect,
and compounds 11 and 9 exhibited only mild
to no increases in lipid accumulation. These observed effects of inverse
NR4A agonists were not fully consistent, but it should be noted that
this type of NR4A modulator still has limited quality (e.g., off-target
activity of 8 on PPARγ) and requires further optimization.
Together with the upregulation of NR4A receptors during adipocyte
differentiation, these results may therefore
suggest that physiological adipose tissue homeostasis requires NR4A
activity and is disrupted by NR4A inhibition.
As NR4A receptors
are particularly related to neurodegenerative
diseases and as such pathologies, including Parkinson’s, are
thought to involve dysfunctional autophagy,

et al. to monitor autophagy flux in rat

to dysfunctional autophagosome–lysosome fusion,
−

 and by statins, which is also a known unspecific effect. The more selective NR4A agonists 6, 16, and 22 as well as the inverse agonists 7–9, 11, and 13 did not alter autophagy. To further validate these observations,
we repeated the experiment in the presence of the autophagy inducer

and generally stronger inhibition by 14, 15, 17, and 21. Given the lack of effects
of the most selective NR4A agonists 6, 16, and 22, an involvement of NR4A receptors in autophagy
in neurons is hence not evident from these observations.
These
results from complex in vitro models demonstrate
that the NR4A CG set is suitable to link the orphan targets with phenotypic
outcomes and to generate new hypotheses according to the concept of
CG
,
 but also to disconnect the targets from
biological effects. The robustness of the set arises from the presence
of several chemically diverse agonists and inverse agonists, which
adds orthogonality for confident target identification and validation
studies.

## Discussion and Conclusions
Several lines of evidence
support therapeutic potential of NR4A
modulation, for example, for neuroprotection and anticancer effects.
These findings have fueled drug discovery for the orphan receptors,
yielding several types of modulators. However, the level of activity
profiling and annotation varied substantially for different compounds,
and a validated set of ligands for application in target identification
and validation studies was lacking. To close this gap and compare
activity profiles of the available NR4A modulators, we characterized
them in several orthogonal systems and under uniform conditions. This
comparative profiling provided valuable insights into the collection
of putative NR4A ligands. Most importantly, the reported activity
of several examples could not be reproduced, which contradicts their
use as chemical tools and puts previous findings about NR4A effects
into question. It should be noted, however, that some NR4A modulators
(e.g., celastrol, THPN, and C-DIM derivatives) were reported to have
atypical modes of action such as induction or inhibition of nuclear
export
,,
 of NR4A receptors,
modulation of their protein–protein interactions,
,
 and alteration of their posttranslational modification, which may not be detected by some test systems.
Moreover, ligands targeting atypical binding sites outside the NR4A
LBD may be omitted by Gal4-hybrid reporter
gene assays (but not the full-length assay settings) and by binding
studies using the NR4A LBD proteins. Nevertheless, therapeutic hypotheses
referring to NR4A modulation derived from observations made with possibly
inappropriate tools may require reconsideration and revalidation.
The second important result of comparative, face-to-face characterization
in uniform test systems is the strong overlap in the ligand binding
of all three NR4A receptors. Almost all compounds displayed activity
on Nur77, Nurr1, and NOR1 with moderate subtype preference of some
examples but no subtype selectivity. This characteristic is common
among NRs with PPARs as an illustrative example, where subtype selectivity
was only achieved with extensive medicinal chemistry efforts and insights
from cocrystal structures.
Interestingly, NR4A modulators whose
activity could be validated
in the various orthogonal settings displayed considerable chemical
diversity with rather basic representatives such as AQ and descendants
as well as acidic fatty acid mimetics such as vidofludimus. As observed
in the few available cocrystal structures, this points to the presence
of more than a single ligand binding site in NR4A receptors. Further
studies are needed to confirm this hypothesis, but the existence of
two or more binding epitopes for pharmacological modulation of NR4A
receptors may open avenues to additive effects by addressing both/several
sites and possibly (gene-?) selective modulation via the individual
pockets. Moreover, NR4A ligands turned out to be quite selective over
NRs outside this family, although most compounds have not been extensively
optimized, yet. NR4A receptors hence seem to display different ligand
specificity than other NRs, and off-target effects appear to be less
relevant than, for example, in the NR1 family, aligning with the hypothesis
that the NR4A ligand binding site(s) differ(s) from the orthogonal
pocket of classical NRs.
Among the validated NR4A ligands, agonists
are substantially more
advanced, and some examples have already achieved chemical probe quality.
Inverse agonists, in contrast, exhibit relatively low potency, and
further optimization is needed to better separate their on-target
activity from unspecific effects. Nevertheless, there are several
good chemical tools to study NR4A biology among both agonists and
inverse agonists if the limitations of the available ligands are taken
into account and the compounds are used as a chemically orthogonal
set. As evident from the proof-of-concept results, the NR4A ligands
validated in this study are suitable to generate new biological hypotheses
on NR4A receptors and to link the orphan targets to phenotypic effects.
Despite the availability of a few good chemical tools, NR4A ligand
development is still at an early stage, with several challenges ahead.
While agonist development has been quite successful mostly without
structural insights, inverse agonists require structural optimization
to achieve potency and specificity. Mechanistic understanding of NR4A
modulation in terms of dimerization and coregulator interactions is
still incomplete but will benefit from more and improved ligands.
Finally, achieving subtype selectivity within the family appears to
be a very difficult task and will require substantially more molecular
insights from cocrystal structures.
The CG set as a tool to
explore the therapeutic potential of NR4A
receptors and further ligand development efforts may advance this
family toward future therapeutic targets in highly underdeveloped
fields like neurodegeneration and address urgent medical needs.

## Computational Methods

## General
Computational analysis was performed with Konstanz
Information Miner (KNIME, version 5.1.2, KNIME AG, Zurich, Switzerland)
software with RDKit (version 4.9.1) and CDK (version 1.5.600) nodes.

## Evaluation of Published NR4A Modulators
NR4A modulator
bioactivities were extracted from the public database ChEMBL35 for the human NR4A1 (CHEMBL1293229), NR4A2 (CHEMBL5002),
and NR4A3 (CHEMBL1961792), respectively. Within each receptor data
set, compound duplicates were filtered based on the molecule ChEMBL
ID while keeping the entry with the highest pChEMBL value. The three
data sets were then combined, and duplicates were filtered again as
before to retrieve unique compounds within the NR4A subfamily. Only
compounds with annotated NR4A bioactivity ≤100 μM were
considered as active. Comparison with the extensively studied NR1C
family was built on the human NR1C1 (CHEMBL239), NR1C2 (CHEMBL3979),
and NR1C3 (CHEMBL235) bioactivities in the same manner. For skeleton
analysis, RDKit Find Murcko Scaffolds with the setting create frameworks
was used.

## Evaluation of Chemical Diversity
The ECFP4 fingerprints
of the molecular structures (as Canonical SMILES) and Murcko Scaffolds
(from RDKit Find Murcko Scaffolds) were calculated via the CDK Fingerprints
node. After cross joining of all entries, the pairwise Tanimoto similarity
was calculated from the fingerprint bit vectors and displayed in a
matrix plot.

## Assay Procedure
HEK293T cells (German Collection of

protocol. Five hours after transfection, cells were incubated with

mean RLU of the untreated control. The following reference ligands
were used: THRα: T3; RARα, tretinoin; PPARγ, pioglitazone;
revERBα: SR9011; RORγ, LXRα: T0901317; FXR: GW4064;

of 1 μM. All samples were tested in at least three biologically
independent experiments.

Junction, New Jersey, USA), and absorbance was measured at 1, 2, and

activity of the cells. Before drug administration, after the first
medium exchange, 24 h after drug administration, and after fluorescence
imaging, cell confluence was assessed using the Tecan Spark Cyto,
to account for changes in cell confluence due to drug administration
and cell handling. The number of apoptotic cells, metabolic activity,
and changes in cell confluence in response to drug treatment were
all normalized to the vehicle control of each biological replicate

taken at a 4× magnification using a MoticAE31E inverted microscope

following 3 days, the puromycin concentration was gradually increased
to 4 μg/mL to enrich pMRX-IP-GFP-LC3-RFP-LC3ΔG-transfected
N27 cells. To monitor autophagic flux over time, cells were seeded
at a density of 20,000 cells per well in 96-well plates using complete
growth medium. After 24 h, the medium was changed to RPMI 1640 medium
supplemented with 1% FCS, penicillin (100 U/mL), and streptomycin
(100 μg/mL), and the cells were stimulated with the NR4A CG
library solubilized in 0.1% DMSO or with 0.1% DMSO alone as vehicle
control. Additionally, autophagy was induced with 250 nM Torin-1 after
24 h of NR4 CG library preincubation. Using a Tecan Spark Cyto (Tecan
Group AG), cells were maintained at 37 °C and 5% CO2 and fluorescence images (green channel: λex 461–487
nm, λem 500–530 nm; red channel: λex 543–566 nm, λem 580–611 nm)
were taken every 3 h for the indicated period. RFP- and GFP-positive
cells were identified and quantified separately using CellProfiler
(Version 4.2.6), and the ratio of GFP- to RFP-positive cells was calculated.
Values for each individual compound and replicate were normalized
to the GFP/RFP cell ratio at time point t
0. Experiments were performed in four repeats and were validated in
independent experiments.
