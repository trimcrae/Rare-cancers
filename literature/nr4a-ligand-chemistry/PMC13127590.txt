## Abstract
Cancer therapeutics frequently fail in clinical trials because of poor therapeutic index (efficacy-to-toxicity ratio). We systematically identified targets likely to have a good therapeutic index, revealing insulin receptor substrate 4 (IRS4) as a dependency in IRS4-expressing cancers. Pan-cancer analysis of pediatric-enriched cancers revealed IRS4 expression consistent with dependency in 68% of choroid plexus, 37% of malignant rhabdoid, 31% of NUT midline, and 5% of osteosarcomas, while in adult cancers, it was expressed in 8% of uterine leiomyosarcomas and 1 to 2% of lung squamous, stomach, and breast carcinomas. IRS4 expression in adult tumors was associated with enhancer hijacking rearrangements, including recurrent GATA3-IRS4 and ANKRD30A-IRS4 in breast cancer, while rhabdoid and NUT midline cancers expressed IRS4 epigenetically. IRS4 fueled cancer dependency through PI3K-Akt activation, and domain analysis revealed the PH and PTB domains, which have a predicted drug pocket, to be dispensable, suggesting degradation-based modalities. These data reveal IRS4 as a target in IRS4-expressing cancers and suggest inhibitory approaches.

IRS4 is predicted to have a high therapeutic window when targeted in cancers with genetic or epigenetic up-regulation.

## INTRODUCTION
Only 3 to 15% of anticancer therapeutics entering phase 1 clinical trials ultimately become US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved ( – ), which is the lowest rate among major diseases ( ). This challenge is partly because cancer relies on therapeutic targets that are also found in normal cells, given that cancer arises from normal tissues. The ability of a therapy to reduce tumor burden while minimizing toxicity is conceptualized as the therapeutic index, with a high index indicating strong antitumor efficacy and low normal-tissue toxicity ( ). The ability to a priori predict targets with a good therapeutic index would increase the likelihood of FDA approval through improved target selection.
The Dependency Map (DepMap) ( ) effort has assessed the fitness effects of knocking out each coding gene in >1000 cancer cell lines, thus facilitating the identification of cancer targets. Selectively dependent genes (necessary in a subset of cancer cell lines but dispensable for most) have been suggested to have a less toxic, targeted therapy–like profile when drugged, while “pan-essential” genes (necessary in all cell lines) are likely to result in chemotherapy-like toxicity when drugged ( ). Moreover, large human genetic datasets such as gnomAD ( ), the UK Biobank ( ), and germline genetic studies have enabled the systematic identification of genes that are nonessential in the human population, as evidenced by tolerated nonsense and frameshift-inactivating variants in relatively healthy individuals ( ). Therapeutically targeting the protein products of these nonessential genes, particularly when such genes are found to be restricted in expression to nonessential tissues, may be well tolerated given that humans can survive without these genes.
Here, we have used the DepMap and human germline data to systematically identify genes predicted to have a high therapeutic index when targeted, as evidenced by cancer cell line dependency and nonessentiality in the human germ line. We then studied the mechanisms of cancer dependency of one of these genes, insulin receptor substrate 4 (IRS4), which may be amenable to therapeutic targeting because of a predicted small-molecule binding pocket. IRS4-adjacent enhancer hijacking deletions promoting IRS4 expression were previously identified in a small proportion of lung squamous, cervical, and other cancers ( ), although models for this alteration are lacking. Here, we identified both genetic (primarily in adult tumors) and epigenetic (primarily in pediatric-associated cancers) mechanisms of IRS4 expression in human cancer while expanding the known repertoire of IRS4 enhancer hijacking partners to include recurrent interchromosomal rearrangements in malignant solid tumors, including breast cancer. We further elucidated the mechanisms of IRS4 dependency in two cancer types, including which domains are essential for its function. Together, these data may facilitate future approaches to therapeutically inhibit IRS4, which represents a key unexploited target in multiple cancer types.

## RESULTS

## Identification of IRS4 as a predicted high therapeutic index target
To identify cancer targets predicted to be nontoxic when drugged, we first sought to identify a characteristic “FDA approval–like” dependence profile for genes with successful FDA-approved targeted therapies, which often have a good therapeutic index. For this, we used DepMap, a genome-wide CRISPR dependence screen of >1000 cell lines ( , ) where dependence scores of 0 indicate no dependence and those ≤−1.0 indicate gene essentiality (as the median of known essential genes is calibrated to be −1.0). We focused on 26 genes targeted by FDA-approved targeted therapies that substantially inhibit only one target ( ), excluding genes only targeted by multikinase inhibitors [such as MEK1/2 (mitogen-activated protein kinase kinase 1/2) inhibitors], given that DepMap analyzes only single-gene knockout ( ). These 26 genes were systematically identified by analyzing all FDA-approved drugs (n = 2857) filtered by use in cancer treatment (n = 323 drugs; table S1). We then annotated the targets of these 323 cancer-associated drugs and identified those only targeting a single gene product (n = 90 drugs). These 90 drugs collectively inhibit 32 unique gene products, including 26 genes inhibited by targeted therapies and 6 by chemotherapies (table S1). Consistent with prior analysis ( ), the 26 genes with FDA-approved targeted therapies generally had selective dependence, with most cell lines nondependent on the gene (scores ≥−0.3) and a few cell lines highly dependent (≤−1.0). This selective dependence is a predictor of tumor-specific effects without affecting normal tissue function, given that only a subset of cell lineages and/or genetic backgrounds are dependent on the gene. By comparison, the six genes inhibited by chemotherapies were more likely to be “pan-essential,” with dependence in virtually all cell lines (red; ), which may predict high toxicity and poor therapeutic index as noted previously ( ). The median dependence scores for the six chemotherapy-targeted genes were significantly lower than the 26 targeted therapy target genes and other genes ( ).
 To identify other genes that fit an “FDA approval–like” selective dependency profile, we used the Generalized Pareto Distribution (GPD) ξ (“xi”) score to model each gene’s dependency distribution, with higher ξ scores indicating the presence of extremes (i.e., selective dependency; ). The 26 targeted therapy genes had significantly higher dependency ξ scores compared to other genes ( ). [A subset of these 26 genes had low ξ scores (table S2) perhaps due to the absence of cell lines in DepMap with the appropriate dependency biomarker (e.g., RET rearrangements) or their pan-essentiality, such as MTOR, which is only partly inhibited by mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR) complex 1 inhibitors in the clinic ( ).] To identify additional selectively dependent genes, we used a stringent GPD ξ z-score minimum threshold of 2.641, representing the 75th percentile score for the 26 FDA-approved targeted therapy genes, a threshold that excluded all six chemotherapy target genes and was only met by 1.98% of the 17,453 genes included in DepMap (n = 346 selectively dependent genes; tables S2 and S3).
To further identify targets predicted to have a high therapeutic index (pHTI) among these 346 genes, we identified genes for which (i) biallelic germline inactivation via nonsense and/or frameshift mutations is tolerated in the human population and reasonably compatible with human health (allowing for medically rescuable phenotypes such as agammaglobulinemia or thyroid deficiency), or (ii) the expression of the gene is restricted to no more than 1 of 24 essential human tissues using the GTEx ( ) normal tissue expression resource (see Materials and Methods for the list of essential tissues), and complete knockout of the mouse ortholog is tolerated with reasonable health ( and table S3). Human and mouse genetic data were curated using OMIM, artificial intelligence, and manual literature review (Materials and Methods). These criteria may enrich for genes that can be therapeutically targeted with manageable toxicity. This resulted in 25 pHTI genes meeting these criteria, some of which are currently targeted by FDA-approved therapies (BTK, LCK, and CD19; , bottom). We chose to focus on IRS4 given its relevance to multiple aggressive cancer types and its potential druggability, as discussed later.
Male individuals with nonsense or frameshift mutations in IRS4 (which constitutes complete inactivation, given that IRS4 is X chromosome encoded) survive to healthy adulthood with manageable thyroid symptoms ( ). In mice, Irs4 knockout does not compromise viability or overall health, with mild effects on growth and glucose homeostasis only in males and moderate reduction in reproductive capability ( ). Together, these data suggest that therapeutic IRS4 inhibition would induce thyroid symptoms, which could be ameliorated with thyroid hormone replacement, and may moderately affect fertility. Compared to many existing chemotherapies and targeted therapies, these toxicities would be relatively manageable.
Using GTEx human normal tissue RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data ( ), we found that IRS4 was absent from most tissues, except for the pituitary (fig. S1), which is required for thyroid function ( ); this may explain the thyroid phenotype observed in humans with IRS4 germline inactivation ( ). Low expression was also observed in reproductive tissues (fig. S1), including the cervix, ovaries, and uterus, which may explain lower reproductive capability in Irs4-knockout mice ( ).
The overall dependence profile of IRS4 in DepMap is shown in , showing selective dependence similar to the 26 genes targeted by FDA-approved targeted therapies ( ). Most cell lines were not IRS4-dependent, but a subset was highly dependent with scores near or below −1.0. IRS4 was a strong (≤−1.0) or borderline (≤−0.8 and ≥−1.0) dependency in two of three malignant rhabdoid cancer cell lines and two of two NUT midline cancer cell lines [which arise in multiple midline anatomic locations and are characterized by NUTM1 fusions ( )] and one Ewing sarcoma cell line ( ). In addition, borderline dependence was found in one basal-like breast cancer cell line.
To understand the genomic mechanisms of IRS4 dependence, we asked whether IRS4 mRNA expression correlates with IRS4 dependence using RNA-seq data from the Cancer Cell Line Encyclopedia (CCLE) ( ). High IRS4 mRNA correlated with IRS4 dependence (r = 0.61; and table S4). IRS4 expression was essentially “on-off” with 994 of 1002 cell lines lacking expression [≤10 transcripts per million (TPM)]. All five cell lines with IRS4 expression ≥40 TPM (including two rhabdoid cancer cell lines, two NUT midline cancer cell lines, and one Ewing sarcoma cancer cell line) had IRS4 dependence scores <−0.9 and borderline dependence (−0.83) in HCC1187 basal-like breast cancer cells ( ). In addition, the lung cancer cell line DMS114, although not in DepMap, is known to be IRS4-dependent based on small interfering RNA (siRNA) knockdown ( ) and had high IRS4 expression (131 TPM) based on CCLE data. This cell line was originally considered a small cell lung cancer (SCLC) but recently reclassified as a SMARCA4-deficient undifferentiated tumor (SMARCA4-UT) ( ). Although IRS4-adjacent somatic deletions have been reported in a small percentage of lung squamous and various other cancers ( ), models representing this alteration were lacking in DepMap. In breast cancer, where IRS4 has been shown to promote mouse mammary cancer and phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K)–Akt signaling ( ), only one cell line (HCC1187) expressed substantial IRS4, as reported previously ( ).
We confirmed that IRS4 protein is expressed in three NUT midline and three malignant rhabdoid cell lines when IRS4 mRNA is expressed ( ; see also ), consistent with CCLE and available NUT midline RNA-seq data (fig. S2) ( ). To verify IRS4 dependence, we performed IRS4 knockdown using high-titer lentivirus bearing IRS4 single guide RNAs (sgRNAs) and Cas9 at 10 to 20 multiplicity of infection (MOI) [titrated in HOS-6A5 osteosarcoma cells as described previously ( , )], followed by rapid puromycin selection (1 or 2 days), which yielded good knockdown when one IRS4 sgRNA was used and better knockdown when two sgRNAs were combined ( , top; 3 days posttransduction), perhaps due to fewer in-frame resistance events. Knockdown upon sgIRS4 was also observed in three additional models (fig. S3). This also confirmed that the higher (expected size of ~150 kDa) and lower IRS4 protein bands (90 to 100 kDa) detected with this C-terminally binding antibody ( ) are likely specific, given that they both decrease after IRS4 knockdown ( ). (As shown later, the lower bands are likely due to posttranslational processing as evidenced by their presence after ectopic expression of full-length IRS4.) We analyzed IRS4 dependence by transducing cells with these high-titer IRS4 lentiviruses (either as a 1:1 mixture of IRS4 sgRNA #1 and sgRNA #2 or by transducing with each sgRNA separately), Polo-like kinase 1 (PLK1) sgRNA lentivirus as a pan-essential control ( ) ( , bottom), or control sgRNA lentivirus in five IRS4-expressing cell lines and three IRS4-absent cell lines to test for IRS4 sgRNA off-target effects on proliferation ( ). This confirmed that IRS4 loss either decreased viability or caused stasis in all five IRS4-expressing cell lines tested ( ), while IRS4 sgRNAs had minimal effects on viability in the three IRS4-absent cell lines ( ; ~20% proliferation inhibition versus sgCtrl at day 6). For example, HCC2429 sgIRS4 viability was a mean of 113.5% at day 6 (relative to day 0), while TC797 sgIRS4 cells increased to 3209.7% at day 6 (P = 1.1 × 10−5 by a two-sided t test). PLK1 loss induced decreased viability or caused stasis regardless of IRS4 expression, as expected. Together, these data indicate that IRS4-expressing cell lines are IRS4-dependent, suggesting IRS4 as a target in such cancers.

## IRS4 dependence as measured using the dTAG system
Given that CRISPR-based knockout can have off-target effects on proliferation, we also assessed IRS4 dependency using the degradation tag (dTAG) system ( ). To do this, we C-terminally tagged one copy of endogenous IRS4 in TTC1240 rhabdoid cancer cells with FKBP12 F36V (a “dTAG” domain; ) using CRISPR-based knockin and selected a single-cell clone that we confirmed had the desired modification (fig. S4 and table S5; see Materials and Methods). The dTAG system enables rapid protein degradation upon treatment with the heterobifunctional small molecule dTAGV-1 through recruitment of VHL (Von Hippel-Lindau) and subsequent ubiquitination ( , bottom). Because of X chromosome inactivation, we reasoned that tagging a single copy of IRS4 in female TTC1240 cells would be sufficient, provided that the active IRS4 copy was tagged.
 The resulting TTC1240IRS4-dTAG knockin cells had reduced basal IRS4 protein, particularly the full-length form, as compared to parental cells ( ), perhaps due to destabilization of IRS4 by the dTAG domain. Treatment with dTAGV-1 for 15 hours (1 μM) led to further IRS4 protein loss in the knockin cells but not parental cells ( ), indicating successful modification. TTC1240IRS4-dTAG cells also proliferated more slowly than parental cells (approximately one-third the rate; ; day 6 viability was 1019% versus 3102% in TTC1240IRS4-dTAG versus parental; P = 2.1 × 10−6 by a two-sided t test), as expected on the basis of diminished IRS4 expression.
Dose-response analysis of TTC1240 parental and TTC1240IRS4-dTAG cells after 4 days of dTAGV-1 treatment revealed minimal effects on parental cell proliferation up to ~1 μM dTAGV-1 but profound effects on proliferation at all doses in TTC1240IRS4-dTAG, down to 25% or less viability versus control ( ), indicating less off-target effects of dTAGV-1 as compared to CRISPR-based IRS4 ablation [see IRS4-absent cells in ]. Time-course analysis of 0.5 μM dTAGV-1 treatment in TTC1240IRS4-dTAG revealed that IRS4 loss was similar at all time points from 2.5 hours up to 48 hours, with complete loss of the ~100-kDa IRS4 band and loss of most, but not all, full-length IRS4 ( ). We note that with the lower IRS4 levels in the knockin cells ( ), longer exposure times were required to detect IRS4, leading to detection of possible nonspecific bands, as shown in . We also found that 0.5 μM dTAGV-1 led to stasis or a slight decrease in viability after 5 days of treatment in TTC1240IRS4-dTAG (decreasing 20% between days 0 and 5; ) and inhibited proliferation relative to vehicle [612% versus 80% in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) versus dTAGV-1 at day 5; P = 3.9 × 10−7 by a two-sided t test], confirming the IRS4 dependence seen in this model using CRISPR ( ). Together, these data reaffirm robust IRS4 dependence in IRS4-expressing cancer cells using an additional system, which we also use later to explore the mechanisms of IRS4 dependence.

## In vivo IRS4 dependence
To test whether IRS4 is a dependency in vivo, we transduced HCC2429 NUT midline cells, which are IRS4-expressing and IRS4-dependent ( ), with either a control sgRNA (10 MOI; “sgCtrl”) or a 1:1 mixture of IRS4 sgRNA #1 and sgRNA #2 (at 5 MOI each for 10 MOI total; “sgIRS4”). Three days later, we mixed the sgCtrl and sgIRS4 cells at a 1:1 ratio and injected these into NSG (nonobese diabetic/severe combined immunodeficient/interleukin-2 receptor γ −/−) mouse flanks to generate xenografts and test whether the IRS4 sgRNAs would drop out over time [ ; a pilot in vitro version of this experiment demonstrated sgIRS4 dropout in HCC2429 cells (fig. S5)]. We performed the same procedure with IRS4-absent PER-624 NUT midline cells as a control. After 10 or 15 days, we harvested tumors (which grew as indicated in , with n = 5 HCC2429 and n = 7 PER-624 mice developing tumors), isolated DNA, polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplified sgRNA sequences, and quantified each sgRNA by next-generation sequence (NGS), along with NGS of the preinjection cells to determine baseline representation. This revealed that in HCC2429 cells, both IRS4 sgRNAs dropped to a mean of 5 to 7% of their preinjection levels on day 10 and 1 to 3% on day 15 ( and table S6). By contrast, in PER-624 cells, the IRS4 sgRNAs were 68 to 72% of their preinjection representation by day 10 and 78 to 81% on day 15, significantly higher than the levels observed in IRS4-expressing HCC2429 cells ( ; P values at the bottom). This moderate decrease in representation was consistent with mild in vitro effects of sgIRS4 in PER-624 cells ( ), perhaps due to slowed proliferation after multiple DNA breaks. (The off-target effects of sgIRS4 in IRS4-absent cells in vitro and in vivo are summarized in fig. S6, indicating only ~20% proliferation inhibition across IRS4-absent models.) Together, these data indicate that IRS4 is a dependency both in vitro and in vivo.
 

## Mechanisms of IRS4 up-regulation in cancer
Given that IRS4 expression is associated with IRS4 dependence, we performed a pan-cancer analysis of IRS4 mRNA levels using patient tumor RNA-seq available from The Cancer Genomic Atlas (TCGA) (primarily adult cancers) ( ), TARGET ( ), St. Jude Cloud (pediatric cancers) ( ), and various published datasets for Ewing sarcoma ( ) and SCLC ( ). For NUT midline cancer, we relied on a panel of NUT midline cell lines ( ), given the paucity of patient gene expression data for this cancer. We focused on determining the frequency of patient tumors with IRS4 expression ≥40 TPM, given that this was associated with robust IRS4 dependence ( ) and IRS4 DNA alterations ( ; details follow). Such tumors may represent a patient population likely to benefit from therapeutic IRS4 inhibition, with the caveat that comparing gene expression between cell lines and tumors and between studies is subject to technical limitations.
 Among pediatric-enriched cancer types (those that occur with similar or greater frequency in children versus adults), choroid plexus brain tumors had the highest frequency of IRS4 expression (≥40 TPM) at 68%, followed by malignant rhabdoid tumors (37%), NUT midline (31%), osteosarcoma (5%), and atypical teratoid/rhabdoid tumors (ATRTs; 4%; , right). In adult cancers, 8% of uterine leiomyosarcoma (SARC), 2% of lung squamous cancer (LUSC), 1% of stomach cancer (STAD), and 1% of breast cancer (BRCA) expressed IRS4 ( , left). [Patient Ewing sarcomas did not express IRS4 ( ) despite IRS4 expression and dependence in one Ewing cell line ( ), leading us to exclude Ewing sarcoma from further studies of IRS4.] Together, these data indicate that a sizeable population of patients with cancer might benefit from approaches to target IRS4.
To explore the mechanisms of the IRS4 up-regulation found in a subset of cancers, we analyzed available whole-genome sequencing (WGS) data from TCGA, St. Jude Cloud, and other sources in the IRS4-expressing cancers (≥40 TPM) in to test for IRS4 genomic alterations, given that enhancer hijacking deletions adjacent to IRS4 have been reported ( ). Among IRS4-expressing tumors with WGS, we observed IRS4 inter- or intrachromosomal translocations in 6 of 10 breast tumors and 1 tumor each from lung squamous cancer, prostate cancer, and osteosarcoma ( ; fig. S7; and tables S7 and S8). These translocations likely juxtaposed IRS4 next to strong promoters/enhancers near highly expressed genes, such as the breast lineage genes ANKRD30A (n = 3 patients) ( ) and GATA3 (n = 2) ( ) in breast cancer ( ). In IRS4-rearranged samples, the rearrangement partner gene was expressed at the 90th percentile or above, relative to other samples in the same dataset (e.g., TCGA; ), and was usually more highly expressed in the specific cancer type where the rearrangement occurred (fig. S8), consistent with studies finding breast-specific expression of ANKDR30A and GATA3 ( , ). Thus, these translocations likely lead to IRS4 expression because of promoter/enhancer hijacking. We also observed focal IRS4 copy gains (from 0.2 to 7.5 Mb in size) in six IRS4-expressing tumors from five cancers (breast, osteosarcoma, lung squamous, ovarian, and stomach; fig. S9 and table S9) and IRS4-adjacent deletions juxtaposing the strong promoters of adjacent collagen genes to IRS4, as previously reported in lung squamous carcinoma and uterine leiomyosarcoma ( ) ( ). In summary, most adult IRS4-expressing cancers bore IRS4 DNA alterations, with a subset (two breast, one stomach, and one melanoma) lacking them and potentially having epigenetic mechanisms. For SMARCA4-UT tumors, WGS and RNA-seq data are not available from TCGA, but our WGS on the known IRS4-expressing SMARCA4-UT cell line DMS114 revealed a potential interchromosomal enhancer hijacking TCF12-IRS4 rearrangement ( , right; , see *; , bottom right; and table S8). We confirmed enhancer hijacking in DMS114 cells using Hi-C ( and fig. S10), assay for transposase-accessible chromatin using sequencing (ATAC-seq), and previously published H3K27ac data. This revealed strong intronic enhancers within TCF12 near the t(15;X) (TCF12-IRS4) breakpoint, which robustly contacted the IRS4 promoter region; the interaction was strictly bounded by the rearrangement breakpoint, consistent with enhancer hijacking ( ). TCF12 enhancer interactions with the IRS4 promoter were absent in negative control K562 leukemia cells (fig. S10).
By contrast, most IRS4-expressing pediatric cancers lacked IRS4 alterations, including tumors from patients with malignant rhabdoid and choroid plexus carcinoma ( ) and malignant rhabdoid and NUT midline cell lines based on our WGS analysis ( , right). Transcriptional clustering of 65 patient malignant rhabdoid tumors using TARGET RNA-seq ( ) revealed two groups with one expressing IRS4 ( ) despite no difference in driver alteration profile as SMARCB1 deletions/mutations are near-ubiquitous in this cancer type ( ), suggesting epigenetic mechanisms of IRS4 expression. To test for this, we performed ATAC-seq ( ) in IRS4-expressing cell lines that lacked IRS4 DNA alterations on the basis of our WGS (HCC1187 breast cancer, HCC2429 and RPMI2650 NUT midline, and TTC709 and TTC1240 malignant rhabdoid cells). All IRS4-expressing cell lines had a ~650–base pair (bp) open chromatin peak in the IRS4 promoter, while nonexpressing cells lacked this peak. No consistent IRS4-adjacent enhancers were observed. Together, these data suggest that IRS4 expression in some cancers, particularly pediatric-enriched cancers, is due to epigenetic up-regulation.
We also analyzed ATAC-seq data in one GATA3-IRS4–rearranged TCGA breast cancer (TCGA-AO-A0J8-01) and a control non–IRS4-expressing breast cancer (TCGA-AO-A03N-01) using available TCGA data. The GATA3-IRS4–rearranged sample had the same ~650-bp IRS4 promoter peak found in nonrearranged IRS4-expressing rhabdoid and NUT midline cell lines, and open chromatin was also observed near the rearrangement breakpoint on the basis of ATAC-seq ( , bottom, see “GATA-IRS4”). These data suggest that IRS4-rearranged samples use the same IRS4 promoter as nonrearranged samples, and this promoter may be activated through promoters/enhancers on the rearranged chromosome.
Given the enrichment of IRS4 expression in NUT midline and malignant rhabdoid cancers, we sought to understand whether the defining genomic alterations in these cancers (BRD4 rearrangements and SMARCB1 loss, respectively) play a role in IRS4 expression. HCC2429 cells bear a BRD4-NUTM1 rearrangement ( ) and express IRS4, while TTC709 rhabdoid cells lack a BRD4 rearrangement, so they were used as a control to study the effects of BRD4 inhibition in both settings. Treatment with the BRD4 inhibitor JQ1 ( ) for 48 hours decreased IRS4 protein levels in HCC2429 NUT midline cells to a greater extent than TTC709 malignant rhabdoid cells, whereas other transcriptional inhibitors (other than the positive control CDK9 inhibitor enitociclib) had a minimal effect in both cell lines ( ). RNA-seq of both cell lines treated with JQ1 for 48 hours revealed the down-regulation of IRS4 mRNA to 38.6% of vehicle in HCC2429 and 59.1% in TTC709 ( , right, and table S10), indicating that wild-type (WT; nonrearranged) BRD4 plays a role in IRS4 expression given the IRS4 decrease in nonrearranged TTC709 cells. The inability of JQ1 to fully suppress IRS4 mRNA in NUT midline cells (with 38.6% of IRS4 mRNA remaining after treatment), coupled with the absence of IRS4 expression in some NUT midline cell lines ( ), which all bear BRD4 rearrangements, suggests that BRD4-NUTM1 may not be the primary initiating cause of IRS4 expression in NUT midline cancers.
In SMARCB1-mutant TTC1240 and TTC709 malignant rhabdoid cancer cells, stable ectopic restoration of SMARCB1 did not suppress IRS4 protein or mRNA expression by RNA-seq ( and table S11), indicating that SMARCB1 loss alone may not account for IRS4 expression in this cancer. Furthermore, although malignant rhabdoid tumors (which arise primarily in the kidney) and ATRTs (a rhabdoid tumor of the brain) both typically harbor biallelic SMARCB1 inactivation ( ), IRS4 expression was more frequent in malignant rhabdoid tumors than ATRTs (37% versus 4%; ), suggesting that IRS4 expression in malignant rhabdoid tumors may be due to the cell or tissue of origin, rather than SMARCB1 loss. Consistent with this, human embryonic kidney (HEK) 293T embryonic kidney cells, which are reported to express IRS4 ( ), had the same ATAC-seq peak as IRS4-expressing malignant rhabdoid cells (fig. S11), suggesting that this kidney-derived cell line may resemble a cell of origin for IRS4-expressing malignant rhabdoid tumors arising in the kidney. Together, these data indicate that IRS4 expression in rhabdoid and NUT midline cells is not primarily due to the founding somatic alterations in these cancers but due to epigenetic mechanisms.

## Mechanisms of IRS4 dependence in cancer
To identify IRS4 binding partners that might explain the IRS4 dependence we observed ( and ), we performed immunoprecipitation-mass spectrometry (IP-MS) of endogenous IRS4 in HCC2429 (NUT midline) and TTC709 (malignant rhabdoid) cells ( and tables S12 and S13). This revealed PI3K subunits associated with IRS4, including p85 (PIK3R1/PIK3R2) and Nischarin (NISCH), in both cell lines, which are known IRS4 binders in HEK293 cells ( ).
 On the basis of these findings, we tested whether IRS4 activates the PI3K-Akt pathway in these cancers. IRS4 ablation using sgRNA in TTC1240 malignant rhabdoid cells ( ; using 10 MOI, which showed proliferation inhibition just as did 20 MOI as in ; see fig. S12) or IRS4 siRNA knockdown in HCC2429 NUT midline cancer cells ( ) decreased phosphorylation of serine-473 (S473) of Akt, which we confirmed is downstream of PI3K in these cells using the PI3K inhibitor GDC-0941 ( ). As further confirmation, IRS4-targeting sgRNA in HCC2429 cells and dTAG-based IRS4 ablation in TTC1240IRS4-dTAG also decreased Akt phosphorylation (fig. S13). These data are consistent with previous findings that IRS4 knockdown decreases Akt phosphorylation in the IRS4-expressing SMARCA4-UT cell line DMS114 (in which we identified a TCF12-IRS4 rearrangement by WGS; ) ( ) and the breast cancer cell line HCC1187 ( ) (in which we found no IRS4 alteration by WGS). Rescue of PI3K-Akt activity using constitutively active Akt (Myr-Akt) rescued IRS4 dependence in HCC2429 NUT midline cells ( ), indicating that PI3K-Akt activation is key to IRS4 dependence in these cells. Notably, siRNA knockdown of Nischarin, the other consistent IRS4-binding protein identified in our MS analysis ( ; NISCH), did not inhibit Akt phosphorylation (fig. S14), consistent with PI3K being the main driver of IRS4-induced Akt phosphorylation.
Given that IRS4 activates PI3K-Akt across multiple cancer types and is responsible for cancer dependence, we hypothesized that it may be mutually exclusive with PI3K-activating mutations. In TCGA breast cancers, we found that none of the 11 IRS4-expressing cancers bore PI3K-activating alterations, including in ERBB2, PIK3CA, PTEN, AKT1, and PIK3R1, compared to 58% of non–IRS4-expressing breast cancers ( ; P = 8.2 × 10−5 by a two-sided Fisher’s exact test), consistent with IRS4 promoting PI3K activation in patient tumors.

## IRS4 domains required for function
We next sought to understand how IRS4 might be targeted therapeutically. We initially tested whether the reported IRS1/IRS2–degrading compound NT219 ( , ), which is currently in phase 1/2 clinical trials, may also inhibit IRS4 protein levels. NT219 inhibited both IRS4 and IRS2 protein levels in relevant cell lines, decreasing levels by 55 and 42%, respectively, after 24 hours (fig. S15, left). However, NT219 dose-response curves were similar in IRS4-expressing and nonexpressing NUT midline cell lines (fig. S15, right), suggesting that NT219’s multiple targets [which also include STAT3 (signal transducer and activator of transcription 3) ( , )] make it too nonspecific to be considered as an IRS4-targeting modality.
Therefore, we sought to understand which domains of IRS4 are required for function, given that this may suggest small molecule–based or other approaches to target its function therapeutically. IRS family proteins (consisting of IRS1, IRS2, and IRS4 in humans) have two N-terminal structured domains: the pleckstrin homology (PH) domain, which may bind to the phospholipid membrane, and the phosphotyrosine-binding (PTB) domain, which binds to phosphorylated receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) such as IGF-1R (insulin-like growth factor 1 receptor) after growth factor binding ( ) ( , ). Based primarily on studies of IRS1 and IRS2, these two domains are thought to recruit IRS proteins to the cell membrane upon RTK phosphorylation, followed by phosphorylation of the unstructured IRS C-terminal tail, which then recruit activated effectors such as PI3K and Grb2 ( , ). While IRS4’s structure has not been experimentally determined, the IRS1 structured regions (PH and PTB domains) have been crystallized ( ), and our analysis using ICM PocketFinder ( ) predicted a small-molecule binding pocket between the PH and PTB domains of IRS1 ( , bottom, and fig. S16). Similarly, a pocket was identified in the AlphaFold ( )–predicted IRS4 structure between these two domains (fig. S16), consistent with IRS4 having >50% sequence identity in this region ( ). Therefore, we sought to understand whether the PH-PTB region in particular is essential for IRS4 function to fuel future efforts to therapeutically target IRS4.
 Ectopic IRS4 expression has been reported to induce resistance to the HER2 kinase inhibitor lapatinib in HER2+ breast cancer cells ( ), likely due to IRS4 maintaining PI3K activation independent of HER2, which also activates PI3K ( ). Therefore, we stably transduced BT474 and SKBR3 HER2+ breast cancer cells (which lack endogenous IRS4) with WT IRS4 or various ectopically expressed IRS4 domain variants each having a C-terminal FLAG tag ( ), followed by lapatinib dose-response assays. [Notably, we found that the loss of the Tail-1 region prevented the lower IRS4 band from developing ( ); further subdeletions of the Tail-1 region revealed the proteolytic cleavage site to be in the middle of the Tail-1 region (fig. S17).] As expected ( ), WT IRS4 caused lapatinib resistance in both cell lines ( and fig. S18). Unexpectedly, IRS4 domain variants lacking the PH and/or PTB domains [thought to be required for RTK binding and membrane localization in IRS proteins ( ); ] induced lapatinib resistance at levels similar to WT (e.g., ΔPH/PTB in , which encodes only the tail region). By contrast, a construct lacking the middle one-third of the tail (ΔTail-2) did not induce lapatinib resistance, indicating that this region is important for IRS4 function. Furthermore, ectopic WT IRS4 was able to sustain PI3K signaling in BT474 cells during lapatinib treatment as evidenced by sustained Akt S473 phosphorylation ( ), whereas control [parental or green fluorescent protein (GFP)–transduced] cells lacked detectable p-Akt S473 after lapatinib treatment. In addition, all tested IRS4 domain variants that promoted lapatinib resistance (ΔPH/PTB, ΔTail-1, and ΔTail-3) sustained p-Akt S473 during lapatinib treatment ( ), while the nonfunctional ΔTail-2 domain variant did not, suggesting that ΔTail-2’s lack of functionality ( ) was due to the inability to activate PI3K.
The lapatinib resistance system above does not address which IRS4 domains are required for IRS4 dependence in IRS4-expressing cancers. To address this, we first stably expressed IRS4 WT, ΔPH, ΔPH/PTB, or ΔTail-2 in endogenously IRS4-expressing HCC2429 (NUT midline) cells, thus creating a system with both endogenous (WT) and exogenous (domain variant) IRS4. We then performed siRNA knockdown using two siRNAs, both of which knock down endogenous WT IRS4 but cannot knock down either exogenous ΔPH and ΔPH/PTB IRS4 (siRNA #1) or ΔTail-2 IRS4 (siRNA #2; , top), enabling us to test whether ectopic IRS4 domain variants can sustain proliferation in the absence of endogenous IRS4. This revealed that the ΔTail-2 variant cannot sustain proliferation in the absence of endogenous IRS4, as evidenced by low viability in cells transfected with siRNA #2, which knocks down endogenous IRS4 but not ΔTail-2 [ ; * indicates endogenous IRS4 in Western blot at the bottom; P = 0.49 by a two-sided t test for siRNA #2 in ΔTail-2 (11.2% viability) versus WT (11.8%), indicating the lack of rescue], consistent with the lapatinib resistance analysis ( ). Furthermore, ΔPH and ΔPH/PTB rescued proliferation to 47.5 to 57.4% of control when endogenous IRS4 was knocked down with siRNA #1, indicating that these variants retain at least partial function [ ; P = 7.7 × 10−11 for siRNA #1 in ΔPH (57.4% viability) versus WT (13.0%); P = 4.0 × 10−6 for siRNA #1 in ΔPH/PTB (47.5%) versus WT (13.0%); both by a two-sided t test], again consistent with the lapatinib resistance readout. To further probe which domains are required for IRS4 dependence, we transduced TTC1240IRS4-dTAG knockin cells with various IRS4 domain variants (fig. S19) to test which could rescue viability loss after dTAGV-1–based IRS4 ablation ( ). Consistent with the HCC2429 siRNA results, ΔPH and ΔPH/PTB partially rescued viability, although not to the level of WT IRS4, whereas ΔTail-2 was completely nonfunctional.
On the basis of the absence of functionality in the ΔTail-2 IRS4 variant, we assessed whether this variant lacked PI3K binding using IP of IRS4 followed by Western blotting of the PI3K subunit p85 in BT474 cells ectopically expressing IRS4 WT or ΔTail-2. This revealed that IRS4 WT, but not ΔTail-2, bound to p85, indicating that the middle tail region of IRS4 may recruit and activate PI3K. This Tail-2 region contains six of the seven YXXM motifs found across the entire IRS4 tail, which have been found to recruit and activate PI3K after tyrosine phosphorylation by kinases such as FER ( ). However, there appeared to be redundancy in their function as deletion of any one-quarter of the Tail-2 region (removing one to three YXXM motifs) maintained at least some IRS4 function, as evidenced by induction of lapatinib resistance in HER2+ breast cancer cells (fig. S20). Together, these data suggest that IRS4 promotes cancer viability by activating PI3K, primarily through the middle region of its C-terminal tail. We further found that IRS4 ΔPH/PTB can bind to PI3K (fig. S21), consistent with its ability to promote Akt phosphorylation ( ).
Given that the PH and PTB domains are not required for full IRS4 function ( ), we asked whether IRS4 lacking these domains could still localize to the cell membrane, where PI3K is activated. We ectopically expressed IRS4 WT or ΔPH/PTB with a C-terminal GFP tag in BT474 ( ; fused protein expression was confirmed by the Western blot in fig. S22) and SKBR3 breast cancer cells (fig. S23A), which revealed membrane localization of IRS4 WT-GFP. ΔPH/PTB-GFP had membrane localization in at least some cells ( ), although not all (fig. S23B), consistent with its ability to activate Akt ( ), while also having nuclear localization of unclear significance, in contrast to WT. IRS4 membrane localization was unlikely due to transmembrane insertion, as IRS4 lacks a predicted transmembrane insertion region (fig. S24A). Furthermore, IRS4 membrane localization was also unlikely due to protein lipidation (such as prenylation and myristoylation), which occurs at protein N and C termini ( ), given that an ectopic IRS4 domain variant lacking the N and C termini (“Tail-1-2” given that it includes only the Tail-1 and Tail-2 segments or the first two-thirds of the tail) was functional, as evidenced by lapatinib resistance (fig. S24B) and membrane localization (fig. S24C). Thus, IRS4 membrane localization is likely due to nonintegral association with membrane lipids or proteins rather than insertion or lipidation.
Together, these data indicate that the PH and PTB domains are not fully necessary for IRS4 membrane localization, downstream pathway activation, and dependence and suggest that small molecules binding to the IRS PH-PTB pocket shown in would have to be linked to a degradation-based moiety (i.e., proteolysis targeting chimera–based degradation) to have a robust therapeutic effect.

## DISCUSSION
This study provides information that may motivate and inform the development of future IRS4-targeting therapeutics, including (i) the identification IRS4 in vivo dependency in an IRS4-expressing NUT midline xenograft model; (ii) the identification of the IRS4 domains required for dependency; (iii) the identification of a predicted small-molecule binding pocket; (iv) identification of interchromosomal IRS4 rearrangements in solid cancers, including recurrent GATA3-IRS4 and ANKRD30A-IRS4 in breast cancer; (v) the identification of epigenetic IRS4 up-regulation in rhabdoid and NUT midline cancers, among others, and associated dependency; (vi) systematic identification of patient populations likely to identify from future IRS4 inhibition, through the identification of an expression threshold associated with dependency, and pan-cancer genomic analysis of cancer types meeting this threshold; and (vii) the identification of an IRS4 enhancer hijacking cancer model (DMS114 cells). This work builds on important studies that identified IRS4-adjacent enhancer hijacking deletions in lung squamous and uterine leiomyosarcoma ( ), rare IRS4 interchromosomal rearrangements in leukemia ( – ), and IRS4-induced PI3K activation in the SMARCA4-UT cell line DMS114 ( ) and in breast cancer ( ).
The latter study by Ikink et al. ( ) used two breast cancer cell lines with IRS4 mRNA expression below the 40 TPM threshold and borderline or absent dependency (HCC1187 and MDA-MB-453; table S4). We have found one of these cell lines (HCC1187) to lack an IRS4 alteration by WGS, while the other (MDA-MB-453) has low IRS4 expression (7.01 TPM) and minimal IRS4 dependency per DepMap (−0.220 dependency score). Ikink and Hilkens ( ) also noted that the mechanism of IRS4 up-regulation in human breast cancer remained to be elucidated. As we have shown, IRS4 rearrangements in breast cancer are associated with IRS4 expression up to 10-fold higher than the two known IRS4-expressing breast cancer cell lines (up to 820 TPM; and tables S4 and S7). This highlights the importance of identifying additional IRS4-rearranged models in the future, particularly for breast cancer where the most abundant IRS4-expressing patient population may be found (with 1% of breast cancers having IRS4 rearrangements, this represents ~3000 new breast cancers per year in the US alone). The mining of patient-derived xenograft and organoid banks for IRS4-rearranged models may be fruitful in this regard.
Two prior studies reportedly analyzed IRS4 dependency in vivo using A549 lung cancer xenografts ( ) or OVCAR-5 ovarian cancer xenografts ( ). However, our Western blot analysis of these cell lines coupled with CCLE RNA-seq data (fig. S25), along with another study using A549 as an IRS4-absent negative control ( ), indicates no IRS4 protein or mRNA expression in these models. Neither cell line expresses any degree of IRS4 (0.0 TPM by RNA-seq) nor demonstrates IRS4 dependency (table S4) based on CCLE and DepMap data. We identified 15 total studies ( , , – ) [including the two in vivo studies cited above ( , )] reporting IRS4 expression in 18 cell lines of various cancer types, yet all 18 of these cell lines lack IRS4 expression based on CCLE RNA-seq, our own Western blot data, and/or other public RNA-seq datasets (fig. S25). Many of these cell lines are from IRS4-nonexpressing cancer types, such as colorectal cancer (HCT116, HT29, and RKO) and liver cancer (HepG2, HUH6, and HUH7); these cancers rarely or never express substantial IRS4 based on TCGA RNA-seq data ( and table S7). While the reasons for this discrepancy are unclear, it may be particularly important to authenticate cell lines and antibodies when studying this rarely expressed gene. [We note that all cell lines used in the present study were authenticated with PowerPlex short tandem repeat profiling, and we have validated the IRS4 antibody used via siRNA and sgRNA knockdown ( and , and fig. S13) and correlation with RNA-seq data (fig. S2).] Furthermore, we were unable to identify substantial IRS4 expression in any of 468 colorectal cancers nor in 178 pancreatic cancers using TCGA RNA-seq ( , “COAD” and “PAAD,” respectively, and table S7) despite reports by one group suggesting IRS4 as a prognostic marker in these cancers on the basis of immunohistochemical or other protein-based measurements ( – ).
We have identified IRS4 epigenetic up-regulation in several pediatric-enriched tumor types, including rhabdoid, NUT midline, and choroid plexus carcinomas, where IRS4 somatic rearrangements are absent and IRS4 expression is found in a higher percentage of tumors. We speculate that IRS4 up-regulation in these cancers is due to the cell of origin based on the higher frequency of expression in these cancers (>30%) compared to most IRS4-rearranged adult cancers (2% or less), the absence of IRS4 DNA alterations, and the expression of IRS4 in embryonic kidney-derived HEK293T cells, which have the same IRS4 ATAC-seq peak as kidney-derived malignant rhabdoid cells.
The identification of a predicted small-molecule binding pocket in IRS4 suggests the value of medicinal chemistry campaigns to identify IRS4-inhibitory drugs. However, this pocket appears to be dispensable for IRS4 function, suggesting that such a compound would need to be coupled to a degradation-inducing moiety to create a functional drug (to generate a proteolysis targeting chimera, for example). Other approaches such as stapled peptides, which have showed initial promise for the IRS4 paralog IRS1 ( ), may also be worth pursuing given IRS4’s function as an adapter protein. Together, these data highlight IRS4 as a key cancer target with a straightforward biomarker (IRS4 expression) and support the concept of developing IRS4-targeted therapeutics that may benefit a sizeable population of patients with lethal cancers spanning multiple tumor types.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

## pHTI target identification
To identify all FDA-approved cancer drugs, we first downloaded the “Drugs@FDA” zip file from fda.gov, which includes a list of all FDA-approved therapies regardless of indication in the “Products.txt” file. To determine which drugs in this file were anticancer agents, we annotated each drug with World Health Organization ATC (Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical) codes, which we purchased from the ATC classification index from https://orders.atcddd.fhi.no/home. We considered drugs with codes L01, L02, L03, and L04 as possible anticancer agents (annotated as Antineoplastic and Immunomodulating Agents), identifying 323 FDA-approved drugs with these codes (those in table S1). We then used ChatGPT to annotate the drug targets and drug class of these agents (table S1) and further manually reviewed this information to identify drugs inhibiting one gene product, resulting in the 26 targeted therapy genes and 6 chemotherapy genes in .
We then analyzed the DepMap dependency scores (Chronos) of these and other genes using 22Q4 DepMap data from depmap.org. For each gene, we calculated the GPD ξ value as a measure of selective dependency by first multiplying dependency scores by −1.0 and adding 1.0, yielding mostly positive scores. This is because the GPD models positive outliers. Each gene’s transformed dependency scores were then analyzed via the genpareto.fit function within Python’s scipy library, and the genes with the top 1.98% of (GPD) ξ scores were further analyzed for essentiality in the human germline and mouse models.
To identify genes nonessential for human and mouse health when both copies are inactivated, we used the OMIM database, manual literature searches, and ChatGPT version 4o Deep Research using the prompt “Please make a table indicating whether homozygous inactivation (such as frameshift or nonsense mutations in all copies) of the following genes is tolerated in humans, or whether knockout is tolerated in mice” and requested information about genes in batches of ~10 to 20 genes; the gene summaries in table S3 are partially derived from the information obtained from ChatGPT. ClaudeAI Opus 4.6 was also used for this purpose for a subset of genes. Manageable and reversible health issues such as antibody deficiency (agammaglobulinemia), thyroid deficiency, dental syndromes, and pigmentation issues were considered allowable health issues for pHTI genes, whereas neurodevelopmental, severe metabolic, debilitating, and lethal health issues led to exclusion as a pHTI gene (see table S3).
To identify genes absent in essential tissues, we used the “GTEx_Analysis_2017-06-05_v8_RNASeQCv1.1.9_gene_median_tpm.gct” file downloaded from the GTEx website, which indicates median expression in TPM of each gene across normal tissues. Essential tissues were defined as the artery, brain, heart, kidney, liver, muscle, nerve, and pancreas, including the following 24 GTEx-defined tissues that belong to these broader tissue categories: “Artery - Aorta,” “Artery - Coronary,” “Artery - Tibial,” “Brain - Amygdala,” “Brain - Anterior cingulate cortex (BA24),” “Brain - Caudate (basal ganglia)”, “Brain - Cerebellar Hemisphere,” “Brain - Cerebellum,” “Brain - Cortex,” “Brain - Frontal Cortex (BA9),” “Brain - Hippocampus,” “Brain - Hypothalamus,” “Brain - Nucleus accumbens (basal ganglia),” “Brain - Putamen (basal ganglia),” “Brain - Spinal cord (cervical c-1),” “Brain - Substantia nigra,” “Heart - Atrial Appendage,” “Heart - Left Ventricle,” “Kidney - Cortex,” “Kidney - Medulla,” “Liver,” “Muscle - Skeletal,” “Nerve - Tibial,” and “Pancreas.” Genes that were expressed at ≥5 TPM in more than of one these essential tissues were excluded from being considered a pHTI gene.

## Western blots
Cells were harvested, and whole-cell extracts were prepared using lysis buffer [50 mM tris-HCl, pH 8, 150 mM NaCl, 5 mM EDTA, 0.1% SDS, and 0.1% Triton X-100, with 1× Halt Protease and Phosphatase Inhibitor Cocktail, EDTA-free (100×) (no. 78441)] and centrifuged at ~13,000g and 4°C for 15 min. Protein extracts were estimated using bicinchoninic acid protein quantification and denatured using Laemmli 6×SDS sample buffer. Fifteen to thirty micrograms of protein from cell lysates was resolved onto a gradient (4 to 15%) SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis and then transferred using the Invitrogen iBlot 3 system using polyvinylidene difluoride membranes.
The membranes were blocked with 5% nonfat milk in Tris-buffered saline with Tween 20 at room temperature for 30 to 60 min and incubated with primary antibodies recognizing IRS4 (Bethyl, A305-344A), phospho-Akt Serine 473 (Cell Signaling, no. 4060), Akt (Cell Signaling, no. 4691), β-Actin (Cell Signaling, no. 4970), PIK3R1 (p85) (4292S), SMARCB1 (Cell Signaling, no. 91735), or FLAG tag (Abcam, EPR20018-251), followed by a horseradish peroxidase–conjugated secondary antibody, with three tris-buffered saline and Tween 20 washes of 5 to 10 min after blocking, primary, and secondary antibody incubations. The signal was then detected using Pierce Chemiluminescent reagents using a LI-COR Odyssey Fc system and LI-COR Biosciences Image Studio software version 5.5.
For Western blot analysis after IRS4 knockdown via siRNA, transfections were performed using PepMute siRNA Reagent (no. SL100566, 504844) and siRNA oligonucleotides targeting IRS4 [SASI_Hs01_00140696 (siIRS4 #1) and SASI_Hs01_00140697 (siIRS4 #2)] or control siRNA MISSION(R) siRNA Universal Negative Control 1 (SIC001) on days 0 and 2, with protein harvested on day 3. For Western blot analysis after Nischarin siRNA knockdown, cells were transfected either with siRNA targeting Nischarin (NISCH), including siNISCH #1 or #2 (s22092 or s22093; Thermo Fisher Scientific), or Silencer Negative Control No. 1 siRNA (no. AM4611; Thermo Fisher Scientific) using PepMute siRNA Reagent on days 0 and 2. After the end of incubation, cells were harvested at day 3, and protein was harvested for Western blotting.

## IRS4 domain analysis
Cells were transduced with GFP control (pLenti-C-Myc-DDK-P2A-Puro-mGFP, catalog ps100123) or IRS4 WT [pLenti-C-Myc-DDK-P2A-Puro-IRS4 (NM_003604), catalog RC218385L3] from Origene or IRS4 with the following regions deleted: amino acids 374 to 668 (ΔTail-1), amino acids 669 to 963 (ΔTail-2), amino acids 964 to 1257 (ΔTail-3),amino acids 2 to 227 (ΔPH), amino acids 228 to 373 (ΔPTB), or amino acids 2 to 373 (ΔPH/PTB). After these variants were generated, an IRS4 H879D point mutation (in WT coordinates) was detected in the Tail-2 region of all applicable domain variants and in the original IRS4 WT plasmid; thus, IRS4 WT in and all applicable domain variants of it have this mutation. The H879D mutation did not apparently affect function, given that after correcting this mutation to true WT, lapatinib resistance was unaffected (fig. S18, “Batch 2,” where WT′ indicates corrected true WT IRS4, as compared to the uncorrected WT in “Batch 1,” which generates similar lapatinib resistance). Cells were selected with puromycin or blasticidin before drug response assays, Western blotting, or other assays as appropriate. The Tail-1-2 variant of IRS4 includes amino acids 374 to 963 of IRS4, in addition to the start codon methionine, thus lacking the PH, PTB, and Tail-3 regions.

## IP and MS analysis
For IP and Co-IP (other than fig. S21), the Pierce Co-IP Kit (no. 26149) was used and performed using the manufacturer’s protocol. Briefly, TTC709 parental, HCC2429 parental, or BT474 cells expressing GFP, WT-IRS4, or ΔTail-2 IRS4 were harvested, and whole-cell extracts were prepared using 10:1 (v/w) IP Lysis/Wash Buffer, 0.025 M tris, 0.15 M NaCl, 0.001 M EDTA, 1% NP-40, and 5% glycerol, pH 7.4, with 1× Halt Protease and Phosphatase Inhibitor Cocktail, EDTA-free (100×) (no. 78441), at 4°C for 10 min and at 8 rpm and centrifuged at ~13,000g and 4°C for 15 min. Protein extracts were estimated using bicinchoninic acid protein quantification. A total of 2.5 mg of protein extracts precleared using Control Agarose Resin slurry was used for each IP reaction that was incubated either with 5 μg of IRS4 antibody (Bethyl, A305-344A) or control rabbit immunoglobulin G (IgG) antibody (Cell Signaling Technology) cross-linked to beads on rotation at 4°C for 16 hours. The bead-bound proteins were then washed three times with IP Lysis/Wash Buffer in the spin columns. Protein complexes were eluted from beads using Elution Buffer provided with the kit at room temperature for 5 min; neutralized using 1 M tris, pH 9.5; and analyzed by either Western blotting, silver staining, or MS analysis.
For MS, samples were run on a short gel as described in a previously published protocol ( ). Proteins in gel bands were reduced using 10 mM dithiothreitol (Sigma-Aldrich) and alkylated by 25 mM iodoacetamide (Sigma-Aldrich). Unreacted iodoacetamide was quenched with 10 mM dithiothreitol. The gel bands were then washed, dried, and rehydrated with a buffer containing trypsin (Promega). Samples were digested overnight and acidified, and the peptides were extracted with 100% acetonitrile. The extracts were dried and reconstituted in 5% formic acid. The peptide samples were loaded on a nanoscale capillary reverse phase C18 column [Thermo Fisher Scientific Easy Spray (ES904), 75 inside diameter, 2 μm, C18, 150 mm] by a high-performance liquid chromatography system (Thermo Fisher Scientific Ultimate3000) and eluted by an acetonitrile gradient. The eluted peptides were ionized and detected by an inline mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific Orbitrap Exploris 480).
The MS and tandem MS (MS/MS) spectra were collected over a 75-min liquid chromatography gradient. The mass spectrometer was operated in data-dependent mode with an MS scan in the Orbitrap [60,000 resolution, 1 × 106 automatic gain control (AGC) target, and 50-ms maximal ion time] and MS/MS high-resolution scans [15,000 resolution, 1 × 105 AGC target, 150-ms maximal ion time, 32 higher-energy collisional dissociation normalized collision energy, 1.5 m/z (mass/charge ratio) isolation window, and 8-s dynamic exclusion].
Database searches were performed using the JUMP ( ) search engine against a composite target/decoy UniProt human protein database ( ). Searches were performed using a 25–ppm (parts per million) mass tolerance for precursor ions and 25-ppm mass tolerance for fragment ions, fully tryptic restriction with two maximal missed cleavages. Carbamidomethylation of cysteine (+57.02146 Da) was used for static modification, and Met oxidation (+15.99492 Da) was considered as a dynamic modification. All matched MS/MS spectra were filtered by mass accuracy and matching scores to reduce the protein false discovery rate to <1%. The spectral counts were compared between anti-IRS4 immunoprecipitates and IgG control immunoprecipitates, for each protein, to calculate the P values in and tables S12 and S13, which were calculated using a G test ( ).
For the IP-Western blot in fig. S21, protein was isolated as above, and lysate was incubated overnight at 4°C with an IRS4 antibody (Bethyl, A305-344A). The next day, immunocomplexes were isolated using protein A/G agarose beads, and beads were then washed and boiled at 95°C for 5 min to release immunoprecipitated protein in Western blot loading buffer, followed by standard Western blot procedures. The same IRS4 antibody was used for Western blotting or anti-p85β (MAB6777).

## IRS4 dependence assays in vitro and in vivo
To study in vitro IRS4 dependence, cells were transduced with 10-MOI (PER-624, TC797, G401, and HCC2429) or 20-MOI [all other cell lines in ] lentivirus with polybrene (5 to 10 μg/ml), selected with puromycin for 1 or 2 days, and plated in 96-well plates with six technical replicates per time point (or three replicates for PER-704) in 100 μl per well of RPMI 1640 with 9% fetal bovine serum and penicillin/streptomycin. Eight to fourteen hours after plating, a day 0 measurement of proliferation was performed using the CellTiter-Glo assay, and all subsequent measurements were normalized to these proliferation values (as 100%) for each condition. CellTiter-Glo measurements were also performed 3 and 6 days after plating, and the medium was refreshed on day 3. For TC797, PER-624, G401, and HCC2429, a combination of two IRS4 or two PLK1 sgRNAs was given, and puromycin was maintained in 96-well plates during the first 3 days of proliferation (for all but G401, which did not tolerate extended puromycin), and 10-MOI lentivirus was used for transduction [titrated in HOS-6A5 osteosarcoma cells, as described previously ( , )]. For other cell lines, IRS4 sgRNAs were administered with sgIRS4 #1 and sgIRS4 #2 separately, 20 MOI was used, and no puromycin was present after plating in 96-well plates. In vivo studies were performed with approval of the St. Jude Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee under approved protocol 3234.
In vivo IRS4 dependence was ascertained by transducing HCC2429 or PER-624 cells with 10-MOI sgCtrl lentivirus or 1:1 sgIRS4 #1 and sgIRS4 #2 (at 5 MOI each) using lentiCRISPR_v2 lentivirus generated as described above, with no puromycin selection. Three days later, sgCtrl and sgIRS4 cells were trypsinized, counted, and mixed at a 1:1 ratio. An aliquot of cells was frozen at −80°C for later genomic DNA isolation and NGS-based sgRNA quantification. For the remaining cells, cells were mixed 1:1 in cold phosphate-buffered saline and Matrigel, and 100 μl of this containing 1.5 million cells was injected into the right flanks of 6- to 8-week-old female NSG mice. Tumors were measured using calipers, and the volume was calculated as [length × (width2)]/2. Tumors were harvested on day 10 or 15, and genomic DNA was isolated from tumors and from preinjection (day 0) cells using a Qiagen QIAamp DNA Mini Kit. sgRNA sequences for each sample were PCR amplified from genomic DNA-integrated lentiCRISPR_v2 using primers GAGGGCCTATTTCCCATGATTCC and AAAGCACCGACTCGGTGCCACT, separated on a DNA gel, and amplicons were gel purified, followed by 151-cycle paired-end sequencing, which was performed using an Illumina NovaSeq with at least 1 million reads per sample. Each read was searched for sgCtrl, sgIRS4 #1, and sgIRS4 #2 sequences, and the percentage of each sgRNA sequence was quantified in each sample. Then, for each sgRNA, these percentages were normalized to the preinjection (day 0) percentages to determine the percent change from day 0. For example, if sgIRS4 #1 was 25% of reads on day 0 and decreased to 20% on day 10, this would represent 80% (20 of 25) of day 0.

## ATAC-seq
ATAC-seq experiments were performed on G401, TTC1240, TTC709, TC797, RPMI2650, HCC2429, HCC1187, and DMS114 cells using the Active Motif ATAC-Seq Kit (no. 53150) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. For each cell type, 100,000 cells were washed with 100 μl of chilled 1× phosphate-buffered saline, followed by lysis using 100 μl of ATAC Lysis Buffer on ice for 5 min, and then spun down at 500g for 10 min at 4°C. Isolated nuclei were then incubated with Tagmentation Master Mix at 37°C for 30 min at 800 rpm, followed by probing of the open chromatin with Tn5 Transposase inserting sequencing adapters. Libraries were generated using purified DNA and multiplexed for sequencing unique I5 and I7 index primers for each sample. Seventy million reads per sample, paired end, with 100 cycles per read, were sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 sequencer.
ATAC-seq data were analyzed by trimming reads with Trimmomatic version 0.36, followed by alignment with Bowtie version 2.4.2. Bigwig files for visualizing coverage were generated using bedtools (version 2.31.0) genomecov followed by conversion to bigwig using bedGraphToBigWig.

## DepMap data
DepMap Chronos scores were downloaded from depmap.org in December 2022 via the CRISPRGeneEffect.csv (22Q4) file. CCLE RNA-seq data were downloaded from depmap.org 2022 (22Q4) using the OmicsExpressionProteinCodingGenesTPMLogp1.csv file, with expression converted to TPM.

## RNA-seq
TCGA RNA-seq data previously processed to gene-level expression (TPM) using Rsubread were downloaded from a prior study ( ) available at Gene Expression Omnibus accession GSE62944, except for TCGA PAAD, SARC, and CESC cohorts, which were not included in that pan-cancer TCGA study; for these cancer types, we downloaded raw TCGA RNA-seq data (dbGaP accession phs000178) and processed them to TPM using Rsubread, making a consistent Rsubread-processed TCGA dataset. For published RNA-seq data from NUT midline cell lines [SRA accession PRJNA339503 ( )], Ewing sarcoma tumors [dbGaP accession phs000768 ( )], SCLC [EGA accession EGAS00001000925 ( )], and pediatric cancer tumors (dbGaP accession phs000218) [from TARGET ( ) and St. Jude Cloud ( )], and for newly generated RNA-seq data, gene expression in TPM was freshly computed from raw data (either fastq or bam files) using Rsubread (using the UCSC hg19 reference) ( ), which performs alignment and summarizing of gene-level expression to TPM. For other datasets, we used previously published expression data already processed to expression (in TPM) as described below.
CCLE raw data are not available for all cell lines; thus, gene-level expression data already computed to TPM for CCLE were downloaded from depmap.org (file “OmicsExpressionProteinCodingGenesTPMLogp1.csv”). GTEx RNA-seq data already processed to gene-level expression (TPM) were downloaded from the GTEx website using the file “bulk-gex_v8_rna-seq_GTEx_Analysis_2017-06-05_v8_RNASeQCv1.1.9_gene_tpm.gct.” We tested whether these GTEx-processed expression data for IRS4 yielded similar results to our RNA-seq pipeline by downloading raw GTEx RNA-seq data for 16 GTEx samples with widely varying IRS4 expression (through dbGaP accession phs000424) and processing using Rsubread to obtain expression in TPM. This confirmed a strong concordance between the GTEx-downloaded IRS4 TPM values in “bulk-gex_v8_rna-seq_GTEx_Analysis_2017-06-05_v8_RNASeQCv1.1.9_gene_tpm.gct” and our Rsubread-based analysis (fig. S26; Pearson r = 0.995, P = 3.6 × 10−15). On the basis of this strong concordance, we elected to use the GTEx-processed TPM expression values rather than reprocessing all >17,000 GTEx samples from raw data.
We performed transcriptional clustering of malignant rhabdoid data ( ) using t-SNE (t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding) as follows. RNA-seq expression counts, determined using Rsubread from raw RNA-seq data downloaded from dbGaP accession phs000218, were transformed using varianceStabilizingTransformation in R. The mean absolute deviation was then calculated for each gene, and the top 1000 mean absolute deviation genes were used for clustering. t-SNE clustering was then performed using the Rtsne function in R.
For RNA-seq of cell lines generated in this study, RNA was isolated after 48 hours of treatment with JQ1 (0.5 μM) or stable lentiviral transduction with SMARCB1 (Origene, RC218809L3) or GFP control. RNA was isolated using a Qiagen RNeasy Mini Kit. RNA-seq was performed using Illumina Total Stranded RNA prep. The library was sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq using 101-cycle paired-end sequencing.

## Structural variant and copy number detection
TCGA bam files previously aligned to GRCh38 were obtained through the GDC Data Portal after obtaining dbGaP approval for accession phs000178. For WGS of cell lines, genomic DNA was isolated using a Qiagen QIAamp DNA Mini Kit, and WGS was performed using 151-cycle paired-end sequencing on an Illumina NovaSeq. Raw reads were aligned to the UCSC human hg38 reference using BWA MEM (version 0.7.12).
Structural variants were identified using Manta version 1.6.0 ( ) or CREST version 1.0 ( ), with the FAT3-IRS4 rearrangement found in data from a structural variant landscape study ( ). Copy number variants were identified using CNVkit version 0.9.10 ( ) in “wgs” mode.

## Generation of dTAG cells
Edited TTC1240IRS4-dTAG knockin cells were made using the CRISPR technology in the Center for Advanced Genome Engineering at St. Jude Children’s Research Hospital (RRID: SCR_027027). Briefly, ~1 × 106 TTC1240 cells were transiently cotransfected with precomplexed ribonucleoproteins comprising 150 pmol of chemically modified sgRNA (IDT), 50 pmol of 3× nuclear localization signal SpCas9 protein (St. Jude Protein Production Facility), and 1 μg of double-stranded DNA donor. Transfection was performed via nucleofection (Lonza, 4D-Nucleofector X-unit) using solution P3 and program DS-150 in a small (20 μl) cuvette according to the manufacturer’s protocol, and cells were plated in prewarmed (37°C) media. Five days postnucleofection, cells were single cell sorted by flow cytometry (St. Jude Flow Cytometry and Cell Sorting Shared Resource) on viability into 96-well tissue culture–treated plates containing prewarmed media. Cells were clonally expanded and screened for the desired modification via PCR-based assays and targeted deep sequencing, as previously described ( ). Correctly edited clones were expanded and sequence confirmed. The final clone cell identity was authenticated using the PowerPlex Fusion System (Promega) performed at the Hartwell Center (St. Jude). All clones tested negative for mycoplasma by the MycoAlertPlus Mycoplasma Detection Kit (Lonza). Genome editing reagents and sequencing primers are listed in table S5.

## Hi-C analysis
Four Hi-C libraries were prepared from DMS114 cells using an in situ Hi-C protocol ( ). Samples were cross-linked followed by chromatin digestion with Dpn II. DNA ends were then biotin labeled, followed by proximity ligation, DNA purification, fragmentation, and enrichment of biotinylated ligation junctions. Libraries were sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq X Plus platform using 150-bp paired-end mode. HiC-Pro (version 3.1.0) ( ) was used to analyze the Hi-C data with default parameters [using GRCh37 to match the publicly available DMS114 H3K27ac data from GSE115124 ( )]. The annotation of restriction fragments after digestion was generated using digest_genome.py for restriction enzyme Dpn II. The allValidPairs output from the four technical replicates of DMS114 cells was merged and converted to hic format using hicpro2juicebox.sh. The hic file was then loaded into Juicebox (2.15 Mac version) ( ) for visualization. K562 Hi-C [GSE63525 ( )] was used as a negative control. H3K27ac chromatin immunoprecipitation sequencing [GSE115124 ( )] and our ATAC-seq of DMS114 cells were aligned in parallel on the side.

## Pocket prediction
The identification of small-molecule binding pockets was performed in ICM-Pro version 3.9-3a using the ICM PocketFinder module ( ). The IRS1 structure used was from accession 1QQG ( ) and was downloaded from the Protein Data Bank ( ) at http://rcsb.org (experimentally determined), and the IRS4 predicted structure was from AlphaFold ( , ) accession AF-O14654-F1-v4, including only the N-terminal PH and PTB regions as the tail was unstructured.

## Live cell imaging
BT474 and SKBR3 cells stably expressing GFP, C-terminal GFP-tagged WT-IRS4, and IRS4 ΔPH-PTB-IRS4 were cultured on a μ-Slide eight-well high-walled chamber for cell imaging (ibidi, no. 80806) in medium supplemented with 9% fetal bovine serum at 37°C in a humidified atmosphere containing 5% CO2 until 75% confluency was achieved. Cells were incubated for 30 min with NucRed Live 647 ReadyProbes Reagent (no. R37106) for nuclear staining in a humidified CO2 incubator at 37°C. Fluorescence microscopy was performed using a Zeiss Apotome microscope at 37°C in a humidified atmosphere containing 5% CO2, and ZEISS ZEN 3.11 software was used for image capturing.
