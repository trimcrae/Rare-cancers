## Abstract
Type 2 diabetes mellitus may result from insulin resistance in skeletal muscle. Prokineticin receptor 1 (Prokr1) improves metabolic phenotype in adipose tissue and the cardiovascular system; however, its effects on skeletal muscle have not been investigated. We investigated the Prokr1 signaling pathways and its metabolic function in murine myoblast, satellite cells, and their differentiated myotubes. We measured the expression levels of Prokr1 in the skeletal muscle of mice as well as human skeletal muscle cell‐derived myotubes. Prokineticin 2 (PROK2), a ligand of PROKR1, induced calcium mobilization in a dose‐dependent manner and altered the mRNA levels of 578 genes in PROKR1‐overexpressed HEK293T cells. Functional enrichment of differentially expressed genes revealed that PROKR1 activated Gq‐mediated PI3K/AKT and MAPK/ERK signaling pathways in skeletal muscle cells. Prokr1 significantly activated the PI3K/AKT signaling pathway in myotubes derived from C2C12 and satellite cells, regardless of the presence or absence of insulin. Prokr1 also promoted the translocation of glucose transporter 4 (GLUT4) into the plasma membrane. In palmitate‐induced insulin‐resistant myotubes, Prokr1 enhanced insulin‐stimulated AKT phosphorylation, GLUT4 translocation, and glucose uptake. mRNA and protein levels of Prokr1 were significantly decreased in skeletal muscle and white adipose tissue of diet‐induced obese mice, and the amount of PROKR1 protein was significantly decreased in human skeletal muscle cell‐derived myotubes under insulin resistance conditions. Taken together, these results demonstrate that Prokr1 plays an important role in insulin sensitivity and is a potential therapeutic target to ameliorate insulin resistance in skeletal muscle.

3.2

## Transcriptome analyses of PROKR1‐overexpressing HEK293T cells
To investigate the molecular mechanism of PROKR1 signaling, we generated PROKR1‐overexpressing HEK293T cells using a PiggyBac transposon system. The transgenic cells exhibited increased expression of PROKR1 mRNA and protein, which were not detected in naïve and mock cell controls (Figure  ). We conducted a calcium mobilization assay and cAMP assay based on treatment with PROK2 to investigate the PROKR1‐coupled G protein. In calcium mobilization assays, the intracellular calcium level significantly increased from 1 nM in transgenic cells compared to naïve cells, and the half‐maximal effective concentration (EC50) of PROK2 on calcium flux was 1.2 nM (Figure  ). However, the EC50 of PROK2 on cAMP accumulation was not determined (Figure  ). These results suggest that PROKR1 couples with Gq to activate the signaling pathway.
 Next, we investigated the transcriptomic response in transgenic cells under PROK2 treatment. We identified a total of 578 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) under PROKR1 activation using a fold change (FC) cutoff of 2. Those included 269 upregulated genes, such as glycoprotein hormones; alpha polypeptide (CGA, FC = 197.6); nuclear receptor subfamily 4, group A, member 2 (NR4A2, FC = 23.3); nuclear receptor subfamily 4, group A, member 3 (NR4A3, FC = 17.4); FBJ murine osteosarcoma viral oncogene homolog B (FOSB, FC = 15.0); and growth arrest and DNA‐damage‐inducible beta (GADD45B, FC = 14.3). In addition, 309 genes were downregulated, such as SLIT and NTRK‐like family, member 5 (SLITRK5, FC = −6.0); protein disulfide isomerase family A member 3 pseudogene 1 (PDIA3P1, FC = −4.7); mitogen‐activated protein kinase kinase 6 (MAP2K6, FC = −4.6); family with sequence similarity 83, member B (FAM83B, FC = −4.2); and pleckstrin homology domain‐containing, family A (phosphoinositide binding‐specific) member 2 (PLEKHA2, FC = −4.1) (Figure  , Table ). KEGG pathway enrichment analysis using 578 DEGs revealed that several signaling pathways were significantly altered by PROKR1 activation, including the MAPK/ERK signaling pathway (q = 2.0E‐11), PI3K/AKT signaling pathway (q = 1.3E‐07), TNF signaling pathway (q = 1.8E‐03), FoxO signaling pathway (q = 4.0E‐03), and Wnt signaling pathway (q = 5.7E‐03) (Figure  , Table ). These findings suggest that the MAPK/ERK and PI3K/AKT signaling pathways are putative target pathways dominantly altered by PROKR1.

3.3

## Alleviation of insulin resistance in myotubes through the Prokr1‐PI3K/AKT signaling pathway
To investigate the effects of Prokr1 signaling on insulin resistance and its underlying mechanism, we examined the Prokr1 signaling pathway by measuring AKT and ERK phosphorylation in insulin‐sensitive (without palmitate) and insulin‐resistant (with palmitate) C2C12 myotubes and satellite myotubes.
As shown in Figure  , the level of AKT phosphorylation significantly increased by 3.9‐fold after insulin treatment in C2C12 myotubes under insulin‐sensitive conditions (P < .0001). In contrast, insulin‐resistant myotubes showed a significant 1.9‐fold decrease of insulin‐induced AKT phosphorylation (P < .05) compared to insulin‐sensitive myotubes. Co‐treatment of PROK2 with insulin significantly enhanced insulin‐induced AKT phosphorylation by 1.9‐fold, especially in insulin‐resistant C2C12 myotubes (P < .05). Furthermore, standalone treatment of PROK2 significantly increased the level of AKT phosphorylation by 1.7‐fold in both insulin‐sensitive and insulin‐resistant myotubes (P < .05). The levels of ERK phosphorylation were not significantly increased by insulin with or without PROK2 in insulin‐sensitive C2C12 myotubes. However, PROK2 alone significantly increased ERK phosphorylation 5.0‐fold (P < .01) (Figure A). Under insulin resistance conditions, there was no significant change of ERK phosphorylation under combinatory treatment of insulin and PROK2. The effect of PROK2 on AKT and ERK phosphorylation in C2C12 myotubes was validated using specific inhibitors of PI3K/AKT and MAPK/ERK signaling molecules (Figure  ). In insulin‐sensitive C2C12 myotubes, U73332 (PLCβ inhibitor) significantly inhibited the PROK2‐induced AKT phosphorylation (P < .001). Wortmannin, a PI3K inhibitor, also significantly decreased the PROK2‐induced AKT phosphorylation (P < .001). However, U0126 (ERK inhibitor) did not exhibit any inhibitory effect on AKT phosphorylation. All of the signaling inhibitors significantly decreased the PROK2‐induced ERK phosphorylation. U7332 inhibited the PROK2‐induced ERK phosphorylation 3.7‐fold (P < .01), Wortmannin 3.1‐fold (P < .05), and U0126 4.4‐fold (P < .01) (Figure B).
 Similar results were observed in satellite‐derived myotubes (Figure  ). The level of AKT phosphorylation significantly increased by 3.0‐fold after acute insulin stimulation in insulin‐sensitive satellite myotubes (P < .05). In contrast, insulin‐resistant myotubes exhibited a substantial 1.5‐fold decrease in insulin‐induced AKT phosphorylation (P < .05) compared to insulin‐sensitive myotubes. Co‐treatment with PROK2 and insulin significantly increased insulin‐induced AKT phosphorylation by 1.4‐fold, mainly in insulin‐resistant myotubes (P < .05). Furthermore, standalone treatment with PROK2 significantly increased the level of AKT phosphorylation approximately by 1.9‐fold in both insulin‐sensitive and insulin‐resistant myotubes (P < .05). Although insulin with or without PROK2 did not significantly increase the degree of ERK phosphorylation in insulin‐sensitive myotubes, PROK2 alone significantly increased ERK phosphorylation by 5.0‐fold (P < .001) (Figure C). Co‐treatment with insulin and PROK2 did not increase ERK phosphorylation under insulin resistance conditions. The effects of PROK2 on AKT and ERK phosphorylation in satellite‐derived myotubes were confirmed using signaling inhibitors (Figure  ). In insulin‐sensitive myotubes, U73332 significantly inhibited the PROK2‐induced AKT phosphorylation (P < .001). Wortmannin significantly decreased the PROK2‐induced AKT phosphorylation (P < .001). However, U0126 had no inhibitory effect on AKT phosphorylation. All signaling inhibitors significantly decreased the PROK2‐induced ERK phosphorylation. U7332 inhibited the PROK2‐induced ERK phosphorylation by 1.6‐fold (P < .05), Wortmannin by 5.2‐fold (P < .01), and U0126 by 3.3‐fold (P < .01) (Figure D).
These findings suggest that PROK2 improves insulin resistance in myotubes via the PLCβ/PI3K/AKT pathway. However, the effects of the MAPK/ERK pathway under insulin resistance conditions appear to be negligible in C2C12 and satellite myotubes.

3.4

## Prokr1‐mediated GLUT4 translocation in insulin‐resistant myotubes
Insulin stimulation causes the translocation of GLUT4 to the cell membrane, thereby facilitating glucose uptake in skeletal muscle. , To understand whether PI3K/AKT signaling activated by Prokr1 increases GLUT4 translocation, we measured GLUT4 protein levels in the plasma membrane of myotubes derived from C2C12 and satellite cells.
In insulin‐sensitive satellite myotubes, GLUT4 protein was concentrated in the cytosol around the nucleus and insulin induced GLUT4 translocation to the cell membrane. In insulin‐resistant satellite myotubes, insulin had little effect on GLUT4 translocation to the membrane. However, co‐treatment with PROK2 and insulin markedly enhanced GLUT4 translocation. Standalone treatment with PROK2 also induced GLUT4 translocation to a similar degree compared with insulin treatment (Figure  ). PROK2‐induced GLUT4 translocation was attenuated in the presence of PI3K/AKT signaling inhibitors, including U73343 and Wortmannin. However, U0126 did not inhibit PROK2‐induced GLUT4 translocation (Figure  ). For better quantification of the translocated GLUT4 by stimulation, we used an ELISA‐based method to determine the amount of GLUT4 in the plasma membrane of satellite cells‐derived myotubes (Figure  ). The quantity of translocated GLUT4 proteins increased significantly by 4.2‐fold under insulin‐sensitive conditions after acute insulin stimulation (P < .001). Co‐treatment with PROK2 and insulin also significantly increased GLUT4 translocation, but there was marginal synergic effect of PROK2 on GLUT4 translocation. PROK2 alone significantly increased GLUT4 translocation by 1.7‐fold (P < .01); however, the level of translocation was 2.4‐fold lower than insulin treatment. In contrast, insulin‐resistant myotubes exhibited a 2.1‐fold reduction in translocated GLUT4 protein levels under insulin stimulation compared with insulin‐sensitive myotubes (P < .05). Co‐treatment with PROK2 and insulin significantly enhanced GLUT4 translocation by 2.3‐fold compared with insulin alone (P < .05). In comparison, PROK2 alone significantly improved the translocation of GLUT4 (P < .05) to the same degree as co‐treatment of PROK2 and insulin. The effects of PROK2 on GLUT4 translocation have been eliminated by PI3K/AKT signaling inhibitors. As seen in Figure  , U73343 and Wortmannin strongly inhibited the translocation of GLUT4 by PROK2 (P < .05). U01226 inhibited GLUT4 translocation to a higher degree than PROK2 alone (P < .05). Similar results were observed in C2C12 myotubes by western blot. As shown in Figure  , the level of translocated GLUT4 proteins significantly increased 5.5‐fold after acute insulin stimulation (P < .01) under insulin‐sensitive conditions. Co‐treatment with PROK2 and insulin also significantly increased GLUT4 translocation, but there was no synergic effect of PROK2 on GLUT4 translocation. PROK2 alone significantly increased GLUT4 translocation 2.1‐fold (P < .05); however, the level of translocation was 2.6‐fold lower than that under insulin treatment. In contrast, insulin‐resistant myotubes exhibited a 5.3‐fold reduction in translocated GLUT4 protein levels under insulin stimulation compared with insulin‐sensitive myotubes (P < .01). Co‐treatment with PROK2 and insulin significantly enhanced GLUT4 translocation 2.5‐fold (P < .01). Furthermore, PROK2 alone significantly increased the translocation of GLUT4 (P < .05) to an equivalent degree compared with PROK2 and insulin. The effects of PROK2 on GLUT4 translocation were abolished by PI3K/AKT signaling inhibitors. As shown in Figure  , U73343 and Wortmannin significantly inhibited GLUT4 translocation by PROK2 (P < .05). U01226 inhibited GLUT4 translocation to a greater degree than PROK2 alone (P < .05).
 These findings suggest that Prokr1 enhances the translocation of GLUT4 via the PI3K/AKT signaling pathway, and contributes to alleviating insulin resistance in myotubes.

3.5

## Prokr1‐mediated glucose uptake in myotubes
To evaluate whether increased GLUT4 translocation through Prokr1 activation enhanced glucose uptake, we examined glucose uptake in myotubes derived from C2C12 cells and satellite cells under insulin‐sensitive or insulin‐resistant conditions under different combinations of insulin and PROK2 treatment.
In C2C12 myotubes under insulin‐sensitive conditions, glucose uptake increased significantly by 1.2‐fold at 100 nM of insulin (P < .05) and 1.5‐fold at 400 nM of insulin (P < .001) in a dose‐dependent manner. There was little or no synergic effect on glucose uptake with co‐treatment with PROK2 and insulin. PROK2 standalone treatment significantly improved glucose uptake by 1.2‐fold, but was lower than insulin treatment (Figure  ). In contrast, palmitate‐induced insulin‐resistant myotubes exhibited unchanged levels of glucose uptake at 100 nM of insulin, which accounted for about 80% of insulin‐sensitive C2C12 myotubes. However, glucose uptake significantly increased by 1.3‐fold at 400 nM of insulin (P < .05). Co‐treatment with PROK2 and insulin significantly enhanced glucose uptake by more than 1.3‐fold (P < .01) compared with insulin alone. Furthermore, glucose uptake was significantly increased by 1.2‐fold by PROK2 alone in insulin‐resistant C2C12 myotubes (P < .05) (Figure  ).
 In satellite myotubes under insulin‐sensitive conditions, glucose uptake improved by up to 1.4‐fold by insulin in a dose‐dependent manner (P < .001). Co‐treatment with PROK2 and insulin further significantly enhanced glucose uptake by up to 1.3‐fold (P < .05), but PROK2 alone did not increase glucose uptake to the same degree as co‐treatment with insulin (Figure  ). Under the palmitate‐induced insulin resistance conditions, satellite cell‐derived myotubes did not respond to insulin at 100 nM in terms of glucose uptake, the levels of which were about 80% those of insulin‐sensitive myotubes. However, glucose uptake substantially increased by 1.5‐fold by 400 nM of insulin (P < .05). Co‐treatment with PROK2 and insulin increased glucose uptake significantly by 1.2‐fold (P < .05). Compared to insulin alone, co‐treatment with PROK2 and insulin synergistically increased glucose uptake by 1.5‐fold (P < .05) and was 1.9‐fold higher than untreated control, especially in the co‐treatment with PROK2 and 400 nM of insulin (P < .001). PROK2 alone further enhanced glucose uptake 1.3‐fold (P < .5) (Figure  ).
These findings suggest that Prokr1 signaling increases insulin sensitivity, leading to increased glucose uptake in C2C12 and satellite cell‐derived myotubes.

3.6

## Reduced expression levels of Prokr1 in skeletal muscle cells under insulin resistance conditions
To understand the in vivo relevance of Prokr1 expression under pathogenic conditions, we investigated the dynamics of Prokr1 expression in several tissues from lean and DIO mice. Based on body weights and fasting glucose levels, DIO mice acquired an obese and diabetic phenotype. The mean bodyweight of DIO mice was 65.3 g (n = 6), which was significantly higher than the bodyweight of lean mice (34.1 g, n = 6, P < .001). The fasting glucose level of DIO mice was 144.6 mg/dL, which was significantly higher than that of lean mice (83.0 mg/dL, P < .001) (Table ). Therefore, it was considered that the DIO mice had an abnormal T2DM metabolic phenotype.
Major metabolic organs (eg, liver, adipose tissue, and skeletal muscle) of mice were harvested, and Prokr1 levels were compared between lean and DIO mice. As shown in Figure  , Prokr1 was detected in the white adipose tissue and skeletal muscle in both lean and DIO mice; however, it was not expressed in the liver. Prokr1 levels were 2.8 times lower in white adipose tissue from DIO mice than from lean mice (P < .05). Similarly, Prokr1 levels in the skeletal muscle of DIO mice was 3.9 times lower than that of lean mice (P < .001). These results demonstrated that Prokr1 expression was inhibited in major metabolic organs such as adipose tissue and skeletal muscle under obese and diabetic conditions.
 To assess the clinical relevance of PROKR1, we examined PROKR1 expression levels in myotubes derived from human myoblasts (HSkM) under different metabolic conditions. As we observed previously, PROKR1 levels in HSkM‐derived myotubes were significantly increased 9 days after differentiation (P < .01) under insulin‐sensitive conditions, which was comparable to levels in C2C12 myotubes. HSkM myotubes exhibited a 1.2‐fold reduction in PROK1 levels under palmitate‐induced insulin resistance conditions (P < .01), which was comparable with palmitate‐treated C2C12 myotubes. Therefore, PROKR1 appeared to be downregulated in both mouse and human myotubes under insulin resistance conditions. This finding may be clinically relevant, particularly for skeletal muscular metabolic disorders.

## DISCUSSION
In the present study, we provide in vitro and in vivo evidence for the metabolic roles of Prokr1 in both mouse and human myotubes. Prokr1 was upregulated in differentiated myotubes and coupled with Gq to induce the PI3K/AKT signaling pathway in myotubes, which contributed to the translocation of GLUT4 into the plasma membrane and increased glucose uptake, especially in insulin‐resistant myotubes. Prokr1 was downregulated under insulin resistance conditions in both mouse and human myotubes, demonstrating that it plays an essential role in insulin tolerance and glucose metabolism in skeletal muscle. Skeletal muscles in mammals are composed of oxidative, slow‐twitch myofibers (type I) and glycolytic, fast‐twitch fibers (type II), and the distribution of skeletal muscle is associated with glucose uptake and insulin resistance. Hence, the capacity of myoblasts to differentiate into various types of fibers plays a significant role in systemic metabolism. Here, we used C2C12 and satellite cells for Prokr1 analysis during myogenic differentiation. Although Myh2 expression was upregulated along with Prokr1 in myotubes derived from these cells, it is difficult to determine whether Prokr1 induces the differentiation of glycolytic fibers. The effects of Prokr1 on skeletal muscle fiber type specification remain to be elucidated.
To further investigate PROKR1 signaling in muscle cells, we generated PROKR1‐overexpressing HEK293T cells and found that PROKR1 preferentially coupled with Gq to increase the levels of intracellular calcium. The EC50 of PROK2 was 1.2 nM for PROKR1‐mediated calcium mobilization, which was consistent with previous findings. Subsequent transcriptomic analysis followed by biochemical confirmation using genetically naïve C2C12 and satellite cells demonstrated that Prokr1 activated the PI3K/AKT signaling pathway upon PROK2‐mediated activation in myotubes. In addition, we did not observe any significant effect of PROK2 activation on Gs‐mediated cAMP accumulation, even at the maximum feasible concentration, nor PROKR1‐mediated MAPK/ERK signaling in myotubes. However, these results differ slightly from previous findings on Prokr1 signaling. For example, PROKR1‐expressing cells have been shown to not only stimulate Gq‐mediated calcium mobilization, , , but also affect cAMP accumulation via Gs or Gi. In addition, it has been shown that PROK2 activates the MAPK/ERK signaling pathway via Gβγ. Although these prior studies help to clarify the Prokr1 signaling pathway and its cellular consequences, most studies on PROKR1 signaling have relied upon PROKR1‐overexpressing cells created via transgenesis. In this study, we investigated the Gq‐mediated PI3K/AKT signaling pathway under Prokr1 activation in myotubes in the presence of various pharmacological inhibitors. Consistent with other recent studies, we found that Gq‐coupled Prokr1 promotes the survival and proliferation pathway by activating AKT signaling. , We cannot rule out the possibility of cell context‐specific Prokr1 signaling pathways; however, Gq‐mediated PI3K/AKT signaling would be the naturally occurring Prokr1 signaling pathway in skeletal muscle.
GPCRs are membrane proteins representing a primary class of drug targets. GPCRs can be activated or inactivated in a ligand‐dependent manner; therefore, it is critical not only to use GPCR‐expressing cells, but also to use proper ligands for activation to understand the precise consequences of GPCR signaling. To characterize PROKR1 signaling, we generated PROKR1‐expressing cells. Interestingly, we observed significantly upregulated PI3K/AKT signaling in cells treated with only PROK2. These results support the importance of using appropriate ligands in analyzing GPCR signatures and other receptor signatures. For instance, the Connectivity Map (CMap) data set provides a valuable way to understand the relationships among drugs, genes, and disease phenotypes through transcriptional signatures. , , CMap contains more than 476 000 signatures of small molecular and biological perturbagens and expands its coverage by more than 7400 genetic perturbagens through loss‐of‐function and gain‐of‐function approaches. However, simple genetic perturbations excluding ligands are restricted in obtaining accurate transcriptional response profiles for particular receptors. Therefore, ligands should be implemented to construct improved CMap data sets.
To investigate the clinical relevance of PROKR1, we evaluated PROKR1 expression levels in human myotubes under various physiological conditions. PROKR1 protein levels were significantly increased in myotubes derived from human myoblast cells, but were downregulated under insulin resistance conditions. Consistent with this finding, PROK2 prevents the conversion of human preadipocytes into mature adipocytes, and PROKR1 mRNA levels were significantly reduced in visceral and subcutaneous fat tissue from obese patients. Therefore, it appears that reduced PROKR1 expression is significantly associated with metabolic phenotypes such as insulin resistance and obesity in human. However, to date there has been no clear clinical evidence establishing the relationship between PROKR1 and metabolic disorders, such as comprehensive profiles of PROKR1 expression in human metabolic organs or clinical genomics studies. Instead, several clinical studies on endogenous PROKR1 ligands indicate a relationship between PROKR1 and metabolic diseases. For example, elevated serum levels of PROK2 are closely correlated with elevated body mass index, blood lipid and glucose levels, serum uric acid levels, and metabolic syndrome in patients with cardiovascular risk factors. Furthermore, melanocortin receptor accessory protein 2 (MRAP2), a recently discovered endogenous Prokr1‐specific inhibitor, has differential effects on obesity‐related traits in human subjects based on their genetic context. For example, Glu24X variants of MRAP2 protein were observed in individuals with extreme obesity, and Asn88Tyr or Arg125Cys variants were observed in moderately obese individuals. Meanwhile, it was observed that the Leu115Val variant did not affect body mass index. The correlation between the blood levels of PROK2, genetic variation in MRAP2, and metabolic phenotype is an interesting result that indicates a cause‐effect relationship between Prokr1 and metabolic diseases. However, further studies are needed to elucidate the role of Prokr1 in metabolic disorders.
Based on the combined results of this study and previous studies, the activation of Prokr1 may be a therapeutic strategy for treating metabolic disorders such as obesity and diabetes while reducing cardiovascular complications. Several selective agonists for Prokr1 have been identified, including the peptide agonists Bv8 (a frog homolog of PROK2), PROK2β (a smaller peptide of PROK2), and mamba intestinal toxin 1 (MIT‐1); and the nonpeptide furan derivatives IS1 and IS20. Peptide agonists bind to the orthosteric extracellular binding site of Prokr1. This is similar to the interaction of other endogenous ligands with cognate GPCRs, except that the synthetic agonists bind to the allosteric transmembrane site of the receptor. IS20 prevents cardiac lesions and improves heart function in myocardial infarction mice. In addition, intraperitoneal administration of 0.5 mg/kg IS20 for 40 days showed no signs of toxicity to mice. Because of its low toxicity and efficacy for treating vascular diseases, insulin resistance, and obesity, IS20 is undergoing preclinical development as a cardioprotective and antidiabetic agent (https://integ​rity.clari​vate.com/integ​rity). Drug discovery through new drug synthesis or drug repositioning holds promise for the development of synthetic PROKR1 agonists with enhanced physiochemical properties and pharmacological activity against obesity and diabetes.
In summary, we have demonstrated that Prokr1 plays an essential role in insulin tolerance and glucose metabolism in skeletal muscle cells. This effect is dependent upon Gq‐mediated PI3K/AKT activation and GLUT4 translocation for insulin‐independent glucose uptake. Prokr1 protein levels were significantly decreased in human skeletal muscle cells under insulin resistance conditions. Taken together, these findings indicate that Prokr1 plays an important role in glucose homeostasis in skeletal muscle and is a potential therapeutic target for improving insulin resistance.
