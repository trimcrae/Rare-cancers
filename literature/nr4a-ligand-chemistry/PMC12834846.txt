## Highlights
•Identification of PRKG1 V219I as an activating variant causally mediated by enhanced cGMP binding.
•A conformational clash of the bulky isoleucine revealed by NMR is the molecular basis for the gain of function.
•Mutant hiPSC-derived VSMCs are larger, more deformable, and mechanically compromised due to impaired actin cytoskeleton and ECM signaling.
•Mutant hiPSC-derived CMs exhibit higher passive stiffness and altered mechanoresponsiveness.
•Functional assays reveal that basal kinase activation is an early hallmark of disease, offering a molecular basis for aortic pathology in patients and a potential framework for improved future risk stratification.

## Summary
Aortic dissection can strike without warning. Whereas the condition is typically linked to aging and chronic hypertension, rare genetic variants emerge as silent culprits. One variant, V219I in PRKG1, has been found in patients with aortic aneurysms despite near-normal aortic diameters. Vascular smooth muscle cells expressing the V219I variant were larger, more deformable, and showed aberrant actin cytoskeleton dynamics. They exhibited altered extracellular matrix signaling and weakened structural integrity, highlighting a shift toward increased tissue elasticity as the causal molecular pathomechanism. These findings offer a mechanistic model for how PRKG1 variants predispose to aortic dissection.

Thoracic aortic disease (TAD) is a major cause of death in Western industrialized nations, responsible for 30,000-50,000 deaths annually in Western Europe. , TAD is complicated by aortic dissection, which shows a 30-day mortality rate of 18% despite optimal medical and surgical treatment. , , 
Identifying high-risk patients by monitoring the progression of aneurysm size through regular imaging and prophylactic surgery can prevent many of these deaths. Currently, risk prediction is primarily based on aortic diameter, but this alone is not always sufficient. For example, 60% of type A dissections occur at diameters <5.5 cm, which currently is the threshold for prophylactic aortic replacement recommended by the European Society of Cardiology. , , Therefore, further risk prediction parameters are needed to improve decision-making for imaging intervals and prophylactic surgery to reduce mortality in patients with TAD.
Because hereditary aortopathies, such as Marfan and Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, cause 20% of thoracic aortic aneurysms, genetic testing appears promising for identifying patients at increased risk for dissection. However, genetic testing identifies the genetic cause in only 10%-25% of these patients. , Numerous new candidate genes have been identified, but causal connections between novel genetic variants and TAD have rarely been established due to the lack of available functional analyses. Therefore, functional characterization of candidate genes and genetic variants are urgently needed to improve risk prediction in TAD and to inspire possible novel treatment targets and strategies.
The variant R177Q in PRKG1, the gene encoding for cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP)-dependent protein kinase I (PKG-I), is associated with the familial onset of TAD occurring at low aortic diameter and at young patient age. , However, the presence of such variant alone is insufficient to guide the clinical management of affected patients. Consequently, clinical decision-making for prophylactic aortic repair remains difficult. It is also not clear how variants in PRKG1 lead to the disease. PKGs are the main effectors of the second messenger cGMP and control physiological functions, such as blood pressure, vascular tone, and homeostasis of the aortic wall, in smooth muscle cells. Other muscle cells such as cardiomyocytes also express PKG and here the kinase plays a prominent role in the regulation of Ca2+ dynamics, passive tension, and myofilament relaxation by phosphorylation of phospholamban, titin, and cardiac myosin-binding protein C. In general, the cGMP-PKG pathway is considered to exert mainly beneficial effects on the cardiovascular system. Therefore, it is not surprising that the cGMP-PKG signal transduction pathway is the target of many cardiovascular drugs including NO vasodilators, neprilysin inhibitors, and guanylyl cyclase stimulators. , , In the human genome, 2 genes encode for PKG isoforms: PRKG1 for the splice variant PKG-Iα and PKG-Iβ and PRKG2 for PKG-II. Mammalian PKGs share a common domain organization, but type I isoforms Iα and Iβ are the main isoforms expressed in the cardiovascular system. 
The present study identified a profound impact on cell and tissue mechanics as the prevalent molecular pathomechanism underlying inherited aortopathy evoked by a gene variant in PRKG1. As the routinely applied clinical correlation between aortic diameter to predict the risk of dissection is difficult in patients with inherited forms of aortopathy, alternative strategies are urgently required. Consequently and as a proof of concept, we established functional assays to perform personalized disease modeling of patient variants in PRKG1 to pave the way for improved risk prediction and to guide intervention in genetically susceptible patients.

## Materials
All materials and media were from Miltenyi Biotec, Invitrogen, Sigma Aldrich, Stemgent, R&D System, Peprotech, Corning, Thermo Fisher Scientific, or Biorbyt if not stated otherwise.

## Cloning and site-directed mutagenesis
FLAG-twin-Strep-tagged human (h) PKG-Iα wild type (FSS-hPKG-Iα WT) and hPKG Iβ WT (FSS-hPKG-Iβ WT) were generated by subcloning the complementary DNA sequences coding for hPKG-Iα WT and hPKG-Iβ WT from pFLAG hPKG-Iα 2-671 WT and pFLAG hPKG-Iβ 5-686 WT into the pcDNA 3.0 SF TAP plasmid vector via NheI and XhoI restriction sites with the following primers: 5ʹ-AAAGCTAGCAGCGAGCTAGAGGAAGACTTTGCC-3ʹ and 5ʹ-AAACTCGAGTTATTAGAAGTCTATATCCCATCCTGA-3ʹ for hPKG-Iα WT and 5ʹ-GAAGCTAGCATCCGGGATTTACAGTACG-3ʹ and 5ʹ-AAACTCGAGTTATTAGAAGTCTATATCCCATCCTGA-3ʹ for hPKG-Iβ WT.
Site-directed mutagenesis polymerase chain reaction for generating FSS-hPKG-Iα V219I and FSS-hPKG-Iβ V234I were performed with the KAPA HiFi PCR Kit (KAPA Biosystems) employing the primers 5ʹ-GGAATTTTTAAAAAGCATTCCAACATTCCAGAG-3ʹ and 5ʹ-CTCTGGAATGTTGGAATGCTTTTTAAAAATTCC-3ʹ. Plasmids were analyzed by Sanger sequencing. The V234I mutation was created by site-directed mutagenesis with WT PKG-Iβ cyclic nucleotide binding domain B (CNB-B) as a template and the primers 5ʹ-TTTTTAAAAAGCATCCCAACATTCCAGAGC-3ʹ and 5ʹ-GCTCTGGAATGTTGGGATGCTTTTTAAAAA-3ʹ. The correct mutation was verified by sequencing of the polymerase chain reaction product.

## Recombinant human full length protein expression and purification
FSS-hPKG-Iα and -Iβ fusion proteins were expressed in HEK293T cells. Cells were grown to a confluency of 80% in medium (Dulbecco modified Eagle medium [DMEM], 10% fetal calf serum, 1% penicillin/streptomycin [P/S]) and transfected with the respective WT or V219I/V234I construct with polyethyleneimine (Polysciences). For protein purification, cells were harvested, freeze-thawed, and subsequently lysed in lysis buffer (20 mmol/L N-2-hydroxyethylpiperazine-Nʹ-2-ethanesulfonic acid [HEPES] [pH 7.4], 150 mmol/L NaCl, 0.5 mmol/L tris(2-carboxyethyl)phosphine [TCEP], ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid [EDTA]-free protease inhibitor [Roche], phosphatase inhibitor [Roche], and 0.4% Tween20). Proteins were purified employing Strep-Tactin Superflow resin (IBA Lifesciences). The resin was washed with lysis buffer, followed by a high-phosphate buffer (366 mmol/L Na2HPO4, 134 mmol/L NaH2PO4, 0.5 mmol/L TCEP), and 2 times with HEPES buffer with 4 column volumes of each buffer. Elution was accomplished with an elution buffer (100 mmol/L Tris pH 8, 150 mmol/L NaCl, 1 mmol/L EDTA) containing 2.5 mmol/L desthiobiotin. Subsequently, a buffer exchange was performed to store the proteins in either HEPES-(20 mmol/L HEPES pH 7.4, 150 mmol/L NaCl, 0.5 mmol/L TCEP) or Tris-(50 mmol/L Tris pH 7.4, 150 mmol/L NaCl, 0.5 mmol/L TCEP) buffer.

## Recombinant human protein expression and purification of WT and mutant PKG-Iβ CNB-B
As previously described, expression of WT and mutant PKG-Iβ CNB-B (amino acids 219-369 = CNB-B) was performed in BL-21 (DE3) Escherichia coli cells. Briefly, the transformed E coli cells were grown at 37 °C in M9 minimal media enriched with 15NH4Cl, and supplemented with trace metals, thiamine-HCl, biotin, and 100 μg/mL of ampicillin. When the cells grew up to an optical density of 1.0 (at λ = 600 nm), expression was induced with 0.4 mmol/L of β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside. The cells continued to grow for 16 hours at 20 °C. The cells were harvested by centrifugation (10,000g for 10 minutes) and stored at −80 °C.
Purification of the WT and mutant PKG-Iβ CNB-B construct was implemented as previously described. Briefly, the cells were lysed with a cell disruptor, and the cell pellet was separated by centrifugation at 20,000g for 1 hour. The expressed protein was firstly purified with an affinity Ni2+-Sepharose resin and excess imidazole. After the protein was eluted from the column with excess imidazole, it was dialyzed and treated with tobacco etch virus protease for 48 hours to cleave the N-terminal poly-l-histidine tag from the PKG-Iβ (219-369) construct. Tobacco etch virus and the cleaved poly-I-histidine tag were removed with a Ni2+-Sepharose gravity column. The eluted cleaved PKG-Iβ CNB-B (219-369) construct was further purified by size exclusion chromatography (HiLoad 16/60 Superdex 200 gel filtration column, GE Healthcare).

## Kinase activity assay and determination of the activation constant Kact
Specific kinase activity and the activations constant Kact were determined using an enzyme-coupled spectrophotometric assay according to Cook et al. All experiments were performed at room temperature. In short, assay buffer (100 mmol/L 3-(n-morpholino)propane sulfonic acid, 10 mmol/L MgCl2, 1 mmol/L adenosine triphosphate, 1 mmol/L phosphoenolpyruvate, 15.1 U/mL lactate dehydrogenase, 8.4 U/mL pyruvate kinase, 230 μmol/L nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide plus hydrogen, 5 mmol/L β-mercaptoethanol) was mixed with the hPKG samples in a glass cuvette and the reaction was started by adding the respective substrate peptide (vasodilator stimulated phosphopeptide [VASPtide]: RRKVSKQE; Kemptide: LRRASLG; PKStide: GRTGRRNSI; GeneCust, France) to a final concentration of 1 mmol/L. For basal activities, measurements were performed without cGMP. Maximal kinase activity was determined in the presence of a final cGMP concentration of 50 μmol/L. The absorption at 340 nm was monitored for 30-120 seconds with a double beam photometer (Specord 205, Analytik Jena), and the slope was determined to calculate the specific kinase activity according to the Lambert-Beer law. Kact was determined by combining assay mix with a kinase mix and a dilution series of cGMP or cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) in a 96- or 384-well plate (Assay plate, 96-well, nonbinding surface, polystyrene, Corning Inc; Microplate, 384-well, PS, F-bottom, clear, Greiner Bio-One International GmbH) in a ratio of 2:1:1 and monitoring the absorption at 340 nm for 15-20 minutes. Protein activity was calculated according to the Lambert-Beer law by a linear regression with the first 200-300 seconds of the blank corrected reaction and converted to the observed catalytic activity at the given substrate concentration of 1 mmol/L (kobs). The logarithmic cGMP concentration was plotted against kobs, and Kact was determined by fitting the data points with a sigmoidal dose-response fit with a variable slope with GraphPad Prism (version 8.0.1 1, GraphPad Software, Inc). The activation constant, Kact, is defined as the cyclic nucleotide concentration at which half-maximal kinase activity occurs.

## Surface plasmon resonance
Direct binding data between cGMP and PKG were obtained via surface plasmon resonance with a Biacore T200 (Cytiva). Strep-Tactin XT was coupled to the CM5 sensor chip surface according to the Twin-Strep-tag capture kit (IBA Lifesciences). Subsequently, Twin-Strep-tagged hPKG-I proteins were immobilized onto the surface to a response level of 1,200-2,700 Response Units (RU) at a flow rate of 10 μL/min. HEPES-buffered saline with surfactant P20 (10 mmol/L HEPES, 150 mmol/L NaCl, 0.05% P20) with 1 mmol/L adenosine triphosphate, 10 mmol/L MgCl2, and 0.5 mmol/L TCEP was used as running buffer. Affinity measurements were performed as single-cycle kinetics with 120- to 300-second association and 150- to 400-second dissociation phases at flow rates of 30-50 μL/min. Before each measurement, 3 initial start-up cycles were run with blank running buffer. Buffer referencing was performed by buffer injections before and after each cGMP dilution series. The sensor chip surface was regenerated with 3 consecutive pulses of 3 mol/L guanidinium hydrochloride with 10 mmol/L NaOH for 30 seconds at 30 μL/min. Biacore T200 Evaluation Software 3.2 (Cytiva) was used to process the sensorgrams. To distinguish between PKG high- and low-affinity binding sites, kinetic on (kass) and off rates (kdiss) and equilibrium constants (KD) were determined, applying the heterogenous ligand binding model. Additionally, steady-state analysis of the data employing a multisite steady-state affinity model at a constant Rmax was performed, to determine the equilibrium constants (KD) in an alternative approach.

## Nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy
All nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy (NMR) spectra were acquired using a Bruker AV 700 spectrometer equipped with a triple resonance cryoprobe. The experiments were performed with uniformly 15N-labeled WT or V234I PKG-Iβ (219-369) at a concentration of 50 μmol/L in NMR buffer (50 mmol/L Tris pH 7.0, 100 mmol/L NaCl, 1 mmol/L dithiothreitol, 0.02% NaN3,). Two-dimensional (2D) 1H-15N heteronuclear single-quantum coherence spectroscopy (HSQC) spectra were acquired for apoenzyme (apo) and cGMP-bound states for both WT and mutant in the presence of 80 μmol/L of the internal chemical shift referencing compound 15N-labeled N-acetyl glycine, at 306 K, with a recycle delay of 1.0 seconds, 16 scans, and 2 K (t2) and 198 (t1) complex points for spectral widths of 16.23 (1H) and 38.00 (15N) parts per million.

## Culture of hiPSCs
The isogenic control (ic) and the mutant V219I-hiPSC line were seeded at a density between 5 × 104 to 6 × 104 cells/cm2 in StemMACS Brew medium (Miltenyi Biotec) supplemented with 1% P/S and Y-27632 (5 μmol/L; Biorbyt) under hypoxic conditions (37 °C, 5% CO2, 5% O2, 90% humidity) in Matrigel (Corning)-coated 6-well plates or T-75 flasks.

## Thawing of hiPSC-CMs
HiPSC cardiomyocytes (CMs) were thawed by defrosting cryopreserved cells in a water bath at 37 °C for 2-3 minutes until only 20% of the cells remained frozen. The cells were transferred to a 50-mL Falcon tube with a 2-mL pipette, and the cryovial was rinsed with 1 mL of prewarmed complete medium (DMEM, 1% P/S, 10% horse serum, 10 mg/L insulin (Sigma-Aldrich), and 33 mg/L aprotinin). This rinse step was repeated twice over 60 seconds, followed by the addition of 7 mL of complete medium. Cells were mixed by swirling, counted with a Neubauer chamber with trypan blue, and centrifuged at 100g for 10 minutes. After centrifugation, cells were resuspended in normal cardiomyocyte medium (DMEM, 1% P/S, 10% fetal calf serum, 10 μmol/L Y-27632, and 1% L-glutamine).

## CRISPR/Cas9 genome editing
To introduce the missense variant V219I (chr10:53667268:G>A) into PRKG1, a guide RNA (gRNA) was designed with the CRISPOR.org web tool and validated with the IDT CRISPR-Cas9 gRNA design tool. A 70-nucleotide single-stranded oligonucleotide symmetric template was designed to introduce the desired G > A- base edit as well as 2 silent mutations creating a new BsmI restriction site for clone identification. The gRNA was annealed to a transactivating CRISPR RNA RNA and co-nucleofected into passage 24 ERC001 (UKEi001-A) hiPSCs with the 4D-Nucleofector System (Lonza). Nucleofected cells were seeded into a 12-well plate, precoated with Matrigel. After 48 hours, cells were detached and seeded at low density in a 6-well plate with mTeSR+ and Y-27632 (10 μmol/L) to obtain single-cell clones. When reaching sufficient size, colonies derived from single cells were picked, replated in a 48-well plate and passaged once 1:2. Cells from each clone were cryopreserved (1× 48 wells) and harvested for DNA extraction (1× 48 wells). DNA extraction was performed with the DNA Blood and Tissue Kit (Qiagen) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Preselected clones were identified initially by BsmI digestion and the correct mutation confirmed by Sanger sequencing at Eurofins Genomics.

## Monolayer differentiation of hiPSC to vascular smooth muscle cells
A monolayer-based differentiation protocol was applied. For this, 1.1 × 106 hiPSC (day −4) were seeded onto Matrigel-coated T-75 cell culture flasks in 15 mL StemMACS Brew medium supplemented with 1% P/S and Y-27632 (1:2,000). The medium was changed to 15 mL StemMACS Brew medium only supplemented with 1% P/S after 2 (day −2) and again after 1 day (day −1). On day 0, the medium was exchanged to mesodermal induction medium following 1 washing step with Roswell Park Memorial Institute medium (RPMI; Invitrogen). The mesodermal induction medium contained basal serum-free medium (RPMI 1640 with GlutaMax, 1% [vol/vol] 100× sodium pyruvate (Invitrogen), 1% [vol/vol] 100× P/S, L-ascorbic acid [200 μmol/L]; Sigma-Aldrich)) with 2% (vol/vol) B27 supplement minus insulin and was additionally supplemented with CHIR-99021 (1 μmol/L; Stemgent), BMP4 (5 ng/mL; R&D Systems), Activin A (9 ng/mL; R&D Systems), and basic fibroblast growth factor (5 ng/mL; Peprotech). The mesodermal induction medium was renewed on day 1. Cardiac induction induced by addition of cardiac differentiation medium after washing once with RPMI started on day 2. The cardiac differentiation medium was based on basal serum-free medium with 2% (vol/vol) B27 supplement minus insulin and was supplemented with inhibitor of WNT production-4 (5 μmol/L; Stemgent). On day 3, cells were passaged and 3 × 106 cells per cell culture flask were seeded onto laminin-coated (Sigma-Aldrich) culture flasks in epicardial differentiation medium, which contained basal serum-free medium with 2% (vol/vol) B27 supplement plus insulin and CHIR-99021 (1 μmol/L), BMP4 (50 ng/mL), and retinoic acid (4 μmol/L; Sigma-Aldrich). Laminin-coating was performed a day before by dissolving laminin, thawed on ice, 1:40 in cold phosphate-buffered saline (PBS; Thermo Fisher Scientific) at 4 °C. Epicardial differentiation medium was exchanged every other day until day 10, when the smooth muscle specification medium (SMSM) was added after 1 washing step with RPMI. SMSM consisted of KnockOut DMEM, 10% (vol/vol; Gibco) KnockOut Serum Replacement, 1% (vol/vol; Gibco) P/S, 1% (vol/vol) L-glutamine (Gibco) and was supplemented with transforming growth factor beta [TGFB] (2 ng/mL; Peprotech) and platelet-derived growth factor subunit B (10 μg/mL; Peprotech). The SMSM was exchanged on days 11, 12, and 13. On day 14, the cells were passaged again and 4.5 × 106 cells were plated onto vitronectin-coated cell culture flasks in SMSM. For the coating, vitronectin (StemCell Technologies), was dissolved 1:100 in cold PBS. From day 14 onward, the cells were fed with SMSM every other day until the end of the differentiation on day 22. On this day, hiPSC vascular smooth muscle cells (VSMCs) were dissociated and used for molecular, functional, and phenotypical analysis. During the entire differentiation protocol, the cells were cultured under hypoxic conditions (37 °C, 5% CO2, 5% O2, 90% humidity) in T-75 cell culture flasks.

## Fluorescence-activated cell sorting
To monitor the pluripotency of hiPSCs, the yield of hiPSC-VSMC or hiPSC-CM differentiation, the percentage of stage-specific embryonic antigen 3–, α smooth muscle actin (α SMA)-, MHY11-, or cardiac troponin T (cTnT)-positive cells was determined, respectively. At least 25 × 104 cells were taken after dissociation, resuspended in PBS, and centrifuged at 1,200 revolutions/min. The supernatant was discarded, and the pellet was resuspended in Roti-Histofix solution (Carl-Roth) and incubated on ice. Cells were centrifuged at 1,200 revolutions/min and washed with PBS. Subsequently, fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) buffer (PBS with 5% fetal bovine serum [vol/vol]; Gibco), saponin (5 mg/mL; Sigma-Aldrich), and sodium azide (0.5 mg/mL; Merck Millipore) was added and the cells were incubated overnight at 4 °C. Cells were resuspended in FACS buffer by vortexing and the sample was split in 2 for providing a negative isotype control. After another centrifugation step, the primary antibody solution (PE-SSEA3: #560237, 1:5 dilution, BD Biosciences; MHY11: # ab133567, 1:100 dilution, Abcam; αSMA: #M0851, 1:100, Dako; APC-cTnT: #130-120-403; 1:100 dilution, Miltenyi Biotec) or negative control (PE-Rat IgM: κ isotype control #553943, 1:40 dilution, BD Biosciences; REA-APC: #130-120-709; 1:100 dilution, Miltenyi Biotec) was added. All dilutions were prepared in FACS buffer. Samples were vortexed briefly and incubated for 1 hour at 4 °C. For the SMA- and MHY11-stained samples, after washing with FACS buffer, a secondary antibody solution (Alexa Fluor 647 goat anti-mouse or Alexa Fluor 488 goat anti-rabbit, 1:200 dilution; Invitrogen) was added, vortexed, and the cells were incubated for 30 minutes at 4 °C in the dark. The incubation time was followed by 2 washing steps with FACS buffer. One more washing step with PBS was performed and after centrifugation and removing of the supernatant, the pellet was stored PBS at 4 °C until FACS analysis. The FACS measurement was performed with the NovoCyte Quanteon Flow Cytometer of the FACS core facility at the University Medical Center Hamburg-Eppendorf.

## Protein extraction from hiPSC-VSMCs
For total protein extraction from hiPSC-VSMCs, cells were collected during dissociation and centrifuged at 100g for 4 minutes. After discarding the supernatant, the cell pellets were washed twice with PBS without Mg2+ and Ca2+. To lyse the cells, the pellets were resuspended in 100 μL of lysis buffer (1% vol/vol Nonidet P-40, 20 mmol/L Tris HCl pH 8, 2 mmol/L EDTA, 137 mmol/L sodium chloride, cOmplete Mini, EDTA-free protease inhibitor cocktail, and PhosSTOP phosphatase inhibitor cocktail) and incubated for 30 minutes at 4 °C. Insoluble proteins were separated by centrifugation at 1,000g for 5 minutes, and the supernatant was collected for further analysis. For engineered heart tissue (EHT) samples, the tissue was frozen in liquid nitrogen and transferred to cryotubes. Two EHT samples were pooled per sample in 70 μL of lysis buffer for EHT (mammalian protein extraction reagent, cOmplete Mini). The samples were homogenized with metal beads in a tissue lyser for 2 × 30 seconds at 30 Hz. After 10-minute incubation on ice, the tubes were centrifuged for 10 minutes at 20,000g, and the supernatant was collected representing the protein sample. All protein samples were either processed immediately or stored at −20 °C for later use. Protein concentration was measured with the Qubit Protein Assay (Invitrogen). Then, 10 μg of total protein or 3 μL of EHT lysate was diluted in 3× reducing buffer (187.5 mmol/L Tris-HCl pH 6.8, 6% vol/vol sodium dodecyl sulfate [SDS], 30% vol/vol glycerol, 15% vol/vol β-mercaptoethanol, 0.015% wt/vol bromophenol blue) and double-distilled H2O to ensure equal loading volumes. Samples were boiled at 95 °C for 5 minutes to denature the proteins before analysis. For analysis of extracellular matrix (ECM)proteins, cells were washed once with PBS after dissociation and then resuspended (500,000 cells in 300 μL) 3× reducing sample buffer (187.5 mmol/L Tris-HCl, pH 6.8; 6% vol/vol SDS; 30% vol/vol glycerol; 15% vol/vol β-mercaptoethanol; 0.015% wt/vol bromophenol blue) and further homogenized using a syringe (0.4 × 20 mm, 27 G × ¾, B. Braun STERICAN). All samples were boiled at 95 °C for 5 minutes to denature the proteins before analysis.

## SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis and Western immunoblot analysis
For SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis, 10 μg of total protein and 5 μL of Precision Plus Protein Standards All Blue (Bio-Rad) were loaded onto Mini-PROTEAN TGX Gels (prepacked; Bio-Rad) and separated for 1.5 hours at 100 V. Proteins were transferred to nitrocellulose membranes with the Turbo Transfer (Bio-Rad). After the transfer, all proteins on the membranes were stained with Ponceau S for 10 minutes at room temperature and imaged with the ChemiDoc system (Bio-Rad). Following a wash with Tris-buffered saline containing 0.1% Tween-20 (TBS-T), nonspecific binding sites were blocked by incubating the membranes for 1 hour at room temperature in 5% nonfat dry milk in TBS-T or 1%-5% bovine serum albumin (BSA; Sigma-Aldrich) in TBS-T. ## Cell elasticity by atomic force microscopy
Measurements of hiPSC-VSMC elasticity were performed with an atomic force microscope (NanoWizard, JPK). The applied force probes (cantilever B, micro sharpened cantilever technology (MSCT), Bruker) had an average spring constant of 0.015 N/m as determined by thermal noise analysis. Subsequently, 15 × 104 hiPSC-VSMCs were cultivated on 1% gelatin in PBS-coated culture dishes (μ-dish 35-mm low, ibidi) in SMSM overnight under hypoxic conditions (37 °C, 5% CO2, 5% O2, 90% humidity) and fixed in 1% (vol/vol) glutaraldehyde in SMSM for 20 minutes at room temperature and stored in PBS at 4 °C until analysis. Guided by phase contrast microscopy, force probes were manually positioned over single cells and 100–1,000 force-distance cycles per cell were recorded. The approach speed was set to 2 μm/s. The data rate was 1,024 Hz. At least 4 cells of 2 independent experiments were analyzed. Semiautomatic data analysis was performed with the JPK data processing software (version 5.0.131) with the Hertz/Sneddon model to determine the Young modulus (YM). The tip of the force probe was conic with a half-cone angle of 25°. The Poisson ratio was set to 0.5. The depth of indention was 200 nm.

## Automated assessment of cell area
For the assessment of cell area, 5.5 × 103 or 10 × 103 hiPSC-VSMCs in SMSM were cultured for 24 hours under hypoxic conditions (37 °C, 5% CO2, 5% O2, 90% humidity) in 96-well cell culture plates that were coated with 1% (vol/vol) gelatin/PBS. After 24 hours of cultivation, cells were fixed in ice-cold acetone-methanol solution (1:1) and incubated for 20 minutes. After washing twice with PBS, cells were stained with Phalloidin-FITC for cell bodies and Hoechst 33342 for nuclei. Image acquisition was performed with an Operetta cLSM (Revvity) with 49 fields per well and 4 planes per field using a 20× objective. Image analysis was performed with Columbus 2.9.01546 software. Nuclei identification was achieved using the Hoechst 33342 channel. Because the cells were multinucleated, additional analysis steps were carried out. Nuclei size was increased by 20%, and nuclei that were in contact with others were fused and afterward reduced in size by 50%. These nuclei served for cytoplasm detection by the Phalloidin-FITC signal. A region was defined around these nuclei that included the nuclei and an additional region of 50% in diameter that was again used for nuclei identification. The identified nuclei served as a basis for differentiating real polynucleated cells from cellular debris and artifacts. Only cells with a cytoplasm previously identified in the Phalloidin-FITC channel remained in the analysis. All remaining cells were analyzed regarding cell area with a 2-tailed Welch test.

## Real-time deformability cytometry
Analysis was adapted to the method previously described. , After dissociation, hiPSC-VSMCs were centrifuged at 200g for 5 minutes (Allegra X-15R, Beckmann Coulter), the supernatant was discarded, and the cells were resuspended to a density of approximately 1.5 × 105 cells/mL. Different flow rates (0.32 μL/s, 0.6 μL/s) were used during the measurement and the output allowed to calculate mechanical properties such as deformation and YM as well as cell area (μm2) and cell volume (μm3).

## 3D differentiation of hiPSCs to hiPSC-derived cardiomyocytes (hiPSC-CMs)
After expansion of hiPSCs in FTDA (DMEM/F12, 2 mmol/L L-glutamine, 5 mg/mL transferrin, 5 μg/mL selene, 5 mg/L insulin, 1× lipid mix, 50 nmol/L dorsomorphin, 2.5 ng/mL activin A, 0.5 ng/mL TGFB, 30 ng/mL basic fibroblast growth factor, 0.1% vol/vol human serum albumin; Biological Industries) in T-75 flasks, 100% confluent hiPSCs were washed with PBS and incubated with EDTA for 5-10 minutes at 37 °C, and after removal of EDTA, cells were dissociated with PBS and RPMI 1640. The hiPSCs were centrifuged at 100g for 10 minutes and the pellet was resuspended in stage 0 medium (FTDA, 10 μmol/L Y-27632, 4 mg/mL polyvinyl alcohol). Then 30 × 106 cells per 100 mL of medium were transferred to spinner flasks of predetermined volume (125-1,000 mL). Embryoid bodies (EBs) were then formed in suspension culture under constant stirring at 40 revolutions/min overnight in a spinner flask on a magnetic stirrer in a hypoxic incubator (5% O2, 5% CO2, 95% relative humidity). EBs were pooled and, after volume estimation, transferred to pluronic-coated T-175 flasks. Mesoderm induction was performed under hypoxic conditions for 3 days with daily replacement of 50% of the stage 1 medium (RPMI 1640, 4 mg/mL polyvinyl alcohol, 5 mg/mL transferrin, 5 μg/mL selene, 10 μmol/L Y-27632, 1× lipid mix, 0.05% vol/vol human serum albumin, 10 mmol/L HEPES pH 7.4, 250 μmol/L 2-phospho-L-ascorbic acid trisodium salt, 3 ng/mL activin A, 10 ng/mL BMP4, 5 ng/mL basic fibroblast growth factor). The EB volume was estimated again and after removing the supernatant, EBs were resuspended in 100 μL EB/1.5 mL fibroblast differentiation medium medium (RPMI 1640, 1% vol/vol P/S, 10 mmol/L HEPES pH 7.4, 5 mg/mL transferrin, 5 μg/mL selene, 0.05% vol/vol human serum albumin, 1× lipid mix, 10 μmol/L Y-27632, 250 μmol/L polyacryl amide, 1 μmol/L XAV939). For the next 3 days, cardiac differentiation was initiated by daily replacement of 50% of the fibroblast differentiation medium medium. This was followed by 100% replacement with stage 2 medium (RPMI 1640, 1% vol/vol P/S, 10 mmol/L HEPES pH 7.4, 500 μmol/L 1-thioglycerol, 1 μmol/L XAV939, 10 μmol/L Y-27632, 2% vol/vol B27, 5 μg/mL insulin) and 4 days of daily replacement of 50% of stage 2 medium. This was followed by 100% replacement with rich defined medium (RPMI 1640, 1% vol/vol P/S, 10 mmol/L HEPES pH 7.4, 500 μmol/L 1-thioglycerol, 10 μmol/L Y-27632, 2% vol/vol B27, 5 μg/mL insulin) and the final 12 days of daily replacement of 50% rich defined medium to complete cardiac differentiation. In the final step the cardiomyocytes inside the EBs were dissociated with collagenase. After washing twice with washing solution (Hank’s balanced salt solution without Ca2+), 12 mL/200 μL EB collagenase solution (Hank’s balanced salt solution without Ca2+, 1 mg/mL collagenase II, 10 mmol/L HEPES pH 7.4) was incubated at 37 °C, 90% relative humidity, 5% CO2, 20% O2 for up to 3.5 hours until the EBs started to disaggregate. This process was stopped with blocking solution (DMEM, 1% vol/vol P/S, 6 μL DNase/mL). After centrifugation at 100g for 10 minutes, the cells were resuspended in basal medium (DMEM, 1% vol/vol P/S) and further processed.

## Generation of EHTs
To generate hiPSC-CM-EHTs, casting molds were first prepared in a 24-well plate format with 2% agarose solution in PBS and polytetrafluoroethylene spacers to create a mold into which the EHT could be cast. After 20 minutes, when the agarose had set, silicone posts were placed in the casting molds. To cast the EHT, 100 μL of the master mix (1.1 × 106 cardiomyocytes, 6 μL 2× DMEM, 2.5 μL fibrinogen, 3 μL thrombin, and 97 μL) per EHT was pipetted between the posts. To allow polymerization of the fibrin, the EHT plate was incubated at 37 °C, 7% CO2, 40% O2, and 98% relative humidity for 1.5 hours. The casted EHT were overlaid with 500 μL of prewarmed complete medium for 15 minutes and then transferred to a new 24-well plate containing 1.5 mL of prewarmed Complete medium.

## Contraction analysis of hiPSC-CM-EHTs
Three times a week, the culture medium was replaced with 1.5 mL of Complete medium containing 1:400 tranexamic acid. A changing plate was prepared to transfer the silicone posts for this process. Approximately 3-7 days after EHT generation, macroscopic beating became visible. Starting on day 5, automated video-optical contraction analysis was performed precisely 2 hours after each medium exchange. To functionally analyze the contraction behavior of the EHT, the "Whitebox" system was used. 

## Electrical synchronization
Functional analysis with short-term electrical stimulation was performed to achieve a fixed beating frequency of the EHT. To enable the analysis of force differences between different EHT groups, the EHT frequencies were electrically synchronized. For this, EHTs were placed between graphite electrodes and incubated with 2 mL of EHT culture medium for 2 hours at 37 °C, 7% CO2, 72% N2, and 21% O2. Initially, EHTs were measured without electrical stimulation, followed by short-term electrical stimulation within a frequency range between 1 and 3.5 Hz, with increments of 0.5 Hz. After pacing, the EHTs were cultured as before until they were used for subsequent Frank-Starling measurements.

## Frank-Starling measurements in hiPSC-CM-EHTs
Prior to the Frank-Starling measurements, EHTs were cultured for several weeks to reach stable forces and then underwent measurements between days 30 and 50 of culture. The physiological Frank-Starling response was modeled in the EHT by progressively increasing stretch to simulate increasing preload, while measuring contractile force as an approximation of cardiac output. The measurement setup allowed for the simultaneous analysis of 4 EHTs by placing the silicone post upside down in the frequency-controlled (6 V, 2 Hz) organ bath. Four force transducers and 4 preload conductors were manually positioned between each EHT to ensure they touched the EHT without stretching it. During the experiment, the organ bath was heated to 37 °C and perfused with Tyrode solution (Aqua bidest, 1.8 mmol/L CaCl2, 1 mmol/L MgCl2, 120 mmol/L NaCl, 5.4 mmol/L KCl, 22.6 mmol/L NaHCO3, 0.4 mmol/L NaH2PO4, 5 mmol/L D (+) glucose, 25 mmol/L HEPES, pH 7.4), with continuous diffusion of carbogen (95% O2, 5% CO2) to stabilize the pH. During the experiment, the preload of each EHT was manually adjusted in 50-μm steps every minute to increase the stretch of the EHT until passive tension began to rise and force started to decrease. This occurred approximately after 12-20 steps, depending on the genotype. At the end of the experiment, the stretch was gradually reduced to the length at which diastolic displacement began. Data were recorded with DataLab Pro and analyzed with DataLab Reader.

## Statistical analysis
All quantitative data shown were obtained from at least 3 biological replicates with multiple technical replicates for each group. Data are presented as SD of the individually plotted data points or as SEM as stated in the individual figure legends with individual data points shown in different colors within figures for each batch. Welch's or Student's t-test were used to compare the ic and mutant groups, whereas analysis of variance was used to compare >2 groups with Holm-Sidak post hoc test for multiple pairwise comparisons. False discovery rate <0.05 was adjusted using the Benjamini-Hochberg method for proteomics analysis.
The logarithmic cGMP concentration was plotted against Kobs, and Kact was determined by fitting the data points with a sigmoidal dose-response fit with a variable slope.
An R (version 4.4.1) integrated linear mixed-models analysis was used to analyze the real-time deformability cytometry data. , 
Software used for analysis included GraphPad Prism (version 8.0.1), ShapeOut, , R (version 4.4.1), LabChart Pro, LabChart Reader, FLOREADA, ImageJ, and Python (version 3.10.0). P values <0.05 were considered statistically significant.

## Results

## Clinical profile
A female patient with tall stature, ectopia lentis, bicuspid aortic valve, arachnodactyly, and early-onset aortopathy was identified as a carrier of a heterozygous missense variant of unknown significance (VUS) in PRKG1 in a cohort of 4,106 patients with congenital heart defects (1,601 syndromic; 2,505 nonsyndromic) and 5,137 healthy individuals subjects. The annotation was performed with the variant effect predictor (API V.90). The annotation was based on PRKG1 (ENSG00000185532), positions refer to ENST00000401604.2/ENSP00000384200.4, respectively. Screening was performed for rare damaging variants based on a CADD_score ≥20 and allelic count <2 in GnomAD and ExAc. The VUS leads to an exchange of guanine to adenine (10:53667268:G:A) and consequently a valine to isoleucine replacement in position 219 (PKG-Iα) or 234 (PKG-Iβ), respectively. The patient was diagnosed with the above-described symptoms in the absence of mutations in prototypical Marfan-causing genes. The VUS is located at the end of the N-terminal helix of the CNB domain and is thus part of the highly conserved N3A-motif of CNB-B that plays a prominent role in the allosteric regulation of cyclic nucleotide-regulated kinases in general ( and ). Alterations in amino acid composition in this region have the potential to allosterically affect cGMP binding, kinase activation, and activity with consequences on smooth muscle and other cell functions.
 

## PKG V219I enhances cGMP-stimulated kinase activity
Basal and maximally cGMP-stimulated kinase activity of recombinantly expressed full-length human PKG-Iα WT (gray) and V219I (green) was determined in the absence (− cGMP) or at a saturating cGMP concentration (50 μmol/L; + cGMP) by monitoring phosphorylation of 3 different peptide substrates Kemptide, PKStide, or VASPtide ( , ). Basal kinase activity measured by Kemptide phosphorylation was significantly higher in V219I (0.8 ± 0.1 U/mg; P = 0.013) than in the WT kinase (0.6 ± 0.2 U/mg). No difference was observed when kinase activity was assessed in the presence of a maximally effective cGMP concentration (maximum kinase activity WT: 10.3 ± 1.2; V219I: 10.6 ± 1.6 U/mg). When PKStide was used as a substrate, kinase activity did not differ between WT and V219I, neither basally (WT: 1.7 ± 0.2 U/mg; V219I: 2.0 ± 0.3 U/mg) nor in the presence of cGMP (WT: 16.1 ± 1.2; V219I: 16.0 ± 1.7 U/mg), demonstrating that the choice of the substrate influences kinase activity. When experiments were performed with VASPtide that is derived from the physiological PKG-I substrate vasodilator-stimulated phosphoprotein (VASP), basal kinase activity was significantly higher in V219I (1.3 ± 0.5 U/mg; P < 0.001) than WT (0.8 ± 0.3 U/mg). Furthermore, maximum kinase activity in the presence of cGMP was also significantly higher for V219I (20.9 ± 1.3 U/mg; P = 0.002) than for the WT kinase (19.4 ± 1.4 U/mg).

## PKG V219I increases cyclic nucleotide sensitivity
V219 is localized in the N3A motif in close spatial proximity to the low-affinity CNB site and as such might influence cGMP-dependent kinase activation. We therefore determined the activation constant Kact for 2 different peptide substrates, namely Kemptide and VASPtide by spectrophotometric activity measurements. Assays performed with PKStide as substrate resulted in single digit nanomolar Kact values and thus cannot be accurately determined with the spectrophotometric kinase assay. Therefore, we refrained from using the PKStide substrate for kinase activation measurements and focused on the Kemptide and VASPtide substrates when comparing the recombinantly expressed full-length human FSS-hPKG-Iα WT (gray) to the V219I mutant (green) ( and , ). For Kemptide, the WT kinase displayed half-maximal kinase activity at 54 ± 3 nmol/L cGMP and V219I at 33 ± 4 nmol/L ( , ). For the PKG substrate peptide VASPtide, the Kact for WT was 41 ± 4 nmol/L and V219I 24 ± 2 nmol/L ( , ), thus half-maximal kinase activation for V219I occurred at 1.7-fold lower cGMP concentrations compared to the WT kinase. These results show that the amino acid substitution of valine to isoleucine consistently leads to sensitization of the kinase toward its second messenger cGMP.

## PKG V219I leads to faster cGMP association
The N3A motif plays a major role in allosteric modulation of CNB domains. Based on the location of V219I at the transition of the αX:N helix to the 310-loop and the higher hydrophobicity and steric bulk of isoleucine over valine, it is possible that CNB might be affected by the amino acid substitution and explains the enhanced sensitivity toward cGMP. Initially, cGMP-binding affinity of FSS-hPKG-Iα WT and V219I was assessed by fluorescence polarization ( ). Determination of the KD value of 8-Fluo-cGMP revealed a 1.8-fold higher affinity of V219I for this cyclic nucleotide analog compared to the WT kinase ( ). This was subsequently confirmed by competition of the fluorescently labeled analog by addition of cGMP (effective concentration, 50% [EC50] WT: 20 ± 2 nmol/L; V219I: 11 ± 1 nmol/L) ( ). Limitations of fluorescence polarization assays are that they determine EC50 values in an equilibrium state, and KD value assessment is dependent on the fluorescent analog 8-Fluo-cGMP, precluding the determination of rate constants, crucial for comparing binding kinetics of a molecular interaction.
To overcome these limitations, direct binding assays by surface plasmon resonance were employed to assess cGMP affinity of WT ( ) and V219I ( ). For the analysis of the sensorgrams ( and ) a heterogenous ligand binding model was employed to distinguish between CNB-A and CNB-B. This revealed that V219I increased cGMP affinity of CNB-B by 2.3-fold (KD WT: 287 ± 38 nmol/L; KD V219I: 124 ± 19 nmol/L) ( ). This difference was caused by a higher kass in V219I (WT kass: 0.99 × 106 ± 0.29 × 106 mol/L−1 s−1; V219I kass: 2.15 × 106 ± 0.72 × 106 mol/L−1 s−1) ( ). Interestingly, the dissociation rate kdiss remained unaffected by the mutation. The increase in kass may be explained by a switch in the conformation dynamic of CNB-B on cGMP binding between the WT and V219I. These results were corroborated by steady-state analysis ( ). The KD of the CNB-A domain did not differ between genotypes (WT KD: 20 ± 4 nmol/L; V219I KD: 21 ± 7 nmol/L). In contrast, cGMP affinity of the mutant CNB-B domain increased 2-fold compared to the WT (WT KD: 280 ± 50 nmol/L; V219I KD: 142 ± 32 nmol/L) ( ).
PKG preferentially binds cGMP, but can also bind cAMP, though with lower affinity, resulting also in kinase activation. , , We could show that cAMP acts as a partial agonist for PKG-Iα, activating the kinase to 90 % of cGMP. The Kact values for cAMP, 8.5 ± 1.3 μmol/L (WT) ( and ) and 5.0 ± 0.4 μmol/L (V234I) ( and ), were 1-2 orders of magnitude higher for cGMP and also showed sensitization of V219I to the binding of cAMP ( , ). The results show that V219I sensitizes PKG-I to CNB in general, resulting in higher activity at lower cyclic nucleotide concentrations.

## PKG V219I induces a constitutive shift of CNB-B to the active state
PRKG1 gives rise to 2 splice isoforms, PKG-Iα and -Iβ that display distinct tissue expression and functions and differ in their N-terminal amino acid composition, but are identical in their CNB domains, where V219I (V234I in PKG-Iβ) is located. Therefore, the impact of V219I on PKG-Iβ activation was investigated as well. Activation assays of PKG-Iβ WT (gray) and V234I (blue) also showed a 1.7-fold lower Kact in V234I ( and , ). PKG-Iβ V234I showed Kact values of 227 ± 24 nmol/L (Kemptide) ( , ) and 104 ± 4 nmol/L (VASPtide) ( , ), which are significantly lower than those of PKG-Iβ WT with 376 ± 40 nmol/L for Kemptide and 169 ± 3 nmol/L for VASPtide ( ). To understand the impact of the mutation on the apo-inactive conformation of the kinase, we performed structural and NMR spectroscopic analyses of the CNB-B domain of WT ( ) and V234I PKG-Iβ ( ). Analysis of the structure of apo PKG-Iβ WT CNB-B (PDB:4KU8) revealed that the V234I mutation introduces steric clashes between residues W304 (W289 in PKG-Iα) and I234 (I219 in PKG Iα), as shown in where the side chains of residues W304 (black) and V234 (blue) are shown as spheres revealing steric overlap that is not present in WT CNB-B ( ). We hypothesized that the steric clashes introduced by the V234I mutation likely causes a partial shift of the N-terminal helices of CNB-B (ie, the N3A motif) from an “in” to an “out” orientation, mimicking the movement of the N3A motif caused by cGMP. To test this hypothesis, we measured the chemical shifts of W304 in the 1H-15N HSQC spectra of WT apo, cGMP-bound WT, and apo V234I PKG-Iβ CNB-B ( ). Considering that the exchange between inhibitory and noninhibitory conformations of PKG-Iβ CNB-B is fast in the NMR timescale, the chemical shift of W304 is expected to report primarily on the position of the inhibitory PKG-Iβ CNB equilibrium. shows the expansions of the overlaid 1H-15N HSQC spectra of residue W304 for WT apo (inactive; black contours), WT cGMP bound (active; green contours), and V234I apo (blue contours). The distinct shift of the apo V234I peak from the black to the green cross-peak ( ) suggests that the V234I mutation induces a constitutive shift toward the activation state, confirming our hypothesis that is based on steric hindrance. Furthermore, the constitutive equilibrium shift induced by I234 (blue) also explains the higher cGMP affinity of this mutant relative to the WT kinase. The V234I mutation increases the population of conformers with high cGMP-affinity samples by the apo CNB-B, thus increasing the average association constant of this domain for cGMP. 
 

## PKG I V219I enhances cGMP sensitivity in cells
To test the effect of V219I on cGMP binding in a cellular context, we used the Förster resonance energy transfer (FRET)-based cGMP sensor cGi-500, which comprises the CNB-A and -B domains of PKG-I with N- and C-terminal fusions to cyan fluorescent protein and yellow fluorescent protein, respectively. Initially, the cGMP concentration-dependent impact on alteration of the FRET ratio was recorded in cell lysates from HEK293 cells stably expressing PKG-I WT (gray) or V219I cGi-500 (green) on addition of cGMP (1 nmol/L-100 μmol/L) ( ). In in vitro FRET assays, cGi-500 has been reported to exhibit its half-maximal response at a cGMP concentration of ∼500 nmol/L, which can also be observed in the cell lysates of WT sensor-expressing cells showing an EC50 value of 540 ± 007 nmol/L (gray). In line with the afore-described experiments, cGi-500 V219I (green) exhibited a higher affinity for cGMP (300 ± 011 nmol/L), illustrating the sensor is suitable for detecting differences between WT and V219I cGi-500 ( ). Next, we performed FRET experiments in intact HEK293 cells stimulated with increasing concentrations of the cell-permeable acetoxymethyl ester of the cGMP analog 8-(4-chlorophenylthio)-cGMP (8-pCPT-cGMP-AM) ( ; ) or the clinically relevant NO donor sodium nitroprusside (SNP) ( ; ). For 8-pCPT-cGMP-AM, the EC50 values of WT an V219I differed by a factor of 3 (WT: 253 ± 49 nmol/L vs V219I: 82 ± 14 nmol/L). In response to SNP, the changes in the FRET response displayed EC50 values of 71 ± 19 μmol/L for the WT sensor and 15 ± 4 μmol/L for the mutant. The differences in cGMP sensitivity between WT ( ) and V219I ( ) cGi-500–expressing HEK293 cells were visualized by live cell–FRET imaging in response to stepwise increasing concentrations of the NO donor diethylamine NONOate (DEA/NO; 1-500 nmol/L) and confirmed the higher cGMP sensitivity of V219I in a cellular environment. Together, these data demonstrate enhanced sensitivity of the mutated PKG also operating in intact cells.

## Repair of PKG-I V219I messenger RNA restores altered cGMP sensitivity
To analyze whether correcting the VUS would restore the enhanced cGMP sensitivity of PKG-I V219I, a self-labeling protein (SNAP)-adenosine deaminase acting on RNA (ADAR)–mediated messenger RNA repair approach was performed in a stably expressing PKG-I V219I-SNAP-ADAR HEK293 cell line ( ). PKG-I V219I originates from a G>A exchange. The gRNA-guided SNAP-ADAR recognizes and deamidates A to I, which is read as G by the cellular translation machinery, thus reversing isoleucine back to valine. PKG-I V219I-SNAP-ADAR HEK293 cells were transfected with a gRNA complementary to the mutant messenger RNA molecule (+ gRNA; gray) or left untransfected (− gRNA; green). Cells were exposed to increasing concentrations of SNP and the average FRET response per well recorded ( ). After transfection with the gRNA, the FRET response to SNP of PKG-I V219I-SNAP-ADAR HEK293 cells was shifted to higher SNP concentrations, suggesting reduced cGMP sensitivity. Sequencing analysis allowed calculation of the editing efficiency achieved with this repair approach, which amounted to approximately 61% ( ).

## Impact of PKG V219I on cell area in hiPSC-VSMCs

## Impact of PKG V219I on elastic properties in hiPSC-VSMCs
To investigate the potential impact of PKG V219I on distensibility, cell elasticity measurements by atomic force microscopy were performed in 2D cultured and fixed hiPSC-VSMCs. This revealed a significantly lower YM in PKG V219I (green) than in PKG ic hiPSC-VSMCs (gray; P < 0.001; 8-10 cells per 3 independent differentiation runs; 500-1000 force-distance cycles per cell), demonstrating significantly higher deformability of PKG V219I hiPSC-VSMCs ( ). Representative distribution of YM across all measurements from hiPSC-VSMCs of 1 differentiation run for PKG ic (gray) and PKG V219I (green) revealed a more uniform distribution from lower to higher YM in PKG ic, whereas PKG V219I hiPSC-VSMCs exhibited predominantly lower YM values, rarely exceeding a YM of 5 kPa ( ). Real-time deformability cytometry enabled high-throughput analysis of the elastic properties of individual hiPSC-VSMCs in live PKG ic hiPSC-VSMCs (gray; n = 23,255 hiPSC-VSMCs from 4 independent differentiation runs) and PKG V219I (green; n = 23,273 hiPSC-VSMCs from 4 independent differentiation runs). PKG V219I hiPSC-VSMCs exhibited a significantly lower YM (P = 0.046) ( ), enhanced deformation (P = 0.005) ( ), and a trend toward increased cell area (P = 0.36) and volume, though not statistically significant (P = 0.72) ( and ).
 

## Impact of PKG V219I on post-translational modification of actin and tubulin
Western blot analysis revealed similar expression of MYH11, vinculin, and SMA, all marker proteins of contractile VSMCs in both genotypes ( ). Protein abundance of COL4A1 and COL2A1 was analyzed with the former showing enhanced abundance. CCN2, which acts downstream of TGFB receptors, senses actin dynamics to modulate collagen expression was also significantly more abundant in PKG V219I than PKG ic, but not CCN1 ( ). Whereas the abundance of VASP, an actin nucleator and polymerase and established PKG substrate, did not differ between genotypes, its phosphorylation at serine 239 (VASP p239) was significantly higher in PKG V219I than in isogenic counterpart hiPSC-VSMCs (P = 0.018; n = 4 differentiation runs) ( ). These data suggest an impact of V219I on the actin cytoskeleton. Microtubules convey elastic properties to cells, therefore, post-translational modification of α−tubulin by tyrosination and detyrosination were investigated. No significant difference in the degree of tyrosination/detyrosination was detected between genotypes (n = 4 differentiation runs) ( ). Analysis of PKG-I V219I cells demonstrated lower production of IL13, IL18 (P = 0.042), MIF, and plasminogen activator inhibitor 1 (PAI-1) (P = 0.002) when compared to PKG ic ( ). PKG-I V219I secreted significantly less IL18 (P = 0.004) and no detectable monocyte chemoattractant peptide 1 (P = 0.002). However, there was a higher secretion of PAI-1 (P = 0.002) and MIF (P = 0.013) compared to PKG ic ( ). Interestingly and relevant to our observations, PAI regulates the cytoskeleton by decreasing cytoplasmic F-actin content, thus impacting stiffness in VSMCs. Additionally, aberrant actin-depolymerization triggers an autoinflammatory disease dependent on IL18 abundance.

## Impact of PKG V219I on protein composition
Proteomic and pathway analysis was performed in hiPSC-VSMCs (PKG ic, gray dots; PKG V219I, green dots; n = 6 from 6 independent differentiation runs). Principal component analysis revealed separate clustering of the genotypes ( ). Significantly higher (red) and lower (blue) abundant proteins in PKG V219I compared to PKG ic are summarized in the volcano plot ( ). Interestingly, the matricellular proteinsTHBS1 and CCN2, the cytoskeletal linker protein EPPK1, the actin assembly regulating protein ENAH actin regulator and collagens (COL4A3, COL4A5, COL4A1, COL4A2) were among the significantly more abundant proteins detected in PKG V219I hiPSC-VSMCs. Pathway analysis for gene ontological biological processes, molecular functions, and cellular components highlighted collagen-activated signaling, ECM organization and structure as well as wound healing among the pathways prominently activated in PKG V219I hiPSC-VSMCs ( ). Enhanced protein abundance of CCN2 and COL4A1 were confirmed by Western immunoblot analysis ( ). These data indicate that PKG V219I hiPSC-VSMCs activate a program resulting in higher production of ECM proteins, likely as a compensation for the lower intrinsic cellular stiffness.

## Impact of PKG V219I on CM Contractile function
PKG-I is also abundantly expressed in CMs and affects important CM functions. , , To investigate whether PKG V219I also affects elastic and contractile properties of CMs, potential consequences of PKG V219I were analyzed in hiPSC-CMs cultured in 3D-EHT format ( ) with hiPSC-CMs exhibiting the typical morphology and structure of contractile elements evidenced by electron microscopy ( ). To generate hiPSC-CMs, the same hiPSC lines used previously for the hiPSC-VSMC differentiation were employed, and a 3D differentiation approach pursued. The yield of each differentiation was assessed by flow cytometry with cTnT as a marker for CMs ( ) and revealed 61%-97% cTnT-positive cells (n = 3 independent differentiation runs). At the age of 5 days, EHTs started to beat spontaneously with beating frequencies declining over time of maturation as expected ( ), paralleled with increasing force development ( , and ) in both genotypes. After 4 weeks, PKG V219I hiPSC-CM-EHTs displayed significantly higher average force (PKG I V219I; n = 15 EHTs from 3 differentiation runs: 0.2 mN vs PKG ic; n = 22 EHTs from 3 differentiation runs: 0.15 mN; P < 0.001) ( ), significantly higher resting length (PKG I V219I; n = 15 per 3 runs: 5.2 μm vs PKG ic; n = 22 per 3 runs: 4.7 μm; P = 0.011) ( ) and contraction velocity (PKG-I V219I; n = 15 per 3: 2.2 mN/s vs PKG ic; n = 22 per 3 runs: 1.5 mN/s; P < 0.001) ( ). Relaxation velocity ( ) was comparable between genotypes, with 0.6-0.7 mN/s. Parameters such as the time to reach 80% of peak (0.15 seconds) and time to reach 80% of relaxation (3.2 seconds) were also unaffected ( and ), indicating no genotype-dependent effect on these aspects of spontaneous beating behavior. PKG ic (white bars) and PKG-I V219I (gray bars) hiPSC-CM-EHTs were electrically synchronized at increasing frequencies (1-3 pulses per second) ( ). Across all applied frequencies, PKG-I V219I EHTs generated significantly higher forces than PKG-I ic (P < 0.001) ( and ), combined with a shorter time to reach peak force (P < 0.001) ( ). The time to reach 80% of peak force is indicated by a vertical line. Furthermore, contraction velocities ( ) of PKG-I V219I hiPSC-CM-EHTs were consistently higher than in PKG ic across all frequencies, suggesting a more rapid force generation. At 3 Hz, the relaxation velocity was also significantly increased in PKG-I V219I (P < 0.001) ( ), indicating a faster return to baseline contraction. Assessment of post-translational modification of tubulin revealed no differences in the ratio of detyrosination to tyrosination ( ). However, the phosphorylation status of the bona fide cardiac PKG substrate, phospholamban at Ser16 and VASP at Ser239 were significantly increased in PKG V219I hiPSC-CM-EHTs compared to PKG ic ( ).
 To further investigate the elastic properties and passive stiffness of hiPSC-CMs, the physiological Frank-Starling response was compared between PKG ic and PKG V219I hiPSC-CM-EHTs. Progressively increasing the length of the EHTs simulates a rise in preload, while simultaneously measuring contractile force as an approximation of cardiac output. The force recorded between contractions, when the EHTs were at rest (ie, resting tension) serves as an approximation of diastolic pressure. When plotted against length, this tension-length relationship can indicate shifts in diastolic stiffness, because a rise in tension with increasing stretch suggests increased diastolic pressure. Representative mechanograms ( , PKG ic top panel, PKG V219I bottom panel) illustrate synchronized contractions of both genotypes. Magnification of the regularly developed contractions at the 600-μm stretch interval is exemplified for representative PKG ic and PKG V219I hiPSC-CM-EHTs ( ). For PKG-I ic, resting tension started to rise at around the 500-μm stretch ( , gray), whereas for PKG I V219I (green), tension began to rise later at around the 800-μm stretch, suggesting lower passive stiffness in PKG-I V219I than in PKG-I ic. When resting tension was plotted against length, PKG-I V219I EHTs showed significantly lower tension at higher stretch levels (P < 0.001) ( ). At the 900-μm stretch, PKG-I V219I hiPSC-CM-EHTs exhibited significantly lower resting tension than PKG-I ic (P = 0.018) ( ). This observation indicates lower passive stiffness in PKG-I V219I hiPSC-CM-EHTs. When developed force was plotted against length, PKG-I ic hiPSC-CM-EHTs displayed a more rapid force increase at increasing stretch ( ). Maximal force development did not differ between the 2 groups; however, PKG-I ic EHTs reached force saturation at the lower stretch, whereas PKG-I V219I continued to increase force more gradually with increasing stretch intervals ( and ). When stretch intervals were extended to 1,000 μm and hiPSC-CM-EHTs then progressively released from the stretch, PKG-I ic hiPSC-CM-EHTs reached a force plateau phase earlier in the Frank-Starling response, whereas PKG-I V219I continued to increase in force development ( and ). Linear regression performed over the stretch phase (550–1000 μm) confirmed this observation. PKG-I ic hiPSC-CM-EHTs reached their force plateau earlier during the Frank-Starling response than PKG-I V219I hiPSC-CM-EHTs (PKG ic: 0.000104, R2 = 0.0096 vs PKG-I V219I: 0.000519, R2 = 0.19). Furthermore, tension regression analysis showed a steeper slope for PKG-I ic than PKG-I V219I (PKG-I ic: 0.000451 vs PKG-I V219I: 0.000124), suggesting lower passive stiffness of PKG-I V219I hiPSC-CM-EHTs. These data demonstrate a disparate Frank-Starling response between the genotypes ( and ).
 

## Discussion
Variants in PRKG1 have been associated with the development of TAD and the occurrence of aortic dissection in patients with an aortic diameter <55 mm and at a young age. , , However, the precise molecular pathomechanisms that lead to an early onset of complications in patients are unknown to date. Our study provides a detailed characterization of the molecular pathomechanisms evoked by PKG V219I. The increase in kinase activity originates from an acceleration of cGMP association to PKG I, leading to higher basal kinase activity at lower cellular cGMP levels and enhanced VASP phosphorylation. Phosphorylated VASP at Ser239 affects actin polymerization and consequently enhances deformability of VSMCs contributing to the early onset human disease phenotype.
The increased kinase activity of PKG V219I can be rationally explained by the collision between I219 and W389 leading to a partially active conformation of the kinase. V219I is localized in the N3A motif of the low-affinity cGMP-binding domain that is commonly found in all cis-regulated CNBs. This fits very well with studies reporting R177Q in PRKG1 causing aortic dissection in young adults by conveying constitutive activation of PKG-I without the requirement for cGMP binding. , For R177Q, basal activity was 58 ± 9% higher than WT PKG-I kinase activity stimulated with cGMP.
In general, N3A motifs occupy an N3A-out position when cyclic nucleotides bind to the CNBs as part of the conformational change of the regulatory domain. , Consequently, the N3A-out position is considered as the active and N3A-in as the inactive conformation of this subdomain. A small movement of the N3A motif is already sufficient to shift the conformational equilibrium of PKG toward the N3A-out conformation of CNB-B, which explains the increase in basal V219I activity that we observed.
Activation constant measurements demonstrated that PKG V219I has a significantly lower Kact vs its WT counterpart. These results could be also confirmed with the corresponding PKG-Iβ WT and V234I kinases, showing a cross-isoform effect of the amino acid exchange V/I similarly reported for R177Q. cAMP also showed a cyclic nucleotide-sensitizing effect, indicating that the impact of V219I on cyclic nucleotide-dependent activation of PKG-I is a general mechanism.
Fluorescence polarization and surface plasmon resonance measurements pinpointed the CNB-B, which harbors the variant, as solely responsible for the higher cGMP affinity. Based on the kinetic data, V219I increased the association rate constant and thus resulted in a 2-fold increase in cGMP affinity.
The amino acid V219 is located in the N3A motif of CNB-B at the transition of the N-terminal helix (αX:NB) to the 310 loop. It is part of a pocket that stabilizes the CNB domain with a network of hydrophobic interactions. A part of the pocket described here is also referred to as a hydrophobic nest. As such, it is enveloped by the side chains of different hydrophobic and nonhydrophobic amino acids. The importance of this pocket in the allosteric regulation of PKG has been described before. Moon et al showed that peptides mimicking the helix that connects the regulatory and catalytic domain are able to activate PKG-Iα in a cGMP-independent manner by binding to the hydrophobic nest. This aspect has important clinical implications because the localization of the variants within the kinase structure affects severity of the disease phenotype. Small molecule modulators that allosterically activate PKG-Iα cGMP independently by binding this hydrophobic pocket have been already developed by Merck, demonstrating druggability of this important kinase. , , These allosteric modulators could present a new strategy to selectively activate or inhibit PKG-I activity.
Our data support previous work implicating dysregulated NO-sGC (soluble guanylyl cyclase)-PKG signaling in experimental and human connective tissue disease. Chronic application of NO donors was sufficient to induce aortopathy in WT mice. Here, nitrated proteins and levels of p239 VASP were elevated in the circulation and in aortic tissue of mice and patients. , Consequently, pharmacological inhibition of PKG-I by KT5823 reversed aortopathy and media degeneration in mice emphasizing a causal role of cGMP-mediated PKG-I activity in disease development. This is supported by our data in recombinant protein preparations that revealed increased kinase activity due to enhanced cGMP sensitivity as the confounding factor of disease development. Similar observations were made in studies with the constitutively active PKG-Iα mutation R177Q in which heterozygous expression was sufficient to cause thoracic aneurysm and dissection in young adults. , , Importantly, patients were either normo- or hypertensive and the humanized mouse model that expressed R177Q PKG in heterozygous form exhibited only mildly reduced mean arterial pressure, suggesting PKG-mediated molecular pathomechanisms other than long-term vasodilation as the disease origin.
Interestingly, a number of patient characteristics of the V219I mutation are those typically associated with Marfan syndrome. , Thereby, no mutations in classical Marfan genes such as FBN1 were detectable, solely the G/A single nucleotide polymorphism in PRKG1. An age-dependent loss of VSMCs as well as elastin breaks and collagen accumulation in the tunica media were reported in mice and in aortic tissue from patients with aortopathy. Schwaerzer et al attributed the aortic aneurysm pathology to an upregulation of PKG-I–mediated transcription of contractile VSMC-specific genes such as SMA, MYH11, and TGFB. Activation of TGFB signaling increases oxidative stress and cell apoptosis leading to degeneration of the tunica media and acceleration of aortic dilation and dissection. Our data obtained in hiPSC-VSMCs revealed a trend toward higher protein abundance of MHY11 and SMA in PKG V219I hiPSC-VSMCs by Western immunoblotting, immunofluorescence, and flow cytometry, although this did not reach statistical significance. Furthermore, features of enhanced TGFB signaling in V219I PKG-I hiPSC-VSMCs were detectable, which is the central signaling pathway affiliated with the occurrence of Marfan syndrome. PAI-1, an inhibitor of the protease plasmin, plays a role in vascular remodeling, proliferation, and migration. The elevated secretion of PAI-1 in PKG-I V219I cells likely reflects increased TGFB signaling, given that TGFB directly drives PAI-1 transcription and expression. , The decreased presence of PAI-1 within cell pellets indicated a shift in localization rather than a change in its overall production. Recent findings highlighted an age-related correlation between PAI-1 localization and cell growth. Specifically, nuclear localization of PAI-1 in promyelocytic leukemia bodies supports proliferation, whereas restriction to the cytoplasm reduced growth. 
Alterations in PAI-1 abundance aligned with the lower proliferation rate of PKG-I V219I hiPSCs, which consistently showed reduced differentiation output during each differentiation run, even when identical cell numbers were seeded. By the end of the differentiation protocol, PKG-I V219I cells appeared less confluent compared to PKG-I ic cells.
PKG acts as a key regulator to ensure balanced and controlled growth, preventing excessive or abnormal proliferation in multiple tissues such as in cardiovascular cells, but also in osteoblasts and chondrocytes. In our study, PKG-I V219I led to significantly enlarged cell area of hiPSC-VSMCs compared to control cells. This strong effect on growth by kinase overactivation is notable because it supports previously published evidence that the lack of PKG-II in mice caused dwarfism due to defective endochondral ossification at the growth plate. Therefore, vosoritide, a US Food and Drug Administration–approved natriuretic peptide receptor B agonist, leads to elevation of cellular cGMP and PKG-II activation and has been approved for the treatment of patients with achondroplasia A is employed to enable bone growth. These observations set the stage for connecting the overactive PKG-I V219I kinase to an aberrant growth phenotype. Our proteomic data revealed CCN2, also known as connective tissue growth factor, as one of the significantly more abundant proteins in V219I hiPSC-VSMCs. CCN2 is a master regulator of bone and cartilage genesis that acts downstream of the TGFB pathway. , It binds to the ECM and scavenges growth factors, inducing endochondral ossification and long bone growth. Cartilage-specific CCN2 expression in mice led to extended length of the long bones and increased proteoglycan and collagen II accumulation. The latter were also detectable in the over-representation of pathways in our proteomic data set.
PAI-1 up-regulation is also associated with reduced cell adhesion and increased detachment through disruption of cell-adhesion interactions, further promoting apoptosis via a THBS1 cascade. Low PKG-I expression was described to increase cell migration and adhesion. , Conversely, increased PKG-I activity can attenuate adhesion and migration, consistent with observations made in isolated VSMCs from humanized Marfan mouse models, in which impairment of cell adhesion was reported. THBS1 was significantly more abundant in PKG V219I hiPSC-VSMCs, which is a critical mediator of mechanotransduction, and its up-regulation disrupts elastin-contractile units and actin cytoskeleton organization via the TGFB–PAI-1 pathway. These alterations contribute to aortic aneurysm development and ultimately culminate in aortic dissection in patients. 
Increased arterial stiffness and the loss of elastic compliance are crucial contributors to the development of cardiovascular diseases. Increasing actin polymerization in VSMCs due to inhibition of NO-sGC-PKG-pVASP signaling consequently enhanced arterial stiffness. Actin filament organization in PKG-I V219I revealed significantly lower anisotropy of actin filaments than in PKG ic as shown by SEM and live cell imaging. Motility ( and ) appeared less coordinated in PKG-I V219I hiPSC-VSMCs. In addition, the rapid and uncoordinated formation of lamellipodia was also reflected in a greater heterogeneity of the shape of PKG-I-V219I cells. VASP, a key PKG-I substrate and potent actin polymerase, plays a significant role in actin filament dynamics. Notably, phosphorylation of VASP at S239 was significantly increased in PKG-I V219I, whereas total VASP levels remained unchanged. VASP phosphorylation directly affects cell motility by promoting actin polymerization into filaments, enhancing elongation, and preventing capping protein binding. , , VASP colocalizes with actin at leading edges, organizing filaments into linear, parallel bundles. When phosphorylated at S239, VASP inhibits this colocalization, potentially disrupting filament organization and resulting in overall shorter filaments. , , Impaired actin network regulation, including dysregulated F-actin turnover has been observed in VSMCs cultured from humanized Marfan mice. Several F-actin regulatory proteins, such as actinin-1, CFL2, and ENAH were differentially abundant in these cells as well as in our V219I PKG-I hiPSC-VSMCs. Relevant to our observations, it was recently proposed that pharmacological stimulation of sGC activity exerts beneficial effects in patients with cardiovascular disease by antagonizing arterial stiffening. These data corroborate our observation that increased PKG-I activity lowers cellular stiffness. It also raises the possibility that prolonged pharmacological boostering of NO-sGC-PKG signaling might cause harm by affecting aortic integrity.
At first sight, the lower cellular stiffness in V219I hiPSC-VSMCs and CM-EHTs in our study appears to be at odds with the consistent clinical observation that thoracic aneurysm and dissection tissue exhibits significantly higher stiffness, lower elastic fiber density, and higher collagen content than healthy control aortas do. These findings led to the concept that reduced elasticity of the aorta in thoracic aneurysm and dissection orchestrates weakening of the aortic wall. , We hypothesize that the apparent discrepancy points to disease development through different stages. It needs also to be considered that the function of the aortic wall is determined both by the cellular and the extracellular compartments. Our data in immature hiPSC-VSMCs and CMs indicate that the primary defect renders VSMCs more elastic, deformable, and less resistant to passive strain, conferred, in our case, by increased kinase activity of PKG V219I and similar to the canonical skin, eye, and joint abnormalities in Marfan syndrome. It is well conceivable that this primary defect of abnormal softness of the cells initiates a program for a stiffer ECM as an adaptive response for protection, potentially by stimulating ECM production. This interpretation is supported by our proteomics data showing ECM structural compartment as the strongest up-regulated pathway in V219I hiPSC-VSMCs.

## Study limitations
This study provides a mechanistic explanation for the molecular consequences underlying the early disease phenotype. However, confirmation in other, more frequently mutated Marfan- and aortopathy-associated genes that cause inherited forms of aortopathy is still required.

## Conclusions
Taken together, our data provide exciting novel insights into the molecular mechanical pathomechanisms that influence the early stages of inherited forms of aortopathy evoked by variants in PRKG1 that may originate initially from lower cellular stiffness as the primary defect. Gaining a deeper understanding of the underlying molecular pathomechanisms may improve risk prediction for earlier intervention in genetically susceptible patients in the future.

PerspectivesCOMPETENCY IN MEDICAL KNOWLEDGE: Aortic dissection can occur in patients without significant aortic enlargement, emphasizing the need to look beyond diameter-based criteria for risk assessment. The PRKG1 V219I variant increases the basal activity of cGMP-dependent PKG-I, resulting in enhanced muscle cell deformability due to altered actin cytoskeleton dynamics and ECM signaling, representing an early disease state. These findings provide a mechanistic explanation for aortic wall fragility despite near-normal vessel size. Clinicians should recognize that genetically mediated aortopathies, such as those associated with PRKG1 variants, may present without conventional morphological risk markers. Integrating molecular diagnostics and functional assays into clinical workflows could enable earlier detection and intervention in genetically susceptible individuals, ultimately improving outcomes in TAD.
TRANSLATIONAL IMPLICATIONS: The identification of a constitutively activated PKG-I signaling mechanism caused by the PRKG1 V219I variant establishes a new framework for understanding genotype-driven aortic vulnerability. Future research should focus on developing targeted functional assays to detect pathogenic PKG-I activation and on incorporating genetic screening in families with unexplained thoracic aortic events. In addition, therapeutic modulation of PKG-I signaling represents a potential precision medicine approach to restore vascular wall homeostasis. Longitudinal, genotype-informed studies integrating molecular, biomechanical, and clinical data are warranted to refine surveillance strategies and guide personalized interventions for heritable TAD, as well as to expand insights to other aortopathy risk gene variants.
