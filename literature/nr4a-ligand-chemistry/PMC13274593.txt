To define the contribution of nutritional factors and related epigenetic regulation, we perform in vivo CRISPR screening and identify the ascorbate transporter Slc23a2 as a key regulator of naive T cell (Tn) reactivity. T cell–specific loss of Slc23a2 reduces intracellular ascorbate, induces regional DNA hypermethylation, enhances differentiation of Tn cells into effector and memory populations, and promotes low-grade autoimmune inflammation. ## INTRODUCTION
T cells harbor a highly diverse antigen receptor [T cell receptor (TCR)] repertoire generated through stochastic gene rearrangement, which inevitably includes receptors capable of recognizing self-antigens and thus poses a risk of autoimmunity. During thymic development, strongly autoreactive T cells are eliminated through negative selection (clonal deletion) or diverted into the regulatory T cell (Treg cell) lineage under permissive conditions, thereby establishing central tolerance ( , ). In addition, tolerance can be enforced in the periphery through mechanisms such as functional inactivation (anergy) of conventional T (Tcon) cells following self-antigen encounter, as well as active suppression mediated by Treg cells, which limit the activation, differentiation, and effector functions of autoreactive Tcon cells ( , , ).
Although thymic negative selection and Treg cell–mediated suppression are well characterized, how autoreactive Tcon cells that escape thymic deletion are intrinsically restrained from responding to self-antigens remains incompletely understood. We propose that this restraint is maintained by precisely tuned cell-intrinsic regulatory networks that preserve Tcon cell quiescence despite persistent, predominately weak stimulation by self-antigens. Several observations support this concept. Modulators of TCR and cytokine signaling can set thresholds for naive T (Tn) cell activation and differentiation into effector and memory lineages. For instance, the coinhibitory receptor VISTA is highly expressed in Tn cells and limits their responsiveness to TCR stimulation, thereby maintaining quiescence ( ). Similarly, dampening inflammatory cytokine signaling, exemplified by interleukin-6 (IL-6) regulation via the ribonuclease Zc3h12a, is essential for preventing spontaneous autoimmune T cell activation ( ). At the transcriptional level, the repression of inappropriate gene expression—such as IL-4 suppression by Kruppel-like factor KLF2—effectively constrains inflammatory cytokine production in resting T cells ( ). Despite these advances, a comprehensive understanding of the cell-intrinsic mechanisms that limit autoreactive Tcon cell reactivity is still lacking.
To address this gap, we investigated environmental influences, particularly nutritional factors, which are known to broadly shape adaptive immune function ( ). These factors may modulate peripheral tolerance in a cell-intrinsic manner by acting on signaling pathways and/or epigenetic machinery, thereby altering the thresholds for Tn cell activation and differentiation into effector and memory states. Epigenetic mechanisms are inheritable and can impart long-lasting effects on Tcon cell behavior ( , ). We therefore sought to identify environmental regulators and their downstream epigenetic pathways ( ) that control Tn cell activation and differentiation under conditions of persistent self-antigen stimulation, functioning analogously to coinhibitory receptors to sustain peripheral tolerance.
Using an in vivo CRISPR screening approach, we identified the ascorbate (ASC or vitamin C) transporter Slc23a2 as a negative regulator of Tn cell activation and differentiation into effector or memory cells at steady state. The phenotypes resulting from conditional Slc23a2 deletion were largely recapitulated by loss of TET dioxygenases in Tcon cells, consistent with the role of ASC as a cofactor for TET-mediated DNA demethylation ( – ). Mechanistically, Slc23a2-ASC and TET-dependent DNA demethylation constrains TCR signaling by attenuating proximal signal transduction and enhancing the expression and chromatin binding of transcription factor Tcf1, thereby opposing Tn cell activation and differentiation in response to self-antigens. Together, our findings define a checkpoint regulatory mechanism involving Slc23a2, intracellular ASC, and TET-dependent DNA demethylation, which maintains Tn cell quiescence and suppresses self-antigen–driven activation, effector and memory differentiation, and chronic autoimmunity. The dysregulation of this process may therefore contribute to aging-associated autoimmune inflammation or inflammaging ( ).

## RESULTS

## Identification of Slc23a2 as a negative regulator of the effector-to-naive T cell ratio
To identify the nutritional factors that constrain Tcon cell reactivity to self-antigens, we conducted an in vivo CRISPR screen using a custom single-guide (sgRNA) library targeting 180 solute carrier (SLC) transporters expressed by T cells (table S1), as these proteins regulate cellular nutrient availability. We generated Cd4Cre RosaLSL-Cas9 (LSL, loxP-Stop-loxP) mice ( ), enabling Cas9 expression in T cells beginning at the CD4+CD8+ double-positive (DP) thymocyte stage. Lineage-negative (Lin−) Sca-1+ c-Kit+ (LSK) bone marrow progenitors from these mice were transduced with the sgRNA library and transferred into lethally irradiated wild-type (WT) CD45.1+CD45.2+ C57BL/6 (B6) recipients to reconstitute the immune system. Three months later, sgRNA representation was assessed in donor-derived CD4 T cells ( , and table S2).
 As expected, sgRNAs targeting known negative regulators of T cell activation, including Pdcd1 (PD-1) and Zc3h12a, were enriched in CD4+Foxp3−CD44hiCD62Llo effector T (Te) cells ( ), consistent with prior reports describing their roles in restraining effector differentiation or maintaining Tn cell quiescence ( , ). Multiple sgRNAs targeting SLC genes were significantly enriched or depleted in the CD4 Te population (P < 0.01). Collectively, these sgRNAs were present in ~3.2% of T cells, suggesting predominately cell-intrinsic effects of the corresponding gene deletions. We validated the screening results by targeted CRISPR deletion of Slc23a2 in all T cells of retrogenic mice generated from Cd4Cre RosaLSL-Cas9 donors, which produced an increase in Te cells comparable to that observed following Zc3h12a deletion ( , and table S3).
Slc23a1 and Slc23a2 encode sodium-dependent ASC transporters ( ). Whereas rodents synthesize ASC in the liver, primates acquire ASC through dietary intake ( ) ( ). T cells selectively express Slc23a2 but not Slc23a1 ( and fig. S1A). In addition, dehydroascorbic acid (DHA) can enter cells through glucose transporters (GLUTs) and be reduced intracellularly to ASC ( ). To directly assess the role of Slc23a2 in T cells, we generated conditional Slc23a2fl/fl mice in which deletion of exons 4 and 5 yields a null allele (fig. S1B). The T cell–specific ablation of Slc23a2 was achieved by crossing Slc23a2fl/fl mice with Cd4Cre mice and confirmed by RNA sequencing (fig. S1C). The Knockout of Slc23a2 (Slc23a2KO) reduced intracellular ASC levels in T cells by more than 88% ( ), demonstrating that Slc23a2 is the dominant ASC transporter in T cells.
Despite this profound reduction in ASC, Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice exhibited no major defects in thymic development, including total DP, CD4+CD8− single-positive (SP), or Treg cells, with the exception of an increase in CD4−CD8+ SP cells (fig. S1, D to K). In contrast, peripheral lymphoid organs showed a marked expansion of CD4 Te cells and CD44hiCD62Llo CD8 T cells ( and fig. S2, A to C). Notably, more than 50% of Slc23a2KO CD8 T cells expressed high levels of CD44 and CD62L, predominately representing CD49dlo virtual memory Tvm cells rather than CD49dhi central memory Tcm cells. By comparison, 20% or fewer of CD8 T cells in WT mice displayed this phenotype (fig. S2, D and E). Together, these data establish Slc23a2 as a critical intrinsic regulator that restrains the spontaneous formation of effector and memory T cells under steady-state conditions.

## Slc23a2 restrains effector differentiation of Tn cells at steady state
We next examined whether thymic positive and negative selections are affected in Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice, leading to more autoreactive Tcon cells in the periphery. Thymocytes that had undergone positive selection, defined as CD4hiCD8int cells ( ), were more than twofold increased in these mice compared with WT controls (fig. S2F). By contrast, thymic negative selection assessed using Foxp3− CD4 T cells expressing Vβ5 TCRs reactive to endogenous mammary tumor virus–encoded superantigens (cSAG-8 and vSAG-9) ( ) was comparable between Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre and WT mice (fig. S2G). The analysis of active caspase-3 as an additional marker of negative selection ( ) revealed a modest reduction in the frequency, but not the absolute numbers, of caspase-3+CD69hiTCRβint thymocytes in Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice (fig. S2H). These findings suggest a subtle impairment of thymic negative selection that may permit increased escape of autoreactive Tcon cells into the periphery.
To determine whether Slc23a2KO in Treg cells contributes to the expanded effector and memory populations observed in Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice, we generated Slc23a2fl/fl Foxp3Cre mice ( ) to selectively delete Slc23a2 in Treg cells. Despite an ~95% reduction in intracellular ASC in Treg cells, these mice exhibited no overt dysregulation of Treg or Tcon cells and did not develop immunological pathology (fig. S2, I to L). In contrast, Treg cells from Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice displayed a more activated phenotype, characterized by increased expression of CD44—defining activated Treg cells (aTreg cells)—and CTLA-4 (fig. S2, M to P), consistent with a secondary response to autoimmune inflammation. This phenotype was not observed in Treg cells from Slc23a2fl/fl Foxp3Cre mice (fig. S2, I to K).
In mixed chimeric mice in which WT Treg cells were present, Slc23a2KO CD4 Te cells remained markedly enriched relative to WT counterparts ( and fig. S2Q). Consistently, single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) revealed reduced frequencies of Slc23a2KO CD4 and CD8 Tn cells, accompanied by increased CD4 Te and CD8 Tvm cells ( , and fig. S3, A and B). In addition, invariant natural killer T (iNKT) cells were expanded in both bone marrow chimeras and Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice ( and fig. S3C). These data support a cell-intrinsic role for Slc23a2 in limiting effector and memory T cell differentiation. Elevated IL-4–producing thymic iNKT cells in Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice (fig. S3, C and D) may promote CD8 Tvm cells through cell-extrinsic mechanisms (fig. S2, D and E) ( , ), in conjunction with heightened intrinsic TCR signaling (described below).
To directly assess the role of Slc23a2 in peripheral Tn cells, we performed inducible deletion of Slc23a2 in CD4 Tn cells from Slc23a2fl/fl RosaCreER mice ( ). Seven days after adoptive transfer and tamoxifen (Tam) treatment, Slc23a2KO T cells exhibited a significant increase in the proportion of CD4 Te cells, including interferon-γ (IFN-γ)–producing subsets, with a more modest induction of IL-13–producing cells ( and fig. S3E). The weaker T helper 2 (TH2) skewing likely reflects limited type II polarizing cues under steady-state conditions and the short duration of the experiment. Nonetheless, these data suggest that Slc23a2 functions as a checkpoint regulator that intrinsically restrains Tn cell activation and differentiation into effector and memory T cells at steady state, independently of its roles in thymic-positive and -negative selections.

## Slc23a2 and ASC control aging-associated autoimmune activation of Tcon cells
To evaluate the role of ASC in Slc23a2-mediated regulation of Tn cells, we analyzed l-gulonolactone oxidase–deficient (Gulo−/−) mice. Because Gulo encodes an essential enzyme for endogenous ASC biosynthesis, Gulo−/− mice rely on dietary ASC, similar to primates ( ) ( ). Withdrawal of ASC for 5 weeks reduced intracellular ASC levels in T cells by ~95% and led to a marked increase in CD4 Te and CD44hiCD62Llo CD8 T cells ( and fig. S4, A and B), closely phenocopying the effects of Slc23a2KO.
 Given prior reports linking aging to reduced ASC availability ( ), we next examined T cells from aged mice and human peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs). Intracellular ASC levels were reduced by ~40% in CD4 Tn cells from 18-month-old mice compared to 2-month-old controls, accompanied by increased frequencies of CD4 Te and CD44hiCD62Lhi CD8 T cells ( ), consistent with previously described aging-associated Tcon phenotypes ( , ). Similarly, the proportion of CD4 Tn cells was significantly decreased in individuals over 60 years of age relative to those under 40 years old ( ). To account for interindividual variations in dietary ASC intake, we compared paired ASC levels in plasma and CD4 T cells and observed a significantly increased plasma-to-cell ASC ratio in aged individuals ( ), suggesting impaired ASC uptake and/or increased intracellular consumption. Direct measurement of ASC uptake using [13C]-ASC confirmed a marked reduction in ASC uptake rates in CD4 Tn cells from aged mice ( ). Conversely, the long-term administration of high-dose ASC substantially mitigated aging-associated increase in CD4 Te, CD44hiCD62Llo CD8 T, and CD44hiCD62Lhi CD8 T cells ( ).
Upon activation with immobilized CD3 and CD28 antibodies, CD4 Tn cells from Slc23a2-deficient and aged mice secreted significantly higher levels of IFN-γ, granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF), IL-4, and IL-10 compared with cells from WT and young mice ( ). Correspondingly, TH1 (IFN-γ+), TH2 (IL-13+), TH17 (IL-17A+), and TFH (PD-1+CXCR5+) cells were markedly expanded in both adult Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice and aged WT mice (fig. S4, C to G). Consistent with enhanced T cell activation, Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice developed low-grade systemic inflammation, most prominently in the stomach, along with plasmacytosis in mesenteric lymph nodes (MLNs). These mice also exhibited elevated serum levels of immunoglobulin A (IgA), IgG2b, IgG2c, IgM, and IgE, including antibodies reactive to double-stranded DNA (dsDNA), nuclear proteins, and multiple organs ( , and fig. S4, H to M). In addition, Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice developed mild glomerulonephritis, characterized by increased urinary albumin excretion and deposition of complement component C1q in the kidney (fig. S4, N and O), resembling features of lupus nephritis ( ).
Markers of cellular senescence—including β-galactosidase (β-Gal) activity, the DNA damage response marker γH2AX, and cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor Cdkn1a ( )—were elevated in CD4 T cells from 18-month-old WT mice and 2-month-old Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice relative to 2-month-old WT controls (fig. S5, A to C). Moreover, the expression of senescence-associated secretory phenotype ( ) was increased in Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells both at baseline and following in vitro TCR stimulation (fig. S5D). Collectively, these findings suggest that high intracellular ASC levels sustained by Slc23a2 restrains Tn cell activation and effector or memory differentiation, thereby suppressing aging-associated low-grade autoimmune inflammation.

## TET-dependent DNA demethylation underlies Slc23a2-ASC control of Tcon activation and autoimmune inflammation
ASC is a well-established cofactor for DNA and histone demethylases, including TET dioxygenases ( ) ( ). TET2 and TET3 function redundantly to maintain Treg lineage stability, suppress iNKT cells, and restrain Tcon cell proliferation ( , , ). We therefore hypothesized that TET enzymes mediate Slc23a2-ASC–dependent regulation of Tn cell quiescence and antigen responsiveness.
To uncouple the intrinsic role of TET enzymes in Tcon cells from secondary effects caused by iNKT activation and Treg dysfunction following Tet deletion by Cd4Cre ( , ), we generated mixed bone marrow chimeric mice using Cd1d−/− recipients ( ). In this system, WT Treg cells compensated for dysfunctional Tet2KOTet3KO (TetKO) Treg cells, while the absence of Cd1d—combined with CD1d-blocking antibody treatment—efficiently prevented iNKT cell expansion (fig. S5E). Under these conditions, TetKO T cells exhibited a marked expansion of CD4 Te, CD44hiCD62Llo CD8, and CD44hiCD62Lhi CD8 T cell populations ( and fig. S5, F and G). Mice harboring TetKO T cells developed multiorgan inflammation; heightened production of proinflammatory cytokines by CD4 Te cells; increased CD8 T cell activation; and elevated serum IgG2b, IgG2c, and IgG3 levels ( ). Consistent with these findings, the inducible deletion of Tet genes in retrogenic mice resulted in a rapid increase in CD4 Te, CD44hiCD62Llo CD8 T, and CD44hiCD62Lhi CD8 T cells within 2 weeks of Tam treatment ( , and fig. S5, H and I). TetKO CD4 T cells also displayed increased β-Gal activity, elevated γH2AX, and up-regulation of other senescence-associated genes (fig. S5, J and K). These results demonstrate that TET dioxygenases intrinsically suppress Tcon activation and autoimmune inflammation, phenocopying the effects observed following Slc23a2 deficiency.
 To define the mechanistic relationship between Slc23a2 and TET enzymes in controlling Tn cells, we next examined whether Slc23a2-ASC and TET regulate overlapping DNA methylation programs. The progressive accumulation of CD4 Te cells in Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice over time suggested cumulative epigenetic dysregulation in CD4 Tn cells (fig. S6A). Whole-genome bisulfite sequencing (WGBS; 40× coverage) identified 18,615 differentially methylated regions (DMRs) between Slc23a2KO and WT CD4 Tn cells despite unchanged global DNA methylation levels (fig. S6B). Notably, 91.2% of these DMRs were hypermethylated ( ). Comparable, although less pronounced, DNA hypermethylation at the same genomic regions was observed in CD4 Tn cells following short-term Tet deletion (8 days after Tam induction) or prolonged ASC deprivation (5 weeks), including at loci such as Cd5 and Lef1 ( , and fig. S6, C to F). Differences in the magnitude of DNA methylation changes likely reflect the slow kinetics of DNA remethylation and variations in the duration of the respective perturbations. Genes differentially expressed in WT versus Slc23a2KO, TetKO, or ASC-deprived CD4 Tn cells showed substantial overlap (fig. S6, G to I). Principal components analysis and comparative methylation profiling further revealed that Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells occupy a distinct epigenetic state, rather than representing an intermediate along the activation trajectory from Tn to Te cells (fig. S6, J and K). Consistent with this altered basal state, puromycin incorporation assays ( ) demonstrated increased protein synthesis and metabolic activity in resting Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells (fig. S6L).
CD4 Tn cells isolated from aged mice similarly displayed increased CpG methylation relative to those from young mice ( ), in agreement with prior reports ( ). Among DMR-associated genes in aged CD4 Tn cells, 906 (28.3%) overlapped with those regulated in Slc23a2KO and TetKO CD4 Tn cells, including Ccr7, Il7r, Lef1, and Tcf7l2 ( and fig. S6M). These findings support a link between aging-associated epigenetic remodeling and reduced ASC levels and TET activity while acknowledging that additional age-related factors likely contribute.
Given the pleiotropic biochemical functions of ASC, we next assessed whether alternative mechanisms—such as histone demethylation or reactive oxygen species (ROS)—contribute to the heightened reactivity of Slc23a2KO Tn cells ( ). Western blotting and CUT&Tag analyses ( ) revealed minimal global or locus-specific changes in H3K9me3, H3K36me3, H3K4me3, or H3K27me3 in Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells (fig. S7, A and B). A modest reduction in H3K4me3 was observed, suggesting an indirect effect on histone methyltransferase activity rather than a primary defect in histone demethylation.
Although total ROS—but not mitochondrial ROS—was increased in Slc23a2KO Tn cells (fig. S7, C to E), similar to reports on aged T cells ( ), cell viability and proliferation makers were unaffected (fig. S7, F and G). Exogenous hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) elevated ROS levels but failed to enhance TH1 differentiation from WT or Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells under TH0 conditions (fig. S7, H and I; further investigated below). Conversely, treatment with antioxidants—including ascorbic acid 2-phosphate (AA2P), glutathione (GSH), or N-acetylcysteine (NAC)—did not rescue the enhanced TH1 differentiation induced by Slc23a2KO (fig. S7J). Although nuclear NFAT1 levels were increased in Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells, likely due to ROS accumulation ( ), this increase was insufficient to drive TH1 differentiation (fig. S7, H and K). Collectively, these results support a mechanistic link between Slc23a2-mediated ASC uptake and TET-dependent DNA demethylation as the primary pathway controlling Tn cell quiescence and antigen responsiveness.

## Slc23a2 exerts stage-specific functions in Tcon cells
Given the heightened reactivity of Slc23a2KO Tn cells and their increased differentiation into effector and memory T cells, we anticipated that these cells might provoke more severe inflammatory pathology in vivo. To test this hypothesis, we used a T cell transfer colitis model ( ). Contrary to expectation, recipients of Slc23a2KO Tn cells exhibited markedly attenuated weight loss and intestinal inflammation, accompanied by reduced numbers of total T cells, TH1 cells, and TH17 cells ( ). Similarly, Slc23a2KO significantly alleviated disease severity in experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis induced by MOG35–55 peptide ( ), as evidenced by reduced clinical scores, diminished weight loss, and fewer TH1 and TH17 cells in the brain and spinal cord ( ).
 These paradoxical findings suggested that Slc23a2KO compromises the fitness or survival of Tcon cells following activation ( ). To test this possibility, we assessed whether enforced expression of the antiapoptotic protein Bcl2 could rescue this defect ( ). Notably, in vitro activation of WT CD4 Tn cells—a necessary step for retroviral transduction—abrogated their ability to induce tissue inflammation upon transfer into Rag1−/− mice (fig. S8A, vector control versus CD4 Tn cells without in vitro activation). Recovered cells displayed an increased proportion of FR4+CD73+ anergic cells ( , ). Despite this limitation, Bcl2-transduced Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells induced severe intestinal inflammation following transfer into Rag1−/− recipients, characterized by shortened colons and increased total CD4 T cell numbers and IFN-γ–producing cells ( ).
Together, these results indicate that Slc23a2 serves at least two distinct, stage-dependent functions in Tcon cells. On one hand, Slc23a2 restrains Tn cell activation and differentiation into effector or memory lineages. On the other hand, it supports T cell survival or fitness after activation, thereby enabling inflammatory pathogenicity. The former function is consistent with TET-dependent DNA demethylation, whereas the latter aligns with the established roles of ASC in promoting T cell maturation and antitumor immunity through additional biochemical pathways ( , ). Consequently, Slc23a2-ASC deficiency enhances effector and memory T cell differentiation while simultaneously impairing T cell survival and pathogenicity, ultimately resulting in chronic, low-grade autoimmune inflammation.

## Slc23a2 and TET restrict Tn cell responses to TCR stimulation
To define how Slc23a2 and TET enzymes constrain the activation and differentiation of Tn cells, we examined TCR-driven responses under conditions of altered Slc23a2 and TET function. TCR repertoire sequencing revealed pronounced clonal expansion of Slc23a2KO CD4 Te and CD8 Tvm cells in mixed bone marrow chimeric mice, as well as CD4 T cells following conditional deletion of Tet genes ( ). These findings suggested that Slc23a2 and TET selectively limit Tn cell activation in response to antigen stimulation that exceeds tonic TCR signaling (fig. S8B). Consistent with this model, CD4 Te cells were markedly reduced among SMARTA TCR-transgenic T cells following CRISPR-mediated deletion of Slc23a2 in the absence of the cognate GP61–80 (or GP61) peptide antigen ( ), in contrast to polyclonal T cells subjected to the same deletion ( and fig. S8C). The small number of residual SMARTA-derived CD4 Te cells likely arose from endogenous TCRs that escaped allelic exclusion ( ). Similarly, CD44hiCD62Lhi CD8 T cells were markedly diminished in OT-I TCR-transgenic Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice and emerged only after challenge with ovalbumin (OVA)–expressing MC38 tumor cells (fig. S8D). Together, these results demonstrate that antigen stimulation above tonic signaling thresholds is required for the enhanced activation and differentiation of Slc23a2KO Tn cells.
 Upon stimulation with titrated TCR agonists for 3 hours, Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells displayed pronounced, dose-dependent transcriptional changes relative to WT counterparts ( and table S4). Genes governing T cell activation and effector function—including Il2, Ifng, Il17a, Tnf, Nr4a3, Egr2, Bcl6, and Myc (clusters C1 and C2)—were more strongly induced upon stimulation. In contrast, transcripts characteristic of the naive state, such as Lef1 and Tcf7 (cluster C7), were reduced at baseline in Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells. Consistent with these transcriptional changes, flow cytometric analysis confirmed global down-regulation of CD5 and up-regulation of CD28 in resting Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells—both of which are regulated by Slc23a2 and TET-dependent DNA demethylation ( ). Notably, CD5 expression was robustly induced by TCR and costimulatory signaling in WT CD4 Tn cells, whereas the same stimulation elicited only a modest increase in CD5 expression in Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells ( , cluster C3). These findings suggest that Slc23a2-dependent epigenetic regulation and acute TCR/costimulatory signaling represent two distinct yet cooperative mechanisms that together ensure maximal CD5 up-regulation before and during Tn cell activation.
At the signaling level, Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells exhibited prolonged and enhanced proximal TCR signaling, as evidenced by increased Ca2+ influx and elevated phosphorylation of PLCγ1, Zap70, and Erk1/2 ( ). Basal Zap70 protein levels were also increased in Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells without a corresponding rise in Zap70 mRNA, indicating posttranscriptional regulation as an additional mechanism contributing to heightened signaling. Moreover, the CRISPR-mediated deletion of Cd5 in CD4 Tn cells—removing a well-established negative regulator of TCR signaling ( )—recapitulated the enhanced signaling phenotype observed in Slc23a2KO cells ( ), indicating that Slc23a2 constrains TCR responses through multiple molecular targets.
Because CD5 down-regulation and CD28 up-regulation occurred after the DP stage in Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice (fig. S8, E to G), elevated proximal TCR signaling raised the possibility of altered thymic negative selection, as suggested by prior studies of Cd5 deficiency ( ). However, this hypothesis was not supported by reduced frequencies of caspase-3+ thymocytes (fig. S2H). To directly assess the impact of enhanced TCR signaling in mature Tn cells, we examined antigen-specific responses using peptides of varying affinities following CRISPR-mediated deletion of Slc23a2 or Tet2 (which has partial, nonredundant overlap with Tet3) in SMARTA TCR-transgenic CD4 Tn cells ( ). The deletion of either gene significantly enhanced TFH differentiation in vivo, regardless of whether stimulation was driven by high-affinity WT GP61 or low-affinity GP61-V71S peptide ( ) ( ).
Collectively, these findings demonstrate that Slc23a2 and TET intrinsically restrict Tn cell responses to antigen stimulation exceeding tonic signaling thresholds by modulating activation-related gene expression programs and indirectly dampening proximal TCR signal transduction.

## Slc23a2 and TET suppress differentiation of CD4 Tn cells into helper T cells
Given the heightened responsiveness of Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells to TCR stimulation, we then examined how this altered reactivity promotes helper T cell differentiation. Following nitrophenyl acetyl-keyhole limpet hemocyanin (NP-KLH) immunization, Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice produced elevated levels of NP-specific IgM and showed increased frequencies of TFH cells, germinal center B (GCB) cells, and plasma cells ( and fig. S8H). In contrast, NP-specific IgG1 was modestly reduced, suggesting impaired antibody class-switching, a phenotype reminiscent of aged human immune responses ( ). The broader increase in immunoglobulin isotypes observed in Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice (fig. S4H) may reflect contributions from additional helper T cell subtypes or prolonged exposure to self-antigens.
 Consistent with these findings, adoptive transfer of CD4 Tn cells from aged mice into young Tcrb−/− Tcrd−/− recipients resulted in a significant increase in plasma cells and elevated CXCR4 expression on TFH cells following NP-KLH immunization ( ). These effects were reversed by Slc23a2 overexpressing. Elevated CXCR4 expression on TFH cells, previously shown to cause mislocalization to the germinal center dark zone and impaired vaccine responses in aged mice ( ), was also observed following the CRISPR-mediated deletion of Slc23a2 or Tet2 in SMARTA CD4 Tn cells during LCMV-Armstrong infection (fig. S8, I and J).
Under defined polarizing conditions in vitro, Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells displayed enhanced differentiation into TH1, TH2, and TH17 cells, accompanied by reduced induction of Treg cells (fig. S9, A to E). Notably, more than 50% of Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells differentiated into IFN-γ–producing cells under neutral TH0 conditions with TCR and costimulation ( ). Similarly, CRISPR-mediated deletion of SLC23A2 in human CD4 Tn cells in the absence of TCR/costimulation according to reported protocols ( ) resulted in accelerated loss of naive markers (CD45RA+) and reduced CD5 expression over 3 weeks, followed by increased IFN-γ–producing cells after subsequent culture under TH0 conditions with TCR/costimulatory signaling ( and fig. S9F).
CreER-mediated Tet deletion, ASC deprivation, and aging similarly increased TH1 differentiation from CD4 Tn cells under TH0 conditions ( ). Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells rapidly down-regulated surface CD62L during a 2-hour post-sorting rest period, a phenotype that correlated with increased TH1 differentiation potential under TH0 conditions (fig. S9, G and H).
To assess cell-intrinsic effects of Slc23a2KO, we cocultured CD45.2+
Slc23a2KO and CD45.1+ WT CD4 Tn cells under TH0 conditions. Blocking IFN-γ markedly reduced TH1 differentiation from Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells ( ; plate B). Approximately 14% of Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells, compared to fewer than 3% of WT cells, retained the capacity to differentiate into TH1 cells even in the presence of IFN-γ–blocking antibodies. These results suggest that a subset of Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells is prepolarized in vivo to produce low levels of proinflammatory cytokines upon activation and that Slc23a2KO increases cellular sensitivity to IFN-γ levels that are insufficient to drive TH1 differentiation in WT cells. These mechanisms may also contribute to impaired Treg induction in vitro or in vivo. These findings indicate that an intrinsic predisposition, coupled with a positive feedback loop driven by inflammatory cytokine signaling, promotes the differentiation of CD4 Tn cells into effector or memory lineages when Slc23a2-ASC or TET activity is compromised.

## Regulatory circuits by which Slc23a2-ASC and TET suppress Tn-to-TH1 differentiation
To define the molecular basis underlying the predisposition of Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells toward TH1 differentiation, we reasoned that DNA motifs enriched within Slc23a2-dependent DMRs and at Tet2-binding sites would provide insights into the relevant regulatory circuits. Motif enrichment analysis identified Ets and Tcf1 motifs as highly represented in both contexts ( and fig. S10, A to C). Ets1 is known to suppress TFH differentiation ( ), whereas Tcf1 (encoded by Tcf7) and its related factor Lef1 are critical regulators of T cell development and fate decisions ( , ). We therefore focused on Tcf1 and Lef1 for further mechanistic investigation. Both factors are regulated by Slc23a2-dependent DMRs and were down-regulated in Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells ( , and fig. S10D).
 Tcf1 binding at Slc23a2-dependent DMRs was markedly reduced in Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells ( ), correlating with the decreased expression of the Tn-associated genes Cd5, Sell, Ccr7, and Lef1 (fig. S10, E to I). Tet2 and Tcf1 co-occupied the majority of these sites; however, Tet2 binding was significantly increased in Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells ( and fig. S10, A and B), likely reflecting reduced enzymatic activity that stabilizes Tet2 association with DNA substrates. The distinct binding patterns of Tcf1 and Tet2 do not support direct protein-protein interactions. Instead, Tcf1 binding is likely indirectly inhibited by DNA hypermethylation, as the Tcf1 motif itself does not contain CpG sites ( ).
The conditional deletion of Tcf7 or Lef1 does not result in overt defects in thymic T cell development or spontaneous activation of mature T cells in vivo ( ). Nevertheless, Tcf7KO CD4 Tn cells exhibited significantly enhanced TH1 differentiation under TH0 conditions ( ). This phenotype was associated with DNA hypermethylation and reduced Tcf1 binding at a downstream regulatory region of the Ifng locus, together with more rapid Tcf1 down-regulation following activation in Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells ( ). The heightened TH1 bias of Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells was largely reversed by overexpression of either Tcf1 or Lef1 ( and fig. S10J). Both Tcf1 p33 and p45 isoforms, as well as a histone deacetylase (HDAC)–defective Tcf1 mutant, were sufficient to suppress TH1 differentiation, whereas a Groucho-binding sequence mutant was not (fig. S10K). These findings indicate that interaction with the transcriptional corepressor Groucho is required for Tcf1-mediated repression of TH1 differentiation ( ). In contrast, the overexpression of Ets1, despite enrichment of its motif at DMRs and Tet2-bound sites, did not alter TH1 differentiation from Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells (fig. S10L).
The deletion of Tcf7 driven by Cd4Cre did not increase CD4 Te cells in vivo ( ), suggesting that loss of Tcf1 alone is insufficient to promote effector differentiation in the absence of enhanced TCR signaling. Consistent with this interpretation, combined deletion (double knockout, DKO) of Cd5 and Tcf7 resulted in a marked expansion of CD4 Te cells and TH1 cells in vivo and significantly enhanced Tn-to-TH1 differentiation under TH0 conditions ( ).
In summary, these findings collectively delineate two key layers by which Slc23a2-ASC and TET dioxygenases maintain Tn cell quiescence and restrict differentiation into effector and memory lineages. By limiting Tn cell reactivity to self-antigens and low-level inflammatory cytokines in the resting state, these cell-intrinsic regulatory circuits preserve peripheral immune tolerance and suppress chronic autoimmune inflammation associated with aging ( ).

## DISCUSSION
Whole-body ASC deficiency was first identified more than a century ago as the cause of scurvy, a finding that largely overshadowed its effects on the hematopoietic and immune systems ( ). Subsequent studies have shown that ASC regulates the quiescence and self-renewal of hematopoietic stem and multipotent progenitor cells and promotes T cell maturation and antitumor immune responses ( , , ). Our findings uncover a critical role for Slc23a2-mediated ASC uptake in maintaining the quiescence of Tn cells and suppressing their activation and differentiation into effector and memory lineages in response to self-antigen stimulation. In humans, ASC insufficiency—such as that arising from seasonal fluctuations—may predispose Tn cells toward effector and memory differentiation, thereby increasing susceptibility to autoimmune disorders. Moreover, the partial DNA hypermethylation observed at DMRs in Slc23a2KO and TetKO CD4 Tn cells suggests a dynamic equilibrium maintained by opposing activities of DNA methyltransferases and TET dioxygenases ( ). Defining the dynamics, kinetics, and reversibility of this balance will be essential for understanding how Tn cell quiescence is maintained and disrupted.
Low-grade chronic autoimmunity associated with aging, termed inflammaging, is characterized by DNA hypermethylation, a reduction in Tn cells, an expansion of effector and memory T cells, elevated β-Gal activity, and enhanced DNA damage response marked by γH2AX ( , ). Our findings suggest that attenuated Slc23a2-ASC and TET activity may contribute causally to this process. Nonetheless, notable differences exist between Slc23a2KO and aged CD4 Tn cells, as expected given the involvement of multiple known and unknown regulators in T cell aging ( , ). For example, aged human CD4 Tn cells display diminished responsiveness to TCR stimulation ( ), suggesting that the effects of reduced ASC and TET activity may be masked or overridden by additional aging-associated mechanisms.
Conditional deletion of Tet2 and/or Tet3 disrupts Treg lineage stability and suppressive function, activates iNKT cells, promotes Tcon proliferation, and enhances TFH recall response ( , , , – ). Our findings extend the function of TET enzymes to the regulation of Tn cell quiescence and antigen responsiveness, identifying a critical cell-intrinsic mechanism that maintains peripheral immune tolerance. Moderate reduction of TET activity—achieved through Slc23a2 deletion or ASC deprivation—preferentially lowers the activation threshold of Tn cells, promoting their differentiation into effector and memory T cells in response to self-antigen stimulation. These observations support a dose-dependent role for TET activity in regulating the fates and functions of T cell subsets.
The pronounced effects of Tet deficiency on Tn cells likely reflect a combination of enzymatic-dependent and potentially enzymatic-independent mechanisms that act in concert during Tn cell activation and differentiation into effector and memory T cells. In contrast, the residual ~12% ASC retained in Slc23a2KO Tn cells likely preserves partial TET enzymatic activity, leading to an underestimation of the full contribution of TET function under conditions of normal ASC availability. Nonetheless, the notable similarities between Slc23a2-ASC and Tet deficiencies—including converging effects on Tn cell phenotypes, Tet2 binding at Slc23a2-dependent DMRs, and the established role of ASC as an essential cofactor for TET dioxygenases—strongly suggest a mechanistic link between Slc23a2-mediated ASC uptake and TET activity in restraining Tn cell reactivity to self-antigens and low-level inflammatory cytokines in the resting state. This checkpoint regulation operates in coordination with other tolerance-enforcing mechanisms—including thymic negative selection, Treg cell induction and function, and anergy—to maintain immune homeostasis. Notably, deficiencies in Slc23a2/ASC or Tet appear to affect each of these processes to varying extents. In Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre mice, the enhanced Tn cell reactivity, impaired peripheral Treg induction, potentially attenuated thymic negative selection, and reduced survival or fitness of activated Tcon cells likely act together to drive the chronic, low-grade autoimmune inflammation.
It is widely accepted that differentiating thymic T cells expressing high-affinity TCRs for self-antigens are eliminated through negative selection, whereas those expressing intermediate-affinity TCRs are induced into Treg cells ( ). The remaining cells, which express low-affinity TCRs, persist as Tcon cells. Consequently, restricting the response of Tn cells to weak self-antigen stimulation represents a critical step in establishing peripheral immune tolerance. Slc23a2-ASC and TET activity regulate this process at multiple levels, including proximal TCR signal transduction and the transcriptional control of inducible gene expression. Together, these mechanisms establish a signaling and epigenetic threshold that limits Tn cell reactivity to weak self-antigen stimulation. This checkpoint regulator further suppresses the basal expression of lineage-inducing and lineage-determining factors—such as Ifng, Bcl6, and Gata3—while enhancing the chromatin binding of transcription factors such as Tcf1. Further research will be required to delineate additional roles and mechanistic details of this regulatory axis.
Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells exhibit reduced Tcf1 expression, a phenotype resembling that of aged human T cells ( ). Decreased Tcf1 may also compromise Te cell function through Tcf1+ stem-like T cells ( ). Similarly, Ets1 expression is diminished in Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells. Given that Ets1 suppresses, whereas Tcf1 promotes, TFH differentiation ( , ), further studies are warranted to elucidate the interplay and potential antagonism between Tcf1 and Ets1 during TFH differentiation from Slc23a2KO CD4 Tn cells.
While the role of ASC as a cofactor for TET dioxygenases is well established ( , , ), ASC may also regulate Tn cell reactivity to antigen stimulation through additional downstream effectors, a possibility that warrants further investigation. Moreover, we propose that the relatively predominant biochemical functions of ASC in Tcon cells are context dependent. Within this framework, ASC may regulate TET and histone demethylases in a concentration-dependent fashion, whereas TET itself may function as an integrator of multiple upstream signals. Consistent with this model, Slc23a2 deficiency impairs the survival or fitness of Tcon cells following activation, whereas Tet deficiency does not. Defining the context-specific downstream effectors of Slc23a2-ASC, as well as the upstream signals converging on TET in Tcon cells, will provide important mechanistic insights and may reveal druggable targets for selectively modulating Tcon cell responses in distinct therapeutic settings.
Our findings support a model in which Slc23a2-ASC and TET-DNA demethylation functions as a checkpoint that restrains Tn cell reactivity and limits their differentiation into effector and memory cells in response to self-antigen stimulation under the resting state. This regulatory mechanism is essential for maintaining peripheral immune tolerance, and its impairment drives chronic autoimmune dysregulation associated with aging.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS
Key resources involved in this paper are listed in table S6.

## Mice
C57BL/6 (B6), CD45.1, Cd4Cre, Rag1−/−, Cd1d−/−, Tcrb−/−Tcrd−/−, OT-I, RosaLSL-Cas9 (LSL, loxP-Stop-loxP), RosaCas9, RosaCreER, and RosaLSL-tdTomato (RosatdT) mice were purchased from the Jackson Laboratory. Tcf7fl/fl mice were obtained from the European Conditional Mouse Mutagenesis Program. Tet1fl/fl Tet2fl/fl Tet3fl/fl and Foxp3Cre mice were described previously ( , ). SMARTA TCR transgenic RosaCas9 mice were kindly provided by H. Chi (St. Jude Children’s Research Hospital). Gulo−/− mice were obtained from S. Morrison (the University of Texas Southwestern Medical Center) under a material transfer agreement with the Mutant Mouse Resource and Research Centers. All genetic models were on the B6 background. Both male and female mice were used for the study unless otherwise specified. All mice were maintained under specific pathogen–free (SPF) conditions in the Animal Resources Center at St. Jude Children’s Research Hospital, and the animal experiments were approved (612-100614) by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee.

## Generation of conditional Slc23a2 mice
Slc23a2fl/fl mice were generated on the B6 background using the CRISPR approach to knock in two loxP sites flanking exons 4 and 5 of the Slc23a2 gene (Biocytogen). A founder was established by breeding F0 mice with WT B6 mice followed by PCR sequencing. To generate conditional Slc23a2KO mice, Slc23a2fl/fl mice were crossed with Cd4Cre, Foxp3Cre, or RosaCreER mice.

## Preparation of retrovirus
The retrovirus was packaged following our published protocols ( ). Briefly, plasmids expressing retroviral sgRNAs targeting individual genes or expressing nontargeting control (NTC) sgRNAs were cotransfected into Platinum-E cells (Cell Biolabs) together with the helper plasmid pCL-Eco (Addgene) to produce retrovirus according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

## Generation of retrogenic mice
To generate retrogenic mice, Lin−Sca-1+c-Kit+ (LSK) cells were sorted from bone marrow cells after depleting erythrocytes and lineage-positive cells. Purified LSK cells were activated in vitro for 20 hours with recombinant stem cell factor SCF (10 ng/ml), thrombopoietin (20 ng/ml), IL-7 (10 ng/ml), and Flt3-ligand (100 ng/ml) in SFEM medium at 37°C. Activated LSK cells were transduced with retrovirus expressing sgRNAs of interest on RetroNectin-coated, nontissue culture–treated plates in the same medium for 2 days before being transferred intravenously into lethally irradiated (10.5 Gy) CD45.1+CD45.2+ WT B6 mice. These recipient mice were subsequently administered neomycin (2 mg/ml) in drinking water for 3 weeks. Mice were euthanized for analysis 10 to 12 weeks later.
To perform conditional CRISPR experiments in retrogenic mice, LSK cells were isolated from RosaLSL-Cas9 Cd4Cre mice, activated in vitro, and transduced with a custom retroviral library targeting 180 SLC genes. Transduced LSK cells were transferred intravenously into lethally irradiated (10.5 Gy) CD45.1+CD45.2+ B6 mice. These mice were administered neomycin (2 mg/ml) in drinking water for 3 weeks and analyzed 12 weeks later.
To reveal the effects of Tet conditional deletion on T cells, CreER-expressing Tet1fl/fl Tet2fl/fl Tet3fl/fl Rosatd-Tomato retrogenic mice were generated. Briefly, LSK cells were isolated from Tet1fl/fl Tet2fl/fl Tet3fl/fl Rosatd-Tomato mice, activated in vitro, transduced with CreER-expressing retrovirus, and then injected intravenously into lethally irradiated (10.5 Gy) CD45.1+CD45.2+ B6 mice. These recipient mice were treated with neomycin (2 mg/ml) in drinking water for 3 weeks. Two months later, they received three doses of tamoxifen or oil intraperitoneally every other day. Mice were euthanized for analysis 2 weeks after the first tamoxifen injection.

## Human T cell isolation and culture
Human PBMCs were collected from donors in EDTA-containing tubes. Plasma was collected and stored at −80°C. Red blood cells were lysed, and mononuclear cells were stained for flow cytometric analysis. PBMCs were purified using Ficoll-Paque, and CD4 Tn cells were enriched with the EasySep Human CD4 T Cell Enrichment Kits (STEMCELL) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. CD4 Tn cells were cultured with plate-bound anti-CD3 (5 μg/ml) and anti-CD28 antibodies (1 μg/ml) in XVIVO-15 medium supplemented with recombinant human IL-2 (100 U/ml) for 3 days and then transferred to a new plate for another 3 days before analysis by flow cytometry for IFN-γ production.

## CRISPR deletion of SLC23A2 in human CD4 naive T cells
CRISPR deletion of SLC23A2 in human CD4 Tn cells was conducted according to a reported method ( ). Briefly, 1 × 106 human CD4 Tn cells sorted from PBMC by CD4+CD45RA+CD45RO−CCR7+ were rested in XVIVO-15 medium for 4 hours. sgRNA and Cas9 protein (RNP) complexes were prepared by incubating 1 μl of 100 μM sgRNA, 1 μl of 40 μM Cas9 protein, and 1 μl of H2O at room temperature for 10 min. The electroporation of Cas9-RNP into CD4 Tn cells were performed with Lonza 4D-nucleofector (Lonza) following the manufacturer’s protocols using program EH-100 in P3 primary cell medium. After electroporation, prewarmed XVIVO-15 medium was added and incubated for 30 min at 37°C. Cells were subsequently cultured in XVIVO-15 medium supplemented with recombinant human IL-7 (rhIL-7) (2 ng/ml) and rhIL-15 (2 ng/ml; BRB Preclinical Biologics Repository, NCI). Medium was replenished with fresh one every 2 days for 3 weeks. Flow cytometric analysis and helper T cell polarization assays were then conducted.

## Generation of mixed bone marrow chimeric mice
Mixed bone marrow chimeric mice were generated as previously described, with a few minor modifications ( ). Briefly, bone marrow cells were harvested from Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre, Slc23a2fl/fl, Tet2fl/fl Tet3fl/fl Cd4Cre, or Tet2fl/fl Tet3fl/fl donor mice and cotransferred at a 1:1 ratio with bone marrow cells from CD45.1+CD45.2− WT B6 mice into lethally irradiated (10.5 Gy) CD45.1+CD45.2+ WT or Cd1d−/− recipients. These mice were then administered neomycin (2 mg/ml) in drinking water for 3 weeks and analyzed 10 to 12 weeks after bone marrow transfer.

## Acute Slc23a2 deletion in vivo
CD4 Tn cells were enriched from spleen and lymph nodes of Slc23a2fl/fl RosaCreER (CD45.2+) mice. Two million CD4 Tn cells were transferred into each CD45.1+CD45.2+ B6 mouse. Two doses of Tam or oil were then injected intraperitoneally on consecutive days. Cells were recovered for analysis 7 days later.

## T cell transfer colitis and experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis
T cell transfer colitis was conducted according to the standard protocols ( , ). Briefly, CD4 Tn cells (CD4+CD25−CD44−CD62L+CD45RBhi) were sorted from the spleen and lymph node (LN) of Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre or Slc23a2fl/fl mice. A total of 0.4 × 106 cells were transferred to each Rag1−/− mouse via intraperitoneal injection. Mice were weighed every 2 to 3 days and monitored for signs of disease. To rescue viability, CD4 Tn cells were transduced with retroviral Bcl2 or vector. A total of 2.0 × 106 transduced cells were sorted and transferred into each Rag1−/− recipient mouse to monitor body weight and disease.
Experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis (EAE) was performed according to standard protocols ( ). Briefly, Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre and Slc23a2fl/fl mice were immunized subcutaneously at two sites with a total volume of 100 μl of emulsified 100 μg of myelin oligodendrocyte glycoprotein peptide 35–55 (MOG35–55) in complete Freund’s adjuvant. Each mouse then received two intraperitoneal injections of 200 ng of pertussis toxin at the time of immunization and 48 hours later. Mice were closely monitored daily for clinical signs and changes in body weight. The severity of disease was scored on a scale from 0 to 10, as follows: 0, no overt signs of disease; 1, a partially limp tail; 2, a paralyzed tail; 3, hind limb paresis and uncoordinated movement; 4, paralysis of one hind limb; 5, paralysis of both hind limbs; 6, paralysis of hind limbs and weakness in forelimbs; 7, paralysis of hind limbs and one forelimb; 8, paralysis of hind limbs and both forelimbs; 9, moribund state; and 10, death.

## In vivo CRISPR screening
A retroviral vector (pSIR-hU6-dsRed) previously described ( ) was used to construct an sgRNA library against SLC family members (table S1). A total of 180 SLC genes expressed by murine T cells and 12 positive control genes were included, with 4 sgRNAs designed per gene. In addition, 48 NTC sgRNAs were introduced as negative controls. A pool of 75-nt single-stranded DNA oligos was synthesized (Twist) and ligated to the pSIR-hU6-dsRed vector using a Gibson assembly and cloning kit. The assembled DNA was electroporated into electrocompetent E. coli, then selected, and propagated on LB plates containing ampicillin. Plasmids were extracted, and library diversity was validated by PCR amplification followed by high-throughput sequencing.
Twelve weeks after reconstitution of the immune system, spleens and LNs from recipient mice were harvested, T cells were enriched, and donor-derived CD4 Tn cells (CD45.2+CD45.1−CD4+CD25−CD44loCD62Lhi) and CD4 Te cells (CD45.2+CD45.1−CD4+CD25−CD44hiCD62Llo) were fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS)–sorted. To reduce clonal effects, CD4 Tn and CD4 Te cells from 25 recipient mice were pooled as one sample for downstream PCR amplification and sequencing. Genomic DNA was extracted from the sorted T cells, and sgRNA sequences were retrieved by PCR amplification, followed by high-throughput sequencing according to published protocols ( ). Approximately 20 million 100–base pair paired-end reads per sample were obtained. The MAGeCk algorithm ( ) was used for a paired comparison of sgRNA representations in CD4 Tn and CD4 Te cells.

## Lymphocyte isolation
Lymphocyte isolation for flow cytometric analysis was performed according to standard methods with minor modifications. Briefly, thymus, spleen, and LNs were dissected after euthanasia, ground, and passed through a 100-μm nylon mesh. To isolate lymphocytes from the central nervous system (i.e., brain and spinal cord), tissues were collected after thorough phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) perfusion, cut into small fragments, and digested with collagenase IV (1 mg/ml) and deoxyribonuclease (DNase) I (0.25 mg/ml) in RPMI 1640 supplemented with 10% newborn calf serum and 15 mM Hepes, at 250 rpm for 30 min at 37°C. Digested samples were passed through a 100-μm nylon mesh, and lymphocytes were separated via centrifugation using a 40 to 70% Percoll gradient.
Lymphocyte isolation from intestines was performed as previously described, with minor modifications ( ). Briefly, the intestines were resected, cleaned of residual fat tissues, cut open longitudinally, and washed in ice-cold PBS. The tissues were then incubated in PBS containing 10% newborn calf serum and 5 mM EDTA for 20 min with shaking at 37°C. The supernatant was discarded, and the remaining tissue was washed with RPMI 1640 and incubated at 37°C for 20 min with shaking in RPMI 1640 with 10% newborn calf serum, collagenase IV (1 mg/ml), and DNase I (0.25 mg/ml). After digestion, the supernatant was passed through a 100-μm nylon mesh, and lymphocytes were enriched by 40 to 70% Percoll centrifugation.

## Flow cytometric analysis
Flow cytometric assays were conducted according to standard protocols with minor modifications ( ). To stain surface markers, suspended single cells were treated with a fixable viability dye and incubated with indicated antibodies for 15 min at 4°C in PBS with 2% bovine serum albumin (BSA). To stain transcription factors, cells were fixed and permeabilized with a Foxp3/Transcription Factor Staining Buffer Set following surface staining. Cells were then stained with antibodies against nuclear proteins. To assess cytokine production, cells were cultured in complete RPMI 1640 [supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum, 2 mM l-glutamine, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, 1% nonessential amino acids, 10 mM Hepes, 20 μM 2-mercaptoethanol, penicillin (100 U/ml), and streptomycin (100 mg/ml)] and stimulated in vitro with phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate (PMA; 50 ng/ml) and ionomycin (0.5 μg/ml) in the presence of 2 μM monensin at 37°C for 4 hours. Cells were then stained with antibodies against surface markers, fixed, permeabilized using an intracellular staining kit (BD Biosciences), and stained with cytokine-specific antibodies. Data were collected using a flow cytometer (LSRFortessa or LSR II, BD Biosciences) and analyzed with FlowJo (V10, BD Biosciences). Cells from EAE mice were cultured in complete RPMI 1640 with MOG35–55 peptide (30 μg/ml) and 2 μM monensin at 37°C for 4 hours before surface marker and cytokine staining.
To assay puromycin incorporation, ~2 × 105 CD4 Tn cells from Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre and Slc23a2fl/fl mice were rested at 37°C for 30 min. Puromycin was added at 10 ng/ml into the medium. After 15 min of incubation, intracellular staining was performed with antipuromycin antibodies using Foxp3/transcription factor staining buffer set.
To assess nuclear NFAT1, 5 × 105 CD4 Tn cells from Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre and Slc23a2fl/fl mice were incubated with mock, 100 μM NAC, or 900 μM H2O2 for 30 min. Nuclei were isolated and stained with anti-NFAT1 antibodies using Foxp3/transcription factor staining buffer set, followed by flow cytometric analysis.

## Calcium flux analysis
CD4 Tn cells were enriched from Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre and Slc23a2fl/fl mice. Fluo-8 staining was performed following the manufacturer’s instructions. After staining, cells were resuspended in Ringer’s solution and incubated with biotinylated anti-CD3 and anti-CD28 antibodies on ice. After baseline recording, cells were treated with streptavidin to crosslink antibodies and then with calcium (2 mM) to measure flux. Ionomycin was used as a positive control.

## Quantification of ROS
CD4 Tn cells were sorted from Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre and Slc23a2fl/fl mice and rested at 37°C for 2 hours in complete RPMI1640. ROS and mitochondrial ROS were measured following the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, 1 μM CellROX Green or 1 μM MitoSOX Red was added to the Hanks’ balanced salt solution medium and incubated at 37°C for 1 hour. During the last 15 min, 0.2 μl of 5 μM SYTOX Red Dead Cell Stain solution was added per 200 μl of reaction volume. Samples were analyzed immediately by flow cytometry.

## T cell differentiation in vitro
In vitro T cell differentiation assays were performed according to standard protocols ( , ). Briefly, to assess T cell differentiation in neutral (TH0) conditions upon activation by TCR agonists and costimulation, CD4 Tn cells were cultured in complete RPMI 1640 and stimulated with plate-bound anti-CD3 and anti-CD28 antibodies (each 1 μg/ml) in the presence of IL-2 (100 U/ml). Cells were transduced with retroviral sgRNAs or overexpression retrovirus 24 hours after activation and then cultured in indicated conditions until analysis. To evaluate T cell differentiation in TH1, TH2, TH17, or Treg cell conditions, CD4 Tn cells were cultured in complete RPMI 1640 and stimulated with plate-bound anti-CD3 and anti-CD28 antibodies (each 1 μg/ml for TH0, TH1, and Treg cell conditions) in the presence of TH1 [murine recombinant IL-12 (5 ng/ml), murine recombinant IFN-γ (10 ng/ml), and anti–IL-4 neutralization antibodies [11B11] (10 μg/ml)], TH2 [2 μg/ml each of plate-bound anti-CD3 and anti-CD28 antibodies, lethally irradiated T cell–depleted splenocytes as antigen-presenting cells, murine recombinant IL-4 (10 ng/ml), IL-2 (20 U/ml), anti–IL-12 neutralization antibodies [C17.8] (10 μg/ml), and anti–IFN-γ neutralization antibodies [XMG1.2] (10 μg/ml)], TH17 [each of plate-bound anti-CD3 and anti-CD28 antibodies (2 μg/ml), human recombinant transforming growth factor–β (TGF-β; 1 ng/ml), murine recombinant IL-6 (20 ng/ml), and anti–IFN-γ neutralization antibodies (10 μg/ml)], and Treg cell [IL-2 (100 U/ml) and human recombinant TGF-β (1 ng/ml)] polarizing cues for 3 days. Cells were then split and cultured in fresh complete RPMI 1640 supplemented with the same inducing cytokines and antibodies for three additional days. To test the effect of ROS on IFN-γ production in TH0 conditions, 100 μM AA2P, 100 μM GSH, 100 μM NAC, or 900 μM H2O2 was added to the medium. Last, cells were stained with a viability dye and antibodies against Foxp3, T-bet, GATA3, RORγt, IFN-γ, IL-13, or IL-17A according to standard protocols for flow cytometric analysis.

## NP-KLH immunization
NP-KLH immunization and antibody induction were conducted according to standard protocols ( ). Briefly, each mouse was injected intraperitoneally with 200 μl of emulsified NP-KLH (1 mg/ml) in alum adjuvant at a 1:1 ratio (100 μl each). Peripheral blood was collected 0, 7, 14, 21, 28, 35, and 42 days postimmunization. Serum samples were separated and stored at −80°C. To assess the effect of aging, CD4 Tn cells enriched from 2-month-old and 18-month-old B6 mice were transduced with vector control or retroviral Slc23a2 and then transferred into Tcrb−/–Tcrd−/− recipients, followed by NP-KLH immunization.

## GP61-KLH immunization
GP61-KLH immunization was conducted according to standard protocols ( ). Briefly, each mouse received an intraperitoneal injection of 200 μl of emulsified GP61 (WT)-KLH or GP61 (V71S)-KLH (1 ng/μl) in CFA at a 1:1 ratio (100 μl each). A total of 1 × 106 transduced RosaCas9 SMARTA CD4 T cells were transferred into each recipient mouse with a 1:1 ratio of sgNTC versus sgSlc23a2 or sgTet2. Seven days later, splenocytes were analyzed by flow cytometry.

## LCMV infection
CD4 Tn cells were isolated from RosaCas9 SMARTA mice and transduced with retroviral sgSlc23a2, sgTet2, or sgNTC. Cells were cultured with IL-2 (20 U/ml) for 3 days and then with mouse IL-7 (10 ng/ml) for 1 day. Transduced cells were sorted and transferred intravenously into recipient mice (2 × 106 each) followed by LCMV-Armstrong infection. Seven days later, splenocytes were analyzed by flow cytometry.

## Histopathology, immunohistochemistry, and immunofluorescence staining
Histopathology and immunohistochemistry were conducted following standard protocols ( ). Briefly, for routine histopathology, tissue samples were fixed in 10% neutral-buffered formalin and processed for hematoxylin and eosin staining. Stained sections were scored to assess the severity of tissue inflammation.
To evaluate the reactivity of autoantibodies against nuclear proteins, HEp-2 cells were fixed in 1% formaldehyde for 10 min and incubated with sera from Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre and Slc23a2fl/fl mice. Cells were then stained with fluorophore-conjugated secondary antibodies against murine IgM or IgG. Images were captured with a compound microscope under the same settings. Median fluorescence intensities (MFI) of IgM or IgG were quantified using SlideBook software (V5.0, Intelligent Imaging Innovations).
To assess the organ or tissue reactivities of autoantibodies, sections from Rag1−/− mice were prepared and incubated with sera from Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre and Slc23a2fl/fl mice according to published protocols ( ). Briefly, following antigen retrieval, the sections were treated with endogenous biotin-blocking kits and incubated with serum (1:50 dilution) overnight at 4°C. The sections were then washed with PBS and stained with biotinylated goat antibodies against mouse IgG, IgM, and IgA for 1 hour at room temperature. Detection was performed with streptavidin-horseradish peroxidase using Tyramide Alexa Fluor 594. The slides were counterstained with DAPI for 5 min and scanned with an Axio Scan.Z1 slide scanner (Zeiss). Scanned images were analyzed using QuPath (GitHub) and ImageJ (NIH).
Similar protocols were used to assess C1q. Kidney sections from Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre and Slc23a2fl/fl mice were prepared. After antigen retrieval, tissues were stained with rabbit anti-C1q antibodies, followed by biotinylated goat anti-rabbit IgG. Detection was then carried out using a tyramide signal amplification kit.

## Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay
Sera were collected from experimental mice, and antibody isotyping 7-plex kits were used according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Samples were quantified using a Luminex Milliplex analyzer (Millipore). Anti-dsDNA and anti-nuclear antibodies were quantified using kits according to the manufacturer’s instructions. To measure serum NP-specific antibodies, 96-well plates were precoated with NP-BSA (15 μg/ml) in PBS at 4°C overnight, blocked with 5% BSA in PBS at room temperature for 2 hours, and then incubated with diluted sera for another 2 hours. The plates were subsequently stained with horseradish peroxidase (HRP)–conjugated anti-mouse IgG1 (0.5 μg/ml) or HRP-conjugated anti-mouse IgM (0.5 μg/ml) for 1 hour. After sufficient washing with 0.1% Tween 20 in PBS, the plates were developed with TMB substrate for 1 to 5 min. The reaction was terminated with 1 M sulfuric acid, and the results were measured using a VersaMax microplate reader (Molecular Devices). To measure urinary albumin, urine was collected from experimental mice and quantified using a mouse albumin detection kit according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Samples were measured with a VersaMax microplate reader. To measure the cytokines produced by CD4 Tn cells after activation, CD4 Tn cells were sorted from Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre, Slc23a2fl/fl, and young and aged mice and cultured in TH0 conditions. Supernatant was collected 3 days later to quantify cytokines using the MILLIPLEX Mouse Cytokine Magnetic Kit according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

## Ascorbate quantification
T cells were pelleted by centrifugation and washed with prechilled 0.9% NaCl. Hydrophilic metabolites were extracted using a 50:50 mixture of water (H2O) and acetonitrile (ACN) containing 6 ng of ASC internal standard [13C]-ASC and 50 pmol of Warfarin. Extraction was carried out in an Omni Bead Ruptor Elite homogenizer (PerkinElmer) for 25 s at the highest speed. Samples were centrifuged at 20,000g at 4°C, and supernatants were collected and dried by lyophilization. Dried extracts were resuspended in 95:5 H2O/ACN for targeted analysis of ascorbate by mass spectrometry (LC/TQ-MRM). An ACQUITY Premier UPLC System (Waters) was used for LC separation with a linear gradient: 0 to 0.75 min 0% B; 0.75 to 2 min 0 to 100% B; 2 to 3.7 min 100% B; 3.7 to 4 min 100 to 0% B; 4 to 5 min 0% B. Mobile phase A consisted of 97:3 H2O/ACN supplemented with 10 mM ammonium acetate and 10 mM ammonium hydroxide. Mobile phase B consisted of ACN. The BEH C18 AX column (50 mm by 2.1 mm, 2.5 μm; Waters) was maintained at 40°C. The flow rate was set to 350 μl/min, and the injection volume was 5 μl. All LC/MS solvents and reagents were of the highest purity (Thermo Fisher Scientific). A Xevo TQ-XS Triple Quadrupole Mass Spectrometer (TQ-XS, Waters) equipped with a UniSpray ion source served as the detector, operating in negative-ion mode using the multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) technique.
The MRM conditions were conFig.d to capture a minimum of 15 points per peak with automatic dwell times. Source operating conditions included: impact voltage at 1.0 kV, cone voltage at 8 V, desolvation temperature at 600°C, desolvation gas flow at 1000 liter/hour, cone gas flow at 150 liter/hour, nebulizer at 7.0 bar, and source temperature at 150°C. Authentic standards (Sigma and Cambridge Isotope) were used to establish MRM and chromatographic conditions. Details of the monitored parent/daughter ions, fragmentation collision energy (CE), and retention-time windows for all analytes are listed in table S7. Acquired MRM data were processed using Skyline 21.2 (MacCoss Lab Software). ASC quantifications were performed by comparing peak areas of nonlabeled ASC to [13C]-ASC in each sample, adjusted for cell numbers, and and sample fractions were used.

## scRNA-seq, TCR V(D)J clonotype profiling, and data analysis
Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) and TCR V(D)J clonotype profiling were conducted as we previously described ( ). Bulk CD45.1−CD45.2+TCRb+ cells were FACS-sorted from pooled spleens and LNs of mixed bone marrow chimeric mice derived from Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre and Slc23a2fl/fl donors. Sequencing reads were mapped to the mouse genome using CellRanger (v6.0.2, 10x Genomics) with references mm10-2020-A and vdj-GRCm38-alts-ensembl-3.1.0 for gene expression and TCR or V(D)J clonotype analyses, respectively. After filtering out low-quality cells, gene expression data from all cells were used to identify transcription clusters using Seurat R (v4.3) ( ). Transcriptional clusters were combined into major cell subsets through manual annotation. Cell-type identification, differential gene expression, and TCR clonotype assessment were conducted as previously described ( ). Marker genes were identified using the Seurat FindVariableFeatures function. The scaled expression of the top 20 differentially expressed genes per cluster was used to create heatmaps. Cells with identical TCR clonotypes were counted across clusters. TCR clonotypes were overlaid with Seurat clusters, and cells with varying copy numbers were color-coded and presented.

## Bulk RNA-seq and data analysis
CD4 Tn cells were FACS-sorted for bulk RNA-seq from pooled spleens and LNs of experimental mice. To determine gene expression changes before and after TCR/costimulations, CD4 Tn cells were cultured in complete RPMI1640 for 3 hours with or without plate-bound anti-CD3 and anti-CD28 antibodies. Total RNA was isolated and sequenced following standard protocols ( ). Sequencing reads were trimmed using Trim Galore (v0.5.0) with default settings and mapped to the mouse mm10 assembly with STAR ( ). Differentially expressed genes were identified using trimmed mean of M values (TMM) normalization factors and raw counts in a Limma-voom analysis with the “voom,” “lmFit,” and “eBayes” functions from the limma (v3.42.2) R package ( ). Genes differentially expressed between comparison groups were identified by fold changes and adjusted P values of less than 0.05. All statistical analyses were conducted in the R environment.

## Bisulfite sequencing and data analysis
CD4 Tn cells were sorted from the pooled spleens and LNs of mixed bone marrow chimeric mice derived from Slc23a2fl/fl Cd4Cre or Slc23a2fl/fl donors, Gulo−/− mice with or without ASC deprivation, and Tet1fl/fl Tet2fl/fl Tet3fl/fl CreER retrogenic mice following tamoxifen or oil treatment. All cells were from male donors to avoid X chromosome random inactivation in females. Genomic DNA extraction, bisulfite conversion, high-throughput sequencing (40× coverage), and data analysis were conducted as previously described ( ). Sequencing reads were mapped to the mouse genome mm10 using Bismark (v0.19.1), and DMRs were calculated with the DSS package ( ). Promoter upstream: the region extending up to 2 kb upstream of the transcription start site (TSS). Promoter downstream: the region extending up to 2 kb downstream of the TSS. Transcription end site (TES): the region spanning 2 kb upstream to 2 kb downstream of the TES. 5′ distal region: 2–50 kb upstream of the TSS. 3′ distal region: 2 to 50 kb downstream of the TES. Intergenic region: sequences located more than 50 kb upstream of the TSS or more than 50 kb downstream of the TES.

## CUT&Tag
CUT&Tag was performed according to published protocols with minor modifications ( , ). CUT&Tag was conducted according to the manufacturer’s instructions (EpiCypher). Nuclei from ~1 × 105 sorted cells were separated, attached to concanavalin A–coated magnetic beads, and then incubated with primary antibodies against H3K9me3, H3K36me3, H3K4me3, or H3K27me3 at 4°C overnight. The next day, samples were washed and incubated with secondary antibodies and CUTANA pAG-Tn5. After washing, the tagmentation buffer [20 mM Hepes (pH 7.5), 300 mM NaCl, 0.5 mM spermidine, 1x Roche cOmplete, EDTA-free Protease Inhibitor cocktail, and 10 mM MgCl2] was added to the tubes and rotated at 37°C for 1 hour. The samples were incubated at 58°C for 1 hour to release Cut&Tag DNA fragments. Fragmented DNA was then amplified by using Phusion High-Fidelity DNA polymerasefor 17 to 19 cycles with i5 and i7 sample index containing primers. Libraries were purified and size selected with SPRIselect beads at a 1.3 ratio, quantified by the TapeStation system (Agilent), and sequenced with paired-end 100 cycles on a NovaSeq 6000 sequencer (Illumina). Sequencing reads were processed and analyzed according to our described algorithms ( , ).

## CUT&RUN
Tcf1 and Tet2 CUT&RUN was conducted following published protocols ( , ) with a few modifications. Briefly, 5 × 105 CD4 Tn cells were attached to concanavalin A–coated magnetic beads, permeabilized, and incubated with primary antibodies against Tcf1 or Tet2 at 4°C overnight. The bead-cell mixture was washed three times with wash buffer, resuspended in protein A-MNase, and incubated at room temperature for 10 min. After washing, beads were resuspended in 50 μl digitonin wash buffer and chilled to 0°C for 5 min. Then, 1 μl of 100 mM CaCl2 was added with gentle vortexing. After incubation at 4°C for 2 hours with rotation, 33 μl of 2× STOP buffer was added. Beads were incubated at 37°C for 10 min and then placed on a magnet or 2 min. The clarified liquid was transferred to a new tube for phenol:chloroform extraction, followed by DNA precipitation with isopropanol. The sequencing library was prepared using the KAPA HyperPrep Kit according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

## ATAC sequencing
Assay for Transposase-Accessible Chromatin with high-throughput sequencing (ATAC-seq) was performed according to standard protocols ( ). Approximately 5 × 104 T cells were processed immediately after sorting. Libraries were prepared using Illumina Tagment DNA Enzyme and Buffer kits. After quantification and profiling with the TapeStation system (Agilent), libraries were sequenced with paired-end 100 cycles on a NovaSeq 6000 sequencer (Illumina). Sequencing reads were processed and analyzed using our published algorithms ( ).

## Differential peak analysis
The same algorithms were used for CUT&Tag, CUT&RUN, and ATAC-seq data analysis. Briefly, reproducible peaks were merged, and the number of overlapping fragments from paired reads was counted for each peak. Normalization was performed using the TMM method. The empirical Bayes method, implemented through the eBayes function from the limma R package ( ), was used to identify differential peaks. Peaks were annotated in this order: “Promoter.Up” within TSS −2 kb; “Promoter.Down” within TSS −2 kb; “Exonic” or “Intronic” within an exon or intron; “TES” within TES ±2 kb; “Distal5” or “Distal3” 2- to 50-kb upstream of TSS or 2- to 50-kb downstream of TES; “Intergenic” if not in previous categories. DNA motifs enriched at the differential peaks were analyzed with the HOMER algorithms (http://homer.ucsd.edu/homer).

## Bulk TCR repertoire profiling
CD45.2+CD4+ T cells were sorted from the spleens and LNs of mixed bone marrow chimeric mice derived from Tet2fl/fl Tet3fl/fl Cd4Cre and Tet2fl/fl Tet3fl/fl donors. Bulk TCR sequencing was performed as we previously described ( ).

## Western blotting
CD4 Tn cells were stimulated with crosslinked CD3 and CD28 antibody at the indicated time points. Cells were immediately lysed in 1x NuPAGE LDS sample buffer, and 2.5 × 105 cells were loaded per lane and separated by SDS-PAGE. Proteins were transferred to a polyvinylidene difluoride membrane (Millipore) and blotted with specific primary antibodies. HRP-linked secondary antibodies and SuperSignal Western Pico Plus Substrate were used for detection with the AI600 Imager workstation (GE Healthcare).

## Statistical analysis
Statistical tests were performed using GraphPad Prism 9 (GraphPad Software) or the R statistical environment. Data are presented as means ± SEMs if applicable. *P < 0.05, **P < 0.01, ***P < 0.001, and ****P < 0.0001.
