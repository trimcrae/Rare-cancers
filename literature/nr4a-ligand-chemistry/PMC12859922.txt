Despite substantial progress, the treatment of solid tumors remains challenging, with suboptimal clinical outcomes and significant unmet needs. To address these ongoing challenges, considerable efforts are being made to enhance the safety, efficacy, and overall applicability of CAR-T cells therapy. This review outlines the key tumor features that impede CAR-T efficacy, focusing on intrinsic tumor factors and the influence of the microenvironment. We then provide a comprehensive overview of the promising next-generation CAR-T designs and discuss the combinatorial approaches aimed at improving antigen recognition, functional adaptability, and antitumor response, thereby expanding the therapeutic impact of CAR-T cells in solid-tumor settings.

## Background
Immunotherapy has revolutionized the field of immuno-oncology in an unprecedented manner [ , ]. Inhibiting the PD-L1/PD-1 axis via immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) has become the standard-of-care treatment for an increasing number of cancers [ ]. Despite this progress, the response rate is far from satisfactory, highlighting the urgent need for new therapeutic strategies. In the recent years, T cells engineered to express chimeric antigen receptors (CARs) have gained particular attention given their success against hematological malignancies and autoimmune disorders [ – ].
CARs are synthetic receptors that enable T cells to target the tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) in a major histocompatibility complex (MHC)-independent manner [ ]. A typical CAR consists of an extracellular antigen-binding domain such as a single-chain variable fragment (scFv) or single-domain heavy chain (VHH), a hinge/transmembrane domain and intracellular costimulatory signaling domains for T cells activation [ , ]. Through iterative optimization and development, 7 CD19 or B cell mature antigen (BCMA)-targeted CAR-T cell products have been approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) [ ]. Nevertheless, a significant number of patients still experience primary resistance and relapse following therapy, primarily due to the antigen escape and dysfunction of CAR-T cells [ ].
Importantly, the efficacy of CAR-T cells in solid tumors is inherently constrained when compared to their efficacy in hematological malignancies and autoimmune disorders. This limitation is mainly related to the antigen heterogeneity and the suppressive nature of tumor microenvironment (TME) [ – ]. Successful tumor recognition relies on the expression of tumor-specific antigens, while effective tumor elimination requires not only efficient trafficking to the tumor site but also the ability to overcome the unfavorable effects of the TME on the T cells [ , ]. For solid tumors, the intratumoral heterogeneity (ITH), particularly antigen heterogeneity, along with secondary target downregulation and loss after treatment, can increase the risk of tumor relapse and treatment failure [ ]. Moreover, the immunosuppressive TME constructs a physical barrier, and its inhibitory signals and cells within the tumor bed collectively impede T-cell homing, infiltration and proliferation, while promoting the dysfunction of T cells with expression of inhibitory molecules and impaired functionality [ , ].
For specific tumors, a comprehensively evaluation of their molecular characteristics is crucial to optimize CAR-T cell design. For example, in the case of gliomas, overcoming the antigen heterogeneity is essential [ ]. In prostate cancer, the homing and efficacy of CAR-T cells against bone metastases must be prioritized, as the majority of advanced prostate cancer patients develop bone metastases [ , ]. In pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), blocking the abundant TGF-β signaling within the tumor is crucial for improving CAR-T cell effectiveness [ , ]. In triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), disrupting the physical barriers posed by a dense and stiff extracellular matrix (ECM) within the solid stroma is necessary to enable adequate CAR-T cell infiltration [ , ].
Instead of reviewing the molecular features of each tumor type, this work emphasizes the barriers encountered by CAR-T cell therapy in solid tumors and outlines the key tumor characteristics relevant to these challenges. Subsequently, we offer a comprehensive overview of promising next-generation CAR-T designs and discuss combinatorial strategies aimed at overcoming these obstacles and enhancing the efficacy of CAR-T cell therapy against solid tumors.

## Key tumor features that limit CAR-T cell therapeutic efficacy
The tumor-inherent characteristics that contribute to CAR-T treatment failure in solid tumors mainly include tumor cell-autonomous properties and immunosuppressive TME influences. Tumor-intrinsic factors primarily involve antigen heterogeneity and the expression of inhibitory molecules on tumor cells, such as PD-L1 and CD47. The negative influences of the TME include the formation of physical barriers that hinders T cell trafficking and infiltration. Additionally, suppressive signals and immunosuppressive cellular components in the tumor bed affect CAR-T cell expansion, survival, persistence and functionality (Fig.  ). 

## Tumor cell-intrinsic properties lead to antigen escape
The immune system plays a dual role in tumor evolution, not only identifying and eliminating nascent tumor cells but also sculpting tumor immunogenicity to facilitate tumor progression [ , ]. This process, also termed as cancer immunoediting, includes three phases: elimination, equilibrium, and escape [ ]. Immune evasion can be reinforced during the tumor formation. The immune system can eliminate some immunogenic subclones. However, the other tumor cells may gain survival advantages, such as the acquisition of driver mutations that promote aberrant proliferation [ ]. The robust selective pressure and influence of the TME result in genotypic diversity and the co-existence of distinct subclones within tumors, in summary defining the ITH [ , ]. Genetic features can exhibit considerable variability not only in the course of disease progression (temporal ITH), but also in the distinct sites of the same tumor (spatial ITH) [ ]. Notably, ITH is closely correlated with the cancer treatment outcome [ – ]. The dynamics of genetic or epigenetic factors, such as target downregulation or loss, secondary mutations and the upregulation of signaling pathways, altogether favor therapeutic resistance [ ].However, technologies to identify patients at risk for immune escape are not yet available [ ]. In the context of CAR-T therapy, antigen heterogeneity allows pre-existing antigen-negative tumor cells to evade CAR-T recognition, gaining survival advantage that drives therapeutic resistance [ ]. The precise mechanisms underlying antigen loss and target downregulation are still under investigation, involving factors such as lineage switching, alternative splicing, frameshift mutation, gene deletion, aberrant target antigen maturation and trafficking, and epitope masking [ , ].
Moreover, the expression of inhibitory receptors expression on tumor cells also promotes the tumor escape from antitumor immunity, such as PD-L1 and CD47. Among these pathways, the PD-1/PD-L1 axis represents one of the most significant immunosuppressive signaling pathways driving tumor immune evasion [ – ]. PD-L1 on the tumor cells can attenuate effector T cell responses through interactions with the PD-1 on T cells. The PD-L1 expression is driven by PDL1 gene amplification, aberrant activation of oncogenic signaling pathways and upregulation in response to inflammatory cytokines within the TME [ , ]. A first-in-human study of a single dose of EGFRvIII-directed CAR-T cells revealed that, compared to pre-infusion tumor specimens, post-CART infusion tumor samples presented markedly increased expression of PD-L1 [ ]. Moreover, phagocytosis checkpoints such as CD47, CD24, and GD2 are critical for escaping immune surveillance [ ]. CD47 is the most thoroughly studied marker and the tumor cells can delivery 'don't eat me' signal though CD47-SIRPα axis to resist macrophage phagocytosis [ ].

## Tumor microenvironment dampens CAR-T cells activity and persistence
The TME is a complex and heterogeneous ecosystem that plays decisive roles in tumor progression and therapeutic response [ ]. It provides metabolic support through nutrient supply, reduces the immune cell infiltration, contains abundant immunosuppressive factors and preferentially recruits leukocytes with immunosuppressive phenotype, collectively favoring the cellular metabolism toward cancer growth, metastasis and treatment resistance [ – ].
The extracellular matrix and stromal cells form a physical barrier that hinders the entry of immune cells into the tumor site [ , ]. Cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) are a dominant component of this physical barrier [ , ]. The composition and transcriptome alterations of CAFs are early events during tumor formation [ ]. CAFs can secrete immunomodulatory molecules such as SDF-1, IL-6 and TGF-β to facilitate the recruitment and activation of immune inhibitory cells [ ]. Moreover, CAFs drive the ECM deposition and remodeling, thereby limiting immune cell infiltration [ ]. They also upregulate the expression of immune checkpoint molecules such as PD-1, PD-L1 and cytotoxic T lymphocyte-associated protein-4 (CTLA4) in various immune cells to modulate antitumor T-cell responses [ ]. Furthermore, the dysregulation of adhesion molecules and mismatching of chemokine-chemokine receptors further limit the immune cell migration to tumor lesions [ , ].
Additionally, immunosuppressive cells within the TME suppress the immune response through direct cell–cell contact and secretion of immunosuppressive factors [ ]. Key components include regulatory T lymphocytes (Tregs), myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) and tumor-associated neutrophils (TANs) [ ]. Immunosuppressive signals, particularly TGF-β signaling, significantly impact the antitumor response [ ]. TGF-β functions as a tumor suppressor in the early stage to maintain immune homeostasis [ ]. However, with the genetic and epigenetic alterations, this role evolves to paradoxically promote tumor progression, serving as a major suppressor of the adaptive and innate immune responses [ ]. It controls adaptive immunity through directly promoting the expansion of Treg cells while hindering effector T cells functions and antigen-presenting dendritic cells activity. Furthermore, TGF-β regulates the innate immune system by inhibiting natural killer (NK) cells and regulating the macrophages and neutrophils. Moreover, TGF-β signaling contributes to the formation of an acidic TME by promoting glycolysis in cancer cells and CAFs, facilitating metabolic coupling between cancer and stromal cells [ , ].
More importantly, this hostile TME poses key challenges to CAR-T efficacy in solid tumors [ ]. The immunosuppressive TME blocks CAR-T cell homing and infiltration and diminishes the functional activity of CAR-T cells. Effective CAR-T cells infiltration into tumors involves multiple events, including the adhesion of T cells to endothelial cells followed by chemokine-chemokine receptor interactions, which regulate their extravasation into antigen-rich tissues. However, the dysregulated vasculature, chemokine-receptor networks and physical barrier collectively hinder T cell migration [ – ]. Although several CAR-T cells successfully localized in the tumor site, their function and proliferative potential can be dampened by the suppressive TME [ , ]. Furthermore, the suppressive immune cells are preferentially recruited into tumors, which further inhibits CAR-T cells infiltration and functionality [ , ]. Alongside external factors, intrinsic T cell factors, such as tonic signaling and immune checkpoint expression, also contribute to CAR-T cell dysfunction [ , – ]. Prolonging the persistence and function of CAR-T cells within tumors remains a critical and unsolved challenge.

## Overcoming antigen escape
In principle, the ideal tumor-associated antigens should have sufficient coverage and high specificity to guarantee both efficacy and safety [ ]. Unlike the hematological malignancies, where exist one or multiple uniformly and highly specific expressed antigen on tumor surface, the solid tumors exhibit greater heterogeneity in target antigen expression [ , ]. Notably, most of the tumor antigens, for both hematological and solid tumors, are also expressed at low levels in healthy cells, which increases the off-target toxicity of CAR-T therapy [ , ].
Although encouraging results have been obtained from clinical trials in hematological tumors, disease relapse after CAR-T cells infusion is still common. Reports indicated that up to 50% of patients with pre-B cell lymphoblastic leukemia by 12 months experience disease relapse following anti-CD19 or anti-CD22 CAR-T cell therapy [ – ]. The main common mechanisms include the target downregulation or loss driven by the long-term treatment pressure and T cells dysfunction [ ]. Moreover, the low antigen abundance on tumor cells also poses a challenge for CAR-T therapy. A lower antigen abundance is insufficient for maximal activation of CAR-T cells [ ]. According to a preclinical study, the antitumor efficacy of CAR-T cells targeting anaplastic lymphoma kinase (ALK) is more limited due to the ALK target density. Similar findings were observed with targets CD20 and B7-H3, highlighting the critical role of antigen density in regulating CAR-T cells function [ , ]. Except for the antigen expression, the trogocytosis also contributes to the antigen escape [ , ]. In this section, we will review the relevant clinical or preclinical data and discuss promising approaches to overcome this key barrier (Fig.  ). 

## Broadening the target spectrum
A potential strategy involves broadening the target spectrum to enable more tumor cells could be recognized by CAR-T cells. This is supported by the engineering of dual-targeting CAR-T cells, designing CAR-T cells to secrete bispecific T cell engagers (BiTEs) that target another TAA or administrating two single-targeted CAR-T products [ – ].
Unlike the AND and AND-NOT logic-gated CAR-T cells which require the copresence of two different antigens to trigger the full activation of T cells, the OR-gate logic can be activated by either antigen input, thus preventing the antigen relapse caused by the loss of the targeted epitope [ , ]. OR-gate logic CARs are engineered by constitutively co-expressing two independent CARs or by expressing a single bivalent CAR comprising tandem binding domain (TanCAR) [ – ]. The antigen selection for OR-gated CARs is more restricted to prevent the underlying toxicity to normal cells. Moreover, the efficacy of TanCAR is closely related to the scFv order, hinge domain, and linker length. These components closely influence the efficient recognition of both antigens [ ]. The efficacy and safety of dual-targeting CAR-T cells have been reported, especially in hematological malignancies. For example, data from a clinical trial demonstrated that bispecific tandem CD19/CD22 CAR-T cells exhibit potent efficacy in treating chemotherapy-resistant or refractory ALL, with 100% of patients achieving remission with no minimal residual disease (MRD) [ ]. Interestingly, biepitope-targeting CAR-T cells targeting one tumor antigen can reduce the risk of antigen escape through conferring avidity [ , ]. Cilta-cel, a tandem dual epitope-binding CAR-T cell therapy directed against BCMA, showed unprecedented clinical activity in a phase 1b/2 study [ ]. The overall response rate was 97% and 67% achieved complete response (CR) among 97 patients. In solid tumors, dual-targeting CAR-T cells also demonstrate favorable antitumor activity and can prevent antigen escape, as supported by preclinical data. For example, bicistronic CARs targeting both FGFR4 and CD276 show efficacy in preventing immune escapes and exhibit potent antitumor effects in models of childhood rhabdomyosarcoma [ ]. Simultaneous targeting of EpCAM and ICAM-1 also enhances cytotoxicity against heterogeneous tumor cells and decreases the tumor recurrence rate [ ].
T cell-redirecting antibodies, which can bind to the tumor antigen and engage with CD3 on the T cell receptor, have emerged as a promising option for cancer treatment [ ]. Recent research revealed that engineering CAR-T cells to secrete bispecific T cell engager (BiTE) targeting another TAA also possess potential to circumvent antigen escape [ , – ]. Choi et al. constructed CAR.BiTE cells through integrating anti-EGFRvIII CAR-T cells with a BiTE targeting wild-type EGFR. The CAR.BiTE cells can avoid the escape of EGFRvIII-negative, EGFR-positive glioblastoma (GBM) cells to achieve a favorable treatment response [ ]. Engineered anti-IL10R CAR-T cells to release anti-CD33 bispecific antibodies also address the barrier of immune escape due to the heterogeneous expression of IL10R and CD33 in myeloid leukemia cells [ ]. In addition to the secretion of antibodies to redirect T cells, bispecific killer cell engager (BiKE)-secreting CAR-T cells to redirect nature killer cells also leads to improved antitumor activity [ ]. Generating CAR-T cells and BiKEs to target distinct antigens successfully suppressed the tumor cells with heterogeneous antigen expression. In vivo, the dual-specificity of anti-CD19 CAR-T cells and EGFR-BiKE-engaged NK cells could induce full clearance of subcutaneous tumors.
The administration of two single-targeted CAR-T products also significantly reduces the risk of tumor relapse caused by antigen escape [ ]. But relevant research reports are mainly focus on the field of hematological malignancies. For example, sequential CD19- and CD22-trageting CAR-T cells induced long-term remission by preventing antigen escape in patients with relapsed/refractory (r/r) B-ALL [ ]. The median interval between the 2 cycles of infusion was 1.65 months, and only 3 patients underwent relapse among 20 patients. Notably, a preclinical study revealed that dual-targeting CD19/CD22 was more effective than sequential infusion. Administering anti-CD19 CAR-T cells to mice with NALM6 cells, followed by a subsequent infusion of anti-CD22 CAR-T cells, proved ineffective due to decreased CD22 expression and limited expansion potential [ ]. Co-administration of CD19- and CD22-targeted CAR-T cells also showed effectiveness and reduced the risk of antigen loss. Moreover, compared with sequential therapy, it avoided repeated lymphodepleting chemotherapy which may diminish the presence of CD19 CAR-T cells in the blood [ ].

## Mitigating the effects of trogocytosis
Trogocytosis, a dynamic process that involving the intercellular material transfer, was originally observed at the immunological synapse between T cells and antigen presenting cells (APCs) [ ]. Recent research found the trogocytosis also exists between the CAR-T cells and tumor cells, leading to the tumor antigen escape and fratricide effects of CAR-T cells [ , ]. In brief, the tumor cells can obtain CAR molecules from CAR-T cells and subsequently cause the CAR-T cells dysfunction and tumor cell antigen-masking. Similarly, the CAR-T cells also can acquire tumor antigens, thereby decreasing the target density on tumor cells and the fratricide effects of CAR-T cells. Notably, trogocytic target acquisition may be a general feature of CAR-T cells that are not limited to a specific antigen [ ]. Hamieh M and colleagues reported the transfer of the CD19 protein from CD19 positive tumor cells to anti-CD19 CAR-T cells. Moreover, CAR-induced trogocytosis was also observed with all tested targets in their study, including CD22, BCMA and mesothelin (MSLN) [ ]. Another study further confirmed that CAR molecules can be transferred to tumor cells and demonstrated that the CAR molecule trogocytosis primarily depends on antigen density, effector-to-target ratio and CAR sensitivity [ ]. Hence, trogocytosis-induced CAR or antigen transfer also could be offset by optimizing CAR sensitivity to align with the tumor antigen density [ , ]. Treating highly expressed antigens with low-sensitivity CAR-T cells and using high sensitivity CAR-T cells to target modestly expressed antigens could mitigate trogocytosis and improve treatment outcomes [ ]. Moreover, the tumor cell cholesterol metabolism is closely related to this process. Treating tumor cells with atorvastatin prior to CAR-T treatment could inhibit the CAR molecule transfer and subsequently promote CAR-T cell performance. The co-stimulatory domain of CAR-T cells is also related to the trogocytosis effects. Although both the CD28- and 4-1BB-based anti-MSLN CAR-T cells have shown evidence of tumor antigen transfer, there was significantly more trogocytozed MSLN antigen on the 4-1BB based CAR-T cells [ ]. Furthermore, engineered CAR-T cells with Serpin B9, a major granzyme B inhibitor, showed decreased fratricide and improved efficacy in preclinical models [ ]. CAR-T cell fratricide is also associated with the small extracellular vesicles (sEVs)-mediated tumor antigen transfer. But co-transfer of Serpin B9 and together with tumor antigens could protect CAR-T cells against fratricide [ ].
Notably, while trogocytosis between T cells and tumor cells can undermine antitumor efficacy, the exchange of CAR molecules between T cells by trogocytosis can enhance their cytotoxic potency. Additionally, the transmembrane domains of CARs play a regulatory role in the transfer of CAR molecules [ ].

## Enhancing the recognition for antigens with low abundance
Each of component of a CAR can affect its antigen sensitivity [ – ]. For example, in the context of CD33 CAR-T cells, targeting membrane-proximal CD33 epitopes with high affinity showed superior tumor control in low antigen density models [ ]. Moreover, CARs incorporating a CD28 hinge-transmembrane (H/T) region demonstrated a more stable and efficient immunological synapse, thus revealing an enhanced capacity to recognize antigens with low abundance [ ]. Additionally, CARs with CD28 costimulatory domains exhibited increased activity compared with CARs with 4-1BB against low antigen density target cells [ , ]. But the CD28 costimulatory domain may reduce the CAR-T cells persistence compared with the 4-1BB endodomain [ ]. A previous study demonstrated that overexpressing the transcription factor c-Jun in CAR-T cells could promote the memory formation, encounter the exhaustion and enhance the proliferative potency [ ]. In addition, c-Jun overexpression combined with CD28 transmembrane and costimulation decreased the CPC2 CAR antigen density threshold and improved the CAR efficacy against tumor cells with low antigen density [ ].
Moreover, combining a CAR and a chimeric costimulatory/switch receptor (CCR/CSR) could also prevent relapse due to the low antigen density [ , ]. CSR alone is not efficient at mediating T cells activation because it lacks the CD3ζ chain. Hence, the selection of the second targeted antigen is not strictly limited by tumor specificity requirements. GD2 and B7-H3 dual targeting with split costimulatory signaling and shared CD3ζ chain CAR-T cells demonstrated durable antitumor effects and prevented tumor escape due to low antigen density in a neuroblastoma model [ ]. Additionally, a CD38-binding CCR enhanced the cytotoxicity and persistence of CAR-T cells by increasing their functional binding avidity, thus preventing relapses of tumor with low target-antigen density [ ].
Recently, Pham-Danis et al. reported that the poor CAR-T cell responses to antigen-low tumor cells are related to the inefficient phosphorylation of the linker for the activation of T cells (LAT) with impaired downstream signaling. Restoration of LAT activation could restore CAR-T cell sensitivity to antigen-low tumors [ ]. To address the inefficient LAT phosphorylation upon antigen-low stimulation, they designed a bicistronic construct consisting of the anti-CD22 CAR co-expressed with a CD19-targeted LAT-activating CAR with the LAT intracellular domain, termed 22 × 19 ALA-CART. Each component CAR of the ALA-CART construct could drive effective responses against tumor cells. But the LAT CAR engagement contributed to the optimal in vivo clearance of antigen-low leukemia cells.

## Modulating the inhibitory or stimulatory signals of immune checkpoints
Among immune checkpoint-mediated pathways, the PD-1/PD-L1 axis is the best characterized pathway. The blockade of the PD-1/PD-L1 axis via ICIs has yielded encouraging results in the clinic. In the field of CAR-T therapy, both genetically engineered disruption of the PD-1 pathway into CAR-T cells and combination with established immune-checkpoint inhibitors have been shown to enhance the antitumor response in preclinical and clinical studies. In this section, we will focus on introduce how to harness immune checkpoints, particularly PD-L1/PD-1, to enhance the function of CAR-T cells (Fig.  ). 

## Converting inhibitory signals into stimulatory signals
The cancer cells can exploit the PD-1/PD-L1 axis to impair T cells function and guide immune escape [ , ]. CAR-T cells can be engineered to convert this inhibitory signal into a stimulatory one, thereby relieving the negative effects on T cells and enhancing the antitumor response.
For example, Ma et al. reported that the function of traditional second generation anti-HER2 CAR-T cells was impaired in the malignant pleural effusion (MPE) and malignant ascites (MA). Based on the PD-L1 overexpression on freshly isolated MPE/MA cells, they constructed a PD-L1 CSR to switch the inhibitory signal into an additional 4-1BB signal. They demonstrated that PD-L1 CSR with 4-1BB engagement enhances the cytotoxicity and fitness of anti-HER2 CAR-T cells and leads to durable eradication of pleural and peritoneal metastatic tumors [ ]. However, PD-L1 is also expressed on the activated CAR-T cells. The influence of trans-recognition between CSRs and PD-L1 in activated CAR-T cells on the antitumor efficacy remains unknown [ , ]. To explore this issue, Qin and colleagues constructed a PD-L1 CSR containing CD28(CARP) and revealed that the T cells overexpressing CARP could improve the antitumor function and promote memory formation of anti-MSLC CAR-T cells via the CD70-CD27 axis [ ].
Switching effects could also be exploited to mitigate the PD-1 signals on the T cells [ ]. PD-L1/PD1 pathway contributes to the suppression of effector T-cell function [ ]. To restore the T cells activity, researchers constructed a PD-1/CD28 CSR to transmit a CD28 activating signal instead of the PD-1 inhibitory signal by binding the PD-L1 [ ]. This design was first introduced by the Prosser and colleagues [ ]. They found that cell surface expressed PD1:CD28 induces the T cells activation with increased levels of ERK phosphorylation, cytokine secretion, proliferative capacity, and expression of effector molecule Granzyme B by converting the PD-1 signal into a CD28 costimulatory signal. Subsequently, researchers infused the PD-1/CD28 CSR into CAR-T cells to improve their antitumor activity. Liu et al. reported that the PD-1/CD28 CSR engagement augments the antitumor efficacy and proliferative capacity of anti-CD19 CAR-T cells while limiting their terminal differentiation [ ]. Based on these preclinical findings, they conducted a phase Ib clinical trial and further confirmed the efficacy of this design with 41.2% patients with R/R large B-cell lymphoma achieved CR among 17 participants. Moreover, a similar design also reported in solid tumors [ – ]. The PD-1/CD28 CSR could significantly enhance the antitumor activity of CAR-T cells by promoting T cells infiltration, decreasing tumor-induced hypofunction and attenuating inhibitory receptor expression in large established solid tumor models [ ]. Furthermore, such improved effects on CAR-T cells beyond the combination of CAR-T cells and PD-1 antibody pembrolizumab.
Beyond blocking the PD-1/PD-L1 axis, mitigating the suppressive effects of other immune checkpoints also obtained promising results, such as T-cell immune receptor with Ig and ITIM domains (TIGIT) and CD47 [ – ]. Zhao and colleagues constructed a PD-1/TIGIT-CD28 CSR that could convert the inhibitory PD1/TIGIT signals into intracellular stimulatory CD28 signal. Integrating this design into anti-EGFR CAR-T cells led to robust tumor infiltration and antitumor efficiency in vivo [ ]. Moreover, TIGIT-CD28 CSR alone also had the potential to improve the anti-CD19 CAR-T cells function, and the improved function relied on the CD155 expression on tumor cells [ ].

## Manipulating the inhibitory regulators expression
Abrogating or decreasing the expression of negative regulators through gene-editing or gene knockdown technologies presents an alternative strategy to enhance the efficacy of CAR-T cells [ – ]. For example, CRISPR/Cas9-mediated PD-1 disruption or LAG-3 disruption improved the activity of CAR-T cells in preclinical studies [ , ]. Additionally, Agarwal et al. demonstrated that deletion of CTLA4 in anti-CD19 CAR-T cells results in superior tumor control and increased surface CAR expression. However, knockout of PD-1 or knockout of PD-1 and CTLA-4 did not boost anti-tumor efficacy in xenograft models of acute leukemia [ ]. In a clinical study, the preliminary safety was confirmed, yet the efficacy of PD-1 knockout CAR-T cells was more limited [ ]. In a phase I study of CAR-T cells with PD-1 and TCR disruption in mesothelin-positive solid tumors, the best overall response was stable disease (SD; 2/15 patients) and CAR-T cells did not persist beyond 6 weeks after the first infusion [ ]. The poor outcomes may be due to the additional TCR disruption in this study which resulted in poor expansion and impaired persistence. Another phase I clinical trial of MUC1-targeted CAR-T cells with PD-1 knockout in advanced breast cancer reported that 5 out of 12 patients achieved stable disease [ ].
Short hairpin RNA (shRNA)-based knockdown has also been used to downregulate inhibitory checkpoints in a cell-intrinsic manner [ , ]. For instance, co-transduction with an shRNA targeting PD-1 in anti-MSLN CAR-T cells has been shown to enhance proliferative capacity, cytotoxicity, and cytokine release under antigenic stress [ ]. Moreover, Lee and colleagues reported a lentiviral two-in-one CAR-T approach in which two immune checkpoint receptors (ICRs) are downregulated simultaneously by a dual short hairpin RNA cassette integrated into a CAR vector. They chose four ICRs and engineered CD19-targeting dual-shRNA CAR vectors with four ICR combinations, including PD-1/TIM-3, PD-1/LAG-3, PD-1/TIGIT, and PD-1/CTLA-4. The results demonstrated that simultaneous downregulation of PD-1 and TIGIT enhances the in vivo functionality of CAR-T cells compared with single downregulation of PD-1 [ ].
In contrast to the CSR, the dominant negative receptor (DNR) does not transmit any signals to further activate the T cells, as it lacks the intracellular inhibitory signaling domain. The PD-1 DNR competes with endogenous PD-1 and binds to the PD-L1 ligand on tumor cells, thereby limiting the transduction of PD-1 signaling [ ]. Co-transduced PD-1 DNR on CAR-T cells also inhibited the PD-1 signaling, resulting in enhanced efficacy and sustained T cell functionality [ , ]. Beyond knockout approaches, downregulating or reversing the inhibitory PD-1 signals on the T cells, PD-1 antibodies also enhance the antitumor response of CAR-T cells. All these regimes can effectively block inhibitory signals. However, no studies have yet compared the effects of these different strategies. Report indicated that the effects of PD1/CD28 CSR engagements on CAR-T cells beyond the additional PD-1 antibody [ ]. Moreover, the co-expression of PD-1 DNR was superior to PD-1 shRNA on CAR-T cells [ ]. PD-1 antibody has been approved to be widely used in the clinic, but repeated administration is needed for enhanced effects. The intrinsic modification of CAR-T cells does not rely on repeated administration, and such designs can circumvent both the T cells intrinsic and extrinsic resistance mechanisms to directly boost the adoptively transferred CAR-T cells performance and improve affordability for patients. However, combining CAR-T therapy with anti PD-1 antibodies may not only enhance the function of CAR-T cells but also reinvigorate other innate immune cells in the TME, including tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes [ ].
CD47 is also overexpressed on tumor cells, which prevents phagocytic clearance and restricts the innate immunity through the engagement of signal regulatory protein α (SIRPα) [ ]. Blockade of the CD47-SIRPα interaction rewires the immune response and results in synergistic effects with ICIs [ ]. Dacek et al. designed CD19 -Orexi CAR-T cells that could secrete a CD47-SIRPα checkpoint blocker CV1 to disrupt antiphagocytic signaling induced by CD47-SIRPα. The Orexi CAR-T cells reversed immunosuppression of the TME and potentiated the antitumor efficacy of rituximab through improving the activity of antibody-dependent cellular phagocytosis (ADCP) [ ]. However, recent studies found the CD47 expression on T cells is critical for the CAR-T cells survival and knocking out the CD47 results in impaired expansion potential and limited persistence in vivo [ , ]. Moreover, Yamada-Hunter et al. revealed that the addition of anti-CD47 antibodies abrogates CAR-T efficacy due to the rapid macrophage-mediated clearance of CAR-T cells. To address this, they engineered a CD47 variant (47E) that engages SIRPα to deliver a ‘don’t eat me’ signal, which cannot be blocked by anti-CD47 antibodies. The antitumor effects of overexpressed 47E CAR-T cells were enhanced by the anti-CD47 antibodies and the combined therapy facilitated the macrophage recruitment to the TME [ ].

## Modulating the stimulatory signals
In addition to converting the inhibitory signals, modulating the stimulatory signals and co-opting novel co-stimulatory signaling domains have demonstrated promising efficacy [ – ], such as the 4-1BB/4-1BBL axis, ICOS/ICOSL axis and CD40/CD40L axis. Co-expression of the 4-1BBL costimulatory ligand with a second generation anti-CD19 CAR-T cells enhanced their persistence and improved tumor control by facilitating targeted delivery of 4-1BB co-stimulation and activating the interferon regulatory factor 7 (IRF7)/interferon beta (IFN-β) pathway [ ]. ICOS, an activating costimulatory immune checkpoint expressed on activated T cells, regulates the T cells differentiation and proliferation through interactions with its ligand ICOSL [ ]. CAR-T cells modified to express the inducible T-cell costimulator (ICOS) also exhibited encouraging efficacy [ ]. Moreover, the addition of ICOS enhanced the anti-B7H3 CAR-T cell proliferative potential and tumor eradication even in a metastatic TNBC model.
Moreover, CAR-T cells with constitutive CD40L also exhibited superior antitumor activity [ ]. CD40L was first characterized on the activated CD4+ T cells. But the CD40 is constitutively expressed on a variety of immune and non-immune cells, including B cells, macrophages, dendritic cells (DCs), and tumor cells. CD40L can interact with the CD40 to enhance the immune function [ , ]. Kuhn et al. reported that the CD40L+ anti-CD19 CAR-T cells could evade immune escape caused by CD19 downregulation or loss via CD40/CD40L-mediated cytotoxicity in settings of tumor CD40 expression [ ]. The CD40L+ CAR-T cells also promoted the recruitment of immune effectors to tumor site and guided a long-term endogenous antitumor response by mobilizing both innate and adaptive components of the immune system. Compared with the conventional signaling molecules 4-1BB and CD28, the introduction of CD79A/CD40 as costimulatory signals also demonstrated promising efficacy. [ ]. Previous studies showed the co-engagement of B cell receptor (BCR) and CD40 receptors leads to synergistic activation of B cells [ ]. Based on these findings and the effects of CD40 signals on T cells, researchers developed a novel co-stimulatory domain with the BCR co-receptor CD79A and CD40. The CD79A/CD40 intracellular domain enhanced the proliferation and persistence of CAR-T cells and mediated increased intracellular signals through p38 and NF-κB [ ]. Additionally, constitutive expression of the toll-like receptor (TLR) adaptor molecule MyD88 and CD40 (MC) has been shown to enhance the in vivo tumor control efficacy of CAR-T cells. The related toxicity associated with MC CAR-T cells can be effectively managed by an inducible caspase-9 (iC9) safety switch [ ].

## Targeting the unfavorable TME
Given the immune resistance property of the TME, current research is focused on developing novel strategies to mitigate these unfavorable factors. Harnessing the chemotactic cues, targeting the tumor stroma and alleviating the negative effects caused by immunosuppressive signals within the tumor have shown great potential for remodeling the hostile TME and improving CAR-T cell efficacy. In this section, we will discuss relevant approaches to overcome this major obstacle and improve antitumor efficacy (Fig.  A-C). 

## Regulating the chemokine-receptor network to improve trafficking and infiltration
Chemokines and their receptors play key roles in mediating the migration of immune cells [ , ]. Moreover, the local expression of specific chemokines and adhesion molecules is closely correlated with the density of immune cells at the tumor site [ ]. However, in cancer, the mismatch of chemokines and homologous receptors on tumors and T cells results in insufficient T cells homing and infiltration [ , ]. In addition, some chemokines in the suppressive TME preferentially recruit suppressive cell types which further excluding the infiltration of immune effector cells [ , ]. Thus, harnessing the chemokine axis to match and respond to chemokines in the tumor bed is a promising way to boost the CAR-T cells infiltration [ ]. For instance, CAFs secrete stromal cell-derived factor 1α (SDF-1α), the ligand of CXCR4, which promotes the migration of MDSCs [ ]. The CXCR4-modefied CAR-T cells have been shown to suppress MDCSs recruitment via the STAT3/NF-κB/SDF-1α axis in pancreatic cancer models [ ]. And co-expression of CXCR4 on CAR-T cells enhances immune cell infiltration and antitumor response without compromising their viability. In addition, tumor cells can secrete IL-8, a chemokine that contributes to treatment resistance and facilitates the infiltration of suppressive immune cells into the tumor site [ , ]. Jin et al. engineered the CAR-T cells to express the IL-8 receptor (CXCR1 or CXCR2), which guided more effective migration of CAR-T cells into tumors and resulted in favorable antitumor responses in solid tumor models [ ]. Similar designs also reported in other studies, highlighting the potential of targeting CXCR1 or CXCR2 to enhance the efficacy of CAR-T cells [ – ]. Additionally, harnessing tumor-derived CCL20 to modify CAR-T cells with CCR6, a receptor of CCL20, also demonstrated notable therapeutic benefits [ ]. Forced expression of CCR2b, a receptor of CCL2 which highly secreted by tumor cells, significantly enhanced the CAR-T cells migration into tumors and improved efficacy in vivo [ – ]. These findings collectively demonstrate the potency in utilizing the chemokine network to optimize CAR-T therapy.

## Targeting the tumor stroma
CAR-T cells must traverse the endothelial junction and navigate the tumor stroma to effectively target the tumor cells [ ]. Fibroblast activating protein (FAP), which is overexpressed on stromal cells, presents an attractive target to overcome the physical barriers imposed by the TME [ , ]. CAR-T cells targeting FAP can effectively eliminate the FAP-positive stroma cells and facilitate endogenous CD8+ T cells antitumor responses [ , ]. Furthermore, anti-FAP CAR-T cells can improve the antitumor activity of sequential CLDN18.2-targeted CAR-T therapy by remodeling the TME [ ]. In PDAC models, the FAP-targeted CAR-T cells not only eliminated CAFs but also inhibited the recruitment of MDSCs and promoted the infiltration of T cells. Although preclinical data indicate that targeting FAP is a promising approach to enhance T cell infiltration, further investigations are needed to determine its safety. A phase I clinical trial (NCT01722149) revealed that intra-pleural administration of anti-FAP CAR-T cells to 3 patients was well tolerated without any evidence of toxicity [ ]. However, Tran and colleagues reported that anti-FAP CAR-T cells induced severe cachexia and dose-limiting bone toxicity in a preclinical study [ ].
The ECM is an integral component of the tumor stroma, acting as a barrier to the penetration of immune effector cells and supporting tumor progression [ – ]. Hence, engineering CAR-T cells to secrete ECM-modifying enzymes represents a promising approach to facilitate the T cells infiltration. Caruana et al. found the long-term ex vivo expanded T cells lack the enzyme heparanase (HPSE), a main component of the ECM to degrade heparan sulfate proteoglycans. Co-expression of HPSE on T cells can restore the ability to degrade the ECM [ ]. Then, they constructed CAR-T cells co-expressing HPSE and confirmed that the additional HPSE facilitates the T cells infiltration within tumors, leading to improved tumor control. However, since the ECM also exists in normal tissues, such modifications may cause damage to normal tissues. To address this issue, Zheng and colleagues engineered CAR-T cells with synNotch receptors, which induce local tumor-specific secretion of the ECM-degrading enzymes HPSE and metalloproteinase (MMPs) when encountering tumor antigens [ ] and found that these synNotch CAR-T cells could effectively degrade the ECM to increase the tumor infiltration and antitumor activity.

## Blocking the inhibitory signals in the tumor bed
The presence of immunosuppressive cells (MDSCs, Tregs) and signals (TGF-β, IL-10, IL-4, and VEGF signals) within the tumor microenvironment severely affects the intratumoral T cells accumulation and T cells function [ , ]. Preclinical and clinical research showed blocking these suppressive cells and pathways, especially TGF-β signaling, contributes to the antitumor response of CAR-T cells.
Given the dual role of TGF-β in promoting tumor growth and suppressing T cells function [ ], a variety of strategies have been developed to counteract its negative effects, such as antibodies, soluble receptors and small molecule inhibitors of TGF-β signal transduction [ ]. However, the development of TGF-β inhibitors is more slowly than ICIs, primarily due to the side effects, pleiotropic nature as well as its homeostatic role [ ]. Recent studies highlighted the potential of synthetic receptor engineering to block TGF-β signals in CAR-T cells without inducing systemic toxicity. One approach is to design dominant negative TGF-β receptor (dnTGF-βRII/TGFβRDN) on CAR-T cells [ ]. Many cancers can secrete the TGF-β to promote the tumor progression and impair the T cells activity. Co-expression of the dnTGF-βRII/TGFβRDN on anti-prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) CAR-T cells improved long-term persistence, cytokine secretion, and antitumor responses in human prostate cancer mouse models. Based on these promising preclinical findings, a phase I clinical trial was conducted (NCT03089203) and confirmed the safety and feasibility of the clinical application of TGF-β-resistant CAR-T cells [ ]. Knocking out the endogenous TGF-β receptor II (TGFβRKO) in CAR-T cells via CRISPR/Cas9 technology also guided long-term efficacy [ ]. Narayan et al. compared two blockade strategies in anti-PSMA CAR-T cells and reported that the TGFβRDN exhibits greater proliferative potential than TGFβRKO. Both regimens enhanced the cytokine production without affecting the cytotoxicity of anti-PSMA CAR-T cells [ ].
Strategies described above block only this suppressive signaling. Zheng and colleagues introduced a novel inverted cytokine receptor (ICR) that could further repurposw TGF-β signaling into a signal that supports the proliferation of T cells [ ]. The ICR construct fused the TGF-βRII extracellular domain to the interleukin-15 (IL-15) receptor α cytoplasmic domain (named TB15). Co-transduction of TB15 into the anti-EGFR CAR-T cells induced potent antitumor activity and significantly enhanced the persistence in a high TGF-β colorectal mouse model. Moreover, Hou et al. engineered a tandem bispecific IL-13Rα2/TGF-β CAR that translates a TGF-β immunosuppressive signal to a stimulant signal. They reported that its tumor control ability is superior to CAR with TGFβRDN in GBM models [ ]. The tandem CAR can be activated by TGF-β and holds potential to remodel the immunosuppressive TME. In addition, co-expression suppressor of TGF-β signaling SMAD7 with CAR-T cells was also sufficient to impede TGF-β signaling, which resulted in enhanced CAR-T-cell infiltration and sustained tumor control [ ]. Moreover, engineering CAR-T cells to secrete bispecific trap protein that co-targeting PD-1 and TGF-β also demonstrated favorable antitumor immunity [ ]. Compared to the anti-PD-1 secreting CAR-T cells and traditional CAR-T cells, co-expressing a secreting trap protein in CAR-T cells presented enhanced effector function and proliferative potential, attenuated inhibitory signaling and mitigated T cells exhaustion.
Anti-angiogenic therapy is an established cancer treatment strategy in the clinic that can ameliorate the suppressive TME through normalizing the tumor vasculature, thereby promoting the infiltration of immune effector cells into tumors [ , ]. Angiogenic associated factors during tumorigenesis inhibit the expression of intercellular adhesion molecule 1 (ICAM-1) and vascular cell adhesion molecule 1 (VCAM-1) on endothelial cells, which are important for T cell trafficking into tumors [ ]. The VEGF signaling is a key promoter of angiogenesis, and most of anti-angiogenic drugs widely used in the clinic focus on targeting the VEGF [ ]. Recent evidence suggests that anti-VEGF therapy can increase the infiltration and performance of CAR-T cells [ , ]. Dong et al. reported that the anti-mouse VEGF antibodies promote the infiltration and distribution of CAR-T cells and significantly delay the tumor growth in GBM models [ ]. Axitinib, a VEGFR inhibitor, also can counter the T cells exhaustion, help to evade terminal differentiation and enhance the efficacy of CAR-T cells by inhibiting the VEGFR2 pathway and activating Wnt/β-catenin pathway [ ]. These findings validate that targeting the VEGF pathway is a promising way to augment CAR-T cell function. Moreover, IL-10 blockade or pretreatment with an IL-10 receptor blocking antibody also potentiated the CAR-T cells function [ ]. IL-10 is traditionally considered an immunosuppressive cytokine, in largely part by inhibiting the inflammatory genes [ ]. However, recent research found the strength and persistence of IL-10 signaling may fine-tune CAR-T cell differentiation states [ ]. The IL-10 expressing CAR-T cells countered T cell exhaustion and induced durable clearance of solid tumors and metastatic tumors in many tumor models. Mechanically, IL-10 may trigger a metabolic reprogramming of CAR-T cells to maintain mitochondrial fitness and resist the exhaustion in the TME [ ]. IL-4 signaling promotes the formation of immunosuppressive TME via recruiting macrophages. Notably, this suppressive signaling also correlated with the immunotherapy resistance and CAR-T cell exhaustion [ , ]. Mohammed et al. generated an ICR in which the IL-4 receptor exodomain was fused to the IL-7 receptor endodomain to transform the IL-4 signal into an immunostimulatory signal [ ]. The ICR endowed CAR-T cells with survival advantages in an IL-4-rich environment and enhanced the antitumor response. In addition, some positive progress has been made in modifying CAR-T cells by targeting immunosuppressive cells, such as Tregs, TAMs and MDSCs [ – ].

## Enhancing the persistence and proliferation of CAR-T cells
The long-term persistence and proliferation of CAR-T cells in vivo are critical determinants of their clinical efficacy and sustained activity [ , ]. The full activation of T cells depends on three signals: antigen, co-stimulation and cytokines [ – ]. Optimizing co-stimulatory signals and cytokines can significantly improve the potency and durability of CAR‑T cells [ – ]. Another promising approach to extend the persistence of CAR-T cells involves regulating T cell differentiation. T cells populations enriched with less differentiated subsets and with reduced proportions of exhausted T cells may possess greater proliferative potential and persistence [ , ] (Fig.  D-F).

## Optimizing the co-stimulatory domain
CAR-T cells bearing different costimulatory domains exhibit distinct transcriptional, metabolic and phenotypic profiles, leading to distinct treatment responses [ ]. Amont them, CD28 and 4-1BB based CARs are the most commonly used CARs in the clinic. CD28 naturally exists as a homodimer and is constitutively expressed on both resting and activated T cells. In contrast, 4-1BB is absent on resting T cells andx binds to its ligand to form a hexameric complex [ , ]. Data from mouse models have demonstrated inconsistent differences in activity between CARs incorporating either CD28 or 4-1BB costimulatory domains [ ]. In general, CARs containing 4-1BB exhibit enhanced proliferative capacity, favor the formation of central memory T cell and greater oxidative metabolism. In contrast, CD28 based CARs tend to differentiate into effector memory T cells and guide enhanced aerobic glycolysis and rapid tumor remission [ – ]. The difference in cytotoxicity could be explained by the CAR-T immune synapse (CARIS) [ ]. The CD28 co-stimulatory domain forms highly lethal CARIS to facilitate serial killing, whereas the 4-1BB based CAR-T cells form lengthy and stable CARIS, supporting its robust expansion and cooperative killing. Although these differences have been identified, the efficacy of anti-CD19 CAR-T cells bearing CD28 or with 4-1BB is similar [ ].
In addition, CAR aggregation, CAR CD3-ζ domain phosphorylation, and tonic T cell activation can drive T cell exhaustion, thereby compromising the in vivo efficacy of CAR-T cells. This phenomenon was observed across multiple CAR constructs. Long et al. reported that 4−1BB co-stimulation could ameliorate the exhaustion of T cells caused by tonic signaling [ ]. In addition, the 4-1BB signaling prevents the activation-induced cell death (AICD) and reduces sensitivity to TGF-β-mediated immunosuppression [ ]. CARs containing CD28 are more prone to express the exhaustion-related markers such as PD-1, TIM3 and LAG3. Regarding the dysfunction of 4-1BB based CARs, Selli and colleagues identified a unique CAR-driven program [ ]. They demonstrated that the transcription factor FOXO3 acts as a driver of the dysfunction of CAR-T cells containing 4-1BB and that knockout of FOXO3 can improve the antitumor function of CAR-T cells.
Other co-stimulatory molecules have also demonstrated promising efficacy in preclinical studies, including OX40, inducible costimulator (ICOS), CD27 and Dectin-1. OX40, a mumber of the tumor necrosis factor receptor (TNFR) superfamily, is expressed on the activated T cells and can suppress the activation and development of Tregs through inhibiting the expression of CTLA4, TGF‐β and FoxP3 [ ]. Hombach et al. examined the impact of different costimulatory endodomains (CD28, 4-1BB and OX40) on the function of anti-CEA CAR-T cells [ ]. Their results demonstrated that the CD28 reinforces T cell immune response and proliferation and induces IL-2 production. OX40 and 4-1BB related to perpetuate the ongoing T cell response without IL-2 induction. Moreover, the OX40 most efficiently induced the propagation and survival of effector memory cells and the addition of co-stimulatory signal OX40 enhanced the function of CD28 based CAR. Furthermore, OX40 was able to suppress the secretion of the immunosuppressive cytokine IL-10, whereas CD28 co-stimulation and IL-2 upregulated IL-10 production upon antigen stimulation [ ].
The ICOS is a member of the CD28 family that is induced following the T cell activation [ ]. CARs incorporating ICOS demonstrated promising antitumor activity in tumor models [ ]. Moreover, anti-B7H3 CARs incorporating full length of ICOS presented enhanced cytokine production and superior tumor eradication compared to anti-B7H3 CAR-T cells [ ].
CD27 co-stimulation has also been shown to augment the therapeutic efficacy of CAR-T therapy [ ]. The interaction between CD27 and its homotrimeric ligand CD70 is critical for the memory properties of T cells [ ]. CD27 co-stimulatory domain promotes CAR-T cells expansion and effector function, resistance to apoptosis and induces tumor regression in vivo [ ]. The enhanced persistence mediated by CD27 co-stimulation is comparable to that of 4-1BB and superior to CD28.
Dectin-1 is a novel co-stimulatory molecule that is widely expressed on dendritic cells, monocytes, neutrophils, and subpopulations of T cells [ ]. Compared with 4-1BB and CD28, CAR-T cells with dectin-1 revealed reduced terminal differentiation, enhanced memory formation and cytotoxicity, and decreased exhaustion phenotypes [ ].
Another approach for optimizing co-stimulation involves incorporating dual co-stimulatory signals, which can be achieved by engineering bispecific CAR-T cells, as previously discussed [ , ]. But tandem of two co-stimulatory endodomains (third-generation CARs) has not revealed superior clinical effectiveness compared with second-generation CAR-T cells [ ]. However, the delivery of two parallel costimulatory molecules provides T cells with optimal stimulation and promotes the proliferation of CAR-T cells. Hirabayashi et al. reported that CAR-T cells simultaneously targeting two tumor-associated antigens and receiving trans-acting CD28 and 4-1BB co-stimulation exhibit favorable performance in term of survival, fitness and expansion [ ]. Metabolic analysis demonstrated the CD28 signaling supports the rapid effects via glycolysis and the 4-1BB helps to preserve the oxidative function and durable persistence of CAR-T cells. These synergetic effects of CD28 and 4-1BB co-stimulation on CAR-T cells have also been confirmed in other studies [ ].

## Harnessing cytokine signaling
Cytokines are dominant factors in the tumor-immune infiltration crosstalk which directly regulate the expansion and survival of tumor-specific T cells [ ]. Consequently, engineering "armored" CAR-T cells to secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines has emerged as a promising strategy to augment their antitumor efficacy [ , ].
IL-2, IL-7 and IL-15 are members of the common γ chain (γc) cytokine family which are not only used for the expansion of CAR-T cells ex vivo, but also closely influence phenotype and proliferative potential of CAR-T cells [ – ]. Among these cytokines, IL-2 is the most extensively engineered cytokine [ , ]. However, its role in boosting and suppressing T cells response, along with its underlying toxicity limits its utilization in CAR-T cells modification [ ]. Moreover, systemic IL-2 treatment can cause serious adverse effects. In a recent study, Rakhshandehroo et al. designed a CAR enhancer (CAR-E) consisting of the BCMA fused to a low-affinity IL-2, which could induce the IL-2 signaling when antigen countered CAR-T cells [ ]. The CAR-E selectively bound to the CAR-T cells and promoted their expansion in a dose-dependent manner in the absence of tumor cells. Moreover, CAR-E supported the memory T cells formation which retained the ability to re-expand upon restimulation, effectively controlling tumor growth upon rechallenge in mouse models. Allen et al. also constructed a synthetic IL-2 cytokine circuit using the tumor-specific anti-CD19 synthetic Notch (synNotch) receptors to locally induce the production of IL-2 at the tumor site without off-target toxicity [ ]. This engineered circuit promoted the proliferation of local T cells and bystander T cells and strongly enhanced T cells cytotoxicity against multiple models of immune-excluded syngeneic tumors. Additionally, Sockolosky et al. developed an orthogonal IL2 and IL-2Rβ pair to transmit the IL-2 signaling while avoiding toxicity and preventing the undesirable expansion of Tregs [ ]. The orthogonal IL2 and IL-2Rβ were not interact with their natural cytokine and receptor counterparts. Subsequently, researchers demonstrated co-transducing the orthogonal human IL-2Rβ augments the anti-CD19 CAR-T cells persistence and proliferation, resulting in enhanced antitumor activity in leukemia models [ ]. Another promising strategy involves the introduction of a truncated cytoplasmic domain of IL-2Rβ and a STAT3-binding YXXQ motif into CAR structure which activates the JAK-STAT pathway and boosts proliferative potency, thereby guiding superior antitumor effects [ ]. Although encouraging results have obtained in preclinical studies, careful consideration is required before translating these constructs into clinical applications.
To avoid the negative attributes of IL-2, researchers have also been turned their focus to other cytokines such as IL-7, IL-15 and IL-18. Recent reports indicated that IL-7 or/and IL-15 treated CAR-T cells possess a naive or T stem cell memory (Tscm) phenotype which is superior to the phenotype induced by IL-2 [ , , ]. In preclinical studies, reversing the inhibitory IL-4 signal to IL-7 signal or IL-15 signal significantly enhanced the proliferation and memory T cells formation of CAR-T cells [ , ]. The addition of membrane-bound IL-7 constructs with CD8 hinge and transmembrane domain also increased the proliferation and tumor control of anti-CD19 CAR-T cells [ ]. Additionally, Shum et al. designed a constitutively signaling cytokine receptor C7R which consists of a CD34 ectodomain and an IL-7Rα endodomain, and reported that the anti-GD2 CAR-T cells expressing C7R exhibited enhanced proliferation and survival during serial tumor challenge, as well as superior tumor clearance [ ]. Incorporating the IL-7 receptor signaling in the endodomain of CAR-T cells also augmented the antitumor activity [ ]. Moreover, co-expressing IL7 and CCL19 on CAR-T cells facilitated the infiltration of dendritic cells and T cells into tumor sites and triggered an enhanced immune response [ – ].
Notably, the IL-15 may be superior in the cancer treatment becasue it has no effect on the Tregs nor promotes the AICD [ ]. Compared with IL-15/IL-7 or IL-2, IL-15 limits dysfunction and differentiation while enhancing the persistence and function of CAR-T cells [ , ]. The IL-15 has been tested in the clinic recently and demonstrated encouraging effects. In a phase I study, patients treated with the IL-15 armored GPC3 CAR-T cells (15.CAR-T cells) achieved a disease control rate of 66.7% and an objective response rate of 33.3% among 12 patients [ ]. The addition of IL-15 facilitated the expansion, persistence and antitumor response of anti-GPC3 CAR-T cells. Similar findings were reported in anti-GD2 CAR-T cells, and the IL-15 promoted the enrichment of memory T cells and stem-cell like phenotypes [ ]. But infusion of 15.CAR-T cells also increased the incidence of cytokine release syndrome. Furthermore, co-expression of IL-15 and CXCL10 or IL15 and IL-21 endowed improved cytotoxic capacity, expansion, viability and in vivo efficacy of CAR-T cells [ , ].
In addition, IL-18 also holds potential to augment the efficacy of CAR-T cells [ ]. IL-18, an IL-1 family cytokine produced by macrophages, was initially described as an inducer of interferon-γ (IFN-γ) expression in 1989 [ ]. Co-expression of IL-18 has been shown to enhance expansion and persistence of CAR-T cells, remodel the TME and boost the antitumor response [ – ]. Moreover, Lange et al. designed a novel chimeric cytokine receptor GM18 which consists of the extracellular domains of the α/β chains of the GM-CSF receptor and the transmembrane and intracellular domains of the α/β chains of the IL18 receptor [ ]. Co-transduction of the GM18 optimized the CAR-T cells proliferative potency and induced tumor regression in vivo.

## Regulating T cells differentiation
In addition to modulating the co-stimulatory molecules and cytokines, regulating the T cells differentiation status also contributes to the long-term persistence of CAR-T cells [ , ]. Upon stimulation, the naive T cells can differentiate into stem cell memory cells, central memory cells, effector memory cells and effector T cells. During this process, effector functions progressively increase, whereas the capacity for memory formation and proliferation correspondingly decreases [ ]. Prolonged exposure to antigens ultimately drives T cells toward terminal exhaustion, accompanied by functional impairment. Maintaining a population of less-differentiated T cells within CAR-T cell products is necessary for the exhaustion resistance and sustained antitumor response [ – ]. Accordingly, numerous studies are underway to explore strategies to preserve the CAR-T cells memory phenotype and avoid terminal differentiation, such as supercharging CAR-T cells with transcription factors (TFs) and epigenetic modifications [ – ].
Recent findings have reported several key TFs, such as c-JUN, TCF1, FOXO1 and FOS, as essential regulators for defining and sustaining T cells memory phenotypes. C-JUN, a member of the activator protein-1 (AP-1) transcription factor family, serves as a master TF in maintaining T-cell activity and promoting the production of IL-2 [ ]. Overexpression of c-JUN has been shown to improve CAR-T cells functionality and confer CAR-T cells resistance to exhaustion [ ]. In addition, the c-JUN overexpression increases the expression of co-stimulatory molecules and cytokines production through reinvigoration of ERK or transcriptional activation [ ]. A phase I trial also reported that overexpression of c-JUN can restore the antitumor response of anti-CD33 CAR-T cells [ ]. In addition, TCF1 is closely associated with T cells stemness. Its expression decreases as the T cells exhaustion progresses [ – ]. The ribonuclease Regnase-1 mediates decay of target mRNAs by recognizing stem-loop motifs in 3' untranslated regions (UTRs). Recent studies confirmed that TCF-1 is a direct target of Regnase-1. The deficiency of Regnase-1 leads to elevated TCF-1 expression and enhances the long-term persistence and effector function of CAR-T cells [ ]. Moreover, FOXO1 has been identified as a key enhancer of CAR-T cells activity which contributes to the memory formation, persistence and metabolic fitness of CAR-T cells [ , ]. In addition, FOXP1 and KLF2, two transcriptional regulators, orchestrate the transition of CAR-T cells from a stem-like state to an effector-like state and guide the critical fate decisions between effector-like and exhausted CAR-T cells [ , ].
Furthermore, knockout of exhaustion-related TFs can augment the functionality of CAR-T cells. For instance, the NFAT/NR4A axis regulates the inhibitory receptor expression and the NR4A TFs serve as central drivers of T cells hyporesponsiveness [ ]. The NR4A TFs (NR4A1, NR4A2, and NR4A3) are highly expressed in exhausted CD8+ CAR+ tumor-infiltrating cells. Compared to the wild-type, CAR-T cells deficient in all three NR4A TFs facilitated tumor regression and prolonged the survival of mice [ ]. Moreover, a positive feedback loop between NR4A and TOX has been identified [ ]. TOX and TOX2 cooperate with NR4A TFs to drive the exhaustion of CD8+ T cells. CAR-T cells doubly deficient in TOX and TOX2 showed decreased expression of inhibitory receptors and greater antitumor effects than TOX-deficient or TOX2-deficient CAR-T cells [ ]. Contrarily, another study revealed that TOX2 coordinates with TET2 to promote the central memory differentiation and proliferation of CAR-T cells [ ]. Depletion of BATF also reduced CAR-T cell exhaustion and endowed CAR-T cells with superior persistence [ ]. Additionally, transcription factors such as BLIMP1, EGR2 and IKZF3 also serve as the major regulators of T cells exhaustion. Knockout of these TFs in CAR-T cells significantly enhances their antitumor activity against tumor cells [ – ].
Advances in high-throughput technologies have offered new insights into epigenetic reprogramming in CAR-T therapy. Epigenetic factors, in cooperation with TFs, directly regulate downstream gene transcription and can fundamentally influence cell fate decisions [ ]. Notably, the epigenetic modifications, including histone modifications, DNA methylation, transcription factors, and genome conformation, profoundly correlate with the differentiation of CAR-T cells, thereby determining therapeutic outcomes [ , ]. For example, deletion of the de novo DNA methyltransferase 3 alpha (DNMT3A) in CAR-T cells helped retain a stem-like phenotype and promoted proliferation as well as favorable antitumor response in solid tumor models. Similarly, SUV39H1-edited CAR-T cells can maintain accessibility to loci to promote T cell memory formation and function. SUV39H1 ablation guided enhanced long-term activity of CAR-T cells through improving their expansion and persistence and delaying the onset of exhaustion [ , ]. Tet methylcytosine dioxygenase 2 (TET2) is a member of the TET family of epigenetic regulators that successively oxidize 5-methyl cytosine in DNA. Loss of TET2 contributes to CAR-T cells expansion and functional persistence, thereby potentiating antitumor responses [ , ]. Additionally, EZH2 is a histone methyltransferase that mediates H3K27me3 and drives transcriptional silencing. Two recent studies revealed the EZH2 inhibitors can enhance the efficacy of immunotherapy including CAR-T therapy by increasing the immunogenicity of lymphoma cells, reprogramming the TME, and boosting T cell function [ , ].

## Exploring the combination strategies to augment the therapeutic effects
While CAR-T therapy has achieved considerable progress against solid tumors in recent years, its efficacy as a monotherapy remains limited to a subset of patients. Consequently, investigators are exploring combinatorial approaches to amplify antitumor responses. Various therapeutic approaches, working through different mechanisms, may help boost the overall effectiveness of CAR-T cell therapy (Fig.  ). 

## Immune checkpoint-targeting antibodies to enhance CAR-T cell therapy
The upregulation of PD-L1 on tumor cells following CAR-T infusion, coupled with the expression of inhibitory receptor on CAR-T cells, provides a rationale for combining ICB and CAR-T therapy [ , , ]. Compared with genetically engineered T-cell approaches blocking the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway, the direct PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor combination strategy not only circumvents the complexities of cell manufacturing but also demonstrates a manageable safety profile and durable tumor regression in clinical and preclinical studies [ , , ]. Adusumilli and colleagues reported that the regional delivery of mesothelin-targeted CAR-T cell therapy followed by administration of the PD-1 blocker pembrolizumab was feasible and safe, and led to encourage antitumor efficacy in patients with malignant pleural diseases [ ]. The median overall survival reached 23.9 months in 18 patients. A preclinical study also investigated the combination therapy of CAR-T cells targeting transmembrane tumor necrosis factor alpha and anti-PD-1 antibody in TNBC models. They reported that combining CAR-T cell therapy with an anti-PD-1 antibody could increase antitumor effects [ ]. In addition to improve CAR-T-cell functionality, additional PD-1 inhibitors hold potential to remodel the immunosuppressive TME by decreasing the proportion of MDSCs [ , ].
However, another study demonstrated that while combining anti-PSMA CAR-T cells with PD-1 blockade synergistically potentiates antitumor efficacy, the duration of such cooperative effects remains transient [ ]. Additionally, a clinical study found the additional treatment of PD-1 inhibitors fail to further augment proliferation or persistence of anti-GD2 CAR-T cells to patients with neuroblastoma [ ]. Currently, the clinical evaluation of the combination of CAR-T and ICB remains at an early stage. To maximize the synergy of individual agents and minimize potential toxicities, further exploration remains essential to define optimal treatment regimens and dosages.

## Radiotherapy to enhance CAR-T cell therapy
Radiotherapy is the cornerstone of cancer treatment which could release high-energy radiation to damage cellular DNA [ ]. More importantly, it has profound impacts on the immune system and TME. Radiotherapy promotes the formation of an inflamed tumor microenvironment by enhancing cytokine and chemokine release, which in turn facilitates lymphocyte infiltration, immune cell homing, and vascular normalization. On the other hand, radiotherapy has also been shown to induce target antigen cross-presentation, neoantigens generation, and abscopal effects to further amplify systemic antitumor responses [ – ].
Recent research showed the radiotherapy can also potentiate CAR-T therapy in solid tumors. Weiss et al. reported that the combination of local radiotherapy and NKG2D-based CAR-T therapy has synergistic effects through promoting the infiltration of CAR-T cells within tumors and the effector functions [ ]. In TNBC mouse models, the EGFR-targeted CAR-T therapy combined with radiotherapy produced sustained tumor control, regardless of their radiosensitivity [ ]. Administration of CAR-T cells 4 h after radiotherapy significantly augmented the infiltration of CD8+ T cells and NK cells into tumor tissues.
Furthermore, incorporating low-dose radiotherapy as a CAR conditioning regimen can help counteract antigen escape. In a model of pancreatic adenocarcinoma heterogeneously expressing sialyl Lewis-A (sLeA), low-dose radiation rendered both sLeA positive and negative tumor cells susceptible to CAR-T therapy, thereby reducing the risk of tumor relapse. Radiotherapy also could upregulate the antigen expression, such as B7H3 [ ]. These studies collectively indicate that the combination of CAR-T therapy with radiotherapy is a highly promising strategy worthy of clinical exploration. Future studies should determine the optimal radiotherapy dosage, fractionation, and sequencing with CAR-T cell administration to maximize therapeutic efficacy and minimize associated toxicities.

## Bispecific antibodies to enhance CAR-T cell therapy
Previous studies have shown that strategically combining CARs with BiTEs into a single gene-modified T-cell product generates potent antitumor effects. The CAR-T.BiTE cells secrete BiTEs that redirect CAR-T cells and recruit un-transduced bystander T cells against tumor cells [ ]. Additional treatment with BiTE also improved the efficacy of CAR-T cells. Brinkmann et al. found the CD20/CD3 bispecific antibody complements anti-CD19 CAR-T cells mediated killing through recruiting and activating endogenous T cells and supporting the activation and proliferation of CAR-T cells [ ]. The sequential administration of bispecific antibodies with CAR-T cell therapy is currently being assessed in hematologic malignancies and has yielded encouraging outcomes [ ]. But the efficacy and safety of combining BiTEs with CAR-T therapy in solid tumors are lacking. Nevertheless, preclinical and clinical evidence from hematologic malignancies suggest that this combination may hold broader applicability across other tumor types.

## Oncolytic virus to enhance CAR-T cell therapy
Oncolytic virus immunotherapy has emerged as a potentially powerful approach for cancer treatment which can specifically infect, replicate, and lyse tumor cells while sparing normal cells [ – ]. The antitumor activity of oncolytic viruses is determined by a combination of direct cancer cell lysis and indirect induction of host antitumor immunity [ ]. Potential mechanisms of action may include the viral replication, induction of primary cell death, interactions with tumor cell antiviral elements, danger signals release and initiation of innate and adaptive antitumor immunity [ , , ]. Furthermore, OVs can be genetically engineered to express immune-stimulatory molecules such as chemokines and cytokines to remodel the suppressive TME and enhance therapeutic efficacy [ – ].
Emerging evidence indicates that OVs can be engineered to arm and empower CAR-T cells, thereby overcoming the the obstacles encountered in treating solid tumors [ , – ]. OVs can enhance CAR-T cell function, promote the CAR-T cells accumulation at the tumor site and overcome the barrier caused by the TME [ , ]. A study combined anti-GD2 CAR-T cells with an OV armed with chemokine RANTES and the cytokine IL-15 (Ad5Δ24) and found the Ad5Δ24 significantly augment the T cells trafficking, infiltration and survival within tumors [ ]. The combinations resulted in better tumor control and prolonged survival of mice. Additionally, OVs armed with CXCL9 and IL-15 (Ad-CXCL9-IL15) also could help to remodel the TME and boost anti-B7H3 CAR-T cells function in xenografted prostate cancer model. Additional treatment with Ad-CXCL9-IL15 promoted the T cells infiltration and decreased the infiltration of Treg cells in the tumor site [ ]. Engineered OVs to target the TGF-β singles also improved the function and therapeutic effects of mesothelin-targeted CAR-T cells [ ]. Beyond engineering with chemokines and cytokines, OVs can also be designed to express BiTEs and checkpoint inhibitors [ , ]. For instance, Porter et al. developed a binary oncolytic-helper adenovirus expressing a BiTE, IL-12, and PD-L1 antibody (CAdTrio) [ ]. The CAdTrio enabled CAR-T cells to eliminate the heterogeneous tumors in several xenograft models.
Additionally, OVs promote the release of TAAs and can be genetically modified to express specific CAR-targeting antigens on the tumor surface, thereby enhancing the targeting efficacy of corresponding CAR-T cells [ ]. Aboalela et al. designed an oncolytic herpes simplex virus type 1 expressing human MSLN (HSV-MSLN). They found the HSV-MSLN could deliver MSLN on the tumor cell surface and reprogram the TME toward an immunogenic state, thus leading to a more robust antitumor response [ ].
These encouraging findings collectively demonstrate that combining OVs with CAR-T therapy represents a highly promising therapeutic strategy, particularly in addressing challenges posed by tumor heterogeneity, suppressive TME and limited tumor accessibility. Despite these promising results, further studies are still needed to balance the safety and efficacy before launching into clinic.

## Vaccines to enhance CAR-T cell therapy
Vaccines also offer a novel solution to amplify CAR-T cells activity in solid tumors [ – ]. Vaccines aim to activate the immune system to kill the tumor cells. In a phase I/IIa dose escalation clinical trial, CLDN6-specific CAR-T cells plus RNA vaccine (CARVac) showed a manageable safety profile and potent clinical activity [ ]. CARVac contains uridine nucleoside messenger RNA (mRNA) encoding the CAR antigen. According to the preclinical data the administration of the vaccine allows body-wide RNA delivery to APCs in lymphoid organs [ ]. Presentation of the target on APCs promotes in vivo stimulation and proliferation of CAR-T cells. In this study, the unconfirmed ORR in 21 evaluable patients was 33%, highlighting the potential for combination therapy with CAR-T cells. Moreover, in another preclinical study, the combination of anti-FAP CAR-T cells with vaccine consisting of adenovirus expressing HPV-E7 induced tumor eradication in large established tumor model [ ].

## Using other therapeutic agents to enhance CAR-T cell therapy
Except for above approaches, combining stimulator of interferon genes (STING) agonists also holds great potential to improve the CAR-T therapy [ ]. The STING agonists can activate both innate and adaptive immunity to augment the treatment response. Internalized STING agonists trigger STING signaling and induce interferon responses to reprogram the immunosuppressive TME and promote the CAR-T cells trafficking, infiltration and function [ – ]. In addition, the epitope spreading driven by the CAR-T cells and STING stimulation counteracts tumor escape via antigen-loss variants [ ]. Moreover, recent approaches show that the gut microbiome and metabolic reprogramming also regulate the CAR-T cells activity and efficacy [ – ].

## Conclusion
Recent preclinical and clinical studies have demonstrated considerable progress in CAR-T cell therapy. However, its application in solid tumor remains challenging. This review underscores the importance of a comprehensive evaluation of the specific characteristics of various tumor types to optimize CAR-T cell design. Here, we first summarize key features of solid tumors that impede CAR-T therapy efficacy, with particular emphasis on antigen heterogeneity and the complexity of the tumor microenvironment. Furthermore, we examine current promising strategies in CAR-T cell engineering and discuss combination therapies aimed at overcoming these obstacles, thereby enhancing the potency and durability of CAR-T cell therapy.
A major limitation of this review is that it does not focus on the toxic effects commonly associated with CAR-T cell therapy and mitigation strategies, as these topics have been extensively reviewed in other studies [ , ]. Notably, addressing the risk of side effects during the development of novel CAR-T cells for solid tumors is critically important in clinical settings. Although numerous engineering approaches have demonstrated promising outcomes, further research is necessary to validate their precise benefits and underlying toxicities before clinical application. The increasing complexity of CAR designs and the gene editing of T cells may exacerbate the risks associated with CAR-T cell therapy and contribute to the already substantial costs of CAR-T cell production [ ]. Combinatorial CAR-T cell therapy presents promising opportunities to enhance CAR-T cell efficacy. However, the optimal combinatorial strategies for specific tumors and CAR targets remain largely un-identified. To expand the application of CAR-T cell therapy in future cancer treatments, it is essential to optimize dosing schedules, minimize additive toxicities, and identify predictive biomarkers.
Given the fast-moving nature of this field, novel CAR designs, new-generation CAR-T cell manufacturing, and extensive clinical experience may further improve the outcomes of CAR-T cell therapy in solid tumors.
