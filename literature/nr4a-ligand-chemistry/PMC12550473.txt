## ABSTRACT
Nurr1 is a member of the nuclear receptor family NR4A that modulates inflammation typically by inhibiting the NF‐κB signalling pathway. In vitro, Nurr1 can interact with the peroxisome proliferator‐activated receptor (PPAR)γ, as well as be recruited to the Pparg promoter in microglial cells; however, their functional relationship is not established. Here, we aimed to investigate the role of Nurr1 on PPARγ activity in human macrophages. Blood monocytes were cultured with GM‐CSF or M‐CSF to generate pro‐ (GM‐MDMs) and anti‐inflammatory (M‐MDMs) macrophages, respectively. The protein levels of PPARγ and Nurr1 were elevated in GM‐MDMs compared to M‐MDMs, and their expression was positively correlated. PPARγ activation in GM‐MDMs with the agonist rosiglitazone did not modify Nurr1 expression. However, Nurr1 activation with the agonist C‐DIM12 increased PPARγ levels through protein stabilisation. Further, agonistic activation of Nurr1 decreased the proportion of PPARγ molecules phosphorylated at Ser84, which is a repressive mark for PPARγ transcriptional activity. Accordingly, exposure of GM‐MDMs to C‐DIM12 enhanced the expression of two PPARγ target genes induced by rosiglitazone, CD36 and PLIN2. Both PPARγ and Nurr1 agonists exhibited anti‐inflammatory effects on LPS‐stimulated GM‐MDMs when administered alone, but C‐DIM12 did not globally increase the effectiveness of rosiglitazone under that condition. These findings suggest that Nurr1 enhances PPARγ transcriptional activity, potentially through stabilising PPARγ protein levels and decreasing its repressive phosphorylation at Ser84. This novel mechanism highlights the role of Nurr1 in targeting not only inflammation but additional pathways regulated by PPARγ in macrophages.

Nurr1 activation in human primary macrophages induced the increment of PPARγ expression through protein stabilisation and reduction of the proportion of PPARγ phosphorylated at Ser84, a repressive mark for PPARγ transcriptional activity. The Nurr1 agonist C‐DIM12 enhanced the rosiglitazone‐induced expression of PPARγ‐responsive genes.

## 
PPARγ Expression Is Increased by Nurr1 Agonists at the Protein Level
Next, we evaluated whether the agonistic activation of Nurr1 or PPARγ could influence the expression of each receptor. Treatment of GM‐MDMs with the Nurr1 agonist C‐DIM12 [ ] (1 and 10 μM) induced PPARγ expression at the protein level, while treatment of GM‐MDMs with the PPARγ agonist Rosi [ ] (1 and 10 μM) did not modify the amount of Nurr1 (Figure  ) or the level of NR4A2 transcripts (Figure  ). An additional Nurr1 agonist, IP7e [ ], significantly elevated the levels of PPARγ at a concentration of 10 μM (Figure  ). The effects of C‐DIM12 were only seen at the protein level, since it did not alter the levels of PPARG transcripts (Figure  ). Rosi did not significantly modify the expression of its own receptor (Figure  ) or the amount of PPARG mRNA (Figure  ). By contrast, C‐DIM12 and IP7e increased Nurr1 expression, as well as the amount of NR4A2 mRNA (only significant for C‐DIM12) (Figure  ).
 PPARγ showed an eminent nuclear localisation in GM‐MDMs, whereas Nurr1 was predominantly cytoplasmic (Figure  ). Neither C‐DIM12 nor IP7e prompted the cytoplasmic translocation of PPARγ; only an elevation in the nuclear abundance of PPARγ was seen in cells treated with the Nurr1 agonists (Figure  ). The subcellular localisation of PPARγ was neither affected by the Rosi treatment. Taken together, these data suggest that Nurr1 agonists augment the levels of PPARγ without affecting its nuclear localisation.
 
3.3

## Nurr1 Agonists Stabilise PPARγ Protein
Since Nurr1 agonists did not increase the levels of PPARG, we evaluated whether they affected the stability of PPARγ at the protein level. GM‐MDMs were exposed for 8 h to C‐DIM12, IP7e, Rosi, or vehicle (DMSO), and then treated with the protein synthesis inhibitor CHX for 8 h. The decay of PPARγ protein was rapid upon treatment with CHX; its half‐life was quantified as ∼1.9 h (Figure  ; Figure  ), similar to what had been previously reported [ , ]. C‐DIM12 and IP7e maintained high levels of PPARγ along the CHX treatment (estimated half‐life: 12.8 h and 10.1 h, respectively, Figure  ; Figure  ). At the same time, Rosi did not substantially modify the rate of PPARγ degradation with respect to the vehicle (estimated half‐life of ∼2.5 h) (Figure  ; Figure  ). The Nurr1 half‐life was also calculated in these experiments and estimated as ~6.5 h (Figure  ).
 It has been demonstrated in several studies that PPARγ is subjected to ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation [ ]. To examine whether the ubiquitination of PPARγ was affected by Nurr1 agonists, macrophages were exposed to the proteasomal inhibitor MG132 for 1 h and then treated with C‐DIM12, Rosi, or vehicle for 8 h. Surprisingly, C‐DIM12 increased the ubiquitination of PPARγ with respect to the vehicle (Figure  ). Rosi also augmented the amount of polyubiquitin‐PPARγ conjugates, as previously reported (Figure  ) [ ]. These data indicate that Nurr1 agonists increased the PPARγ half‐life by 5–6‐fold, but concomitantly also enhanced its degree of ubiquitination.

3.4

## C‐DIM12 Affects PPARγ Phosphorylation
One of the most well‐described post‐translational modifications of PPARγ is the phosphorylation at Ser84 (Ser82 in mouse) on PPARγ1 or Ser114 (Ser112 in mouse) on PPARγ2, which is a repressive mark for PPARγ transcriptional activity [ , , ], and has been associated with the PPARγ turnover [ ]. We determined that, besides the increment of PPARγ protein, C‐DIM12 and IP7e significantly decreased the amount of PPARγ phosphorylated at Ser84 with respect to the total protein content, similar to that observed with the Rosi treatment (Figure  ). The mitogen‐activated protein kinases Erk1/2 and JNK mediate this phosphorylation [ ], thus, we next analysed whether C‐DIM12 modifies these kinases' activity. We found that C‐DIM12 or IP7e treatments lower the extent of Erk1/2 kinase activation by 30%–40% (Figure  ). Rosi also diminished the amount of phosphorylated Erk1/2 by 20% (Figure  ). Neither the mitogen‐activated protein kinase p38 nor JNK activation was altered by C‐DIM12, IP7e, or Rosi treatments (Figure  ). These results suggest that agonist‐induced Nurr1 activation reduced the percentage of PPARγ transcriptionally inactive, potentially through the suppression of Erk1/2 activation.
 
3.5

## C‐DIM12 Increases the Rosi‐Induced Transcription of PPARγ Target Genes
Given that C‐DIM12 altered the proportion of phosphorylated PPARγ, we investigated whether Nurr1 activation also affects PPARγ transcriptional activity. GM‐MDMs were transfected with a reporter plasmid containing the firefly luciferase gene under the control of multimerised PPAR‐responsive elements. As expected, the PPAR transcriptional activity was significantly increased in Rosi‐treated cells, whereas C‐DIM12 alone did not induce this activity (Figure  ). However, when cells were pre‐treated with C‐DIM12 and Rosi in combination, the transcriptional activity of PPAR tended to increase compared with the effect observed with Rosi alone, although it did not reach statistical significance (p = 0.06) (Figure  ). The synergy between Rosi and C‐DIM12 became apparent with some PPARγ‐responsive genes, such as CD36 and PLIN2. The combined Rosi/C‐DIM12 treatment showed significantly greater induction of these transcripts than that obtained with Rosi alone (Figure  ). Therefore, Nurr1 activation by C‐DIM12 upregulates the transcription of PPARγ target genes induced by Rosi, likely by increasing the abundance of PPARγ at the protein level and reducing the proportion of PPARγ transcriptionally inactive.
 These results were verified at the protein level with CD36 (Figure  ). The enhanced CD36 expression upon Rosi treatment was accompanied by higher incorporation of Dil‐oxLDL by GM‐MDMs, while the exposure to C‐DIM12 did not substantially modify their oxLDL uptake (Figure  ). Strikingly, C‐DIM12 significantly diminished the rate of oxLDL internalisation achieved with Rosi (Figure  ), even though the co‐treatment still induced the expression of high levels of CD36 (Figure  ). Other molecules known to be regulated by PPARγ are the fatty acid binding protein (FABP) 4. Contrary to CD36, the expression of FABP4 was induced not only by Rosi but also by C‐DIM12 (Figure  ); moreover, the combined treatment did not potentiate the individual effects of the agonists (Figure  ).

3.6

## Suppression of Macrophage Inflammatory Response Upon Nurr1 and PPARγ Activation
Both Nurr1 and PPARγ have been shown to control the magnitude of the inflammatory response, mainly through the blockade of the NF‐κB signalling pathway [ , , , , ]. Given the synergy between Nurr1 and PPARγ activity on the induction of PPARγ‐responsive gene transcription, we next investigated whether this synergy operated at the level of the suppression of inflammatory gene transcription. Treatment of macrophages with C‐DIM12 and Rosi in combination showed a significant decrease of IL6, IL12B and IFNB1 mRNA production in cells stimulated with LPS. This suppression was not significantly different from that obtained with Rosi alone, although its extent was more profound (Figure  ). At the protein level, several cytokines were down‐regulated by Rosi and/or C‐DIM12 (TNF, IL‐1β, IL‐6 and IL‐12 p40), but again, the combination of Rosi/C‐DIM12 did not show a deeper diminution with respect to the agonists alone (Figure  ). Globally, these data indicate that C‐DIM12 did not potentiate the anti‐inflammatory effects of Rosi in pro‐inflammatory macrophages.

## Discussion
In the present work, we established a positive correlation between the expression of Nurr1 and PPARγ in human monocyte‐derived macrophages. This correlation was independent of the macrophage inflammatory profile and was corroborated in cells with pro‐inflammatory (GM‐MDMs) and anti‐inflammatory attributes (M‐MDMs). The latter macrophage subset expressed low levels of Nurr1 and almost negligible amounts of PPARγ, and both molecules were induced by exposure of macrophages to IL‐4. Nurr1 expression also positively correlated with PPARγ protein levels in visceral ATMs from lean and obese individuals, supporting the association found in vitro. Moreover, our data showed that both nuclear receptors were expressed at higher levels in ATMs from lean compared to obese subjects. In agreement with these results, Nr4a2 mRNA was found increased in the AT of wild‐type with respect to obese ob/ob mice [ ]. However, Veum and colleagues [ ] showed higher expression of NR4A2 mRNA in the subcutaneous AT of extremely obese patients compared to lean individuals and normalisation after fat loss. Similar discrepancies exist for PPARγ [ ]. Most research examines gene expression in whole tissue, whereas our study analysed protein levels in ATMs, finding higher expression of Nurr1 and PPARγ in visceral ATMs from lean donors than obese individuals.
Once we determined the association between Nurr1 and PPARγ expression in macrophages, we examined their induction with agonists for both receptors. It has been previously shown that Rosi cannot induce NR4A receptor expression at the mRNA level [ ]. We confirmed this result and, additionally, found that Rosi did not alter Nurr1 protein expression. In contrast, the two Nurr1 agonists C‐DIM12 and IP7e increased the protein levels of PPARγ without affecting the transcription of the PPARG gene. In GM‐MDMs, PPARγ was eminently nuclear; Nurr1 agonists did not affect its cellular localisation, and only an enhanced abundance of nuclear PPARγ was observed.
PPARγ is a short‐lived protein (t
1/2 = 1–2 h) [ , ]. Consistent with other studies, we found that PPARγ was rapidly degraded upon inhibition of de novo protein synthesis by CHX, with a half‐life of ∼1.9 h. Rosi did not substantially vary the half‐life of its receptor. However, C‐DIM12 and IP7e raised the PPARγ half‐life to 12.8 and 10.1 h, respectively. Nurr1 was found to be a stable protein in the experiments with CHX (t
1/2 = ∼6.5 h), meaning that even with protein synthesis inhibition, Nurr1 agonists could still exert their effects through Nurr1 activation at the times evaluated. The increased PPARγ stability induced by C‐DIM12 paralleled an enhancement in the rate of PPARγ ubiquitination, similar to that found for Rosi. The increased PPARγ ubiquitination induced by Rosi and other PPARγ ligands has been previously reported [ , ]. Since C‐DIM12 increased PPARγ half‐life, we hypothesise that Nurr1 may facilitate proteasomal‐independent ubiquitination, whereas Rosi would induce a more proteasomal‐dependent process. In this regard, several studies have associated the activity of E3 ubiquitin ligases such as NEDD4 [ ] or TRIM23 [ ] with PPARγ protein stabilisation. Therefore, our results suggest that C‐DIM12 could stabilise PPARγ protein through non‐degradative ubiquitin signals [ ], though we cannot discard alternative molecular mechanisms. Among them, Floyd & Stephens [ ] suggested that phosphorylation of PPARγ2 at Ser112 contributes to the targeting of PPARγ to the ubiquitin‐proteasome pathway. Moreover, the authors demonstrated that the inhibition of Erk1/2 activation prolonged the PPARγ1 and PPARγ2 half‐lives in 3 T3‐L1 adipocytes, suggesting that Erk1/2 plays a role in PPARγ degradation. Here, we demonstrated that C‐DIM12 significantly diminished the abundance of PPARγ phosphorylated at Ser84 with respect to total PPARγ, which could also contribute to the increment of its half‐life. The activation or repression of Erk1/2 signalling by Rosi or Nurr1 has been previously reported in different cell contexts [ , , , , ]. We found a decrease in the amount of activated Erk1/2 when macrophages were treated with Rosi or Nurr1 agonists. It is worth mentioning that GM‐CSF activates Erk1/2 in human monocytes and macrophages [ ], resulting in high Erk1/2 phosphorylation levels in steady‐state GM‐MDMs. The mechanism by which Nurr1 deactivates the Erk1/2 pathway in GM‐MDMs is currently unknown. However, it has been reported that Nurr1 overexpression induces the expression of two dual‐specificity phosphatases, DUSP2 and DUSP14, and blocks the phosphorylative activation of Erk1/2 in adult rat cardiomyocytes at least partly through DUSP‐mediated inhibition [ ]. An additional mechanism could be related to the modulation of RasGRP1 expression by Nurr1 found in LPS‐activated BV2 microglial cells. RasGRP1 is one of the Ras guanine nucleotide exchange factors. The amount of RasGRP1 was reduced in BV2 cells overexpressing Nurr1, and the RasGRP1 deficiency ultimately leads to a decrease of Erk1/2 activation [ ].
The diminished Ser84 phosphorylation achieved by the treatment with C‐DIM12 could also contribute to enhancing the Rosi‐induced PPARγ transcriptional activity since it constitutes a repressive mark for this activity [ , , ]. An important finding was that the combination of Rosi and C‐DIM12 tended to increase the transcription of a reporter plasmid containing tandem PPRE sites compared with Rosi alone. It is unlikely that C‐DIM12 acts as a direct agonist of PPARγ since previous studies have demonstrated that 1,1‐bis(3′‐indolyl)‐1‐(p‐substituted phenyl) methanes containing halogen groups were inactive as PPARγ agonists [ ]. However, ChIP analysis indicated that NR4A2 binds consensus NBRE found within the PPRE of fatty acid oxidation genes (ACOX, CPT1M, FABP2, FABP4) and induces its transcription in colon cancer cell lines [ ]. Unlike Nur77, which represses CD36 transcription [ ], C‐DIM12 did not affect the transcription of CD36 and PLIN2, nor the expression of CD36 at the protein level, whereas the combination of Rosi and C‐DIM12 enhanced the expression of these molecules with respect to the treatment with Rosi alone. This effect could be ascribed to the C‐DIM12‐dependent increase of PPARγ transcriptionally active. However, C‐DIM12 alone did increase FABP4 protein expression; therefore, Nurr1 may also regulate some PPARγ‐targeted genes by interacting with NBRE sequences within PPRE sites, as reported [ ].
A striking finding was the effect of the Rosi/C‐DIM12 combination on oxLDL uptake. Whereas Rosi enhanced oxLDL internalisation, likely via CD36 upregulation, C‐DIM12 counteracted this effect despite its induction of CD36 expression. The mechanism remains unclear. CD36 and SR‐A1 are key receptors for modified lipoprotein uptake, and mice lacking either receptor show a reduction in atherosclerotic lesions [ , ]. PPARγ agonists do not change SR‐A1 expression but upregulate the expression of CD36 [ ], and inhibit the development of atherosclerosis in the LDLR−/− murine model [ ]. Studies on Nurr1's influence on CD36 expression show contradictory results. Mahajan et al. [ ] reported an increment of CD36 protein in mouse bone marrow‐derived macrophages overexpressing Nurr1. Contrary, Bonta et al. [ ] demonstrate that Nurr1 diminished the amount of CD36 and SR‐A mRNA in THP‐1 macrophages, as well as the 24 h‐uptake of oxLDL, and Amoasii et al. [ ] reported a decrement of CD36 in the liver of mice on a high‐fat diet when treated with amodiaquine, a Nurr1 agonist. Although C‐DIM12 alone did not impact the CD36‐oxLDL uptake axis, its ability to reduce Rosi‐induced oxLDL uptake suggests its potential as a therapeutic target for atherosclerosis.
The biological consequences of the PPARγ upregulation by Nurr1 agonists have a more profound impact on genes directly regulated by PPARγ than on genes regulated by NF‐κB and involved in PPARγ transrepression mechanisms. Previous studies have reported that PPARγ can suppress the activation of NF‐κB in mouse macrophages and inhibit inflammation by interacting with p65 and inducing its ubiquitination and degradation [ ]. Jodeiri Farshbaf et al. [ ] demonstrated downregulation of p65 levels when PC12 cells were treated with Nurr1 or PPARγ agonists, but the effect was not additive when both receptors were activated simultaneously. Besides, we have recently demonstrated that Nurr1 also interacts with p65 and prevents its complete translocation to the nucleus [ ]. Then, we reasoned that agonists for both receptors may have redundant and, perhaps, some non‐redundant effects on NF‐κB transrepression mechanisms. However, our findings indicated that the Rosi/C‐DIM12 combination did not have an additive or synergistic effect on the expression of pro‐inflammatory cytokines, which suggests that Nurr1 and PPARγ have coincident activities in the blockade of the NF‐κB signalling pathway.
In summary, our findings suggest that Nurr1 not only suppresses the macrophage inflammatory response but also supports macrophage homeostasis by maintaining PPARγ expression and enhancing its activity. However, the interaction between these receptors is complex, as Nurr1 agonists can counteract certain PPARγ‐dependent functions, such as oxLDL uptake. Overall, our results underscore the role of Nurr1 in macrophage biology, influencing inflammation and, likely, lipid processing and metabolism.
