SongLuyao QiGen ShiWenqiang ZengQiongya QinYing WangZejian LiWei SongZilan ZhangAo https://orcid.org/0000-0002-8748-7899LuHuili Earlier reported immunocytokines mainly target tumor-associated antigens, such as fibronectin, fibroblast activation protein, and carcinoembryonic antigen. Notably, L19-interleukin (IL)-2 has advanced to phase 3 trials for the treatment of patients with advanced melanoma (NCT03567889; NCT02938299). Recently, immunocytokines targeting immune checkpoints have become a major research focus, owing to their ability to effectively overcome resistance to checkpoint inhibitors while demonstrating significant antitumor efficacy. However, the high affinity of cytokines for their cognate receptors may overshadow antibody-mediated targeting, ultimately causing immunocytokines to exhibit toxicity similar to that of their parental cytokines. 
Engineering cytokines to reduce their affinity towards the cognate receptor has been proposed to enable antibody-directed tumor accumulation of immunocytokines to achieve reduced toxicity. For instance, Bayer’s IL-2v (A122T) exhibits an approximately 100-fold lower affinity for CD25 (IL-2Rα), attenuating regulatory T cell activation, but it also impairs the function of effector T cells and natural killer (NK) cells. To address this limitation, low-affinity cytokine mutants can be fused to antibodies targeting immune cell surface markers, enabling selective activation of specific immune subsets. One example is programmed cell death protein-1 (PD1)-laIL-2, an immunocytokine combining an IL-2 mutant with a PD-1 antibody, which selectively enhances tumor-infiltrating PD-1+ CD8+ T cells to boost antitumor immunity. However, developing cytokine mutants with reduced receptor affinity requires careful optimization to balance therapeutic efficacy with safety concerns.
Prodrug strategies enhance cytokine safety through receptor/antibody-mediated masking, enabling tumor-selective reactivation via protease cleavage. ProIL-15 exemplifies this approach as an inactivated IL-15 superkine fused to a masking domain, requiring tumor-overexpressed proteases for activation. A Phase I trial (NCT05052268) is investigating a non-IL-2Rα-targeted prodrug with analogous mechanisms in advanced solid tumors. Xilio, Arrow Immune and Werewolf Therapeutics are employing this tumor-specific cleavage approach to develop prodrugs of various cytokines, including IL-2, IL-12 and IFNα2b. However, the structural complexity of these multidomain constructs elevates immunogenicity risks. Additionally, postcleavage recapture by residual receptors or antibodies may impede cytokine bioavailability and activity.
Previously, we have developed an anti-programmed death-ligand 1 (αPD-L1)/IL-15 prodrug LH05, evolved from LH01 with enhanced safety by using a whole PD-L1 antibody and IL-15Rα-sushi domain to conceal IL-15 activity. Only on conditional release, can LH05 yield complexes of IL-15 with IL-15Rα-sushi, which have been validated as IL-15 superagonists. However, due to the unique nature of the IL-15Rα-sushi domain, the strategy employed in the development of LH05 is not applicable to other cytokines. Notably, LH05 exhibits robust antitumor activity in immunologically cold tumors; however, the previously elucidated mechanisms only confirmed its capacity to engage intratumoral innate and adaptive immunity, while the specific key effector cell subsets and spatiotemporal dynamics of their tumor infiltration remain unknown.
Here, we report a universal Fab-Fc-masked cytokine prodrug platform and develop two αPD-L1/IL-15 prodrugs—P-T (dual Fab) and P-Y (single Fab)—which leverage steric hindrance from the antibody Fab and Fc domains to shield cytokine activity in circulation, thereby improving therapeutic safety. The platform’s generality is further demonstrated with IL-12, which also benefits from this masking strategy. The spatial hindrance-based masking strategies in this work could notably extend the treatment window without introducing exogenous components, along with a maximal reinvigoration of cytokine activity through targeted cleavage at the tumor site. Moreover, we identified tumor-reactive cells in tumor-draining lymph nodes (TDLNs) that responded to P-T, contributing to overcoming immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) resistance and enhancing the antitumor efficacy of other immune agonists. In summary, P-T and P-Y provide a blueprint for designing safe and universal next-generation immunocytokines and highlight the potential roles of TDLNs in immunotherapy.

## Results

## A universal immunocytokine prodrug design enables the tunable activity of cytokines
LH01, an αPD-L1/IL-15 immunocytokine, was designed to overcome resistance to checkpoint blockade via triggering innate and adaptive immune responses. LH01 demonstrated significant antitumor effect ( ) in RM-1, a typically “cold” mouse tumor model. However, the robust antitumor effect of LH01 was accompanied by severe toxicity, as evidenced by obvious body weight loss ( ). The toxicity was likely due to the exposure of the IL-15 active site during circulation, which enabled on-target, off-tumor activation of immune cells. To achieve reduced toxicity without compromising the antitumor efficacy, we have previously developed LH05, a conditionally activatable variant engineered from LH01, which demonstrated a higher safety profile. However, the non-universal nature of this sushi-domain-dependent masking structure limits the development of other types of immunocytokines. Here, we innovatively propose a “sandwich” structure that uses spatial hindrance from antibody Fab and Fc to mask IL-15 and incorporates a tumor-specific protease (urokinase-type plasminogen activator/uPA or matrix metallopeptidases/MMPs) cleavage site at the IL-15 N-terminus, to eliminate the need for additional receptors. We hypothesize that dual Fabs create an “umbrella” hindrance, while a single Fab, stabilized by the CH1-CL disulfide bond, acts as a “lock” to block IL-15’s receptor binding, thereby generating the homodimeric P-T (designated P-T-UPA when cleaved by uPA, and P-T-MMP on MMP2/14-mediated activation) and heterodimeric P-Y (P-Y-UPA, uPA-dependent) ( ). This would prevent peripheral immune activation by IL-15 and achieve a targeted release within the tumor. Another advantage of such a structure is that the Fc domain was linked to the C-terminus of IL-15 after being cleaved, potentially extending the cytokine’s half-life. AlphaFold 2’s predictions for the structures of P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA support the feasibility of this design ( ). Both IL-15 and αPD-L1 sequences were detected by western blot in P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA proteins, confirming correct expression ( ).
 In ELISAs, P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA have similar affinity for human PD-L1, comparable to the αPD-L1 (EC50=0.07, 0.17 and 0.10 nM, respectively) ( ), indicating the binding affinity of Fab of both proteins was unaffected. To investigate whether cleavage of P-T and P-Y could restore the IL-15 domain, P-T-UPA or P-Y-UPA was incubated with uPA. Site-specific removal of the mask on cleavage was confirmed by sodium dodecyl sulfate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) ( ). Then we compared the bioactivity of P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA before and after uPA digestion through a Mo7e proliferation assay. P-T-UPA induced a 52-fold attenuation in proliferative activity compared with LH01 (EC50=64.6 nM vs 1.3 nM). On cleavage by uPA, its activity was restored by sixfold (EC50=11.0 nM) ( ). P-Y-UPA demonstrated a 153-fold masking effect relative to LH01 (EC50=152.8 nM vs 1.0 nM), with activity restored by 18-fold (EC50=8.6 nM) on uPA cleavage ( ). These results demonstrate that the sandwich-like structures enable the tunable activity of cytokines.
Next, this approach was verified with IL-12, another well-known T-cell activator with high toxicity, to demonstrate its universality in designing other checkpoint-targeted immunocytokines ( ). The maximum safe dose of P-Y-IL12 was at least 4-fold higher (by IL-12-equivalent) than IL12-Fc, attributable to its diminished splenomegaly and reduced peripheral immune activation (NK cells: 5.8% vs IL12-Fc’s 25.9%; NKT cells: 4.5% vs 25.3%) ( ). These results suggested that systemic treatment with P-Y-IL12 resulted in minimal peripheral immune stimulation, validating the feasibility of this universal steric hindrance masking strategy.

## Sandwich-like αPD-L1/IL-15 prodrugs exhibit extended half-life and tumor-targeted activation
Immunocytokines can undergo rapid systemic clearance due to ubiquitous cognate receptor expression, limiting their tumor accumulation. We verified whether spatial masking could mitigate this target-mediated drug disposition (TMDD) known to result in the short half-life of cytokines. Healthy BALB/c mice showed that both P-T-UPA (t₁/₂=13.27 hours) and P-Y-UPA (t₁/₂=13.17 hours) had approximately double the half-life of LH01 (6.68 hours) and significantly exceeded soluble IL-15 ( ). Consistently, in C57BL/6 mice, the prodrugs demonstrated markedly extended half-lives relative to LH01, although P-T-UPA’s half-life was shorter than P-Y-UPA’s ( ). The divergent drug-time curves between P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA indicate that the Fab number may predominantly affect distribution rather than half-life. To track prodrugs’ movement, we conjugated P-T-UPA or P-Y-UPA with a Cy5.5-maleimide tracer and administered it to tumor-bearing mice for whole-body imaging. Bioluminescence data demonstrated that P-T-UPA or P-Y-UPA specifically accumulated in the tumor and remained detectable for up to 96 hours, significantly longer than LH05 at an equivalent dose ( ). 
To further investigate the tissue distribution of αPD-L1/IL-15, mouse tissues were collected 12 hours after treatment. The in vivo biodistribution of total αPD-L1/IL-15 displayed considerable tumor-targeting specificity. Notably, in comparison with LH01 and P-Y-UPA, P-T-UPA is much more prominently accumulated within the tumor than normal tissues ( ). We also aimed to quantify whether and how much αPD-L1/IL-15 prodrugs would cleave in tumors in vivo. Using western blot analysis, we quantified intact and cleaved forms of P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA in tissue homogenates ( ). On average, tumors exhibited significantly higher cleavage of P-T-UPA (>65%) compared with heart, liver, spleen, lung, and kidney tissues ( ). Similarly, approximately 65% of P-Y-UPA was cleaved in tumors, though the spleen and kidney also showed substantial cleavage ( ). We also compared the amount of cleaved P-T-UPA/P-Y-UPA across tissues. For each mouse, we normalized cleaved P-T-UPA/P-Y-UPA levels per tissue to the total cleaved product (100% per mouse). Notably, tumors contained the highest proportion of cleaved P-T-UPA/P-Y-UPA relative to all other tissues ( ). Altogether, these data demonstrate that sandwich-like αPD-L1/IL-15 prodrugs are preferentially localized and cleaved in tumors.

## P-T attenuates peripheral toxicity and increases the tolerated dosage of αPDL1/IL-15 in vivo
While both P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA exhibited potent lymphocyte proliferation inhibition in vitro, we prioritized P-T-UPA due to its lower masking efficiency, which enabled identification of the minimally elevated safe dose, to investigate whether the αPD-L1/IL-15 prodrug’s reduced in vitro activity can translate to decreased toxicity in vivo. To evaluate the dose-limiting toxicity of IL-15, we administered the highest possible doses of molecules to healthy mice. After only two doses, we observed that 3 mg/kg of LH01 caused significant weight loss and even mortality. In contrast, P-T-UPA at 5 mg/kg did not display substantial weight loss, and 10 mg/kg only reduced body weight without leading to death ( ). LH01 at 3 mg/kg caused striking enlargement of the spleen in mice, but P-T-UPA at 10 mg/kg even showed weaker splenomegaly compared with LH01 at 3 mg/kg ( ). Alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST), indicators of acute liver injury in peripheral blood, revealed that LH01 at 3 mg/kg exhibited greater toxicity over P-T-UPA at 10 mg/kg ( ). Consistent with this, urea and creatinine, indicators of kidney injury in peripheral blood, revealed that only LH01 exhibited obvious toxicity ( ).
 We also analyzed immune cells—CD8+ T cells, CD4+ T cells, and NK cells—in peripheral blood and spleen, as their activation may drive significant cytokine production and immune toxicity. LH01 at 3 mg/kg increased CD8+ T cells and NK cells in peripheral blood by 4.6-fold and 1.7-fold, respectively, compared with P-T-UPA at 10 mg/kg ( , and ). Furthermore, LH01 led to a significantly decreased CD4/CD8 ratio than P-T treatments, indicating a stronger peripheral immune response ( ). Similar peripheral toxicity was observed in the spleen. LH01 treatment significantly increased splenic lymphocyte counts and elevated the CD8+ T cell proportion from 38.2±0.7% to 61.2±1.3%. On the contrary, 10 mg/kg of P-T-UPA showed an approximate percentage of 50.5% CD8+ T cells, whereas dose reduction to 5 mg/kg resulted in a return to baseline stimulation levels (43.9±0.2%) ( ). Overall, these data suggest that the masking strategy effectively reduces systemic toxicity of αPD-L1/IL-15 at higher doses.
Following a comprehensive evaluation of the improved safety profile conferred by P-T-UPA, we further validated the enhanced in vivo safety of P-Y-UPA. At 5 mg/kg, neither P-T-UPA nor P-Y-UPA caused significant changes in mouse body weight ( ). Notably, P-Y-UPA stimulated 1.3-fold more CD8+ T cells and 1.6-fold more NK cells than P-T-UPA ( ). However, given that the molecular weight of P-T-UPA is 1.3-fold higher than P-Y-UPA, equimolar doses of the two prodrugs induced comparable peripheral immune activation in vivo. Altogether, these results demonstrate that P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA exhibited comparable in vivo safety profiles, with their maximum tolerated doses being significantly higher than that of LH01.

## P-T effectively suppresses cold tumor growth and enhances intratumoral CD8+ T cell infiltration
After confirming that P-T significantly reduces systemic toxicity, we evaluated whether its antitumor efficacy was compromised compared with unmasked αPD-L1/IL-15. Due to lethality at 3 mg/kg, LH01 was adjusted to a tolerated dose of 2 mg/kg, and αPD-L1 combined with IL-15-Fc served as a non-fusion control. The results showed that P-T-UPA (tumor growth inhibition (TGI)=77.32%, median overall survival (mOS)=21.5) caused less weight loss and greater tumor regression compared with LH01 (TGI=55.85%, mOS=20), and P-Y-UPA (TGI=71.59%, mOS=21) showed a similar result. The poor tumor control observed with P-T-N (TGI=28.52%, mOS=18.5) confirmed that tumor regression with P-T-UPA relies on intratumoral cleavage by the specific protease to release IL-15. Combination treatment with αPD-L1 and IL15-Fc led to dramatic weight loss even at lower doses, underscoring the safety of the masking fusion protein ( ). Moreover, histopathological section analysis illustrated that LH01 caused liver and kidney injury, while P-T-UPA was barely observed ( ).
 To test whether cytotoxic T cells engage in αPD-L1/IL-15-mediated solid tumor control, tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells were quantified by immunohistochemistry analysis. P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA showed a promoted intratumoral CD8+ T cell infiltration than P-T-N, validating their intratumoral conditional activation ( ). However, the combination treatment did not increase intratumoral CD8+ T cell infiltration compared with the fusion protein, suggesting its tumor control may rely on toxicity-related effects. T cells responding to αPD-L1/IL-15 may originate from preexisting T cells within the tumor or newly activated T cells that migrate from peripheral lymphoid tissues into the tumor, necessitating further investigation into the mechanisms driving CD8+ T cell infiltration. To this end, we screened the correlation between CD8+ T cells and various functional genes in several human cancer samples, and we noticed that high CD8+ T cell infiltration was always correlated with high CD44 levels ( ). Moreover, both CD8+ T cell number and CD44 expression level within tumors positively correlate with better survival in human cancer patients ( ). We then analyzed CD44+ CD8+ T cell changes in the periphery versus the tumor microenvironment (TME). Notably, LH01 markedly increased the frequency of CD44+ CD8+ T cells in TDLNs compared with tumors and spleens ( ). Thus, the αPD-L1/IL-15 may overcome αPD-L1 resistance by expanding CD44+ CD8+ T cells in TDLNs and enhancing their migration and infiltration into tumors.
Given comparable safety profiles and antitumor efficacy between P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA at equivalent doses, P-T-UPA was selected as a representative sandwich-like prodrug for further mechanistic studies. To investigate whether αPD-L1/IL-15 prodrug stimulates memory-activating CD8+ T cells in TDLNs as shown for LH01, we analyzed the percentages of CD44+ CD8+ T cells in TDLNs from IgG or P-T-UPA-treated tumor-bearing mice. The results demonstrated that P-T-UPA increased the percentage of CD44+ CD8+ T cells to 48.0±1.8%, much higher than 14.1±0.5% in the IgG group ( ). P-T-UPA, primarily accumulated and conditionally released intratumorally, significantly activates the immune response in TDLNs, presumably due to being drained into TDLNs from the tumor, as observed for endogenous macromolecules carrying other drugs. Additionally, RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) analysis of tumor tissues revealed that P-T-UPA, similar to LH01, induced the expression of factors like IL-1, CCL5 and granzyme to promote a proinflammatory and T-cell activating TME ( ).

## The therapeutic efficacy of P-T relies on the migration of T cells in TDLNs to the tumor
The therapeutic potency of αPD-L1/IL-15 prodrug in vivo prompted us to further evaluate its effectiveness across various refractory tumor models. Given the specificity of protease expression in various tumors, the uPA substrate linker in P-T-UPA was substituted with an MMP-2/14 substrate linker to generate P-T-MMP, aiming to broaden its applicability across a wider range of tumors ( ). The substrate linker can be effectively cleaved by MMP-2/14 in vitro suggested by SDS-PAGE ( ). Following the demonstration that P-T-UPA promotes T cell activation in TDLNs of the RM-1 model, we further validated the necessity of TDLN T cell activation for P-T-MMP-mediated tumor control in the B16F10 model. FTY720, a sphingosine 1-phosphate receptor agonist that blocks lymphocyte egress, was employed to inhibit T cell migration in B16F10-bearing mice treated with P-T-MMP. Administration of FTY720 before tumor implantation completely abolished the antitumor effects of P-T-MMP, whereas administering FTY720 prior to treatment (TGI=36.94%) preserved some of P-T-MMP’s antitumor efficacy (TGI=71.87%) ( ). Hence, both pre-existing and peripherally recruited T cells are crucial for P-T-MMP effectiveness.
 We then surgically removed TDLNs to confirm their role as a major source of recruited peripheral T cells. Consistent with the effects of FTY720, TDLN ablation (TGI=33.29%) retained only a weak antitumor effect of P-T-MMP (TGI=68.55%), indicating that the peripheral lymphatic circulation related to therapeutic efficacy primarily depends on TDLNs ( ). Flow cytometry ( ) showed that P-T-MMP significantly facilitates the infiltration of CD3+ and CD8+ T cells within the tumor, but this effect will diminish after TDLN removal ( ). For all CD8+ T cells in the tumor, P-T-MMP strikingly suppressed the expression of the exhaustion marker Tim3 and upregulated the expression of the activation marker interferon-gamma (IFN-γ), likely contributing to the residual efficacy post-TDLN removal ( ). In summary, the antitumor efficacy of P-T-MMP highly relies on the migration and infiltration of CD8+ T cells from TDLNs into the tumor.

## CD44+CD8+ T cells in TDLNs stimulated by P-T exhibit superior tumor control when adoptively transferred
Next, we assessed the role of TDLN-derived CD44+ CD8+ T cells in tumor therapy through adoptive transplantation experiments ( ). In B16F10 tumor-bearing mice treated with P-T-MMP, CD44+ CD8+ T and CD44− CD8+ cells were sorted from TDLNs and characterized ( ). Notably, compared with CD44− CD8+ T cells, CD44+ CD8+ T cells demonstrated higher expression levels of the proliferation marker Ki67 and the effector molecule Granzyme B (GzmB). Additionally, the activation marker PD-1 was appreciably upregulated ( ). These sorted TDLN-derived CD44+ CD8+ T cells and CD44− CD8+ T cells were adoptively transferred into B16F10-bearing lymphodepletion mice respectively, and tumor volumes were monitored consecutively.
 Encouragingly, TDLN-derived CD44+ CD8+ T cells exhibited a more pronounced capacity in tumor repression than CD44− CD8+ subsets ( ), in agreement with the expression of the effector markers we observed. Moreover, in irradiated tumor-bearing mice, CD44+ CD8+ T cells displayed a more durable survival in vivo whereas their counterparts failed to retain as many CD8+ T cells by day 10 post-adoptive reinfusion ( ). These findings indicate that TDLN-derived CD44+ CD8+ T cells are crucial for tumor control and can be exploited as a promising strategy for adoptive T cell immunotherapy.

## P-T synergizes with αPD-1 to control orthotopic and metastatic solid tumors by expanding stem-like CD44+ CD8+ T cells in TDLNs
Mounting evidence revealed that PD-1 expression serves as a marker of T cell activation in TDLNs while indicating T cell exhaustion within tumors. To probe the activation status of T cells, we therefore evaluated PD-1 expression in CD44+ CD8+ T cells derived from TDLNs before and after migrating to the tumor. Our findings showed that within TDLNs, P-T-MMP elevated PD-1 expression compared with IgG (14.8±1.1% vs 11.7±0.8%). In contrast, once these cells migrated to the TME, PD-1 levels rose to 54.0±6.9%, with no significant difference between the groups ( ). Given that high PD-1 expression would lead to exhaustion and inactivation of CD44+ CD8+ T cells, we next explore reactivating PD-1+ CD44+ CD8+ T cells by combining P-T-MMP with αPD-1 to achieve a better antitumor outcome. Tumor metastasis is the clinic’s primary cause of treatment failure and cancer-related mortality. To model both in situ and metastatic tumors, we subcutaneously inoculated B16F10 cells followed by intravenous injection on day 3. P-T-MMP combined with αPD-1 showed significant synergy in suppressing the orthotopic tumor growth ( , and ). Similarly, the combination markedly reduced lung metastasis and B16F10 colony formation, further confirming their synergistic effects ( and ).
 To delineate the underlying mechanism further, the phenotype and percentage of T cells within TDLNs or tumors were examined. In TDLNs, P-T-MMP treatment alone increased the proportion of CD8+ T cells and promoted their differentiation into CD44+ CD8+ subsets, while the combination treatment further upregulated CD44 expression. We further analyzed the functional subsets of CD44+ CD8+ T cells using the stemness marker TCF-1 and the exhaustion marker Tim3. The results revealed that the combination treatment increased the proportion of TCF-1+ Tim3− cells, indicating an expansion of stem-like subsets in TDLNs ( ). This subset serves as a reservoir for antigen-specific CD8+ T cells through self-renewal and can differentiate into “terminally exhausted” TCF-1− TMI-3+ CD8+ T cells. In tumors, both P-T-MMP and αPD-1 monotherapies increased T cell infiltration, particularly of CD44+ CD8+ T cells ( ). Notably, unlike in TDLNs, TCF-1+ Tim3− CD44+ CD8+ T cells are scarce within tumors ( ). We further evaluated the T cell exhaustion markers Tim3 and PD-1. P-T-MMP, αPD-1, or their combination significantly reduced Tim3+ exhausted T cells, revitalizing T cell antitumor activity ( and ). In comparison to P-T-MMP monotherapy, co-administration of αPD-1 remarkably decreased PD-1 expression in tumor-infiltrating CD44+ CD8+ T cells, assisting in the maintenance of its cytotoxicity ( ). Additionally, CD44+ CD8+ T cells in the combination group exhibited a pronounced ability to produce IFN-γ, comparable to the P-T-MMP group but better than the αPD-1 group ( ). Overall, P-T-MMP can synergize with αPD-1 to amplify the proportion of CD44+ CD8+ T cells in TDLNs, particularly stem-like subpopulations, and suppress the expression of PD-1 in tumor-infiltrating T cells, yielding superior antitumor effects compared with monotherapies.

## P-T and the HPK1 inhibitor synergistically potentiate intratumoral infiltration of CD44+ CD8+ T cells
The activation of HPK1, a negative regulator of T-cell receptor (TCR) signaling, can result in the disassembly of the TCR complex, which in turn suppresses T-cell activation, proliferation, and cytokine production. Inhibiting HPK1 has the potential to rejuvenate the antitumor potency of T cells. We hypothesized that the efficacy of CD44+ CD8+ T cells can be constrained by the HPK1 pathway in the TME. Therefore, the combination of P-T-MMP and HPK1 inhibitor may exert a more favorable immune activation and antitumor effect. To verify this hypothesis, we evaluated the impact of Comp K, a previously developed HPK1 inhibitor, coupled with P-T-MMP in an RM-1-bearing mouse model ( ). While Comp K alone displays moderate antitumor efficacy, its combination with P-T-MMP enables a more pronounced suppression of tumor growth ( , and ). Minimal changes in splenic weight indicate the safety of the combination, despite previous studies suggesting that Comp K causes slight weight loss ( ).
 Next, RNA-seq was performed with treated tumor tissues to unveil the underlying mechanisms of the combination therapy. The volcano plot analysis identified 824 differential genes (differentially expressed genes (DEGs)) in the combination group compared with Comp K, comprizing 630 upregulated genes and 194 downregulated genes. Notably, the cytotoxicity-related genes such as GzmB and Il2ra were elevated in the combination group ( ). Compared with P-T-MMP alone, 394 DEGs, with 223 upregulated and 171 downregulated were identified in the combination group, among which key T cell activation signaling molecules, Rock1 and Nck1, were upregulated. Additionally, the combination suppressed MMP2/9, which may attenuate immunosuppression and subsequently improve T-cell antitumor efficacy ( ). Moreover, KEGG (Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes) functional clustering analysis of DEGs revealed that combination treatment led to a significant increase in immune response-related pathways, including “Leukocyte transendothelial migration”, “Chemokine signaling pathway” and “Cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction” compared with the alone ( ).
We further observed that the coadministration of HPK1 inhibitor promoted more intratumoral infiltration of CD8+ T cells than P-T-MMP alone (32.1±2.0% vs 18.8±2.0%) ( ), although it exerts no effect on the expansion of TDLN-derived CD44+ CD8+ T cells stimulated by P-T-MMP ( ). Intratumoral CD8+ T cells whether tissue-resident or migrated from TDLNs, almost exclusively expressed CD44 in the tumor, likely due to sustained direct exposure to tumor antigens ( ). More importantly, in the TME, the combination significantly increased the frequency of stem-like CD44+ CD8+ tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) compared with HPK1 inhibitor monotherapy ( ). Compared with IgG, both P-T-MMP and the combination elevated the percentage of TCF-1− TIM-3− cells within CD44+ CD8+ TILs, referred to as “effectors”, whereas no significant difference was observed with the HPK1 inhibitor alone ( ). While P-T-MMP monotherapy decreased terminal exhaustion in CD44+ CD8+ TILs and sustained T-cell activity, coadministration with Comp K partially attenuated this benefit ( ). Collectively, the combination of HPK1 inhibitor and P-T-MMP promotes intratumoral infiltration of CD44+ CD8+ T cells through interactions involving multiple pathways and molecules, rationalizing the combination of the tumor-conditional αPD-L1/IL-15 with other immune-stimulating molecules to further improve the therapeutic efficacy.

## Discussion
In this study, we generated two unique immunocytokine prodrugs involving P-T and P-Y that shield cytokine activity in circulation via steric hindrance from Fab and Fc of the antibody, and allow reactivation on cleavage by tumor-specific proteases. This study demonstrates the following advantages and features of immunocytokine prodrug in tumor therapy. (1) Its structure is universal, requiring no additional receptor for masking. (2) The cytokine remains Fc-linked before and after conditional release, extending its half-life. (3) High-dose prodrug administration, permitted by its safety profile, ensures therapeutic efficacy. (4) P-T stimulates CD44+ CD8+ T cells in TDLNs and promotes their infiltration into the TME, overcoming ICB resistance. (5) P-T enhances the efficacy of αPD-1 and other immune agonists.
The clinical limitations of cytokine therapies may stem from TMDD, where systemically administered cytokines are sequestered by ubiquitous peripheral receptors before reaching tumor targets, resulting in severe dose-limiting toxicity. To address this bottleneck across cytokines (eg, IL-15, IFN-α, IL-12), we propose a dual-masking strategy leveraging antibody Fab and Fc domains to enable tumor targeting while reducing TMDD. The P-T or P-Y platform both enables cytokine substitution (eg, IL-12 replacing IL-15), substantially diminishing peripheral activation. While P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA showed extended half-lives compared with LH01 or the prodrug LH05, their tumor-targeting efficiency remained limited, aligning with the known ∼0.01% tumor accumulation rate of systemic antibodies. Pharmacokinetic analysis revealed that the peak serum concentrations (Cmax) at 5 min of all three immunocytokines (LH01, P-T-UPA, and P-Y-UPA) were significantly lower than their theoretical initial concentrations (C0). For instance, in C57BL/6 mice, the Cmax of P-T-UPA (35,218 ng/mL) was 17.8% lower than its C0 (42,857 ng/mL), while LH01 and P-Y-UPA showed reductions of 53.3% and 30.9%, respectively. This likely reflects immunocytokine-specific PK behaviors: (1) Fc glycan-mediated rapid hepatobiliary clearance and (2) rapid tissue distribution, collectively reducing blood concentrations. Despite PK improvements over free cytokines, these prodrug-based immunocytokines (P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA) maintained shorter half-lives than full-length antibodies, indicating cytokine moiety-dominated half-life regulation rather than antibody scaffold control.
Research on immunocytokines has shifted from early tumor antigen targeting to immune checkpoint targeting, offering a promising approach to overcome resistance to checkpoint blockade therapy. On one hand, the PD-(L)1 antibody blocks the PD-1/PD-L1 immune inhibitory pathway and acts as a “missile” to deliver cytokines. On the other hand, the cytokines delivered to the tumor site effectively stimulate immune cells to synergize with the PD-(L)1 antibody, enhancing its therapeutic efficacy. The current strategy for overcoming resistance to PD-(L)1 is mainly to warm up cold tumors by combining with other treatments such as chemotherapy, radiotherapy, bispecific antibodies, and intratumoral therapies like oncolytic viruses. While these combinations can lead to rapid tumor regression in the short term, the long-term use often results in significant toxicity and lymphocyte exhaustion, shifting the cooperation from beneficial to detrimental. Our results highlight IL-15 and other similar cytokines as attractive candidates for developing checkpoint-derived therapies to broaden existing ICB. IL-15 is a well-known potent stimulator of CD8+ T cells and NK cells, as well as sustaining long-term memory of immune responses. As demonstrated by our data, αPD-L1/IL-15, exhibits long-term tumor control and significantly increases CD8+ T cell infiltration within cold tumors. The specific release of IL-15 activity within the tumor largely circumvents the peripheral toxicity associated with the combination of αPD-L1 and IL-15.
Traditionally, PD-(L)1 checkpoint inhibitors were believed to act within the TME until recently mounting research primarily demonstrated that their priority responders may actually be immune cells in TDLNs. A population of TdLN-derived tumor-specific memory CD8+ T cells (TTSM) has been identified as the primary responder to PD-1/PD-L1 blockade. Thus, limited stimulation or migration of lymphocytes in TDLNs may induce resistance against checkpoint blockade. We find P-T, capable of overcoming αPD-L1 resistance, markedly elevated CD44+ CD8+ T cells in TDLNs. Experiments involving FTY720 inhibition and TDLNs resection revealed that the P-T induces an influx of CD44+ CD8+ T cells from TDLNs to the TME, with these cells exhibiting enhanced tumor control on adoptive transfer. Our study underscores the critical role of TDLNs as an immense immunoregulatory “arena” for the next generation of checkpoint-derived immunocytokines.
TDLN-derived CD44+ CD8+ T cells are dramatically different from CD44− CD8+ T cells in terms of functional characteristics. The presence of CD44 on CD8+ T cells indicates prior activation. These cells are typically more responsive to stimuli and equipped to exert cytotoxic functions, such as the production of perforin and granzymes, to effectively eliminate malignant cells. CD44+ CD8+ T cells are also crucial for memory T cell differentiation, which can persist long-term and mount effective responses on secondary antigen exposure. There is a scarcity of previous research investigating the importance of these subsets in TDLNs for treatment. While tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells consistently showed high CD44 expression regardless of treatment, TDLN CD44+ CD8+ T cell expansion critically required αPD-L1/IL-15 stimulation, positioning TDLNs as the priming hub for memory-activating T cell differentiation. The expression of PD-1 on CD44+ CD8+ T cells is much higher in the TME than in TDLNs, prone to developing adaptive resistance. Consequently, αPD-1 was combined with P-T instead of using a higher dose of αPD-L1, to enhance T cell function without causing competition between αPD-L1 and P-T for binding with PD-L1 expressed on dendritic cells (DCs) or tumor cells. The data indicated that simultaneous blockade of PD-1/PD-L1 immune checkpoints indeed enlarged P-T’s efficacy in orthotopic tumors and lung metastasis models by expanding stem-like CD44+ CD8+ T cells in TDLNs. Furthermore, P-T synergized with HPK1 inhibitors to amplify CD44+ CD8+ T cell infiltration and functionality. Our study highlights CD44+ CD8+ T cells in TDLNs as both predictive biomarkers and actionable targets for combinatorial immunotherapy.
There is ongoing clinical debate as to whether there is any benefit of simultaneous lymph node clearance during surgical removal of tumors. We identified TDLN presence as an essential factor for the effectiveness of P-T. Our findings highlight that remodeling TDLNs can facilitate the delivery of cytotoxic cells to the tumor, providing valuable insights into the necessity of lymph node dissection and the optimal timing for its implementation in future treatments. In addition, the activated tumor-specific CD44+ CD8+ T-cell subset in TDLNs is suggested to be an alternative to TIL therapy, with promising applications in the treatment of cancers where draining lymph nodes are easily accessible.
Our work still has limitations. Although TDLN-generated CD44+ CD8+ T cells are mechanistically critical for P-T efficacy, direct evidence of P-T-mediated bridging between αPD-L1-targeted DCs and IL-15-engaged T cells in TDLNs remains unvalidated, despite prior reports identifying DCs as primary αPD-L1 targets in this niche. Additionally, while P-T exhibits tumor-selective activation potential and a safer profile than LH01, challenges persist in clinical translation. First, protease-dependent targeting carries off-target risks, as high-dose or prolonged P-T exposure may undergo non-specific cleavage by low-level proteases in normal tissues ( ). Second, murine models exhibit significant limitations: their rapid tumor kinetics (weeks vs months or years in humans) may lead to inaccurate estimates of therapeutic windows and cumulative toxicity. More critically, their simplified immune microenvironments fail to recapitulate human stromal complexity, potentially overestimating drug efficacy. Additionally, intertumorous (eg, MMP expression differences between hepatocellular and pancreatic cancers) and interpatient heterogeneity in protease profiles may cause orders-of-magnitude variations in activation efficiency, significantly reducing clinical response predictability. 
Overall, we developed a universal prodrug strategy of immunocytokines, which presents restrained activity in the peripheral tissues but recovers its bioactivity within tumors through site-specific digestion by the tumor-enriched uPA/MMP. In particular, P-T, a tumor-conditional PD-L1/IL-15 generated from this platform, selectively targets tumors and stimulates activated-memory CD44+ CD8+ T cells in TDLNs, emerging as a promising therapy to overcome ICB resistance and enhance the antitumor efficacy of other immune stimulants.

## Cell lines
The HEK293E and Mo7e cell lines are maintained in our laboratory and cultured according to previously described protocols. The RM-1 and B16-F10 cell lines were cultured in a complete Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM)-10 medium, which consists of DMEM (Gibco), 10% FBS (Gibco), 1% penicillin/streptomycin (Gibco), and 1% L-glutamine (Gibco). All of the aforementioned cells were maintained under aseptic conditions and incubated at 37°C with 5% CO₂.

## Animals and tumor models
The Animal Care and Use Committee of Shanghai Jiao Tong University approved all animal experiments (Ethics number: A2023153-004). BALB/c and C57BL/6 mice aged 6–8 weeks were purchased from Beijing Vital River Laboratory Animal Technology. All mice were housed in pathogen-free conditions and were treated humanely throughout the experimental period. To set up mouse tumor models, generally, 5×105 RM-1 or 4×105 B16-F10 tumor cells were subcutaneously implanted into the right flank of male and female C57BL/6 mice, respectively. 5×105 B16F10 cells intravenously injected caudally into mice for 14 days formed a model of lung metastasis. Mice were randomly grouped according to tumor size and treatment was started when tumors reached 50∼100 mm3. Tumors were sized every 2 or 3 days with a digital caliper and volumes were calculated as (length×width2)/2. TGI: TGI (%)=100 ×(1-T/C). T and C indicate the mean tumor volumes of the treated and control groups, respectively.

## Cloning, expression, and in vitro protein characterization of immunocytokines
LH01, αPD-L1, and IL15-Fc (ILR) were constructed and produced as previously described. The αPD-L1 antibody (atezolizumab) was isolated by screening a human phage display library (Genentech) against a recombinant extracellular domain–Fc fusion of human PD-L1. A high-affinity antibody on a human IgG1 backbone. Notably, this construct retains cross-reactivity with murine PD-L1. For P-T-UPA construction, the human IL15 mutant (IL15N72D)/IL15Rα-Sushi domain (Ile31 to Val 115) complex (ILR) is directly covalently fused to the N-terminus of the Fc hinge region and the C-terminus of the VH-CH1 region of αPDL1 via a GSSGGSGGSGGSG-LSGRSDNH-GSSGGSGGSGGSG (substrate) linker. The linker was changed to (GGGGS)3 (non-substrate) or GSSGGSGGSGGSG-QLLGFLTA-GSSGGSGGSGGSG to generate P-T-N or P-T-MMP, respectively. The knob-in-hole design of Fc facilitates the heterodimerization of P-Y-UPA, a technique that has been reported previously. Briefly, one ILR is covalently fused to the C-terminus of VL-CL and the N-terminus of “Knobs” (T366W) Fc (CH2CH3), and the other ILR is covalently fused to the C-terminus of VH-CH1 and the N-terminus of “Holes” (T366S, L368A, and Y407V) Fc. Replacement of the different linker is also applicable to P-Y-UPA. IL-12 P35 and P40 subunits replace the IL-15 subunit in P-Y-UPA to obtain P-Y-IL12. The corresponding amino acid sequences are provided in . For the expression of P-T-UPA or P-Y-UPA, two plasmids, one encoding the αPD-L1 light chain and the other encoding VH-CH1-Cleavable linker-ILR-Fc, or one encoding VL-CL-Cleavable linker-ILR-Knob-Fc and the other encoding VH-CH-Cleavable linker-ILR-Hole-Fc inserted into pM09 vector, were transiently cotransfected in HEK293E cells. Collect the supernatant on day 7 after transfection. Fusion proteins were purified using a protein A–Sepharose column according to the instructions (Cytiva) and analyzed by reduced and non-reduced SDS-PAGE.

## Western blot analysis
Purified proteins (P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA) were separated by reducing SDS-PAGE and transferred to PVDF (Polyvinylidene Fluoride) membranes (220 mA, 100 min). After blocking with 5% milk/PBST (phosphate-buffered saline with Tween 20.) for 90 min, membranes were incubated with rabbit anti-human IL-15 primary antibody (1:1,000 dilution) overnight at 4°C followed by incubation with Horseradish Peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG (H+L) secondary antibody (1:1,000) for 1 hour at room temperature to detect IL-15 expression, or after blocking, the membranes were directly incubated with HRP-conjugated goat anti-human IgG (H+L) (1:1,000) for 1 hour at room temperature to verify PD-L1 antibody expression in the immunocytokines. Protein signals were visualized using Enhanced hemiluminescence chemiluminescence (ECL) substrate and captured by an automated chemiluminescence imaging system (Tanon 5200 Multi).

## In vitro digestion for cleavable immunocytokines
For cleavable Linker with uPA as substrate, in vitro digestion was carried out by incubating 10 µg of P-T-UPA or P-Y-UPA with 0.5 µg of pharmaceutical uPA in a total reaction volume of 10 µl of phosphate buffered saline solution at 37℃ for 12 hours. For cleavable linker using MMP as substrate, human MMP-2 (1 µg) was first activated by incubation with 0.2 mM APMA in TCNB activation buffer (50 mM Tris, 10 mM CaCl2, 150 mM NaCl, 0.05% (w/v) Brij 35, pH 7.5) for 1 hour at 37℃. Subsequently, 25 µg of P-T-MMP was incubated with the above reaction buffer at 37℃ for 12 hours.

## Mo7e cell proliferation assay
Mo7e cells were stably cultured in a complete medium (Roswell Park Memorial Institute (RPMI) 1640 + 10% FBS + 1% Pen /Strep+10 ng/mL human GM-CSF) for at least three passages. 2×104 cells were seeded per well of a 96-well plate in free cell culture media (RPMI 1640 + 10% FBS + 1% Pen/Strep) and starved for 4 hours. Cells were then cultured for 96 hours at 37℃ in the presence of LH01, P-T-UPA (cleaved with or without uPA), or P-Y-UPA (cleaved with or without uPA) at concentrations ranging from 0.001 to 1000 pM. Cell viability was detected using the Cell Counting Kit-8. Absorbance was read at 450 nm with an ELISA reader (Teacan, Infinite 200 PRO) (OD630 as reference) and the final result was obtained by subtracting the control from the sample.

## In vitro affinity assay of P-T-UPA/P-Y-UPA for PD-L1
Standard ELISA procedures were followed, including plate coating, blocking, primary antibody incubation, secondary antibody incubation, color development, and termination. 1 µg/mL, 100 µl recombinant human PD-L1 was added to 96-well plates and incubated overnight at 4°C, then washed five times with 5% PBST immediately blocked with 5% BSA for 2 hours at room temperature. After washing the plate, P-T-UPA, P-Y-UPA, or αPD-L1 ranging from 0.0001 to 300 nM were added and incubated at room temperature for 2 hours. Peroxidase AffiniPure Goat Anti-Human IgG (H+L) (1:10,000 dilution) was incubated for 1 hour at room temperature as a secondary antibody. After washing, incubate the plate with Tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) single component substrate solution for 3–5 min away from light. The reaction was stopped with 2 M sulfuric acid and the absorbance was read at 450 nm, with 630 nm as reference (Teacan, Infinite 200 PRO).

## Flow cytometry
About 200 mg of tissue was taken from the tumor, minced and resuspended in digestion solution (RPMI 1640 medium with type IV collagenase (1 mg/mL) and hyaluronidase (1.2 mg/mL)], and then placed on a shaker at 37°C for 60 min at 180 rpm. Tumor single-cell suspension was obtained after filtration through a 70 µm filter. For TDLNs, the TDLNs on the same side are placed in sterile FACS buffer (phosphate-buffered saline + 2% fetal bovine serum). With a syringe plunger, the TDLNs are thoroughly triturated and then passed through a 70 µm filter to yield a single-cell suspension. For spleens, approximately 1/3 of the size was placed in a lymphocyte isolation solution and milled sufficiently, followed by density gradient centrifugation to acquire splenic lymphocytes. The preparation of peripheral blood mononuclear cells is reliant on lymphocyte separation medium.
Fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) analysis of T cells was performed on cells within the lymphocyte gate of forward/side scatter plots, excluding doublets and dead cells. Fc blockade and isotype controls were applied to exclude unspecific antibody binding and staining. Surface staining was performed with monoclonal antibodies for 30 min at 4℃ in PBS supplemented with 2% fetal bovine serum (FACS buffer) using antibodies CD45, CD3, CD8, CD335, CD44, Tim3, and PD-1. For intracellular Ki67, GzmB, IFN-γ and TCF-1 staining, samples were fixed, permeabilized, and stained with mouse antibody for 30 min at 4℃ in Perm/wash buffer. Then, cells were washed again and resuspended in FACS buffer. Samples were analyzed on a FACSCalibur or Fortessa flow cytometer (BD Biosciences). Data were analyzed using FlowJo software (Tree Star).

## Fluorescence imaging
P-T-UPA and P-Y-UPA were labeled with Cy5.5 and purified after washing away the free Cy5.5. Intravenous injection of 25 µg of fluorescently labeled fusion protein into C57BL/6 mice bearing subcutaneous RM-1 tumors. Fluorescence at different time points was measured using the IVIS Spectrum.

## Quantitative biodistribution studies and western blot assay
Tumor, heart, liver, spleen, lung and kidney tissues were collected from B16F10-bearing mice at 12 hours after a single 3 mg/kg protein intravenous administration. Approximately 100 mg of each tissue was suspended in PBS, homogenized, and centrifuged to yield a 10% supernatant. The above ELISA assay, developed to evaluate αPD-L1/IL-15 (or αPD-L1-Fab) affinity for hPD-L1, was used to detect the total amount of P-T-UPA or P-Y-UPA in both cleaved and uncleaved forms. The 10% tissue supernatant was separated by non-reducing SDS-PAGE and transferred to PVDF membranes (220 mA, 100 min). After blocking with 5% milk/PBST for 90 min, membranes were incubated with goat anti-human IgG (Fc fragment) (1:1,000 dilution) at room temperature for 1 hour. Protein signals were visualized using ECL substrate and captured by an automated chemiluminescence imaging system (Tanon 5200 Multi). Quantitative analysis was performed using Image J.

## Pharmacokinetic analysis of αPD-L1/IL-15
Healthy male BALB/c or C57BL/6 mice (four animals per group, randomized) were injected intravenously with 3 mg/kg LH01, P-T-UPA, or P-Y-UPA (equimolar). The blood was drawn into K2EDTA tubes via orbital at various time points (5 min, 30 min, 1 hour, 4 hours, 8 hours, 24 hours, 48 hours, and 72 hours) and processed to plasma. Protein concentrations were calculated by co-incubating plasma samples with 96-well ELISA plates previously coated with 1 µg/mL of recombinant human PD-L1 at 4°C overnight. The theoretical C₀ at time zero was 42,857 ng/mL for LH01 and P-T-UPA (calculated from a 60 µg dose and 1.4 mL blood volume), and 32,571 ng/mL for P-Y-UPA (from a 45.6 µg dose in 1.4 mL blood). Pharmacokinetic parameters were calculated with WinNonlin for a non-compartmental model.

## Histopathological and immunohistochemistry analysis
The tumor tissues, livers, and kidneys were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde, embedded in paraffin, and sectioned (4 µm). The liver and kidney sections were prepared and stained with H&E. Tumor sections were evaluated by immunohistochemical analysis using antibodies specific to CD8 (Abcam, ab217344, 1:2,000), and antigen retrieval was first completed. Slides were incubated with the primary antibody at 4°C overnight, and then exposed to HRP-conjugated goat anti-rabbit secondary antibody. Finally, the following coloring was developed by a DAB detection kit (Dako, Copenhagen, Denmark). Color images were obtained with a KFBIO KF-PRO-120 digital pathology slide scanner.

## RNA sequencing and cluster analysis
Total RNA was extracted from RM-1 tumors and used for RNA-seq analysis. Complementary DNA library construction and sequencing were performed by the Shanghai Majorbio Bio-Pharm Technology using the Majorbio cloud platform. The clean reads were mapped to the reference genome using HISAT2 (V.2.0.4), and expression level of gene was calculated by RSEM (V.1.2.12). Essentially, according to the transcripts per million reads (TPM), differential expression analysis was performed using the DESeq2 with Q value ≤0.05. Further, KEGG enrichment analysis of DEGs was performed by Python scipy software. A list of genes associated with inflammation and T-cell stimulation was derived from the gene set enrichment analysis website, the TPMs of these genes were logarithmically (TPMtreatment/mean TPMIgG) converted, and differences between groups were visualized through heatmap, which was performed by GraphPad Prism V.8 software. The Majorbio cloud platform visualizes volcano and chordal plots.

## FTY720 experiment
FTY720 can effectively hinder the migration of lymphocytes from the periphery to the tumor. FTY720 stock solution (10 mg/mL in Dimethyl Sulfoxide (DMSO)) was diluted to a concentration of 125 µg/mL in PBS immediately prior to dosing. A group of mice injected intraperitoneally with 25 µg FTY720 the day before subcutaneous inoculation with B16F10 cells served as the group without preexisting TILs. Another group of mice, which were injected intraperitoneally with 25 µg of FTY720 the day before the immunocytokine treatment, acted as the group containing only pre-existing TILs. FTY720 was administered every other day throughout the experiment.

## TDLN excision experiment
B16-F10 tumors were subcutaneously inoculated. When the tumors reached a volume of about 100 mm³, the axillary and inguinal TDLNs ipsilateral to the tumor were surgically removed, and the control group underwent a sham operation. 24 hours postsurgery, mice were treated with P-T-MMP or IgG control once every 3 days for a total of three times. Tumors were collected on day 6 after the third dose to evaluate TIL.

## Cell sorting and adoptive T cell transfer
Donor mice inoculated with B16-F10 were sacrificed after three treatments with P-T-MMP to collect all TDLNs. CD8+ T cells were enriched according to the standard procedure of the CD8+ T cell negative selection kit (BioLegend, Cat: 480008) and the purity was over 90% as detected by FACS. Subsequently, CD8+ T cells were incubated with biotin anti-mouse/human CD44 antibody for 15 min on ice, then with streptavidin nanobeads again for another 15 min. Magnetic pole sorting was performed to obtain CD44+ CD8+ T cells and CD44− CD8+ T cells. Recipient mouse manipulations were performed in parallel. C57BL/6 mice bearing established tumors were sublethally lymphodepleted by total body irradiation (4 Gy) on day 7. On day 8, irradiated mice were randomly assigned to three groups and received intravenous adoptive transfer of CD8+ T cells (CD44+, CD44−, 5×104 cells) or PBS. Tumor growth was continuously monitored up to day 10 after the infusion, at which point the mice were sacrificed and their spleens collected to analyze the number of viable cells reinfused.

## Statistical analysis
Statistical analysis was performed using Prism 8.0 software (GraphPad, USA). The two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used to compare tumor growth and body weight change, while one-way ANOVA or unpaired two-tailed Student’s t-test was employed to determine statistical significance between experimental groups (*p<0.05, **p<0.01, ***p<0.001, ****p<0.0001, ns, not significant). The log-rank (Mantel-Cox) test was used to assess survival.
