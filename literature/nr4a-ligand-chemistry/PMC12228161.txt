## Abstract

## Background
Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb) infection is well known to be challenging to treat. Treatment effectiveness is restricted by the pathogen’s inherent drug resistance. A deeper understanding of essential bacterial pathways that influence Mtb growth and survival can expediate the development of more effective therapies from various sources. Here, using mycobacterial tkt gene as a model target, we report on the use of CRISPR interference gene knockdown to potentiate the antimycobacterial activity of selected medicinal plant extracts.

## Methods
The growth phenotypes were investigated on solid and liquid media. Broth micro-dilution assay was used to determine minimum inhibitory concentrations. Potential cytotoxicity of the extracts was investigated using Vero cell lines. LC–MS was used for phytochemical analysis. In silico docking was performed on transketolase (Rv1449c), NADH-dependent enoyl-[acyl-carrier-protein] reductase (Rv1484), catalase-peroxidase (Rv1908c).

## Results
Phenotypic characterisation of the CRISPRi mutants showed that gradual tkt knockdown fully, led to growth disruption. Chemical-genetic interactions showed that tkt knockdown increased the antimycobacterial activity of acetone extracts from Peltophorum africanum and Croton gratissimus by twofold in CRISPRi hypomoprhs. Molecular docking data revealed that Phlorizin from C. gratissimus (-8.1 kcal/mol), Ficus sur tritepernoid (-9.6 kcal/mol) and P. africanum (6-hydroxydelphinidin 3-glucoside) (-8.9 kcal/mol) had the best binding affinities to the TKT active site pocket. Moreover, these compounds had better binding affinities to both NADH-dependent reductase and catalase-peroxidase than Isoniazid.

## Conclusion
The research demonstrated that the mycobacterial tkt gene is crucial for bacterial growth, and CRISPRi-mediated knockdown of this gene enhanced the anti-mycobacterial activity of phyto-compounds, which showed multiple binding affinities to established anti-Mtb targets.

## Introduction
Tuberculosis (TB), a disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb) that can be spread by the inhalation of aerosols that contain bacterial cells [ ]. Although it mostly affects adults in their most productive years, people of all age groups are at risk with over 80% of cases and deaths in low-and middle-income countries. Around 87% of TB incidence has been reported to emanate from the 30 high TB burden countries in South-East Asian Region (46%), African Region (23%) and followed by Western Pacific (18%) [ ]. While the reasons for the failure in TB drug discovery are multifactorial, some have been widely highlighted and studied such as the inability of drugs to penetrate the mycobacterial cell walls, the effect of drug efflux pumps, metabolism of the bacteria and insufficient chemical diversity in screening libraries [ ]. Research into Mtb metabolism is fascinating as it aims at identifying essential processes within the complex metabolic pathways and identifying potential vulnerabilities that might lead to novel intervention strategies. The discovery of bedaquiline as an antimycobacterial drug against the active and multi-drug TB demonstrated the efficiency of the disruption of metabolism as an effective drug target [ ]
Genetic tools used in molecular biology to disrupt enzymatic activities, and regulatory pathways had enabled the identification of targets that are essential for the survival and pathogenesis of Mtb [ ] and attractive approach. Tools such as RNA interference, expression of engineered transcription activator-like effector nucleases, zinc-finger nucleases and clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) technology are commonly used to study gene interaction mechanisms. CRISPR interference is more advantageous due to its compatibility to a large pool of microorganisms and enables high throughput in developing gene knockdowns for the study of gene functionality without inducing irreversible gene knockout [ , ].
CRISPRi technique involves the expression of small guide RNA (sgRNA) that targets and binds the target gene sequence in a genome by complementary nucleotide base pairing of 12–20 nucleotides whereby the sequence is homologous to the target gene [ , ]. Target specificity is determined by protospacer adjacent motif sequences (2–8 base pair) downstream the target DNA sequence [ ]. The presence of the inactive endonuclease enzyme, dCas9, at the sgRNA-DNA complex leads to the transcriptional block of the target gene [ ]. The DNA sequence is not altered, so when the cell naturally clears the dCas9 enzyme, the targeted gene is free to be expressed again [ ]. Rock et al. [ ] advanced an optimized CRISPR interference (CRISPRi) system for targeted silencing in Mtb genes using the plasmid PLJR962. The plasmid enables the control of sgRNA expression using anhydrotetracycline (ATc) [ ]. Thus, prioritizing essential and vulnerable gene targets for anti-TB drug screening may favour success rates in obtaining hit compounds [ ].
The transketolases (TKT) (Rv1449c) in Mtb are involved in the non-oxidative portion of the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP) by catalyzing two main reactions: 1) the transfer of a two-carbon ketol from D-xylulose-5-phosphate to D-ribose-5-phosphate, producing D-sedoheptulose-7-phosphate, and 2) the transfer of a two-carbon ketol from D-xylulose-5-phosphate to D-erythrose-4-phosphate producing fructose-6-phosphate [ ]. Through proteomic and two-dimensional liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry studies, a functional tkt gene and its encoded TKT enzymes were also identified in Mtb and were distinct from human TKT [ , ]. It has been shown that Mtb TKT and human TKT enzymes differ in three important ways: (1) the hydrophobic core that binds the pyrimidine ring of TPP in Mtb enzyme is more hydrophilic in the human TKT homolog, (2) the absence of five-histidine cluster from human TKT, (3) TKT in human is shorter than that of Mtb. [ ]. The mycobacterial TKT inhibitors can specifically target the bacterial enzyme without affecting the human enzyme, reducing the likelihood of side effects. Current TB drugs mostly target cell wall synthesis, protein synthesis, and DNA replication. Inhibiting TKT can disrupt the metabolic processes of Mtb, potentially leading to its death, especially under the stress conditions that the bacterium encounters within the host providing a novel mechanism of action. [ ]. This can be crucial in treating multi-drug resistant (MDR).
Medicinal plants are rich in bioactive compounds such as alkaloids, flavonoids, tannins, terpenoids, and polyphenolics. These natural compounds often have unique structures and mechanisms of action that can serve as templates or leads for new TB drugs. Alkaloids (oxysophocarpine, berberine, piperine), terpenoids (andrographolide, tanshinones IIA, ursolic acid), phenols (curcumin, luteolin, bergenin, baicalein, isoliquiritigenin) are under investigation against TB [ ]. In different studies, plant phenolics have been shown to have a dope-dependent inhibition of Mtb and M. smegmatis [ , ]. Peltophorum africanum Sond. is one of the widely known members from the Fabaceae family. The root and bark decoctions of P. afrianum have been reported to be used for the treatment of abdominal disorders, diarrhoea, rashes and blisters, venereal diseases, anthelmintic, and coughs [ ]. Croton gratissimus var gratissimus Burch. species is widely distributed in different parts of the world with varying ethnobotanical uses which include the treatment of cancer, constipation, diabetes, digestive problems, intestinal worms, pain, ulcers and weight loss [ ]. Ficus sur Forssk. is a medicinal tree traditionally used to treat various respiratory infections. The milky white latex from the bark, root and bark decoctions have been used to treat pulmonary TB, influenza and skin infections [ ].
The study aims to explore whether reducing the expression of the tkt gene can boost the bactericidal or bacteriostatic effects of certain plant-derived compounds, potentially uncovering new therapeutic strategies for treating mycobacterial infections. To that end, an optimized CRISPR-dCas9-mediated transcription interference system was utilized to transcriptionally knockdown the mycobacterial tkt gene [ ] using an integrating CRISPRi PLJR962 plasmid expressing tkt targeting sgRNA. The sgRNA is equipped with the targeting region and the dCas9 handle both under the control of the anhydro-tetracycline (ATc) inducible promoters. This approach revealed that prolonged knockdown of the mycobacterial tkt gene confirmed its essential role and vulnerability in M. smegmatis tkt hypomorphs, leading to increased sensitivity of M. smegmatis to medicinal plant extracts.

## Materials and methods

## Designing of oligonucleotides (Single-guide RNA)
The sequence of tkt (MSMEG_3103), the ortholog of Mtb tkt Rv144c was obtained from Mycobrowser (http://mycobrowser.epfl.ch). Cloning protocols optimized by Rock et al. [ ] were followed, whereby the non-template strand of the tkt gene was targeted and the selection of the PAM sequences was based on their differential fold repression strengths. The sequences of the sgRNA and PAM sequences with varying fold-repressions were designed as shown in Table . The oligonucleotides were amplified using a PCR program following a temperature increase to 95℃ for 2 min, a temperature decrease from 95 to 25℃ followed for annealing (T100™ Thermal Cycler, Bio-Rad, Cape Town). PLJR962 CRISPRi plasmid carried by Escherichia coli XL1 Blue strain were procured from Addgene (Contact: S007015, MTA Order: 582,237).

## Generation of recombinant tkt CRISPRi plasmids

## Plasmid digestion
The CRISPRi plasmid was cut at 37℃ for 6 h using the Esp3I endonuclease (5’ CGTCTC 3’) (Thermo Scientific™, Cape Town) in the presence of 1 mM DTT (Merk, Johannesburg). After restriction, the endonuclease was inactivated by heating the reaction at 65℃ for 20 min. The digested single stranded backbone was purified using 1% agarose gel electrophoresis at 95 V for 3 h and recovered using Zymoclean Gel DNA Recovery Kit (Inqaba (Pty) Ltd, Pretoria).

## Cloning tkt sgRNA into CRISPRi backbone
The recovered CRISPRi backbone (0.5 µL) was ligated with tkt oligonucleotides (1 µL) using T4 DNA ligase (NEB; 2,000,000 units/mL) (Inqaba (Pty) Ltd, Pretoria) 0.5 µL in 7 µL of nuclease-free water. The reaction was incubated overnight at room temperature. To thermally T4 DNA ligase was inactivated in a heat block at 65℃ for 10 min. To obtain the sgRNA-containing tkt sgRNA:PAM 1 and tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 CRISPRi plasmids, competent E. coli XL1 Blue were transformed using the heat-shock at 42℃ for 90 s. Recombinant plasmids were amplified using E. coli cells and their sequences were validated by using Sanger sequencing method (Central Analytical Facility at Stellenbosch University). Electroporation of recombinant CRISPRi plasmids was conducted using a 0.2 cm cuvette and Biorad Gene Pulser II set at 2500 V, 25 µF, and 1000 Ω. PLJR962: empty insert was used a positive control, and competent cells were used as negative controls. The Cultures were inoculated on OADC supplemented Middlebrook 7H10-KAN selective agar plates. Escherichia coli cells and culture plated on Luria Broth (LB) agar plates supplemented with Kanamycin (KAN) (Figure A). As expected, the cells that possessed the functional CRISPRi plasmids grew and were selected based on their KAN resistance. Similarly, successfully electroporated M. smegmatis mutants were grown on Middlebrook 7H10-KAN plates. Growth on the antibiotic supplemented plates was indicative of CRISPRi plasmid assimilation (Figure B).

## Phenotypic characterisation of the CRISPRi mutants

## Qualitative phenotypic characterisation of CRISPRi mutants
The spot assay was used to assess the functionality and essentiality of the tkt gene, in both M. smegmatis mutants after CRISPRi induction using ATc. The strains were grown to OD600 of 0.5–0.6 and serially diluted 100-fold (10–2 to 10–5) and inoculated (20 µL) on solid media with or without ATc (500 ng/mL) (Merk, Johannesburg). The mutant carrying an empty insert (control PLJR962) (sgRNA:empty insert) was used as a negative control. Growth on the plates was evaluated by incubating the plates at 37℃ for 48 h.

## Phenotypic characterisation of CRISPRi mutants using growth kinetic curves
CRISPRi mutants were grown to OD600 of 0.5–0.6 in complete liquid media. The cultures were then inoculated into fresh complete media with and without ATc to an OD600 of 0.1 and incubated at 37℃. The mutant carrying an empty insert (control PLJR962) (sgRNA:empty insert) was used as a negative control. The growth was monitored at 3, 6, 9, 18, and 24-h intervals using a UV/VIS spectrophotometer (AEC-Amersham SOC LTd, Johannesburg).

## Anhydrotetracycline titration of the CRISPRi mutants
CRISPRi mutants were cultured at 37℃ in a shaking incubator (200 rpm) to OD600 0.5–0.6 and were inoculated to a final OD600 0.1 into fresh complete liquid media supplemented with different concentrations of ATc (2–0.05 µg/mL). The mutant carrying an empty insert (control PLJR962) (sgRNA:empty insert) was used as a negative control. The flasks were shaken at 200 rpm, 37℃ for 24 h. OD600 were measured with UV/VIS spectrophotometer (AEC-Amersham SOC LTd, Johannesburg).

## RNA extraction protocol and RT-qPCR
Tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 M. smegmatis strain were cultured at 37℃ in a shaking incubator (IncoShake incubator, Labotec, Cape Town) 200 rpm until reaching 0.5–0.6 OD600. The culture was centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 10 min at 25℃. RNApro Solution/TRIzol (1 mL) was used to resuspend the pellet followed by cell lysis through ribolysation at a speed of 4 m/s for 40 s. Chloroform (300 μL) was added to the collected supernatant and centrifuged at 12,000 × g for 15 min the lysate collected. Pre-chilled 500 µL of ethanol was added to the reconstituted lysate transferred to a NucleoSpin RNA II column and centrifuged at 11,000 × g for 30 s. The supernatant was mixed with a membrane desalting buffer and centrifuged. The same centrifugation setups were used after each treatment with DNase solution (96 µL), RAW2 buffer (200 µL) and RAW3 buffer (600 µL) and during elution step. The RNA was quantified and sampled stored at −80 °C. The isolated RNA samples were used as temples for the generation of cDNA. TapeStation Analysis Software 4.1 and RNA ScreenTape® were used for RNA quantification (Central Analytical Facility (CAF) at the Stellenbosch University). The forward and reverse primers for qPCR were designed as shown in Table . The RNA samples were sent to Inqaba (Pty) Ltd, Pretoria.

## Plant collection and extraction
The selection of the plants used in this study, Croton gratissimus var. gratissimus Burch (Voucher number: SSS28), Ficus sur Forssk (Voucher number: SS89). and Peltophorum africanum Sond (Voucher number: SSS47) was based on the ethnopharmacological use for the treatment of tuberculosis. The plants were collected at Lowveld Botanical Garden, in Mpumalanga, South Africa. Plant collection adhered strictly to the ethical guidelines of the botanical garden, prioritizing sustainable practices and biodiversity preservation in compliance with the South African National Biodiversity Institute's (SANBI) protocols, under whose authorization the collection was conducted. The plants were identified by Dr. Egan Bronwyn, a botanist and the curator of the Larry Leach Herbarium, based at the University of Limpopo. Acetone was used for the extraction of dried and powdered leaves. Briefly, 1 g of plant material was extraction with 10 mL of acetone at ambient temperature. The mixture was shaken at 200 rpm for 30, 20 and 10 min. The plant extracts were filtered using Whatman No.1 filter into pre-weighed glass vials after every cycle.

## Chemical-genetic interactions

## Antimycobacterial activity of extracts and standard drugs
The broth microdilution assay using resazurin as growth indicator was used to determine the antimycobacterial activity of the extracts, amino-benzothiazole and naringin [ ]. Fresh cultures of CRISPRi mutants were grown to OD600 0.5–0.6 in complete Middlebrook 7H9 liquid medium. A 100-fold dilution of the cultures were prepared using complete Middlebrook 7H9 liquid medium. The stock solutions of plant extracts (20 mg/mL), amino-benzothiazole (10 mg/mL) (Merk, Johannesburg), Naringin (10 mg/mL) (Merk, Johannesburg), Rifampicin (1 mg/mL) (Merk, Johannesburg), ATc (2 mg/mL) (Merk, Johannesburg) were serially diluted in the microtiter plates such that the final volume of the solution is 100 µL. The positive control of the experiment was rifampicin. Prepared CRISPRi mutant cultures (100 µL) were added to the wells with the different concentrations of the test chemicals. Media and culture only wells were used as negative control. The prepared plates were incubated at 37 °C for 48 h. Aqueous resazurin (0.02% w/v) (Inqaba (Pty) Ltd, Pretoria) was added to the appropriate wells and plates were incubated for 4 h at 37℃. Viable bacterial cells convert the colour from blue to pink and no change in colour will occur in the presence of inhibited bacterial cells. MIC was determined at the lowest tested concentration that inhibit bacterial growth.

## Growth curves of CRISPRi mutants treated with shikimate inhibitors
Growth curves of the CRISPRi strains in the presence of for the shikimate pathway inhibitors was performed as described earlier. Different concentrations of 2-amino-benzothiazole (200–0.1 μg/mL) and Naringin (200–0.1 μg/mL) were used to treat the CRISPRi mutants in the presence of ATc (500 ng/mL). The mutant carrying an empty insert (control PLJR962) (sgRNA:empty insert) was used as a negative control. UV/VIS spectrophotometer (AEC-Amersham SOC LTd, Johannesburg) was used to measure OD600.

## Growth curves of CRISPRi mutants treated with plant extracts
Growth curves of CRSIPRi mutants, tkt sgRNA:PAM 1 and tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 in the presence of the extracts were investigated as described earlier. Briefly, the log phase tkt mutants culture were inoculated in complete Middlebrooks 7H9 liquid media with and without ATc (500 ng/mL) The sub-inhibitory concentration of the extracts (1 mg/mL) was used. The mutant carrying an empty insert (control PLJR962) (sgRNA:empty insert) was used as a negative control. The OD600 values were determined using a UV/VIS spectrophotometer (AEC-Amersham SOC LTd, Johannesburg). Results were expressed as means of duplicate ± standard deviation (SD) of two biological replicates.

## Phytochemical analysis of the crude extracts
The LC–MS analysis was carried out using a Waters Synapt G2 qTOF mass spectrometer as described in Matotoka et al. [ ]. A waters HSS T3 column, 2.1 × 150 mm was used and the sample flow rate was set at 0.4 mL/min. The MS spectra were acquired in the positive ion mode. The based software app ChemCalc (https://www.chemcalc.org/mf_finder/mfFinder_em_new) was used to calculate elemental composition possibilities using the accurate mass value of the compound from the spectra (Fig.  ). Metfrag (https://msbi.ipb-halle.de/MetFrag/) is an on-line in-silico fragmentation package that can also be used for compound identification based on fragmentation spectra and was used to retrieve possible candidates from the accurate mass elemental composition obtained from ChemCalc. The chemical database PubChem (www.pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov) was used to obtain the chemical structures.
 

## Cytotoxicity evaluation of extracts
Vero cells (African green monkey kidney cells, ATCC CCL-81), obtained from Stellenbosch University were cultured in Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium (Merk, Johannesburg, South Africa) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum. Vero cells were harvested by trypsinization. Plant extracts (200 mg/mL) were prepared in DMSO (Merk, Johannesburg) and filter sterilized. Vero cells (100 µL) were seeded in a 96-well microtiter plate to 4 × 104 cells/well and incubated for 24 h in a 5% CO2 incubator set at 37℃. MTT solution (5 mg/mL) (20 µL) was added to appropriate wells and plates were incubated for 4 h under optimal conditions. The liquid was aspirated and DMSO (100 µL) was added to the wells to solubilize the formazan product. A microplate reader was used to measure the absorbance of the produced formazan at a wavelength of 570 nm. Untreated cells were used as negative control and media only was used as a blank in the experiment. Results were obtained from three independent experiments and duplicate assays were performed for each experiment [ ]. GraphPad Prism v. 9.5.0 was used to calculate the LC50 (lethal concentration 50%) through non-linear regression. The LC50 is the concentration of a substance that is expected to cause death in 50% of a test population, often used in toxicity studies.

## Molecular docking
The three-dimensional structure of the ligands obtained from LC/MS chromatogram were downloaded from pubchem database. The structures of all the compound were energy minimized by BIOVIA Discovery studio 4.1. The ligand was then converted into PDBQT format using Auto Dock 4.2 (MGL tools1.5.6). The crystal structure of receptor molecule Mtb transketolase (Rv1449c, PDB DOI https://doi.org/10.2210/pdb3rim/pdb), Mtb catalase-peroxidase (katG) (Rv1908c, PDB DOI: https://doi.org/10.2210/pdb1SJ2/pdb) and M. tuberculosis NADH-dependent enoyl-[acyl-carrier-protein] reductase (InhA) (Rv1484, PDB DOI: https://doi.org/10.2210/pdb3FNG/pdb) were downloaded from protein data bank. The protein preparation was done by removing the co-crystallized ligands, water molecules and cofactors. The protein structure was then converted into PDBQT format using Auto Dock 4.2 (MGL tools1.5.6) [ ]. Molecular docking of the compounds was conducted in a grid box created to cover the whole of the active site pocket. All compounds were docked within the active pocket of the receptors using AutoDock’s default genetic algorithm as the scoring function. The most stable configuration possessing high affinity was selected for the further analysis and development of 2D and 3D models to understand the binding interactions of the compound within the active site of proteins using BIOVIA Discovery Studios.

## Results and discussion

## Phenotypic characterisation of CRISPRi mutants

## Spot assay
To phenotypically characterize the tkt sgRNA:PAM 1 and tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 mutants a qualitative spot assay was conducted. The growth and viability of M. smegmatis sgRNA:empty insert (control PLJR962) was not visibly affected by the presence of ATc suggesting that the induction of dCas9Spy in M. smegmatis may not be toxic to the cells. Both the tkt sgRNA:PAM 1 and tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 strains showed defects in their growth in the presence of ATc. The silencing of the tkt gene by both strong and weak PAMs lead to visible distortion of culture growth thus substantiating the essentiality of the tkt gene for in vitro growth of M. smegmatis (Fig.  A). Minor variations in spotting technique, inoculum density, or agar drying likely explain differences in colony intensity between control PLJR962 replicates, particularly at lower dilutions; however, both panels consistently show robust growth of the control strain with or without ATc, confirming that ATc and dCas9 expression alone do not affect viability. Our results are in support of a previous study by Kolly et al. [ ], where it was shown that the depletion of TKT using the RNA silencing and protein degradation (RSPD) system was able to arrest Mtb growth. The Mtb TKT enzymes are important in the decaprenyl-phospho-D-arabinofuranose (DPA) biosynthetic pathway [ ]. While Kolly et al. [ ] previously demonstrated the essentiality of the tkt gene in Mtb using an inducible protein degradation system, our study builds upon this foundational work by employing CRISPRi system to transcriptionally repress tkt. This approach enables a tunable and reversible knockdown, offering advantages in assessing gene vulnerability over time without inducing permanent genetic changes.
 

## Growth-kinetic curves
To further analyse the phenotypic growth of the CRISPRi strains, their growth was monitored in liquid media with and without ATc for 24 h, and the growth patterns were recorded. In the first 9 h of incubation, the growth patterns of the CRISPRi hypomorphs did not differ significantly from the negative controls (Fig.  B,C). The kinetics of gene knockdown have been reported to be dependent on the accumulation and rate of dCas9 expression [ ]. This could have explained the non-significant gene silencing during the lag phase of incubation. After 18 h, significant growth inhibition was observed, indicating that phenotypic growth inhibition due to tkt gene silencing may require at least 18 h of incubation. The results support previous work [ ], where peak gene silencing was suggested to be at the 18 h of ATc treatment. One of the important characteristics of a vulnerable gene target is that even sgRNA with low fold repressions induce growth disruption on the test strains. In that regard, the tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 strain showed that the tkt silencing with a weaker PAM sequence (64.5 fold repression) can lead to significant in vitro growth inhibition.

## Treatment of hypomorphs with different ATc concentrations
Different concentration of ATc were used to treat the CRISPRi mutants and was determined that growth inhibition was concentration dependent (Fig.  D). Our results demonstrated that ATc treatment up to 500 ng/mL does not affect in vitro growth of the strains. The optimum concentration of ATc for the maximum repression of tkt was taken as 500 ng/mL. McNeil and Cook [ ] and Singh et al. [ ] also reported observing a dose–response between ATc concentrations and mutant cultures. The increased cell death at higher concentrations of ATc may be due to the overexpression of the dCas9 cascade. Previous studies corroborate that overexpression of dCas9 from S. pyrogenes has cidal effects on M. smegmatis and poor expression of the dCas9 in a CRISPRi system may lead to low knockdown efficiencies [ ]. Therefore, the expression of dCas9 requires to be at concentrations that do not impede with the normal bacterial growth but are adequate to accomplish consistent and physiologically relevant inhibition of gene expression.

## RNA isolation and RT-qPCR
To confirm the transcriptional suppression of the tkt gene, transcript levels were measured in the tkt sgRNA 2 strain, which exhibited significantly reduced growth upon silencing, in contrast to the tkt sgRNA 1 strain. Agarose gel electrophoresis of isolated messenger ribonucleic acid (mRNA) showed the 16S and 23S subunits (Fig.  A). Confirmed by low fold change, in tkt gene expression of tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 strain compared to untreated empty vector control (Fig.  B). RT-qPCR revealed that the CRISPRi system achieved a significant reduction of tkt expression in the tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 after ATc for 24 h. The observations are in line with previous work by Singh et al. [ ] and Choudhary et al. [ ] which demonstrated that transcriptional silencing of essential genes in M. smegmatis hypomorphs results in growth inhibition. Although we did not observe unexpected phenotypic changes in the sgRNA:PAM1 and sgRNA:PAM2 mutants beyond tkt-specific repression, we acknowledge that off-target effects cannot be fully excluded without whole transcriptome or genome-level analysis. However, the use of a non-targeting control plasmid and consistent phenotypic responses across both PAM1 and PAM2 support the specificity of the CRISPRi system in this context.
 Protein-level validation (e.g., via Western blot) was not conducted in this study. Future work should include immunoblotting to confirm the reduction in TKT protein and complement the observed transcript-level and phenotypic effects.

## Minimum inhibitory concentrations of extracts and standard drugs
The antimycobacterial activity of the selected plant extracts, ATc, rifampicin and shikimate inhibitors were determined against the CRISPRi mutants. The MICs of all the acetone crude extracts (1250 μg/mL), rifampicin (1.56 μg/mL), amino-benzothiazole (320 μg/mL), naringin (1250 μg/mL) remained unchanged between the tkt sgRNA:PAM 1 and tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 strains (Table  ). The antimycobacterial activity of the extracts improved two-fold (MIC 630 µg/mL) against the CRISPRi hypomorphs in the presence of ATc (500 ng/mL) provides strong potential evidence of a synergistic interaction. It is important to clarify that CRISPRi-mediated tkt knockdown is used here as a mechanistic tool to investigate the vulnerability of tkt and assess whether its depletion increases bacterial susceptibility to phytochemicals. Our goal was to assess whether tkt depletion increases bacterial sensitivity to phytochemicals, thereby revealing its potential as a druggable metabolic vulnerability. The observed synergy highlights tkt as a promising target, rather than suggesting genetic knockdown as a co-treatment. This approach offers a useful screening framework for identifying metabolic nodes that can enhance the activity of low-potency natural product leads.
 Mtb H37Rv strain was inhibited by C. gratissimus (1250 μg/mL), F. sur (2500 μg/mL) and P. africanum (2500 μg/mL) (Table  ). ATc is a derivative of tetracycline that is generally known to have no antibiotic activity [ ]. In this study, both M. smegmatis tkt sgRNA:PAM 1 and tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 hypomorphs were susceptible to ATc with MIC values of 0.976 and 0.488 µg/mL respectively. In a study by McNeil and Cook [ ], ATc concentrations as low as 1 ng/mL were shown to affect Mtb hypomorphs containing the mmpL3-targeting sgRNA. This showed that compared to mmpL3, a higher level of tkt depletion was required to inhibit mycobacterial growth. In addition, MAB_0055c-sgRNA strain is inhibited at ATc concentrations of ≥ 150 ng/mL [ ].
The 2-amino-benzothiazole have been shown to inhibit mycobacterial growth by inhibiting the shikimate kinase (aroK) which is downstream of TKT [ , ]. The tkt knockdown in the CRISPRi strains had no synergistic effects with the activity of shikimate inhibitors (amino and naringin) and rifampicin (Table  ) as the MIC values remained unchanged between the gene knockdown strains treated with ATc and untreated. This indicated that the depletion of the tkt gene did not potentiate the activity of the used drugs, nor did it sensitize the mutants. Compared to the standard drugs, the selected medicinal plant extracts had low antimycobacterial activity against the CRISPRi strains (MIC 1.25–2.5 mg/mL). However, the tkt knockdown increased the antimycobacterial activity of the extracts by twofold in both tkt sgRNA:PAM 1 and tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 strains (MIC 0.63 mg/mL).

## Investigation of the growth-kinetics of CRISPRi mutants
Without activation of the CRISPRi system (-ATc), the sub-MICs of the crude extracts (1 mg/mL) did not inhibit or significantly alter the growth kinetics of tkt sgRNA:PAM 1 (Fig.  A) and tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 (Fig.  B) strains during a 24 h incubation. Upon adding ATc to trigger gene knockdown in the tkt sgRNA:PAM 1 and tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 strains, the crude extracts showed enhanced inhibitory activity against the hypomorphs. C. gratissimus (1 mg/mL) had the highest inhibition followed by P. africanum (1 mg/mL) and F. sur (1 mg/mL) (Fig.  C). Our findings preliminarily establish a functional connection between tkt gene knockdown and enhanced antimycobacterial activity of the plant extracts. The CRISPRi-mediated depletion of tkt leads to metabolic vulnerability in M. smegmatis, particularly affecting the pentose phosphate pathway. This compromised metabolic state likely sensitizes the bacteria to additional external stresses, such as the phytochemicals in C. gratissimus and P. africanum. Sub-minimal inhibitory concentrations (sub-MICs) are vital to investigate the effect of drugs on bacterial pathways and gene expression. Many studies have shown that sub-MICs of antibiotics can act as signal molecules and may alter their physicochemical characteristics and expression of bacterial virulence [ – ]. Accordingly, the sub-MIC of 1 mg/mL of the extracts was selected and the growth-kinetic curves showed that the concentration had no significant effect on the growth patterns of the CRISPRi mutants compared to the untreated culture. It is acknowledged that the MIC values of the crude plant extracts (ranging from 630–2500 µg/mL) exceed the typical thresholds of 100–300 µg/mL cited in drug discovery literature, including the work by Tekwu et al. [ ]. However, the purpose of this study was not to present these crude extracts as final therapeutic candidates, but rather to explore whether genetic depletion of the tkt gene could potentiate antimicrobial activity even in compounds with modest standalone antimycobacterial effects. The observed activity of the sub-MICs upon tkt knockdown highlight the potential synergistic interactions and chemical-genetic sensitization, which serve as valuable leads for future in silico studies, bioassay guided fractionation, purification, and structure elucidation of active compounds. Thus, while the crude MICs may not meet strict hit criteria, the observed potentiation supports the broader goal of identifying metabolic targets that enhance natural product efficacy.
 Gene knockdown in tkt sgRNA:PAM 1 and tkt sgRNA:PAM 2 strains using ATc increased the sensitivity of the hypomorphs to the sub-MICs of the crude extracts, where P. africanum and C. gratissimus showed significant growth inhibition (> 75%) after 24 h incubation (Fig.  C). In Yuliani et al. [ ] report combinations of ATc and one-half (1/2) of the MIC of various antimycobacterial drugs such as isoniazid, ceftriaxone, rifampicin resulted in a significant decrease in the viability M. smegmatis mmpL3 CRISPRi hypomorphs. Different studies have showed that silencing genes using CRISPRi can lead to increased susceptibility of Mycobacterial species to antibiotics [ , ]. The increased antimycobacterial activity of the plant extracts demonstrated that CRISPRi/dCas9 mediated essential gene knockdown can be as an effective tool to potentiate/improve inhibitory activity of compounds with moderate antimycobacterial activity and may, in future, provide a useful preliminary information about existence and subsequent purification of specific enzyme inhibitors contained in mixtures such as natural product extracts. These results align with previous work [ ] which support that tkt is an attractive target due to its essentiality, vulnerability and druggability. M. smegmatis served as the model organism in this study; however, its metabolic profile differs from that of Mtb. Consequently, the findings should be validated in Mtb infection models in subsequent studies.
 

## Chemical-genetic interaction with shikimate pathway inhibitors
M. smegmatis tkt CRISPRi mutants were treated with different concentrations of shikimate inhibitors in the presence of ATc. A clear dose–response was observed between the shikimate pathway inhibitors and culture growth. Corresponding to its MIC, 2-amino-benzothiazole was more efficacious at inhibiting M. smegmatis hypomorphs than naringin (Fig.  D and ). At a concentration of 20 µg/mL, 2-amino-benzothiazole and C. gratissimus crude extract demonstrated synergistic interaction after 24 h incubation with the CRISPRi strains (Fig.  F). There was a general lack of synergy between 2-amino-benzothiazole and the rest of the tested extracts from the different plant species.
By including the shikimate inhibitors as standards, the results validated that indeed inhibition of the shikimate pathway leads to growth inhibition. The titration of the inhibitors was further important to establish a sub-MIC which would combine with the plant extracts to evaluate possible synergy. Drug combinations are increasingly used in the treatment of TB. The 2-amino-benzothiazole (20 µg/mL) and C. gratissimus acetone extract (1 mg/mL) resulted in synergistic effects against both M. smegmatis tkt mutants after 24 h of incubation (Fig.  C). TKT is a crucial enzyme in the non-oxidative phase of the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP), which generates precursors for nucleotide and amino acid biosynthesis. The shikimate pathway is interconnected with the PPP through common intermediates, particularly in the synthesis of aromatic amino acids. By using a shikimate inhibitor, one can assess how disrupting TKT affects the broader metabolic network, including the shikimate pathway. However, the combination of F. sur and P. africanum extracts with 2-amino-benzothiazole resulted in antagonistic (activity suppression) effects. It is well-known that medicinal plants are targeting microbes through the combined actions of structurally and functionally diverse active compounds that act at multiple targets. The chemical complexity and variability of crude extracts may have facilitated the antagonism with 2-amino-benzothiazole. The occurrence of antagonism between extracts from different plant species (Hibiscus sabdariffa L., Kaempferia galanga L., and Piper crocatum N.E. Br) and anti-TB drugs such as streptomycin, ethambutol, and isoniazid has been reported elsewhere [ ]. Antagonism between antibacterial drugs and the crude extracts can be a significant challenge in developing effective antimycobacterial therapies. Extracts may need to be fractionated/or purified to reduce possible off-targets brought about by the bioactivity of the different phyto-compounds [ ].
 

## Cytotoxicity evaluation
The Vero cell line was used to determine the cytotoxicity of the plant crude extracts. At 100 μg/mL and 500 μg/mL, C. gratissimus and P. africanum extracts were non-toxic. However, F. sur significantly reduced cell viability at all tested concentrations (p < 0.001) (Fig.  ). Recent toxicological studies have set the threshold of cytotoxic concentrations of extracts, such that IC50 concentrations below 20 µg/mL are to be regarded as toxic, and above 20 µg/mL to be non-toxic as previously reported [ , ]. Studies by Bizimenyera et al. [ ] corroborate the non-cytotoxicity of the acetone leaf extract of P. africanum on Vero monkey cell line and the brine shrimp larval mortality assay. Moreover, low toxicity of P. africanum was also reported by Adebayo et al. [ ]. The methanolic extract of F. sur fruit was found to be toxic using the higher grade of heart rate reduction method and caution was recommended on the use of F. sur [ ]. In addition, F. sur ethanol extract was reported to have toxic, deleterious and fatal effects at doses beyond 1000 mg/kg body weight on adult mice after 24-h treatment [ ]. In a different study, C. gratissimus acetone leaf was reported to have an IC50 of 462.88 ± 7.71 μg/mL on Vero cell line [ ].
 

## Phytochemical analysis of plant extracts
LC–MS chromatograms were used to identify possible compounds in the crude extracts (Fig.  ). Several polyphenolics were identified, notably: barbatic acid, phlorizin, hirsutidin in C. gratissimus, and 6-hydroxydelphinidin 3-glucoside and 3-dimethylallyl-4-hydroxybenzoate in P. africanum. Triterpenoids were detected in F. sur at 8.61 min and in C. gratissimus at 7.68 min. In addition, a diterpenoid carboxylic acid, gibberellin A38, was detected at 6.72 min in C. gratissimus (Table  ). Gibberellins (gibberellic acids) are diterpenoids with broad-spectrum antibacterial activity against phytopathogens and clinical pathogens [ ]. Previous studies have reported several diterpenoids in C. gratissimus such as cembranoid, labdane, clerodane, kaurane, halimane, isopimarane, neoclerodane, phorbol, secokaurane, and trachylobane compounds [ ]. The mechanism of action of terpenoids is not fully understood but is speculated to involve membrane disruption by the lipophilic compounds, disruption of the protein motive force, and coagulation of cell contents [ ]. Phlorizin exerts its antibacterial activity by reducing intracellular DNA agglutination, reducing intracellular protein and energy synthesis, and destroying intracellular metabolism [ ].
 

## Molecular docking
Computational studies like molecular docking help to predict the best binding energies and affinities as well as a better framework between protein and ligand interactions [ ]. Targeting the tkt gene allowed for the study of its transcriptional regulation and expression, while targeting the protein would enable the understanding of its functional role within the cell. This dual approach provides a complete picture of how the gene contributes to cellular processes and how its protein product exerts its effects. Molecular docking aids in studying the protein by predicting and analyzing its interactions with other molecules. In general, a ligand-receptor complex with a more negative binding energy is expected to exhibit greater stability. This is because a stronger binding energy reflects stronger attractive forces between the ligand and receptor, which helps stabilize the complex in its bound state [ ].
In the present work, the molecular docking data revealed that Phlorizin (−8.1 kcal/mol) from C. gratissimus, triterpenoid from F. sur (−9.6 kcal/mol) and (6-hydroxydelphinidin 3-glucoside) (−8.9 kcal/mol) from P africanum had the best binding affinities to the TKT active site pocket (Table  ). In a different study it was shown that sulfo-substituted compounds form a hydrogen bond with the His85 residue in the TKT enzyme. In addition, favourable hydrophobic contacts with the side chain of Ile211, Leu402, and Phe464 take place [ ]. Here, phlorizin (C. gratissimus), F. sur tritepernoid and P africanum (6-hydroxydelphinidin 3-glucoside) form covalent hydrogen bonds with ARG residues of the active site, whereas hydrophobic interactions involve HIS and VAL where appropriate (Fig.  ). Hydrogen bonds are relatively stronger than hydrophobic interactions [ ] thus the interaction of the phyto-compounds by forming covalent hydrogen bonds may explain the low binding energies obtained. The 2D and 3D visualisations of the ligand-receptor interactions showed that forces that could have contributed to the phytochemical’s strong affinity for the enzymes include hydrogen bonds, Van der Waals, and cation-pi formed with other amino acid residues of the protein (Figs.  , , ).
 The proteins encoded by essential genes may exhibit lethal phenotypes in Mtb upon inactivation or deletion, making these gene products excellent targets for drug development [ , ]. Isoniazid and ethionamide, which form the backbone of the TB treatment regimens, are both clinically confirmed as InhA targets [ ] and the enzyme inhibition in mycobacteria has been clinically demonstrated to be one of the most successful mechanisms of action against Mtb. Antibiotic resistance primarily occurs due to mutations in the catalase-peroxidase enzyme (KatG) that is required for INH activation [ ]. Therefore, the LC–MS identified compounds from the different plants were subjected to in silico computational analysis against the enoyl-ACP reductase (InhA) and catalase-reductase (KatG) of Mtb.
Phlorizin from C. gratissimus, the triterpenoid from F. sur, and 6-hydroxydelphinidin 3-glucoside from P. africanum demonstrated significant binding affinities for the active site pockets of both reductase and catalase-peroxidase. Pitaloka and coworkers [ ] demonstrated that different triterpenoids have notable binding affinities for Leu63, Gly96, and Lys165, Ala191 amino acid residues on the reductase binding site. Substrate Binding Pocket consists of hydrophobic residues that include I202, M161, I215, M103, Y158, M199, L218, F149, A198 [ ]. In this study, we show that F. sur triterpenoid interacted with this binding site using hydrophobic forces (ILE, A16) and (ALA,198) (Fig.  B) and with catalase/reductase active site by hydrogen bonds with GLY(A34) and GLN (A36) (Fig.  B). The most frequent isoniazid resistance mechanism is mutation in the 315th amino acid in the KatG gene found in 64.2% of resistance specimens, and 95.3% cases are due to serine-to-threonine mutation. The rest of the mutations cause amino acid changes to asparagine (3.6%), isoleucine (0.5%), arginine (0.4%), and glycine (0.2%) [ ]. The results of this current study strongly support future in vitro studies to evaluate the efficacy of the hit compounds for treating INH-resistant Mtb strains and targeting TKT enzyme inhibition. Immunoblotting is further recommended for future work to validate the interaction between the compounds and the target proteins and complement findings from the computational docking studies.

## Conclusion
The study demonstrated the essentiality of the tkt gene in M. smegmatis using CRISPRi as a gene knockdown strategy. Silencing of tkt in hypomorphs potentiated the antimycobacterial activity of selected medicinal plant extracts, with sub-MICs showing increased bacteriostatic effects. Moreover, phlorizin from C. gratissimus, F. sur triterpenoid and P africanum (6-hydroxydelphinidin 3-glucoside) had notable binding affinities to the TKT, InhA and KatG active site pockets demonstrating their potential to inhibitory activities. This necessitates further purification and/or isolation of the docked compounds to validate their anti-TB drugs. One of the main challenges with targeting the shikimate pathway is that the same AAA (L- tryptophan, L-tyrosine, and L-phenylalanine) that are essential for Mtb viability, are also essential to animals and humans. Therefore, future work should investigate the capacity of tkt-depleted strains to assimilate exogenous intermediates.
