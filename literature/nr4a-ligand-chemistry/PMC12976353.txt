## Abstract
The involvement of intestinal microbiota in the process of neutrophil-mediated colorectal cancer liver metastasis (CRCLM) is not yet fully understood. Here, we show that Escherichia coli (E. coli) is prevalent in CRC tissues with LM using 2bRAD-M-Seq and is linked to the release of neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs). Utilizing multi-omics and molecular techniques, we establish that E. coli recruits RIPK2, which promotes the binding of HNRNPK to the Atf3/Relb promoters in neutrophils, thereby enhancing their transcription. This process results in the upregulation of Ncf4, which triggers p-MLKL-mediated NET formation. NETs, in turn, increase the expression of TRPC1 and NFATC3 in CRC cells, promoting the calcium-dependent assembly of the STAT3/S100A8/9 heterotrimer. This trimer stabilizes STAT3-enhancer-promoter loops (EPLs), thereby reinforcing the Tns1 transcription and facilitating CRCLM. Our findings elucidate the mechanism by which E. coli-induced NETs promote CRCLM through epigenetic modifications, offering an insight into the role of EPLs in immune regulation and tumor progression.
Subject terms: Cancer microenvironment, Colon cancer, Bacterial pathogenesis

Intestinal flora is known to play a role in colorectal cancer metastasis via disruption of the intestinal barrier. Here, the authors discover that Escherichia coli promotes colorectal cancer liver metastasis by promoting neutrophil extracellular trap formation.

## Introduction
Intestinal flora plays a key role in colorectal cancer (CRC) metastasis by providing nutrients and inhibiting immune responses, fostering a tumor-supportive environment that allows CRC cells to evade detection . This occurs mainly through the disruption of the intestinal barrier, facilitating CRC cell spread . Neutrophils, the primary immune cells against bacterial infections, interact closely with intestinal flora and are pivotal in regulating tumor metastasis . They express pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) like TLRs and Dectin-1 and are activated by opsonized particles, immune complexes, and chemokines . While their role in neutrophil extracellular trap (NET) release remains unclear, PRR activation leads to degranulation, ROS production, and inflammation to combat pathogens . Understanding neutrophil response mechanisms is essential for discovering therapeutic targets for cancers linked to abnormal neutrophil activation.
Calcium influx and S100A8/S100A9, a calcium-binding heterodimeric protein complex, are emerging as critical regulators in tumor progression. Physiological calcium signaling maintains cellular homeostasis, but dysregulated influx (via channels like TRPV, Orai) activates pro-oncogenic pathways (e.g., NF-κB, MAPK), upregulating S100A8/A9 expression , . S100A8/A9, released by myeloid cells or tumor cells, acts as a damage-associated molecular pattern, driving inflammation, immune evasion, angiogenesis, and metastasis by binding receptors (e.g., RAGE, TLR4) and amplifying calcium flux through TRP channel modulation , . However, key questions persist:(1) Spatiotemporal dynamics of calcium-S100A8/A9 crosstalk during tumor evolution and therapy resistance remain uncharacterized;(2) Differential roles of S100A8/A9 homodimers vs. heterodimers in calcium signaling are poorly defined;(3) Off-target effects limit therapeutic targeting of this axis (e.g., S100 inhibitors in clinical trials);(4) Whether S100A8/A9 contributes to calcium-driven tumorigenesis in non-inflammatory contexts is unclear , . Elucidating these gaps will advance precision strategies to disrupt calcium-S100A8/A9 interactions in cancer.
Enhancers and promoters form physical loops (enhancer-promoter loops, EPLs) via chromatin folding to precisely regulate gene expression, a process critically dysregulated in cancer. Aberrant EPLs drive oncogenesis by hyperactivating oncogenes (e.g., MYC, EGFR) through super-enhancer stabilization or silencing tumor suppressors (e.g., TP53) via enhancer hijacking, while mutations in chromatin remodelers (e.g., Cohesin, CTCF) further perturb loop architecture to rewire transcriptional programs , . Despite advances, key questions remain unresolved:(1) The spatiotemporal dynamics of EPLs during tumor evolution and therapy response are poorly characterized, limiting understanding of adaptive transcriptional reprogramming;(2) Tumor heterogeneity leads to context-dependent EPL configurations, yet pan-cancer principles are lacking;(3) Targeting EPLs via phase separation modulators (e.g., Cohesin inhibitors) faces challenges of off-target effects and incomplete efficacy ; (4) How cell interactions reshape EPLs in the tumor microenvironment remains unclear. Addressing these gaps is essential for translating EPL biology into precision oncology.
This study found that Escherichia coli (E. coli) from primary lesions in CRC liver metastasis (CRCLM) was crucial to the metastatic process. It recruited RIPK2, promoting NCF4-mediated NET release, which raised TRPC1 and NFATC3 expression and calcium influx in CRC cells. Calcium influx facilitated the S100A8/9 dimerization, activating STAT3 to form a STAT3/S100A8/9 heterotrimer, which enhanced STAT3-related enhanced-promoter loopsfor TNS1 transcription, thereby promoting CRCLM. This research highlighted E. coli’s role in CRC evasion and tumor immunity interaction, suggesting a potential therapeutic approach for CRC immune escape.

## Results

## E. coli triggered NET release to promote CRCLM
To identify bacteria associated with CRCLM, we analyzed 24 tissues with and without liver metastasis (LM) using type IIB restriction endonucleases site-associated DNA sequencing (2bRAD-M-Seq) (Supplementary Data  ). Principal coordinate analysis (PCA) revealed distinct microbiota between patients with and without LM (Fig.  ), indicating that there were differences in the composition of the microbiota in CRC tissues with and without LM. We then examined the link between clinical factors, dietary habits, or genetic mutations and microbiome diversity. The smoking patients showed lower Chao1, Shannon, and Simpson indices than non-smoking people, indicating smoking disrupted the α-diversity of the microbiota (Supplementary Fig.  , Supplementary Data  ). Other factors, including gender, age, tumor volume, alcohol consumption, diabetes, preoperative chemotherapy, genetic mutations, pathological grading, and T or N stage, didn’t influence the microbiome α-diversity (Supplementary Fig.  , Supplementary Data  ). Subsequently, we investigated the differential abundance of bacterial species between the LM and non-LM groups, specifically focusing on their contribution to CRCLM. Notably, E. coli and Klebsiella pneumoniae (K. pneumoniae) exhibited increased prevalence in cases with LM (Figs.  b, c, Supplementary Fig.  , Supplementary Table  ). To elucidate the effects of these two bacterial species, we developed various murine models to identify the most appropriate one for our study. We found that LM occurred only with MC38 cell injection into the posterior rectal wall (Supplementary Fig.  ). Furthermore, E. coli and K. pneumoniae were found to promote LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression, with E. coli being the most impactful (Supplementary Fig.  –h). We then created an LM model in huHSC-C-NKG-ProF mice using human neutrophil infusions, showing that results from C57BL/6 J mice were replicable in humanized immune system mice (Supplementary Fig.  –l). Moreover, E. coli was elevated in the portal vein and liver of patients with LM (Supplementary Fig.  , n), indicating that it migrated retrograde from the intestine through the portal vein to the liver. This suggested that E. coli still played a carcinogenic role in metastatic foci.
 Next, we examined how E. coli contributes to CRCLM, finding it reduced E-Cadherin and increased N-Cadherin and Vimentin levels in mouse CRC tissues (Fig.  d–f). Previous report has indicated that in vitro stimulation of CRC cells by E. coli didn’t alter Cadherin protein expression ; however, our findings suggested that the in vivo stimulation facilitated such alterations, suggesting E. coli relied on the tumor microenvironment (TME) to enhance CRC malignancy. To eliminate the confounding effects of other microorganisms, we compared metastasis progression following the removal or retention of the entire intestinal microbiota, followed by the transplantation of E. coli. We found that E. coli promoted LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression, irrespective of prior antibiotic use (Supplementary Fig.  , ). It is well-established that symbiotic bacteria play a synergistic role in immune regulation . We defined the bacteria whose content in CRC tissues was highly correlated with E. coli (with R value > 0.8 and P value < 0.05) as symbiotic bacteria . K. pneumoniae, Sphingomonas aerolata, and Lutimaribacter sp023703375 were identified as its symbionts (Supplementary Fig.  ). Only K. pneumoniae showed a cancer-promoting effect like E. coli, but weaker (Supplementary Fig.  –f). Similar results were found in huHSC-C-NKG-ProF mice (Supplementary Fig.  –i).
E. coli causes inflammation and attracts immune cells to tumors, affecting tumor immunity , . Therefore, we investigated whether E. coli promotes metastasis through specific immune cells by selectively removing macrophages, neutrophils, or T cells. E. coli was indicated to increase LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression, but this was reduced by removing macrophages or neutrophils, and the depletion of macrophages or neutrophils didn’t influence each other’s infiltration (Supplementary Fig.  –g). In contrast, removing T cells increased LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression (Supplementary Fig.  –c, –g). Furthermore, neutrophil removal had a stronger inhibitory effect than macrophage removal, a finding consistent in huHSC-C-NKG-ProF mice (Supplementary Fig.  –c, –j). Therefore, we focused on E. coli’s role in promoting metastasis via neutrophils, as bacterial infection triggers NET release , aiding tumor evasion . We found that disrupting NETs with DNase I or the PAD4 inhibitor GSK484 hydrochloride reduced LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression (Supplementary Fig.  ). Furthermore, E. coli boosted NET release in CRC (Fig.  ), and E. coli abundance was positively correlated with the number of NETs (Supplementary Data  – , Fig.  ), but its effects on Cadherins and Vimentin were weakened by neutrophil depletion (Fig.  i–l). Therefore, we believed that E. coli induced CRCLM by promoting NET release.
Subsequently, we employed multicenter clinical samples to investigate the association between E. coli abundance and epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) marker expression or NET levels in CRC tissues. In CRC tissues with LM obtained from our two centers, we observed an increase in E. coli and NET abundance, a decrease in E-Cadherin, and an elevation in N-Cadherin and Vimentin levels compared to non-LM tissues (Supplementary Fig.  –e, Supplementary Data  , ). E. coli abundance correlated positively with NETs levels, and N-Cadherin or Vimentin expression, but negatively with E-Cadherin levels (Supplementary Fig.  , Supplementary Data  , ). Moreover, higher E. coli or NET levels indicated poor prognosis (Supplementary Fig.  , Supplementary Data  , ). These results were consistent with findings from other centers (Supplementary Fig.  –n, Supplementary Data  ). We then examined the link between clinical factors, dietary habits, or genetic mutations and either the occurrence of CRCLM or the prevalence of E. coli to identify any unknown factors affecting both latter. Our findings showed that older age, mucous adenocarcinoma and diabetes increased metastasis risk, while the absence of lymph node metastasis or reduced E. coli abundance lowered the risk, with the latter having the most significant protective effect (Supplementary Data  ). Additionally, skipping preoperative chemotherapy reduced the risk of infection by E. coli (Supplementary Data  ), as some drugs like 5-fluorouracil suppress fast-growing bacteria , potentially enabling drug-resistant E. coli to thrive and increase infection risk. Overall, E. coli contributed to CRCLM.
Neutrophils are known to express PRRs and are activated by bacterial infection , so we studied how E. coli affects PRR expression and found it increased NOD1/2 and TLR2/4 levels in neutrophils (Fig.  ). Using inhibitors on tumor-bearing mice, we discovered that blocking NOD1/2 (NOD1/2 antagonist-1) more effectively reduced NET formation in normal colorectal or CRC tissues than inhibiting TLR2/4 (Sparstolonin B) (Supplementary Fig.  ). Moreover, inhibiting NOD1/2 weakened the NET release induced by E. coli, and this effect was stronger than that of inhibiting TLR2/4 (Supplementary Fig.  ). Therefore, our focus was then on the role of RIPK2, a downstream molecule of NOD1/2 , in E. coli-induced CRCLM. Since RIPK2 is crucial in pathogen defense , we first evaluated its off-target effects. Our results showed that altering RIPK2 in neutrophils didn’t change systemic immune cell counts post-infection (Supplementary Fig.  , c), indicating it was safe. Functional assays revealed that Ripk2 conditional knockout (cKO) in neutrophils reduced E. coli’s effect on EMT marker expression and NET release in CRC tissues, while Ripk2 conditional knock-in (cKI) in neutrophils increased it (Fig.  , Supplementary Fig.  ), highlighting RIPK2’s promoting role in E. coli-driven LM. Furthermore, in vivo assay indicated E. coli boosted NET release and LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression, and these effects were blocked by NOD1/2 antagonist-1 or Ripk2-cKO (Supplementary Fig.  –g). In huHSC-C-NKG-ProF mice, E. coli-induced NET release and metastasis were also inhibited by NOD1/2 antagonist-1 or RIPK2-KO neutrophils (Supplementary Fig.  –l), indicating E. coli promoted CRCLM by engaging RIPK2 through NOD1/2 in neutrophils.

## RIPK2 recruited HNRNPK to activate Atf3/Relb expression, thus inducing NET release
To elucidate the role of RIPK2 in neutrophils in enhancing NET release, we conducted single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-Seq). Initially, we assessed the quality of the sequencing data, noting that a sequencing saturation of 80% and a median gene count of 2,700 per cell, indicating high-quality data (Supplementary Fig.  ). Subsequently, we identified genes whose expression was modulated by Ripk2-cKO and investigated the functional role of RIPK2 through pathway enrichment analysis of these differentially expressed genes. Our findings revealed an increase in E. coli rRNA expression in Ripk2-cKO neutrophils and the upregulation of genes associated with the “Response to bacterium” pathway (Fig.  a, b). qPCR further confirmed the upregulation of the expression of these 24 genes in neutrophils, including Hba-a2 (Supplementary Fig.  ), suggesting that RIPK2 in neutrophils facilitated an effective immune response. To investigate which neutrophil subpopulations are regulated by RIPK2 and thus participate in LM, Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) was performed. Cyba+ neutrophils (Fig.  c, d). We then sorted and reinfused these subpopulations into mice to evaluate their functions. We indicated that Atf3+
Relb+ neutrophils, along with Ifit3+
Ifit1+
Ifi27l2a+
Isg15+, and Ifitm1+
Slpi+ neutrophils, promoted LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression, with Atf3+
Relb+ neutrophils having the greatest impact (Supplementary Fig.  –h). In contrast, Cybb+
Cyba+ neutrophils had the opposite effect (Supplementary Fig.  –h). Similar outcomes were observed in huHSC-C-NKG-ProF (Supplementary Fig.  –k). Given the pronounced metastasizing effect and predominance of Atf3+
Relb+ neutrophils, coupled with the observation that Ripk2-cKO most significantly reduced their infiltration compared to the other three subpopulations (Fig.  e, f), our investigation centered on Atf3+
Relb+ neutrophils. Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) indicated the downregulation of genes associated with “Transcriptional misregulation in cancer” in Atf3+
Relb+ neutrophils (Fig.  ). Furthermore, qPCR analysis confirmed that Ripk2-cKO downregulated the expression of these 13 gene in Atf3+
Relb+ neutrophils, including Bcl2a1a (Supplementary Fig.  ), thereby disrupting the transcriptional network in neutrophils. The findings indicated that RIPK2 served a regulatory function in neutrophil activity by sustaining Atf3+
Relb+ neutrophils. Subsequently, we conducted an exploration of the primary transcription factors within the Atf3+
Relb+ neutrophils utilizing Single-Cell Regulatory Network Inference and Clustering (SCENIC), and revealed ATF3 and Relb as the principal regulatory elements (Fig.  ). Therefore, we explored RIPK2’s role in regulating the expression of ATF3/RelB and their effect on NET release. Ripk2-cKO was found to reduce ATF3/RelB expression in neutrophils (Fig.  ). Rescue assays revealed that E. coli enhanced ATF3/RelB expression in neutrophils, whereas Ripk2-cKO mitigated these effects (Supplementary Fig.  ). Furthermore, DNA pull-down-mass spectrometry (MS) revealed that HNRNPK bound to the promoters of Atf3/Relb in neutrophils (Fig.  , Supplementary Data  , ). Molecular docking and Chromatin Immunoprecipitation (ChIP)-qPCR confirmed HNRNPK’s binding in neutrophils, which was diminished by Ripk2-cKO (Fig.  k–m). We further indicated that Ripk2-cKO reduced the levels of ATF3/RelB in neutrophils, while Hnrnpk-cKI alleviated this inhibitory effect (Fig.  n, o), indicating RIPK2 enhanced their expression via recruiting HNRNPK. Rescue experiments indicated that E. coli induced the protein expression of ATF3/RelB, and this promoting effect was inhibited by Ripk2-cKO (Fig.  ). Additionally, to understand how the RIPK2 signaling axis, activated by E. coli, influences NET release, we examined NET-related markers and observed that E. coli increased the levels of p-MLKL/MLKL in neutrophils (Supplementary Fig.  ). What’s more, the absence of Nod1/2, Ripk2, Hnrnpk, Atf3, or Relb inhibited this phosphorylation (Supplementary Fig.  ). Rescue assay further indicated that the phosphorylation of MLKL, which was induced by E. coli, was attenuated by the presence of NOD1/2 antagonist-1, Ripk2-cKO, Hnrnpk-cKO, and Atf3/Relb-cKO (Supplementary Fig.  ).
 We then explored whether E. coli uses RIPK2 to recruit HNRNPK, facilitating NET release. In C57BL/6 J mice, E. coli and Ripk2-cKI increased NET release, LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression, while Ripk2-cKO or Hnrnpk-cKO reduced these effects, with Hnrnpk-cKO diminishing the tumor-promoting influence of E. coli and Ripk2-cKI (Fig.  , Supplementary Fig.  –g). In huHSC-C-NKG-ProF mice, neutrophils overexpressing RIPK2 (RIPK2-OE) and E. coli enhanced the release of NETs and facilitated LM (Supplementary Fig.  –k). Conversely, neutrophils deficient in RIPK2 (RIPK2-KO) or HNRNPK (HNRNPK-KO) exhibited a contrary effect, with HNRNPK-KO neutrophils attenuating the tumor-promoting effects associated with E. coli and RIPK2-OE (Supplementary Fig.  –k). We also investigated whether E. coli induced NET release by recruiting HNRNPK to increase ATF3/RelB expression, promoting LM. In C57BL/6 J mice, E. coli and Hnrnpk-cKI increased NET release, LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression, while Atf3/Relb-cKO countered these effects (Supplementary Fig.  –g). Similarly, in huHSC-C-NKG-ProF mice, E. coli and HNRNPK-OE neutrophils promoted these cancer-promoting outcomes, whereas ATF3/RelB-KO neutrophils had the opposite impact (Supplementary Fig.  –k). This suggested HNRNPK, activated by E. coli, enhanced ATF3/RelB expression in neutrophils to promote NET-dependent LM.

## ATF3/RelB collaborated to activate Ncf4 expression to induce NET release
To investigate the mechanism of ATF3/RelB in NET release, we analyzed their DNA-binding sites. We identified thousands of binding peaks for them, primarily at transcription start sites and in distal intergenic regions, introns, and promoters (Supplementary Fig.  –c). It is important to highlight that the binding sites for ATF3/RelB exhibited a high degree of proximity, and the distribution of signals was positively correlated (Fig.  a, b), suggesting co-localization of ATF3/RelB facilitated NET release. Therefore, we aimed to ascertain whether ATF3/RelB enhances the release of NETs by facilitating the transcription of common genes associated with NETs. We noted that the overlap of peaks associated with ATF3 or RelB in infiltrating neutrophils within CRC was relatively minimal across their respective three replicates (Fig.  ). We proposed that this observation might be attributed to the pronounced heterogeneity of neutrophils within the TME , which led to variability in the peaks elicited by the same transcription factor across different tumor specimens. Among their common target genes, Ncf4 was associated with “NET formation” , , (Fig.  c, d). NCF4 is part of the NADPH oxidase complex , which facilitates MLKL translocation to the cell membrane, promoting chromatin DNA release through MLKL phosphorylation . This observation aligned with the hypothesis that E. coli induced MLKL phosphorylation via the HNRNPK-ATF3/RelB signaling pathway (Supplementary Fig.  ). Subsequently, we examined the regulatory role of ATF3/RelB in the expression of NCF4. According to JASPAR predictions, ATF3/RelB were likely to bind to the TGAATCA and AGATTCCTCAGGGGGAAAGC sequences within the Ncf4 promoter, respectively (Fig.  , Supplementary Table  ). This binding was substantiated through molecular docking and ChIP-qPCR assays (Fig.  e, f). Furthermore, the deletion of these sequences resulted in the loss of Ncf4 promoter binding to ATF3/RelB in neutrophils (Fig.  , Supplementary Fig.  ), and while ATF3/RelB enhanced NCF4 expression, this effect was abolished following the sequence deletions (Fig.  h, i).
 Given the close proximity of the ATF3/RelB binding sites on the Ncf4 promoter in neutrophils (Fig.  ), we hypothesized an interaction between these two transcription factors, a hypothesis that was subsequently validated through molecular docking and immunoprecipitation analyses (Fig.  j, k). Additionally, we explored whether disrupting the ATF3/RelB complex would impede the binding of either component to the Ncf4 promoter. To investigate this, we engineered cells with site mutations at the ATF3/RelB complex interaction site. We indicated that mutations in ATF3 (K78A/K52A/K89A/H53A/H56A/C55A/K97A, collectively referred to as ATF3-7A) and RelB (R93A/L94A/D149A/G151A/P101A/H258A/P103A/R102A/E146A, collectively referred to as RelB-9A) inhibited their interaction and binding to the Ncf4 promoter, reducing NCF4 expression in neutrophils (Fig.  l–o). Before proceeding to phenotypic studies, we also assessed the off-target effects associated with the cKO in neutrophils of four genes mentioned earlier: Hnrnpk, Atf3, RelB, and Ncf4. Similar to observations with Ripk2, mice harboring modifications in these four genes exhibited no alterations in systemic immune cell counts, suggesting the absence of off-target effects (Supplementary Fig.  , f).
Next, we analyzed whether E. coli facilitates NET-dependent CRCLM by activating NCF4 expression in neutrophils via the ATF3/RelB pathway. We discovered that E. coli and Atf3/Relb-cKI increased NET release, LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression, while Ncf4-cKO reduced these effects, counteracting E. coli and Atf3/Relb-cKI (Fig.  , Supplementary Fig.  –f). In huHSC-C-NKG-ProF mice, E. coli and ATF3/RelB-OE neutrophils enhanced these cancer-promoting processes, but NCF4-KO neutrophils inhibited them (Supplementary Fig.  –j). Hence, we proposed that ATF3/RelB interacted at the Ncf4 promoter, boosting Ncf4 transcription and NET release.
The MAPK and NF-κB pathways are known to facilitate NET release , and RIPK2 is an activator of these pathways ; however, the relationship between these pathways and the RIPK2-ATF3/RelB-NCF4 axis has not been fully elucidated. Our study demonstrated that inhibitors targeting ERK1/2 (SCH772984), p38 (p38 MAPK-IN-1), JNK (JNK-IN-8), and NF-κB (JSH-23) didn’t influence the expression levels of RIPK2, ATF3, RelB, or NCF4 in neutrophils (Supplementary Fig.  ). Furthermore, while Ripk2-cKO or cKI was involved in the phosphorylation of MAPK and NF-κB (Supplementary Fig.  ), the Atf3/Relb/Ncf4-cKO or cKI didn’t impact the activation of these pathways (Supplementary Fig.  ). Consequently, we hypothesized that RIPK2 facilitates NET-dependent CRCLM via activation of the MAPK and NF-κB pathways, independent of the ATF3/RelB-NCF4 axis. Our findings indicated that Ripk2-cKI enhanced NET formation, LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression, whereas the aforementioned inhibitors mitigated these phenotypes and diminished the effects of Ripk2-cKI (Supplementary Fig.  –g). These observations were corroborated in huHSC-C-NKG-ProF mice (Supplementary Fig.  –k). Therefore, RIPK2 appeared to promote NET-related CRCLM through two parallel pathways: the activation of MAPK/NF-κB and the ATF3/RelB-NCF4 axis.
Additionally, PAD4 releases NET independently of MLKL , and its relationship with RIPK2-HNRNPK-ATF3/RelB-NCF4-MLKL axis was also unclear. We found that Ripk2, Hnrnpk, Atf3, Relb, Ncf4-cKO or inhibiting MLKL didn’t affect PAD4 expression in neutrophils, and GSK484 hydrochloride didn’t influence RIPK2, HNRNPK, ATF3, RelB, NCF4 protein expression or the MLKL phosphorylation level in neutrophils (Supplementary Fig.  , b). Thus, RIPK2 didn’t rely on PAD4 for NET formation, and PAD4 and MLKL operated as two parallel pathways in promoting NET formation.

## NETs stimulated the TRPC1-S100A8/9-STAT3 pathway to facilitate CRCLM
To explore how NETs aid CRCLM, we used UMAP to identify 15 cell subpopulations in CRC tissues from our scRNA-Seq data (Supplementary Fig.  , b). Cellchat revealed that Ripk2-cKO neutrophils influenced CRC cells without receptor-ligand interactions (Fig.  ). However, we observed ligand-receptor interactions between neutrophils and macrophages (Fig.  ), prompting a detailed analysis of the interaction patterns and intensities between them. Our findings indicated that, although communication existed between neutrophils and macrophages, the signals were predominantly moderate or weak (Supplementary Fig.  ). Furthermore, the removal of either neutrophils or macrophages didn’t impact the infiltration of the other cell type (Supplementary Fig.  ), suggesting that the interaction between these two cell types was relatively weak. Consequently, we shifted our focus to investigate the impact of neutrophils on CRC cells, rather than that of macrophages. Since neutrophils affect tumor growth by modulating calcium signaling , we examined the impact of Ripk2-cKO neutrophil depletion on calcium channel gene expression in CRC cells (Supplementary Fig.  ). Our investigation revealed that Ripk2-cKO inhibited only Cacnaic and Trpc1 mRNA expression (Supplementary Fig.  ). Given that CACNAIC predominantly operates in myocardium, smooth muscle, and the brain, rather than colorectal cells , our research concentrated on examining TRPC1 in CRC cells. Ripk2-cKO neutrophil removal was shown to reduce TRPC1 expression in CRC cells, while PMA-activated NETs increased TRPC1 levels, and DNase I-inhibited neutrophils decreased them (Figs.  b– , Supplementary Fig.  ). Ripk2-cKO neutrophil removal also blocked calcium influx in CRC cells (Fig.  ). We then studied how NETs up-regulate the expression of TRPC1 in CRC cells. It has been reported that TRPC1 enhances NFATC3’s nuclear translocation via the Ca²⁺-calcineurin pathway, while NFATC3 increases TRPC1 transcription, influencing vascular remodeling and hypertension , . We proposed a TRPC1-Ca²⁺-NFATC3-TRPC1 positive feedback loop. Ripk2-cKO neutrophil removal showed reduced NFATC3 expression in CRC cells; Nfatc3-KO in CRC cells decreased TRPC1 expression, whereas Nfatc3-OE increased it (Supplementary Fig.  –g). Furthermore, Ca2+ channel agonist 1 elevated NFATC3 and TRPC1 levels in CRC cells, counteracting the effects of Ripk2-cKO neutrophil loss (Supplementary Fig.  ). Nfatc3-OE also mitigated the negative impact of Ripk2-cKO neutrophil loss on TRPC1 expression in CRC cells (Supplementary Fig.  ), highlighting the feedback loop’s importance for maintaining TRPC1 expression in CRC cells. Therefore, NETs facilitated calcium ion influx via TRPC1, which subsequently upregulated NFATC3 expression. As a transcription factor, NFATC3 enhanced the expression of TRPC1.
 To explore how TRPC1-mediated calcium influx promotes CRC escape, pathway enrichment analysis was performed and revealed that Ripk2-cKO neutrophil depletion inhibited the JAK-STAT pathway (Fig.  ). Both Ripk2-cKO neutrophil removal and Trpc1-KO in CRC cells reduced STAT1/3 phosphorylation in CRC cells, while Trpc1-OE countered this effect (Fig.  g–i, Supplementary Fig.  –l). Unlike neutrophil clearance, PMA-stimulated NETs increased STAT1/3 phosphorylation in CRC cells, which was diminished by Trpc1-KO (Fig.  , Supplementary Fig.  –p). This suggested NETs promoted CRCLM by boosting TRPC1 expression and activating STAT1/3 phosphorylation in CRC cells. Additionally, scratch and Transwell assays indicated that Trpc1-KO reduced CRC cell migration and invasion, but this inhibition was mitigated by STAT1 (2-NP) and STAT3 (Colivelin) agonists (Figs.  k–m, Supplementary Fig.  –c).
Additionally, we conducted an analysis of the mechanism by which NETs facilitate CRCLM through the TRPC1-STAT1/3 pathway. S100A8 and S100A9, part of the calcium-binding S100 protein family, form heterodimers upon calcium binding . We discovered that S100A8 interacted with S100A9 in CRC cells, but this interaction was disrupted in Trpc1-KO (Fig.  n, o), indicating S100A8/9 were downstream of TRPC1. TRPC1 appeared to stabilize the S100A8/9 complex through calcium ions, as shown by rescue assays where Trpc1-OE increased S100A8’s interaction to S100A9 in CRC cells, an effect reduced by the calcium blocker Diltiazem hydrochloride (Supplementary Fig.  ). However, the STAT1/3 agonist didn’t affect the S100A8/9 interaction (Supplementary Fig.  ). Therefore, we next conducted an in-depth investigation into the regulatory effect of S100A8/9 interaction on the activation of STAT1/3 and its subsequent impact on CRCLM. Molecular docking and immunoprecipitation showed specific hydrogen bonds between S100A8 and S100A9, and mutations in these sites (S100A8-H27A/N25A/E6A/Q44A, S100A9-N99A/R101A/T48A, collectively referred to as S100A8/9-7 A) disrupted their interaction in CRC cells (Figs.  p, q). Trpc1-OE in CRC cells was found to enhance CRC cell migration and invasion, but S100A8/9-7 A in CRC cells reduced this effect (Figs.  r–t). Moreover, PMA-stimulated NETs increased STAT1/3 phosphorylation in CRC cells, but S100A8/9-7 A inhibited p-STAT3/STAT3 without affecting p-STAT1/STAT1 (Fig.  , Supplementary Fig.  ). Consequently, we concentrated on examining the role of STAT3, as opposed to STAT1, in facilitating CRCLM mediated by S100A8/9. Trpc1-OE was indicated to increase p-STAT3/STAT3, which S100A8/9-7A inhibited it (Fig.  ). Lastly, S100a8/9-KO was shown to reduce CRC cell migration and invasion, but Colivelin countered these effects (Fig.  w–y, Supplementary Fig.  ). This suggested NETs promoted S100A8/9 dimer formation by increasing TRPC1 expression, enhancing STAT3 phosphorylation and CRCLM.

## STAT3/S100A8/9 EPLs promoted CRCLM
S100A8/9 acts as the transcriptional coactivators and shares the motifs with STAT3 , suggesting they might function as STAT3 cofactors. Immunofluorescence showed these proteins colocalized in CRC cell nuclei (Fig.  , Supplementary Fig.  ), and molecular docking and immunoprecipitation confirmed their interaction (Figs.  b- ). Furthermore, Trpc1-KO disrupted this interaction in MC38 cells (Fig.  e, f), indicating this interaction was driven by the “NETs-CRC cell” signal. Subsequently, we investigated the impact of disrupting this heterotrimer on CRCLM. Site mutations in STAT3 (S372A/K488A/K244A/E455A/K318A/N315A/E311A, collectively termed STAT3-7A) or S100A8 (S62A/D59A/N64A/D63A/Q23A/K35A/N32A/K36A, collectively termed S100A8-8A) disrupted their interaction in CRC cells (Figs.  g–i), as did mutations in STAT3 (E506A/R245 A/I498A/Q503A/N489A, referred to as STAT3-5A) or S100A9 (R25A/T14 A/R11A/T15A, referred to as S100A9-4A) (Fig.  g, j, k). Functional assay showed that site mutations (STAT3-7A + 5 A/S100A8-8A/S100A9-4A, STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A) reduced the migration and invasion of MC38 cells (Fig.  l–n).
 Given that S100A8/9 interacted with STAT3 and shared the motifs , we hypothesized that the enhancer, promoter, and trimer of STAT3/S100A8/9 formed EPLs , reducing the distance between the enhancers and promoters of specific genes, thereby facilitating CRCLM. Therefore, we disrupted the STAT3/S100A8/9 complex and compared STAT3-mediated chromatin changes before and after STAT3/S100A8/9 trimer disruption. Initially, we examined the influence of STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A on the DNA-binding capability of STAT3. Cleavage Under Targets and Tagmentation (CUT&Tag) data indicated that, in wild-type (WT) CRC cells, STAT3 shared binding peaks with S100A8 (26.897%) and S100A9 (27.130%) (Supplementary Fig.  ). S100A8 shared peaks with STAT3 (19.390%) and S100A9 (35.199%), and S100A9 shared peaks with STAT3 (16.449%) and S100A8 (29.624%) (Supplementary Fig.  ). Moreover, in STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A CRC cells, the shared binding peaks were elevated (Supplementary Fig.  ), STAT3 exhibited a shared binding peak with S100A8 at 34.649% and with S100A9 at 36.349% (Supplementary Fig.  ), suggesting that the disruption of the complex upregulated STAT3’s DNA binding activity. As expected, STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A increased STAT3’s DNA-binding affinity (Supplementary Fig.  ), suggesting that the complex obscured part of STAT3’s DNA-binding domain. Following this, we assessed the effects of STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A on the formation of the STAT3/S100A8/9-EPLs. Our analysis revealed that the EPL structure on 12 chromosomes, including chromosome 1, exhibited damage (Supplementary Fig.  ). In WT cells, a total of 4,459 EPLs were identified, whereas in STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A cells, 652 EPLs were detected, indicating the mutation disrupted the integrity of the EPLs (Fig.  o, p, Supplementary Fig.  ). As anticipated, in situ Hi-C followed by chromatin immunoprecipitation (HiChIP) data showed that STAT3’s DNA binding was elevated after mutations (Supplementary Fig.  ), indicating that the mutations decreased EPL formation and exposed STAT3’s DNA-binding domain. Moreover, STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A decreased EPL size from a median of 80 to 50 kb (Fig.  ). It was notably that a gene’s cumulative EPL intensity wasn’t directly proportional to its number (Fig.  ), implying that active enhancers didn’t solely interact with their promoters or enhancers. Further analysis pointed out that EPLs linked many enhancers to promoters, including oncogenes like Tns1, but these connections weakened after STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A (Fig.  ). Bioinformatics analysis demonstrated that, of the 5,111 EPLs, only 3,864 were defined as structures between enhancers and promoters of individual genes, rather than cross-structures among multiple genes. Consequently, our focus was on elucidating how the EPLs of these 3,864 genes was modulated by the STAT3/S100A8/9 complex. STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A were indicated to downregulate 50 out of 3,864 EPLs (Fig.  , Supplementary Data  ). It was noteworthy that STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A exclusively disrupted the loops of the link enhancer-promoter, whereas the loops of the link enhancer-enhancer or promoter-promoter remain unaffected by such mutations (Supplementary Data  ). We further analyzed STAT3’s binding to oncogene promoters or enhancers to determine if STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A disrupt their expression or STAT3’s DNA binding on their cis-acting elements. We found that the STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A didn’t impact STAT3’s binding ability at these gene locus (Supplementary Fig.  , b), but impede their transcription and protein expression, including Tns1 (Supplementary Fig.  , d), suggesting that interference with this complex inhibited EPLs, resulting in a down-regulation of their expression, while preserving STAT3’s affinity for DNA. Finally, we employed pathway enrichment analysis to explore the functions of these differential EPLs. Enrichment analysis revealed that genes with EPLs at promoters and enhancers were related to migration and invasion (Fig.  ). All in all, the STAT3/S100A8/9 complex played a critical role in the formation of EPLs. While it diminished the overall DNA-binding affinity of STAT3, it simultaneously reduced the spatial distance between enhancers and promoters on the cis-acting elements of various oncogenes, thereby facilitating their transcription. Notably, this process occurred without altering the affinity of STAT3 for the cis-acting elements of these oncogenes.

## STAT3/S100A8/9-EPLs stabilized Tns1 transcription in CRC cells
Given the observed reduction in EPL binding at the Tns1 locus within the STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A group, which was the most pronounced among the 50 differential EPLs (Fig.  ), our study concentrated on TNS1. Visualization and ChIP-qPCR analyses confirmed STAT3 binding to the Tns1 promoters and enhancers, while STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A were found to disrupt the EPLs at this gene locus (Figs.  a–c). STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A was shown to inhibit TNS1 mRNA and protein expression (Figs.  d, e). Therefore, we examined whether STAT3/S100A8/9-EPLs facilitated CRC cell metastasis by stabilizing Tns1 transcription. Tns1-OE in CRC cells was found to reduce E-Cadherin and increased N-Cadherin and Vimentin expression, boosting cell migration and invasion, while Tns1-KO reversed these effects (Fig.  f–i). Additionally, STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A were indicated to upregulate E-cadherin expression, downregulate N-cadherin and Vimentin expression in MC38 cells, inhibit cell migration or invasion, and counteract the metastasis-promoting effects of TNS1 (Figs.  j, k). Given that NETs facilitated metastasis through activation of the TRPC1-S100A8/9-STAT3-TNS1 axis in CRC cells (Figs.  – ), it was imperative to determine the role of this axis in NET release. Our findings indicated that this axis didn’t regulate NET formation in CRC tissues (Supplementary Fig.  , b). This indicated that the signal axis by which NETs promoted CRCLM was unidirectional rather than positive feedback.
 Next, we investigated whether the disruption of the STAT3/S100A8/9 heterotrimer inhibits CRCLM. Functional analysis in C57BL/6 J mice showed that the STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A reduced LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression (Supplementary Fig.  –f). Subsequently, we explored whether human STAT3 binds to S100A8/9 to form a complex and whether disruption of this complex inhibits CRCLM in patients. Molecular docking predicted hydrogen bond interactions between human STAT3 and S100A8 or S100A9 (Supplementary Fig.  ). Immunoprecipitation assays showed that mutations in human STAT3 (S514A, Q511A, G254A, E407 A, C251A, C328A, and R325A, referred to as STAT3-7A) or human S100A8 (K56 A, R31A, Q69A, T62A, I60A, and E70A, referred to as S100A8-6A) disrupted STAT3’s interaction with S100A8 in CRC cells (Supplementary Fig.  , i), while mutations in human STAT3 (K409A, R325 A, D334A, E397A, and S405A, referred to as STAT3-5A) or human S100A9 (E66A, R87A, and K59A, referred to as S100A9-3A) impaired STAT3 interaction to S100A9 (Supplementary Fig.  , k). The combined STAT3/S100A8/9-20 A variant (comprising STAT3 mutations S514A, Q511A, G254A, E407A, C251A, C328A, K409A, R325A, D334A, E397A, S405A; S100A8 mutations K56A, R31A, Q69A, T62A, I60A, E70A; and S100A9 mutations E66A, R87A, K59A) was found to reduce LM, CRC growth, or Vimentin expression in huHSC-C-NKG-ProF mice (Supplementary Fig.  –n). Therefore, our results demonstrated that inhibiting the formation of the STAT3/S100A8/9 heterotrimer suppressed CRCLM.

## RIPK2 promoted CRCLM by activating the TRPC1-S100A8/9-STAT3 signaling in CRC cells
Building upon the aforementioned findings, we hypothesized that RIPK2 in neutrophils facilitated LM by upregulating TRPC1 expression and activating STAT3 via the S100A8/9 pathway, then sustaining TNS1 expression through the STAT3/S100A8/9-EPL axis. To validate the robustness of this signaling mechanism, we conducted experiments using C57BL/6 J mice with intact immune function and huHSC-C-NKG-ProF mice possessing humanized immune systems. In mice, RIPK2-deficient neutrophils were found to reduce LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression, but TRPC1-OE in CRC cells reversed this (Fig.  a–c, Supplementary Fig.  –c, Supplementary Fig.  –d). Moreover, TRPC1-OE in CRC cells was shown to increase LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression, but STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A mitigated this effect (Figs.  d–f, Supplementary Fig.  – , Supplementary Fig.  – ). Similarly, TNS1-OE in CRC cells was indicated to increase LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression, but STAT3/S100A8/9-24 A reduced its impact (Figs.  g–i, Supplementary Fig.  –i, Supplementary Fig.  – ). Furthermore, S100A8/9 inhibitor Paquinimod and the STAT3 inhibitor Stattic decreased TNS1-driven LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression (Figs.  j–l, Supplementary Fig.  –l, Supplementary Fig.  –n), suggesting the S100A8/9-STAT3-TNS1 axis as a potential drug target. Finally, we explored whether blocking RIPK2, STAT3, and S100A8/9 together more effectively inhibits E. coli-induced CRCLM. The combination of GSK583, Stattic, and Paquinimod proved more effective than individual inhibitors in reducing LM, CRC growth, and Vimentin expression (Supplementary Fig.  –f). These results were consistent in mice with humanized immune systems (Supplementary Fig.  –i).
 

## Discussion
This study found that RIPK2 in neutrophils played a crucial role in E. coli-induced CRCLM by recruiting HNRNPK to boost Atf3/Relb transcription, activating Ncf4 expression, and triggering p-MLKL-mediated NET release. NETs then increased TRPC1-NFATC3 expression in CRC cells, enhancing calcium influx, S100A8/9 dimer formation, and STAT3 activation. STAT3, in turn, formed a complex with S100A8/9, increasing TNS1 expression and promoting LM (Supplementary Fig.  ). These findings underscored several critical insights:(1) The involvement of E. coli in LM and the exploration of potential therapeutic strategies;(2) The impact of NETs on CRC cells through calcium influx; and(3) The role of STAT3 in stabilizing EPLs to facilitate oncogene expression.
This study explored how NETs influenced CRC cell behavior in the LM models. It revealed that the calcium channel protein TRPC1, rather than neutrophil-secreted cytokines or chemokines, facilitated CRCLM, aligning with previous findings on neutrophil-driven tumor cell regulation via ion signaling-TRPM2 . While TRPM2 is associated with neutrophil-mediated tumor cell death through calcium signaling , no change in TRPM2 expression was observed in CRC cells after Ripk2-cKO neutrophil depletion. In contrast, NETs have been shown to upregulate the expression of TRPC1 on CRC cells, with TRPC1 activation being closely associated with increased metastatic potential. These findings underscored neutrophils’ dual role in tumor growth and escape, emphasizing the need to understand their function in the TME for improved therapies.
S100A8/9 exhibit opposing roles in different cancers , : they promote lymph node metastasis in CRC but inhibit metastasis in breast cancer . Our study confirmed their role as key promoters of CRCLM, demonstrating their tumor-specific effects. Moreover, S100A8/9 acts both extracellularly and intracellularly, affecting tumor cells differently , . Extracellularly, they induce breast cancer metastasis via the MCAM/ETV4/ZEB1 axis , while intracellularly, they promote CRC metastasis through the Wnt/β-catenin pathway . We found that intracellular S100A8/9 was crucial for CRC cells’ response to NETs, stabilizing multiple oncogene expression via STAT3/S100A8/9-EPLs. Thus, S100A8/9 worsen CRC prognosis, and inhibiting their expression or polymerization could be therapeutic.
Previous models indicated enhancer activity was multiplicative . Our study found a strong link between EPLs and Tns1 transcription in CRC cells, suggesting that merely connecting enhancers and promoters wasn’t sufficient for transcriptional activation; recruiting cofactors like S100A8/9 was essential. Furthermore. We observed STAT3 accumulating at EPLs with complex loop structures, highlighting that gene expression necessitated a sophisticated regulatory network. STAT3 formed a trimer with S100A8/9, stabilizing the transcriptional framework of many oncogenes once EPLs were established.
Given that E. coli is a significant contributor to NET-induced CRCLM, the feasibility of employing antibiotics to eradicate E. coli as a treatment strategy warranted further investigation. In comparison to the direct anti-tumor effects, the modulation of intestinal flora by antibiotics and their significant influence on cancer immunotherapy represent prominent and contentious areas of current research. Substantial evidence suggests that the composition and diversity of the gut microbiome are critical determinants of the efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors, including anti-PD-1/PD-L1 and anti-CTLA-4 antibodies . The administration of broad-spectrum antibiotics markedly disrupts the homeostasis of the gut microbiota, resulting in reduced microbial diversity and the depletion of beneficial microbiota. In comparison to the conventional use of antibiotics for the eradication of E. coli, targeted therapeutic interventions focusing on STAT3 or RIPK2 present a more dependable alternative. Persistent activation of STAT3 is a hallmark of numerous cancers, playing a pivotal role in the regulation of several core genes associated with tumor malignancy . Consequently, the inhibition of STAT3 could facilitate a synergistic effect across multiple pathways in tumor suppression. Current strategies for targeting STAT3 encompass non-specific small molecules, such as Sta-21 and Stattic, highly selective inhibitors aimed at the SH2 domain, such as BP-1-102, as well as simulated peptides and protein degradation-targeted chimeric (PROTACs) technology . In comparison to the targeted therapy of STAT3, targeting RIPK2 presents the advantage of potentially offering a more favorable therapeutic window. Genetic studies indicate that RIPK2 is not essential for normal physiological processes in adult organisms, suggesting that its inhibitors may exhibit a relatively high safety profile . Recent advancements have led to the development of highly selective RIPK2 small molecule inhibitors, such as compound IA, and degraders, such as PROTAC, which have demonstrated efficacy in inhibiting inflammation-driven tumor growth in preclinical models . Similar to STAT3 inhibitors, the combination of RIPK2 inhibitors with immunotherapy is considered a highly promising approach, with the objective of reversing immunosuppression by targeting tumor-associated chronic inflammation – .
This study had some limitations, notably not examining the impact of STAT3/S100A8/9-EPLs on topological structure. Topological integrity is closely tied to CTCF and cohesion, which define topologically associating domains (TAD) boundaries and enable multi-level chromatin structures via “loop extrusion” . This process was crucial for gene regulation, 3D chromatin architecture, genomic stability, and disease progression . The role of CTCF in EPLs could be summarized in three ways(1) No effect: Many promoter-enhancer interactions remain stable even without CTCF or cohesin, relying on the existing chromatin environment . (2) Positive effect: CTCF binding sites enhance promoter-enhancer interactions by connecting with distal regulatory elements . (3) Inhibitory effect: Removing CTCF increases chromatin accessibility at promoters, leading to transcriptional activation . Therefore, our future research would focus on using Hi-C assays to study how STAT3/S100A8/9-EPLs affect TADs and gene expression. An effective approach involves deleting CTCF and its binding sites to analyze the role of STAT3/S100A8/9-EPLs in TAD structures via HiChIP experiments. Additionally, examining the effects of removing certain cohesion and studying loop extrusion and TAD exposure to enhancers would help understand the formation of the STAT3/S100A8/9-EPLs. Furthermore, the PDX used in this study was subject to several limitations. Firstly, the PDX model, developed using mice with humanized immune systems and organoids, might not accurately replicate the microenvironmental characteristics of human CRC tissues. The process of establishing and propagating PDX models inevitably involves clonal selection, resulting in the enrichment of tumor cell subpopulations that are either adapted to in vitro culture conditions or possess growth advantages in mice, while other subpopulations might be lost . Additionally, there exists a degree of spatial heterogeneity (variation in cell characteristics across different regions of the tumor) and temporal heterogeneity (evolution of tumor clones before and after treatment) between the PDX model and patient-derived CRC tissues , . Consequently, the PDX model might not fully capture the interactions between human tumors and other cells within the TME , . Therefore, in our future research endeavors, it is imperative to conduct Phase I clinical trials that assess the targeting of RIPK2 in neutrophils or STAT3/S100A8/9-EPLs in CRC cells as a strategic approach to enhance the understanding of the interaction between NETs and CRC.
It is important to highlight that CitH3 isn’t exclusively associated with neutrophils. CitH3, a citrullination modification product of histone H3, is catalyzed by PAD4 . Traditionally, it has been understood that CitH3 is predominantly expressed by neutrophils during the formation of NETs. However, recent research has demonstrated that a variety of cells, including macrophages, epithelial cells, and endothelial cells, are also capable of expressing CitH3 through distinct mechanisms. The functions of CitH3 extend beyond NET formation, encompassing roles in inflammatory regulation and tissue repair – . Evidence of CitH3 expression across various cell types is as follows:(1) In macrophages, PAD4 facilitates the CitH3, leading to chromatin depolymerization and the formation of NETs-like structures that are instrumental in pathogen capture and the enhancement of phagocytosis , . (2) In epithelial cells, PAD4 plays a crucial role in regulating cell proliferation and migration, thereby contributing to tissue repair . (3) In endothelial cells, CitH3 is involved in maintaining vascular homeostasis by modulating vascular permeability and inhibiting platelet aggregation , .(4) In mast cells, PAD4 catalyzes the generation of extracellular traps composed of DNA and CitH3. This process closely resembles NETosis and constitutes a crucial antibacterial defense mechanism for mast cells . Consequently, it was justifiable that the CitH3 antibody employed in our study did not exclusively stain neutrophils. While the field has mostly relied on the Abcam antibody ab5103, we used a different one as the Abcam antibody is not recommended for immunohistochemistry and immunofluorescence. However, further validation using as well the standard Abcam antibody for CitH3 could be needed in future work.
In conclusion, our research identified a model in which intestinal flora-driven NETs modulate the expression of oncogenes in CRC cells, thereby enhancing our understanding of the interplay between intestinal microbiota and tumor immunity.

## Patient samples

## Study design
This study was structured as an open-label, multicenter, parallel-group investigation with dual objectives:(1) to discern the differences in bacterial composition between CRC tissues with and without LM, and(2) to examine the influence of distinct bacterial profiles on CRCLM. The dataset was segmented into four components, two of which originated from large hospitals in Fujian Province, including a specialized liver disease hospital. Furthermore, specimens from two external centers (Changsha, Shanghai) serve as validation sets. A total of 136 CRC specimens with or without LM were collected over a seven-year period, from January 2018 to January 2025, with comprehensive clinical information meticulously documented. The designated follow-up duration for all enrolled patients was 80 m from the time of enrollment. Nevertheless, early termination of follow-up might occur due to patient mortality (either from disease progression or unrelated causes) or loss to follow-up (inability to contact patients despite repeated attempts via telephone, mail, or clinical visits). In such instances, the date of the last known contact was documented as the conclusion of follow-up. The data were subjected to sensitivity analyses to assess the impact of missing data, incorporating multiple imputation techniques for censored observations. The consent has been obtained for reporting and sharing individual-level data.
This study involved human participants, and all procedures were conducted in accordance with the ethical principles of the Declaration of Helsinki. Written informed consent was obtained from all participants after they were fully informed of the study objectives, procedures, potential risks, benefits, and their right to withdraw at any time without penalty. Participants were compensated for their time and travel expenses with a $50 gift card or equivalent currency, which was provided upon completion of the study to acknowledge their contribution without inducing participation. Our study was designed with explicit attention to equity, ensuring no sex-based discrimination in participant recruitment, inclusion/exclusion criteria, or data analysis. The experimental conclusions were not dependent on a specific sex.
We included a total of 136 CRC patients, ensuring comprehensive data such as survival information, clinical indicators, lifestyle habits, and gene mutations. Specifically, 24 samples from our center were utilized for 2bRAD-M-Seq, 60 samples from our two centers were exclusively used for immunofluorescence detection, and 84 specimens from external centers were also employed for immunofluorescence detection. Of these, 105 were non-LM specimens, while 31 exhibited LM. Given the limited availability of CRC specimens with LM, we maintained a 1:3 ratio between the LM and non-LM groups. This approach ensured an adequate number of LM specimens while mitigating potential statistical biases due to an imbalanced ratio. CRCLM samples were derived from (1) CRC patients undergoing hepatectomy in Fujian Medical University Union Hospital and Mengchao Hepatobiliary Hospital of Fujian Medical University, or (2) tissue chips provided by ZK bioaitech (China) and Shanghai Xinchao (China). The inclusion criteria required participants to satisfy the following conditions at the time of enrollment:(1) be at least 20 years of age,(2) have a diagnosis of primary CRC,(3) have not undergone radiation, chemotherapy, or any other adjuvant therapy, and(4) have undergone their first CRC resection. Conversely, the exclusion criteria specified that participants would be disqualified if they met any of the following conditions:(1) a history of other cancers,(2) participation in other clinical trials or use of investigational drugs, including antibiotics, within the previous 30 d,(3) development of distant metastases beyond the liver, or(4) pregnancy.
To account for potential confounding variables, we excluded individuals who had received antibiotics or chemo/radiotherapy within the past three months. All biological samples were immediately processed and stored at -80 °C. For transport, samples were packed with dry ice to maintain -80 °C. Standardized procedures for sample collection were implemented, followed by sequencing or MS with a unified bioinformatics analysis. By implementing these controls, we aimed to minimize false associations.

## Mouse model
Experimental design was made to explicitly avoid sex-based bias, ensuring that the conclusions were not contingent upon any specific sex. To maintain experimental accuracy and consistency, particularly considering potential variations in hormonal fluctuations or physiological responses between sexes in the model system, female mice were exclusively used for all experiments. Each experimental group consisted of a minimum of six mice, meeting the required sample size for statistical robustness. Female C57BL/6 J mice (GemPharmatech, China) or huHSC-C-NKG-ProF mice (Cyagen, China), aged four weeks, were housed in a specific pathogen-free barrier facility. The environmental conditions were stringently controlled, with a 12-hour light (07:00-19:00, approximately 300 lux at cage level) and 12-hour dark cycle, an ambient temperature of 22 ± 2 °C, and relative humidity maintained at 50 ± 10%.

## Animal ethics
The criteria for maximum tumor burden in mouse CRCLM are primarily focused on minimizing animal suffering and adhering to the 3 R principle. These criteria are comprehensively assessed based on tumor volume, weight, and the overall condition of the animal. The specific guidelines are as follows: the maximum tumor diameter is generally restricted to 15 mm, and the tumor weight should not exceed 10% of the animal’s body weight. Regardless of whether the tumor diameter and weight reach these limits, euthanasia must be promptly administered if any of the following conditions are observed (1) Functional impairment, characterized by reduced activity, decreased appetite, or rapid weight loss (>10% of body weight within 24 h).(2) Tissue damage, evidenced by the presence of ulcers (>1 mm), necrosis, or hemorrhage.(3) Systemic deterioration, manifested as cachexia, dehydration, or organ failure.
The specific model construction strategy was as follows:
Construction of a patient-derived xenograft (PDX) model: It was initiated using fresh CRCLM tissue obtained from patients. The tissue was mechanically dissociated into small fragments, which were then cultured in Human Liver Organoid Culture Medium (abs9529, Absin, China) at 37 °C for a period not exceeding 14 d. Organoids, approximately 300 μm in diameter, were isolated using DPBS (10010001, Gibco, NY, USA) and resuspended to achieve a cell density of 2 × 10⁷ cells/mL. For engraftment, each huHSC-C-NKG-ProF mouse was administered a subcutaneous-like injection containing 1 × 10⁶ organoid cells in a total volume of 100 μL, targeting the posterior anal wall. The initial passage (P1) was sustained until the mice exhibited significant weight loss and decreased appetite, at which point humane euthanasia was performed. To propagate the model, P1 organoids were derived by processing the minced xenograft tissue in a manner analogous to the original patient sample, with this procedure being repeated for multiple subsequent passages. The PDX model was deemed successfully established by the third passage (P3), as the tumor characteristics had attained greater consistency and stability.
Construction of cell- or patient organoid- derived CRCLM model: The mouse primary CRC model developed using AOM and DSS exhibits limitations in accurately representing both the early adenoma stage and the advanced metastatic progression of the disease . Following a comparative analysis of various models, we selected the highly metastatic mouse MC38 cell line for our experimental study. MC38 cells (YC-A002, Ubigene, China) or patient organoids (5 × 106) were resuspended in 100 μL DPBS. After anesthesia, fix the mouse in a homemade fixator, fully expose the anus after disinfection, insert the needle into the back wall of the anus, and slowly inject the cell suspension . The food intake and body weight of mice were monitored regularly. When the mice lost their weight and appetite, they were euthanized with CO2, and the CRC or LM tissues were removed and photographed. The formula for measuring the size of the tumor tissues was: Volume = Length × Width × Height. For comparison, CT26 cells (YC-B003, Ubigene) were injected into the back wall of the anus in the same manner.
Infusion of neutrophils: After 3 d of MC38 cell or patient organoid injection, resuspend 5 × 106 human or mouse neutrophils cells in 100 μL Normal Saline and inject into the tail vein of C57BL/6 J or huHSC-C-NKG-ProF mice once every three days for a total of five times.
Bacterial transplantation: To confirm the active involvement of E. coli or other bacteria in CRCLM and to prevent any confounding effects due to bacterial ectopia or colonization of other regions within the gastrointestinal tract, we opted to inject the bacteria into the posterior wall of the anus 3 d following MC38 cell or patient organoid injection . This approach was designed to replicate the extent of infection observed in CRC lesions in patients . The specific details of this procedure were outlined as follows: After 3 d of the injection of MC38 cells or patient organoids, the bacteria (5 × 107 CFU/mouse in 100 μL DPBS) was injected into the posterior wall of the anus once every day for a total of ten times.
To eliminate the bacterial flora in mice, mice were orally fed with antibiotic premix (1 mL/kg/d, Metronidazole (1 mg/mL, M1547, Merck), Ampicillin (1 mg/mL, A5354, Merck), Neomycin (1 mg/mL, N1142, Merck), Gentamicin (1 mg/mL, G1914, Merck), and Vancomycin (0.5 mg/mL, V2002, Merck)) daily, and antibiotic premix (50 μL/mL) was added to the drinking water for 16 d. We used Normal Saline as the control.

## Data analysis
Briefly, we employed ComBat to estimate and eliminate batch effect parameters from the dataset. Subsequently, we normalized the data using ChIPseqSpikeInFree . Fastp was used to trim adaptor and remove low quality reads to get high quality clean reads. Clean reads were aligned to mm39 reference genome using Bowtie 2 . Deeptools was utilized to calculate and plot the correlations and read distribution heatmaps. MACS3 was performed for peak calling. ChIPSeeker was used for peak annotation. MANorm2 was utilized to identify differentially enriched regions between two groups. The enriched peaks were visualized using the IGV software (NIH, MD, USA) . We used Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient analysis to evaluate the correlation between the enrichment level of each ATF3/RelB with an R value > 0.30 and a P value < 0.05 being considered significant. Fisher’s exact test was performed to examine the significant differences in the number of significantly correlated ATF3-RelB pairs, and false discovery rate (FDR) was computed using the Benjamini-Hochberg approach. Bioinformatics data analysis was provided by NewCore Biotechnology (China).

## DNA pull-down assay
A total of 500 μg of nuclear protein extract was incubated overnight at 4 °C with 5 μg of biotin-labeled double-stranded oligonucleotide probes specific to the Atf3, Relb, Ncf4, or Tns1 promoters, or the Tns1 enhancer, which were designed and synthesized by Genecreat, China. Additionally, 100 μL of streptavidin magnetic beads (Bes5004, Bersinbio, China) were included in the incubation. Following this, the mixture was subjected to centrifugation at 5,000 × g for 5 min using a precooled fixed-angle centrifuge. The resulting protein-DNA-probe complex was then resuspended in 30 μL of loading buffer and subsequently boiled for 10 min. The complexes were separated using SDS-Polyacrylamide Gel Electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE, 89888, Thermo Scientific) and analyzed by silver staining using Fast Silver Stain Kit (P0017S, Beyotime) or immunoblotting. Antibodies used in this study: 4 μg anti-ATF3 and anti-RelB antibodies. The specificity and suitability of these antibodies were determined based on publicly available data from suppliers’ official websites.
Sample preparation for liquid chromatography-MS/MS (LC-MS/MS) analysis: The samples were incubated in 80 μL of a Urea-based digestion buffer, comprising 30 mM Tris-HCl-pH 8.0 (15568025, Invitrogen), 0.4 μg Trypsin (90057, Thermo Scientific), 1 mM Dithiothreitol (15508013, Invitrogen), and 2 M Urea (56180, Merck), at 25 °C with agitation at 1,000 rpm for 1 h. Subsequently, the supernatant was collected, and a second incubation was conducted using an additional 80 μL of the same buffer under identical conditions for 30 min. The supernatant from this second incubation was also collected. The beads were subjected to two washes with 60 μL of a wash buffer containing 2 M Urea and 50 mM Tris-HCl-pH 8.0, with both wash fractions being collected. The combined supernatants and wash fractions, totaling 280 μL per sample, were reduced with 2.3 μL of 500 mM Dithiothreitol for 30 min at 1,000 rpm at 25 °C. This was followed by an alkylation reaction using 4.6 μL of 500 mM Iodoacetamide (I1149, Merck) for 45 min under the same conditions. The samples were then subjected to overnight digestion with 0.5 μg of Trypsin at 700 rpm at 25 °C. The reactions were terminated by the addition of Formic Acid (FA, 5.33002, Merck) to a final concentration of 1%, followed by desalting using reverse-phase C-18 StageTips, and subsequently dried . The peptides were reconstituted in 80 μL of 50 mM HEPES buffer (51558, Merck), followed by the addition of 8 μL of Acetonitrile (1.00029, Merck). Subsequently, isobaric labeling was performed using 500 μg of the Tandem Mass Tag (TMT) 18-plex reagent (A44520, Thermo Fisher) for 1 h at 25 °C with agitation at 1000 rpm. Following confirmation of successful labeling, indicated by greater than 95% label incorporation, the reactions were quenched with 4 μL of 5% Hydroxylamine (159417, Merck). The samples were then combined and subjected to drying. The dried sample was reconstituted in 300 μL of 0.1% FA to facilitate fractionation using a basic reverse-phase (bRP) strategy on StageTips. The StageTips were prepared with three plugs of Empore Extraction Disks SDB-RPS 47 mm material. Conditioning of the StageTips was carried out sequentially with 100 μL of Methanol (34860, Merck) and 100 μL of a 50% Acetonitrile/0.1% FA solution. The sample was loaded onto the StageTips and washed twice with 100 μL of 0.1% FA. A buffer exchange was performed by adding 25 μL of 20 mM Ammonium Formate (714690, Merck) to the StageTip. Elution was conducted into eight consecutive fractions with increasing organic content, using 100 μL per elution step (comprising 5%, 10%, 15%, 20%, 23%, 27%, 30%, and 45% Acetonitrile in 20 mM Ammonium Formate). The fractions were subsequently transferred to High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) vials and dried.

## LC-MS/MS analysis of samples
The samples were analyzed using a Q Exactive HF-X mass spectrometer (IQLAAEGAAPFALGMAZR, Thermo Fisher) coupled with a Vanquish Neo UHPLC system (VN-S10-A-01, Thermo Fisher). The solvents employed for the HPLC system comprised solvent A, consisting of 0.1% FA, and solvent B, containing 0.1% FA in 100% Acetonitrile. The samples were injected into a 75 μm inner diameter PicoFrit column (PF360-75-10-N-5, New Objective, MA, USA), which was manually packed to an approximate length of 28 cm with ReproSil-Pur C18 AQ 120 Å, 1.9 μm (r119.aq.s0113, Dr. Maisch, Germany) and maintained at 50 °C to facilitate chromatographic separation. The bRP fractions were introduced into the column at a flow rate of 200 nL/min. The chromatographic method spanned 110 min, featuring a gradient of 1.6–5.4% solvent B for 1 min, 18–27% B for 22 min, 27–54% B for 9 min, 54–72% B for 1 min, followed by a hold at 72% B for 5 min, and a final gradient of 72–45% B over 10 min. The mass spectrometer operated in data-dependent acquisition mode, with the following MS parameters: MS1 resolution set at 60,000; MS2 resolution at 15,000; MS1 automatic gain control (AGC) target of 3 × 106 with a maximum injection time of 10 ms; MS2 analysis for the 20 most abundant ions using an AGC target of 5 × 104 and a maximum injection time of 105 ms; and a normalized collision energy of 28.
Analysis of the LC-MS/MS experimental data was conducted using the Spectrum Mill software suite (Broad Institute, MA, USA). The extracted spectral data were aligned with a UniProt database containing mouse reference proteome sequences. For TMT labeling, lysine residues were obligatorily modified, while peptide N-termini could be either labeled or left unmodified. Protein quantification was performed by calculating the ratio of TMT reporter ion intensities for each sample relative to the median intensity across all channels. To correct for isotopic impurities, the TMT18 reporter ion intensities were adjusted using the afRICA method within the protein/peptide summary module of Spectrum Mill. This correction method employed determinant calculations based on Cramer’s Rule, incorporating adjustment factors derived from the reagent manufacturer’s certificate of analysis for batch numbers XD343709 and XD346675 .

## Cell sorting and culture
Sorting purity was confirmed to be >95% by post-sort reanalysis. Approximately 1 × 10⁵ viable target cells were collected into sterile tubes containing complete culture medium. ## Gating strategy for the identification of neutrophil subpopulations
The initial step involved the exclusion of debris and dead cells by employing forward scatter versus side scatter parameters to select intact cells, followed by the application of Fixable Viability Stain 575 V to eliminate dead cells. For the identification of immune cells, gating was performed on CD45⁺ cells to delineate the pan-leukocyte population. Subsequently, within the CD45⁺ population, CD11b⁺ cells were gated to enrich for myeloid lineage cells. Neutrophils were specifically identified within the CD11b⁺ myeloid population by gating on Ly6G⁺ cells, leveraging the characteristic high expression of Ly6G in this granulocytic subset. The classification of neutrophil subpopulations was achieved within the Ly6G⁺ neutrophil gate by analyzing the combinatorial expression of indicated markers (ATF3, RelB, CYBB, CYBA, IFITM1, SLPI, IFIT3, IFIT1, IFI27L2A, and ISG15) (Supplementary Fig.  ).

## Basic analysis
We used ComBat to remove batch effects from the dataset and normalized it with ChIPseqSpikeInFree . HiChIP paired-end reads were aligned to the mm39 genome using the HiC-Pro pipeline , with default settings for duplicate removal, MboI fragment assignment, and valid interaction filtering. Enhancers were identified from dataset GSE184591 by H3K27ac enrichment in MC38 cells. To evaluate EPLs in HiChIP data, we calculated +/−250 kb contacts from transcription start sites in MC38 cells using hicPlotViewpoint at a 5 kb resolution. For background ligation frequency, we sampled 1,000 random intragenic regions over 10 kb from promoters and calculated relative counts within a +/−250 kb window for each HiChIP experiment. For each distance, a Z-statistic was calculated using background estimation, and adjusted P values determined statistical significance of contact, removing 50-80% of noisy chromatin interactions. Significant EPLs showed enrichment across replicates, passing a 5% FDR. HiChIP compartment analysis was done at 250 kb resolution using the eigenvector function on KR normalized maps . Differential EPLs were identified using biological replicate EPL counts with diffloop . FDR was used for adjusting multiple comparisons in multi-group or pathway enrichment analyses.

## Advanced analysis
(1) Identification of Super Interactive Promoters (SIPs) involved calculating a cumulative score for each gene by summing the log10FDR of its EPLs. SIPs were identified at the curve’s inflection point (slope = 1) on ranked cumulative interaction score plots.(2) Analysis of EPLs involved categorizing STAT3/S100A8/9-EPLs based on STAT3 presence at promoters, enhancers, or both. Genomic heatmaps centered on STAT3 positions were created, and STAT3 mode enrichment was analyzed using Metascape . (3) EPL plots were made with DNARchitect , and HiChIP heatmaps were generated and visualized using HiC-Pro and Juicebox .
To mitigate the potential interference from false positive or false negative signals, we implemented several measures in both the experimental design and data analysis processes. In the experimental design, we(1) selected an appropriate anti-STAT3 antibody (710077, Superclonal, Invitrogen) for the ChIP experiment;(2) optimized the Paraformaldehyde concentration (1-2%) and fixation time (5-10 min) to prevent excessive cross-linking that could result in DNA fragmentation;(3) ensured that the cell count was no less than 1 × 107 cells per sample; and(4) maintained a low PCR duplication rate, with a duplication rate of less than 30%. In the data analysis process, we implemented several strategies to mitigate the impact of false positive signals:(1) Interactions exhibiting a low degree of overlap (<30%) among independent biological replicates were excluded;(2) Interactions with low support, such as loops with fewer than five valid pairs, were removed;(3) Known loops were selected from established databases, such as ENCODE and Roadmap , for interaction analysis;(4) The analysis was repeated using the FitHiChIP /HiCCUPS to retain only consistent results; and(5) Short-distance interactions (<10 kb), which were susceptible to random connection interference, and ultra-long-distance interactions (>2 Mb), which had low capture efficiency, were excluded. To address the potential interference of false negative signals, we adopted the following measures:(1) Exclusion of the loops that continue to increase, indicating insufficient depth;(2) Ensuring a high detection rate of known loops;(3) Gradual reduction of the analysis threshold, such as adjusting P values from 0.05 to 0.01, to ensure the identification of the interactions with high confidence; and(4) Comparison of loop counts using HiC-Pro versus Cooltools to confirm the absence of significant discrepancies.

Ligands were docked to the receptors using AutoDock Vina , and the conformations with the highest scores were selected for visualization with PyMol and LigPlus .

## scRNA-Seq
scRNA-Seq libraries were generated utilizing the Chromium Single Cell 3’ Reagent Kits (PN-1000121, 10× Genomics, CA, USA). The process initiated with the emulsification step, during which individual cells were encapsulated into droplets containing gel beads. These beads were functionalized with unique primers that included 10× Genomics cell barcodes, unique molecular identifiers (UMIs), and poly(dT) sequences. Reverse transcription was subsequently conducted to synthesize barcoded full-length complementary DNA (cDNA). Following this, the emulsions were disrupted using a recovery agent, and the cDNA was purified. The bulk cDNA produced was then subjected to amplification and additional purification. Library construction was carried out using the Chromium Next GEM Single Cell 3’ Reagent Kits v3.1 (PN-1000121, 10× Genomics), involving a series of steps: fragmentation, end-repair, and A-tailing; size selection via Solid Phase Reverse Immobilization (SPRI); adaptor ligation; post-ligation purification using SPRI selection; sample index PCR; and final purification with SPRI selection beads. The indexed libraries were pooled based on cell counts and sequenced on a NovaSeq 6000 platform with paired-end 150 bp reads. Sequencing data analysis was outsourced to 10 K Genomics (China).

## Basic processing of scRNA-Seq data
(1) Data batch effect removal and normalization: We employed ComBat to estimate and eliminate batch effect parameters from the dataset. Subsequently, we normalized the data using RPKM/FPKM metrics.(2) Gene quantification quality control and data preprocessing: Based on the initial quality control by Cell Ranger, Seurat was used to perform further quality control on the data. Filter low-quality cells: the cells with gene values > 200, UMI values > 1,000, log10GenesPerUMI > 0.7, mitochondrial UMI ratio <5%, and red blood cell gene ratio <5% were retained as high-quality cells, and DoubletFinder was applied to remove double cells. The data were normalized using NormalizeData .(3) Dimensionality reduction and clustering analysis: FindVariableGenes was performed to screen the top 2,000 highly variable genes, principal components analysis was utilized based on the expression profiles of highly variable genes, and the results were visualized in 2D space using UMAP .(4) Marker gene identification: Marker gene identification was performed using FindAllMarkers . The identified marker genes were visualized using VlnPlot and FeaturePlot .(5) SingleR was employed to juxtapose the expression profile of the unidentified cell against a publicly available reference dataset, ultimately assigning the unidentified cell to the cell type in the reference dataset with the highest correlation. For multi-group or pathway enrichment analyses, we employ the FDR to adjust for multiple comparisons.

## Advanced analysis of scRNA-Seq data
(1) Single-cell Analysis of Host-Microbiome Interactions (SAHMI) . The Kraken2 database was used to classify the reads with the parameters set to “-use-names” and “-report-minimizer-data”. First, barcode denoising was performed. The true taxa were identified by Spearman correlation calculations using the number of unique and total k-mers in the barcodes. Taxa that were found to be significantly related in at least one sample were retained. For all retained taxa, the genomic contigs belonging to that taxon were extracted from the Kraken2 database, and then the reads assigned to that specific taxon were mapped to those genomic contigs using Bowtie 2 . In addition, rRNA as well as extracted genomic contigs were annotated using Barrnap. BEDTools was used to calculate aligned reads that overlapped the annotated rRNA. We calculated the fold enrichment of reads in rRNA relative to the entire genome and then normalized by rRNA and genome length, respectively. Bacterial groups found to contain at least fivefold enrichment of rRNA sequences relative to the entire genome were retained. From the host reads, we determined which cell types the barcodes corresponded to. Since reads classified as microbial taxa also contained the same barcodes, we assigned cell types to microbial reads. To calculate the relative abundance of bacterial populations in each cell type, we divided the number of reads of bacterial populations assigned to the barcodes of that cell type by the total number of reads assigned to that cell type.(2) GSEA. Signature gene sets were selected from the MSigDB gene set for enrichment analysis. For each enriched signature gene set, the normalized enrichment score (NES) and P value were calculated. Entries with |NES| value > 1 and P value < 0.05 were considered statistically significantly enriched.(3) Single-Cell Regulatory Network Inference and Clustering (SCENIC) was used to evaluate regulatory networks associated with transcription factors and to find regulators. A gene expression matrix with gene names in rows and cell types in columns was input into SCENIC . Genes were filtered using default parameters and available genes were obtained using the RcisTarget database. The co-expressed genes of each transcription factor were constructed using GENIE3, and then Spearman correlation analysis was performed between transcription factors and potential targets, and then the “runSCENIC” program was utilized to assist in the generation of regulators. The activity of regulators was analyzed using AUCell, in which a default threshold was used to binarize specific regulators.(4) Cell communication analysis. CellChat was performed to calculate the interactions between ligands and receptors among different cell populations. Briefly, the “IdentifyOverExpressedGenes” and “IdentifyOverExpressedInteractions” functions were used to find ligand-receptor interactions between neutrophils and other cells. The “ComputeCommunProb” function was used to calculate the communication probability and interaction strength among different cell subpopulations. To identify differences among datasets, the “MergeCellChat” and “CompareInteractions” functions were used. The difference edge list was utilized to generate a heatmap with the help of the “netVisual_heatmap” function and to find the receptor-ligand edges between neutrophils and other cells, and then a circular plot was generated using “netVisual_circle” function. We selected several neutrophil-associated receptor-ligand edges with high communication probability for visualization.

## Whole blood analysis
The whole blood analysis was conducted by collecting blood samples from the portal vein of the mice. The whole blood was transferred into a 1.5 mL anticoagulant Eppendorf tube containing Heparin (HY-17567, MedChemExpress). The tube was gently inverted to ensure thorough mixing of the blood with Heparin, thereby preventing coagulation. Subsequently, the prepared samples were analyzed using a DxH 500 Hematology Analyzer (Beckman Coulter, CA, USA) to determine the counts of white blood cells, monocytes, lymphocytes, and neutrophils. The abundance of E. coli in the portal vein was detected by RT-qPCR.

## 2bRAD-M-Seq

## Sample size estimation
The analysis of bacterial composition employed β-diversity to assess community-level differences. Our preliminary experiments revealed low within-group variability, as indicated by an intra-class correlation coefficient of less than 0.3 for Bray-Curtis distances, which consequently reduced the necessary sample size. Additionally, we prioritized biological feasibility, constrained by sample availability and cost, while ensuring that a sample size of n = 6 surpassed the minimum requirement. Previous studies has demonstrated that a sample size of n = 4 per group effectively balances statistical rigor with practical limitations.
2bRAD-M-Seq has several advantages over 16 s sequencing. 2bRAD-M-Seq utilizes just 1% of genomic data compared to 16 s sequencing, making it cost-effective for low biomass and degraded samples. It can accurately analyze microbial samples with as little as 1 pg of total DNA, even if highly fragmented or with 99% host DNA contamination, providing precise species-level results for bacteria, archaea, and fungi . The specific operation steps were as follows: Library preparation: Genomic DNA was extracted using TIANamp Micro DNA Kit (DP316, Tiangen, China). The 2bRAD-M-Seq library was prepared according to the following protocol : DNA (200 ng) was heated at 37 °C for 3 h with 4 IU of BcgI enzyme (R0545, New England Biolabs, MA, USA). The DNA fragment was then ligated to the vector. 5 µL of digested DNA, 10 µL of vector, and 800 IU of T4 DNA Ligase (M0202S, New England Biolabs) were mixed and ligated at 4 °C for 12 h. The products were amplified by PCR and electrophoresed on 8% polyacrylamide gel at 400 V for 35 min. A band of approximately 100 bp was excised from the polyacrylamide gel and the DNA was diffused from the gel into DEPC-Treated Water (AM9916, Invitrogen). PCR was performed using platform-specific barcoded primers. The PCR products were purified using the QIAquick PCR purification kit (28104, Qiagen, MA, USA) and then sequenced using the Illumina Nova PE150 platform. The sequencing read depth for each sample was set at 3 M. 2bRAD-M-Seq was performed at OE BioTech (China).

## Bioinformatics analysis
(1) In order to characterize species-specific 2bRAD-M markers, a comprehensive dataset comprising 404,199 microbial genomes sourced from the GTDB and Ensembl databases was analyzed using custom Perl scripts. These scripts facilitated the generation of restriction fragment profiles for 16 distinct type IIB restriction enzymes, thereby enabling the construction of an extensive 2bRAD-M reference database. Each genome’s 2bRAD-M tags were annotated with its corresponding GCF identifier and taxonomic classification. Tags that were uniquely associated with a single species, without overlap with tags from other taxa, were identified as species-specific markers, thus forming a specialized 2bRAD-M marker repository.(2) To estimate relative abundances, microbial species within individual samples were identified by aligning quality-filtered 2bRAD-M tags to a curated database of unique 2bRAD-M markers encompassing 86,022 species, employing a Perl-based script. To mitigate FDR, a G score was calculated for each candidate species, defined as the harmonic mean of read depth and the total count of potential 2bRAD-M markers for that taxon. A G score threshold of 5 was applied to filter out spurious identifications. , where s was the read count mapped to all 2bRAD-M markers of species i in a sample, and t was the total number of 2bRAD-M markers for species i sequenced in that sample. 
 . Here, s was as previously defined, and t was adjusted to represent the number of potential 2bRAD-M markers for species i. Average read coverage of 2bRAD-M markers per species was used to estimate individual counts, with relative abundance expressed as the proportion of individuals of a species relative to the total detectable individuals from all known species in the sample. Microbial α-diversity was assessed using Chao1, Shannon, and Simpson indices via the Vegan package . Group comparisons were conducted using two-tailed Student’s t-tests or Wilcoxon tests, and multi-group or pathway enrichment analyses applied FDR correction for multiple testing. For the assessment of β-diversity, an operational taxonomic unit abundance table was generated through QIIME2-based quality control, UPARSE clustering, and subsequent normalization of relative abundance. The Bray-Curtis distance matrix was utilized to quantify β-diversity, and differences in community structure across groups were evaluated using permutational multivariate analysis of variance, executed via the adonis2 function in Vegan package. With 999 permutations and a significance threshold set at α = 0.05, this analysis assessed the explanatory power of grouping factors on community variation. Statistical comparisons included Kruskal-Wallis tests to evaluate overall group differences and LEfSe to identify discriminant taxa for each group. Indicator analysis was employed to identify species characteristic of specific groups, while Pearson correlation analysis was conducted to explore genus-level associations based on relative abundance. To compare compositional differences between two groups, a centered log-ratio centered log-ratio transformation was applied prior to conducting PCA .

## Statistics and Reproducibility
Sample sizes for all experiments were prospectively determined through statistical power analysis, aiming for a target power of ≥ 80% with an α level of 0.05, to detect biologically meaningful effects. Effect sizes were derived from preliminary data or existing literature. We employed parametric tests and simulation-based methods for complex designs, striving to balance statistical rigor with ethical and practical constraints. Regarding data exclusion, no data were omitted from the analyses; all collected data points were retained to accurately represent the full experimental scope. The experiment group was randomized. Additionally, investigators were blinded to allocation during experiments and outcome assessment. Our measures ensured transparency in methodological rigor, aligning with standards for reproducible research. We confirmed that all experiments were independently replicated at least three times under identical conditions to ensure reliability. We used SPSS (IBM, NY, USA) and R software for statistical analysis, and GraphPad Prism (GraphPad, CA, USA) and R software to generate visual images. All data were expressed as mean ± standard deviation (SD), and P value < 0.05 were considered statistically significant. Normality analyses were conducted using the Shapiro-Wilk test. Homogeneity of variance analyses were conducted using the Levene test. When comparing two samples, a two-tailed Student’s T-test was used if the data were normally distributed and the variances were homogeneous. Otherwise, a two-tailed Wilcoxon rank sum test was used. Kaplan-Meier curves with 95% confidence intervals were plotted, and the Log-rank test was used to compare survival curves. In this study, low-quality reads and samples were filtered using standard bioinformatics pipelines. For reads:(1) quality assessment via FastQC identified bases with Phred scores <20 (error rate > 1%);(2) Trimmomatic removed adapter sequences, trimmed low-quality ends (sliding window: 4 bp, Q < 20), and discarded reads <50 bp post-trimming. For samples:(1) depth filtering excluded samples with <10 M clean reads (or <20% of mean depth);(2) FastQ Screen detected host/contaminant DNA ( > 5% contamination led to exclusion);(3) GC content (38-42%) and duplication rates (<30%) were checked via Qualimap to flag anomalies. These steps ensured data integrity for downstream analysis.
