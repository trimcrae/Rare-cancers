## Background
Head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) affects more than half a million people annually, and nearly 80% of oropharyngeal cancer cases are caused by human papillomavirus (HPV). Current treatments include chemo- and radiotherapy, though the effectiveness of these therapies is limited by the viral oncoprotein E6, which disrupts apoptotic pathways by binding and accelerating the degradation of molecules such as E6AP and caspase-8. Our lab has identified an E6 inhibitor, 30-hydroxygambogic acid (GA-OH), that is able to maintain these apoptotic signaling molecules.

## Method
To further explore the therapeutic potential of this small molecule, we determined its antitumor efficacy in vivo. We developed an optimized xenograft model for HPV+ HNSCC, assessed GA-OH’s toxicity, and evaluated the effectiveness of GA-OH in combination with chemotherapy utilizing the optimized concentration of 0.6 mg/kg.

## Results
GA-OH significantly increases (* p = 0.0105) cisplatin’s efficacy in HPV+ HNSCC in vivo without overt clinical manifestations. The only toxicities noted were a 4-fold increase in creatine kinase (**** p < 0.0001) and a 2.4-fold increase in aspartate aminotransferase (** p = 0.0057) in the cisplatin and GA-OH combination group compared to the vehicle group.

## Conclusion
The small molecule GA-OH was tolerable in our murine model, significantly amplifying the efficacy of cisplatin treatment.

## Introduction
Head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) is the sixth most common cancer worldwide, affecting more than half a million people annually. HNSCC is an epithelial tumor arising in the mucosal lining of the oral cavity, nasal cavity, oropharynx, larynx, and hypopharynx. The traditional etiology for HNSCC is the use of tobacco and alcohol; however, a steady HNSCC rise in developed countries in the past couple of decades has established high-risk human papillomavirus (HR-HPV) as a risk factor [ – ]. Nearly 70% of oropharyngeal cancers are associated with HPV infection, with HPV type 16 accounting for 82% of HPV-positive HNSCC [ – ].
Once tumors develop, current therapeutic approaches for HNSCC are limited and do not accommodate the evolving subtypes of HNSCC. Each subset of HNSCC is characteristically different and exhibits divergent clinical outcomes, as HPV-infected patients experience better cancer treatment response and survival than their non-HPV counterparts [ ]. Current treatment guidelines do not stratify based on HPV status, and standard of care consists of invasive surgery, chemotherapy, radiation, or a combination of these modalities [ ]. The challenge with HPV+ HNSCC patients is the high prevalence of long-term post-treatment morbidity (82%) [ ]. The classical therapeutic regimen used to treat HNSCC patients leads to long-term sequelae in the otherwise high-functioning, nonsmoking young male population, which is the representative demographic of this cancer subtype [ , ]. Therefore, treatment side effects frequently create functional complications, such as difficulties and impairments with chewing, swallowing, and breathing; these sequelae compromise a young patient’s quality of life with decades left to live [ , , ]. Due to the myriad of adverse events associated with chemotherapy, targeted therapy has become an attractive treatment strategy due to its typically favorable side effect profile and increased efficacy. To date, cetuximab is the only targeted therapy that is widely used for HNSCC [ ]. However, cisplatin, a cytotoxic agent and DNA intercalator, remains the standard of care for HNSCC. There remains a significant clinical need for new, improved treatment approaches that can target the tumor with minimal associated toxicities.
To develop strategies for increasing the effectiveness while decreasing the toxicities of current treatment regimens, our lab screened and tested several small-molecule libraries, leading to the identification of 30-hydroxygambogic acid (GA-OH) as an inhibitor of E6 [ ]. The oncoprotein E6 of HR-HPV has been identified as a major contributor to the oncogenicity of HPV. E6 blocks multiple apoptotic pathways, mainly through the degradation of proteins such as p53 and caspase 8, thereby allowing the virus to persist in the host and increasing the probability of tumor formation. Hence, E6 has become an attractive target, and a myriad of therapeutic agents and E6 antagonists have been developed. The repertoire of E6 inhibiting agents includes therapeutic vaccines, ribozymes, antisense oligonucleotides, small interfering RNA (siRNA), peptidomimetics, zinc finger nucleases (ZFN), transcription activator-like effector nucleases (TALENs), clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeat-associated nuclease (CRISPR/Cas9), and small-molecule compounds [ – ].
Our E6 inhibiting small molecule GA-OH was derived from the parent compound, gambogic acid (GA). GA and many of its other derivatives have indicated efficacy in cancer treatment. GA has exhibited anticancer activities such as inducing apoptosis and inhibiting metastasis and angiogenesis [ , ]. Moreover, it has demonstrated cancer growth inhibition in breast, pancreatic, prostate, and lung cancer. A GA isomer, gambogenic acid, has demonstrated more potent antitumor efficacy while exhibiting lower toxicity in a wide range of cancers, i.e., colorectal, gastric, liver, bladder, and breast cancers [ ]. Another GA derivative, N-(carboxymethyl)gambogamide, GA3, is a more soluble compound demonstrating distinct anticancer activity in many different cancers [ ]. Our lab has initially identified GA as a lead compound and followed up on several GA derivatives: morellic acid, gambogenic acid, neo-gambogic acid, gambogin, and morellinol [ ]. After testing the different GA derivatives, we selected the derivative GA-OH as our lead small molecule. Subsequently, our lab has demonstrated that GA-OH is able to selectively inhibit E6 activity by binding to the oncoprotein, which then increases the levels of p53, caspase 8, p21, and caspase 3, thereby sensitizing tumor cells to chemotherapy-induced apoptosis [ ].
The subsequent step in our research was to make the transition to animal models and test the effectiveness of the application of GA-OH alone and in combination with chemotherapy in vivo, using cisplatin and cetuximab as model agents. Our hypothesis was that GA-OH due to its ability to inhibit E6 and sensitize HPV+ cells to apoptosis, would increase the effectiveness of chemotherapeutic agents such as cisplatin. We therefore tested the ability of GA-OH to reduce or eliminate tumor growth in a mouse xenograft model, along with a GA-OH dose-finding study and toxicological assessments and found that GA-OH amplifies the efficacy of cisplatin in vivo. This study is anticipated to advance the development of effective therapeutic approaches to combat HPV-associated malignancies.

## Materials & methods

## Cell culture
For in vivo visualization, the human HPV+ head and neck cancer cell line UM-SCC47, which was a gift from Dr. Thomas Carrey, University of Michigan (Michigan, USA), was transduced with the UMSCC47 ffLuc-eGFP lentiviral vector [ ]as described in Data S1 (Figures S1-S3). The cells were cultured in DMEM (Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium) (Genesee, San Diego, CA), supplemented with 9% fetal bovine serum (Genesee), penicillin (100 units/ml), and streptomycin (100 µg/ml) (Sigma-Aldrich), and maintained at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere of 95% air/5% CO2.

## Reagents
30-hydroxygambogic acid (GA-OH) obtained from Quality Phytochemicals LLC (New Jersey, USA) was dissolved in DMSO to a stock solution of 6447.5 mg/L (10 mM) concentration before diluting it into a final concentration of 120 mg/L (186.119 µM) immediately prior to use in the tumor inhibition study. Cisplatin (Accord Healthcare Inc, Durham, NC) and cetuximab (Erbitux) (ImClone LLC, Branchburg, NJ) were a donation from Dr. Kofi Donkor and his pharmaceutical team at the Loma Linda University Medical Center Cancer Center. Caspase 3 (Cat No. 9661), p21 (Cat No. 2947) (Cell Signaling, Danvers MA), and p53 (Cat No. sc-126x) (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Dallas TX) were used to stain the immunohistochemical slides. Cultrex (R&D Systems, Minneapolis, MN), a basement membrane mixture, was used for the engraftment and implantation of the tumor grafts. Sterile saline solutions (0.8%) were administered for vehicle treatment.

## Dose-finding study
We induced HPV+ HNSCC in mouse xenograft models by subcutaneously injecting 107 of UMSCC47 ffLuc-eGFP cells into CD-1 (Crl: CD1-Foxn1nu-086) immunodeficient nude mice as previously optimized in our lab [ – ]. The mice received variable dosages of GA-OH, starting with 5 mg/kg and ranging down to 0.25 mg/kg. 125 µL of the appropriate GA-OH dose or saline was delivered locally on the tumor on Days 1 and 2. No injections were made on Day 3, and this 3-day cycle was repeated 5 times for a total of about 2 ½ weeks. Following GA-OH injections, the spot of injection was inspected for redness, swelling, discharge, or other signs of toxicity. Mice were weighed before every procedure, and tumor growth was measured by Vernier calipers and monitored by bioluminescence imaging. Mice were monitored for general health (hydration, mucus membranes, discharge, weight, mobility) throughout the experiments. If an animal exhibited signs of pain or distress, or their tumors reached 20 mm in one dimension, they were euthanized by CO2 narcosis followed by exsanguination.

## Tumor implantation and animal maintenance
Sixty naïve 4–6 weeks old immunodeficient nude mice (CD-1 (Crl: CD1-Foxn1nu-086, 30 female and 30 male) were obtained from Charles River Laboratories (Wilmington, MA) and housed in pathogen-free conditions with individually ventilated cages. The tumor cells were resuspended in 100 µL DMEM and 100 µL Cultrex (R&D Systems, Minneapolis MN) in a 1:1 ratio and implanted subcutaneously into the flanks using a 1 mL syringe. Each injection consisted of 1.5 × 107 UMSCC47 ffLuc-eGFP as previously established in our lab [ – ], and monitoring of tumor growth was performed through IVIS imaging and caliper measurements.
Ten animals were randomly assigned to each experimental group based on tumor size and gender: [A] Vehicle; [B] GA-OH alone; [C] Cisplatin alone; [D] Cisplatin + GA-OH; [E] Cetuximab alone; [F] Cetuximab + GA-OH. The number of mice per group was estimated based on our preliminary experiments with xenograft mice, to obtain a statistically significant difference in tumor growth between control and tested groups. Power calculations indicated that to detect a difference of at least 35% in mean tumor volume when administering the therapeutic agent, with a power of 90% and an alpha (Type I) value of 0.05, 10 mice would be necessary for each group (10 mice x 6 groups = 60 mice/experiment).
The treatment regimen started a week after tumor injection with 120 mg/L GA-OH or saline direct intratumoral injections to each group. Saline was utilized as a treatment in the vehicle and single-treatment control groups. The chemotherapeutic drugs cisplatin (1 mg/mL) or cetuximab (2 mg/mL) were administered intraperitoneally. Approximately 125 µL of GA-OH or chemotherapeutic agent were injected into each group, using sterile techniques and under isoflurane anesthetic inhalation at a 3% induction rate delivered in 100% oxygen. Assessments such as tumor size, weight, level of activity, posture, and changes in behavior were carried out every 2–3 days during the treatment period. We monitored the mice for another 3 weeks to assess the long-term efficacy of the treatments. If an animal exhibited signs of pain or distress or tumors larger than 20 mm in one dimension, they were euthanized earlier. Some of the mice in our inhibition study reached these criteria and were euthanized prior to the long-term endpoint. Mice were sacrificed by CO2 inhalation in a chamber with a percent volume displacement rate of 30–70%, followed by exsanguination through cardiac puncture. Blood was collected for serum and hematological tests. Tumors were dissected, photographed near a ruler (for size estimation), and weighed. The following end-point parameters were evaluated: mortality, clinical signs, body weight, hematology and serum biochemistry, and post-mortem major organ evaluation.
All animals were housed at the Loma Linda University animal care facility (ACF) and acclimated for at least one week before the start of the studies, and experimental procedures were conducted under sterile conditions. Standard irradiated chow diet and sterilized water were provided ad libitum. All animal procedures and protocols were reviewed and approved by the Loma Linda University Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC 20–127, 8 March 2022).

## Tumor growth measurements
Tumor volume was determined twice weekly by Vernier caliper measurements (Carolina Biological Supply, Burlington, NC). The length and width of each tumor were measured, and tumor volumes (V) were calculated using the following formula: V = LW2/2. The length (L) was considered as the longest diameter, whereas the width (W) was determined as the maximum diameter perpendicular to the direction of the longest diameter measured by the vernier.

## Immunochemistry
All tumor growth tissue was initially fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde and subsequently processed at the CTTR (California Tumor Tissue Registry). Tumor specimens were placed in 10% neutral buffered formalin, processed in the Tissu-tek VIP processor for 8 h, and embedded in paraffin. Formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded (FFPE) blocks were cut at a 4-µm thickness and put on positively charged glass slides. The prepared slides were stained with Caspase 3, p53, and p21 antibodies using an automated Leica Bond III platform (Leica Biosystems). Appropriate positive controls were used in all cases, and DAB chromogen was utilized. The slides were digitally scanned using the Leica Aperio CS2 and the Olympus Slideview vs200 Slide Scanner. In order to detect the largest difference in apoptosis and necrosis, we analyzed tumor slides from the combined group [D] of cisplatin + GA-OH, compared to the vehicle group [A]. The quantification of immunohistochemistry (IHC) was measured using QuPath software. Moreover, results were evaluated and interpreted by three independent pathologists (YCW, RAF, and MW).

## Statistical analysis
Data on tumor growth is presented as a group average in mm3 at each time point +/- standard error (SE). Within each group, tumor size at a certain time point is expressed as a percentage of tumor size at the treatment inception. One-way ANOVA was used to determine statistical significance of any observed differences between groups regarding end-point organ weight, tumor weight, and any blood test and toxicity panel analysis. Statistics were calculated using Prism software 9.4.1 (GraphPad Software Inc, San Diego, CA).
Generalized Least Squares Regression (GLSR) was used to assess the effects of group assignment, time, and group x time interaction on tumor size. Three group comparisons were made: [1] Cisplatin + GA-OH compared to Vehicle, [2] Cisplatin + GA-OH compared to GA-OH, and [3] Cisplatin + GA-OH compared to Cisplatin. Data analysis was performed using R (version 2022.07.2). Generalized least squares regression (GLSR) was used to compare the longitudinal effect or the differences in tumor growth over time between groups. The Intercept coefficient represents the reference group’s tumor size (in mm3) at baseline (Day 0). When time (in Days) is described in the analysis, it is referring to tumor growth per unit time (days) in the reference group. The group x time interaction coefficient represents the difference in tumor growth per unit time between groups. GLS regression was chosen because it does not assume that the error terms of the regression model are identically distributed or have a constant variance. Instead, GLS allows for heteroscedasticity and autocorrelation in the error structure and adjusts the estimates of the coefficients accordingly. A p-value of < 0.05 was considered statistically significant. * p < 0.05, ** p < 0.01, *** p < 0.001, **** p < 0.0001.
All in vitro experiments were repeated at least three times (three biological replicates, carried out on different days), with each experimental group measured in triplicate within each of these individual experiments.

## Results

## Dose-finding study
Before proceeding to the efficacy experiment, we optimized the concentration of GA-OH in vivo to a tolerable dose in mice. Our initial GA-OH dose was selected based on literature search of a parent analog compound, gambogic acid (GA), which yielded 5 mg/kg as relatively tolerable in rodents [ , ]. However, when we tested this concentration with our lead molecule, GA-OH, we observed toxicity: the day following the first injection, the animals presented with swelling, redness, and tenderness at the injection site We therefore tested two lower dosages of GA-OH: 3 mg/kg and 1 mg/kg. The GA-OH concentration of 3 mg/kg still manifested reactions and minor irritations to the treatment. Hence, we focused next on dosages below 2 mg/kg with a goal of finding a dose more tolerable and manageable in mice. We concluded that concentrations below 1 mg/kg were non-toxic to these mice, therefore we proceeded with 0.6 mg/kg as a dose predicted to minimize toxicity and maximize efficacy.

## GA-OH improves cisplatin efficacy in vivo HNSCC xenograft model
Tumor growth was monitored by caliper measurements. Based on our caliper measurements, we conclude that, as anticipated, tumors grew in a relatively linear manner in control animals, indicating a functional in vivo model. Additionally, both groups treated with cetuximab (alone or in combination with GA-OH) not only slowed but regressed tumor growth. Moreover, we found that GA-OH alone did not decrease the rate of HNSCC tumor growth compared with control. However, GA-OH in combination with cisplatin boasted better tumor control compared with cisplatin monotherapy, as the combination also showed higher regression of tumor growth than cisplatin alone when compared to Vehicle [A] (Fig. C). These tumor patterns can be visualized quantitatively through the tumor growth regression lines shown in Fig. A. Regression line analysis was performed using the Vehicle group [A] as a reference. When we analyzed the addition of GA-OH in each of the chemotherapeutic regimens before and immediately after the treatment period, there were no differences in tumor sizes between Vehicle [A] and GA-OH [B] after treatment, nor between Cetuximab [E] vs. Cetuximab + GA-OH [F], as depicted in red bars (Fig. B). In contrast, the only treatment where we observed a difference between the single and combination treatment was cisplatin, [C] vs. [D] (Fig. B). ## Immunohistochemical staining
The H&E staining indicates a significantly higher level of necrosis in the GA-OH and Cisplatin combined group [D] compared to Vehicle [A] (Fig. ). The apoptotic markers caspase 3, p53, and p21 did not show any significant difference in intact cells between the vehicle and the combined treatment; however, in areas adjacent to necrotic regions, these markers demonstrated focally intensified staining patterns in the cisplatin combined treatment compared to vehicle (Fig. A). Consistently, immunohistochemistry quantification of all cells revealed a significantly higher detection of Caspase 3, p53, and p21 positive cells in the Cisplatin + GA-OH treated groups [D] compared to Vehicle [A], as shown in Fig. B. Caspase 3, p53, and p21 were immunoreactive in areas of the tumor that exhibited apoptosis and necrosis histopathology. Tumors in the combined treatment group demonstrated increased necrosis as well as apoptotic expression compared to vehicle samples, indicating elevated levels of cell death and apoptosis in the GA-OH + Cisplatin combined group [D] compared to the Vehicle group [A]. 

## Toxicity results
We performed clinical observations for any changes indicative of declining health and watched for signs of toxicity including hunched posture, lethargy, or severe weight loss. Based on these assessments, physical or behavioral observations were deemed acceptable with no overt signs of toxicity noted. For example, a significant decrease in weight is indicative of toxicity, and we found that mice from each group had a relatively minor variance in weight with no significant differences observed (Fig. A). Moreover, the evaluation of major internal organs (liver, kidneys, and spleen) post-mortem showed no toxicity and relatively consistent organ weight between the treatment groups (Figure S4). We noted that one mouse in the cetuximab-treated group had an enlarged liver upon gross necropsy. Upon close observation, we noticed that this same mouse also appeared to have very elevated levels of Alanine Transaminase (ALT), as indicated in Fig. B-a.

 

We also performed hematological and clinical chemistry tests on blood collected immediately after euthanasia. The complete blood count panel had minor changes when comparing treatment regimens, but no significant disparity overall (Table S1). The toxicity chemistry panel showed small variations between treatments (Table ), except when it came to aspartate aminotransferase (AST) (** p = 0.0057) (Fig. B-b) and creatine kinase (CK) (**** p < 0.0001) (Fig. B-c), which were significantly higher in the Cisplatin + GA-OH group [D] compared to vehicle (A). AST in the Cisplatin & GA-OH combination therapy had a 2.4-fold increase compared to vehicle (Average AST [D]: 415.75 U/L vs. [A]: 173 U/L), whereas CK had nearly a 4-fold increase (Average CK [D]: 2264.25 U/L vs. [A]: 585.25 U/L). Additionally, when compared with the single-treated cisplatin [C], the combined treatment [D] had an appreciable elevation of AST that did not reach statistical significance (p = 0.1265) (Fig. C-a). However, creatine kinase continued to exhibit a highly significant increase in group [D] vs. group [C] (*** p = 0.0005) (Fig. C-b).

 

## Discussion
The number of small-molecule E6 inhibitors discovered has increased over the years. Some small compounds inhibit the interaction between E6 and p53, such as RITA, jaceosidin, nicandrenone, and Compound 12 [ , , – ]; other molecules block the E6-E6AP interactions, such as gossypetin, baicalein, brazilin, luteolin, CAF-24, and lig1-3 [ , – ]; while others prevent the interaction of E6 with targets such as p300, in the case of CH1iB [ ]. Our compound, GA-OH, obstructs the interactions between E6 and E6AP as well as E6 and caspase 8. 
In this study, we evaluated the effect of the small molecule compound GA-OH in an animal model and tested its efficacy in combination with two chemotherapeutic compounds, cetuximab and cisplatin. Both chemotherapies selected are commonly used in head and neck cancer management. We were able to demonstrate that GA-OH improves the efficacy of cisplatin in a xenograft model. Moreover, we demonstrated that GA-OH had relatively low toxicity in mice.
The targeted therapy cetuximab is a monoclonal antibody targeting the epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR), which is frequently overexpressed in HNSCC [ – ]. EGFR has multiple procarcinogenic effects, ranging from proliferation to metastasis, and plays a significant role in recurrences. According to previous studies, acceptable cetuximab dosing in rodents ranged between 4 and 40 mg/kg [ ]. In addition, UM-SCC47 cells, which we used for our HNSCC model, are EGFR-positive [ , ]. Our study has demonstrated very high efficacy with cetuximab in our HPV+ HNSCC xenograft model established through UM-SCC47 tumor injections, consistent with findings reported in similar studies of xenografts from different disease models [ ]. There is controversy about whether cetuximab has clinical utility in either HPV+ or HPV− patients [ ]. Cetuximab was widely used in the 2000 s; however, cisplatin has been shown to have a better response in terms of patient outcome and survival, and is the first-line treatment [ ]. In optimizing the concentrations of cisplatin, we found in the literature that it was tolerable to mice between the ranges of 1–10 mg/kg [ ]. Because we wanted to test the efficacy of combination treatments, we selected dosages in the mid-to-lower ranges to observe the effectiveness of any combined treatments.
We found cetuximab to demonstrate profound efficacy in regressing and eliminating tumor growth, to the extent that we were unable to delineate any possible effect of GA-OH in the combination treatment when compared to cetuximab treatment alone. In the case that we may use cetuximab as a component of combination therapy in the future, we would consider a lower dosage to discern an amplification effect when combined with lead molecules. Based on the growth curve, GA-OH alone was shown to have little to no effect on tumor growth, indicating low toxicity and low efficacy by itself, as expected. Nonetheless, when GA-OH was administered with cisplatin, the combination significantly increased the effect of cisplatin in vivo (* p = 0.0105), significantly decreasing tumor size and confirming our initial prediction. Furthermore, we were able to demonstrate that our tumor growth model was functional, and the xenograft model established in our lab is apt for future small-molecule testing. Previous experiments in our lab have shown that small-molecule inhibitors of E6 could sensitize HPV+ cells in combination with chemotherapy in vitro, and the animal study described here has provided proof of concept that inhibiting E6 can reduce tumor growth in combination with chemotherapy in vivo. This has strengthened the evidence that a combination of a small E6 inhibitor with chemotherapy may be a feasible therapeutic approach. In addition, the immunohistochemical staining showed that the tumors collected from the combination treatment indicated not only higher levels of the apoptotic markers caspase 3, p53, and p21 next to the necrotic regions but also an elevated level of necrosis compared to the vehicle. Therefore, GA-OH, increases the apoptotic molecules, inducing apoptosis and eventual necrosis in HPV+ HNSCC tumors when combined with cisplatin.
While the data is promising regarding GA-OH antitumor activity, some limitations are present in our study. The high individual efficacy of cetuximab obscured the potential effects of GA-OH in that combination therapy, indicating a need for lower cetuximab dosages in future investigations. Moreover, the BLI measurements obtained were not consistent with caliper measurement findings, and thus we were unable to use and present quantitative data from IVIS imaging. The IVIS images acquired throughout the study were still included under the Supplemental Section for visualization purposes. Furthermore, this is a preliminary study that was performed to show proof of concept that reconstituting the apoptotic pathway by a small molecule inhibitor facilitates tumor cells to undergo apoptosis in vivo. Therefore, further investigation is necessary to establish the drug action, efficacy, and safety of the drug through PK/PD, ADME, and druggability assays. Future studies should address the drug’s properties as well as carry out RNA sequencing to uncover its mechanism of action in more depth.
Regarding the toxicity of GA-OH, concentrations above 2 mg/kg manifested visible reactions of redness of skin, inflammation, tenderness, and swelling of the skin at the site of injection in the dose-response study. These reactions to GA-OH were evident within 24 h and most apparent at 5 mg/kg dosages. After a series of lower dosage testing between 0.25 mg/kg and 2 mg/kg, we narrowed down the higher end of doses likely to be nontoxic between 0.5 and 0.75 mg/kg. The 0.75 mg/kg concentration accounted for some minor signs of toxicity in the form of tiny tumor ulcerations and cavitations, while no visible signs were evident in dosages of 0.5 mg/kg GA-OH. In search of the highest dose that would fail to demonstrate toxicity, we selected 0.6 mg/kg of GA-OH for our tumor inhibition study.
The toxicity of each treatment group was further evaluated during the antitumor efficacy study. Physical and behavioral assessments were found to be mostly normal. All full body weights as well as the weight of major internal organs appeared to be within the acceptable parameters, with the exception of one mouse from the cetuximab-treated group, which was observed post-mortem to have hepatomegaly. Upon delving into the serum chemistry test of this mouse, the toxicity results were mostly within range except for very elevated levels of ALT (179U/L, [normal ranges 19-58U/L]), indicating severe liver injury. Liver damage can be caused by liver infection or drug toxicity, among other causes. Cetuximab is shown to cause hepatic enzyme elevation but no clinical liver injury. Since only one mouse in the treatment group exhibited an enlarged liver and elevated levels of ALT, there is not enough evidence to know whether any liver injury was due to the cetuximab treatment.
Blood chemistry comparison and analysis between the treatment groups demonstrated higher levels of CK (2264 U/L, **** p < 0.0001) and AST (415 U/L, ** p = 0.0057) in the combined cisplatin and GA-OH treated group as compared to vehicle (Fig. B). AST is another liver enzyme that may indicate liver damage at very elevated levels. When we compared the levels of the combined group to the cisplatin single-treated group, CK continued to be significantly higher (*** p = 0.0005) (Fig. C). Creatine kinase is an enzyme prevalent in skeletal muscle that plays a key role in high-energy phosphate metabolism [ ]and it is a biomarker of muscle damage or intense exercise [ ]. In extreme cases (> 5000IU/L), rhabdomyolysis, a rare muscle disease where the muscles break down, followed by acute kidney injury, is diagnosed. Elevated CK levels can also emerge from drug interactions metabolized through the cytochrome P450 3A4 pathway [ ]. Lipid-lowering drugs such as statins are commonly known to raise the levels of CK in patients. Other drugs that might elevate CK are antiretrovirals and oncologic drugs such as BRAF inhibitors and PD-L1 inhibitors [ ]. Interestingly, cisplatin has also been shown to be metabolized or cleared systemically through cytochrome P450 (CYP) CYP3A and to increase muscle wasting [ , ]. Additionally, GA, the parent analog of GA-OH, is known to be a potent inhibitor of CYP3A [ ]affecting drug interactions by concomitant drug clearance and metabolism reduction, and thus enhancing the drug’s toxicity as well as its therapeutic effect [ , ]. Hence, the observed toxicities should be examined before testing the proposed combination therapy in humans. The agents’ concentrations and levels should be further evaluated to determine whether they are within the human tolerable ranges.
GA has been widely studied and researched over the last couple of decades as a cancer therapeutic option, more generally in breast, lung, and liver cancer [ ]. In addition, preclinical and clinical evidence have indicated that GA has antitumor effects on several cancers, including but not limited to: breast cancer, lung cancer, prostate cancer, ovarian cancer, glioma, melanoma, head and neck cancer, and cervical cancer [ ]. In addition, GA has been demonstrated to have chemosensitization effects on several cancer types and, thus, garnered interest as an anti-cancer agent in combinatorial therapies [ ]. Furthermore, Wang et al. have shown that GA specifically increases cisplatin’s chemotherapeutic effect in non-small cell lung cancer [ ]. Therefore, we speculate that when GA-OH, a derivative of the P450 inhibitor GA, is combined with the cytotoxic agent cisplatin, clearance of cisplatin is reduced, leading to an elevated plasma concentration of cisplatin in the body. Hence, cisplatin’s chemotherapeutic effect as well as its toxicity become evidently more pronounced as observed in our study, increasing its antitumor efficacy as well as its muscle toxicity, indicated through CK’s significant elevation. This finding warrants further analysis with a larger sample to confirm the results. We will keep this toxicity marker in mind as we perform any future experiments pairing GA-OH and cisplatin together. In conclusion, behavioral and physical assessment, body weight, organ necropsy, and blood tests, with the possible exception of CK, all pointed toward a lack of significant toxicity, leading us to predict that this agent will be well tolerated at the appropriate dosage.

## Future directions
Future studies combining different HNSCC treatment therapies in the mouse xenograft would corroborate our hypothesis. We have shown that combined GA-OH treatment enhances radiotherapy efficacy in vitro [ ]. Consequently, translating this study into an in vivo context would further validate our working model. We have also established an HPV+ cervical cancer xenograft model that can be used to test multiple small-molecule inhibitors. Evaluating the small compound’s efficacy in this model could lead to demonstrations of GA-OH’s versatility in combating other HPV malignancies in vivo. This small molecule therapy approach has also been shown to display low toxicity at low concentrations, allowing for reduced concentrations of cytotoxic chemotherapeutic agents that could potentially lower side effects, all while increasing the effectiveness of chemotherapy treatment. We will test other chemotherapeutic agents, especially other platinum-based agents, to evaluate any improvement in efficacy. Additionally, GA-OH would require further development to improve potency and drug-likeness for clinical purposes. The limited number of effective therapies for HNSCC provides impetus for developing an effective, safe, and targeted therapy for HPV patients. For these compelling reasons, efforts to continue to optimize the safety and effectiveness of GA-OH and other small molecule E6 inhibitors remain prioritized. Such continued endeavors to develop innovative and effective therapeutic approaches to combat HPV infection and its associated malignancies will considerably impact not only therapeutic but also functional outcomes in this subset of HPV patients.

## Conclusion
In this study, we evaluated the small molecule compound GA-OH’s optimal dosage, toxicity, efficacy, and potential mechanism of action in an established head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) xenograft mouse model. GA-OH was optimized to be safe in our study with minimal to no observed toxicities at doses around 0.6 mg/kg. Our group examined the efficacy in combination with other chemotherapeutic drugs (cetuximab and cisplatin), and our results indicated that GA-OH increases tumor cell susceptibility to chemotherapy regimens in HPV+ HNSCC by increasing the apoptotic pathway molecules caspase 3, p53, and p21. GA-OH was found to have very little toxicity in the various treatment groups, while evidence of muscle and potential liver damage was found in the cisplatin and GA-OH combination group, warranting further investigation. Overall, this work supports the feasibility of GA-OH as an adjunct agent in combination with the chemotherapeutic agent cisplatin for HNSCC and demonstrates its potential as an antiviral and antitumor agent.
