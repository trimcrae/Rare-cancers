https://orcid.org/0009-0009-5357-4845WangXianglei XueShiyao LiYuwei YangMiaomiao YuHaotian WangMengyan LiSuxin LuZhigang https://orcid.org/0000-0001-5732-4848LuoMin Therapeutic cancer vaccines induce potent antitumor T-cell responses through the delivery of tumor antigens with immune adjuvants, are being evaluated in clinical trials, and have shown promising outcomes. However, although rarely reported in preclinical studies, multiple clinical trials have shown the overall response rate of cancer vaccine is <10% in unresectable advanced cancers. For instance, a phase III clinical trial of granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) plus peptide vaccination in patients with advanced melanoma failed to demonstrate significant improvements in recurrence-free survival or overall survival. Similarly, clinical trials of GVAX vaccines, which are composed of tumor cells engineered to secrete GM-CSF, have shown restricted efficacy to stimulate immune responses in advanced prostate cancer, melanoma, pancreatic cancer, and lung cancer. The mechanisms underlying the poor response of advanced tumors to cancer vaccines remain obscure, necessitating innovative strategies to overcome vaccine resistance and optimize therapeutic outcomes.
PMN-MDSCs, also termed tumor-associated neutrophils or granulocytic MDSCs, exhibit potent immune suppressive activity and have emerged as central regulators of the tumor microenvironment (TME). These cells are aberrantly expanded in most cancer types, employing diverse mechanisms to promote tumor progression and limit anticancer immune responses. For instance, PMN-MDSCs express a repertoire of immunosuppressive mediators, including reactive oxygen species (ROS), inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), arginine decarboxylase 1 (Arg1), and prostaglandins, causing T-cell dysfunction and disrupting antitumor immunity. Additionally, PMN-MDSCs can also produce pro-angiogenic factors, such as Bv8, matrix metalloproteinase 9, and vascular endothelial growth factor A, to promote tumor neovascularization. Growing evidence underscores a strong correlation between high frequencies of circulating and tumor-infiltrating neutrophils and adverse prognosis in patients with cancer. In particular, a landmark study involving 18 000 patients revealed neutrophils as the single cell population most strongly associated with mortality across diverse cancer subtypes. Moreover, elevated neutrophil infiltration has been linked to diminished responsiveness towards chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and immune checkpoint inhibitors. 
Targeting PMN-MDSCs has emerged as an attractive strategy for cancer treatment. Preclinical studies have shown that several PMN-MDSC-targeted agents enhance antitumor efficacy when combined with checkpoint inhibitors. CXCR2 inhibitors reduce tumor growth by blocking PMN-MDSC tumor infiltration and synergize with antiprogrammed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) antibodies, inhibition of FATP2 abrogates PMN-MDSC suppressive functions and sensitizes tumors to anti-CTLA4 (cytotoxic T lymphocyte-associated antigen-4) therapy, and agonistic DR5 antibodies induce PMN-MDSC apoptosis, potentiating anti-CTLA4 efficacy. However, concerns remain regarding off-target toxicities and compensatory myelopoiesis due to physiological homeostasis mechanisms. Recently, we identified CD300ld as a specific surface marker and critical immune suppressor on PMN-MDSCs to promote tumor progression and demonstrated that CD300ld blockade remodels the immunosuppressive TME and exerts potent antitumor activity. 
We previously developed a STING-activating nanovaccine by a simple mixing of antigen peptides with a synthetic polymer, PC7A, eliciting robust antitumor T-cell response in multiple tumor models. PC7A facilitates peptide delivery and cytosolic release and acts as a potent adjuvant to activate innate immunity as a polyvalent STING agonist and is currently undergoing phase I clinical trial (NCT06022029) for solid cancer treatment. In this study, we reveal that PC7A nanovaccine yields minimal antitumor effects when applied at the late tumor stage, primarily by enhancing PMN-MDSC recruitment and immunosuppressive function. CD300ld blockade effectively restores the efficacy of cancer vaccines in advanced tumor models, providing a potential translational strategy for cancer immunotherapy.

## Methods

## Preparation of vaccine
For PC7A nanovaccine preparation, micelles were prepared following a solvent evaporation method as previously published. PC7A polymer was dissolved in methanol and then added into distilled water under sonication. Methanol was removed using an ultrafiltration tube (Mw=100 kDa). The nanoparticles (NPs) were characterized by dynamic light scattering (Malvern MicroV, He-Ne laser, λ=632 nm) to determine the hydrodynamic diameter (Dh). Antigenic peptide was dissolved in distilled water, mixed with PC7A NPs, and diluted with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) to a final concentration of 10 µg of antigen peptide per milliliter or 300 µg of PC7A NP per milliliter. For poly I:C vaccine preparation, dissolved antigenic peptide was mixed with poly I:C adjuvant (Sigma, P0913) and diluted with PBS to a final concentration of 50 µg of antigen peptide per milliliter or 500 µg of poly I:C per milliliter.

## Animals
Wild-type (WT) C57BL/6 mice were purchased from GemPharmatech (Shanghai, China). CD300ld-knockout (KO) mice and huCD300ld mice were constructed in GemPharmatech. All mice were maintained under specific pathogen-free conditions at 22–26℃ with a 12:12-hour dark/light cycle and 40%–70% humidity. WT female mice were used at an age of 6–8 weeks. For genetically modified mice, age-matched and sex-matched mice were used for each experiment.

## Cell culture
B16-OVA and TC-1 cells were cultured in complete medium (DMEM, 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS), 100 U/mL penicillin G sodium and 100 µg/mL streptomycin (Pen/Strep). Cell cultures were frequently monitored for mycoplasma contamination, and only mycoplasma-negative cells were used for experiments.

## Antitumor efficacy studies
C57BL/6 mice (n=5–10 for each group) were injected subcutaneously with B16-OVA (1.5×105) and TC-1 cells (1.5×105) into the right flank. Mice were randomized before the start of therapy to different groups based on equal tumor size. Tumor growth was subsequently measured two times a week using a digital caliper with cage labels blinding for treatments from 7 days after tumor cell inoculation and calculated as 0.5×length×width2. Mice were considered humanely euthanized due to tumor burden when observation of tumors exceeding 2000 mm³ or when adverse signs (such as pain, apathy, dehydration, and necrotic tumor) were observed, and this event was recorded as death in the survival analysis. For vaccination, mice were subcutaneously injected at the tail base with PC7A vaccine (1 µg antigen peptide, 30 µg PC7A NP) or poly I:C vaccine (5 µg antigen peptide, 50 µg poly I:C) on 5 or 9 days after tumor inoculation. For mouse CD300ld-ECD and human CD300ld-ECD treatment, 9 days after tumor cell inoculation, mice were injected intraperitoneally with ECD protein or control human Fc (hFc) protein (10 mg/kg) every other day.

## Flow cytometry
Spleens and bone marrow were harvested under sterile conditions. Blood was harvested with heparin, and red blood cells (RBCs) were removed using RBC lysis buffer. Tumor tissues were digested by 0.25 mg/mL collagenase IV (Sigma-Aldrich) and 0.2 mg/mL DNase I (Sigma-Aldrich) for 20 min at 37°C. The murine antibodies purified antimouse CD16/32 (clone: 93), PE-Cy7 antimouse B220 (clone: RA3-6B2), Pacific Blue antimouse/human CD11b (clone: M1/70), Brilliant Violet 510 antimouse CD11c (clone: N418), fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC) antimouse CD3 (clone: 145-2C11), Allophycocyanin (APC) antimouse CD3 (clone: 17A2), APC-Cy7 antimouse CD4 (clone: GK1.5), APC antimouse CD45 (clone: 30-F11), APC antimouse CD49b (clone: DX5), FITC antimouse CD69 (clone: H1.2F3), PE-Cy7 antimouse CD8a (clone: 53-6.7), PE-Cy7 antimouse CD25 (clone: 3C7), PE-Cy7 antimouse CD86 (clone: GL-1), FITC antimouse Ly-6C (clone: HK1.4), APC/Cy7 antimouse Ly-6G (clone: 1A8), PE-Cy7 antimouse F4/80 (clone: BM8), PE antimouse MHCII (clone: M5/114.152.2), PE antimouse interferon (IFN)γ (clone: XMG1.2), PE antimouse FoxP3 (clone: MF-14), FITC antimouse CD80 (clone: 16-10 A1), PE antimouse CD62L (clone: MEL-14), FITC antimouse CD44 (clone: IM7), PE-Cy7 antimouse SiglecF (clone: S17007L), PE antimouse CD300LD (clone: TX69), APC antimouse CXCR2 (clone: A044G4), PE antimouse Arg1 (clone: A1exF5), PE-Cy7 antimouse CD14 (clone: Sa14-2), PE antimouse Tim3 (clone: RMT3-23), FITC antimouse tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-α (clone:), PE anti-H-2Kb bound to SIINFEKL (MBL, cat. no. TS-5001-1C), PE antimouse iNOS (clone: CXNFT), APC-Cy7 antimouse PD-1 (clone: 29F.1A12), and PE antimouse PD-L1 (clone: 10F.9G2) were used for flow cytometry. 7-Aminoactinomycin D (7-AAD, BioLegend) was used to distinguish live from dying or dead cells. Flow cytometry data were acquired on a BD LSR II flow cytometer and analyzed using FlowJo software.

## Intracellular cytokine staining
Cells were subjected to intracellular cytokine staining (ICS) with a staining buffer set (Invitrogen, cat. no. 00-5523) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. For FOXP3 staining, cells were labeled with anti-CD45-BV510, anti-CD3-APC, anti-CD4-APC-Cy7, and anti-CD8-Pacific Blue before membrane permeabilization and intracellularly labeled with anti-FOXP3-PE. For IFNγ and TNF-α staining, 5×105 cells were incubated with 1 µg/mL OVA257-264 peptide and 1 µg/mL brefeldin A for 6 hours before intracellular cytokine staining. Subsequently, cells were labeled with anti-CD45-BV510, anti-CD3-APC, anti-CD4-APC-Cy7, and anti-CD8-Pacific Blue before membrane permeabilization and later intracellularly labeled with anti-IFNγ-PE or anti-TNF-α-FITC. For Arg1 and iNOS staining, cells were labeled with anti-CD45-BV510, anti-CD11b-Pacific Blue, anti-Ly6C-FITC, anti-Ly6G-APC-Cy7, and anti-F4/80-PE-Cy7 before membrane permeabilization and later intracellularly labeled with anti-Arg1-PE or anti-iNOS-PE.

## Quantitative PCR
For gene expression analysis in tumor, tumor tissues were homogenized for RNA extraction. Total RNA was extracted using TRIzol (Invitrogen) and reverse transcribed into complementary DNA using SuperScript III Reverse Transcriptase (Invitrogen) according to the protocol provided. Reverse transcription-quantitative PCR (qPCR) was performed using SYBR Green PCR Master Mix (Roche) in 96-well or 384-well plates. Plates were read with Light Cycler 480 (Roche). The following primers were used for qPCR:
mCXCL1: TGAGCTGCGCTGTCAGTGCCT, AGAAGCCGACGTTCACCCAGA;
mCXCL2: GAGCTTGAGTGTGACGCCCCCAGG, GTTAGCCTTGCCTTTGTTCAGTATC;
mCXCL5: GCATTTCTGTTGCTGTTCACGCTG, CCTCCTTCTGGTTTTTCAGTTTAGC;
mCXCL8: CACCTCAAGAACATCCAGAGCT, CAAGCAGAACTGAACTACCATCG;
mS100A8: AAATCACCATGCCCTCTACAAG, CCCACTTTTATCACCATCGCAA;
mS100A9: ATACTCTAGGAAGGAAGGACACC, TCCATGATGTCATTTATGAGGGC;
GAPDH: GTCTACATGTTCCAGTATGACTCC, AGTGAGTTGTCATATTTCTCGTGGT.

## Construction and protein purification
The extracellular domains of mouse or human CD300ld were cloned and fused with hFc into pLVX-IRES-ZsGreen (Takara). The pLVX constructs were co-transfected with the psPAX2 and pMD2G plasmids (at a ratio of 4:3:1) into 293T cells using Lipofectamine 2000 (Invitrogen) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Lentiviruses were collected 72 hours after transfection to infect HEK293E cells, and stable cell lines were obtained by flow cytometry. hFc-tagged proteins in the cell supernatant were purified using a Protein A affinity column (SMART-Lifesciences, SA015C11) and then desalted into PBS using an AKTA purifier system.

## Immunofluorescence staining
Mice tumor tissues were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde for 2–3 hours, followed by incubation in 30% sucrose overnight at 4℃. The tissues were embedded in optimal cutting temperature compound (OCT) and kept at −80℃. Staining for the cytotoxic T cell marker CD8 (53–6.7, BioLegend, 1:50) and the PMN-MDSC marker Ly6G (1A8, BioLegend, 1:50) was done on frozen sections (10 µm thickness), followed by staining with secondary antibody (Abcam: donkey antirabbit IgG (H+L) (Alexa Fluor 568, 1:100), donkey antimouse IgG (H+L) (Alexa Fluor 488, 1:100), donkey antigoat IgG (H+L) (Alexa Fluor 647, 1:100)) in dark, humid chambers at room temperature. The slides were counterstained for cell nuclei with DAPI (2-(4-Amidinophenyl)-6-indolecarbamidine dihydrochloride). Fluorescent images were obtained with an Olympus FV1000 confocal laser-scanning microscope.

## Isolation of PMN-MDSCs
Single-cell suspensions were prepared from spleen and followed by RBC removal using RBC lysis buffer. PMN-MDSCs were then isolated by using EasySep Mouse Neutrophil Enrichment Kit (Stem cell, 19762), following the manufacturer’s instructions.

## T cell-suppression assay
2×105 PMN-MDSCs were plated in U-bottom 96-well plates in RPMI with 10% FBS and co-cultured with 2×105 splenocytes from healthy mice labeled with CellTrace™ Violet (CTV) in the presence of anti-CD3 antibody (1 µg/mL, Invitrogen, clone: 145-2C11). After 48 hours, proliferation was measured by using FACS Canto II Cell Analyzer (BD Biosciences). CTV-labeled splenocytes without anti-CD3 antibody stimulation were used as a control. Cells were cultured in complete RPMI-1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, 0.1 mM non-essential amino acids, 100 IU/mL penicillin, and 100 IU/mL streptomycin.

## In vitro T cell restimulation
Peripheral blood from mice with indicated treatments experienced RBC removal using RBC lysis buffer. The peripheral blood mononuclear cells were plated at 2×105 cells per well in 96-well plates in RPMI medium containing 10% FBS and 1 µg/mL OVA257-264, which were added for 48 hours. Then 1 µg/mL brefeldin A was added 6 hours before the cells were collected to detect IFNγ and TNF-α by ICS.

## RNA-sequencing
PMN-MDSCs in single-cell suspensions sorted from spleen by FACS Aria III cell sorter (BD Biosciences) with BD FACSDiva Software (V.9.0, BD Biosciences), and the tumor single-cell suspensions were both subjected to RNA-sequencing (Shanghai Biotechnology). For cell sorting, the single cells were labeled with anti-CD45, anti-CD11b, and anti-Ly6G. Postsorting, cells were counted and loaded onto the 10X device (10X Genomics). Samples were processed per the manufacturer’s protocol and sequenced on an Illumina NextSeq sequencer.
The quality of sequencing reads was evaluated using FastQC. Adaptor sequences and low-quality score bases were trimmed using Trimmomatic (V.0.36). Differential expression analysis was conducted using the ‘edgeR’ R package. The standards defined as differentially expressed genes were p value ≤0.05. Pathway analysis was performed using the software clusterProfiler (V.4.10.0), and the plot was generated using ggplot2 (V.3.4.4) in R. Pathways with p values ≤0.05 were considered significant.

## Detection of ROS
Intracellular ROS was detected by means of an oxidation-sensitive fluorescent probe (DCFH-DA). PMN-MDSCs were incubated with 10 µmol/L DCFH-DA at 37°C for 20 min according to the manufacturer’s instructions (Beyotime, Shanghai). DCFH-DA was deacetylated intracellularly by non-specific esterase, which was further oxidized by ROS to the fluorescent compound 2,7-dichlorofluorescein (DCF). DCF fluorescence was detected by flow cytometry.

## Statistical analysis
Statistical analysis was performed using Microsoft Excel and Prism V.8.0.1 (GraphPad). Data are expressed as mean±SEM. Data were analyzed by Student’s t-test or one-way analysis of variance and were considered statistically significant if p<0.05, unless otherwise indicated.

## Results

## Cancer-vaccine resistance in advanced tumors
We previously demonstrated that the STING-activating PC7A nanovaccine elicits potent antitumor responses in multiple tumor models. To determine the antitumor efficacy of cancer vaccine in advanced tumor stage, we vaccinated B16-OVA tumor-bearing mice with PC7A-OVA257-264 NP at day 9 post-tumor inoculation when the tumor size reaches ~80–100 mm3, followed by a booster shot 5 days later (Vax-D9) ( ). In parallel, vaccination at an early stage, from day 5 post-tumor inoculation, was set for comparison (Vax-D5); by this point, the tumors have attained ~50 mm3 in size and TME has also been established ( ). Consistent with our prior findings, Vax-D5 significantly inhibited tumor growth by ~90%, with 50% animals surviving to 50 days compared with control animals dying within 22 days. In contrast, Vax-D9 exhibited only a modest tumor suppression when compared with control treatment, and limited extension of mice survival was observed ( and ), indicating that tumors at late stage are resistant to vaccine treatment.
 TME was then analyzed. Although both vaccinations resulted in higher infiltration of total CD45+ immune cells (~twofold to 2.5-fold) and CD8+ T cells (~twofold to threefold) than control treatment, the CD8+ T cells in Vax-D9-treated tumors secreted significantly less IFNγ than those of Vax-D5 ( ), suggesting lower cytotoxic T-cell response induced by PC7A NP when applied at advanced tumor stage. Given the essential roles of multiple immune inhibitory cells to regulate T-cell response, we analyzed the frequency of myeloid cell subpopulations in TME, including the immune inhibitory PMN-MDSCs, tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), or monocytic myeloid-derived suppressor cells (M-MDSCs). No significant difference was observed in the frequency of TAMs and M-MDSCs among all three groups. However, Vax-D5 reduced PMN-MDSC tumor infiltration by ~3.1-folds relative to control ( ), with simultaneous decreases in the bone marrow, spleen, peripheral blood, and tumor-draining lymph nodes (tdLN) ( ). In comparison, Vax-D9 resulted in ~2.8-fold higher PMN-MDSC infiltration in tumor than control ( ), so did in the aforementioned tissues, although less pronounced than in tumors ( ). The reduced PMN-MDSC recruitment and increased CD8+ T-cell infiltration were further confirmed by immunofluorescence staining of these tumor tissues ( ). Consistently, PMN-MDSCs of the Vax-D9 group expressed the highest level of CXCR2, a receptor required for PMN-MDSC tumor trafficking ( ). And among the chemokines, including CXCL1, 2, 5, and 8, the expression of CXCL2, a chemokine for PMN-MDSC recruitment, showed a ~10-fold increase in Vax-D9-treated tumors compared with control tumors ( and ).
We also used the TC-1 cervical tumor model, in which PC7A-E7p NP potently inhibited tumor progression when vaccinated at the early established tumor. Similar to the B16-OVA model, PC7A-E7p vaccination at a later stage of tumor also resulted in a significantly increased PMN-MDSC infiltration and exhibited minimal effects on tumor growth ( ). Collectively, these data reveal that PMN-MDSC increase when established tumors develop resistance to the PC7A nanovaccine.

## Enhanced PMN-MDSC immunosuppression is associated with vaccine resistance
PMN-MDSCs represent a major immunosuppressive cell population in the TME, causing T cell dysfunction and alteration of T cell-mediated antitumor immunity. We then determined whether vaccination at the late tumor stage alters the immunosuppressive activity of PMN-MDSCs. Phenotypic immune inhibitory markers were first examined. As shown in , PD-L1 exhibited a ~3.3-fold higher expression in PMN-MDSCs from tumors of the Vax-D9 group compared with the control group. Similar upregulation was observed for SiglecF, a marker associated with protumor neutrophils, and CD14 that was recently identified as a surface marker to distinguish PMN-MDSCs from classical neutrophils. Notably, key immunosuppressive effectors, including Arg1, an enzyme required for PMN-MDSCs to suppress T cells by depleting extracellular L-arginine, iNOS, and ROS that causes T cell damage, were all significantly upregulated ( ), suggesting enhanced T-cell suppressive activity in the PMN-MDSCs of the Vax-D9 group. We have previously demonstrated CD300ld as a critical immune suppressor on PMN-MDSCs that drives their migration and T-cell suppressive function. Indeed, the expression of CD300ld and its downstream effectors, S100A8/A9, was significantly upregulated by ~1.8-fold and ~fivefold, respectively, in PMN-MDSCs from Vax-D9 mice when compared with the control mice ( ). Moreover, upregulation of these inhibitory markers was also observed in PMN-MDSCs that derived from either spleen or peripheral blood of these B16-OVA tumor-bearing mice ( ), and we obtained similar results in the TC-1 tumor model ( ).
 To directly assess their T-cell suppressive activities, we isolated the splenic PMN-MDSCs and co-cultured them with OT-1 splenocytes for stimulation with ovalbumin (OVA) peptide. As revealed in , PMN-MDSCs from control-treated tumor-bearing mice remarkably inhibited OVA peptide-induced T-cell proliferation, and this inhibitory effect was further significantly potentiated in PMN-MDSCs by Vax-D9 treatment. Taken together, these findings indicated that vaccination at the late tumor stage resulted in increased PMN-MDSC infiltration and elevation of their immune suppressive activities, which may promote the development of vaccine resistance in tumors.

## CD300ld deficiency reverses resistance of advanced tumors to cancer vaccine
CD300ld is specifically expressed in PMN-MDSCs and is critical for their functionalities. Notably, CD300ld-KO significantly reduced the recruitment of PMN-MDSCs into tumors and their capacity to suppress T cells. To determine the role of PMN-MDSC in mediating resistance to cancer vaccines in advanced tumors, we investigated whether CD300ld deletion would affect cancer vaccine resistance. CD300ld-KO mice were inoculated with B16-OVA tumor cells and administered two doses of PC7A-OVA vaccine starting from day 9 post-tumor inoculation. Vaccination showed minimal efficacy in WT tumor-bearing mice ( and ). By contrast, it significantly suppressed tumor growth and prolonged survival in CD300ld-KO mice, with 40% of the animals surviving longer than 45 days, whereas no unvaccinated controls exhibited such long-term survival ( ). Similar results were obtained in the TC-1 tumor model ( ), indicating that CD300ld-KO overcomes cancer vaccine resistance at the late tumor stage.
 Given the importance of CD300ld in PMN-MDSC migration and T-cell suppression, we subsequently analyzed tumor infiltration and T-cell suppressive activity of these cells. Flow cytometry results revealed that CD300ld-KO significantly reduced PMN-MDSC infiltration compared with WT, with or without vaccination; this observation was confirmed by immunofluorescence staining of the tumors ( ), indicating that CD300ld deletion blocks vaccine-induced PMN-MDSC infiltration. We then isolated PMN-MDSCs from these mice and assessed their suppressive activity by a coculture with OVA peptide-stimulated T cells. As shown in , vaccination increased the T-cell suppressive activity of PMN-MDSCs in WT mice, while PMN-MDSCs from CD300ld-KO mice showed significantly reduced suppressive capacity, even after vaccination ( ). To further determine the effects of CD300ld KO on PMN-MDSCs in the presence of cancer vaccine, we sorted PMN-MDSCs from the spleens of vaccinated CD300ld WT and KO mice for direct transcriptome comparison. Consistent with the essential roles of CD300ld in PMN-MDSC migration and immune suppression, CD300ld deficiency led to a marked alteration in pathways including migration, chemotaxis, and immune effector process ( ). In particular, compared with WT cells, CD300ld KO cells were more similar to classical neutrophils rather than immune suppressive PMN-MDSCs, and immunosuppressive genes associated with the activated CD14high signature and the intratumoral neutrophil signature were significantly downregulated in KO cells ( and ). Collectively, these results indicated that CD300ld-KO abrogated vaccine-induced PMN-MDSC recruitment and their T-cell suppression activity.
Next, we examined the TME according to the gating strategy shown in . In CD300ld-KO mice, vaccination promoted higher infiltration of CD45+ immune cells compared with all other groups ( ). The resulted TME exhibited the most pronounced antitumor phenotype among all groups, with the highest proportions of antitumor cell populations, including natural killer (NK), CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, and lowest proportion of tumor-promoting regulatory T cells (Tregs) ( and ). Additionally, the myeloid cell populations, including TAMs and dendritic cells (DCs), showed a more inflammatory profile. TAMs and DCs had ~twofold higher M1/M2 ratio and ~1.5-fold higher CD80 expression, respectively, than other groups, despite no significant difference in their frequencies ( ). Of note, CD8+ T cells in the vaccinated CD300ld-KO mice displayed enhanced activation when compared with other groups, as indicated by the significant upregulation of IFNγ and CD69 ( ).
Systemic immune responses in blood and tdLN were also evaluated. A high neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR) in the blood has been shown to be associated with poor prognosis and shorter survival in multiple cancers. In vaccine-treated CD300ld-KO mice, the NLR in blood decreased to ~10 ( ). Moreover, in the tdLN, CD300ld-KO plus vaccine treatment significantly promoted T-cell expansion and a bias toward CD8+ T cells, as well as enhanced T-cell activation ( ); further T-cell subtyping revealed a greater shift of both CD8+ and CD4+ T cells from naïve (CD62L+CD44−) to effector memory (CD62L−CD44+) and central memory (CD62L+CD44+) phenotypes ( ). Overall, these data indicate that KO of CD300ld enhances both local and systemic antitumor immune responses to cancer vaccines.
Collectively, these findings revealed that CD300ld deficiency abrogates the vaccine-induced PMN-MDSC infiltration and T-cell suppression and restores the antitumor efficacy of cancer vaccine in tumor late stage, highlighting the essential role of PMN-MDSCs in the establishment of vaccine resistance in advanced tumors.

## CD300ld blockade restores the antitumor efficacy of cancer vaccine in advanced tumors
To determine the therapeutic potential of CD300ld blockade in overcoming vaccine resistance at the late tumor stage, we used recombinant proteins of hFc-tagged CD300ld extracellular domain (LD-ECD) to competitively inhibit CD300ld, which we previously showed modulate PMN-MDSCs in a manner similar to CD300ld-KO. As shown in , the LD-ECD protein or the control hFc protein was administered intraperitoneally into B16-OVA tumor-bearing mice four times with a 2-day interval from day 9 post-tumor inoculation, at which time point, the mice were vaccinated with two dosages of PC7A-OVA by a 5-day interval. LD-ECD treatment reduced tumor growth by ~45% and extended mice survival by 9 days compared with hFc control. PC7A vaccine has little effect on tumor growth and mice survival in the presence of hFc treatment. In contrast, in the presence of LD-ECD, PC7A vaccine achieved significant tumor inhibition and remarkably extended survival, with 40% of mice surviving for >45 days ( and ). The LD-ECD-mediated restoration of the vaccine’s therapeutic efficacy was also observed in the TC-1 tumor model ( ).
 The TME was then evaluated. Flow cytometry analysis showed that combination therapy resulted in greater infiltration of CD45+ immune cells compared with other groups, accompanied by high proportions of antitumor cell populations, including NKs, CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, and a lower frequency of protumor PMN-MDSCs ( and ). To gain deeper insights, we collected tumors for transcriptome analysis. Consistently, among the immune-cell-type signature genes, LD-ECD plus vaccine enriched those for antitumor cell populations, including cytotoxic T cells, CD8+ T cells, T helper cells, NKs, and activated DCs, while reducing those for tumors, such as PMN-MDSCs and Tregs ( ). In addition, genes associated with immune activation, antigen presentation, and the innate and adaptive immune system were strongly stimulated by the combination therapy ( ). Furthermore, compared with vaccine alone, LD-ECD plus vaccine significantly enriched the pathways such as inflammation, antigen presentation, and IFN signaling while downregulating the STAT3 pathway ( and ), suggesting that the combination therapy remodels the TME into an antitumor state.
Similar to CD300ld KO, LD-ECD treatment efficiently diminished PMN-MDSC recruitment without inducing compensatory myelopoiesis regardless of vaccination ( and ). Notably, this treatment also significantly reduced PMN-MDSC proportions across peripheral tissues, including bone marrow, blood, spleen, and tdLN ( ). PMN-MDSCs from combination therapy exhibited reduced immunosuppressive activity, with significantly decreased ROS and iNOS levels ( ).
Consistently, the combination therapy significantly increased CD8+ T-cell tumor infiltration compared with monotherapies ( ), and promoted their transition from an exhausted to an activated status, as indicated by the marked upregulation of IFNγ and CD69 and downregulation of exhaustion markers, PD-1 and Tim3 ( and ). The induction of antigen-specific T cells was further assessed. Compared with the Vax-D9 alone, the combination therapy increased the frequency of OVA-specific CD8+ T cells by ~fivefold ( ). Moreover, T cells from the combination group produced significantly higher levels of IFNγ and TNF-α on restimulation with OVA peptide ( ), indicating that LD-ECD treatment restores tumor-specific T cell activation when combined with cancer vaccine.
Taken together, these data confirmed the central role of PMN-MDSC in establishing vaccine resistance in advanced tumors and suggest that targeting PMN-MDSCs via CD300ld blockade could offer a translatable, therapeutic strategy to improve the efficacy of cancer vaccines.

## Combined therapy of vaccines and human CD300ld blockade for advanced tumors
We next evaluated the translational potential of this combination therapy. Building on our prior findings that human CD300ld serves as a marker for PMN-MDSCs and correlates with poor clinical outcomes across multiple cancer types, this observation is further supported by data from patients with sarcoma showing significantly higher CD300ld expression at tumor sites compared with the paracancerous ( ). Additionally, we generated CD300ld humanized mice (huCD300ld) and demonstrated that human-CD300ld shows conserved tumor-promoting functions, providing a useful preclinical model to investigate whether inhibiting human-CD300ld by human CD300ld-ECD (huLD-ECD) could restore vaccine efficacy. HuCD300ld mice exhibited tumor growth rates comparable to WT mice. Similar to WT mice, in tumor-bearing huCD300ld mice, the PC7A vaccine significantly inhibited tumor growth when applied from day 5 post-tumor inoculation, but failed to do so from day 9, indicating the development of late-stage vaccine resistance ( ). To assess whether blocking huCD300ld could reverse this resistance, the huCD300ld mice were treated with huLD-ECD, PC7A vaccine, or their combination starting at day 9 post-B16-OVA tumor inoculation ( ). PC7A vaccine alone did not inhibit tumor growth, while huLD-ECD monotherapy reduced tumor growth by ~40%. Compared with either single therapy, the combination therapy potently inhibited tumor progression, with 40% of mice surviving over 40 days ( and ). Consistently, TME analysis revealed that huLD-ECD markedly increased the proportions of antitumor immune cell populations, including NKs, CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, while reducing the frequency of protumor PMN-MDSCs, regardless of vaccination status, and resulted in a significant increase in CD8+ T-cell activation when combined with the PC7A vaccine ( ). Thus, blockade of huCD300ld could also reverse the resistance of advanced tumors to PC7A vaccine.
 To further validate this approach, we also tested another Food and Drug Administration-approved poly I:C vaccine. Similarly, neither the PC7A vaccine nor the poly I:C vaccine alone inhibited tumor progression when administered at the late stage of tumor development ( ). However, when combined with huLD-ECD treatment, it restored the antitumor efficacy of poly I:C vaccine, leading to significant tumor inhibition, prolonged mice survival, as well as an optimized TME characterized by reduced PMN-MDSCs and enhanced CD4+ T cells, NK cells, as well as CD8+ T-cell infiltration and activation ( and ). Collectively, these results demonstrate that advanced tumors exhibit resistance to diverse cancer vaccine formulations, which can be overcome by hCD300ld blockade, offering a promising clinical strategy for late-stage cancer immunotherapy.

## Discussion
In advanced tumors, the immune-evasive TME contributes significantly to immunotherapy resistance. Our findings reveal that resistance to the PC7A vaccine is associated with elevated levels of PMN-MDSCs. These PMN-MDSCs exhibit superior immunosuppressive activity relative to those from unvaccinated controls, along with upregulated CD300ld-S100A8/A9 expression, thus impairing T-cell immunity. CD300ld blockade altered the TME, thereby restoring the vaccine outcomes in advanced tumors. Of note, human CD300LD blockade consistently favored PC7A vaccine in advanced tumor-bearing CD300ld-humanized mice and proved effective in combination with poly I:C vaccine therapy, highlighting CD300ld blockade as a promising broad strategy to counteract cancer-vaccine resistance in advanced-stage patients.
Although other immune cells may have a role in vaccine resistance, our findings highlight that among immune inhibitory subpopulations within TME, only PMN-MDSCs exhibit an increased frequency in tumors resistant to PC7A vaccine. This observation aligns with clinical and murine cancer models, where elevated PMN-MDSC levels have been associated with poor survival outcomes following vaccine therapy. Multiple studies have reported that PMN-MDSCs have negative effects on vaccine outcomes through their immunosuppressive function. PMN-MDSCs upregulate immunosuppressive factors to impair T cell responses. In our data, we observed a threefold to fourfold increase in the T-cell suppressors Arg1 and PD-L1, along with upregulated protumor markers CD14 and SiglecF on PMN-MDSCs in vaccine-resistant tumors. This confirms that heightened immunosuppressive activity is a hallmark of PMN-MDSC-driven vaccine resistance.
CD300ld has recently emerged as a key tumor-favored immunosuppressive receptor, which is specifically expressed in neutrophils and is upregulated on tumor development and promotes cancer progression. Consistent with its immunosuppressive role, we detected increased expression of CD300ld and its downstream effector during vaccine resistance development. Notably, genetic deletion or competitive blockade of CD300ld reversed the immunosuppressive function of PMN-MDSCs in advanced-stage vaccinated tumors, thus synergizing potently with vaccine therapy in both B16-OVA and TC-1 tumor models.
A handful of drugs targeting PMN-MDSCs have already entered clinical trials. While some therapeutic attempts aim to target PMN-MDSCs by neutralizing their downstream effectors, such as Arg1 (CB-1158), NO (L-NMMA), or ROS (Setanaxib), their clinical impact remains modest. This is largely due to the redundancy of immunosuppressive pathways in the TME. Blocking a single downstream mediator often triggers compensatory resistance, underscoring the need to target upstream regulators instead. In this context, CD300ld mediates immunosuppression through multiple inhibitory mechanisms, positioning it as a promising therapeutic target capable of simultaneously disrupting several of these redundant pathways. Another challenge with current approaches to reduce PMN-MDSC recruitment is compensatory myelopoiesis. For instance, Ly6G-targeted depletion has been reported to induce a ~threefold expansion of Mo-MDSCs with similarly robust immunosuppressive activity. Likewise, CXCR2 inhibition (CXCR2i) has been associated with an increase in TAMs, and the persistence of pre-existing, long-lived PMN-MDSCs within tumors may further contribute to sustained immunosuppression in this context. In contrast, CD300ld blockade suppressed PMN-MDSC infiltration and function without triggering myeloid expansion. Moreover, it synergized with vaccination even in advanced tumors, as shown in murine and humanized-CD300LD models. Thus, targeting CD300ld represents a superior strategy to overcome PMN-MDSC-mediated immunotherapy resistance.
This study is subject to several limitations. First, although vaccine resistance strongly correlates with PMN-MDSC infiltration across multiple platforms, whether such resistance in other models mechanistically depends on PMN-MDSCs remains to be elucidated. Second, while CD300ld blockade showed efficacy combined with several vaccines, testing it with additional clinically relevant vaccines (eg, GVAX) would enhance the generalizability of our findings. Third, despite consistency across our tested tumor types, evaluation in more cancer subtypes would be better. Nevertheless, PMN-MDSC accumulation in the context of cancer vaccine is associated with poorer clinical outcomes, supporting the therapeutic rationale for targeting CD300ld. However, further validation in primary human samples remains challenging, largely due to the very short lifespan of neutrophils (~24–48 hours) and inherent limitations of current humanized myeloid models. Therefore, additional studies in humanized systems are warranted.
In conclusion, our study reveals that increased infiltration and enhanced immunosuppression of PMN-MDSCs contribute significantly to cancer vaccine resistance. Functional PMN-MDSC alteration by CD300ld blockade, in combination with the vaccine, synergistically inhibits tumor progression and doubles survival time. Importantly, the efficacy is conserved in humanized CD300LD blockade, indicating CD300ld blockade on PMN-MDSCs as a promising strategy to overcome vaccine resistance in advanced-stage patients.
