## Background
Zinc finger proteins (ZFPs) represent the largest and most structurally diverse family of transcription factors in the human genome. They function through characteristic zinc finger domains that enable specific binding to DNA, RNA, and proteins, playing a central regulatory role in the tumor immune microenvironment.

## Main body
This review systematically examines the dual functions of ZFPs in dynamically regulating both innate and adaptive immune responses in cancer. At the innate immunity level, ZFPs precisely control dendritic cell (DC) fate determination, dictate macrophage polarization, balance natural killer (NK) cell activation, mediate myeloid-derived suppressor cell (MDSC) immunosuppressive function, and modulate innate immune sensors and inflammasomes. Within adaptive immunity, ZFPs critically influence T cell effector function and regulate B cell differentiation. Building on these, diverse immunotherapeutic strategies targeting ZFPs are now emerging. These include gene-editing, small molecules and proteolysis-targeting chimeras (PROTACs), synergistic combinations with immune checkpoint blockade, and ZFP-engineered chimeric antigen receptor T (CAR-T) cells.

## Conclusions
As pivotal nodes within the tumor immune regulatory network, ZFP-targeting strategies offer novel opportunities to overcome current therapeutic bottlenecks.

## Introduction
Zinc finger proteins (ZFPs) constitute the largest and structurally most diverse family of transcription factors in the human genome, characterized by containing one or more zinc finger domains composed of cysteine and histidine residues [ ]. Their defining feature is the ability to bind DNA and regulate gene transcription, typically acting as suppressors or activators of lineage-specific and context-dependent transcriptional programs [ ]. ZFPs also interact with RNA, proteins, and lipids, participating in ubiquitin-mediated protein degradation, protein folding and assembly, signal transduction, DNA repair, cell migration, and diverse cellular processes [ , ].
ZFPs are classified into nine types based on sequence and structure, including C2H2, C4, C6, C8, C2HC5, C3HC4, C2HC, C3H, and C4HC3 [ ]. Furthermore, zinc finger motifs are categorized into eight classes based on backbone conformation and secondary structure surrounding the zinc-binding sites, which are C2H2-like, Zn2/Cys6, Treble clef, Zinc ribbon, Gag knuckle, TAZ2 domain-like, Zinc binding loops, and Metallothionein [ ]. The most prevalent subtype is the C2H2 zinc finger, where two cysteines and two histidines coordinate a zinc ion to stabilize a ββα fold that interacts with specific DNA sequences, typically located at the C-terminus of ZFPs [ , ]. Beyond these core motifs, ZFP functional diversity also stems from additional modular domains and combinatorial arrangements enabling context-dependent regulation. Major subgroups include Krüppel-associated box (KRAB) domain-containing zinc finger proteins (KZFPs), Broad complex, Tramtrack, Bric à brac (BTB) or poxvirus and zinc finger (POZ) domain ZFPs, and SCAN domain ZFPs. These domains exhibit functional diversity and mediate domain-specific protein-protein interactions, further expanding their regulatory scope [ ]. For instance, KZFPs, the largest human subfamily of C2H2 ZFPs, primarily function as transcriptional repressors by recruiting the tripartite motif containing 28 (TRIM28, also known as KAP1) corepressor complex and histone modifiers to silence endogenous retroviral elements (REs) and lineage-specific genes [ , ].
Although ZFPs have been extensively studied in developmental biology and tumorigenesis, and the immunologic roles of some important transcription factors like signal transducers and activators of transcription (STATs) and nuclear factor kappa B (NF-κB) have been well documented [ , ], a systematic review dedicated to the ZFPs in immunity is absent. Accordingly, this review aims to fill this gap by examining their emerging and unique roles in the tumor immune microenvironment, considering that ZFPs are uniquely compelling due to their three key characteristics. First, their unparalleled diversity and structural versatility allow them to control a broad spectrum of immune cell functions [ ]. Second, they possess a remarkable and clinically validated “druggability” that largely distinguishes them from other transcription factor classes [ ]. Finally, they often function as master regulators or terminal selectors that lock immune cells into specific functional states [ , ].
To this end, this review intends to summarize the dual functions of ZFPs in dynamically regulating the innate and adaptive immune responses in cancer. We first provide a comprehensive analysis of ZFPs as core regulators of innate immunity, detailing their specific functions and mechanisms in dendritic cells (DCs), macrophages, natural killer (NK) cells, myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), and innate immune sensors and inflammasomes. And subsequently we delve into their precise control over adaptive immune components, including T cells and B cells. Building on this mechanistic foundation, the review will critically assess the translational landscape, encompassing recent advances in gene-editing, small molecules and proteolysis-targeting chimeras (PROTACs), synergistic combinations with immune checkpoint blockade (ICB), and innovative synthetic biology approaches such as ZFP-engineered chimeric antigen receptor T (CAR-T) cells.
By integrating fundamental biological insights with therapeutic perspectives, this review aims to firmly establish ZFPs as a unique and pivotal class of immunoregulatory targets, thereby providing a comprehensive framework and strategic guidance for advancing the next generation of cancer immunotherapies.

## ZFPs as regulators of innate immune responses in the tumor microenvironment (TME)
The innate immune system plays a crucial role in tumor progression and immune surveillance through antigen-presenting cells (APCs), tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), NK cells, and MDSCs. Recent studies have begun to delineate the repertoire of ZFPs that influence the phenotype and function of innate immune cells within the TME, thereby impacting anti-tumor immunity and immune evasion [ ].

## ZFPs regulate DC differentiation and function
DCs, central antigen-presenting cells in the immune system [ ], are primarily classified into conventional dendritic cells (cDCs) focused on antigen presentation and T cell activation, while plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDCs) specialized in antiviral responses [ – ]. Notably, ZFPs, as important transcriptional regulators, profoundly influence DC maturation processes and antigen-presenting capacity. For example, members of the Ikaros family zinc finger (IKZF) family possess two Krüppel-like zinc finger domains [ ]. Heterozygous mutation of IKZF1 (also known as IKAROS) reduces the number of pDCs while increasing the number of conventional type 1 dendritic cells (cDC1s) [ ]. Similarly, DCs expressing CCCTC-binding factor (CTCF) in vivo exhibit an immature phenotype, leading to an increased pDC proportion [ ]. Conversely, zinc finger E-box binding homeobox (ZEB) 2 promotes expansion of both pDCs and conventional type 2 dendritic cells (cDC2s) by repressing inhibitor of DNA binding 2 (ID2), thereby reducing cDC1 populations [ ]. And Krüppel-like factor (KLF) 4 also facilitates the development of cDC2s [ , ]. This indicates that IKZF1, CTCF, ZEB2, and KLF4 precisely coordinate hematopoietic stem cell differentiation to mature DCs through a complex transcriptional network, maintaining DC subset homeostasis.
Conversely, zinc finger and BTB domain containing (ZBTB) 46, a key signature transcriptional repressor for DCs, directly impairs DC function when deficient. Specifically, myeloid progenitors lacking ZBTB46 upregulate CCAAT enhancer binding protein beta (CEBPB) expression, diverting the DC differentiation program towards an immunosuppressive myeloid output [ ]. This aberrant shift not only weakens anti-tumor immune responses but also promotes tumor angiogenesis, accelerating tumor progression [ ]. Functionally, ZBTB46 consolidates cDC identity by restricting responses to non-cDC growth factors, making it a crucial marker for identifying rare cDC progenitors and maintaining their essential antigen-presenting function [ , ]. Furthermore, deletion of zinc finger protein (ZNF) 587 or ZNF417 activates the cyclic GMP-AMP synthase-stimulator of interferon genes (cGAS-STING)-mediated inflammatory pathway by triggering heterochromatin redistribution and replication stress [ , ]. The consequences are twofold. On the one hand, it promotes tumor cell human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-I upregulation and neoantigen presentation. On the other hand, it concurrently enhances macrophage immunosuppressive activity and significantly impairs DC anti-tumor function. Since this detrimental effect on DC function predominates, ultimately facilitating immune evasion [ ].
Collectively, ZFPs play a vital role in the tumor immune microenvironment by directly regulating DC differentiation fate, profoundly affecting their core antigen-presenting function, and in specific contexts, mediating complex reactions leading to immunosuppression and escape.

## ZFPs regulate macrophage polarization and TAM reprogramming
Macrophages, as highly plastic immune cells, play central roles in development, homeostasis maintenance, tissue repair, and immune responses, exhibiting significant functional diversity [ ]. The classical M1/M2 classification framework distinguishes pro-inflammatory macrophages (M1) with anti-tumor activity from anti-inflammatory macrophages (M2) supporting tumor development [ ]. In the TME, TAMs are typically polarized towards an immunosuppressive M2-like phenotype, acting as key drivers of tumor progression by promoting angiogenesis, metastasis, and T cell function suppression [ – ]. Recent studies indicate that ZFP family members profoundly influence the TME immune landscape and tumor evolution by precisely regulating macrophage polarization state and functional differentiation, acting as key transcriptional regulatory switches.
ZFPs function as molecular switches governing macrophage polarization through transcriptional regulation. Prototypically, KLF4 cooperates with STAT6 to transcriptionally activate M2-associated genes (arginase 1 (ARG1), mannose receptor C-type 1 (MRC1), resistin like alpha (RETNLA), and chitinase 3 like 1 (CHI3L1)) while repressing M1 markers (inducible Nitric Oxide Synthase (iNOS), tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-α, C-C motif chemokine ligand (CCL) 2, and CCL5) [ ], thereby enforcing a pro-tumorigenic M2 phenotype [ ]. Conversely, zinc fingers and homeoboxes (ZHX) 2 maintains polarization homeostasis via NF-κB p65-mediated interferon regulatory factor (IRF) 1 activation. Its deficiency promotes interleukin (IL)-4 and TME-driven M2 polarization and correlates with reduced hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) patient survival [ ]. Similarly, ZBTB17 (also known as MIZ1) suppresses NF-κB transcriptional activity by competing with p65 for binding to the oncoprotein metadherin (MTDH) and by inhibiting IkappaB kinase (IKK)-mediated MTDH phosphorylation [ , ]. ZBTB17 knockout drives macrophages toward a pro-inflammatory M1 state, significantly suppressing HCC progression [ ]. In non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K)/protein kinase B (PKB, also known as AKT)-mediated ZEB2 upregulation enhances colony stimulating factor (CSF)-1/transforming growth factor (TGF)-β1 secretion, compelling macrophage differentiation toward M2, whereas ZEB2 loss reprograms TAMs into immunostimulatory major histocompatibility complex (MHC) II+ M1 macrophages [ , ].
Beyond transcriptional control, ZFPs orchestrate TAM function through epigenetic and metabolic reprogramming. In triple negative breast cancer (TNBC), ZNF451 overexpression recruits the transcriptional repressor snail family transcriptional repressor 2 (SNAI2, also known as SLUG) to facilitate the acetyltransferase p300/CBP-associated factor (PCAF) binding at the CCL5 promoter. By enhancing the acetylation of SLUG and local chromatin, it selectively promotes the transcription of CCL5, thereby leading to the recruitment and activation of TAMs [ ]. Concurrently, ZEB1 amplifies glycolytic flux in breast cancer cells by directly transactivating rate-limiting enzymes (hexokinase 2 (HK2), phosphofructokinase platelet-type (PFKP), and pyruvate kinase M2 (PKM2)) and activating the PI3K/AKT/hypoxia-inducible factor-1 alpha (HIF-1α) axis [ ]. The resultant lactate accumulation activates the protein kinase A (PKA)/cAMP response element-binding protein (CREB) pathway within macrophages, consequently driving their M2 polarization [ – ]. Furthermore, zinc finger DHHC-type palmitoyltransferase (ZDHHC) 2 and zinc finger CCCH-type containing 12 C (ZC3H12C, also known as MCPIP3) modulate macrophage responsiveness by regulating inflammatory cytokines like TNF-α and IL-6 [ – ].
Critically, ZFPs establish self-reinforcing immunosuppressive circuits through tumor-TAM crosstalk. In ovarian cancer, M2-TAM-derived CCL18 induces tumor cell expression of ZEB1, which transcriptionally activates macrophage colony-stimulating factor (M-CSF) to further entrench TAM M2 polarization, creating a persistent CCL18-ZEB1-M-CSF-M2 feedback loop [ ]. In breast cancer, ZEB1 exerts multifaceted immunosuppression. It simultaneously upregulates C-X-C motif chemokine ligand (CXCL) 1 to activate C-X-C motif chemokine receptor (CXCR) 2-janus kinase (JAK)-STAT3 signaling in TAMs, driving paracrine M2 polarization. And it also promotes epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) to elevate programmed cell death ligand 1 (PD-L1) and cluster of differentiation (CD) 47, thereby reprogramming the local immune landscape [ , ].
From a mechanistic perspective, ZFPs profoundly influence macrophage polarization direction and functional state in the TME through transcriptional regulation, metabolic-epigenetic integration, and self-reinforcing tumor-TAM feedback loops. Deep dissection of ZFPs’ core role in TAM reprogramming provides new perspectives on tumor immunosuppressive microenvironment formation and reveals the significant potential of novel immunotherapies. However, it is worth noting that these ZFPs form a network with inherent functional redundancy. For example, ZEB1 and ZEB2 drive immunosuppressive polarization of M2 macrophages through different but interrelated pathways. If another provides compensatory signals, then intervening in only one may not be enough, highlighting a key challenge in therapeutic intervention.

## ZFPs regulate NK cell development, activation, and function
NK cells, core innate immune lymphocytes and “first-line defenders” of immune surveillance, independently recognize and eliminate tumor and virus-infected cells without prior antigen sensitization or antibody involvement [ ]. Recently, accumulating evidence indicates that the ZFP family plays essential roles in anti-tumor and anti-infection immunity by precisely regulating NK cell development, activation, and effector function.
ZFPs play critical and stage-specific roles in governing NK cell development and maturation. During early hematopoiesis, IKZF1 promotes hematopoietic stem cell differentiation towards lymphoid progenitors by regulating key genes like lymphoid enhancer-binding factor 1 (LEF1), perforin 1 (PRF1), natural cytotoxicity triggering receptor 3 (NCR3), and IRF8 [ , ], establishing the NK lineage developmental foundation. Loss of IKZF1 significantly reduces NK cell precursors [ , ]. Subsequently, in human NK cell differentiation, ZNF683 (also known as HOBIT) acts as a core factor specifically regulating the generation, proliferation, and terminal maturation of CD56−CD14− NK progenitors [ ]. Knockdown of ZNF683 severely impairs NK progenitor development and expansion [ ].
Functionally, ZFPs precisely regulate NK cell responsiveness by balancing activating and inhibitory signals. As a key transcriptional repressor, ZHX2 constrains excessive NK cell activation by limiting maturation and effector function. Conditional knockout of ZHX2 in NK cells triggers abnormal accumulation of mature NK cells and enhances cytotoxicity [ ]. Pathologically, early growth response 2 (EGR2) functions as a negative regulator. Under chronic stress or in the TME, EGR2 cooperates with diacylglycerol kinase alpha (DGKα) to enforce an inhibitory-dominant functional balance in NK cells by suppressing activating pathways [ , ]. ZNF683 is overexpressed in NK cells from multiple myeloma patients, where it directly binds and represses the SH2 domain containing 1B (SH2D1B, also known as EAT-2). This suppression downregulates EAT-2 expression, leading to impaired cytotoxicity and an exhausted phenotype characterized by elevated inhibitory receptors (e.g., lymphocyte-activation gene 3 (LAG-3)) [ ]. Rescue experiments confirmed ZNF683 knockout restores cytotoxicity and reverses exhaustion, revealing its mechanism in dampening NK reactivity via inhibition of activating signaling pathways [ ]. Furthermore, deficiency in ZBTB16 (also known as PLZF) disrupts NK cell activation through a “variegated” reduction in key signaling molecules—including high-affinity immunoglobulin epsilon receptor gamma chain (FcεRγ), spleen tyrosine kinase (SYK), and EAT-2. These molecules are downstream effectors of activating receptors natural killer group 2, member D (NKG2D) and signaling lymphocyte activation molecule family member 7 (SLAMF7) [ , ]. Their loss impairs NK cell activation and drives functional defects [ , ].
At the effector level, ZFPs directly orchestrate NK cell cytotoxicity and exhaustion transition. Knockout of ZFP335 significantly reduces expression of Janus kinases JAK1 and JAK3, consequently impairing NK cell proliferation, cytotoxic protein synthesis, degranulation capacity, and cytotoxicity against malignant cells [ ]. Clinically, this mechanistic dysregulation manifests in human tumors as functionally compromised NK states. In clear cell renal cell carcinoma (ccRCC), tumor-infiltrating NK cells exhibit a characteristic exhausted phenotype, characterized by high co-expression of tissue-resident markers (CD49a, CD103) and ZNF683 [ ], accompanied by significant downregulation of cytotoxic genes (PRF1, granzyme B (GZMB)) [ ]. This molecular reprogramming thereby diminishes intrinsic NK killing capacity, critically correlating with advanced tumor progression and reduced patient survival in clinical cohorts [ , ].
In summary, ZFPs govern the entire NK cell lifecycle—from development and differentiation to activation balance and effector function—through multi-tiered transcriptional control. They establish developmental foundations, fine-tune activation thresholds, and directly command cytotoxicity and exhaustion transitions. Functioning as molecular rheostats, ZFPs maintain homeostatic inhibition to prevent autoimmunity while dynamically coordinating anti-tumor responses upon challenge, thereby underscoring their pivotal role in NK biology and immunotherapy development.

## ZFPs regulate MDSC migration and function
MDSCs represent a heterogeneous population of myeloid cells originating from immature precursors in the bone marrow. While scarcely detectable in healthy individuals, they undergo marked expansion under pathological conditions (e.g., inflammation or chronic diseases) and acquire potent immunosuppressive functions [ ]. Although research remains limited, emerging evidence demonstrates that ZFPs sculpt the immunosuppressive properties of MDSCs through multidimensional mechanisms, thereby driving tumor immune evasion.
ZFPs are involved in the regulation of MDSC survival, recruitment, expansion, and function. Regarding MDSC survival, TNFAIP3 (TNF alpha induced protein 3, also known as A20), a ZFP harboring seven C2H2-type zinc finger domains. It suppresses MDSC apoptosis when overexpressed, whereas small interfering RNA targeting A20 (si-A20) activates the JNK pathway and elevates cleaved caspase-3 and caspase-8 levels, inducing apoptosis in TNF-α-stimulated MDSCs [ ]. Regarding expansion and recruitment, KLF4 sustains the bone marrow MDSC pool via CXCL5-mediated granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) production and drives their tumor recruitment through the CXCL5/CXCR2 axis, accelerating breast cancer progression [ ]. And ZEB1 also facilitates MDSC accumulation within the TME [ ]. Notably, some ZFPs can enhance MDSC function. ZNF93 is recruited by lncRNA OIP5 antisense RNA 1 (OIP5-AS1) to the serine/threonine kinase 24 (STK24) promoter, upregulating this kinase to activate the AKT pathway, which enhances MDSC migratory capacity while suppressing CD8+ T cell activity [ ]. Glioma-associated oncogene homolog (GLI) 1 transcriptionally activates the chemokine C-X3-C motif chemokine ligand 1 (CX3CL1), promoting expansion of polymorphonuclear MDSCs (PMN-MDSCs) and augmenting their T cell-suppressive function [ ]. Moreover, GLI2 upregulates wingless-related integration site (WNT) ligands and prostaglandin synthesis to facilitate recruitment, activation, and functional polarization of granulocytic MDSCs (G-MDSCs), concurrently impairing DC, T cell, and NK cell effector functions to mediate immunotherapy resistance [ ].
Thus, as pivotal transcriptional regulators and signaling hubs, ZFPs orchestrate an integrated “apoptosis resistance–bone marrow expansion–tumor homing–immunosuppression” regulatory network. Targeting these key ZFPs can disrupt both spatial localization and effector output of MDSCs, reversing the immune microenvironment.
Overall, ZFPs orchestrate fate decisions and functional polarization of innate immune cells (DCs, macrophages, NK cells, and MDSCs) (Fig.  ), serving as central regulatory hubs within the tumor innate immune microenvironment. These multifaceted roles reveal ZFPs as dual-function regulators, serving as potential initiators of anti-tumor immunity and master regulators of immune evasion, thereby offering novel therapeutic dimensions for targeting the tumor immune microenvironment.

 

## Mechanism of ZFPs in regulating innate immune sensors and inflammasomes
Innate immune sensors are crucial cytoplasmic pathogen and danger signal detection systems vital for immune defense. They recognize pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) and danger-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), recruit adaptor protein apoptosis-associated speck-like protein containing a CARD (ASC) and pro-caspase-1 via oligomerization, and assemble into active inflammasome complexes [ , ]. Activated inflammasomes cleave pro-caspase-1 to generate active caspase-1, promoting maturation and secretion of pro-IL-1β and pro-IL-18, while inducing pyroptosis via Gasdermin D cleavage [ , ]. Recent studies show ZFP family members regulate immune sensor activation and inflammasome formation through diverse mechanisms, profoundly influencing host immunity.
ZFPs regulate diverse innate immune sensors. ZBP1, the most extensively characterized member of the innate immune sensors, is an interferon (IFN)-inducible ZFP. It serves as a DNA or RNA sensor that recognizes Z-form nucleic acid via its Zα domain, thereby activating IFN production and inflammatory responses [ ]. Mechanistically, Z-DNA binding protein 1 (ZBP1) drives cell death via the receptor interacting serine/threonine kinase (RIPK) 1-RIPK3-caspase-8 axis and activates the NACHT, LRR and PYD domains-containing protein 3 (NLRP3) inflammasome [ – ]. It cooperates with sensors like absent in melanoma 2 (AIM2) and pyrin to induce PANoptosis—a distinct programmed cell death modality providing host protection during infections like herpesvirus [ ]. Beyond ZBP1, zinc finger CCHC-type containing 3 (ZCCHC3) acts as a potent positive regulator of toll-like receptor (TLR) 3 signaling. Upon poly(I: C) stimulation, ZCCHC3 overexpression potentiates transcription of antiviral genes (e.g., IFNs) [ ]. Its deficiency specifically ablates TLR3-mediated cytokine production without significantly impairing TLR4 responses [ ]. Conversely, ZNF593 functions as a cGAS negative regulator. Post-viral infection, it translocates from nucleus to cytoplasm, directly binds cGAS, and inhibits cGAS-DNA interaction, blocking cGAS activation and downstream type I IFN production. ZNF593 deletion significantly enhances IRF3 nuclear translocation and DNA virus-induced IFN responses [ ].
ZFPs balance inflammasome activation through a multifaceted regulatory network. Notably, ZFPs orchestrate NLRP3 inflammasome activation through spatiotemporal and post-translational coordination. Specifically, zinc finger NFX1-type containing 1 (ZNFX1) enforces spatiotemporal regulation. Under homeostatic conditions, it sequesters NLRP3 in the cytoplasm, thereby preventing Golgi apparatus accumulation and consequently suppressing inflammasome assembly. Upon activation, however, caspase-1-mediated cleavage of ZNFX1 drives NLRP3 aggregation in the trans-Golgi network (TGN), establishing a self-amplifying inflammatory loop [ , ]. Beyond direct complex modulation, ZFPs also govern inflammasomes via post-translational modifications. ZDHHC7 catalyzes NLRP3 palmitoylation at cysteine 126, a modification essential for NLRP3 trafficking to the TGN during homeostasis and its dynamic dispersion upon activation, ultimately licensing inflammasome activation [ ]. Counteracting this activation, ZDHHC12 constrains excessive responses via regulated palmitoylation, thereby maintaining immunological balance [ ]. Parallel to canonical pathways, ZFP91 drives non-canonical inflammasome activation by facilitating caspase-8 complex assembly, which directly induces mature IL-1β secretion. Simultaneously, ZFP91 potently amplifies inflammation through enhancing mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) signaling cascades (extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK), p38 MAPK, c-jun N-terminal kinase (JNK)) in macrophages [ , ], and synergistically promoting IL-1β processing and release [ , ].
In conclusion, ZFPs are key modulators of innate immunity, functioning both as direct sensors and as fine regulators of other sensors and inflammasomes (Fig.  ). This multi-layered control precisely modulates TME inflammation, prevents excessive pyroptosis, and helps sustain a tumor-favorable low-grade immunosuppressive state, offering new perspectives on immune regulation.

 

## ZFPs as regulators of adaptive immune responses in the TME
Adaptive immunity, which orchestrates precision-targeted strikes in tumor immunosurveillance, is primarily composed of T cells and B cells. T cells mediate cellular immunity, while B cells are responsible for humoral immunity [ ]. Accumulating evidence indicates that ZFPs critically govern the fate of T and B cells, acting as master regulators of the adaptive immune system.

## ZFPs regulate T cell development and function
As core effectors of adaptive immunity, T cells undergo tightly regulated development, activation, and functional differentiation governed by intricate transcriptional and post-transcriptional networks. Within these networks, ZFPs have emerged as central regulators, exerting control over critical nodes spanning from thymic selection to peripheral effector differentiation and exhaustion. Notably, recent research underscores that ZFPs also influence T cell function by orchestrating metabolic adaptation essential for T cell persistence and activity.

## ZFPs participate in thymic T cell development
The thymus serves as the primary site for T cell development, coordinating the transformation of hematopoietic progenitors into self-tolerant, antigen-responsive T lymphocytes. This complex process entails T cell receptor (TCR) gene rearrangement, positive and negative selection, and lineage commitment. All of these require precise spatiotemporal control of gene expression [ ]. Crucially, ZFPs emerge as vital transcriptional regulators that govern these developmental checkpoints by fine-tuning gene networks essential for thymopoiesis.
ZFPs facilitate thymic homing and early settlement. IKZF1 deletion causes thymic epithelial cell (TEC) developmental abnormalities. As TECs provide cytokines and signals for T cell precursor homing and settlement, IKZF1 deficiency may disrupt thymic integrity, impairing precursor homing [ ]. Furthermore, T cell precursor homing relies on the interaction between precursor surface receptors (e.g., P-selectin glycoprotein ligand-1 (PSGL-1), encoded by selectin P ligand (SELPLG)) and adhesion molecules on thymic vascular endothelium [ , ]. ZFP281 directly transactivates SELPLG, thereby facilitating efficient precursor homing into the thymus [ , ].
Multiple ZFPs act synergistically to orchestrate critical checkpoints in positive selection and lineage differentiation. B-cell CLL/lymphoma 11B (BCL11B) is crucial during positive selection. It maintains T lineage fidelity by suppressing ID2 to prevent innate-like conversion [ ] and integrates TCR signals to regulate the lineage-specifying factors ZBTB7B (also known as ThPOK) and runt-related transcription factor 3 (RUNX3), thereby promoting CD4+/CD8+ single-positive differentiation [ ]. Crucially, ZFPs govern the CD4+/CD8+ lineage bifurcation. For example, ZFP335 ensures developmental progression by enabling the double-negative (DN) to double-positive (DP) transition, with its deficiency causing DN3 accumulation and impaired differentiation [ ]. ZBTB19 (also known as PATZ1, MAZR) cooperates with RUNX3 to drive CD8+ lineage development and sustain ThPOK repression in CD8+ T cells [ , ]. And ThPOK itself acts as the master regulator for the CD4+ lineage, directly activating CD4+ genes and repressing CD8+ genes through the nucleosome remodeling and deacetylase (NuRD) complex-mediated chromatin remodeling [ , ]. ZFP281 and ZFP148 further promote CD4+ commitment by opening chromatin at loci like ThPOK [ ]. Conversely, twist family bHLH transcription factor 2 (TWIST2) disrupts CD4+ differentiation and enhances CD8+ commitment by recruiting RUNX3 to repress ThPOK transcriptionally [ ]. Beyond conventional lineages, PLZF is indispensable for invariant natural killer T (iNKT) cell thymic development [ , ], while human immunodeficiency virus enhancer binding protein 3 (HIVEP3) regulates post-settlement iNKT cell expansion and functional maturation [ ].
Finally, mature T cells egress from the thymus. KLF2 permits the emigration of mature T cells from the thymus and their recirculation within secondary lymphoid organs by binding to and activating the sphingosine-1-phosphate receptor 1 (S1PR1) promoter [ ].

## ZFPs regulate CD8+ T cell activation and effector function
Following cognate antigen recognition via MHC I, naïve CD8+ T cells undergo profound transcriptional and functional reprogramming to differentiate into cytotoxic effector cells, a critical transition for adaptive anti-tumor immunity [ ]. This rapid differentiation necessitates extensive, coordinated reorganization of the transcriptome. Emerging evidence indicates that ZFPs act as essential molecular orchestrators driving this complex transcriptional rewiring program.
Certain ZFPs maintain quiescence and suppress premature activation. BTB domain and CNC homolog 2 (BACH2), which contains a BTB/POZ domain, maintains the naïve state by suppressing effector and memory-associated genes. (e.g., C-C motif chemokine receptor (CCR) 4, suppression of tumorigenicity 2 (ST2), and PR/SET domain 1 (PRDM1, also known as BLIMP1) [ – ]. Its downregulation in naïve CD8+ T cells is essential for full effector differentiation [ ]. Other ZFPs provide rapid negative feedback post-activation. Early growth response 4 (EGR4), swiftly upregulated following TCR engagement, acts as an activation “brake”. Its loss leads to enhanced calcium signaling, persistent nuclear factor of activated T-cells (NFAT) activation, and aberrant proliferation, highlighting its role in preventing CD8+ T cell overactivation [ ]. Furthermore, cytokine gradients, such as IL-2, are pivotal in shaping effector and memory populations [ ]. RNA-binding protein ZFP36L1 acts as a TCR affinity sensor, mediating graded IL-2 responses and competitive selection, promoting the clonal expansion of high-affinity CD8+ T cells [ ].
Emerging evidence indicates that specific ZFP expression directly modulates CD8+ T cell anti-tumor activity. For instance, in colon cancer ZFP335 knockout increases the proportion of infiltrating CD8+ T cells and enhances the frequency of cells secreting IFN-γ, TNF-α, and GZMB [ ]. High ZNF683 expression correlates with a tissue-resident memory T cell (Trm) phenotype in colon cancer, enhancing anti-tumor immunity through the upregulation of TCR and IFN-γ signaling pathways [ , ]. However, in TNBC, the ZNF689-TRIM28 complex silences long interspersed element-1 (LINE-1) retrotransposons via H3K9me3. ZNF689 deficiency reactivates LINE-1, exacerbating genomic instability and intratumoral heterogeneity, which inhibits T cell activation and infiltration, conferring immunotherapy resistance [ ]. It is worth noting that tumor cell-expressed ZFPs also critically modulate CD8+ T cell infiltration and function within the TME. ZEB1 promotes immune evasion through distinct transcriptional mechanisms. In melanoma, it suppresses T cell-attracting chemokines (e.g., CXCL10, CCL4) [ ], reducing CD8+ T cell infiltration [ ]. And in lung cancer, ZEB1 transcriptionally suppresses miR-200, thereby alleviating miR-200-mediated inhibition of tumor cell PD-L1 expression. This PD-L1 upregulation drives CD8+ T cell immunosuppression and facilitates metastasis [ ]. In contrast, GATA binding protein (GATA) 4 exerts a protective, non-cell-autonomous effect by promoting pro-inflammatory secreted factors like CCL2, which enhance CD8+ T cell recruitment and consequently suppress lung cancer initiation and progression [ ].
Some ZFPs are associated with the formation of memory CD8+ T cells [ ]. KLF2 downregulation suppresses S1PR1 transcription, promoting Trm generation crucial for local defense [ ]. Similarly, ZNF683 and PRDM1 can inhibit the expression of CCR7, S1PR1, and T cell factor (TCF) 7, thereby facilitating the formation of Trm [ ].

## ZFPs participate in T cell immune dysregulation
Persistent antigen exposure within the TME drives T cells into a state of functional exhaustion, characterized by progressive loss of effector function, sustained expression of inhibitory receptors (e.g., programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1), T cell immunoglobulin and mucin-domain containing-3 (TIM-3), and LAG-3), and impaired proliferative capacity. This adaptive dysfunction not only impedes durable anti-tumor immunity but also limits the efficacy of checkpoint blockade therapies [ – ]. Accumulating evidence identifies ZFPs as dominant factors shaping this exhaustion program.
ZFPs directly regulate the transcriptional program of T cell exhaustion and are major regulators of the PD-1/PD-L1 axis across diverse cancers. In TNBC, ZNF652 acts as a PD-L1 transcriptional repressor. Its loss causes PD-L1 upregulation, reduced CD8+ T cell infiltration, T cell exhaustion, and immune escape [ ]. In melanoma, ZEB1 directly binds the PD-L1 promoter to enhance its transcription and membrane expression [ ]. Similarly, ZFP64 transcriptionally activates PD-1 and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte associated protein 4 (CTLA-4) expression via promoter binding in esophageal cancer [ ], while cancer-associated fibroblast (CAF)-derived exosomal ZNF250 transcriptionally activates PD-L1 expression, promoting HCC immune escape [ ]. Beyond direct promoter binding, PRDM1 enhances PD-L1 transcription in HCC by increasing ubiquitin specific peptidase (USP) 22 expression, reducing SFFV proviral integration 1 (SPI1, also known as PU.1) ubiquitination and degradation, causing infiltrating CD8+ T cell exhaustion [ ]. Hypoxia-induced ZNF207 exemplifies dual roles in HCC, promoting progression by inhibiting CD8+ T cell infiltration via the MAPK-CX3CL1 axis and directly inducing exhaustion through transcriptional upregulation of indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase 1 (IDO1) and kynurenine production [ ].
Notably, ZFPs can regulate different states of T cell exhaustion. According to the research finding, T cell exhaustion can be divided into four stages, namely precursor exhausted T cell (Tex pre), progenitor exhausted T cell (Tex pro), intermediate exhausted T cell (Tex int), and terminally exhausted T cell (Tex term) [ ]. ZBTB27 (also known as BCL6), upregulated by TGF-β-mothers against decapentaplegic homolog 2 (SMAD2) but downregulated by IL-2-STAT5 in CD8+ T cells, represses PRDM1, transcriptionally suppresses Tex term-related genes, and induces Tex pro-related genes, enhancing anti-PD-1 therapy efficacy [ ]. ZNF683 inhibits a terminal exhaustion state overlapping with features of anti-PD-1 responsive solid tumors in Richter syndrome by directly targeting key T cell genes (TCF7, CD69) [ – ], repairing cytotoxicity and activation pathways [ ].
Similarly, there are redundancy and compensation mechanisms existing in T cell exhaustion. The BCL6-PRDM1 axis constitutes a critical interaction loop where inhibiting one factor may simply shift the balance rather than reverse exhaustion. Awareness of these interactions is crucial for designing effective combination therapies.

## ZFPs participate in tumor metabolic reprogramming of T cells
Unlike homeostatic conditions, the TME exhibits severe nutrient deprivation, hypoxia, and elevated immunosuppressive metabolites [ ]. These hostile conditions drive profound metabolic dysregulation in tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), disrupting core energy acquisition pathways (e.g., glycolysis, oxidative phosphorylation, and fatty acid oxidation) and biosynthetic balance. This metabolic dysfunction impairs T cell energy supply, proliferative capacity, and effector function, while promoting exhaustion [ , ]. Critically, aberrant expression or activity of ZFPs is now recognized as actively reprogramming T cell metabolic pathways, thereby impacting anti-tumor immunity.
For instance, ZFP91 disrupts TIL metabolism and anti-tumor activity. In colorectal cancer, ZFP91 deletion enhances T cell proliferation and function. Mechanistically, ZFP91 undergoes TCR-dependent cytoplasmic translocation, promoting protein phosphatase 2 A (PP2A) complex assembly, limiting mammalian target of rapamycin complex 1(mTORC1)-mediated metabolic reprogramming, particularly glycolysis [ ]. Similarly, chromobox 4 (CBX4) is induced in tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells and suppresses their function by regulating glucose metabolism. CBX4 increases aldolase, fructose-bisphosphate B (ALDOB) expression via interactions with specificity protein 1 (SP1) and KLF3. ALDOB suppresses T cell glycolysis and ATP synthesis by reducing AKT phosphorylation, impairing CD8+ T cell function [ ]. And the BTB-ZF family transcriptional repressor ZBTB20 negatively regulates the mitochondrial respiratory and glycolytic metabolic capacity and memory differentiation of CD8+ T cells [ ]. Conversely, the metabolic pathways also affect the expression of ZFPs, thereby influencing T cells. Sex comb on midleg like 4 (SCML4) recruits the histone acetyltransferase binding to ORC1 (HBO1)-bromodomain and PHD finger containing 2 (BRPF2)-inhibitor of growth 4 (ING4) complex to reprogram T cell-specific gene expression, enhancing the formation and maintenance of Trm as well as promoting the survival and effector function of TILs. Notably, this process is metabolically regulated, because fatty acid metabolism drives SCML4 expression via the mTOR-IRF4-PRDM1 signaling axis. This metabolically-induced epigenetic modification promotes Trm and TIL tissue residency and polyfunctional gene expression [ ].
Beyond the aforementioned metabolic pathways, pan-cancer bioinformatics analyses have begun to elucidate how ZFPs directly regulate ferroptosis. For instance, ZNF419 has been identified as a transcriptional repressor of ferroptosis in multiple cancer types, such as bladder urothelial carcinoma (BLCA) and liver hepatocellular carcinoma (LIHC). Mechanistically, ZNF419 may interact with fanconi anemia complementation group D2 (FANCD2), which modulates genes related to iron metabolism and lipid peroxidation, leading to increased iron storage and reduced lipid peroxidation [ ]. Additionally, the study indicates that ZNF419 is associated with the degree of T cell infiltration, but whether its regulation of T cells is linked to ferroptosis requires further investigation [ ].
Thus, ZFPs act as master regulators programming T cell fate and function. They regulate thymic development, balance the activation, effector, and memory states of CD8+ T cells, and drive T cell exhaustion along with metabolic reprogramming within the TME (Fig.  ). By profoundly shaping anti-tumor immune responses, ZFPs provide crucial molecular targets for enhancing the efficacy of T-cell therapies.

 

## ZFPs regulate B cell development and differentiation
B cells develop and mature in the bone marrow, giving rise to naïve B cells endowed with a highly diverse repertoire of B cell receptors (BCRs). Upon antigen-specific recognition via the BCR, B cells become activated, proliferate, and form germinal centers (GCs) within lymphoid follicles. Ultimately, these activated B cells differentiate into two key effector populations, including antibody-secreting plasma cells and long-lived memory B cells [ ]. Emerging evidence indicates that ZFPs act as central orchestrators of the B cell life cycle, determining the fate of B cells.
ZFPs are pivotal regulators of B cell development. IKZF1 governs early B cell development by regulating the expression of key genes, including integrin subunit alpha 5 (ITGA5), endonuclease domain containing 1 (ENDOD1), and receptor activity modifying protein 1 (RAMP1) [ ]. Loss of IKZF1 disrupts the differentiation of hematopoietic stem cells into B cells and is implicated in the pathogenesis of B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (B-ALL) [ ].
In later stages of B cell differentiation, ZFPs continue to exert essential functions. At the GC stage, BCL6 serves as a master regulator that maintains B cells in a proliferative and mutation-prone state by repressing genes involved in plasma cell differentiation (e.g., PRDM1) and the DNA damage response pathway (e.g., tumor protein p53 (TP53), checkpoint kinase 1 (CHEK1), and cyclin dependent kinase inhibitor 1 A (CDKN1A)) [ – ]. Dysregulation of BCL6 is associated with diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) [ ]. Concurrently, ZBTB17 can form a complex with myelocytomatosis viral oncogene homolog (MYC), inhibiting cyclins such as CDKN1A [ ], thereby co-regulating the expansion of GC B cells. Its deficiency leads to reduced plasma cell generation and an increased population of memory B cells [ ]. In contrast, PRDM1 serves as the master regulator of plasma cell differentiation [ ]. Its induction represses BCL6 and activates plasma cell genes such as IRF4, thereby driving the transition from B cells to plasma cells [ ]. Deficiency in PRDM1 expression is implicated in the development of DLBCL [ ]. Interestingly, BACH2 serves as a critical brake on plasma cell differentiation. It interacts with BCL6 to suppress PRDM1 transcription, thereby maintaining B cells in a non-plasma cell state and promoting the formation of memory B cells [ – ]. Meanwhile, ZFP318 supports the fitness of specific memory B cell subsets by maintaining mitochondrial health, thereby enhancing the secondary immune response [ ].
In summary, ZFPs form a sophisticated transcriptional network that precisely orchestrates B cell development, differentiation, and fate decisions—determining whether B cells mature into antibody-secreting plasma cells or long-lived memory B cells. Consequently, dysregulation of this network underlies various B cell pathologies, including lymphomas and autoimmune diseases, and highlights promising targets for future therapeutic intervention. ## Therapeutic potential: targeting ZFPs for cancer immunomodulation
ZFPs, as key regulators of immune cell differentiation, activation, and exhaustion, represent an underexplored class of immunomodulatory targets. Unlike traditional checkpoints or cytokines, ZFPs provide critical nodes for reprogramming immune responses transcriptionally or post-transcriptionally. Recent studies of their context-specific functions indicate therapeutic ZFP modulation can reshape the tumor immune landscape, enhancing anti-tumor immunity.

## Research progress on ZFPs as immune targets
Advances in genetic technology, mechanistic understanding, and clinical need drive rapid development of genome editing methods (clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) and CRISPR-associated protein (Cas) system, zinc-finger nucleases (ZFNs), transcription activator-like effector nucleases (TALENs), and meganucleases) [ – ]. Targeting ZFPs in immune cells via editing, small molecule inhibitors and activators, or RNA interference (RNAi) to regulate associated pathways is a key strategy to enhance tumor recognition and killing.
Targeting ZFPs improves CD8+ T cell anti-tumor response. The zinc finger transcription factor GATA3 drives a dysfunctional phenotype in tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells. CRISPR-Cas9 GATA3 knockout reverses dysfunction while preserving activation, significantly enhancing anti-tumor responses [ ]. Similarly, ZEB1 promotes CCL18 via M-CSF, fostering M2-type TAM polarization and CD8+ T cell apoptosis. Its knockout significantly reduces M2-type TAM accumulation and CD8+ T cell apoptosis, suppressing ovarian cancer growth [ ]. Consequently, targeting ZFP-related pathways is gaining significant attention. For instance, the USP7 inhibitor P5091 disrupts PRDM1 via ubiquitination, inhibiting fibrinogen like 1 (FGL1) expression, enhancing CD8+ T cell activity and suppressing liver cancer growth [ ]. Furthermore, methionine intervention activates IKZF1, promoting CD8+ T cell recruitment, and enhancing immune checkpoint therapy efficacy [ ].
Regarding innate immune cells, ZEB2 emerges as a master regulator of TAM properties, inducing immunosuppression while suppressing antigen presentation and type I IFN responses. Notably, ZEB2 knockout inhibits pro-tumor modules and restores anti-tumor macrophage activity, establishing it as a promising TAM reprogramming target [ ]. In multiple myeloma, ZNF683 overexpression in NK cells downregulates SH2D1B expression, impairing cytotoxicity and inducing exhaustion. CRISPR-mediated ZNF683 knockout reverses this, significantly improving NK killing, providing a basis for gene therapies [ ]. Distinctly, in MDSCs, treating tumor-bearing mice with si-A20 treatment can inhibit tumor growth. Compared to the control group, si-A20 treatment significantly reduces MDSC infiltration and improves T cell-mediated tumor-specific immunity, highlighting A20 as a potential target for anti-cancer therapy [ ].
The clinical translation of ZFP-targeting therapy mainly faces two major challenges, including cell specific delivery and safety control. The current research aims to enhance the targeting precision of nonviral vectors, particularly lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) [ ], by functionalizing their surfaces with antibodies or ligands. These target immune cell markers (such as CD8) and use cell specific promoters (such as the CD68 promoter of macrophages) to restrict therapeutic effects to target cells [ ]. Monitoring and mitigating off-target toxicity and immune-related adverse events (irAEs) is equally critical [ ]. Beyond unbiased detection methods like genome-wide, unbiased identification of DSBs enabled by sequencing (GUIDE-seq) [ ], epigenetic regulation strategies have shown unique advantages. For example, engineering CAR-T cells to express ZFP-KRAB fusion proteins enables reversible silencing of exhaustion related genes [ ]. This strategy not only enhances the persistence and anti-tumor efficacy of CAR-T cells, but also significantly reduces the risk of genetic toxicity and severe irAEs caused by permanent gene knockout.

## Small-molecule and PROTAC-based ZFP modulators
Directly targeting ZFPs is challenging due to perceived “undruggability”, highly conserved domains, and nuclear localization. However, protein degradation technologies, particularly PROTACs and molecular glues, offer promising strategies for selective ZFP degradation in immune cells [ ].
PROTACs are bifunctional molecules designed such that one end binds the target protein while the other recruits an E3 ubiquitin ligase. This facilitates the formation of a ternary complex, leading to ubiquitination and subsequent proteasomal degradation of the target protein [ , ]. The application of this technology is well exemplified by BCL6, a critical oncogene in DLBCL. The novel BCL6-targeting PROTAC DZ-837 effectively degrades BCL6, inhibits DLBCL cell proliferation. Importantly, in xenograft models, daily administration of DZ-837 significantly suppressed tumor growth. Furthermore, DZ-837 demonstrated synergy with the Bruton’s tyrosine kinase (BTK) inhibitor Ibrutinib, overcoming acquired resistance [ ].
Molecular glues, conversely, are small molecules that bind an E3 ligase, inducing a conformational change that enables the ligase to interact with and promote the ubiquitination and degradation of a target protein that it would not normally recognize [ , ]. Classic examples include the immunomodulatory drugs thalidomide and its derivatives (lenalidomide, pomalidomide), used to treat multiple myeloma and holding promise for other B-cell malignancies. Mechanistically, lenalidomide binds the Cereblon (CRBN)-Cullin-RING ligase 4 (CRL4) ubiquitin ligase, selectively mediating the ubiquitination and degradation of IKZF1 and IKZF3 (also known as AIOLOS), thereby exemplifying clinically successful molecular glue degraders [ , ]. Beyond these classics, novel CRBN modulators like CC-3060 and CC-647 effectively target the degradation of PLZF by binding distinct sites on its zinc finger domain [ ].
Significant attention is now focused on targeting IKZF2, a key factor for regulatory T cell stability and suppressive function within the TME, where it inhibits anti-tumor immunity [ , ]. Consequently, developing molecular glue degraders for IKZF2 is an active area of research, with several reported examples including ALV1/ALV2 [ ], DEG-35 [ ], and DKY709 [ ]. Promisingly, the fourth-generation IKZF2 degrader PVTX-405 specifically degrades IKZF2 without affecting other CRBN neo-substrates. PVTX-405 increases inflammatory IL-2 production, reduces regulatory T cell (Treg) suppression, and promotes effector T cell proliferation. Strikingly, in humanized CRBN mouse models bearing MC38 tumors, daily oral PVTX-405 monotherapy significantly delayed tumor growth. Moreover, combining PVTX-405 with either anti-PD-1 or anti-LAG-3 antibodies significantly improved survival compared to either antibody alone [ ].
Despite their potential, the advancement of PROTACs and molecular glues is complicated by the dual challenges of off-target degradation and theoretical functional redundancy. The hybridization of E3 ligases may lead to unexpected toxicity [ ]. And it is crucial to empirically determine whether the degradation of specific ZFPs will trigger compensatory upregulation of their homologs. Therefore, it is necessary to select E3 ligases more accurately and conduct more in-depth pharmacological studies in vivo to balance efficacy and safety.

## Combination strategies with ICB
Although immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) have revolutionized cancer treatment, their efficacy is frequently limited by resistance and low response rates [ – ]. Consequently, combining ZFP targeting with ICB represents a promising strategy to achieve synergy, overcome resistance, and improve patient outcomes, thereby overcoming key bottlenecks in immunotherapy.
Compelling evidence from multiple preclinical studies strongly supports this approach. For instance, in an HCC model, tumors derived from Hepa1-6 cells overexpressing PRDM1 were significantly smaller following combination with PD-1 antibody therapy compared to controls, indicating the therapeutic potential of combining PRDM1 targeting with PD-1 blockade [ ]. Similarly, tumors formed by ZNF207-knockdown Hepa1-6 cells also exhibited significantly reduced size after combination with anti-PD-1 treatment, further confirming the feasibility of targeting ZNF207 to enhance anti-PD-1 therapy [ ]. Increased BCL6 expression correlates with poor colorectal cancer prognosis and melanoma lymph node metastasis. Importantly, specific BCL6 knockout in Tregs not only impedes colon cancer growth and melanoma metastasis but also synergizes effectively with anti-PD-1 or anti-CTLA-4 therapy [ ]. In melanoma settings, high ZEB1 expression drives immune escape. Notably, ZEB1 knockout promotes CD8+ T cell infiltration and synergizes potently with anti-PD-1 antibodies to induce tumor regression [ ]. Furthermore, GATA4 suppresses lung cancer via CCL2-mediated CD8+ T cell recruitment. Inducing a GATA4-dependent secretory program significantly enhances anti-PD-1 efficacy, suppressing tumor growth in vivo [ ]. In glioblastoma (GBM), ZNF638 expression correlates with local immunosuppression. Critically, ZNF638 knockdown promotes CD8+ T cell infiltration, and its combination with anti-PD-L1 therapy reduces tumor volume and improves survival [ ]. Finally, addressing T cell metabolic dysfunction, CBX4 participates in suppressing CD8+ T cell glycolysis and effector function in tumors. Specific CBX4 knockout in CD8+ T cells improves the efficacy of anti-PD-1 therapy and suppresses melanoma growth [ ].
At present, there is a serious lack of safety data on ZFP modulators, especially when used in combination with ICIs. ZFP regulation may further disrupt immune homeostasis, thereby exacerbating irAEs caused by ICIs [ ]. For example, systemic disruption of IKZF2, aimed at reducing Treg mediated inhibition, may theoretically trigger severe autoimmune reactions similar to those caused by CTLA-4 inhibition [ ]. To address this risk, future preclinical studies must adopt systematic safety assessment strategies. These include comprehensive immune monitoring through detailed analysis of immune phenotypes in peripheral blood and healthy organs in immunocompetent models, as well as optimization of dosing regimens by exploring intermittent or low-dose combination regimens to balance the efficacy and the toxicity.

## Synthetic biology approaches and CAR-T engineering
CAR-T cell therapy has demonstrated significant success in hematologic malignancies, but its broader application faces substantial challenges, including poor persistence, high relapse rates, and low response in solid tumors [ – ]. Integrating ZFP-targeting strategies with CAR-T therapy represents a promising approach to synergistically enhance CAR-T cell potency, longevity, and tumor infiltration.
Emerging preclinical evidence strongly supports this combinatorial strategy. Notably, genome-wide CRISPR screening of CAR-T cells and patient-derived GBM stem cells identified IKZF2 as a key target. IKZF2 knockout significantly enhanced CAR-T cell anti-tumor efficacy, with edited cells exhibiting enhanced effector function and suppressed exhaustion signatures [ ]. Similarly, CRISPR-Cas9-mediated knockout of PRDM1 enhances chromatin accessibility at memory formation genes. This reprogramming helps maintain an early memory phenotype and polyfunctional cytokine secretion, promoting the expansion of less differentiated memory CAR-T cells in vivo, ultimately enhancing persistence and efficacy in tumor models [ ]. Building on this, dual knockout of PRDM1 and nuclear receptor subfamily 4 group A member 3 (NR4A3) effectively shifts CAR-T cells from an exhausted TIM-3+CD8+ state to a stem-like memory TCF1+CD8+ state, counteracting exhaustion and significantly improving anti-tumor responses [ ]. Alternatively, rather than knockout, inducing specific phenotypes shows promise. For example, overexpression of BCL6 in CD4+ CAR-T cells promotes their transdifferentiation into an induced T follicular helper-like state, which significantly enhances anti-tumor efficiency in a pancreatic cancer model [ ].
While knockout of ZFPs in CAR-T cells via gene editing is promising, its clinical translation is complex. Current reliance on ex vivo editing underscores the immaturity of safe, efficient in vivo editing tools [ ]. Further concerns include the inherent risks of off-target effects and the potential for long-term dysfunction in edited T cells [ ]. These safety uncertainties necessitate rigorous evaluation before clinical progression.
In summary, targeting ZFPs through four major strategies (gene editing, protein degradation, combination with ICB, and CAR-T engineering) has emerged as a multifaceted research paradigm for enhancing antitumor immune responses. ## Conclusion and perspectives
ZFPs are increasingly recognized as key regulators of tumor-immune interactions, playing multifaceted roles in innate and adaptive immunity. As transcription factors, mRNA-binding proteins, and epigenetic modifiers, ZFPs orchestrate immune cell development, activation, metabolic adaptation, and exhaustion. These functions are often exploited or dysregulated within the TME [ , , ].
Moving beyond generalized discussions on transcription factors, our focused analysis on the ZFP family reveals shared attributes that establish them as a distinct class of immunoregulatory targets, a premise built on three points. First, structurally, their modular DNA-binding zinc finger domains confer precise target gene specificity, a diversity that forms the foundation for their functional versatility [ ]. Second, functionally, they frequently operate as “terminal selectors” or “identity gatekeepers” (e.g., PRDM1 in plasma cell terminal differentiation [ ], ZEB2 in driving macrophage immunosuppressive polarization [ ]). This functional duality is evident in their direct roles in tumor cells. For instance, ZNF471 acts as a tumor suppressor in cervical cancer by upregulating Cadherin (CDH) 1 and downregulating CDH2 to arrest the cell cycle and inhibit EMT and WNT signaling, thereby suppressing growth and metastasis [ ]. Conversely, ZNF593 promotes breast cancer progression by facilitating cell cycle progression and DNA damage repair [ ]. This overarching capacity to lock cells into specific functional states makes ZFPs powerful levers for reprogramming the TME. Third, and most critically from a translational perspective, ZFPs have demonstrated a “druggability” that remains largely elusive for other transcription factor classes. The clinical success of molecular glue degraders targeting IKZF1/3 (e.g., lenalidomide) provides powerful evidence, challenging the historical notion of transcription factors as “undruggable” [ ].
These unique attributes have spurred the development of diverse therapeutic strategies. From genetic engineering (e.g., ZEB1 knockout in ovarian cancer [ ]) and chemical degraders (e.g., IKZF degraders in hematologic malignancies [ , ]) to combination with ICB (e.g., PRDM1 overexpression combined with anti-PD-1 therapy [ ]) and synthetic biology (e.g., IKZF2-engineered CAR-T cells [ ]), targeting ZFPs represents a vibrant frontier for next-generation immunotherapy.
Beyond preclinical validation, the translational potential of ZFPs is gaining increasing attention in clinical trials. While direct ZFP-targeting therapies in immuno-oncology are still evolving, clinical successes with drugs targeting zinc finger-containing proteins in other contexts provide strong precedents. Notably, poly (ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitors (e.g., olaparib) have become standard treatments for cancers with homologous recombination repair defects [ , ]. These agents effectively target the zinc finger domain-containing protein PARP1 to induce synthetic lethality [ ]. This model demonstrates the feasibility of utilizing small molecules to target zinc finger domains in clinical trials. Furthermore, evidence suggests that specific ZFPs are associated with treatment resistance, highlighting their significance in therapy. For instance, ZNF830 has been shown to promote DNA double-strand break repair and contribute to chemotherapy resistance in lung cancer [ ]. And ZNF32 has been implicated in protecting tumor cells from oxidative stress-induced death and fostering chemotherapy resistance in lung cancer [ ], making them potential targets for overcoming treatment resistance. In immunotherapy, PROTAC-based approaches have demonstrated clinical potential in degrading zinc finger transcription factors, as evidenced by the development of IKZF2 degraders (e.g., DKY709) in clinical-stage trials for solid tumors [ ]. These advances indicate a growing momentum in translating ZFP-targeting strategies into tangible clinical benefits.
It is worth noting that the clinical implementation strategy targeting ZFP is expected to be enhanced through biomarker guided patient stratification. Recent studies have identified several ZFPs as independent prognostic and immunological biomarkers. For example, high expression of ZNF514 and ZNF419 is associated with aggressive disease progression and poor prognosis in ccRCC, HCC, and lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) [ , ]. Crucially, the expression levels of these ZFPs can predict response to conventional therapies or resistance to immunotherapy, thereby helping to identify patients most likely to benefit from ZFP-directed interventions.
Despite promising preclinical data, successful clinical translation still requires addressing key challenges, including functional redundancy and context dependency, delivery precision, and pleiotropic toxicity [ – ]. The forward path must be organized around the core mission of systematically defining and therapeutically exploiting the ZFP immunomodulatory network. This can be achieved by first deploying single-cell and spatial multi-omics to create high-resolution atlas of ZFP networks in the TME. The atlas will then guide the prioritization of non-redundant targets through functional genomics. Meanwhile, structural biology must resolve key ZFP architectures to unravel ZFP-specific druggable pockets. All discoveries must be rigorously validated using physiologically relevant humanized models, ensuring that novel therapies are both effective and specific.
In conclusion, ZFPs constitute a promising reservoir of therapeutic targets. This review not only provides a unique conceptual framework for understanding immune cell fate but also promises new therapeutic avenues in cancer immunotherapy. Integrating genomics, proteomics, combination therapy, structural biology and synthetic biology, this field is preparing to utilize ZFP biology for clinical translation, ultimately aiming for more precise, durable, and broadly applicable immunotherapies.
