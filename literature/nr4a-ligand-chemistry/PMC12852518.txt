## Abstract

## Objective
Understanding the discordance between RNA and protein expression can help elucidate underlying disease mechanisms that cannot be fully explained using transcriptomic information alone. Unveiling novel mechanisms of tumorigenesis through multi-omics data using real-world data is central to advancing precision in oncology and tumor prevention. In this study, we investigated the expression discordance between non-tumor and tumor patient samples to elucidate the underlying mechanism of tumorigenesis in lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD).

## Methods
We performed a correlation analysis of RNA and protein using normal tissue adjacent to tumor and tumor samples to identify distinct expression profiles to unveil tumorigenesis. Publicly available multi-omics data, including RNA, proteins, phosphoproteins, and acetyl proteins, were obtained from the Clinical Proteomic Tumor Analysis Consortium. Gene expression analysis at the single-cell level was performed using single-cell RNA sequencing data. A transformer model was used for in silico drug screening.

## Results
Most genes in tumors showed a positive correlation between RNA and protein expression. DNA topoisomerase II alpha (TOP2A) and Cyclin-dependent kinase 1 showed a drastic correlation during tumorigenesis in patient with LUAD. TOP2A gene and protein expression were associated with overall survival in patients with LUAD. Single-cell RNA sequencing revealed that highly correlated genes were clustered as distinct subpopulations. TOP2A expression was associated with TP53 genomic status and miR-26A1 expression, which targets TOP2A gene. In silico screening identified novel drugs that could specifically target TOP2A.

## Conclusions
Our findings elucidated the multi-layer expression profiles of LUAD, revealing its tumorigenesis and potential clinical implications and interventions.

## Introduction
Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) accounts for approximately 85% of all lung cancer cases [ ]. Lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) is the most common NSCLC subtype, followed by squamous cell carcinoma (LUSC) [ ]. Previous studies have shown that at least 670 protein complex subunits of human protein-coding genes are differentially expressed among tissues [ ]. Proteogenomics provides a functional view of the gene and protein expression landscapes in cancer and can enhance our understanding of cancer biology. A draft map of the human proteome created by profiling 30 histologically normal samples became available in 2014, facilitating our understanding of proteogenomic-related disease development [ ].
The advantages of analyzing proteomics with other multi-omics data have been reported. For example, it has been used to study drug resistance and for proteomic analyses of biopsies of pretreated patients with triple-negative breast cancer, identifying key metabolic pathways and chemotherapeutic responses [ ]. Plasma proteomic profiles of 54,219 UK Biobank participants revealed protein quantitative trait locus mapping of 2,923 proteins identified from 14,287 primary genetic associations, highlighting genetic influences on ligand-receptor interactions [ ]. The Clinical Proteomic Tumor Analysis Consortium (CPTAC) gathered over 1,000 tumors from 10 cohorts to investigate tumors from a proteomic perspective and understand of the molecular basis of cancer in biomedical studies. A pan-cancer analysis identified seven immune subtypes in 10 cancers and revealed various DNA alterations associated with the immune subtypes that affected the proteome profile [ , ]. Another pan-cancer analysis of CPTAC data showed a genomic effect (cis effects) on RNA, protein, and phosphoprotein levels by comparing normal tissue adjacent to tumor (NAT), and tumor samples with genomic mutations [ ]. For specific tumor subtype analysis, integrated genomic and proteomic analyses of endometrial carcinoma have revealed perturbations in the p53 and Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathways [ ]. Proteogenomic analysis of LUAD using the Applied Proteogenomic OrganizationaL Learning and Outcomes (APOLLO) dataset for deep proteogenomic profiling identified various subtypes depending on smoking status, and revealed RNA and protein expression signatures associated with patient prognosis [ ]. Furthermore, we previously demonstrated the advantage of using proteomics data for patient stratification in LUAD [ ].
In this study, we aimed to reveal the molecular mechanism of tumorigenesis by comparing matched NAT and tumor patient samples and identify the molecular discriminants of outcomes, prognostic markers, and protein targets for cancer therapy by analyzing integrated proteogenomics using real-world multi-omics data from the CPTAC.

## Materials and methods

## Datasets
CPTAC pan-cancer proteogenomics data, processed using standardized data processing pipelines by the CPTAC pan-cancer working group, were used in this study. Briefly, proteome, phosphoproteome, and acetylome mass spectrometry data of LUAD were downloaded from the Proteomic Data Commons (PDC). The TMT-10 workflow protein relative quantitation of the unshared log ratio, which includes relative quantitative values from all peptides and uniquely mapped peptides, was used to compute relative protein abundances. The corresponding somatic mutations for mutational landscape analysis and transcriptome data that contained counts per million (CPM) and transcripts per million data for cluster and expression analyses were downloaded from the Genomic Data Commons (GDC) data commons. Clinical data related to LUAD, such as patient characteristics, were also downloaded from the PDC and GDC for downstream survival analyses. Primary LUAD and LUSC data were extracted and analyzed in this study; however, LUSC data were only used for correlation analysis to compare the LUAD results. To validate the prognostic value of the genes of interest, The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) LUAD dataset containing transcriptome, proteome, and clinical information was downloaded from the GDC. Reverse-phase protein array data, which contains protein expression data processed by The University of Texas, MD Anderson Cancer Center, were available in the TCGA LUAD dataset.

## Cluster analysis
Genes with a CPM of less than 1 that were not detected in at least two samples were excluded as low-expression genes [ , ]. Transcriptome, proteome, and phosphoproteome expression data were quantile normalized against samples and standardized against genes during data preprocessing to obtain the best clustering results, as previous paper have shown the usefulness of normalized expression values for downstream analyses [ ]. Ward’s method with Euclidean distance was used for sample-wise and omics-wise cluster analyses, whereas the average linkage method was used for DNA methylation clustering. DNA methylation data preprocessing and missing values above 5% were removed (probe- and sample-wise). This resulted in a complete dataset (i.e., no missing values) for cyclin-dependent kinase 1 (CDK1) and DNA topoisomerase II alpha (TOP2A) methylation analysis. Probes analyzed in the study for CDK1 were cg15172601, cg27457323, cg13954297, cg25228510, and cg25793692, while the probes for TOP2A were cg18998022, cg15769921, cg08817965, and cg27039612.

## Correlation analysis
Spearman’s rank correlation analysis was performed to determine the correlation for each gene [ , – ]. We performed a correlation analysis for both the NAT and tumor (LUAD and LUSC) datasets. The adjusted P values (false discovery rate) were obtained using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure for the correlation analysis.

## Network visualization
To visualize the network associated with TOP2A expression, the top 100 experimentally confirmed upregulated and downregulated genes were downloaded from the Humanaizome 3.0 data base [ ]. Overlapped genes from the 200 genes identified from the Humanizome data base and highly correlated genes identified in our study (ρ > 0.75 in LUAD) were extracted, and the gene network was visualized using Cytoscape software [ ].

## Identification of highly correlated kinase genes
Genes used in the correlation analysis were filtered using three criteria: (1) genes that had a low correlation in NAT samples (ρ < 0.35) were considered as low correlation in NAT; (2) genes that had a high correlation in LUAD samples (ρ > 0.75) were considered as high correlation in LUAD, and (3) extracted kinases remaining after filtering genes from (1) and (2), because kinases, such as oncogenic forms of receptor tyrosine kinases have been implicated in almost all human cancers [ ]. To obtain a list of all human kinases, we utilized the publicly available KinaseMD dataset for kinase extraction [ ]. The correlation coefficient > 0.75 was also used in other correlation analyses.

## Gene and protein expression analysis
Gene expression and protein expression analyses, including identified genes of interest such as CDK1 and TOP2A, mechanism-related genes such as RNA cap-related genes, ubiquitination-related genes, and SUMOylation-related genes were performed in R and plotted as box plots using the ggplot2 package. Log2-transformed TPM values were used for the RNA expression analysis, whereas normalized values were used for the protein expression analysis.

## Over-representation analysis
Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) and Gene Ontology (GO) analyses were performed using the interoperable gprofiler web server [ ]. Differentially expressed gene (DEG) analysis was performed using R package DESeq2. Volcano plots were visualized using the ggplot2 package.

## Patient survival analysis and Cox proportional hazard (Cox-PH) model
Kaplan–Meier analysis was performed using the survfit function in the R survival package. Kaplan–Meier estimates were drawn using the ggsurvplot function in the R survminer package. A multivariable Cox-PH model was computed using the survival package in R for CDK1 and TOP2A gene and protein expression subgroups.

## Mutational landscape
MAF files containing somatic mutations related to proteomic data were downloaded from the GDC. The Maftools package in R was used to visualize the mutational landscape.

## Single-cell RNA sequencing analysis
The GSE131907 dataset, which contained data from NAT and LUAD samples, was downloaded from the Gene Expression Omnibus database. To prove our concept of tumorigenesis in this study, we extracted patients with early stage (stage I) and no driver-mutated (EGFR-mutations negative) by referring to the accompanying clinical information. For comparison, we further analyzed patients across all stages. The scMAE model was used for single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) analysis [ ]. Genes with low expression (< 1 CPM) were removed from the dataset. We used 24,339 input genes for dimension reduction. The hyperparameters we used were a learning rate of 0.0001, 100 epochs, a batch size of 50, and the Adam optimizer for training. The Leiden parameters for the clustering were n_neighbors of 70 and a resolution of 1.0. Canonical marker expression was annotated to determine cell lineages based on previous studies [ , ]. The scanpy package was used for CPM normalization, cluster analysis, and the dot plots for expression visualization [ ]. The dot plot represents two values; dot size, which indicates the fraction or proportion of the cells expressing the genes; and the mean expression, which is visualized by color. The fraction of cells reflects the proportion of cells expressing the genes of interest in the group (cell types or clusters). For dot plot visualization, the CPM values were transformed using a logarithmic scale.

## In silico drug screening and docking study
Before performing drug screening using a machine learning (ML) model, the structure of TOP2A was analyzed to determine whether it had a potential drug-binding pocket in its structure. ML-based algorithms from the Prank web server were used to identify the possible ligand binding pockets in TOP2A. The input structure AF-P11388-F1-model_v4.pdb (UniProt ID: P11388) was obtained from the AlphaFold Structure Database [ , ]. After confirming the existence of potential binding pockets, a list of 1,615 U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA)- approved drugs was obtained from the ZINC20 database [ ] and used for in silico screening analysis. A transformer model was implemented and its performance was confirmed before conducting screening analysis because different virtual environments (CPU, GPU, node, and seed) may behave differently. We utilized a previously screened and validated small compound (221C7) as a positive control that binds to SPOP protein, resulted in a score of 0.716, indicating that the implementation succeeded in our environment. We then performed in silico screening to identify potential TOP2A-binding molecules for drug repositioning [ ]. Aspirin, DMSO, and DMF were used as negative controls. The identified small compounds were subjected to a docking study to validate their interaction with TOP2A. In the docking study, myPresto software [ ] with default settings was used and potential ligand binding pockets identified by Prank were investigated.

## Statistical analysis
The non-parametric Wilcoxon signed-rank test was used in R to compare two independent sample groups. For multiple comparison analysis, we performed the analysis of variance and Tukey’s test. Statistical significance was set at a p-values of less than 0.05. The Benjamini-Hochberg procedure was used to control the false discovery rate and FDR < 0.05 was set as the significance threshold.

## Results

## Characterization and expression profiling of non-tumor and tumor samples
To clarify the sample characteristics used in the study, we performed an integrated cluster analysis. This analysis revealed the distinct subtypes between NAT and LUAD (Fig. S1). Although previous studies have mainly examined the correlation between gene expression and proteins in patient tissue samples [ , , ], distinct expression profiles in tumorigenesis utilizing matched NAT and tumor samples remain to be elucidated. Therefore, we performed a correlation analysis between NAT and LUAD samples to examine their expression discordance. A positive correlation was observed between RNA and protein expression (Fig. A). To investigate whether the positive shift in correlation was LUAD-specific, we performed the same analysis using LUSC samples and obtained similar results (Fig. S2). This suggests that the shift is a common feature of NSCLC. The highest correlation for LUAD was ANLN (ρ = 0.963), followed by TOP2A (ρ = 0.953) and WDHD1 (ρ = 0.948) (Table S1). Moreover, TOP2A expression was drastically correlated with LUAD (Fig. B), suggesting that TOP2A may have a pivotal role in tumorigenesis. Another dataset of APOLLO showed a data similarity between CPTAC and APOLLO (ρ = 073 in [ ]). In our study, the correlation coefficient for TOP2A and WDHD1 in the APOLLO dataset was 0.883 and 0.870, respectively (Table S2), indicating that TOP2A and WDHD1 had high correlations compared with the mean correlation in APOLLO (ρ = 0.47).
 GO analysis was performed to elucidate the biological processes related to their expression dynamics. Seventy-nine genes showed a negative correlation in NAT but a highly positive correlation (ρ > 0.75) in LUAD, and the top GO biological process term was “Cell cycle” (GO0007049) for those genes (Fig.  C (subgroup E), Table S3). Most genes showed a positive correlation (subgroup I), and the most enriched GO term for these genes was “Organonitrogen compound metabolic process” (GO1901564). In contrast, genes that had a small positive or negative change, or no change in the correlation (subgroup H), were primarily related to “Organelle organization” (GO0006996).
To explore expression dynamics, differentially expressed gene (DEG) analysis was performed using top 10% genes (ρ ≥ 0.794 in LUAD), revealing that 196 genes were differentially expressed: 130 of these were upregulated (Log2 Fold Change (Log2FC) > 1) and 66 were downregulated (Log2FC < -1) (Fig.  D). Gene set enrichment analysis using DEG genes identified enrichment in the nuclear and mitotic nuclear divisions (Table S4). To examine whether they could form a subset of LUAD, a hierarchical cluster analysis was performed using the top 50 DEGs, enabling patient stratification between the NAT and LUAD samples (Fig.  E).

## Dynamic change in RNA and protein expression correlations in tumors
Next, to idefy the kinases that were associated with tumorigenesis, we extracted genes using the following criteria: (1) genes with a low correlation in NAT (Spearman’s correlation, ρ < 0.35), indicated by the blue line in Fig. A; (2) genes with a high correlation in tumors (we set ρ > 0.75); and (3) availability of all human kinases in the KinaseMD database [ ] because kinases, such as oncogenic forms of receptor tyrosine kinases, have been implicated in almost all human cancers [ ]. The analysis identified 29 genes including CDK1, CHEK2, EGFR, ERBB2, MAP family, MET, PAK1, and STK family genes (Table S5). Of the identified kinases, CDK1 showed the highest RNA and protein correlations in LUAD samples. Taken together, we identified TOP2A through a comprehensive analysis and CDK1 using a targeted approach. Of note, correlation analysis using CDK1 protein expression revealed the expression network, identifying the highest correlation between CDK1 and KIF11 (ρ = 0.889, False discover rate (FDR) < 0.0001) followed by TOP2A (ρ = 0.877, FDR < 0.0001) (Table S6).

## RNA and protein expression regulation in CDK1 and TOP2A
The expression dynamics of CDK1 and TOP2A were investigated to elucidate the mechanisms underlying their high correlation in tumors. We first analyzed the RNA and protein expression of CDK1 and TOP2A and identified their upregulation (Fig. A, B). We then hypothesized that mRNA stability may be attenuated to enhance protein synthesis; therefore, we focused on mRNA capping-related genes because cap formation affects mRNA stability [ , ]. As expected, the protein expression of cap formation-related genes was upregulated (Fig. C) and overall protein expression tended to be upregulated in LUAD (Fig. D). Regarding the specific expression regulation for CDK1 and TOP2A, RBM7 protein directly binds with the 3’-UTR of CDK1 to prolong its mRNA half-life [ ], and RBM7 protein expression was upregulated (Fig. E). A previous study reported that TOP2A expression was suppressed by miR-26 A [ ]. We found that miR-26A1, but not miR-26A2, was downregulated (Fig. F). Collectively, these results suggest that CDK1 mRNA is stabilized in LUAD, driving its upregulation and stimulating protein synthesis. In contrast, the upregulation of TOP2A mRNA was caused by the downregulation of miR-26A1, followed by the upregulation of protein expression.
 To examine the protein stability, the ubiquitin-proteasome protein degradation pathway was investigated. The results showed that HDAC3, EIF2AK2, and SQSTM1 were upregulated, whereas HDAC6 and TNFSF12 were downregulated in the CDK1 ubiquitin pathway (Fig. S3). For the TOP2A stability, CDH1 and CSNK2A1 were upregulated, whereas PTEN, APC, and GSK3β were downregulated compared with those in the NAT samples. Our analysis yielded controversial results, indicating that its role may be context-dependent. Therefore, further studies are required.

## CDK1 and TOP2A expression and DNA methylation
The gene and protein expression of CDK1 and TOP2A were upregulated in LUAD; however, there were no differences among the stages (Fig.  A-D). Additionally, CDK1 and TOP2A RNA and protein expression correlations among the stages remained consistent, as the scatter plot showed an even distribution of samples from each stage across the plot (Fig. S4A-F), indicating that they were independent of the tumor stage. These results suggest that CDK1 and TOP2A are associated with tumorigenesis but not with progression.
 Characterization of DNA methylation revealed epigenetic landscapes from normal and precancerous regions to invasive LUAD [ ], suggesting the existence of distinct DNA methylation profiles depending on the cellular status. Cluster analysis revealed a distinct methylation pattern with two highly methylated and seven poorly methylated sites in the CDK1 and TOP2A loci (Fig. E). Among the methylation sites analyzed, cg27039612 in the TOP2A gene was significantly decreased in LUAD samples compared with NAT samples, with no significant association with tumor stages (Fig. F). This suggests that DNA hypomethylation at cg27039612 is associated with TOP2A upregulation.

## Prognostic value of CDK1 and TOP2A
We analyzed patient outcomes at the transcriptomic and proteomic levels to investigate their prognostic value. Samples were dichotomized according to the expression levels of CDK1 and TOP2A for survival analyses. Notably, high expression of TOP2A RNA and protein resulted in poor survival, whereas CDK1 RNA, but not protein expression, exhibited prognostic value (Figs.  A, B and S5A, B). To validate our findings, TCGA gene expression data were used for survival analysis, which showed concordant results with the CPTAC cohort (Fig. S5C, D). CDK1 protein expression and phosphorylation of CDK1 at threonine 14 and tyrosine 15, which suppress CDK1 activity, were not clinically relevant (Fig. S5E-G). Multivariable Cox proportional hazard model was used to assess clinical covariate in both gene and protein expression because detailed health metrics are available in the CPTAC dataset. In the RNA-seq data, neither CDK1 nor TOP2A showed correlation with clinical features other than age. In the proteomic data, no correlations were detected, suggesting that CDK1 and TOP2A expression at both gene and protein levels is largely independent of conventional indicators (Fig. S6A-D).
 

## Genomic alterations and regulation of TOP2A expression
We believe that TOP2A is a valuable biomarker, with an advantage as a potential druggable target over CDK1 because (1) both RNA and protein expression are associated with overall survival, (2) TOP2A gene expression was higher than CDK1 and the difference in protein expression between NAT and LUAD samples was greater than that of CDK1, (3) TOP2A had the second-highest correlation value among the genes investigated in this study. To investigate whether other genes are potentially co-operatively involved in tumorigenesis with TOP2A, the top 100 genes identified in the TOP2A knockout (KO) study were examined [ ]. This yielded 39 genes that overlapped with the highly correlated genes we previously identified (ρ > 0.75 in LUAD) (Fig. S7A). Of these 39, AHNAK, CDK1, FEN1, KIF11, and MCM2 out of 39 genes were found in the top 50 DEGs shown in Fig. E. Except for AHNAK, the protein expression levels were highly positively correlated with each other (Fig. S7B). This suggests that TOP2A may orchestrate tumorigenesis by associating with other genes.
Mutational landscape analysis of TOP2A stratified by its protein expression revealed the accumulation of TP53 mutations in the high-risk subgroup, with a prevalence of 39% and 12% in the low-risk subgroup (Fig. C, D). To examine whether genomic alterations affected TOP2A expression, we analyzed TP53-, KRAS-, and EGFR-mutated subgroups. The TP53-mutated subgroup exhibited TOP2A upregulation and miR-26A1 downregulation. Neither the KRAS- nor the EGFR-mutated subgroups contributed to the expression of these genes (Fig. E-G). A previous study reported that doxorubicin-treated p53 promotes expression of miR-16 and miR-26A1, followed by the suppression of Chk1 and Wee1 [ ]. We found that Chk1 was suppressed in response to mR-26A1 expression and TP53 genomic status (Fig. H-J). Collectively, TP53 mutation played key roles in tumorigenesis by altering TOP2A and Chk1 expression through the downregulation of miR-26A1. To validate the abovementioned cascade, the TCGA dataset was used. TOP2A expression was upregulated in TP53-mutated LUAD, whereas miR-26A1 expression was decreased, suggesting that the TP53-miR-26A1-TOP2A axis is a common mechanism in patients with LUAD (Fig. S8A).
Protein expression of BID, CDK1, CDK4, CDK6 analyzed by immunoblotting was upregulated, whereas BAX and cytochrome c were suppressed in miR-26 A-KO HeLa cells [ ]. We examined these protein expressions and found that only CDK1 expression was significantly upregulated in the TP53-mutated subgroup, coinciding with low miR-26A1 expression (Fig. S8B, C). This suggests CDK1 protein expression might be involved in the TP53 and miR-26A1 axis in the CPTAC real-world data.

## Single-cell analysis for expression profiling
To examine TOP2A expression profiles at single-cell resolution, publicly available scRNA-seq data were used [ ]. We extracted NAT and LUAD samples that were not driver-mutated (EGFR mutation-negative) and were at an early stage (stage I) as a representative dataset for tumorigenesis and compared with patients with all stages (Materials and methods). We first analyzed total TOP2A expression to confirm whether the scRNA-seq dataset could mimic our study conditions. The scRNA-seq data are generally zero-inflated, with zeros as high as 90%. Therefore, we used a zero-inflated negative binomial model for statistical analysis to examine whether TOP2A expression was elevated [ ]. The expression in LUAD was higher than that in NAT (P < 2 × 10− 16), indicating that TOP2A was upregulated in samples from patients with early stage LUAD. Cells of eight distinct lineages were annotated by identifying canonical marker gene expression (Fig. S9A) [ , ]. This suggests that our scRNA-seq analysis enabled clustering of cell types or expression profiles, similar to those observed in previous studies. The transcriptomic data were clustered after dimensionality reduction and we identified 22 clusters that were assigned to B lymphocytes, endothelial cells, epithelial cells, fibroblasts, MAST cells, myeloid cells, NK cells, and T lymphocytes (Fig. A, B). To compare cell identities and gene expression profiles between NAT and LUAD samples, we extracted cells from NAT and LUAD separately and plotted them (Fig. C-F). Clusters 0, 1, 7, 9, 11, and 13 were enriched in NAT samples, whereas clusters 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 15, 16, 18, and 20 were enriched in the LUAD samples.
 The epithelial cells of cluster 15 were only expressed and cluster 4 were specifically enriched in early stage LUAD samples. We extracted 57 genes: (1) TOP2A; (2) 29 kinases listed in Table S5; (3) MKI67 which was highly co-expressed with CDK1 and is often used as a marker gene in scRNA-seq together with TOP2A to annotate proliferative cells [ , ]; and (4) 26 genes that were upregulated alongside TOP2A upregulation and are known cell cycle genes (genes highlighted in red in Fig. S9B). TOP2A, MKI67, and other kinases we identified by target approach were expressed in cluster 15. This cluster showed a higher fraction of cells and increased expression of MET, WNK1, PAK1, and ERBB2 compared with the other clusters (Fig. G). Cluster 4 exhibited lower expression overall but showed a trend similar to that of cluster 15 in LUAD; however, cluster 4 was also found in NAT, where it comprised fewer cells. Collectively, these findings indicate that clusters 15 and 4 in epithelial cells display proliferative characteristics, with cluster 15 representing a distinct population in the early stage LUAD. Moreover, TOP2A, MKI67, and CDK1were uniquely expressed in cluster 20 in LUAD, which consisted of immune cells (B lymphocytes).
To investigate whether gene expression profiles in each cluster in early stage LUAD were associated with tumorigenesis, we further analyzed patients across all stages (Fig.  H-N). TOP2A was expressed in clusters 8, 13, and 18 but not in NAT. Cluster 18 was observed only in the LUAD tissue samples, suggesting that this cluster may reflect a LUAD phenotype. Notably, all of the cell cycle-related gene expression were found in cluster 15 in early stage LUAD. However, these gene expression were enriched in cluster 20 in all stages and there was no obvious difference in expression compared with NAT (Fig.  N). Taken together, cluster 15 in early stage shows a proliferative phenotype, which might be a key role in tumorigenesis.

## SUMOylation and TOP2A
SUMOylated TOP2A interacts with the genome to reduce topological constraints, whereas the deSUMOylated enzyme, SENP3, removes the small ubiquitin-like modifier (SUMO), inducing genome instability. Multiple phosphorylation sites of SENP3 have been reported and P53 suppresses its phosphorylation, limiting its interaction with TOP2A [ – ]. Here, we utilized proteomic data and found that the expression of unphosphorylated SENP3 was elevated in the LUAD compared with NAT samples (Fig. A). In LUAD tissues, the expression of SENP3 was upregulated in the high-risk subgroup (Fig. B), whereas the phosphorylated SENP3 expression remained stable in the high- and low-risk subgroups as well as in the TP53 wild-type and mutated subgroups (Fig. C, D). These findings suggest an underlying mechanism, which SENP3 phosphorylation was intact regardless of TP53 genomic status and SENP3 expression upregulation may promote the detachment of TOP2A from the genome (Fig. E).
 

## Implication of possible TOP2A druggability using a machine learning model
One of the problems encountered in drug development is low target selectivity, particularly for TOP2A. Here, we utilized ML to screen for possible specific inhibitors from the U.S. FDA-approved drugs in the ZINC20 database (Materials and methods) [ ]. We implemented the TransformerCPI 2.0 model [ ], and the results are summarized in Table S7. Although most compounds had similar scores for TOP2A and TOP2B, which is consistent with previous findings, Azelastine (rank #14), Atazanavir (rank #15), Netupitant (rank #16), Naratriptan (rank #21), Prilocaine (rank #25), Salbutamol (rank #32), Carteolol (rank # 34) might preferentially interact with TOP2A compared with TOP2B. To examine these possible interactions, we performed a docking study against the potential ligand binding pockets predicted by machine learning (Material and methods) [ ]. We examined five predicted binding pockets and the docking study revealed that Netupitant was a strong predicted binder to the pocket 1 and 5, whereas Atazanavir and Netupitant were strong predicted binders to the pocket 4 based on the score distribution (Table S8). Nonetheless, additional experiments such as in vitro binding studies are required to further validate the screening results.

## Discussion
We identified that global mRNA expression and protein expression were upregulated in LUAD compared with NAT. It has been reported that global miRNA expression is downregulated across cancers [ , ], indicating that protein expression upregulation may be a common feature of cancer. CDK1 is a cyclin-dependent kinase that promotes cell cycle progression [ , ]. TOP2A is also specifically expressed in the S to G2 phases of the cell cycle [ ]. CDK1 and TOP2A proteins orchestrate many cellular events by regulating gene expression, cell division, and genome stability; therefore, the dysregulation of their expression is associated with cancer [ , ].
A previous study showed that TOP2A has a half-life of approximately 3 h in normal fibroblasts compared with that in transformed cells, suggesting that prolonged protein expression of TOP2A is related to cell disorders [ ]. Functionally, TOP2A resolves topological constraints in DNA and its functional abnormality induces genome instability; therefore, it is associated with oncogenicity because topological constraints can be formed through many cellular events, such as transcription, replication, and chromatin condensation [ ]. TOP2A correlates with metastasis and epithelial-mesenchymal transition in NSCLC [ ]. In glioma, a comparison of NAT and tumor samples from four patients showed that TOP2A upregulation in tumors and high TOP2A protein expression were associated with poor prognosis [ ]. Here, our findings indicate that TOP2 plays a pivotal role in tumorigenesis and can stratify patients with LUAD based on its expression. Furthermore, TP53 is associated with miR-26A1 expression and SENP3 phosphorylation.
Bioinformatics analysis by other groups identified TOP2A as a hub gene in LUAD [ ]. A weighted gene co-expression network analysis was performed for other cancer subtypes, identifying CDK1 and TOP2A in hepatocellular carcinoma and glioblastoma [ , ], and TOP2A in clear cell renal cell carcinoma [ ]. These results suggest that CDK1 and TOP2A may govern tumorigenesis in various cancers. The network between TP53 and miRNAs is well documented, with p53 playing a role in multiple steps of miRNA biosynthesis, including pri-miRNA processing, nuclear export, and pre-miRNA maturation. Additionally, p53 inhibits the function of the RNA-induced silencing complex. Consequently, TP53 loss or alterations may affect miRNA-dependent mRNA regulation [ ]. The TP53 – Rho-GTPase axis in epithelial cells is a potential therapeutic target for early stage LUAD [ ]. Our results showed that the frequency of TP53 mutations was higher in the high-risk subgroup and TP53 mutations suppressed miR-26A1 expression, resulting in TOP2A overexpression. In TP53-KO HCT116 cells, DNA topological constraints increased during the S phase, indicating the accumulation of TOP2-DNA complexes. Treatment with the TOP2 inhibitor doxorubicin induced cell death in TP53-KO cells, which specifically required TOP2A but not TOP2B. Furthermore, TP53 alterations have been associated with chemotherapy resistance. Specifically, p53-lacking cells are sensitive to DNA-damaging agents, expanding therapeutic opportunities for patients with TP53-mutated cancers [ , ].
The benefits of scRNA-seq data analysis have been previously demonstrated. For example, the profiling of tumor-specific total mRNA expression has enabled the prediction of disease progression in 15 cancer types [ ]. Metastasis is a hallmark of cancer; thus, the integrity of epithelial cells is of utmost importance [ , ]. We identified the expression of TOP2A, MKI67, CDK1, and other kinases and genes related to DNA damage signaling in the epithelial cell cluster. These findings increase the potential for targeted epithelial cell-specific drug delivery [ ]. Another scRNA-seq analysis using epithelial cells in LUAD identified malignant cells in KRT8+ alveolar intermediate cells with the KRASG12D mutation, which represent injured or mutated cells in normal-appearing lungs that have an increased likelihood of tumorigenesis in lung tumor cells [ ]. TOP2A play a role in NSLCL metastasis using A549 cells that harbor KRASG12S, with the KRASG12V and YAP1S127A mutations upregulating TOP2A expression [ , ]. KRASG12D promotes global suppression of miRNA activity through hypo-phosphorylation of Ago2, resulting in the post-transcriptional upregulation of miRNA targets in colon cancer [ ]. Although accumulated knowledge suggests a global association between KRAS alterations and TOP2A expression, we did not find an obvious contribution of KRAS to TOP2A expression. KRAS and EGFR mutations were not associated with TOP2A and miR-26A1 expression in the present study. Furthermore, KRAS mutations were detected at similar frequencies in the TOP2A-high and -low expression subgroups (20% and 17%, respectively). This suggests that tumorigenesis or cancer initiation and TOP2A upregulation likely depend primarily on TP53 abnormalities through miR26A1 downregulation; however, they have little association with KRAS and EGFR genomic status.
Osimertinib is a third-generation molecule-targeting drug (EGFR-tyrosine kinase inhibitor) for NSCLC. A recent study showed that the TOP2A inhibitors doxorubicin and etoposide showed a synergistically effect in suppressing cell survival by increasing DNA damage and apoptosis. This was observed in EGFR-mutated osimertinib-resistant cells, providing a scientific rationale for targeting TOP2A in patients with NSCLC and with osimertinib resistant [ ]. ENHERTU is an FDA-approved HER2-directed antibody and topoisomerase inhibitor conjugate used for the treatment of HER2 positive metastatic breast cancer, solid tumors, HER2-mutated advanced stomach cancer, and HER2-mutated metastatic lung cancer. To identify potential TOP2A poisons, we screened FDA-approved chemical compounds and performed ML-based virtual screening. Although we identified several compounds to provide evidence for drug repositioning for TOP2A treatment, in vitro and in vivo studies are required because in silico screening cannot confirm the actual binding and inhibition of the enzyme.

## Limitation
This study has a few limitations. First, patient sample collection was limited to patients who had undergone surgery, and the follow-up of patient tissue samples or a second dissection for further proteogenomic analysis was technically difficult. Second, not all the proteomic data were available because of missing values. Therefore, we removed some genes to avoid providing a misleading conclusions, thereby hampering a comprehensive analysis. Third, the drug screening methodology was performed only in silico; hence, further validation studies using in vitro and in vivo experiments are required to confirm their binding capabilities, binding affinities, and specificities. Moreover, these studies should determine whether they can bind to drug-binding pockets identified using the P2Rank ML model, thereby optimizing the structure-activity relationship at the target binding sites. Finally, the genomic alterations in LUAD differ according to ethnicity. In the TCGA LUAD database, mutations were observed in KRAS (32.2%), EGFR (11.3%), and BRAF (7.9%). However, the frequency of EGFR mutations is high (approximately 50%) in the East Asian LUAD population [ , – ]. This suggests that distinct Asian and other cohorts, such as those of European, African, and Latin descent, are required for precise proteogenomic analyses.

## Conclusion
In conclusion, our integrated multi-omics analysis using real-world data revealed the mechanisms of RNA and protein expression dynamics during tumorigenesis in LUAD, demonstrated the importance of TOP2A as a prognostic marker, and provided fundamental evidence for the development of possible therapeutic targets for LUAD.
