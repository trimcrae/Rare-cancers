In the CNS, DIM modulates the activation of the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) and inhibits its pro-inflammatory effects. Although capable of crossing the blood brain barrier, DIM's bioavailability is limited by its low solubility. Dispersed BR4044 provides a nanoscale high-solubility DIM suspension with the potential for treating traumatic brain injury (TBI). The present study aimed to determine whether BR4044 treatment could reduce pathology and improve behavioral recovery following moderate TBI. Male Sprague Dawley rats received moderate fluid percussion injury or sham surgery followed by vehicle or BR4044 treatment in the acute recovery period. TBI BR4044 animals showed significantly reduced cortical and hippocampal edema and lower levels of serum-derived extracellular vesicles compared to TBI Vehicle animals. BR4044 treatment of TBI animals preserved sensorimotor function and associative fear memory. Cortical contusion size and neuronal loss in the parietal cortex and CA3 region of the hippocampus were also significantly reduced with BR4044 treatment. BR4044 also decreased microbleeding and nuclear AhR at the contusion site. This translational study demonstrates that BR4044 ameliorates pathology and improves neurological outcomes following TBI by reducing brain edema, lowering acute extracellular vesicle release, modulating AhR, preserving cortical and hippocampal neurons, reducing red blood cell (RBC) extravasation into the injured brain, and promoting behavioral recovery.

## Introduction
Traumatic brain injury (TBI) is a major public health burden globally, with over 64 million estimated cases worldwide and 2.87 million cases in the US alone [ , ]. Moderate and severe TBI result in significant morbidity and mortality, with 44 % of survivors affected by lasting disability [ , ]. Intracranial lesions are prevalent in this patient population and contusions represent the most common non-operative finding in TBI, placing patients at increased risk of secondary injury and long-term sequelae [ , ]. Post-traumatic intracranial bleeding presents another common complication which occurs in 5–10 % of moderate and 25–35 % of severe TBI cases, contributing to neuroinflammation and injury progression [ ]. As a consequence of these pathologies, an estimated 3.17 million Americans are living with long-term disabilities due to TBI [ ]. Therefore, an urgent need exists for a therapeutic intervention treating TBI patients at risk of developing long-term deficits after moderate to severe injury.
3,3′-Diindolylmethane (DIM) is a naturally occurring bis-indole produced by the gut microbiome and found in cruciferous vegetables which has recently emerged as a neuroprotective agent in models of CNS injury [ , ]. Oral and parenteral DIM have demonstrated neuroprotective effects in pre-clinical models of middle cerebral artery occlusion, intra-cerebral hemorrhage, hypoxic ischemia, and lipopolysaccharide-induced neuroinflammation [ , , , ]. In ischemic stroke, DIM reduces behavioral deficits, promotes blood-brain barrier (BBB) integrity, lowers brain edema, and prevents histopathology abnormalities [ ]. Similarly, after hemorrhagic stroke, DIM improves motor function, increases neuron survival, attenuates microglial activation, and lowers neutrophil infiltration into hematomas [ ]. In the setting of hypoxic ischemia, DIM treatment reduces hippocampal cell death via aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) signaling [ ]. These improved outcomes in models of CNS injury suggest targeting the AhR via DIM is a promising avenue for the treatment of shared pathological features seen in TBI.
AhR is widely expressed in the CNS and indoles such as DIM, which are ligands of AhR, are now recognized as potential agents for treating neurovascular and neuroinflammatory diseases [ , ]. Expression and activation of AhR increases following cerebral ischemia and has been implicated in worsened histopathology and neurological outcomes [ , ]. Following ischemic stroke, inhibition of AhR reduces infarct volume and neurological impairments [ ]. Similarly, inhibition of AhR limits hematoma expansion, improves motor function, increases neuronal survival, and enhances BBB integrity in models of hemorrhagic stroke [ , ]. DIM's neuroprotective actions involve modulating the activation of AhR in the brain, thereby inhibiting AhR's pro-inflammatory and pro-apoptotic activity [ ]. DIM's regulation of AhR combined with its strong safety profile in multiple clinical trials point to its potential utility to target TBI pathology [ , , ].
As part of normal metabolism, endogenous and dietary DIM enters the circulation and crosses the BBB; however, synthetic DIM's bioavailability and dissolution in blood is limited by its low solubility [ , , , ]. As an active pharmaceutical ingredient in drug formulations, synthetic DIM is a fine crystalline material that is insoluble in water and only partially lipid soluble. In mice, oral crystalline DIM administered in oil suspension at a pharmacologic dose of 250 mg/kg reaches a concentration of ∼20 μM in the brain within 1 h of administration [ ]. For intravenous (IV) use at clinically relevant doses, the low water solubility of synthetic DIM requires the use of specialized lipid-based formulation technology. The BR4044 DIM premix was developed for dilution before IV use with spontaneous formation of vesicular nanoparticles providing DIM in a stable dispersion. The BR4044 premix formulation contains DIM dissolved in an ethanolic solution with co-dissolved nanovesicle forming excipients. Once diluted, DIM from BR4044 is in a nanoscale suspension forming 200 nm particles which increases its solubilization and supports a high DIM plasma to CNS concentration gradient. The IV administration of DIM through a therapeutic product like BR4044 has the potential to become an acute clinical TBI intervention.
In view of DIM's AhR-modulating activity in models of CNS injury, the present study aimed to investigate BR4044 as a pleiotropic intervention for moderate TBI. Recognizing that pathogenic extracellular vesicles (EVs) are immediately released following TBI, the effect of BR4044 on release of EVs within the first hours post-injury was investigated [ ]. EVs are extruded from cells activated and damaged by mechanical trauma such as TBI, and serve as damage-associated molecular patterns interacting with multiple surface receptors [ , ]. Brain-derived EVs pass through the disrupted BBB and initiate acute neuroinflammatory cascades systemically and within the CNS [ , ]. EV loading with inflammatory cargo and release into the brain parenchyma, cerebrospinal fluid, and systemic circulation provides a link to neutrophil activation, attraction, and migration into the CNS after TBI [ , ]. Modification and reduction of EV release has the potential to limit initial BBB disruption and diminish the severity of the secondary injury after TBI [ ]. This prior evidence provided motivation for evaluating possible inhibition of early EV release using BR4044.
Based on DIM's neuroprotective activity, rapid accumulation in brain tissue, lack of toxicity in pre-clinical safety testing, and excellent tolerability using a first-generation DIM formulation in clinical trials, BR4044 was investigated in the parasagittal fluid percussion injury (FPI) model of TBI. The present translational study in rats evaluated the efficacy of parenterally administered BR4044 to alter contusion development, modulate AhR activation, prevent red blood cell (RBC) extravasation at the contusion site, reduce neuronal loss, and improve neurological deficits from moderate TBI.

## Methods

## Animals
Adult 2 to 3 month-old male Sprague Dawley rats were obtained from Charles River Laboratories and maintained on a 12:12-h light:dark cycle with food and water ad libitum. Animals were singly housed within a temperature and humidity-controlled environment and acclimated for 4 days prior to experimentation. A total of 61 animals were included in this study. Experimenters remained blinded to treatment groups throughout the study. All experimental procedures were compliant with the National Research Council Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals and the American Association for Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care International standards. Experimental design adhered to the ARRIVE guidelines. All animal experimental procedures were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at the University of Miami.
A priori exclusion criteria were established for removal of animals from the study. Criteria for exclusion were: mortality, >15 % loss of body weight, autotomy, infection at the surgical site, inability to feed or drink, motor paralysis, listlessness, self-mutilation, excessive grooming causing loss of dermal layers, excessive spontaneous vocalization when touched, excessive activity, rapid respiratory rate, and poor grooming. Additionally, animals with paw placements <1 during the cylinder task were excluded. Four animals died post-injury from complications resulting from the TBI before drug or vehicle treatment, and an additional four animals were excluded based on the a priori exclusion criteria.

## BR4044 formulation and quality evaluation
The BR4044 premix formulation excipients consist of a mixture of ethanol, medium chain triglycerides, polyethylene glycol hydroxy-stearate, a blend of tri-block copolymers of polythene oxide and polypropylene oxide, and phosphatidyl choline. This specific excipient combination was developed to fully dissolve crystalline DIM. With dilution and mixing, BR4044 forms a stable, spontaneous nanoemulsion in aqueous media. Emulating future clinical use, BR4044 quality was evaluated following dilution in saline using a Brookhaven Instruments Dynamic Light Scattering apparatus. Results from triplicate samples showed an average effective diameter of 218 nm (SEM = 1.33) and average polydispersity index of 0.259 (SEM = 0.09).

## Surgical procedures
Per the ARRIVE guidelines, animals were randomized prior to the start of the experiment to receive either a parasagittal FPI or a sham surgery. Sham surgery animals had identical surgical procedures to the TBI animals including a craniotomy but without delivery of the fluid percussion pressure pulse. Anesthesia was induced with 3 % isoflurane, 70 % N2O, and 30 % O2, and maintained with 1 % isoflurane, 70 % N2O, and 30 % O2. A 4.8 mm craniotomy (−3.8 mm bregma, 2.5 mm lateral) was prepared by trephination over the right parietal cortex. An 18-gauge syringe hub was secured to the craniotomy window with cyanoacrylate and dental cement. At 14–16 h after the craniotomy surgery, animals were re-anesthetized (induction with 3 % isoflurane, 70 % N2O, 30 % O2 and maintained with 1 % isoflurane, 70 % N2O, and 30 % O2), intubated, and mechanically ventilated while paralyzed with pancuronium (1 mg/kg, IV). Rectal and temporalis muscle thermistors were used to maintain core and brain temperatures at 36.6°-37.0 °C. A catheter was placed in the tail to maintain blood gases (pO2 and pCO2), blood pH, and mean arterial blood pressure (MABP) within normal physiological ranges ( ). Following ventilation, animals in the TBI BR4044 and TBI Vehicle groups had inspired O2 reduced to 21 % to maintain blood pO2 levels at 85–95 mmHg. pCO2 levels were monitored to remain within the physiological range of 34–46 mmHg. Sham animals received 3 % isoflurane, 70 % N2O, and 30 % O2 without reduction of O2 to 21 %. 
After stabilization of physiological parameters, animals received a moderate parasagittal FPI (2.2–2.3 atm) or sham injury. At 10 min after FPI, all animals received an initial IV infusion over 5 min of BR4044 dispersed in saline (30 mg/kg DIM) or vehicle (diluted with saline to an equivalent volume to the 30 mg/kg DIM dose). Vehicle treatment of TBI animals was composed of the same lipid-based excipients as BR4044 minus DIM and forms similar nanoscale vesicles on dispersion in saline. Sham Vehicle animals received 1.5 mL saline IV. Saline was administered as vehicle for Sham animals to avoid potential digestive and peritoneal effects from the lipid-based formulation used in TBI Vehicle animals. Following initial IV infusion, all animals received additional intraperitoneal (IP) doses at 4 h post-surgery and then twice daily at 24 and 48 h post-surgery. IP doses for each group were as follows: TBI BR4044 animals received 60 mg/kg DIM, TBI Vehicle animals received vehicle at a volume equal to the 30 mg/kg DIM dose, and Sham Vehicle animals received 1.5 mL saline per dose. After surgery, animals were returned to an individual home cage and re-supplied with food and water. Each animal group received similar total weights of injected saline or diluted formulation over the 3 day course of treatment. Sham Vehicle animals received approximately 9 g of saline, TBI Vehicle animals received 9 g of diluted vehicle (3 g lipid-based excipient and 6 g of saline to achieve a volume equivalent to that of a 30 mg/kg DIM dose), and TBI BR4044 animals received 16.5 g of diluted BR4044 (5.5 g of lipid-based excipient-containing DIM and 11 g of saline for a 60 mg/kg DIM dose).

## Blood capture for EV isolation and assessment
At 24 h post-surgery, animals were re-anesthetized with 3 % isoflurane, 70 % N2O and 30 % O2 for 5 min. Trunk blood was collected in EDTA tubes, centrifuged and plasma was stored at −80 °C until processing for isolation of EVs using the Total Exosome Isolation Kit (Invitrogen). Assessment of EV size and particle concentration was carried out by nanoparticle tracking analysis (NTA) using NanoSight technology (Malvern Panalytical). Trunk blood was collected from a total of 16 animals (Sham Vehicle n = 5, TBI Vehicle n = 6, TBI BR4044 n = 5) for EV isolation. Prior to sacrifice, animals received treatment via IV infusion at 10 min post-surgery, followed by an IP dose at 4 h and one additional morning IP dose at 24 h post-surgery.

## Edema measurements
At 24 h post-surgery, animals were re-anesthetized with 3 % isoflurane, 70 % N2O and 30 % O2 for 5 min, decapitated, and the brain was removed. The right parietal cortex and hippocampus were dissected and weighed using an analytical balance. The tissue was placed in a 100 °C vacuum oven for 72 h until fully dry, and then re-weighed to calculate the percent wet weight. A total of 16 animals (Sham Vehicle n = 5, TBI Vehicle n = 6, TBI BR4044 n = 5) were sacrificed at 24 h post-surgery for cortical and hippocampal edema measurement. Prior to sacrifice, animals had received treatment via IV infusion at 10 min post-surgery, followed by an IP dose at 4 h and one additional morning IP dose at 24 h post-surgery.

## Cylinder task
To evaluate forelimb use asymmetry and sensorimotor function recovery after TBI, we quantified the rodent's spontaneous forelimb use via the cylinder test task. Animals were placed in a transparent plexiglass cylinder (20 cm diameter × 30 cm height) for 5 min. Animals were first evaluated for baseline behavior in the cylinder test at 3 days pre-surgery, and then re-evaluated at day 3 post-surgery at least 8 h after last IP dose to avoid any sedative effects. The number of times the right or left forelimb contacted the wall was counted. Asymmetry index was calculated by dividing the number of contralateral (left) forelimb touches by total forelimb touches. For each animal, asymmetry index post-surgery was normalized to baseline asymmetry index to account for pre-operative bias. A total of 37 animals (Sham Vehicle n = 12, TBI Vehicle n = 12, TBI BR4044 n = 13) were analyzed for sensorimotor function via the cylinder task. At time of post-surgery assessment in the cylinder task, animals had received treatment via IV infusion at 10 min post-surgery, followed by an IP dose at 4 h and twice daily IP dosing at 24 and 48 h post-surgery.

## Contextual and cue fear conditioning
On day 7 post-surgery, animals were habituated for 10 min to a cage equipped with a shock grid floor (FreezeFrame, Actimetrics). At 24 h after habituation, animals were trained by being placed in the apparatus for 120 s, and then a 30 s tone (75 dB, 2.8 kHz) was delivered that co-terminated with a 1 mA, 1 s foot shock. Animals remained in the apparatus for 60 s after the foot shock. At 24 h after training, animals were placed in the apparatus and freezing was measured for 210 s to assess contextual fear conditioning. At 1 h after contextual fear memory testing, animals were placed in an altered apparatus, and freezing was measured for 90 s and then during replay of the tone duration (120 s). Freezing behavior was quantified by video analysis (FreezeFrame, Actimetrics). A total of 37 animals (Sham Vehicle n = 12, TBI Vehicle n = 12, TBI BR4044 n = 13) were analyzed for learning impairments in contextual and cue fear conditioning. At time of learning assessment, animals had received treatment via IV infusion at 10 min post-surgery, followed by an IP dose at 4 h and twice daily IP dosing at 24 and 48 h post-surgery.

## Histological analyses
On day 10 post-surgery, animals were deeply anesthetized, transcardially perfused, and brains were dissected and fixed in 4 % formalin. A total of 37 animals (Sham Vehicle n = 12, TBI Vehicle n = 12, TBI BR4044 n = 13) were analyzed for contusion volume, cortical and hippocampal neuronal loss. At time of perfusion, animals had received treatment via IV infusion at 10 min post-surgery, followed by IP dosing at 4 h and twice daily IP dosing at 24 and 48 h post-surgery. The fixed brains were embedded in paraffin and sectioned at 10 μm thickness in a stereological series (150 μm apart). One series of sections was stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) and analyzed for cortical contusion volume. Cortical contusions of injured animals were contoured in H&E slides at 5× (0.15 NA objective) using an Axiovert 200 M (Zeiss) microscope and Neurolucida (MBF Bioscience). Total contusion volume was calculated using Neurolucida Explorer (MBF Bioscience).
A second series was immunostained with anti-NeuN antibody and neuronal numbers were quantified [ ]. Sections were antigen retrieved using antigen IHC Antigen Retrieval Solution (Invitrogen) for 20 min, blocked for 1 h at room temperature in blocking buffer (5 % normal goat serum in PBS and 0.4 % TX-100), then incubated overnight at 4 °C with mouse anti-NeuN (clone A60, MAB377, 1:200, EMD Millipore) in blocking buffer. The following day, slides were rinsed with PBS and 0.4 % TX-100, incubated in biotinylated goat anti-mouse (BA-9200, 1:300, Vector Laboratories) for 2 h at room temperature in blocking buffer, washed with PBS, and developed with 3,3′-diaminobenzidine (SK-4100, Vector Laboratories) for 5 min.
A third series was immunostained with anti-AhR antibody and quantification of nuclear AhR+ cells in the ipsilateral contusion site was performed (Sham Vehicle n = 12, TBI Vehicle n = 11, TBI BR4044 n = 13). Animals showing no visible contusion were quantified at the equivalent anatomical site as TBI Vehicle animals. Sections were antigen retrieved using Tris-Based Antigen Unmasking Solution (H-3301-250, Vector Laboratories) for 60 min, blocked for 1 h at room temperature (10 % normal goat serum in PBS and 0.4 % TX-100) then incubated overnight at 4 °C with rabbit anti-AhR (LS-B4900-50, 1:100, Lifespan Biosciences) in 10 % blocking buffer. Slides were then rinsed with PBS and 0.4 % TX-100, incubated in biotinylated goat anti-rabbit (BA-1000, 1:300, Vector laboratories) for 2 h at room temperature, washed with PBS, and developed with 3,3′-diaminobenzidine for 10 min.
NeuN+ cell counts in the ipsilateral parietal cortex, hippocampus dentate hilus, hippocampus CA3 region, and nuclear AhR+ cell counts in the contusion site were quantified using unbiased stereology with StereoInvestigator (MBF Bioscience). Contours of the region of interest (ROI) were placed using an Axio Imager M2 (Zeiss) microscope at 5x (0.15 NA objective). Representative images were obtained at 10x (0.30×NA objective) and 63× magnification (1.4 NA oil immersion objective). Parietal cortical NeuN+ cell counts were obtained at bregma levels −3.3, −4.3, and −5.3 mm using a square counting frame of length 125 μm. Hilus NeuN+, CA3 region NeuN+, and nuclear AhR+ cell counts were obtained in one section per animal. Hilar neuron counts were obtained at bregma level −3.3 mm using a square counting frame 50 μm in length. CA3 neuron counts were quantified at bregma level −5.3 mm using a square counting frame 50 μm in length. Nuclear AhR quantification in the contusion area or equivalent anatomical region for animals with no visible contusion was obtained at bregma level −5.8 mm using a rectangular counting area of 0.772 mm2. NeuN+ and nuclear AhR + cell quantification were performed at 63× magnification.

## Microbleed quantitation
H&E brain sections from TBI Vehicle and TBI BR4044 animals obtained at 10 days post-injury following perfusion were analyzed using an unbiased RBC quantification algorithm ( ) [ ]. A total of 24 animals (TBI Vehicle n = 12, TBI BR4044 n = 12) were analyzed for RBC extravasation at the contusion site. At time of perfusion for RBC quantification, animals had received treatment via IV infusion at 10 min post-surgery, followed by IP dosing at 4 h and twice daily IP dosing at 24 and 48 h post-surgery. Slides were selected from TBI Vehicle and TBI BR4044 animals at bregma levels −5.2 mm to −6.04 mm. Each representative slide per animal contained 3–4 sections spaced 150 μm apart. Slides were scanned at 20× magnification using an Aperio AT2 whole slide scanner with Console v102.0.7.5 and Controller v102.0.7.2. Image analysis was performed using HALO v3.6.4134.95 with HALO AI June 3, 4134 (https://indicalab.com/haloai) . Analysis of ROI was conducted using the area quantification module. A consistent ROI of 7.33 mm2 (length 2.805 mm, height 2.615 mm) was used for each section. The ROI was manually placed on scanned slides using histologic constants with the dentate hilus in the left upper quadrant, centered over the cortical contusion, and the lower border following the brain's pial edge. An algorithm was developed based on color differences using red-green-blue values sampled from background brain tissue (blue violet with various shades [Stain 1]) and RBCs (pink/red [Stain 2]). The algorithm identified areas containing RBCs through color analysis at 20× magnification. Image analysis quantified the difference in total RBC area relative to background. Stain 2 areas included color from individual RBCs, RBC clusters, and microbleeds that were not identifiable through visual evaluation.

## Data analysis
Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism. Physiological data including blood pH, pCO2, pO2, MABP, weight, and fear conditioning were assessed using a two-way repeated measures ANOVA with the factors animal group × time and post-hoc Bonferroni's correction for multiple comparisons. EV concentration and normalized particle size were analyzed with a one-way ANOVA with Holm-Šídák's multiple comparisons test. Edema, asymmetry index, neuronal counts, and nuclear AhR+ cell counts were analyzed with a one-way ANOVA and post-hoc Bonferroni's correction for multiple comparisons. Contusion volume and percent bleed area were analyzed with a Student's t-test. The pressure pulse delivered during FPI and mean bleed optical density were analyzed with a Mann-Whitney U test. Normality was assessed using the Shapiro-Wilk test. EV particle size and percent bleed area were log-transformed for analysis.

## Results

## Physiological parameters remained within normal range across groups
To monitor physiological differences between animal groups and ensure parameters remained within normal physiological range in the perioperative period, we measured blood pCO2, pO2, pH, and MABP at 15 min pre- and post-FPI. Blood pCO2, pO2, pH, and MABP remained within normal physiological range at 15 min pre-FPI. As expected, arterial pO2 was significantly lower in TBI Vehicle (p < 0.0001) and TBI BR4044 animals (p < 0.0001) compared to Sham Vehicle animals ( ; interaction of animal group × time F(2, 50) = 2.95, p = 0.0616), although for all groups, oxygenation levels were within physiological range. MABP was also significantly lower in TBI Vehicle animals (p = 0.0066) compared to Sham Vehicle animals (interaction of animal group × time F(2, 50) = 4.21, p = 0.0205).
Blood pCO2, pO2, pH, and MABP continued to remain within normal physiological range at 15 min post-FPI. MABP was significantly decreased in TBI Vehicle (p < 0.0001) and TBI BR4044 animals (p = 0.0002) compared to Sham Vehicle animals. MABP for TBI Vehicle (p = 0.0002) and TBI BR4044 animals (p < 0.0001) was also significantly decreased following FPI compared to pre-surgery. After surgery, pO2 in TBI Vehicle (p < 0.0001) and TBI BR4044 animals (p < 0.0001) was significantly lower compared to Sham Vehicle animals.
To monitor weight change post-surgery, animals were weighed on surgery day and at 10 days post-surgery (perfusion day). TBI BR4044 (p < 0.0001) and Sham Vehicle animals (p < 0.0001) showed significant weight gain compared to the day of surgery (interaction of animal group × time F(2, 50) = 7.80, p = 0.0011). Additionally, TBI Vehicle animals had significantly lower weight compared to TBI BR4044 (p = 0.0138) and Sham Vehicle animals (p = 0.0146) at day 10 post-surgery. Weight remained within normal physiological range across animal groups.

## BR4044 increases EV size and decreases EV concentration after TBI
To assess acute EV release changes following injury, trunk blood from sacrificed animals was collected for isolation of EVs and quantification of particle concentration and size at 24 h post-surgery. TBI Vehicle animals exhibited a significantly higher concentration of EV particles in plasma compared to the Sham Vehicle animals ( a; F(2, 13) = 4.36, p = 0.0355). In contrast, TBI BR4044 animals demonstrated recovery of plasma EVs concentration to levels comparable to Sham Vehicle and significantly lower than TBI Vehicle animals. Analysis of EV particle size revealed a significant reduction in TBI Vehicle animals compared to Sham Vehicle ( b; F(2, 13) = 9.29, p = 0.0031). TBI BR4044 animals exhibited significantly increased EV particle size compared to TBI Vehicle animals. These findings indicate that BR4044 treatment mitigates the acute changes seen in EV release at 24 h post-TBI. 

## BR4044 reduces cortical and hippocampal edema after TBI
To evaluate brain edema in the acute post-TBI recovery period, we analyzed percent water content in the parietal cortex and hippocampus at 24 h post-surgery. Percent water content in the injured parietal cortex of TBI Vehicle animals was significantly increased as compared to Sham Vehicle animals ( a; F(2, 13) = 42.17, p < 0.0001). TBI BR4044 animals showed significantly reduced percent water content in the injured cortex compared to TBI Vehicle animals (p = 0.0078). Similarly, in the hippocampus, there was a significant increase in percent water content in TBI Vehicle animals compared to Sham Vehicle animals ( b; F(2, 13) = 44.35, p < 0.0001). TBI BR4044 animals had significantly reduced hippocampal edema as compared to TBI Vehicle animals (p < 0.0001) and the percent water content was comparable to Sham Vehicle levels (p > 0.05). These results demonstrate that BR4044 treatment decreases cortical and hippocampal edema. 

## BR4044 recovers sensorimotor and memory deficits after TBI
Sensorimotor and learning recovery were assessed using a battery of tasks that included forelimb paw placement in the cylinder task as well as contextual and cue fear conditioning. Animals were analyzed in the cylinder task at 3 days post-surgery to assess asymmetrical forepaw use as a measure of sensorimotor function post-TBI. TBI Vehicle animals showed significant asymmetry in forelimb use as compared to Sham Vehicle animals ( ; F(2, 34) = 63.81, p < 0.0001). BR4044 treatment significantly restored symmetrical forelimb use in TBI BR4044 animals as compared to TBI Vehicle animals. These results demonstrate that BR4044 treatment rescues sensorimotor function in the cylinder task at 3 days post-TBI. 
Furthermore, to assess learning and memory effects of BR4044 post-TBI, animals were analyzed for contextual and cue fear memory at 7–9 days post-surgery. TBI Vehicle animals showed significantly decreased freezing behavior in the contextual fear conditioning task as compared to Sham Vehicle animals at 24 h post-training ( a; interaction of animal group × trial F(2, 34) = 30.24, p < 0.0001). TBI BR4044 animals showed normal contextual fear memory comparable to Sham Vehicle animals. Similarly, we observed decreased freezing behavior in the cue fear conditioning task for TBI Vehicle animals compared to Sham Vehicle animals ( b; interaction of animal group × trial F(2, 34) = 12.18, p = 0.0001), and this behavior was rescued to levels comparable to Sham Vehicle animals in the TBI BR4044 group. These results demonstrate that BR4044 treatment rescues contextual and cue fear conditioning memory at 7–9 days post-TBI. 

## BR4044 reduces neuronal loss after TBI
To quantify neuronal cell loss and recovery during the post-TBI recovery period, brain sections obtained at 10 days post-surgery were immunohistochemically stained for NeuN and quantified using stereology. Histopathology analysis using NeuN showed a visible increase in parietal cortex neuronal cell loss in TBI Vehicle animals, which was reduced by BR4044 treatment ( a). Quantification of ipsilateral parietal cortex neuronal loss revealed a significant decrease in NeuN+ cells in TBI Vehicle animals compared to Sham Vehicle animals at bregma levels −3.3 mm ( b; F(2, 34) = 20.53, p < 0.0001), −4.3 mm ( c; F(2, 34) = 9.83, p = 0.0004), and −5.3 mm ( d; F(2, 34) = 16.88, p < 0.0001). Neuronal loss in the parietal cortex was rescued by BR4044 treatment at all three bregma levels. These results demonstrate that BR4044 treatment prevents cortical neuron loss at 10 days post-TBI. 
To assess the effects of BR4044 treatment on hippocampal cellular recovery following TBI, we quantified NeuN+ cells in the ipsilateral hilus and CA3 region at 10 days post-surgery. There was a non-significant decrease in NeuN+ cell counts in the hilus of TBI Vehicle animals compared to Sham Vehicle and TBI BR4044 animals ( ). Neuronal loss in area CA3 was rescued with BR4044 treatment ( ; F(2, 34) = 30.95, p < 0.0001). These results demonstrate that BR4044 treatment attenuates hippocampal CA3 neuronal loss at 10 days post-TBI. 

## BR4044 reduces contusion development after TBI
To evaluate the anatomical extent of injury, the contusion perimeter in the ipsilateral parietal cortex was traced in H&E slides, and the total contusion volume was calculated for TBI Vehicle and TBI BR4044 animals. Sham Vehicle animals displayed no visible contusions and were excluded from analysis. TBI Vehicle animals displayed a significant increase in contusion volume compared to TBI BR4044 animals ( ; p < 0.0001). Furthermore, nine animals in the TBI BR4044 group were noted to have no observable contusion following injury. These results demonstrate that BR4044 treatment significantly decreases cortical contusion size at 10 days post-TBI. 

## BR4044 reduces RBC accumulation in contusion sites
To evaluate the effects of BR4044 treatment on RBC extravasation at the contusion site following TBI, H&E slides from TBI Vehicle and TBI BR4044 animals at 10 days post-injury were analyzed for quantification of microbleeds using an unbiased algorithm. TBI BR4044 animals had a visible reduction in extravasated RBCs at the contusion site compared to TBI Vehicle animals ( a). Algorithm-based quantification revealed that TBI BR4044 animals displayed significantly reduced percent bleed area compared to TBI Vehicle animals ( b; t(76) = 9.84, p < 0.0001). TBI BR4044 animals also displayed significantly decreased mean bleed optical density at the contusion site compared to TBI Vehicle animals ( c; U = 145, p < 0.0001). These results demonstrate BR4044 treatment provides protection to the neurovascular unit at 10 days post-TBI. 

## BR4044 reduces nuclear AhR activation and nuclear accumulation in contusion sites
To investigate the effects of BR4044 on modulating brain AhR activation following TBI, brain sections obtained at 10 days post-surgery were immunohistochemically stained for AhR and nuclear AhR-expressing cells were quantified at the ipsilateral cortical contusion site using stereology. TBI Vehicle animals showed a significant increase in nuclear AhR-expressing cells compared to Sham Vehicle animals ( ; F(2,33) = 51.56, p < 0.0001) and this was significantly reduced by BR4044 treatment (p < 0.0001). Furthermore, BR4044 treatment reduced the number of nuclear AhR-expressing cells in the cortical contusion site to levels comparable to Sham Vehicle. These results demonstrate that BR4044 treatment attenuates AhR activation and nuclear accumulation following TBI. 

## Discussion
In this study, we explored the therapeutic potential of BR4044 to alleviate histopathology and functional deficits following TBI. Using FPI to model moderate TBI, we observed that BR4044 inhibited post-injury EV release. BR4044 also significantly reduced cortical and hippocampal edema in the acute recovery period. Additionally, BR4044 significantly decreased TBI-induced sensorimotor and learning deficits. Histopathology analysis demonstrated that BR4044 was neuroprotective and prevented neuronal loss in the parietal cortex and the CA3 region of the hippocampus. BR4044 treatment significantly reduced AhR activation and nuclear accumulation following TBI. Furthermore, quantification of RBC extravasation in the contusion site demonstrated a reduction with BR4044 treatment, suggesting preservation of BBB integrity. BR4044 also resulted in significant weight gain in TBI animals to levels comparable to Sham animals. In contrast, TBI Vehicle animals experienced no weight gain despite receiving comparable amounts of lipid-based excipient as TBI BR4044 animals, suggesting a pharmacologic effect of BR4044 in preventing changes in body weight after TBI. These findings highlight BR4044 as a novel treatment option for ameliorating deficits following TBI.
BR4044 was developed for IV administration to treat acute brain injury including TBI and ischemic stroke. The BR4044 premix provides DIM in solution by virtue of a mixture of interactive, water-free excipients optimized for forming a spontaneous and stable nanoemulsion with acceptable particle size and polydispersity when diluted for IV use. Once absorbed from oral or parenteral administration, DIM crosses the BBB as shown in mice using a first-generation absorption-enhanced, microencapsulated formulation, reaching brain concentrations of ∼40 μM [ ]. Tested in cell culture, DIM protects mouse primary hippocampal cells against hypoxia/ischemia at concentrations from 0.1 to 10 μM, and was most effective at 10 μM [ ]. DIM has also been reported to provide neuroprotection from brain injury in a perinatal rat model of hypoxia by dampening elevations in AhR expression [ ]. These findings provided the rationale for reducing inspired O2 in TBI animals during surgery to 21 %, thereby including any possible contribution of hypoxia during the TBI. In contrast, 30 % O2 delivery during the sham surgery was implemented to avoid any possible hypoxic injury from pancuronium-induced paralysis. Lower blood pO2 levels were therefore expected in injured versus Sham animals due to differences in inspired O2 during surgery, yet notably, blood pO2 levels remained within normal physiological range for both TBI Vehicle and TBI BR4044 animals. In addition to models of hypoxia, neuroprotection with oral and parenteral DIM has also been demonstrated in pre-clinical models of intra-cerebral hemorrhage in mice and lipopolysaccharide-induced microglial neuroinflammation in mice [ , , ].
AhR inhibition and prevention of apoptosis are central to the neuroprotection mediated by DIM administration [ ]. AhR is a ligand-activated cytoplasmic receptor and nuclear transcription factor [ ]. Inactive AhR is located in the cytoplasm complexed with several chaperones that stabilize the complex. Ligand binding triggers conformational change and translocation of the ligand/AhR complex into the nucleus, where AhR exchanges its chaperones with the AhR nuclear translocator protein (ARNT, also known as hypoxia-inducible factor-1 β). The AhR/ARNT/ligand heterodimer interacts with DNA xenobiotic responsive elements [ ]. Upon translocation to the nuclei of cells within the CNS, AhR regulates genes involved in various neuronal functions including neuroinflammation, learning, and memory as well as neuronal proliferation, differentiation, and survival [ ]. In the adult mouse brain, neural progenitor cells express AhR and overactivation of AhR by toxic xenobiotic agonists inhibits hippocampal neural progenitor cell proliferation and impairs contextual fear memory [ ]. Glial cells, including astrocytes, and endothelial cells isolated from the BBB, also express the AhR protein and low levels of AhR activity are associated with neuronal survival [ , ]. Regulation of AhR expression in the brain depends not only on internal exposure to activating indole metabolites, but also on external stimulation from the physical damage of TBI and from the hypoxic-ischemic injury due to stroke [ , ]. In the CNS, DIM functions as a translocating AhR ligand but with inverse agonist activity, resulting in inhibitory modulation of multiple neuroinflammatory and stroke-related genes [ , ].
Interrupting early EV release and subsequently reducing EV quantity have been shown in other pre-clinical studies to improve TBI outcome , , , . The benefits of inhibiting pathogenic EV release have been shown in hepatocytes, where non-DIM inverse agonists of the AhR were found to reduce EV release and protect cells from apoptosis [ ]. After TBI, therapy initiated in the acute phase post-injury aims to control early pathogenic events, such as inhibiting the initial damage-associated molecular patterns [ ]. Accordingly, our data demonstrated amelioration of acute EV changes with BR4044, including limiting increased EV size and reducing EV concentration. Other small-molecule therapeutic candidates that inhibit EV release show similar benefits after TBI. These include neutral sphingomyelinase inhibitors, however these require further development to improve target specificity and demonstrate translational evidence of benefit [ ]. More specific inhibition of early EV release has been achieved with inhibition of the plasma membrane P2X7 receptor, whose overactivity and release from initial membrane disruption promotes TBI-related injury. Direct inhibition of the P2X7 receptor using the specific antagonist, A804598, and the sphingosine 1-phosphate receptor agonist FTY720 (Fingolimod) both proved successful in improving FPI outcome and reducing the number of microvesicle-like particles [ ]. Further studies are needed to determine the optimal timing of EV sampling after TBI and to identify the therapeutic window and dose-response curve of BR4044 for future clinical translation.
Brain edema during the acute phase post-injury is a well-established contributor to mortality after TBI [ ]. Prior studies have demonstrated various mechanisms contributing to cerebral edema development, including BBB disruption, dysregulated cellular volume controlled by ionic pumps, and inflammatory responses [ ]. Targeting these processes through pharmaceutical interventions has resulted in promising outcomes, although further investigation is required [ ]. Our study is the first to assess BR4044's efficacy in cerebral edema, and treatment post-injury significantly decreased hippocampal and cortical edema to sham levels. Reduction in extravasated RBCs evaluated at 10 days post-injury suggests lasting protection of the BBB. Further studies aimed at assessing the mechanism of action are required; however, these preliminary findings provide strong evidence for BR4044 as a treatment for cerebral edema.
Alongside acute edema, deficits in motor movement, learning, and memory have been demonstrated in patients after TBI [ , ]. Our study is the first to assess the efficacy of BR4044 treatment in reversing these behavioral deficits. BR4044 treatment post-injury attenuated the significant asymmetry in forelimb use in the cylinder task to sham levels. Similarly, BR4044 recovered TBI-induced impairments in associative fear memory, demonstrating its efficacy in improving both motor and learning deficits post-injury. These results are promising; however, further studies assessing long-term neurobehavioral outcomes are necessary.
The current study found significant preservation of neuronal cell numbers in the parietal cortex of BR4044 treated TBI animals, supporting the improvement in behavior observed after treatment. Similar trends were observed in the hippocampus, where treatment reduced neuronal loss. DIM has been shown to inhibit Nr4a1 (Nur77) which is a pro-apoptotic transcription factor contributing to neuronal loss associated with TBI [ , ]. DIM has shown anti-apoptotic activity in cancer cell culture through induction of astrocyte elevated gene-1 (AEG-1) protein which supports protective autophagy and activation of AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) which play relevant roles in astrocyte response and neuronal preservation in TBI [ , ]. DIM also inhibits glutamate-induced apoptosis in hippocampal cells via activation of the neuroprotective TrkB/CREB/BDNF pathway [ ]. The improvement in neuronal loss within the parietal cortex and hippocampus after BR4044 treatment suggests its potential in alleviating behavioral deficits post-injury.
DIM is well established as an inhibitory modulator of the AhR which may provide a significant therapeutic benefit for TBI recovery. Our study demonstrated the AhR-modulating capabilities of BR4044 by reducing TBI-driven AhR activation and nuclear accumulation to levels comparable to Sham Vehicle animals. The diverse influence of indole-supported AhR activity on brain health, including learning, memory, mood, and adult neurogenesis, is increasingly documented [ ]. Inhibitory control by indoles over neuroinflammation is highly relevant for TBI, which is now linked to future Alzheimer's disease (AD) [ ]. Early post-traumatic cognitive deficits following TBI are predictive of subsequent AD-like abnormalities in brain structure and function [ ]. Consistent with the therapeutic potential of BR4044, the use of indole ligands of the AhR has proven to be supportive of cognitive functioning in pre-clinical AD studies [ ]. Administration of the DIM precursor, indole-3-carbinol, inhibits AD-related neuroinflammation and decreases formation of the NLRP3 inflammasome in AD mice [ ]. Additionally, prior pre-clinical studies have demonstrated therapeutic benefits of DIM treatment in experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis [ , ]. These studies demonstrate the potential benefits of BR4044 treatment for other CNS injuries and diseases.
While the current study assessing BR4044 demonstrated significant evidence of its therapeutic potential and efficacy, future studies need to evaluate cognitive outcomes at later time points following TBI to understand the long-term treatment potential of BR4044. The present study was limited to adult male animals. A follow-up study including both females and older animals of either sex would improve the translational potential of BR4044. Based on the present findings, pre-clinical evaluation of IV BR4044 is of great promise as a future TBI therapeutic, but requires a multi-center approach for additional confirmatory findings.
