## Significance
Lipid nanoparticles deliver mRNA to the liver in people, but delivery to other tissues is challenging. Liver detargeting is important because it increases the availability of drug to deliver to other organs (e.g., the spleen). To produce lipid nanoparticles that avoid uptake by the liver, scientists often modify them by adding a permanent charge; here, we report charge-independent liver detargeting using glycolipids.
Keywords: mRNA, lipid nanoparticle, LNP, nanomedicine

## Abstract
Lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) formulated with a neutral helper lipid can deliver RNA to the liver in humans. However, clinically relevant delivery to other tissues has remained challenging. To avoid the liver, scientists often add antibodies or helper lipids with a permanent charge. Here, we report an alternative approach: antibody- and charge-independent liver detargeting. Using DNA barcoding to test 109 chemically distinct LNPs in vivo, we found that replacing a neutral helper lipid with a neutral glycolipid reduced liver delivery and increased splenic delivery. Consistent with this differential tropism, these glycolipid nanoparticles caused differences in downstream cellular signaling in vivo compared to traditional LNPs. These data suggest that extrahepatic LNPs can be designed without the imposition of a net negative or positive charge.

Lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) have carried therapeutic RNA in three FDA-approved products ( – ) and several ongoing clinical trials ( ). The most clinically advanced LNP-RNA drugs are intramuscularly administered vaccines ( , ) and intravenously administered genetic therapies that target the liver ( , – ). In these cases, the LNPs are often formulated by combining an ionizable lipid, a poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG)-lipid, cholesterol, and the zwitterionic helper lipid 1,2-distearoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (DSPC), which has a neutral charge.
These clinical data support efforts to design LNPs that target other tissues ( , ). To deliver RNA outside the liver, scientists often use three approaches. The first is to pre-treat animals with molecules that block drug entry or activity in the liver ( – ). The second is to add an active targeting ligand such as an antibody ( – ). The third is to include a permanently charged anionic or cationic helper lipid. Several labs have reported charge-dependent liver detargeting and subsequent increases in lung or spleen delivery ( – ). However, data also suggest that adding large molar percentages of permanently charged cationic helper lipids can lead to embolisms in mice ( ), especially when a 40:1 nanoparticle:RNA mass ratio is used. These results are concerning since nonhuman primates (NHPs) and humans can have stronger immune responses to drugs than mice ( ). We therefore considered whether extrahepatic delivery could be achieved without antibodies or charged helper lipids. To test this hypothesis, we used DNA barcoding ( , ) to measure how 109 LNPs formulated with glycolipids (termed GlycoLNPs) delivered mRNA in vivo. The screen revealed two lead GlycoLNPs that decreased liver mRNA delivery and increased splenic mRNA delivery relative to control LNPs formulated with DSPC.

## Results
We formulated two libraries, each with 64 chemically distinct nanoparticles. To isolate the effect of the helper lipid, we used two established ionizable lipids: SM-102 ( ) and cKK-E12 ( ). We formulated the first LNP library with SM-102, cholesterol, four PEG-lipids, and either C8 galactosyl(α) ceramide (C8GαCer) or C16 galactosyl(α) ceramide (C16GαCer); the second library used cKK-E12 in place of SM-102 ( ). We chose C8GαCer and C16GαCer for two reasons. First, the lipid length in PEG-lipids affects how quickly LNPs shed them in vivo ( ). As with PEG, the sugar component of the glycolipid is hydrophilic; we therefore reasoned that varying the lipid length could change the rate at which it desorbs. Second, sugars can bind serum components ( , ) and extracellular receptors ( ); interactions with serum components and extracellular receptors can affect LNP delivery.
 To control for molar ratio-dependent effects, we mixed the components eight ways ( ); this led to 64 distinct LNPs. To evaluate how all the LNPs worked in vivo, we formulated them to carry mRNA encoding Thy1.1 as well as a unique DNA barcode. For example, LNP-1, with chemical structure 1, contained Thy1.1 mRNA and DNA barcode 1, whereas LNP-N, with chemical structure N, contained Thy1.1 mRNA and DNA barcode N. We then characterized the hydrodynamic diameter and polydispersity of all 64 LNPs individually and pooled monodisperse LNPs with hydrodynamic diameters between 30 and 180 nm. We discarded LNPs that did not meet these biophysical inclusion criteria. In the SM-102–based library, 52 of the 64 LNPs met the criteria and were intravenously injected into mice at a total dose of 1.5 mg/kg (i.e., 0.03 mg/kg/particle, on average). In the cKK-E12–based library, 57 of the LNPs met the criteria and were injected at a total dose of 1.6 mg/kg (i.e., 0.03 mg/kg/particle, on average). As a control, we measured the hydrodynamic diameter of the pooled LNPs; it was within the range of the LNPs composing the pool, suggesting the mixed LNPs did not fall out of solution ( ).
At 16 h after injection, we isolated tissues from mice and quantified Thy1.1 protein expression in 25 cell types from the liver, spleen, blood, and lymph nodes (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). We observed differences in functional delivery (i.e., Thy1.1+ cells) in the two libraries. In the SM-102–based experiment, we observed delivery in liver endothelial cells, immune cells, Kupffer cells, and hepatocytes; splenic macrophages, dendritic cells, and helper T cells; and blood dendritic cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S2). By contrast, in the cKK-E12–based experiment, we observed delivery in liver endothelial cells, immune cells, Kupffer cells, and hepatocytes; splenic macrophages, dendritic cells, helper T cells, and B cells; dendritic cells and macrophages in the lymph nodes; and macrophages in the blood (SI Appendix, Fig. S3). In the SM-102–based screen, we found evidence that the LNP pools delivered to spleen as well as or better than to the liver. Specifically, the percentage of Thy1.1+ splenic F480+ cells was higher than the percentage of Thy1.1+ liver endothelial cells ( ). We compared these cell types because they were the most transfected populations in each organ. We then repeated the analysis for the cKK-E12–based screen and found that delivery to splenic F480+ cells was similar to liver endothelial cell delivery ( ). These data provided one line of evidence that an LNP with neutral charge could deliver mRNA equally to the spleen and liver.
We then used fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) to isolate and sequence Thy1.1+ cells ( ). This identified barcodes that were colocalized with cells in which functional delivery occurred. We analyzed the SM-102–based experiment and found that DNA barcodes associated with GlycoLNPs containing C16GαCer had significantly higher normalized delivery than GlycoLNPs containing C8GαCer ( ). The same relationship was true with the cKK-E12–based library ( ).
Predictions made by any pooled LNP screen must be confirmed using individual particles. We ranked GlycoLNPs based on their spleen-to-liver ratios (SI Appendix, Fig. S4) and selected five lead GlycoLNPs, which we named GlycoLNP45, GlycoLNP59, GlycoLNP60, GlycoLNP61, and GlycoLNP62 (SI Appendix, Fig. S5). We formulated all five to carry mRNA encoding Thy1.1; they formed monodisperse particles with hydrodynamic diameters between 80 and 132 nm but had low encapsulation efficiencies ( ). Based on these analyses, we excluded GlycoLNP60 and intravenously injected GlycoLNP45, GlycoLNP59, GlycoLNP61, and GlycoLNP62 at a dose of 0.8 mg/kg. Using PBS-injected mice as a gating control, we then quantified the percentage of Thy1.1+ cells in the liver and spleen ( ). In mice treated with GlycoLNP61, the percentage of Thy1.1+ cells was higher in the spleen than the liver. These data led us to focus on GlycoLNP61.
 To isolate the effect of the glycolipid within GlycoLNP61, we formulated Control LNP61, an LNP with identical molar ratios but formulated with DSPC instead of C16αCer ( ). Both LNPs were monodisperse with hydrodynamic diameters of 115 and 60 nm and encapsulation efficiencies of 58 and 76% for GlycoLNP61 and Control LNP61, respectively (SI Appendix, Fig. S6). We then analyzed their pKa ( ) and shape, finding that LNP61 formed more blebbed LNPs ( ) than its DSPC control ( ). The biophysical differences led us to hypothesize that changing the helper lipid from C16αCer to DSPC might alter the surface chemistry of the LNP. We therefore compared NMR spectra for each nanoparticle suspended in an aqueous solution ( ) ( ). We also lyophilized intact LNPs and dissolved them in chloroform-d to analyze all the components in the LNP, not just on the surface ( ). As expected, the surface spectra differed from the dissolved spectra, suggesting that the surface chemistry was distinct from the internal chemistry (SI Appendix, Fig. S7). When we compared the surface spectra from the two particles, we did not observe any differences ( ).
We then injected both LNPs into mice at a dose of 0.8 mg/kg, isolated cells 16 h later, and quantified Thy1.1 protein expression using flow cytometry. Compared to the Control LNP61, GlycoLNP61 delivered more mRNA to splenic macrophages and less mRNA to liver endothelial cells. Specifically, GlycoLNP61 delivered mRNA to 78% of splenic macrophages and 54% of liver endothelial cells, whereas Control LNP61 delivered mRNA to 66% of splenic macrophages and 82% of liver endothelial cells ( ). When plotted as a ratio of spleen-to-liver delivery, there was a significant difference between the two particles ( ). Neither particle efficiently delivered RNA to other tested tissues ( ). We then performed a dose–response and observed GlycoLNP61-mediated delivery in the spleen at doses as low as 0.2 mg/kg ( ).
 To ensure our results were not specific to Thy1.1 mRNA, we compared how well GlycoLNP61 and Control LNP61 delivered mRNA encoding Cre recombinase. We intravenously injected both LNPs into Ai14 mice at 0.8 mg/kg, waited 3 d, which is sufficient time for Cre-mediated recombination to drive tdTomato expression ( ), and then imaged tdTomato. Consistent with the Thy1.1 data, mice treated with GlycoLNP61 had more tdTomato+ cells than mice treated with Control LNP61 ( ). This image also suggested that delivery to the splenic red pulp was higher than to the splenic white pulp. Finally, we compared delivery between GlycoLNP62 and its DSPC control (i.e., GlycoLNP62 molar ratios, replacing the glycolipid with DSPC) and again found a higher spleen-to-liver ratio when using the glycolipid (SI Appendix, Fig. S8).
To complement the flow cytometry and imaging readouts, we performed a multiomics assay to quantify delivery with single-cell resolution ( ). We formulated the two LNPs to carry mRNA encoding Thy1.1, intravenously injected them at 0.8 mg/kg, isolated the spleen 24 h later, and analyzed the transcriptome of 18,585 cells using single-cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA-seq). We added DNA-conjugated anti-Thy1.1 antibodies to quantify functional delivery and overlaid the reads on the scRNA-seq dataset ( ). The 18,585 cells were divided into 11 clusters based on differential gene expression ( ) and analyzed for Thy1.1 protein. Both particles delivered Thy1.1 to all 11 clusters, and GlycoLNP61 delivery tended to be higher than Control LNP61 delivery ( ). Since delivery was highest in macrophages (cluster 7), we focused on two macrophage subsets (termed 7a and 7b) ( ). Subset 7a was positive for sialic-acid-binding immunoglobulin-like lectin-1 (Siglec-1) and other markers associated with marginal zone macrophages, while subset 7b was positive for integrin subunit alpha 9 and other markers associated with red pulp macrophages ( ). We performed a differential gene expression analysis on the macrophage subclusters ( ) and identified 56 genes that were only expressed in cluster 7a and nine that were only expressed in cluster 7b ( and SI Appendix, Fig. S9). Thy1.1 expression was highest in cluster 7b ( ), suggesting that GlycoLNP61 did not deliver to all macrophages equally.
 Taken together, these delivery data led us to hypothesize that the in vivo cellular response to GlycoLNP61 could be different from the cellular response to Control LNP61. We performed differential gene expression and pathway analyses on Thy1.1+ macrophages, comparing both GlycoLNP61 treatment and Control LNP61 treatment to PBS. Compared to PBS-treated mice, macrophages in both groups expressed inflammatory cytokines. Interestingly, macrophages in both groups also exhibited nuclear factor-kappa B (NF-κB) activation but did so via different mechanisms ( ). The GlycoLNP61 group activated NF-κB via the noncanonical pathway (R-HSA-5676590, R-HSA-5607761, R-HSA-5668541), while the Control LNP61 group activated NF-κB via the Fas-associated death domain and receptor-interacting protein 1 pathway (R-HSA-933543) ( ). For example, dectin-1 (CLEC7A), a glucan-responsive receptor that stimulates the noncanonical NF-κB pathway (R-HSA-5607764), was upregulated in the GlycoLNP61 group ( , ).
 Given that cells respond dynamically to LNPs ( ), we repeated the experiment using a 1 h timepoint instead of a 24 h timepoint. Eighteen genes were upregulated in the GlycoLNP61 group relative to the Control LNP61 group, including dectin-1 (CLEC7A) ( ). When we performed a pathway analysis using Reactome ( ), we identified 33 upregulated pathways in GlycoLNP61 relative to Control LNP61 group (SI Appendix, Fig. S10). Of the 33 upregulated pathways, six were related to SDC4 including the syndecan interactions pathway (R-HSA-3000170), three galactosyltransferase enzyme pathways (R-HSA-4420332, R-HSA-3560783, and R-HSA-3560801), and two glycosyltransferase enzyme pathways (R-HSA-3656237 and R-HSA-3656253) ( ). Finally, we reasoned that the differential cellular response to GlycoLNP61 meant that the systemic response to the LNP might change. We therefore quantified serum cytokines at one and 24 h after administering a 0.8 mg/kg dose of GlycoLNP61 and Control LNP61. Compared to PBS-treated mice, we observed a significant increase in interleukin-4 (IL-4) and granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM–CSF) after 1 h ( ). At the 24 h timepoint, both IL-4 and GM–CSF resolved, but we observed an increase in interleukin-6, monocyte chemoattractant protein-1, and interferon-γ ( ). Taken together, the scRNA-seq data and the serum cytokine data suggest that GlycoLNP61 led to a different cell response than its DSPC control.

## Discussion
These data are consistent with the hypothesis that nanoparticles can be detargeted from the liver without using active targeting or permanent charge. Specifically, GlycoLNP61 detargets the liver via the inclusion of a neutral glyco-helper lipid. In contrast, the selective organ targeting spleen particle uses an anionic helper lipid to detarget the liver ( ). Additionally, the lipid:mass ratio of GlycoLNP61 is 10:1 while that of the anionic LNP is 40:1 (SI Appendix, Fig. S5). These data also lead to several questions that should be addressed in future studies. One question is what amount of glycolipid is needed to change tropism. GlycoLNP61, which contained 25% C16GαCer, was identified using a barcoded screen. Future screens or individual studies may reveal that less glycolipid is sufficient. A second question is whether this LNP can deliver more complex payloads. While Cre recombinase leads to genomic changes, delivery readouts from Cre-mediated genomic changes are not equivalent to CRISPR Cas-mediated gene editing. We also anticipate future insights related to the scRNA-seq data. Specifically, we found the ability to measure delivery in single cells useful, especially when heterogenous and responsive cells (e.g., macrophages) are being studied. In this case, scRNA-seq enabled us to identify delivery differences within macrophage subsets. Whether specific receptors or functionalities of these cells are responsible for the differences in delivery cannot be inferred using this scRNA-seq dataset. Instead, these data can serve as the basis for iterative LNP screening experiments evaluating new GlycoLNPs designed around GlycoLNP61 to target a specific subset of macrophages. Based on our experience using other techniques to measure in vivo delivery, we believe that identifying differences in these subsets would have been challenging without scRNA-seq.
The scRNA-seq data also led us to pathways that responded differently when DSPC was replaced by C16GαCer at one and 24 h. In particular, we found the upregulation of three pathways related to galactosyltransferase at 1 h interesting. This response was not observed in the control LNP group, suggesting that adding the glycolipid can alter the cellular response. We also noted an upregulation of NF-κB activation pathways in both LNP groups relative to the PBS group at 24 h. However, the mechanism of NF-κB activation differed between GlycoLNP61 and Control LNP61 (noncanonical and Fas-associated death domain/receptor-interacting protein 1 pathways, respectively). These data do not suggest that cells activated via the noncanonical NF-kB pathway are more susceptible to GlycoLNP delivery, but they do suggest that GlycoLNP61 induces noncanonical NF-κB activation after LNP delivery and Control LNP61 does not. Relatedly, although we did not observe any embolisms in treated mice, we did measure increased serum cytokines relative to the DSPC control. These data provide a single, early line of evidence suggesting that noncanonical NF-κB signaling could increase systemic LNP toxicity. They also make it unlikely that GlycoLNP61 will be used for clinically relevant delivery. Nonetheless, we believe that these data justify the exploration of other glycolipids, including those that may suppress immune responses.
It is important to acknowledge the other limitations of this study. First, the in vivo data were generated in mice. Since the relationship between delivery in mice and NHPs remains understudied ( ), it is possible this effect will not translate to NHPs. This may be especially difficult for nonliver delivery, since the relative weights of tissues are not conserved across species ( ). Second, given that LNP tropism can be affected by any of the four components, it is possible that these effects were limited to the formulations we tested. Additional studies are required to understand whether charge-independent liver detargeting occurs with many other LNP formulations. Third, given that modulating one of the LNP components (in this case the helper lipid) can affect LNP size, we cannot exclude this as a potential confounding effect. However, GlycoLNP62 and Control LNP62 were similarly sized, and yet, we observed the same liver detargeting and subsequent spleen delivery. We also analyzed the relationship between hydrodynamic diameter and spleen-to-liver delivery ratios in the LNP pool and found no correlation (SI Appendix, Fig. S11). Fourth, although we identified pathways that responded differently to GlycoLNP61, we did not identify a lead gene, create a knockout mouse, and show a downstream change in delivery. This type of experiment is difficult but required in order to rigorously relate a gene to nanoparticle delivery. For example, neutral LNPs that target hepatocytes without GalNAc-based ligands ( ) demonstrate reduced delivery in ApoE−/− and LDLR−/− mice ( ). Similarly, genetic knockout studies in zebrafish and mice suggest that LNPs access liver endothelial cells by interacting with Stabilin-2 ( ). Despite these limitations, we believe that these data provide the rationale to pursue charge-independent liver detargeting approaches in future studies.

## Materials and Methods

## mRNA Synthesis.
Cre mRNA was synthesized following the previously reported protocol ( ). First, the mRNA sequence was obtained from Integrated DNA Technologies (IDT) as a DNA gBlock containing a 5′ UTR with Kozak sequence, a 3′ UTR derived from the mouse alpha-globin sequence, and extensions for Gibson assembly. The sequence was designed using the IDT website and codon optimized for human. Next, using New England Biolabs (NEB) Builder, the gBlock was cloned into a PCR-amplified pMA7 vector through Gibson assembly with three molar excess of insert. The Gibson assembly was performed after purification of transcripts with 0.8% agarose gel. Resultant plasmid sequences were confirmed using Sanger sequencing. Plasmids were linearized with NotI-HF (NEB) at 37 °C for 16 h. Before resuspension in nuclease-free water, linearized templates were purified by ammonium acetate (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Following the manufacturer’s protocol (N1-methyl-pseudouridine modified), mRNA was transcribed in vitro at 37 °C for 16 h using the HiScribe T7 kit (NEB). After treatment with DNase I (Aldevron) for 30 min, the RNA product was purified using lithium chloride precipitation (Thermo Fisher Scientific). RNA was then capped with a Cap1 structure (guanylyl transferase and 2′-Omethyltransferase, Aldevron) after being heat-denatured at 65 °C for 10 min. Transcripts were then enzymatically polyadenylated (Aldevron). mRNA was then purified by lithium chloride precipitation before and after treatment with alkaline phosphatase (NEB). Concentrations of the products were quantified using a NanoDrop and measured 3 to 5 mg/mL. Finally, the purified RNA products were visualized using gel electrophoresis to ensure purity.

## Nanoparticle Formulation.
Nanoparticles were formulated using either NanoAssemblr™ Spark™ or Ignite™ cartridges from Precision Nanosystems. Lipid components were purchased from Avanti Polar Lipids or Cayman Chemical and were dissolved in 100% ethanol. C8GαCer was dissolved in 90% ethanol. Lipid components were combined at predetermined molar ratios to make the lipid phase. Thy1.1 mRNA and DNA barcodes were diluted in citrate buffer at 0.13 and 0.02 mg/mL, respectively. After formulation, LNPs were collected in 1 X PBS and underwent buffer exchange in a Pierce™ 20 K MWCO microdialysis plate for 2 h at room temperature.

## Nanoparticle Characterization.
The hydrodynamic diameter and PDI of nanoparticles were determined by dynamic light scattering (DynaPro Plate Reader II, Wyatt). LNPs were diluted in 1 X PBS and sterile purified using a 0.22 μm filter. LNPs with diameters between 30 and 180 nm and PDIs lower than 0.4 were included. For screens, particles that met these criteria were pooled and dialyzed at 100 K MWCO for 1 h. The nucleic acid concentration of pooled particles was measured using NanoDrop™ (Thermo Scientific).

## Encapsulation Efficiency.
mRNA encapsulation was measured using Quant-it™ RiboGreen RNA Assay Kit according to a protocol developed by Precision Nanosystems. In duplicates, 50 μL of 6 ng/μL LNP was added to 50 μL of 1 X TE (Thermo Fisher) or 50 μL of 2 X Triton (Sigma Aldrich). Following 10 min of incubation at 37 C, 100 μL of 1:100 RiboGreen reagent (Thermo Fisher) was added to each well. The fluorescence was quantified using a plate reader (BioTex Synergy H4 Hybrid) at an excitation wavelength of 485 nm and an emission wavelength of 528 nm.

## Characterization of LNPs by cryo-TEM.
LNP samples were placed on 300-mesh copper grids, with a holey carbon substrate (1.2 µm holes spaced by 1.3 µm; C-flat, Electron Microscopy Sciences). Grids were previously glow-discharged (negative charge) for 15 s using a GloQube Plus glow discharge system (Quorum Tech). Samples (3 µL) were blotted with filter paper for 1 s at room temperature and 100% humidity and plunge-frozen into liquid ethane using a Vitrobot Mark IV (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Cryo-TEM grids were stored in liquid nitrogen until cryo-TEM data acquisition. Cryo-TEM grids were loaded under liquid nitrogen temperatures, using a Gatan 914 cryo-TEM sample holder, into a JEOL JEM-2200FS TEM (JEOL) operating at 200 keV. Micrographs were acquired under low-dose conditions using the Serial EM software (Mastronarde 2005) with a DE20 direct electron detector device (Direct Electron, San Diego, CA), at nominal magnifications of either 3kx (overview images), 40kx, or 80kx, yielding pixel sizes of 21.2, 1.4, and 0.8 Å per pixel, respectively.

## NMR Spectra of LNP Surface.
For characterization of the LNP surface, LNPs were dialyzed in 1 X PBS and prepared in a final solution containing 10% D2O. For characterization of disrupted LNPs, LNPs were frozen and lyophilized overnight before being dissolved in chloroform-d at 1 mg/mL lipid. NMR spectra were recorded on Bruker 800 MHz spectrometer.

## TNS Assay.
The pKa of GlycoLNP61 was measured as previously described ( ). Briefly, a stock solution of 10 mM sodium phosphate, 10 mM sodium borate, 10 mM sodium citrate, and 150 mM sodium chloride was prepared and pH adjusted using hydrogen chloride and sodium hydroxide to a range of pH between 3 and 12. Using three replicates for each nanoparticle at each pH, 90 μL pH-adjusted buffer was added to a 96-well plate, followed by the addition of 2 μL of 300 mM 2-(p-toluidino)-6-naphthalene sulfonic acid, and 3.26 μL of each nanoparticle was added to each well. After 5 min of incubation under gentle shaking, fluorescence absorbance was measured using excitation wavelengths of 325 nm and emission wavelengths of 435 nm (BioTek Synergy H4 Hybrids). Fluorescence absorbance was then plotted versus pH, and a sigmoidal curve was generated. The apparent pKa was calculated as the log of the inflection point of the resultant curve.

## Animal Experiments.
All animal experiments were performed in accordance with Emory University’s IACUC. All animals were housed in Emory University’s animal facility. C57BL/6 J (B6/000664) were purchased from Jackson Laboratories. Ai14 mice were bred at Emory University Division of Animal Resources. In all experiments, we used N = 3 mice/group, unless otherwise noted.

## Cell Isolation and Staining.
Cells were isolated 16 to 24 h after IV injection of LNPs unless otherwise noted. Mice were perfused with 10 mL of 1 X PBS through the right atrium. Tissues were finely cut and then placed in respective digestion enzyme solutions. Liver, lung, and kidney were placed in a solution containing collagenase type I (Sigma-Aldrich), collagenase type IX (Sigma-Aldrich), and hyaluronidase (Sigma-Aldrich) at 37 C at 500 rpm for 30 min. For the heart, collagenase type IV was included. For the spleen and nodes, RPMI 1640 (1X) was used. After digestion, cell suspensions were filtered through 70 μm mesh and centrifuged to remove digestive enzymes. Fc receptors were blocked (TruStain fcXTM anti-mouse CD16/32, BioLegend) to avoid nonspecific binding. Cells were stained to identify specific cell populations and sorted on BD FACs Aria II Cell Sorter or analyzed on BD FACSymphony A5 in the Pediatric/Winship Flow Cytometry Core at Emory University. The antibody clones used were anti-TER119 (BioLegend), anti-CD45 (30-F11 BioLegend), anti-F4/80 (BM8, BioLegend), anti-CD3 (145-2C11, BD Horizon), anti-CD11c (N418, BD Optibuild), anti-CD19 (6D5, BioLegend), anti-CD4 (GK1.5, BD Horizon), anti-CD8 (53-6.7), anti-CD31 (390, BioLegend), anti-CD45 (104, BioLegend), anti-CD68 (FA-11, BioLegend), anti-CD326 (G8.8, BioLegend), anti-Thy1.1 (OX-7, BioLegend), and near IR fluorescent reactive dye (Invitrogen).

## Spleen Sectioning and Imaging.
Spleens were isolated from C57BL/6 J mice and briefly perfused in situ for 30 s with ice-cold PBS followed by 1 min of 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA) and then left to incubate overnight, shaking at 4 °C. Samples were embedded in paraffin and cut into 5-μm-thick sections. Antigen retrieval and deparaffinization was performed using Citrate Antigen Retrieval Buffer (Abcam, ab93678), followed by 5 min of permeabilization (0.5% Triton X-100), 1 h of blocking, and the addition of primary antibody (Anti-RFP pAB, MBL PM005) in blocking solution (3% BSA in 0.1% Triton X-100) for overnight incubation at 4 °C. Slides underwent three cycles of 10-min PBS washes, followed by the application of secondary antibody (Goat Anti-Rabbit IgG H&L Alexa Fluor® 555, Abcam ab150078) with DAPI for 40 min at room temperature. The slides were washed and mounted with VECTASHIELD® Antifade Mounting Medium (Vector Laboratories H-1000-10) and imaged on a Leica Stellaris 8 Confocal Microscope.

## PCR Amplification.
All samples were amplified and prepared for sequencing using a one-step PCR protocol as previously described ( ). Briefly, 1 μL of primers (5 µM for Final Reverse/Forward, 0.5 µM for Base Forward) were added to 5 μL of Kapa HiFi 2X master mix and 4 μL template DNA/water. Amplified samples were observed using 2% E-Gel™ EX Agarose Gel. When the PCR reaction did not produce clear bands, the primer concentrations, DNA template input, PCR temperature, and number of cycles were optimized for individual samples.

## Deep Sequencing.
Illumina deep sequencing was performed on an Illumina MiniSeq™. Primers were designed based on Nextera XT adapter sequences.

## Data Analysis and Statistics.
Counts for each particle, per tissue, were normalized to the barcoded LNP mixture we injected into the mouse. This “input” DNA provided the DNA counts and was used to normalize DNA counts from the cells and tissues. Sequencing results were processed using a custom Python-based tool to extract raw barcode counts for each tissue. These raw counts were then normalized with an R script prior to further analysis. Statistical analysis was done using GraphPad Prism 10. Data are plotted as mean ± SD.

## Cytokine analysis.
1 h and 24 h after LNP administration, blood was drawn from the heart. Serum was isolated, and cytokine levels were quantified by IDEXX BioAnalytics using the Mouse 25-Plex Panel.

## Cell isolation and mRNA extraction for scRNA-seq.
1 h and 24 h post-injection, spleens were dissected and finely cut. Cell suspensions were passed through 70 μm mesh and resuspended in RoboSep buffer. Dead and red blood cells (RBCs) were removed using EasySep dead cell (Annexin V) and RBC (anti-TER119) removal kit (StemCell Technologies) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Fc receptors were blocked (TruStain fcXTM anti-mouse CD16/32, BioLegend) to avoid nonspecific binding. Then, oligo-tagged anti-mouse cell hashes (BioLegend) and anti-Thy1.1 antibodies (BioLegend) were added. After washing the labeled cells twice with RoboSep buffer, the cell viability and numbers were recorded for each sample. The cells from each sample were then pooled at the same ratio and a BD Rhapsody cartridge was loaded with 40,000 cells followed by BD Rhapsody™ Enhanced Cell Capture beads. Cells were then lysed and oligo cell labels and transcriptome were captured by the beads. cDNA libraries were prepared using the BD Rhapsody™ Whole Transcriptome Analysis (WTA) Amplification Kit following the BD Rhapsody™ System mRNA WTA and Sample Tag Library Preparation protocol (BD Biosciences). The final libraries were quantified using a Qubit™ Fluorometer and sent to Novogene for sequencing. After the assessment of library quality using Bioanalyzer (Agilent), sequencing was performed by Illumina NovaSeq Plus PE150 at Novogene.

## Processing of Sequencing Data.
The data were processed using STARsolo (v2.7.9a) 52. All samples were mapped to GRCm39, and only exonic regions were counted. All output files were loaded into Seurat (v 4.0.4), and in summary, cells were log normalized to a scale factor of 10,000, then scaled using a linear transformation ( ). DoubletFinder (v3) was used to identify doublets as previously described ( ). This was followed by PCA dimensional reduction and UMAP clustering. The cell types were identified, and the clusters were annotated from marker genes in mouse spleen. Thy1.1 oligo-tag counts were combined with RNA counts in Seurat and treated similarly to other multimodal datasets such as cellular indexing of transcriptomes and epitopes by sequencing ( ). Visualization of gene, barcode, and Thy1.1 expression levels was performed using R/Seurat commands (FeaturePlot and DotPlot).

## Differential Gene Expression and Pathway Analysis.
Differential gene expression datasets were generated using BioTuring Browser. Pathway analyses of expression data were conducted using Reactome pathway browser and analysis tools (v. 89) ( ). Mouse identifiers were converted to human equivalents according to Reactome electronic inference methodology.
