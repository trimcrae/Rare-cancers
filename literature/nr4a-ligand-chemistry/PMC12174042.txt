## Abstract
Multiple sclerosis (MS) is the consequence of early-onset inflammatory demyelination and progressive neurodegenerative lesions in the central nervous system (CNS). Despite the unclear pathogenesis of MS, two main processes occur, oligodendrocyte repair and lipid metabolism. Nuclear receptors have been observed to have a role as ligand sensors, regulators of transcriptional factors, and modulators of gene expression. One member of this family, the retinoid X receptor (RXR), which forms a heterodimeric complex with liver X receptor (LXR), have been implicated in the pathophysiology of MS. Moreover, it has been expressed that LXR receptors play pivotal role in the regulation of inflammation, oxidative stress responses, and cholesterol metabolism in phagocytes in active MS lesions. This review aimed to study role of RXR and LXR in MS pathogenesis.
Keywords: Multiple sclerosis, LXR, RXR, Inflammation, Neurodegeneration

## Introduction
MS, a neurological disease of the central nervous system, is prevalent in two million people worldwide. It was introduced by Jean-Martin Charcot in 1886 and its clinical and pathological features have been explained (Kumar et al. ). MS is an autoimmune disorder characterized by CNS inflammation and demyelination. Its pathogenesis is not clear yet, and studies have revealed that both genetic and environmental factors lead to its progression (Chen et al. ). The pathogenesis of MS involves two intertwined processes: inflammatory demyelination and progressive neurodegeneration. Both approaches are pivotal to disease onset. At the onset of the disease, the infiltration of circulating monocyte-derived macrophages indicates that inflammation is predominant in the early stages. Subsequently, a gradual transition occurs, which leads to demyelination and neurodegeneration. Despite profound achievements in MS medication, the potential mechanisms for reversing disease progression remain unclear (Sharma et al. ; Willems et al. ). While advancements in pharmacological treatments have provided symptomatic relief and slowed disease progression in multiple sclerosis (MS), there remains a critical need to develop therapies that target the underlying molecular mechanisms driving the disease. One promising avenue is the modulation of nuclear receptors (NRs), which play pivotal roles in regulating inflammation, lipid metabolism, and cellular homeostasis—processes that are profoundly dysregulated in MS. Among these NRs, liver X receptors (LXRs) and retinoid X receptors (RXRs) have emerged as key regulators of cholesterol metabolism and immune responses. LXRs, activated by oxysterols, promote cholesterol efflux and reduce inflammatory gene expression, while RXRs serve as obligate heterodimerization partners for LXRs and other NRs, amplifying their regulatory effects (Fellows Maxwell et al. ). The stimulation of these receptors has been shown to modulate microglial activation, astrocyte function, and endothelial cell integrity, all of which are implicated in MS pathogenesis (Lan et al. ). For instance, RXR agonists have been demonstrated to enhance the expression of ATP-binding cassette transporter A1 (ABCA1), a critical mediator of cholesterol efflux, in cultured endothelial cells, thereby reducing lipid accumulation and inflammation in vessel walls. By exploring the therapeutic potential of NRs, this review aims to bridge the gap between current MS treatments and emerging strategies that address both the inflammatory and degenerative components of the disease. Understanding the intricate interplay between NR signaling pathways and MS pathophysiology could pave the way for novel, multi-targeted therapies that not only alleviate symptoms but also halt or reverse disease progression.
After stimulating nuclear receptors by cytokines, peptides, and growth factors, they bind to the nuclear receptor (NR) to DNA to control cell growth and differentiation, and the metabolism of proteins produced by cell receptors changes (Almeida and Conda-Sheridan ). Nuclear receptors comprise a family of 48 members. These proteins serve as ligand sensors, transcription factors, and controllers of gene expression (Willems et al. ). Each NR has a common structural pattern, with an activation factor (AF-1) in the N-terminal domain (NTD), DNA-binding domain (DBD) in the middle, flexible hinge region, and activation factor 2 (AF-2) in the C-terminal ligand-binding domain (LBD) (Königshofer et al. ). NRs can be categorized into four groups based on their DNA-binding capacity and mechanism of action: Type 1 receptors are illustrated by steroid receptors and are homodimeric. They bind to DNA sequences, such as inverted repeats specific to hormones, including androgen (AR), estrogen (ER), progesterone (PR), and glucocorticoid (GR). Type 2 receptors, also known as nonsteroidal receptors, are primarily located in the nucleus. There are groups of these proteins that work together, such as retinoid X receptor (RXR) and coreceptor proteins, such as retinoic acid receptor (RAR), vitamin D receptor (VDR), thyroid hormone receptor (TR), nerve growth factor-induced gene B (NGFIB), and peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors (PPAR). This heterodimeric structure binds to specific DNA repeats and remains in the nucleus (Xie, et al. ). Type 3 receptors are homodimeric NRs that pair with identical NRs and bind to particular DNA repeats. Type 4 receptors directly bind to DNA as monomers or dimers, recognizing half-site hormone-response elements within the DNA sequence. The liver X receptor (LXR) and the retinoid X receptor (RXR) are both parts of the NR family as they do not belong to any of the receptor groups such as small heterodimer partner (SHP), testicular receptor 2 and 4 (TR2 and 4), photoreceptor-specific NR (PNR), and estrogen-related receptor (ERR). They are termed ‘orphan receptors’ because their specific ligands have not been identified. Furthermore, their specific ligands have not been identified (Almeida and Conda-Sheridan ). LXR is notably abundant in the adult brain, where it acts as an intrinsic sensor for intracellular cholesterol levels and plays an essential role in regulating cholesterol homeostasis. LXR and RXR can join together to form heterodimer complexes. This facilitates reverse cholesterol transport, which happens when extra cholesterol or cholesterol precursors, such as oxysterols, are taken out of the body. This mechanism helps maintain proper cholesterol balance in the body (Alnaaim ; Wang et al. ; Huang, et al. ). Thus, LXR plays a pivotal role in cholesterol metabolism and phospholipid homeostasis and is recognized as an anti-inflammatory regulator (He et al. ). It is also acknowledged for its anti-inflammatory properties by reducing the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, LXR agonists have been shown to protect neurons from damage caused by neuroinflammation and neurodegenerative diseases in vitro and in vivo models (Sacchetti et al. ). Furthermore, LXR knockout mice exhibit neurodegeneration, which is attributed to cholesterol-induced synaptic impairment and neuronal loss (Yang et al. ). Cholesterol metabolism plays a fundamental role in maintaining central nervous system (CNS) homeostasis, particularly in the context of myelin stability and neuronal function. In MS, dysregulation of cholesterol synthesis, transport, and efflux contributes to demyelination, neuroinflammation, and neurodegeneration. Myelin, the lipid-rich sheath surrounding axons, relies heavily on cholesterol for its structural integrity and efficient signal transmission. However, excessive cholesterol accumulation or impaired efflux can lead to toxic effects, including oxidative stress and inflammation, which exacerbate MS pathology. Oxysterols, oxidized derivatives of cholesterol, serve as natural ligands for LXRs and act as critical signaling molecules in the brain. For example, 24(S)-hydroxycholesterol (24-OHC), the most prevalent oxysterol in the brain, facilitates cholesterol turnover and has been implicated in both neuroprotective and neurotoxic processes. Notably, alterations in 24-OHC plasma levels have been observed in patients with neurodegenerative diseases, including MS, suggesting its potential as a biomarker and therapeutic target. Furthermore, neuroinflammation, a hallmark of MS, involves the activation of resident immune cells such as microglia and astrocytes, leading to the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, reactive oxygen species (ROS), and matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs). While neuroinflammation and neurodegeneration are distinct processes, they are interconnected in MS, with chronic inflammation contributing to neuronal damage and disease progression. Thus, targeting cholesterol metabolism and neuroinflammatory pathways through NR modulation offers a dual-pronged approach to managing MS. This review will explore the roles of LXRs and RXRs in regulating these pathways and their potential as therapeutic targets for mitigating disease severity. Given the critical role of cholesterol metabolism in MS and the involvement of nuclear receptors in modulating lipid homeostasis, this review explores RXR and LXR functions in MS pathogenesis, highlighting their potential as therapeutic targets.

## Cholesterol Homeostasis in MS
The brain contains 20% whole-body cholesterol and bile acids. CYP7 A1, the rate-limiting enzyme in bile acid synthesis, is expressed in the CNS (Bhargava et al. ). Cholesterol (C27H46O) is a sterol that is crucial for immune regulation and signaling and is the main component of myelin and the cell membrane in addition to being the precursor of steroid hormones and vitamin D (Lorincz et al. ). In blood circulation, cholesterol is transported by various lipoprotein particles that cannot cross the BBB (Gao and Li ). Thus, astrocytes synthesize most of the cholesterol in the brain from glucose, glutamine, or acetate derived from acetyl COA. In addition, it receives cholesterol from HDL-containing ApoE to promote regeneration of the peripheral nervous system (Villa et al. ). ApoE combines with apo A1 and LDLR to facilitate lipid transfer (Boyles et al. ). It has three major isotypes, apoE2 (cys 112, cys 158), apoE3 (cys 112, arg 158), and apoE4 (arg 112, arg 158) (Zhang et al. ). At high cholesterol levels, LXR and RXR heterodimers in astrocytes together help make cholesterol transport proteins. Therefore, ATP-binding cassette transporter A1 (ABCA1), a key regulator of cholesterol efflux, facilitates the transfer of cholesterol to ApoE-containing lipoproteins, thereby improving cholesterol clearance in MS (Borràs, et al. ). Additionally, studies have demonstrated that ApoE levels increase in microglia in aging or disease conditions, potentially altering cholesterol homeostasis in astrocytes, with the ApoE4 isoform being associated with dysregulated lipid metabolism (Tcw et al. ). ApoE isoforms are risk factors for cardiovascular, neurological, and infectious diseases. Carriers of two E4 alleles have a 9- to 15-fold higher risk of late-onset AD progression. In contrast, the E2 allele has a protective effect against Alzheimer’s. In addition, ApoE has been shown to have a higher expression level of ADP ribosylation of factor 6 (ARF6) (a protein involved in ABCA1 recycling) in the late endosome (Begcevic Brkovic, et al. ). Apo E3 is defined as neutral (Zhang et al. ).
The myelin membrane, which is made of cholesterol, is the target of immune cells, and in MS disease, neurons of the CNS are demyelinated as their primary component is cholesterol (Gao and Li ; Berghoff et al. ). After acute injury, cholesterol is recovered from myelin debris and transported to astrocytes and endothelial cells. This decline in cholesterol production improved the production of divergent oxysterols. In addition, local cholesterol synthesis is necessary for chronic trauma and demyelination. In the pathological state, myelin repair is suppressed (Berghoff et al. ). Pajoohesh Ganji et al. reported that blocked myelin requirement might be associated with the impact of myelin debris in the microenvironment on the differentiation of the OPC cell (Pajoohesh-Ganji et al. ). Berghoff et al. suggested that during the MS repair phase, microglia are required to eliminate cholesterol-rich myelin debris and change the cell environment from lower inflammation to inhibiting it so that oligodendrocyte differentiation can occur (Gao and Li ) (Fig.  ).
 Janet K. Boyles’ research declared that the nerve is repaired at the beginning of cholesterol transport mechanism. This study suggests that Boyles et al. suggested that cholesterol transfer mechanisms play a crucial role in axon recovery and remyelination by supplying the necessary lipids for myelin repair (Boyles et al. ). CYP27 A1 also changes cholesterol into 27-hydoxycholestrol (27-OHC) in the bloodstream, while CYP46 A1 converts 24-hydroxycholestrol, which is the most oxysterol in the brain and spinal cord, and it has been proposed that the 24-OHC plasma level could be raised or diminished in patients with neurodegenerative disease in the early and late stages of the illness, respectively (Leoni and Caccia ). It has been proposed that 24-OHC plasma levels could be raised or reduced in patients with neurodegenerative disease in the early and late stages of the illness, respectively (Nury et al. ). In the case of neurodegenerative diseases, 24-OHC leaks into the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) and hurts fat cells in the brain and spinal cord, mainly oligodendrocytes (Vejux et al. ). This changes the breakdown of cholesterol and the formation of myelin. Since 24-OHC damages fats, there would be less lanosterol, lathosterol, and desmosterol, which are cholesterol precursors. Because oligodendrocytes provide a large amount of cholesterol for the myelin sheath, when their metabolism goes wrong, it causes neurons to lose myelin, experience oxidative stress, and die. In addition, oligodendrocyte differentiation is suppressed by myelin debris and inflammatory conditions (Table  ).
 

## RXR Family
Retinoic acid receptor (RAR) and retinoid X receptor (RXR) are two prominent retinoid families. Each family consists of three subtypes, α, β, and γ. These subtypes may exhibit splicing variants that can potentially lead to cancer or neurodegenerative diseases such as MS and Alzheimer’s disease (Hoog, et al. ; Andavar et al. ). The α isoform consists of 462 amino acids, with a molecular weight of 50,811 Da, and is placed on chromosome 9q34.2. It is found in the nucleus, cytoplasm, and mitochondria of cells. Alternative splicing can result in heterogeneity. A considerable subtype is typically present in the kidneys, liver, and intestine (Lefebvre et al. ). The β isoform is primarily located on chromosome 6p21.32, close to the HLA class II gene (Numasawa et al. ). In addition, it has been identified in endothelial cells and monocytes and is predominantly observed in various cancer cell lines (Sharma et al. ). The γ isoform is primarily localized in the gland, muscle, and brain. It spans 14 kb in length, contains nine introns with sizes ranging from 107 base pairs to 30 kb, and is situated on chromosome 1q23.3 (Sharma et al. ; Almeida and Conda-Sheridan ). RXR functions as a ligand-dependent transcription factor and regulates inflammation, metabolism, and immune response (Ting et al. ). In BALBcByJ mice, RXRγ-/-KO mice exhibit memory loss and depressive behavior (Wietrzych-Schindler et al. ; Kamrani et al. ). According to Zhou et al., RXR can play a role in suppressing inflammation by managing the NF-KB pathway or activating c-Jun kinase as a consequence of the c-Jun phosphorylation mechanism (Zhou et al. ; Zhang et al. ). However, the role of RXR signaling remains unclear. There are two primary types of RXR heterodimers. The first involves ligand-associated or permissive heterodimerization. These dimers work when they connect to RXR agonists or NR partners such as RXR/PPARγ, RXR/FXR, or RXR/LXR (Fig.  ).
 These associations can enhance a synergistic biological response, resulting in the release of co-repressor proteins. In the second group, non-permissive heterodimerization, an agonist binds to the heterodimerization partner and initiates transcriptional activity. In this scenario, RXR acts as a subordinate or silent partner, as seen in the RXR/VDR and RXR/TR examples. RXR can bind and connect with coactivator proteins, whereas co-repressor proteins are released only when they bind to an RXR partner (Almeida and Conda-Sheridan ; Modi et al. ). Homodimers consist of RXR-RXRs. As a part of the heterodimeric complex, RXR is important for controlling transcriptional activity, especially in cell differentiation and metabolic processes, such as glucose and lipid metabolism. For example, heterodimerization occurs when RXR interacts with RAR (RXR-RAR) or another nuclear receptor. When it comes to RAR, RXR-RAR binds to a specific DNA sequence called RARE, which is found in the regulatory sequence of the target gene. Depending on the type of cell, promoter, and DNA-binding site, different cofactors control RXR-RAR activity. Similar to Jurutka, et al. ( ); Osz et al. ) Zhuang et al., 9-cis retinoic acid (9-cis-RA), a naturally occurring isomer of all-trans-retinoic acid (ATRA), is an RXR-specific ligand that acts as an agonist by binding to RXR and forcing the formation of RXR homodimers. This then causes RXR-responsive elements (RXREs) to activate or inhibit genes in the promoter region (Zhang et al. ). Metabolism happens in the central nervous system (CNS) and peripheral nervous system (PNS)(Sharma et al. ). In addition, 9-cis retinoic acid significantly enhanced remyelination in vivo after toxin-induced demyelination of cerebellar peduncles (Hanafy and Sloane ; Sadat Razavi, et al. ; Mehrnia ) (Table  ).
 

## RXR Homeostasis in Cholesterol Metabolism
Cholesterol, the most important sterol in mammals, is pivotal in fluidity, permeability, and signaling in cell membranes, and has divergent aspects in immunity and inflammation (Endo-Umeda and Makishima ). Cholesterol transportation is a vital mechanism for cellular homeostasis. ATP-binding cassette transporters (ABC), particularly ABCA1, ABCG1, ABCG4, and ABCG5, are responsible for transporting cholesterol. ABCA1 and ABCG1 are crucial cholesterol transporters in several cell types. They facilitate the removal of cholesterol and inhibit foam cell formation (Seeree et al. ). ABCA1 is mostly expressed in the liver, intestine, brain, and macrophages (Chen et al. ; Shamabadi et al. ). In a process called reverse cholesterol transport (RCT), extra cholesterol and phospholipids move from the cell to apolipoprotein A (apo) and apo E, which are the main components of HDL. Therefore, Cholesterol is transferred to nascent or mature high-density lipoprotein (HDL). These transporters, such as RXR, PPARγ, and LXR, are stimulated by NR transcription (Xu et al. ; Zhao et al. ). In vitro studies have demonstrated that ABCA1 and ABCG1 in the cell membrane are necessary to eliminate cholesterol and protect neurons by suppressing cell death, inflammation, and oxidative stress (Koldamova et al. ). Moreover, RXR agonists have been shown to enhance ABCA1 expression in cultured endothelial cells (Manzano et al. ; Kolseth et al. ). Furthermore, these RXR agonists have been shown to suppress inflammation in cultured microglia cells.
The ABCA1 transporter regulates lipid metabolism and axonal growth via its interaction with LDLR (Borràs, et al. ; Chen et al. ). In addition, ABCA1 dysfunction in the brain is associated with reduced apo E levels (Wolf et al. ). The liver has role in apoE secretion in lipid metabolism to enter the blood circulation, and apoE is mainly expressed and secreted by astrocytes. These astrocyte functions are controlled by LXR and RXR transcription factors, which boost the immune system during inflammation (Fernández-Calle et al. ). In addition, earlier studies have demonstrated that ABCG4 transfers cholesterol from cells to HDL and causes cell death. Several sterols, such as cholesterol, 7β-hydroxycholesterol, 7-ketocholesterol, and desmosterol, have been reported to bind to ABCG1 in cells that express ABCG1 (Xu et al. ; Hegyi et al. ; Motavaselian et al. ). The regulation of the immune response by the LXR and RXR transcription factors in astrocytes suggests their crucial role in the inflammatory process. Additionally, the identification of various sterols as substrates of ABCG1 highlights the diverse functions of this transporter in cholesterol transport and cell apoptosis.

## RXR Signaling Role in Neuroinflammation
Neuroinflammation is a process that can contribute to neurodegeneration but should not be equated with it. It represents a response to disrupted homeostasis in the central nervous system (CNS), often triggered by pathogens or tissue injury, and involves various cells, including astrocytes and microglia (Teleanu, et al. ). Neuroinflammation is triggered by four essential factors: macrophage activation (microglia), increment of cytokine and chemokine levels, recruitment of peripheral immune cells, and local tissue damage (Woodburn et al. ). At the inflammatory response beginning in the brain, the higher level of peripheral leukocyte infiltration and immunogenic molecules, microglia, the product pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-16, TNF-a, IL-18, matrix metalloproteinase (MMP), reactive nitrogen species (RNS), reactive oxygen species (ROS), and chemokine (CCL2) will be started (Woodburn et al. ). Microglia, as resident brain macrophages, are capable of phagocytosis due to their amoeboid morphology, cleaning foreign organisms, and cellular debris, and increasing neurotoxicity by production of ROS via NADPH oxidase (Teleanu, et al. ; Jayaraj et al. ).
Astrocytes are housekeeping cells and have an obligation to regulate the CNS. Thus, they are involved in homeostasis, neurotrophic factor release, clearing transmitters, shuttle metabolites, and BBB formation (Jayaraj et al. ). Therefore, neuroinflammation and oxidative status can synergistically affect each other (Teleanu, et al. ).
RXR signaling has a neuroprotective role in dopaminergic neurons. The heterodimeric partner of RXR, NURR1, has been shown to improve dopaminergic neurons and several fields in the hippocampus and cerebral cortex (Wietrzych-Schindler et al. ). Moreover, it heterodimerizes RXR with a permissive partner to determine the unfolded protein response (UPR) and ER stress (Aranda and Pascual ).
RXR prompts PPARγ to manage oxidative stress responses and suppresses autophagy to improve NRF2 expression in epileptic conditions in vivo. Moreover, previous studies have shown that the PPARγ/RXR complex alleviates the mitochondrial stress response by increasing the expression of UCP2, eNOS, and mn SOD scavenger protein (Wietrzych-Schindler et al. ; Yu et al. ).
NURR1, a highly expressed nuclear receptor, is heterodimerized in the CNS with RXR, and this complex stimulates ligand LG-268 to induce neuroprotection in ventral midbrain cultures (Wallen-Mackenzie et al. ). It appears that synthesized NURR1/RXR/HX600 agonists can repress the expression of microglial pro-inflammatory cytokines and anticipate cell death due to inflammation (Loppi et al. ). Furthermore, this transcription complex is triggered in midbrain dopaminergic and Serotonergic neurogenesis correlated with the stimulation of PI3 TX3 and Wnt/β-catenin signaling (Zhang et al. ; Xu et al. ). Vassilatis et al. found that exposing the NURR1/RXR-α heterodimer as monotherapy for Parkinson’s disease may have a double function by expanding striatal dopamine levels and upregulating the NURR1 target gene (Spathis et al. ; Lou et al. ). Likewise, it suppressed pro-inflammatory genes in LPS-treated astrocytes and microglia via the NF-KB pathway. It can be mentioned that RXRα separately impedes the β-catenin transcription and adjusts the WNT/β-catenin cascade in colon cancer, so this axis RXR- β-catenin is reported as a potential therapeutic target (Ruan et al. ).

## RXR in MS
Expression of the retinoid X receptor (RXR), specifically RXR-γ, has been observed in MS lesions, and it has been noted that RXRs are activated within phagocytes in these lesions. These findings imply that RXR-γ is a significant physiological indicator of damage in acute injury in brain (Huang et al. ). RXR signaling is of great significance in remyelination and is closely linked to the regenerative reaction of the CNS. RXR-γ is typically expressed at nominal levels in all glial cells of intact mammalian CNS (Fig.  ).
 However, after CNS injury, its expression intensifies in triggered microglia or macrophages, reactive astrocytes, and oligodendrocytes (Moreno et al. ; Schrage et al. ). The capacity of RXR to heterodimerize with a multitude of nuclear receptors implies that RXR can regulate the expression of a diverse array of genes, contingent on the timing and hetrodimerized receptor. RXR can form permissive and non-permissive heterodimers with various nuclear receptors (Altucci et al. ). Possible partners of permissive RXR-γ heterodimers include RXR-α, RXR-β, LXRα, COUP-TFI, and Nurr1 (Huang et al. ). Upregulation of LXR response genes, which serve as downstream targets of RXR, has been observed in phagocytes in active MS lesions. This finding suggests that RXR stimulation occurs in actively demyelinating phagocytes within MS lesions (Mailleux et al. ).
The obligatory formation of heterodimer complexes between liver X receptor alpha (LXR-α) and retinoid X receptors (RXR) is a significant aspect of molecular biology. These heterodimers bind to LXR response elements (LXREs) and subsequently regulate gene expression. Notably, the interactions between LXR-LBDs and RXRα-LBD can be considerably influenced by both LXR and RXR ligands (Yue et al. ; Wang et al. ).
The mediation of various facets of lipid metabolism is attributed to RXR and its associated partners, PPAR and LXR. The regulation of gene product expression for cholesterol transport, de novo synthesis, and catabolism is mediated by both LXR α and LXR β (Prüfer and Boudreaux ). The impact of LXR/RXR heterodimers on cholesterol metabolism in MS occurs through diverse mechanisms. The regulation of genes involved in cholesterol transport is overseen by LXR/RXR heterodimers (Murthy et al. ) as well as the regulation of genes implicated in de novo cholesterol production. LXRs are transcription factors sensitive to cholesterol, and their activation results in the upregulation of genes that mediate cholesterol (Zelcer and Tontonoz ). Moreover, LXR/RXR heterodimers have been found to significantly affect cholesterol catabolism by controlling the expression of genes involved in bile acid synthesis. Notably, the gene responsible for encoding 7α-hydroxylase, a crucial enzyme involved in bile acid synthesis, has been identified as a direct target of LXRs (Svensson et al. ). LXRs are crucial components in the integration of metabolic and inflammatory signaling pathways. The regulation of genes implicated in cholesterol metabolism and inflammation profoundly affects the metabolic state and immune response (Zelcer and Tontonoz ). According to previous research, LXRs, which necessitate the formation of a heterodimer with RXR, facilitate the promotion of myelin and remyelination in the cerebellum. This suggests a plausible role for LXR/RXR heterodimers in the regulation of myelination processes (Meffre et al. ). The investigation of RXR agonists, such as IRX4204, as a prospective therapy for MS and additional autoimmune disorders, is currently under investigation. This suggests that targeting RXR signaling may have therapeutic ramifications for treating MS (Chandraratna et al. ). The significance of RXR in MS is intricately linked to its dimerization with LXRα, and its value as a therapeutic target (Wang et al. ). The immunomodulatory effects of RXR agonists on human cells have been identified, and a novel RXR agonist is currently under development for treating MS and other autoimmune diseases (Gaunt et al. ) (Table  ).
 

## LXR
LXR, first discovered in the liver, has two isotypes, LXRα and LXRβ, which are encoded by NR1H3 and NR1H2, respectively. These two nuclear receptors function in hepatic lipid metabolism and are essential for lipogenesis because of their response to SREBP1, FASN, and SCD1 regulators. It also plays a role in phospholipid remodeling by controlling LPCAT3 expression. Moreover, other tissues control cholesterol homeostasis; hence, by heterodimerization with RXR and conjugation with a DNA sequence named LXR response element (LXREs) in the regulatory domain of the target gene, it induces genes involved in cholesterol homeostasis, including ABCA1, ABCG5, and ABCG8, and blocks LDL uptake via the IDL, thus improving cholesterol transfer to the bile salt. Stimulation of LXR by various types of oxysterols (such as 24(s)−25-epoxycholestrol, 20(s) hydroxy cholesterol, 22R-hydroxycholestrol, 24(s) hydroxy cholesterol, 7α-hydroxycholesterol, 25-hydroxycholstrol, and 27-hydroxyxholestrol) can induce lipoprotein gene expression, contain phospholipid transfer protein (PLTP), lipoprotein lipase (LPL), CETP, and apo E (Endo-Umeda and Makishima ; Griffett and Burris ) (Fig.  ).
 Furthermore, LXR is involved in inflammation through a different mechanism, suppressing pro-inflammatory genes such as pro-inflammatory cytokines IL-1β and IL-6, inducible nitric oxide synthase (INOS), cyclooxygenase 2 (COX2), or signal-dependent transcription factors like the NF-KB pathway and activator protein 1 (Matalonga et al. ; Voisin, et al. ; Ramírez et al. ). Because LXR and its ligand exist in oligodendrocytes, mice lacking LXR had lower motor coordination and spatial learning, as well as a thinner myelin sheath, whereas stimulation of LXR by its ligand (whether 25-OHC or synthetic TO901317) resulted in higher expression of myelin gene and protein levels. Therefore, LXR controls the expression of myelin genes. Additionally, stimulation of LXR improves oligodendrocyte cell maturation and remyelination after induced demyelination in organotypic cerebellar slice cultures (Meffre et al. ). A study confirmed that mice with LXRα/β double knockout had some weakness in axonal atrophy and neuronal loss in ventral midbrain neurogenesis (Sacchetti et al. ). Beaven SW, in an in vitro and in vivo study, has shown that isolated HSC (hepatic satellite cell) LXR-/- animals had increased levels of collα1 and other pro-inflammatory cytokines (acta2, PDGFβ, and CCL2) compared to wild-type animals. Further analysis showed that exposure to a CCL4 or methionine-choline-deficient diet in LXR-/- mice increased α-SMA and collagen, respectively. Subsequently, LXR in wild-type HSC resulted in decreased levels of CCL2 and IL-6 and repressed the expression of the collα1 and acta genes (Beaven et al. ). Another role of LXR is the upregulation of the glucose transporter GLUT4 (Ikonen ); it has been shown that LXRα plays a significant role in the expression of GLUT4 in adipocytes and is a potential target in diabetes treatment (Bhattarai et al. ).
LXRα is primarily expressed in the liver, kidney, intestine, macrophages, and adipose tissues (Griffett and Burris ; Xie et al. ). Its gene is located on the short arm of chromosome 11 (11p11.2), which encodes a protein with 447 amino acids (Zhao et al. ). These capacities as cholesterol sensors were illustrated in a study in which LXRα-invalid mice were bolstered by high cholesterol and ate less. As for disability in LXR-dependent action, they cannot exchange cholesterol for bile salt (Peet et al. ). Likewise, another study showed that the loss of LXRα leads to cholesterol accumulation and a deficiency of cholesterol in bile acid (Wang and Tontonoz ). In contrast, LXRβ is expressed in several tissues. The LXR β gene is situated on the long arm of chromosome 19 (19q13.3), which encodes a protein with 460 amino acids (Zhao et al. ; Wang and Tontonoz ).

## LXR in Cholesterol Homeostasis
The two main pathways regulating cholesterol metabolism are the LXR and transcription factor SREBPs. With a decrease in intracellular cholesterol, SREBPs enter the nucleus to trigger the transcription of adipose and cholesterol synthesis-related genes to ameliorate the levels of cellular lipids and steroids. High levels of oxysterol or its metabolites in cells stimulate LXR transcription. Thus, LXR and SREBP organize a negative feedback loop to control cellular cholesterol metabolism (Guo et al. ). The other lipogenic process of LXR is performed with the commencement of SREBP1-c transcription. Increasing cholesterol levels can initiate LXR, and then LXR can activate SREBPs to stimulate fat synthesis (Yan, et al. ). As a result of LXR activation, the expression of ABCA1 and ABCG1 promotes cholesterol efflux and manage degradation of the LDL receptor by modifying E3 ubiquitin ligase to diminish cholesterol uptake (Guo et al. ).
24(s)-hydroxy cholesterol (24-OHC), the major oxysterol in the brain, is produced by hydroxylation of cholesterol by 24-hydroxylase (CYP46 A1), and this 24-OHC is cleaved from a neuron in an ABCA1-dependent pathway. It can also affect either pro-survival or pro-death factors in the neurons. Under high-concentration conditions of 24-OHC, LXR transcription is suppressed to ameliorate necroptosis-like death pathways in neurons (Endo-Umeda and Makishima ). Evidence has revealed that 24-OHC and 25-epoxycholestrol play a role in the development of midbrain dopaminergic neurogenesis through LXR (Theofilopoulos et al. ; Theofilopoulos et al. ; Wang et al. ) (Table  ).
 

## LXR Signaling in Neuroinflammation
The primary source of cholesterol in the brain arises from de novo biosynthesis. In the CNS of adult individuals, a significant proportion of cholesterol is situated in the lipid-rich myelin membranes (Björkhem et al. ; Dietschy ). The nuclear receptors play a fundamental role in various cellular mechanisms and are responsible for translating various metabolic, hormonal, and nutritional signals into alterations in gene expression (Calkin and Tontonoz ). LXRs play a crucial role in the regulation of the overall cholesterol homeostasis of the body, and oxysterol, being the natural ligand of LXR, effectively stimulates its activation (Calkin and Tontonoz ). Neuroinflammation is widely acknowledged as a crucial factor in the initiation and progression of neurodegenerative mechanisms, and several scholars have posited that the foundation of therapeutic intervention should involve the modulation of neuroinflammation (Bastias-Candia et al. ) (Fig.  ).
 LXR agonists have demonstrated via the inhibition of Nuclear Factor Kappa B (NF-κB), a pivotal controller of inflammatory reactions, a propensity to diminish cerebral harm and mitigate neuroinflammatory response in intracerebral hemorrhage experimental models (Wu et al. ). LXR agonists repress neutrophil migration genes and neutrophil recruitment during inflammation, emphasizing the possible role of LXR agonists in suppressing inflammation (Thomas, et al. ). The LXRs are an essential signaling junction that connects lipid metabolism, inflammation, and neuroinflammation (Schulman ). In macrophages and other immune cells, the transcriptional role of pro-inflammatory genes is effectively repressed by LXRs (Schulman ). LXR agonists can repress the inflammatory responses elicited by microglia and astrocytes in the CNS. LXRs diminish the generation of pro-inflammatory agents, including IL-1β, IL-6, NO, iNOS, and MCP-1, in both microglia and astrocytes (Song et al. ). The isoforms of LXRα and LXRβ have been discovered to play a crucial role in preserving blood–brain barrier (BBB) function. Elevated activation of the LXR may be a potential treatment for intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) and promote neuroprotection. This is achieved via the modulation of the cytotoxic activities of neuroinflammatory cells and, likewise, the regulation of BBB function during a neuroinflammatory insult (Wu et al. ; Wouters et al. ). Recent research has depicted the potential impact of LXR signaling on the pathology of Alzheimer’s disease (AD). By triggering LXR, it is possible to manage neuroinflammation and reduce the accumulation of amyloid-β peptide, thus offering promising therapeutic options for AD (Kang and Rivest ). The anti-inflammatory characteristic of LXRs may alleviate the capacity of microglia to phagocytose fAβ within an enduring inflammatory environment, thereby diminishing neuroinflammatory responses and attenuating the progression of Alzheimer’s disease pathology (Zelcer et al. ).
LXR regulates inflammatory gene expression in different ways: LXRs, through their activation and repression mechanisms, govern various facets of inflammatory gene expression in macrophages (Noelia and Castrillo ). Activation of LXR promotes cholesterol efflux and the repression of inflammatory genes, resulting in anti-atherogenic consequences (Thomas, et al. ).
Commencement of LXR has the potential to regulate inflammation in the CNS and impede the activity of nitric oxide synthase 2 in microglia (Secor McVoy et al. ). Previous research has indicated that LXR knockout animals exhibited heightened clinical disease severity during EAE. This phenomenon was attributed to the absence of inhibitory influence on lymphocyte Th17 lineage differentiation. Hence, it is apparent that LXR exerts physiological regulation over peripheral lymphocyte activation in the induction phase of EAE (Secor McVoy et al. ). LXRs are the main regulators of cholesterol homeostasis and inflammation in the CNS. LXRs probably serve as sensors that intricately regulate cholesterol turnover within the brain (Courtney and Landreth ). The LXRs are crucial in regulating the basal and inducible expression of cholesterol homeostatic genes in the brain. Furthermore, they exhibit remarkable potency as inhibitors of inflammatory gene expression (Zelcer et al. ). LXRs, due to their cholesterol-sensing and anti-inflammatory activities, hold a central position in the routine maintenance of CNS function (Courtney and Landreth ) (Table  ).
 

## LXR in MS
Cholesterol, the most dominant lipid present within myelin, performs a pivotal obligation in the myelination process (Saher et al. ). The Breakdown of myelin leads to an aberrant metabolism of cholesterol in the CNS among individuals with MS. This is evidenced by changes in the plasma concentrations of CNS-specific cholesterol metabolites (Kraats et al. ; Karrenbauer et al. ). Macrophages and resident microglia result in myelin phagocytosis, leading to an elevation in cholesterol absorption and the subsequent formation of intracellular oxysterols. Notably, metabolites of the cholesterol biosynthetic pathway serve as natural ligands for the LXR, stimulating cellular sterol efflux (Zhao and Dahlman-Wright ). CNS and immune cells express the LXRα and LXRβ isoforms (Fan et al. ; Joseph et al. ).
LXR signaling, particularly LXR activation, may play a role in MS by controlling cholesterol and lipid metabolism, modulating the inflammatory response, and potentially influencing disease progression (Wang et al. ). The upregulation of LXR response genes in phagocytes in active MS lesions indicates the stimulation of LXRs in actively demyelinating phagocytes. These data suggest the involvement of LXRs in the pathogenesis of MS (Mailleux et al. ). The activation of LXR in phagocytes within MS lesions is correlated with the absorption of myelin and the consequent generation of LXR ligands (Mailleux et al. ) in such a way that disruption of myelin by phagocytes is a crucial process in the development of MS (Mailleux et al. ). The myelin sheath is a layer abundant in cholesterol that envelops nerves. MS and other demyelinating diseases are characterized by the impairment of this safeguarding layer and the resulting deceleration of nerve impulses (Spady and Dietschy ). The breakdown of myelin leads to changes in the plasma levels of oxysterols specific to the central nervous system. These oxysterols serve as natural ligands for transcription factors known as LXRs. The process of myelin uptake by macrophages results in the activation of LXR in phagocytes by increasing the concentration of 27-hydroxycholesterol (27-OHC) and subsequently inducing response genes of LXR (ABCA1, ABCG1))(Ghisletti et al. ; Joseph et al. ). LXRs play a crucial role in the regulation of myelination and remyelination in oligodendrocytes by regulating the expression of genes involved in myelination, including proteolipid protein (PLP), myelin basic protein (MBP), and ATP-binding cassette subfamily G member 1 (ABCA1) (Song et al. ).
LXRs control the regulation of endogenous cholesterol levels in individuals with MS. The disruption of LXR-mediated pathways probably plays a role in the pathogenesis of MS (Pineda-Torra et al. ; Podbielska et al. ). The potential of LXR agonists has been demonstrated in various experimental models of Multiple Sclerosis. These agonists can alter the expression of cytokines and other immune mediators, as well as polarizing immune cells, ultimately improving clinical symptoms (Pineda-Torra et al. ).
Mutations affecting the NR1H3 gene, responsible for encoding LXRα, have been linked to familial MS, potentially impairing transcriptional regulation (Wang et al. ). The gene LXRα exhibits high levels of expression within the hepatic, adipose, intestinal, and differentiated macrophage tissues (A-Gonzalez, N.,, et al. ).
Certain findings have indicated that inducing LXRs in phagocytes could potentially have a favorable impact on the advancement of MS. The activation of LXR can modify the expression of cytokines and other immune mediators, consequently guiding immune cells toward anti-inflammatory phenotypes (Mailleux et al. ). The LXRs can reduce inflammatory responses in both macrophages and microglia. This suggests that the LXRs can regulate neuroinflammation and microglial activation (Mailleux et al. ).
The modulation of inflammation, stimulation of oligodendrocyte repair, and regulation of lipid metabolism are the core functions that LXR performs in MS. The activation of LXR has been identified as a promising therapeutic target for MS (Wang et al. ; Pineda-Torra et al. ; Jäger ) (Table  ).
 

## Discussion
As a novel approach to controlling complicated central nervous system disorders, the RXR and LXR signaling pathways have the ability to be therapeutic targets in MS and other neurodegenerative diseases. Their utility in creating new treatments is highlighted by the interaction between these nuclear receptors and the metabolic and inflammatory pathways. Notably, RXR has a multi-functional role in regulating physiological processes ranging from oxidative stress reduction to cholesterol efflux due to its heterodimerization with several receptors (including LXR, PPAR, and NURR1). Because of their wide variety of receptor interactions, RXR agonists are well suited to treating the various pathological aspects of MS because they influence a wide range of biological processes. An additional critical component of RXR and LXR’s function in MS is its influence on central nervous system cholesterol metabolism, specifically in myelination and myelin repair. Myelin sheaths, which encase neurons and allow them to communicate and be insulated, necessitate a high concentration of cholesterol, which the central nervous system produces from new sources. One possible strategy for repairing the damaged myelin found in MS could be to activate LXR, which promotes cholesterol efflux and myelination. Microglia are involved in cleaning myelin debris as part of myelin repair. LXR activates genes that are involved in cholesterol metabolism, which helps microglia clear debris effectively and keeps cells healthy. Accurate regulation is necessary to prevent side effects in non-targeted tissues when transforming these systems into successful medicines. Their significance in MS is further enhanced by the hereditary component of RXR and LXR signaling. As an example, changes in NR1H3, linked to LXRα, can affect the way the disease progresses in familial form of multiple sclerosis, indicating that hereditary variables can influence how the receptor works. These results suggest that genetic screening and other tailored treatment approaches could improve therapeutic outcomes by adapting therapies to each patient’s unique genetic profile. Additionally, learning about RXR and LXR polymorphisms could help in optimizing treatment options by shedding light on the diversity in patient responses to receptor-targeted medications.
The systemic roles of RXR and LXR agonists pose obstacles that must be addressed in clinical applications, despite the fact that they have great therapeutic promise. The extensive expression of RXRs and LXRs across several organs and tissues makes their broad activation a potential cause of systemic metabolic alterations or immune suppression, which could make the safety profile of these medicines more complex. The development of tailored delivery methods capable of selectively activating the RXR and LXR pathways inside the central nervous system is, hence, an area of active research. The potential for CNS-specific therapeutic advantages to be maximized while peripheral side effects are minimized using this method. In order to overcome these obstacles and enable the clinical usage of RXR/LXR-based therapeutics, advancements in nanotechnology and CNS-targeted drug delivery could be crucial. The role of RXR and LXR in conjunction with additional neuroprotective and anti-inflammatory drugs is another field of investigation. Combining RXR/LXR agonists with other medications may have a synergistic impact, especially in treating relapses or advance stages of MS, because they lower neuroinflammatory reactions. By tackling numerous pathogenic processes at once, combination therapy may improve anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective effects, offering a more all-encompassing method of treating multiple sclerosis. There is hope for the development of multimodal therapeutics that address the complex pathophysiology of MS through the ongoing research of RXR/LXR combination with other anti-inflammatory drugs. Finally, as a means to regulate inflammatory response, lipid metabolism, and the immunological response, RXR and LXR nuclear receptors show tremendous potential as MS treatment targets. Additional study is needed to create tailored techniques that reduce systemic effects, improve delivery to central nervous system tissues, and account for genetic factors in patient-specific responses, in order to fully utilize their therapeutic potential. These developments pave the way for RXR and LXR signaling pathways to play a central role in MS treatment, opening up new possibilities for the management of this difficult neurodegenerative illness.

## Conclusion
The significance of nuclear receptors, particularly RXR and LXR, in the development and treatment of MS highlights the multi-faceted nature of neurodegenerative diseases’ effects on myelination, neuroinflammation, and cholesterol metabolism. Nuclear receptors have a role in the control of cellular processes that impact the development of the disease and possible treatment paths. Because of their roles in anti-inflammatory pathways and cholesterol homeostasis, LXRs and RXRs are potential targets for regulating neuroinflammatory and neurodegenerative reactions. The RXR/LXR pathways have the potential to hinder the progression of MS and aid in CNS repair processes due to their unique capacity to control gene expression associated with lipid metabolism, inflammation, and immune response. Additionally, these receptors help keep the blood–brain barrier intact and influence immune cell activity in the central nervous system, both of which mitigate the impact of neuroinflammatory insults in multiple sclerosis.
In addition to multiple sclerosis, other neurodegenerative illnesses may be amenable to the therapeutic effects of RXR and LXR agonists due to their effects on inflammation reduction, myelin regeneration, and metabolic regulation. In order to combat myelin loss and encourage remyelination in multiple sclerosis, it is crucial to understand how LXRs regulate cholesterol efflux and myelination. Similarly, RXRs are a diverse target in the therapy of neuroinflammatory diseases due to their ability to form heterodimers with other nuclear receptors. This allows them to control a broad spectrum of cellular responses. The RXR/LXR pathway’s potential in MS treatment is bolstered by its roles in oxidative stress response regulation, microglial activation reduction, and inflammatory cytokine production management. However, there are obstacles to the practical use of RXR and LXR-based therapies, such as the requirement for selective targeting of CNS cells without harmful effects on peripheral tissues. The extensive expression of RXRs and LXRs in different tissues makes this specificity all the more important, since incorrect activation could cause unwanted side effects. Also, genetic factors like NR1H3 mutations in MS families suggest that genetic profiling could improve treatment outcomes by enabling more targeted therapies tailored to each patient’s unique genetic makeup. To fully use the therapeutic potential of RXR/LXR pathways while limiting dangers associated with broad systemic activation, advancements in targeted delivery methods are necessary. Additionally, the promotion of anti-inflammatory phenotypes in immune cells is evidence that the RXR and LXR pathways contribute to immune regulation. An important symptom of MS is the infiltration of immune cells from the periphery into the central nervous system (CNS). This immune modulation has the potential to lessen this inflammatory-induced damage. Further investigation into the mechanisms by which RXR and LXR agonists can reduce neuroinflammatory responses and enhance neuroprotective activities by targeting microglia and astrocytes specifically is warranted. Further investigation into the interconnections between RXR and LXR and other receptors is encouraging, since it may lead to synergistic therapeutic results when used in conjunction with other medications for MS. To summarize, RXR and LXR regulate cholesterol levels, neuroinflammation, and immune cell activity, three distinct but related biological processes that play a part in MS management. Researchers hope that by focusing on these receptors, they can alleviate some of the many symptoms of MS, including the disease’s progressive nature, the impact on quality of life, and the frequency of relapses. It is crucial to keep investigating the mechanisms and therapeutic uses of RXR and LXR since their research shows great potential for creating sophisticated, multi-targeted treatments that deal with the inflammatory and degenerative components of multiple sclerosis.
