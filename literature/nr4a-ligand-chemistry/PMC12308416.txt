## Abstract
In situ electrocatalytic conversion of low-concentration
carbon
dioxide (CO2) from anaerobic fermentation gas (AFG) into
high-value chemical products can effectively mitigate the costs associated
with carbon capture, enrichment, and transfer. In this study, we synthesized
a guanidine-based porous organic polymer (G-POPs-1), which efficiently
enriches and activates diluted CO2 to form a “carbon-pool”.
Subsequently, through indirect electromediated oxidation, the 4-pentenamine
substrate underwent sequential cyclical reactions to produce proline
carbamate. Moreover, after six cycles of use, G-POPs-1 exhibited minimal
degradation in catalytic performance, with its microstructure remaining
intact.
Keywords: guanidine, low-concentration CO2
, anaerobic fermentation gas, indirect electrooxidation

## Introduction
To mitigate the severe impacts of global
warming, reducing anthropogenic
carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions has become an urgent and
critical priority. Current strategies
emphasize the promotion of renewable energy sources and the optimization
of industrial processes to minimize the CO2 emission. Furthermore,
CO2 capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS) technologies
are receiving considerable attention and are subject to extensive
research.
−

To capture CO2 from exhaust gases and convert it
into
high-value products, traditional methods typically rely on adjustments
in the temperature or pressure.
,
 Electrochemical approaches,
which are both environmentally friendly and energy-efficient, demonstrate
superior performance.
−

 Conventional electrochemical systems often use an amine-containing
solution to concentrate CO2; however, regenerating this
solution is energy-intensive ( a). Direct electrolysis of captured CO2 can
bypass the desorption step, thereby saving energy and reducing operational
costs ( b). Nevertheless, CO2 electrochemical
reduction frequently encounters challenges such as hydrogen evolution,
low mass transfer efficiency, and reduced Faraday efficiency, particularly
at low concentrations CO2.
−

 Efficient media, including covalent
organic frameworks (COFs),
,
 metal–organic
frameworks (MOFs),
−

 ionic liquids (ILs),
,
 and nanomaterials,
−

 are essential for addressing these challenges and producing C2 or
lower compounds such as carbon monoxide, methanol, and methane ( c). However, many
of these media require specialized equipment or operate under harsh
conditions. Developing novel catalytic media for the electrochemical
conversion of low-concentration CO2 into complex organic
compounds is an urgent critical challenge.
,

 To design this catalyst, it is essential to define
its key functionalities:
(1) the catalyst must exhibit stability and resistance to electrooxidation
and electroreduction; (2) it should demonstrate efficient capabilities
for CO2 adsorption, release, and activation; (3) the catalytic
core must possess antipoisoning properties against other waste gases
to prevent deactivation; (4) the material selection should prioritize
readily available components, and the preparation process should be
straightforward. As the development and advancement of our laboratory’s
research,
−

 we investigated the use of heterogeneous catalytic
porous organic polymers (POPs) for CO2 capture and release,
followed by indirect electrooxidation to sequester carbon products.
−

 We chose a guanidine group as the core of the polymer due to its
ability to form carbamate structures with CO2, thereby
effectively isolating it.
−

 The guanidine group also exhibits excellent chemical
stability. By carefully designing the catalyst’s binding mode,
modifying its core structure, and adjusting the polymerization ratio,
we can precisely control the pore size and nitrogen content, significantly
enhancing gas adsorption performance. The nonmetallic core ensures maximum resistance to poisoning.
The strong guanidine base 1,5,7-triazabicyclo[4.4.0]­dec-5-ene (TBD)
was functionalized with vinyl groups. Subsequently, TBD porous materials
were synthesized via solvent-thermal polymerization using ethylene
glycol dimethacrylate (EGDMA). These materials exhibit abundant pores
and numerous N-containing active sites, enabling
efficient CO2 adsorption from anaerobic fermentation gas
(AFG).

Compared with unpolymerized
TBD, this material significantly enhances
the electrochemical reaction efficiency for low-concentration CO2. Herein, we report for the first time the development of
an innovative electrochemical activation method utilizing TBD porous
materials to capture CO2 from AFG at ambient conditions
and convert it into bicyclic carbamate ( d). This approach achieves both carbon capture
and storage (CCS) and carbon capture and utilization (CCU) under electrochemical
conditions.
−

## Results and Discussion
In this study, we investigated
the bicyclization of 50 vol % CO2/N2 with 2,2-diphenylpentyl-4-ene-1-amine
to form
tetrahydro-6,6-diphenyl-1H,3H-pyrrolo­[1,2-c]­oxazol-3-one using G-POPs-1 as a catalyst to determine
optimal reaction conditions (Table S1).
Initial solvent screening ( a) revealed that tetrahydrofuran (THF) yielded only
16%, indicating that nonpolar solvents are unsuitable for this reaction.
Neither methanol (MeOH) nor acetone, both polar solvents, produced
the target product. In contrast, yields increased significantly to
66% in acetonitrile (MeCN) and 82% in N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF),
both aprotic solvents. THF’s low dielectric constant and moderate
polarity limit its ability to dissolve ionic electrolytes such as
NH4I and stabilize charged intermediates, leading to low
reaction efficiency. In contrast, MeCN’s higher polarity effectively
dissolves NH4I and stabilizes ionic intermediates, but
its slightly lower dielectric constant compared to DMF weakens stabilization
of strongly polar intermediates (e.g., CO2-amined adducts).
DMF, with a high polarity and dielectric constant, stabilizes charged
species (e.g., [G-POPs-1]­H+ and I–) through
hydrogen bonding and dipole interactions, significantly enhancing
the CO2 activation and cyclization efficiency.
 The impact of water dosage on the reaction yield
is illustrated
in b. Increasing
the water volume from 1.0 to 2.0 mL raised the yield from 38% to 82%.
However, further increases in water volume led to a decline in yield,
with a reduction to 67% at 2.5 mL. We hypothesize that organic guanidine
compounds form reversible charged cations with CO2 in the
presence of water, thereby enhancing material wettability. Excessive water, however, impairs solvent mass
transfer, leading to a reduced yield.
After optimizing the electrolyte,
we found that NH4I
provided the best catalytic effect ( c). When different electrolytes were tested,
Br– yielded 45%, while Cl–, F–, and PF6
– showed minimal
reactivity. This aligns with our proposed mechanism as I– and Br– have lower oxidation potentials, making
them more likely to lose electrons and form radicals for subsequent
reactions. Moreover, platinum (Pt) effectively catalyzes iodide (I–) oxidation to iodine radicals (I•), initiating 4-pentenamine bicyclization via radical pathways, as
confirmed by cyclic voltammetry (Figure S11). Stainless steel (SS) or carbon (C) anodes fail to generate products
due to inadequate oxidative power (Table S1, Entry 2), while Pt/SS pairing optimizes the oxidation–reduction
balance. Exclusive Pt use (Table S1, Entry
1) reduces the yield to 33% from mismatched potentials or side reactions.
The cathode primarily drives proton reduction (H+ →
H2).

 d illustrates
the catalytic performance of various catalysts under the optimal conditions.
Using a guanidine core achieves yields exceeding 75%. In contrast,
other organic base porous organic polymers typically yield no more
than 60% (Table S2). Specifically, direct
use of TBD, 1,8-diazabicyclo[5.4.0]­undecane-7-ene (DBU), and 1,1,3,3-tetramethyl
guanidine TMG results in yields of 41%, 38%, and 36%, respectively.
These findings indicate that porous materials play a significant role
in electrochemically mediating the conversion of low concentration
CO2. Subsequently, the catalyst dosage was screened. Optimal
performance was achieved with 1.0 equivalent of the catalyst, and
excess catalyst did not improve yield further. Comparing the structural
distinctions between the guanidine-based compound TBD and other organic
bases, its three nitrogen atoms form synergistic interaction sites,
where two bind to CO2 via hydrogen bonding or electrostatic
interactions, creating a stable zwitterionic intermediate. This coordination
enhances selective CO2 adsorption even at low concentrations.
The bicyclic framework optimizes nitrogen atom distribution, facilitating
the CO2 activation.
The catalytic performance of
G-POPs-1 synthesized with varying
ratios of monomer to cross-linking agent (Int-1: EGDMA)
was systematically investigated ( f). Increasing the Int-1: EGDMA
ratio from 1:2 to 1:4 resulted in a significant increase in BET surface
area from 244.94 m2/g to 387.38 m2/g. Further
increasing the EGDMA content to a ratio of 1:5 led to a decrease in
the BET surface area to 272.76 m2/g (Figure S8). The nitrogen sites in Int-1 play
a critical role in CO2 activation, and both insufficient
and excessive amounts of EGDMA can adversely affect the catalyst performance.
Consequently, G-POPs-1 with an Int-1: EGDMA ratio of
1:4 was chosen for subsequent experiments.
A preliminary structural
analysis of G-POPs-1 was conducted using
FT-IR, as illustrated in a. Characteristic peaks at 1729.9 cm–1 and 1158.9 cm–1 were identified in the spectrum
of EGDMA, corresponding to the stretching vibrations of the CO
and C–O–C bonds within the ester group, respectively.
The absence of these peaks in the spectrum of Int-1 suggests
that Int-1 and EGDMA have successfully undergone polymerization
to form G-POPs-1. The specific surface area of G-POPs-1 is 387.38
cm2/g, and the pore volume is 0.858 cm3/g ( b). These characteristics
confirm its porous nature. We subsequently evaluated the CO2 adsorption isotherms of G-POPs-1 at 273 and 298 K ( c). The maximum adsorption
capacities were 12.67 cm3/g and 22.73 cm3/g,
respectively. The N2 adsorption–desorption and CO2 adsorption isotherms of other catalysts are shown in Figures S1 to S7.
 These results indicate that G-POPs-1 exhibits excellent
CO2 adsorption capacity, which can create a localized high
concentration
of CO2 in the heterogeneous catalytic system, thereby enhancing
the reaction efficiency. In situ infrared spectroscopy ( d) reveals the changes in the
G-POPs-1 catalyst upon CO2 adsorption. Distinct CO2 absorption peaks appear at 2360 cm–1 and
2339 cm–1, reaching saturation within approximately
30 min. Solid-state 13C NMR spectroscopy of G-POPs-1 ( e) reveals distinct
carbonyl peaks in the chemical shift range of 170–180 ppm,
corresponding to the upper phase, as well as methylene peaks within
the 15–65 ppm range.
Furthermore, the surface composition
of G-POPs-1 was determined
using XPS, providing further confirmation of FT-IR analysis. The
scanning results revealed the presence of C, N and O elements in the
polymer structure ( ).
 Subsequently, no significant weight loss occurred
between 100 and
300 °C ( a). Above 300 °C, the weight loss increases sharply due to polymer
decomposition, indicating that the polymer can withstand temperatures
up to 300 °C. To demonstrate its recyclability, a recycling experiment
of G-POPs-1 was conducted under optimal conditions.
 After each reaction cycle, the solid catalyst was
recovered by
filtration; washed sequentially with MeCN, deionized water, ethyl
acetate (EtOAc), and ethanol (EtOH); and dried under a vacuum. It
was then reused for subsequent cycles without any additional treatments.
As shown in b, no significant yield loss was observed after six cycles. The SEM
images of fresh G-POPs-1 reveal well-developed pores with interconnected
channels ( c).
Consistent with expectations, after six cycles of use, G-POPs-1 maintains
these structural features ( d).
As shown in , substrates with different R1 and R2 substituents
(1b–1h) were examined. For monosubstituted 1c, product 2c was isolated at 70%. Ortho-, meta-, or para-CH3 substituted aryl rings (1d–1f) efficiently yielded products 2d–2f efficiently. 4-Pentenamine reacted well with electron-rich
aromatics to produce 2g at 89%. Doubly substituted 1h gave 2h in 80% yield with a 1:1 diastereoselectivity
ratio. Elongating the carbon chain in 1i produced 2i at 57%. Electron-withdrawing groups on R1 and
R2 (1j, 1k) led to high yields
of 2j and 2k. Benzyl, allyl, and 2-thienyl
substrates also yielded 2l, 2m, and 2n effectively.

 Specifically,
when actual AFG is used as a CO2 source,
all substrates can be efficiently converted into target products ( , 2a–2n).
,

We conducted
reactions under optimal conditions by using various
concentrations of CO2 ( a). The results revealed a positive correlation
between the CO2 concentration (vol %) in N2 and
product yield. Notably, even at a CO2 concentration as
low as 10 vol %, the target product was obtained with a yield of 65%.
These findings are significant for guiding practical applications
in waste gas treatment. To comprehensively evaluate the CO2/N2 selectivity, we conducted N2 adsorption
isotherm measurements at both 273 K and 298 K (consistent with the
conditions of the CO2 adsorption tests). As clearly illustrated
in b, G-POPs-1
demonstrates significantly enhanced CO2 selectivity over
N2 at both temperatures. After undergoing six adsorption–desorption
cycles, G-POPs-1 retains a CO2 adsorption capacity of more
than 7.9 cm3/g, confirming its robust adsorption performance
(entry 11, Table S2).
 To gain a deeper understanding of the reaction
mechanism, a series
of control experiments were conducted. Specifically, 5.0 mL of DMF
and 2.0 mL of H2O were added to 2,2-diphenyl-4-pentenamine
(1a) along with 1.0 equiv of G-POPs-1 at room temperature.
After thorough stirring for 1 h, 1.0 equiv of NH4I was
introduced, followed by electrolysis at a constant current of 10 mA,
resulting in the desired product 2a with an 83% yield
( a).
 These results indicate that G-POPs-1 can function
as a base. Under
standard conditions, when 1.0 equiv of iodine was used instead of
electro-oxidized ammonium iodide, no target product was detected ( b), confirming that
the reaction does not proceed via the oxidation of NH4I
to I2. When 3.0 equiv of 2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-1-piperinedinyloxy
(TEMPO) was added under standard conditions, only trace amounts of 2a were produced ( c), suggesting that the reaction proceeds via a radical
mechanism. To further demonstrate the crucial role of electrochemistry
in this reaction, the experiment was repeated under inert gas argon
protection ( d). The yield of the target product 2a (80%) was not
significantly different from that obtained under optimal conditions
(82%), indicating that the reaction is driven by electro-oxidation
rather than atmospheric oxygen. Additionally, a scale-up experiment
using 5.0 mmol of 1a as the substrate and reacting at
60 mA for 4 h resulted in a 76% yield of 2a ( e).
Based on
our mechanistic studies and the work of others,
−

 we propose
a plausible mechanism for the carboxylated cyclization
of 4-pentenamine catalyzed by G-POPs-1 at low concentrations CO2 ( ).
In contrast to the iodonium-ion intermediate, the mechanism of this
reaction involves a sequential process of electrooxidation, iodine
radical formation, and subsequent electrooxidation. Initially, CO2 from AFG is adsorbed and activated on G-POPs-1 via nitrogen-activating
groups. This process creates a concentrated
CO2 environment within the pores, providing thermodynamically
and kinetically favorable conditions for CO2 fixation. 4-pentenamine (1a) is captured
and reacts with the activated CO2 to form carbamate intermediate 3. Concurrently, ammonium iodide is anodized to generate an
iodine radical, which attacks the CC bond to produce carbon
radical 4. The 4 is then anodized to form
carbocation intermediate 5. Simultaneously, cyclic voltammetry
(CV) tests revealed that substrate 1a underwent two oxidation
events (Figure S11), consistent with our
proposed mechanism. The nitrogen in intermediate 5 acts
as a nucleophile, attacking the carbocation while releasing [G-POPs-1]­H+ to form intermediate 6, detected by HRMS (Figure S9). The H+ in [G-POPs-1]­H+ combines with I– to form HI, regenerating
G-POPs-1 for the next cycle. G-POPs-1 subsequently captures H+ from carbamate 6 to form intermediate 7. In intermediate 7, the iodine is intramolecularly
replaced by oxygen from the carbamate, forming product 2a and releasing HI. Throughout this process, G-POPs-1 is regenerated.
 

## Conclusions
To address global warming, developing efficient
carbon capture
and conversion technologies is crucial. Traditional electrochemical
reduction methods, while environmentally friendly, face challenges
such as high energy consumption for adsorbent regeneration and low
efficiency in low-concentration CO2 conversion. This study
presents a novel nonmetallic porous organic polymer (G-POPs-1) catalytic
system. Utilizing guanidine-based polymers as the backbone, it significantly
enhances gas adsorption through precise control of pore size and nitrogen
content. Synthesized via solvothermal polymerization with ethylene
glycol dimethacrylate, the material features abundant pores and nitrogen-rich
active sites, enabling efficient capture of trace CO2 from
anaerobic fermentation gas under ambient conditions and its conversion
to bicyclic carbamate. Compared to unpolymerized monomers, this material
demonstrates remarkable improvement in electrochemical reaction efficiency
for low-concentration CO2, achieving synergistic carbon
capture-storage utilization under electrochemical conditions for the
first time. The catalyst design emphasizes four core functionalities:
electrochemical stability, dual CO2 adsorption-activation
capability, antipoisoning properties, and facile preparation, offering
new insights for developing nonmetallic catalytic materials.

## Methods

## Synthesis of G-POPs-1
Into 2.5 mL of dimethyl sulfoxide
(DMSO) in a pressure tube were added Int-1 (0.18 g, 1.0
mmol), Ethylene dimethacrylate (EGDMA, 0.79 g, 4.0 mmol), and 2,2’-Azobis­(2-methylpropionitrile)
(AIBN, 0.08 g) and stirred
at 50 °C for 30 min. The mixture was hydrothermally treated at
100 °C for 24 h and filtered. The residue was washed with EtOAc,
water, and MeOH 3 times and dried under vacuum to obtain yellow powder,
which was denoted as G-POPs-1.

## Synthesis of G-POPs-2 (or 3) and POPs-4 (5, 6, 7 or 8)
Into 2.5 mL of DMSO in a pressure tube, Int-2 (0.16
g, 1.0 mmol), 1,2-divinylbenzene (DVB, 0.13 g, 1.0 mmol), and 2,2’-Azobis­(2-methylpropionitrile)
(AIBN, 0.08 g) were added and stirred at 50 °C for 30 min. The
mixture was hydrothermally treated at 100 °C for 24 h and filtered.
The residue was washed with EtOAc, water and MeOH 3 times and dried
under vacuum to obtain yellowish powder, was denoted as G-POPs-2.
The G-POPs-3, POPs-4, POPs-5, POPs-6, POPs-7 and POPs-8 were synthesized
using a similar synthetic procedure to that of G-POPs-2.
