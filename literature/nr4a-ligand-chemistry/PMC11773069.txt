It seems reasonable to argue that the complex pathology of neurodegenerative diseases (ND) cannot be reduced to a single target to modulate a broad range of cellular signaling, associated pathologies, and symptoms. It is this idea that has brought the attention of the scientific world towards phytochemicals and traditional drugs that are notoriously multi-targeted. A systematic study of these formulations and establishing the molecular pathways of individual molecules can lead to a standardized multi-component product that can modulate a broad range of activities on different targets of ND. This could provide an accessible and affordable solution to the significant disease burden of ND. With this idea in mind, a systematic review was carried out on an Ayurvedic product Manasamitra Vatakam (MMV), known to be a neuroprotective formulation and highly effective against Alzheimer's disease. MMV can be a source of phytomolecules for treating neurodegenerative diseases. The multifactorial nature of these diseases makes them suitable candidates for testing phytochemicals due to the inherent multitargeting capabilities of these compounds. The primary objective of this review is to provide a comprehensive understanding of the phytomolecules from MMV that are responsible for its multitargeted effect against neurodegenerative diseases. From the reported literature, it is clear that many phytoconstituents and extracts of the herbal ingredients from MMV have demonstrated their efficacy against AD models. However, the combination of these molecules in AD models has never been tested. Scientific studies should be done to explore the bioactive compounds in the formulation and the druggability of these identified compounds can be evaluated using experimental methods.

## Introduction
The high incidence rate of Alzheimer's disease (AD) due to a continually aging global population has prompted significant research interest in this area. AD is the most common cause of dementia, characterized by chronic, irreversible neurodegeneration that eventually leads to brain atrophy and death. AD has a complex pathophysiology involving different cellular pathways [ ]. The most common cause of neuronal death is the aberrant accumulation of β-amyloid peptides, also known as Aβ-plaques, which disrupt communication at the synapses. Aβ-plaques are produced by the enzymatic cleavage (by β- and γ-secretase) of β-amyloid precursor protein (APP), a transmembrane protein found on the plasma membrane of cells [ ]. Another protein known to cause cell death is tau, which accumulates in neurons and prevents the entry of nutrients into cells. Mitochondrial dysfunction is another key element of AD, caused by proteasome dysfunction or the accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) [ ]. The number of Alzheimer's patients has been projected to increase from 35 million to a whopping 135 million by 2050 [ ], which will be a huge financial burden on the healthcare budget of low and middle-income countries. Currently, there are only two classes of approved drugs to treat AD, including the cholinesterase enzyme inhibitors and N-methyl D-aspartate (NMDA) antagonists, which provide only symptomatic treatment and often produce adverse effects such as vomiting, nausea, diarrhea, and extrapyramidal side effects [ ]. Currently, the focus of AD research is on understanding its pathology by targeting several mechanisms, aiming to develop successful treatments capable of preventing the disease or modifying the course of AD [ ]. In this context, herbal drugs being multitargeted are gaining attention as a promising approach for the treatment and management of AD [ ]. A very huge gap in the drug discovery landscape for AD exists with a meager success rate of 0.4 %. Over 200 potential drug candidates have shown insignificant results at different stages of clinical trials resulting in poor translation [ ]. The reverse pharmacological approach integrates traditional clinical knowledge and experiential observations into the modern drug discovery process, involving the screening of lead molecules followed by robust preclinical and clinical examinations [ ].
1.1

## The science of ayurveda and its role in neurodegenerative diseases
Since the allopathic system of medicine has yet to provide a radical cure for AD, there is an urgent need for novel and effective treatments, highlighting the importance of research in traditional medicine as an alternative resource. Reports show that modern, evidence-based research examining traditional and complementary medicines for AD has generated promising results. India and the Indian subcontinent are home to Ayurveda, a traditional medical system that dates back over 2000 years [ ]. According to Ayurveda, imbalances in the Tridosha (Vata, Pitta, and Kapha) and Triguna (Sattva, Raja, and Tama) give rise to functional disorders in the Indriya (cognitive and motor organs), Mana (psyche), and Buddhi (intellect), resulting in impaired memory or cognitive deficits [ ]. Ayurvedic medications play a crucial role in maintaining and restoring the equilibrium between Tridosha and Triguna. Classical Ayurveda texts, detail numerous medicinal preparations for the treatment of various ailments. Ayurveda mainly emphasizes a holistic healing approach with single/multi-herbal formulations [ ]. Ayurvedic medicine mentions and explains the use of remedies from natural sources (extracts of plants, animals, and minerals) for the treatment of neurological disorders [ ]. Among various medicinal Ayurvedic herbs, Bacopa monnieri L, Centella asiatica (L.) Urban (Mandukaparni), Withania somenifera (L.) Dunal (Ashwagandha), Curcuma longa L (turmeric), Convolvulus pluricaulis Choisy (Shankhpushpi) are well-known ayurvedic plants that enhance memory and rejuvenate cognitive functions [ ]. Recently mechanistic studies in different in vivo/in vitro models have demonstrated that they may offer beneficial effects on central nervous system (CNS) disorders, including AD [ ].
Manasamitra vatakam (MMV) is a herbomineral formulation mentioned in Sahasrayogam, which contains more than 55 plant species, and mineral contents. This formulation has a long history of use in Ayurvedic medicine for the treatment of memory impairment and manic illness [ ]. MMV demonstrated an improvement in behavioral changes resulting from cognitive dysfunction induced by aluminum chloride toxicity in a rodent model [ ]. Further investigations into the formulation have documented its pharmacological activity as a neuroprotective, antioxidant, agent [ , ]. In a clinical study effectiveness of MMV was studied for addressing generalized anxiety disorder in 72 patients with comorbid social phobia. The formulation showed a significant reduction in clinical parameters in the treatment groups and effectively managed anxiety disorders [ ]. Despite the widespread usage over hundreds of years, there is no sufficient scientific data available on the effectiveness of this formulation. The multitargeted effect of MMV is attributed to the presence of several phytochemicals from different plants that comprise the components of the polyherbal formulation. These compounds, in their pure forms, have been proven to be effective against AD and other neurodegenerative diseases through various mechanisms. MMV is a polyherbal formulation containing several phytochemicals known to affect the CNS. Therefore, the combination of these different molecules may exert a multitargeted effect on AD. Hence, there exists a gap in knowledge regarding various phytoconstituents/herb combinations that work synergistically for their beneficial effect in AD. This article aims to explore MMV for drug discovery using modern scientific techniques. MMV can be a source of phytomolecules for treating neurodegenerative diseases. The multifactorial nature of these diseases makes them suitable candidates for testing phytochemicals due to the inherent multitargeting capabilities of these compounds. The primary objective of this review is to provide a comprehensive understanding of the phytomolecules from MMV that are responsible for its multitargeted effect against AD.

## Methodology
We systematically retrieved scientific data from PubMed, Web of Science, Google Scholar, Science Direct, and Scopus. The search strategy involved the retrieval, and extraction of published literature about in vitro, in vivo, and clinical studies of the plants and their phytoconstituents listed as ingredients in MMV, in suitable models relevant to AD. The search was done utilizing specified keywords including “Manasamitra Vatakam,” “cognitive enhancer,” “Neuroprotective,” “CNS activity,” “Alzheimer's disease,” “Curcuma longa,” “Glycyrrhiza glabra,” “Acorus calamus,” “Withania somnifera,” “Prunum avium,” “Cinnamomum zeylanicum,” “Uraria picta,” “Emblica officinalis,” “Vitis vinifera,” “Pueraria tuberosum,” and “Terminalia chebula.” The scientific and common names of each plant were cross-referenced with the keywords “Neuroprotective,” “CNS activity,” and “Alzheimer's disease.” All the articles obtained were thoroughly reviewed by examining both their titles and abstracts. Following an initial screening, rigorous inclusion and exclusion criteria were applied, with preference given to studies providing full texts or abstracts. The gathered information from diverse sources underwent a comprehensive review and categorization based on the identified mechanisms of action relevant to AD. Inclusion criteria were defined by a) studies published between 2010 and 2023 (selected the time frame to include all the scientific reports on MMV which started in 2010), b) neuroprotective activities exhibited by plant ingredients in AD models, and c) documented activities of various extracts and active principles isolated from the selected plants. Exclusion criteria were established to eliminate a) secondary studies, b) studies in languages other than English, and c) studies demonstrating CNS activity in disease models other than Alzheimer's disease. The details of the selection process are shown in the PRISMA flow diagram (see ). 
 shows the studies reporting the anti-AD activity of the extract/phytoconstituents of herbal ingredients of MMV. According to the literature, these plants/phytoconstituents have been found to alleviate AD through multiple and diverse mechanisms, such as inhibiting acetylcholinesterase (AChE), monoaminoxidase (MAO), β-secretase enzymes, synaptic damage, tau protein hyperphosphorylation, attenuating Aβ, reducing oxidative stress, preventing neurodegeneration, improving cognitive deficits, and enhancing memory (see , ). Many herbal compounds have the potential to be neuroprotective, but poor absorption and their selective permeability across the BBB restrict their bioavailability and may reduce their effectiveness. The BBB and gastrointestinal (GI) permeability of the major phytoconstituents found in the herbs of MMV were included as supplementary data.

## Pharmacological activities of individual medicinal plant constituent of Manasamitra vatakam
3.1

## Antioxidant
This section addresses the potential of various herbal ingredients and phytoconstituents in MMV to counteract oxidative stress. Oxidative stress, a significant marker in AD, can cause an accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), mitochondrial dysfunction, and decrease the levels of antioxidant enzymes. Antioxidants prevent dementia by reducing free radical-mediated toxic reactions in neuronal cells and increasing the levels of enzymes superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT), and other antioxidant enzymes [ ].
β-asarone, a major phytoconstituent found in the Acorus calamus L, has been shown to have potential neuroprotective effects. In hydrogen peroxide (H2O2)-induced PC12 cells, β-asarone improved cell viability and increased levels of necessary antioxidant enzymes, such as SOD, CAT, and glutathione (GSH), activating the nuclear factor erythroid-2 related factor 2 (Nrf2)/heme oxygenase (HO-1) pathway [ ]. The aqueous root extract of Glycyrrhiza glabra L was found to have nootropic effects, improving learning and memory in Wistar rats at doses of 150 and 225 mg/kg. The extract-treated group reduced the accumulation of free radicals, which can lead to oxidative damage and neurodegenerative diseases. The extract also significantly reduced Transfer latency (TL) values in the Elevated Plus Maze and Hebb William Maze tests. These findings suggest that G. glabra may hold potential for the treatment and prevention of neurological disorders through its neuroprotective and antioxidant properties [ ]. Sharma et al. (2023) conducted a network analysis of the major phytoconstituents of liquorice, revealing that the interactions of these compounds with the major target proteins of AD make it a potential candidate for AD treatment. The study found that isoliquiritigenin interacts with β-secretase 1, 18β-glycyrrhetinic acid interacts with the calcium channel (Type L), quercetin interacts with glycogen synthase kinase-3 β (GSK3 β), cyclin-dependent kinase 5 (CDK5), and formononetin interacts with the dopamine receptor [ ].
The root extract of Withania somnifera L. (100 mg/kg) decreased the elevated malondialdehyde (MDA) level and increased the level of SOD and CAT in the hippocampal region against bisphenol A-induced memory impairment and oxidative stress in mice. NMDA receptor expression was also enhanced in treated animals [ ]. In an in vitro study using BV-2 microglia cells, withaferin A and withanolide inhibit lipopolysaccharide (LPS) induced nitric oxide (NO) and reactive oxygen species (ROS), regulating the Nrf2 pathway. The study shows that withaferin A is 10 times more potent than withanolide A [ ]. Puerarin, a major isoflavone glycoside from Pueraria tuberosa (Roxb. ex Willd.) DC. showed a protective effect on vascular dementia by reducing oxidative stress. It decreased MDA level and increased GSH-Px and thiol levels in the hippocampus and the frontal cortex of rats. The glycoside treatment resulted in a significant (P < 0.05) upregulation in the gene expression of both the forkhead box O (FoxO) family and Nrf2 [ ].
A forebrain ischemic insult resulted in an elevated brain MDA level and reduced GSH and CAT levels in male albino Wistar rats. This alteration was attributed to the overproduction of reactive oxygen species, leading to oxidative stress. The hydroalcoholic extract of Alpinia galanga (L.) Willd rhizome (200 and 400 mg/kg) improved the levels of endogenous antioxidants CAT and GSH and reduced the level of MDA in the brains of rats [ ]. Another ingredient of MMV, Cinnamomum camphora (L.) Nees & Eberm., was found to possess a neuroprotective effect by reducing oxidative stress in rats with electroshock-induced seizures. Methanolic extract of C. camphora (50 and 100 mg/kg b. w., p. o.) decreased the elevated levels of MDA and AChE and increased GSH levels [ ]. Scopolamine-induced cognitive impairment and elevated oxidative stress in rat brains were effectively managed by treatment with hydroalcoholic extract of C. zeylanicum bark (200 and 400 mg/kg). It was observed that the plant extract could reduce the elevated level of MDA and increase the GSH level in extract-treated rats [ ]. The standardized lyophilized aqueous extract of C. zeylanicum bark decreased the MDA level and increased the brain GSH levels in the cortical and hippocampal regions of the brain in rats at an oral dose of 200 mg/kg [ ].
In an in vitro study by Dhanalakshmi et al. (2015) the neuroprotective effect of vanillin, a phenolic compound of Hemidesmus indicus (L.) R. Br. ex Schult., in rotenone-induced neurotoxicity in SH-SY5Y neuroblastoma cells was reported. It has been shown that pretreatment of neuroblastoma cells with vanillin (100 nM) reduced mitochondrial dysfunction, the level of ROS, and downregulated proapoptotic markers [ ]. Vanillin showed similar antioxidant activity in neuroblastoma SH-SY5Y cells with an IC50 of 19.5 μM [ ]. Lauric acid attenuated oxidative stress in haloperidol-induced parkinsonism by reducing the elevated levels of MDA, NO, and tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α) and improving SOD levels [ ]. In an in vivo study, nerolidol (H. indicus) improved rotenone-induced dopaminergic degeneration in Wistar rats by reducing oxidative stress and inflammation. The treatment significantly increased the levels of antioxidant enzymes and inhibited the release of proinflammatory cytokines interleukins (IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α) and inflammatory mediators cyclooxygenase 2 (COX-2) and inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS)) [ ]. The antioxidant effects of nor cepharadione B (an alkaloid from the fruit of Piper longum) prevent H2O2-induced oxidative stress by increasing heme oxygenase (HO-1) in PI3K/Akt-dependent ways and by preventing (Volumes sensitive outwardly rectifying chloride) VSOR Cl channel obstruction in hippocampal neuronal cell line (HT22) cells [ ].
Cis carveol present in Curcuma longa L showed a neuroprotective effect in the Aβ1-42 induced rat model by decreasing oxidative stress and increasing SOD and CAT [ ]. Piccialli et al. (2021) reported the neuroprotective effect of limonine in primary cortical neurons exposed to Aβ1-42 oligomers by preventing ROS production and Kv3.4 channel hyperfunction [ ]. Ferulic acid, an active compound in Cynodon dactylon Pers., has been reported to possess antioxidant activity. It increases the levels of reduced glutathione, SOD, and CAT while suppressing cholinergic enzyme activity, aggravating adenosine triphosphatase (ATPase) activity, and altering oxidatively dysregulated metabolic pathways in oxidative brain injury [ ]. Ethanolic extract of Prunus avium (L.) fruits significantly reduced the biochemical markers such as AChE, corticosterone, TBARS, tissue nitrite levels, and increased levels of antioxidants. Also, it ameliorated the deficits in learning and memory impairment in streptozotocin (STZ) induced AD mouse model [ ]. The memory impairment in mice caused by scopolamine was improved by the ripe fruit extract of Terminalia chebula Retz. (100 mg/kg & 200 mg/kg) via modulation of the cholinergic transmission system and decreased the levels of ROS, NO, and MDA [ ]. S. cordifolia L. and S. veronicaefolia, both species of the Sida genus, have been widely employed in traditional medicine as powerful treatments for neurodegenerative disorders [ ]. In a study by Khurana and Gajbhiye (2013), the aqueous extract of S. cordifolia exhibited significant neuroprotective properties against rotenone-induced oxidative stress. This was evidenced by a decrease in TBARS levels and superoxide anion generation, as well as an increase in GSH and CAT levels within the midbrain region of rats [ ].

## Cholinesterase inhibitors (ChEIs)
The potential of the herbal ingredients in MMV to maintain acetylcholine levels in the brain is summarized in the section. AChE is an important enzyme responsible for the degradation of the neurotransmitter acetylcholine (ACh), which is essential for normal cognitive function of the brain, including memory and learning. AD is characterized by a progressive decline in acetylcholine levels in the brain, resulting in impaired cognitive function [ ]. Anti-acetylcholinesterase drugs, also known as cholinesterase inhibitors (ChEIs), are a class of medications used to treat AD. These drugs work by inhibiting AChE, thereby increasing the levels of acetylcholine in the brain. By increasing acetylcholine levels, ChEIs can improve cognitive function in AD patients [ ].
The standardized lyophilized aqueous extract of C. zeylanicum bark (200 mg/kg po.) significantly (p < 0.05) decreased the acetylcholinesterase activity in the cortical and hippocampal regions of the brain in rodents [ ]. Thenmozhi et al. (2016) showed that tannoid principles of Emblica officinalis L (standardized mixture containing emblicanin A (37%), emblicanin B (33%), punigluconin (12%), and pedunculagin (14%)) significantly improved Morris water maze (MWM) performance in rats with aluminium chloride (AlCl3) induced neurotoxicity, indicating the relieving memory deficits. The study reported that the tannoids decreased the activity of AChE and the generation of insoluble Aβ fragments by inhibiting amyloid precursor proteins (APP), β-, and γ-secretases [ ]. The subsequent study by the same group showed that treatment with tannoid can enhance learning and memory deficits by suppressing oxidative stress and preventing tau hyperphosphorylation through the GSK-3β/Akt signaling pathway [ ].
Convolvulus pluricaulis var. macra Choisy, a perennial herb widely known as Shankhpushpi in Ayurveda, has a history of being used to alleviate nervous system disorders such as insanity, epilepsy, hysteria, insomnia, and psychoneurosis. Both in vitro and in vivo studies have demonstrated its neuropharmacological effects, which include enhancing memory, reducing anxiety, inducing calmness, alleviating depression and stress, providing neuroprotection, and preventing convulsions [ ]. The aqueous root extract of C. pluricaulis (150 mg/kg) decreased AChE activity by 60% in the cerebral cortex of Al-induced neurotoxicity of albino rats [ ]. The clinical study of the plant showed promising results in various individual/polyherbal formulations that were tested in different clinical trials. Shankhpushpi tablets showed significant improvement in the long-term memory of the young population [ ]. Also, a clinical study of 138 patients aged 55–75 years in the ayurvedic polyherbal formulation containing C. pluricaulis showed significant enhancement in senile memory impairment [ ]. The network pharmacological analysis revealed significant interactions between various phytoconstituents of C. pluricaulis and key targets of signaling pathways associated with AD. Specifically, convolamine, convoline, confoline, convolvine, subhirsine, and convolvidine were found to interact with the serotonin 3a (5-HT3a)/3b (5-HT3b) receptor. Glycyrrhetinic acid demonstrated interaction with the calcium channel (Type L), while quercetin and kaempferol exhibited interactions with cyclin-dependent kinase 5 (CDK5) and glycogen synthase kinase-3 β, emphasizing their relevance in AD pathology [ ].
Similarly, the hydroalcoholic extract of Vitex negundo Linn. leaves (250 and 500 mg/kg/day, p. o.) also produced a ChEIs activity in the scopolamine-induced mice model [ ]. Dragomanova et al. (2022) reported that myrtenal (40 mg/kg) derived from the plant Alpinia galanga (L.) Willd ameliorated cognitive decline by reverting the reduced SOD, CAT levels and decreased AChE by 19.4% in the cortex and by 22% in the hippocampus of scopolamine-induced dementia in rats [ ]. Ma et al. reported that flavones from Vitis vinifera L improved learning and cognitive performance in rats with AD. The mechanistic study showed that it upregulates the expression of phosphorylation of cyclase response element binding protein (p-CREB), brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), and synaptotagmin (SYT1) in rat hippocampi dose-dependent manner, resulting in enhanced synaptic plasticity. Also, it inhibited the activity of AChE, thereby increasing the cholinergic transmitter level [ ]. (−)-cis-carveol (1% and 3%) exhibited a neuroprotective effect in the hippocampusof Aβ1-42 induced rat model by improving the cognitive defects, by inhibiting the AChE activity with an IC50 value of 0.057 mg/mL [ ]. The ethanolic extract of Prunus avium (L.) fruits showed neuroprotective action by reducing AChE in the intracerebroventricular-streptozotocin (ICV-STZ) model of dementia in mice model [ ]. MMV contains various ingredients that are flavonoid-rich, including V. vinifera, G. glabra, P. emblica, and Tinospora cordifolia (Willd.) Miers ex Hook. f. & Thomson. Several studies have indicated that flavonoid compounds such as kaempferol, genistein, apigenin, quercetin, naringin, and diosmin may have the ability to inhibit AChE and butyrylcholinesterase [ ].

## β-site amyloid precursor protein cleaving enzyme 1 (BACE1) inhibitors
β-site APP cleaving enzyme, or BACE-1, which is the key enzyme responsible for the cleavage of APP, integral proteins found mainly in the synapses of neurons. Elevated levels of BACE result in the accumulation of β-amyloid peptides and Aβ plaques in the brain. Inhibition of BACE results in the reduced deposition of Aβ plaques. Hence, BACE1 is a key target for treating AD [ ]. Curcuminoids from C. longa inhibited the expression of APP/BACE-1 in the Drosophila melanogaster model. Among the tested curcuminoids, bisdemethoxy curcumin was found to have the strongest inhibitory activity with an IC50 value of 17 μM [ ]. A molecular docking study has revealed that the phytoconstituents from the tuber of P. tuberosa, namely daidezin, genistin, mangiferin, puerarin, and tuberosin, demonstrate potent BACE1 inhibitory activity. Furthermore, in vivo studies have indicated that P. tuberosa can effectively alleviate cognitive decline, as demonstrated in various behavioral assays conducted on a Drosophila model [ ]. In vitro assays have demonstrated that neferine, an alkaloid isolated from N. nucifera seeds, exhibits an anti-amnesic effect. Specifically, it has demonstrated potent inhibition of cholinesterase and BACE1, with IC50 values of 14.19 ± 1.46 and 15.48 ± 0.20 μM, respectively. Additionally, neferine has been found to inhibit oxidative stress and reduce inflammation by suppressing NO production, with an IC50 value of 30.54 μM in LPS-induced NO formation, and by blocking NF-κB activation in RAW 264.7 cells [ ].

## Anti-amyloid β
APP is a transmembrane protein containing an extracellular domain. The cleavage of APP by β and γ secretases leads to the production of soluble Aβ oligomers, which can form fibrils and be deposited as Aβ plaques. These Aβ oligomers interact with various receptors, contributing to the progression of AD. Inhibiting the formation of amyloid plaques can lead to decreased Aβ accumulation and improved cognitive function in patients with AD [ ].
A study found that withanone, an active constituent of Withania somnifera, significantly improved cognitive function by inhibiting Aβ-42 and reducing proinflammatory cytokines. The oral intervention also resulted in a notable increase in acetylcholine and a decrease in β and γ secretase levels [ ]. Gene expression analysis of AD mouse brains demonstrated that an aqueous root extract of C. pluricaulis (150 mg/kg) decreased the elevated protein and mRNA levels of tau, APP, and Aβ in scopolamine-induced cholinergic impairments. Histopathological examination of the extract-treated animals effectively protected them from scopolamine neurotoxicity, indicating its neuroprotective effects [ ]. Ampelopsin A (10 ng/L), a phytoconstituent derived from V. vinifera, showed substantial neuroprotective effects in mice, improving scopolamine-induced impairment of hippocampal CA3-CA1 synapses. Chronic treatment of ampelosin A reversed the cholinergic deficits via the BDNF/CREB pathway. Therefore, central administration of ampelopsin increased the neuroprotective effect [ ]. It was observed that extracts from N. nucifera seeds exhibited a robust inhibitory effect on the aggregation of Aβ in differentiated PC-12 cells, and this effect was concentration-dependent (10–50 mg/mL) [ ]. Oral administration of cinnamon powder in a transgenic mouse model of AD improved antioxidant levels and reduced Aβ load in the hippocampus, thereby delaying the progression of AD [ ].

## Glutamate-induced excitotoxicity
This section summarizes the beneficial effects of different plant ingredients of MMV against glutamate excitotoxicity. Glutamate is the primary excitatory neurotransmitter present in the brain, playing a crucial role in maintaining proper brain function, including memory. However, overactivity of glutamate receptors results in an increased influx of calcium, leading to mitochondrial dysfunction and the generation of ROS/RNS. These activities collectively contribute to apoptosis and neuronal death [ ]. The butanol extract of T. cordifolia suppressed glutamate-induced structural degradation, mitochondrial dysfunction, NO generation (iNOS), secretion of proinflammatory cytokines, and stress protein expression. Additionally, it enhanced the synaptic plasticity, neurite outgrowth, and migration of primary cerebellar neurons [ ]. Research has demonstrated that ß-caryophyllene, a bicyclic sesquiterpene commonly present in the essential oils of various plants such as Piper nigrum and Cinnamomum spp., can safeguard C6 glioma cells against glutamate-induced toxicity by activating the Nrf2 pathway [ ]. Curcumin, a polyphenolic compound obtained from Curcuma longa L., is a potential candidate for preventing or mitigating pathological processes linked to neurodegenerative disorders. Studies conducted on different cellular models have reported the beneficial effects of curcumin against glutamate excitotoxicity [ ]. Chang et al. (2014) demonstrated that curcumin protects PC12 cells from glutamate excitotoxicity by mediating both the glutathione-dependent NO/ROS pathway and the mitochondria-dependent NO/ROS pathway [ ].

3.6

## Anti-neuroinflammatory
Agents with anti-neuroinflammatory properties can play a significant role in managing and treating AD. Neuroinflammation, marked by the activation of microglia and astrocytes, constitutes a crucial element of AD pathology. The activation of these cells triggers the release of proinflammatory cytokines and chemokines, intensifying neuroinflammation and contributing to the gradual loss of neuronal function and cognitive decline. The reported studies indicate that the phytoconstituent MMV has a significant anti-neuroinflammatory effect, which could play a crucial role in the therapeutic potential of the formulation.
Glabridin, a phytoconstituent of Glycyrrhiza glabra, has been found to exhibit memory-enhancing effects in mice through various mechanisms. Glabridin inhibits the enzyme cholinesterase, reduces chronic inflammation in the brain, and decreases the levels of ROS via its antioxidant properties [ ]. Methyl salicylate has also been found to have neuroprotective effects by suppressing neuronal inflammation and oxidative damage via regulating p38 and c-Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK)/mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) signaling pathway [ ].
Piperlongumine, an alkaloid from Piper longum, effectively inhibits the production of NO and prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) induced by LPS. Moreover, piperlongumine reduces the expression of iNOS and COX-2, and proinflammatory cytokines like TNF-α and IL-6. Its anti-neuroinflammatory effects are exerted by suppressing the NF-κB signaling pathway in LPS-stimulated BV2 microglia cells [ ]. Mazzio and colleagues conducted a high throughput screening on approximately 1400 natural plants and derived products to investigate their effects on LPS/interferon-gamma (IFNγ) induced inflammatory response. In their study, they evaluated various inflammatory markers such as IL-2, IL-4, thymus chemokine-1, leptin, TNF-α, etc., in C6 glioma cells. The results showed that ashwagandha, turmeric, red sandalwood, quercetin, cardamonin, galangin, apigenin, and curcumin exhibited promising anti-inflammatory effects at low concentrations (<250 μg/mL) comparable to NSAIDs. These compounds, which are present in MMV, may exert their activity through their anti-neuroinflammatory mechanism [ ].

## The role of various bhasma constituents in MMV
The Ayurveda literature emphasizes the need for metals such as mercury, gold, silver, copper, iron, tin, lead, and zinc to maintain the metabolic equilibrium of the human body [ ]. The herbo-mineral formulations encompass mineral/metallic components subjected to different processes like Shodhana (purification/potentiation), Bhavana (impregnation/levigation), and Marana (incineration/calcination), which foster increased absorption and potency. The process converts metals into nanoparticles, which undergo chelation with organic ligands in the plant liquids, making these elements non-toxic, easily assimilable, and enhancing their biocompatibility [ ]. Different scientific studies have been conducted on the usefulness of metallic preparations. Deviation in methods or processes can, at times, lead to deleterious effects, so stringent regulations on manufacturing and quality control are essential to avoid toxicity issues [ ]. Studies on herbomineral formulations such as Rasa sindhura, Garbha Cintamani Rasa, Makaradhwaja, and Mahamrutyunjaya Rasa have confirmed the aforementioned facts [ , ]. Rasa-Sindoor is a mercury-containing traditional ayurvedic formulation that suppresses apoptosis, prevents the accumulation of inclusion bodies, and improves clearance of defective proteins by activating the ubiquitin-proteasomal pathway. This was demonstrated in a fly model of AD [ ]. The role of metal constituents like Cinnabar (HgS) and Realgar (As2S4) along with some plant constituents in neuroprotection has been demonstrated in Chinese traditional medicine. The neuroprotective effect was shown in an LPS-induced model by its inhibitory effect on microglial activation and the upregulation of proinflammatory markers. Both the metal constituents were shown to have the activity as the removal or reduction of either of them made the formulation ineffective. However, the detoxification of mercury was done using a defined process [ ].
Rajata (silver) Bhasma, Swarna (gold) Bhasma, Makshika (copper-iron Pyrite) Bhasma, Mukta Pishti (paste prepared from Pearl), Loha (iron) Bhasma, and Shilajat contributed to the mineral content in MMV. Recent investigations suggest that metallic nanoparticles, (Bhasmas), exhibit the ability to impede oxidative and nitrative stress. Additionally, these nanoparticles preserve the expression of tight junction proteins between neurons, activate the anti-inflammatory pathways, and enhance neuro haemodynamic perfusion efficiency [ ]. Therefore, metal constituents of MMV may have a similar role in neuroprotection that needs to be explored in future studies on this formulation. MMV is prepared by mixing the powdered crude drugs with Kashayas (decoction) of V. vinifera, A. calamus L, C. pluricaulis, Aegle marmelos, S. cordifolia L. and juices of B. monnieri, Ipomoea sepiaria Roxb., Sarcostemma acidum, C. dactylon. Further, the mineral ingredients (in the form of Bhasmas) and other excipients are added and made into tablets.
Polyherbal formulations are an inevitable part of most traditional systems of medicine all over the world including Ayurveda and they are believed to offer a synergistic effect. Numerous studies have indicated that the synergistic effects of polyherbal treatments exhibit greater efficacy against AD compared to single-herb treatments [ , , ]. Through network pharmacological approach the Chinese traditional formulation Bushen-Yizhi has been demonstrated to possess multi-modal actions against AD [ ]. Several polyherbal formulations from China, such as Ba Wei Di Huang Wan and the Yi-Gan San formula have advanced to clinical trial levels in AD [ ]. A combination of Camellia sinensis L, Hypericum perforatum L, and B. monnieri demonstrated robust antioxidant effects compared to individual herb treatments [ ]. These findings point out the synergistic effect of polyherbal formulations compared to single-herb therapies. ## Conclusion
The herbal formulations within the traditional healthcare systems of India, encompassing Ayurveda, Siddha, and Unani, typically involve a combination of numerous medicinal plants. These formulations exhibit significant potential in the treatment of various ailments, including neurodegenerative conditions. The current review analyzes the use of MMV for its role in neurotransmission and neuroprotection. From the reported literature, it is clear that many phytocompounds and extracts of the herbal ingredients from MMV have demonstrated their efficacy against AD models in in vitro and in vivo experiments. However, none of these compounds have reached the market. The combination of these molecules in AD models has never been tested. As AD is a disease of multifactorial origin, multitargeting is a rational approach for managing and treating the disease. However, this hypothesis needs to be tested in controlled in-vitro and in-vivo studies. Molecules that can cross the blood-brain barrier can only show their CNS effects, and those with the correct physicochemical properties will reach the CNS. Although the formulation may contain several compounds, identifying those with the right physicochemical properties is a good starting point. Scientific studies using modern analytical techniques will help identify the bioactive compounds in the formulation that reach the target site. Furthermore, the druggability of these identified compounds can be evaluated using experimental methods. A formulation containing enriched fractions of identified phytomolecules would serve as a better therapeutic option for AD. One drawback of this article is that neurodegenerative diseases can have an etiological significance connected to the gut-brain axis and this aspect of the formulation is not explored.
