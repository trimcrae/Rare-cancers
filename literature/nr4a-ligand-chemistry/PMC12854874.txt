## Abstract

## Background
Triclosan (TCS), a widely used environmental antimicrobial agent, is associated with cardiovascular risks such as coronary heart disease; however, its effect on post-myocardial infarction (MI) prognosis remains unclear. This study investigated whether TCS exacerbated post-MI outcomes and the underlying mechanisms, with the goal of identifying potential preventive strategies.

## Methods
MI models were established using mice with left anterior descending coronary artery ligation, alongside hypoxia-treated neonatal rat cardiomyocytes (NRCMs) and human AC16 cardiomyocytes. A comprehensive set of methodologies was employed, including RNA sequencing, echocardiography, Western blotting, co-immunoprecipitation, dual-luciferase reporter assays, molecular docking, quantitative real-time PCR, histological/immunofluorescence staining, and oxidative stress parameter analyses. Mechanistic investigations utilized Nur77 knockout mice, AAV9-based viral vectors targeting Nur77 and NTRK2, adenoviruses, plasmids, and small-molecule inhibitors/activators.

## Results
Exposure to environmentally relevant TCS concentrations dose-dependently aggravated short- and long-term post-MI cardiac dysfunction and ventricular remodeling in both male and female mice. Mechanistically, TCS induced TRIM13-mediated K48-linked ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation of the nuclear receptor Nur77, leading to reduced transcription of NTRK2. Downregulated NTRK2 suppressed the AKT/mTOR/YY1 signaling cascade, ultimately decreasing PGC-1α expression and impairing mitochondrial function—specifically mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation. This bioenergetic deficit triggered excessive reactive oxygen species (ROS) production, promoting lipid peroxidation and exacerbating cardiomyocyte ferroptosis, cellular senescence, and the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP). These pathological effects collectively exacerbated acute post-MI injury and facilitated the progression of long-term ventricular remodeling. Validation in NRCMs and human AC16 cardiomyocytes confirmed conserved phenotypes and mechanisms. Pharmacological activation of PGC-1α with ZLN005 mitigated TCS-induced deterioration of short- and long-term post-MI cardiac function and attenuated ventricular remodeling.

## Conclusions
TCS exacerbates post-MI injury by disrupting the Nur77/NTRK2/PGC-1α axis, triggering mitochondrial dysfunction-mediated ferroptosis and senescence in cardiomyocytes of both male and female mice. Pharmacological activation of PGC-1α represents a potential strategy to counteract TCS-induced adverse outcomes after MI.

Keywords: Triclosan, Myocardial infarction, Cellular senescence, Ferroptosis, Mitochondrial dysfunction

## Introduction
Triclosan (TCS), a widely used antimicrobial agent, has become an environmental contaminant due to its extensive use in personal care products, household items, and industrial applications, and as a persistent pollutant, it is found in water systems, soils, and living organisms, posing potential risks to human health [ , ]. In recent years, the detection rates of TCS in human urine and plasma have reached as high as 80 %–99.5 % in male and female individuals [ , , , , ]. Previous studies have demonstrated that TCS impaired cardiac ECC, weakened cardiac muscle contractility in vitro [ ], and led to myocardial hypertrophy, abnormal heart rate and histopathological injury in zebrafish model [ , , ]. Recent studies have highlighted the significant association between TCS exposure and cardiovascular diseases, including a potential role in promoting the development of coronary artery disease (CAD) [ , ]. However, the exact impact of TCS on post-myocardial infarction (MI) outcomes, particularly in relation to cardiac damage and ventricular remodeling, remains unclear.
MI, as a severe consequence of CAD, is a major global health concern, leading to substantial morbidity and mortality [ , ]. Following MI, acute myocardial cell death, severe inflammatory responses during the acute phase, and long-term ventricular remodeling in survivors collectively lead to heart failure, which is a critical determinant of poor prognosis and reduced quality of life for survivors [ , , ]. Recently, there has been increasing attention on the impact of environmental factors on the occurrence and prognosis of MI, with several environmental factors, such as air pollution, extreme temperature events, and endocrine disruptors [ , , , ]. Although TCS has been shown to promote the occurrence of CAD at the population level [ ], its effects on outcomes post-MI remain unexplored. ​Therefore, as a widely present emerging contaminant, it is essential to investigate the impact of TCS exposure on MI prognosis and its underlying mechanisms.
TCS has been widely considered a mitochondrial toxin, leading to inhibition of oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) and increased production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) [ , ]. ​Mitochondria play crucial roles in cellular homeostasis, and mitochondrial dysfunction and the subsequent surge in ROS are pivotal factors that promote ferroptosis and cellular senescence post-MI [ , , ]. Ferroptosis contributes to cardiomyocyte loss, exacerbating ventricular remodeling post-MI [ ]. Similarly, recent studies have confirmed that MI-induced acceleration of cardiomyocyte senescence resulted in subsequent ventricular remodeling and the development of heart failure [ , ]. Researchers have found that TCS led to hepatic ferroptosis and fibrosis [ ], and TCS caused lifespan reduction through regulation of oxidative stress in the monogonont rotifer [ ] as well as promoted the upregulation of the senescence marker P53 in zebrafish [ ]. Therefore, we further investigated whether TCS promoted ferroptosis and cellular senescence post-MI through mitochondrial function-dependent ROS burst.
TCS has been previously shown to suppress NTRK2 expression in non-cardiac models [ ], yet its effect on myocardial NTRK2 levels following MI remains unexplored. Here, we demonstrated that TCS exposure further diminished NTRK2 expression in post-MI hearts. NTRK2 (also known as TrkB), a high-affinity receptor for brain-derived neurotrophic factor, has been widely confirmed to exert a protective effect after MI [ , , ]. Notably, NTRK2-mediated activation of mTOR has been shown to enhance peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma coactivator 1-alpha (PGC-1α) transcription through direct interaction with the transcription factor YY1 at the PGC-1α promoter, forming a protective axis against mitochondrial dysfunction [ , ]. PGC-1α —— a central regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis and OXPHOS, which is critical for restoring mitochondrial function after ischemic heart injury, and its deficiency exacerbates ROS accumulation and cardiomyocyte death [ , , ]. Consistent with this, our data revealed that TCS exposure suppressed PGC-1α expression in post-MI hearts, suggesting disruption of the NTRK2/mTOR/PGC-1α protective axis.
Nur77, also known as Nuclear receptor subfamily 4 group A member 1 (NR4A1), is a stress-inducible transcription factor and has emerged as a key mediator of myocardial protection against ischemic injury by regulating inflammation and fibrosis [ , , ]. Combined with database prediction and experimental validation, we found that Nur77 levels were significantly reduced in TCS-exposed cardiomyocytes and acted as a transcription factor for NTRK2 in cardiomyocytes. Therefore, we proposed that TCS promoted K48-linked ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation of Nur77, leading to reduced NTRK2 expression and subsequent inactivation of the AKT/mTOR/YY1 pathway. This cascade weakened PGC-1α-dependent mitochondrial repair mechanisms, exacerbating excessive ROS production in post-MI cardiomyocytes. This, in turn, promoted cardiomyocyte ferroptosis and senescence, thereby worsening post-MI ventricular remodeling and the progression of heart failure.
The aim of this study is to elucidate the impact of TCS on the prognosis of MI and its underlying mechanisms, and to propose that activating PGC-1α with ZLN005 serves as a potential strategy to prevent TCS-induced poor MI outcomes. Our findings may provide new insights into the toxicological effects of TCS and its potential implications for cardiovascular health, particularly for individuals with ischemic myocardial injury.

## Methods
2.1.

## Chemicals and reagents
TCS (HY–B1119), ANA-12 (HY-12497), ZLN005 (HY-17538), SR-18292 (HY-101491), LY294002 (HY-10108), MG-132 (HY-13259) and chloroquine (CQ, HY-17589A) were purchased from MedChemExpress Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Rapamycin (SJ-MA0031,Sparkjade), Mito-TEMPO (SJ-MX4908, Sparkjade) and Rotenone (SJ-MN0078,Sparkjade) were acquired from Shandong Sparkjade Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Shandong, China). Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO, D2650) was sourced from Sigma-Aldrich Co., Ltd. (USA). All reagents were stored and prepared in strict compliance with the manufacturer's specifications.

2.2.

## Protocol for TCS exposure in mouse models
Six-week-old male and female C57BL/6 mice were purchased from Slac Laboratory Animal Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Based on previous research, daily human exposure to TCS through common environmental pathways was roughly 0.073 mg/kg/day, corresponding to 0.88 mg/kg/day in mice [ , , , ]. For consumers using TCS-containing mouthwashes and toothpastes, human TCS exposure rose to approximately 1 mg/kg/day—a level that converted to 10 mg/kg/day in mice when normalized by body surface area [ , ]. Reported no-observed-adverse-effect levels (NOAELs) for TCS in mice ranged from 25 to 40 mg/kg/day [ ]. Thus, to explore dose-response relationships relevant to human environmental exposure, three TCS doses were chosen for mice: low (1 mg/kg/day), medium (10 mg/kg/day), and high (30 mg/kg/day). According to previous studies examining TCS administration via gavage in mice [ ], doses of 1, 10, and 30 mg/kg resulted in plasma concentrations ranging approximately from 0.55 to 16.35 ng/mL, and these levels were comparable to the serum concentrations detected in human studies [ , , ]. In this study, mice in the TCS-exposed groups received daily oral gavage of TCS dissolved in vehicle, while control mice received an equal volume of vehicle alone. After 8 weeks of exposure, all mice underwent either MI modeling or sham surgery to assess the impact of TCS exposure on post-MI outcomes.

2.3.

## Mouse MI model construction and intervention
Mouse MI models were induced using a modified surgical protocol reported previously [ ]. Briefly, male mice were placed on a thermostable surgical platform without mechanical ventilation, receiving inhalation anesthesia with 1.5 % isoflurane delivered at an oxygen flow rate of 0.5 L/min. A left fourth intercostal incision was made to expose the cardiac surface, followed by gentle exteriorization of the heart. The left anterior descending (LAD) coronary artery was permanently ligated 2 mm distal to its first branch using 6-0 silk suture. Immediately after ligation, the heart was repositioned into the thoracic cavity, and the incision was sequentially closed. Sham-operated controls underwent the same surgical steps except for LAD artery occlusion. Investigators remained blind to group allocations during both surgical procedures and subsequent echocardiographic assessments. At predefined time points post-MI, surviving mice were randomly selected (with group sizes standardized across all groups before being euthanized via isoflurane overdose, and their hearts were harvested for subsequent histological and molecular analyses. All myocardial tissue samples used for biochemical and molecular analyses, immunohistochemistry, and Western blot were harvested from the well-defined infarct core following LAD coronary artery ligation. This approach excluded interference from the border zone or remote normal myocardial tissue, with the boundary of the infarct area confirmed by gross visual inspection and hematoxylin-eosin (HE) staining prior to experiments.
To inhibit the AKT pathway activation following MI, mice received intraperitoneal injection of LY294002 at a dose of 30 mg/kg 1 h prior to surgery, as previously described in cardiac research [ ]. To block NTRK2 in mice, ANA-12 was orally administered at a dose of 0.5 mg/kg for four consecutive weeks, consistent with previous study [ ]. ZLN005 (15 mg/kg) was orally administered for 6 weeks [ ] and SR18292 was intraperitoneally injected into mice at a dose of 30 mg/kg once a day for 3 consecutive days before surgery [ ] to activate and inhibit the activity of PGC-1α, respectively. All mice in the control group were administered with an equal volume of vehicle. Nur77 knockout (KO) mice were successfully generated, and wild-type (WT) littermates were used as controls ( ). Building on prior research, we adopted Mito-TEMPO as a mitochondrial ROS scavenger (0.7 mg/kg daily i.p. for 6 days) and rotenone as a mitochondrial ROS inducer (1.5 mg/kg daily i.p. for 21 days) [ , ]. The detailed procedure of animal experiments has been illustrated and presented in .

2.4.

## Neonatal rat cardiomyocyte (NRCM) isolation
NRCMs were isolated from 1–3-day-old Sprague-Dawley rat ventricles (Slac Laboratory Animal Co., Ltd., Shanghai, China) using a previously described method [ ]. Briefly, neonatal rats were anesthetized, and hearts were perfused with ice-cold phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) to remove residual blood cells. Ventricular tissues were dissected, minced, and digested with type II collagenase (Gibco, USA) at 37 °C. After centrifugation and resuspension, cells underwent differential adhesion-based isolation to remove fibroblasts, with bromodeoxyuridine (BrdU, Beyotime, China) added to culture medium to further inhibit non-cardiomyocyte proliferation. Purified NRCMs were maintained in Dulbecco's modified Eagle medium (DMEM) supplemented with 10 % fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1 % penicillin-streptomycin.

2.5.

## Cell culture and intervention
The AC16 cell line was purchased from Merck-Millipore (SCC109, USA), which was derived from non-proliferating primary cultures of adult ventricular heart tissue [ ]. The AC16 cells and NRCMs were grown in DMEM supplemented with 10 % FBS and 1 % penicillin-streptomycin solution. To mimic an in vitro model of MI, as previously described [ ], hypoxic cell models were established as follows: AC16 cells or NRCMs were cultured in serum-free, glucose-free DMEM within a hypoxic chamber (Thermo, HERA cell 150i) under conditions of 5 % CO2, 1 % O2, and 94 % N2 for 6 h. As controls, cells were maintained in serum-free medium under normoxic conditions (5 % CO2, 21 % O2, 74 % N2).
To mimic in vivo exposure to TCS, AC16 cells or NRCMs were treated with 10 μM or 20 μM TCS for 24 h. These concentrations were comparable to those used in previous analogous in vitro studies [ , , , ] and were lower than or equivalent to the reported TCS concentration in human urine (13 μM) [ ]. During the final 6 h of the 24-h TCS exposure, either AC16 cells or NRCMs were subjected to the aforementioned hypoxic stimulation as described above. To antagonize NTRK2, AC16 cells were treated with ANA-12 (10 μM) for 24 h [ ]. To inhibit the activation of the mTOR pathway, AC16 cells were treated with 0.1 μM rapamycin for 24 h [ ]. Additionally, Chloroquine (CQ, 10 μM) was employed to prevent lysosomes from degrading autophagosomal contents, while the proteasome inhibitor MG132 (10 μM) served to suppress proteasome activity. In all in vitro experiments, equal volumes of vehicle control were added as controls.

2.6.

## RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) and data analysis procedures
Cardiac tissues from six mice were collected, comprising three MI mice without TCS exposure and three MI mice exposed to 10 mg/kg/d TCS for 8 weeks. RNA-seq experiments were conducted by Wuhan Metware Biology and Technology Co., Ltd., involving sample quality control, transcriptome library preparation, and high-throughput clustering-based sequencing. Differential gene expression analysis was performed using the DESeq2 R package (v1.20.0), with genes identified as differentially expressed (DEGs) based on an adjusted p-value <0.05.
Functional enrichment analyses were conducted using the clusterProfiler R package. For Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment, DEGs were analyzed across biological processes, cellular components, and molecular functions, with statistical modeling accounting for gene length bias; GO terms with adjusted p-values <0.05 were considered significantly enriched. Leveraging the KEGG database, which integrates molecular and systemic functional annotations, hypergeometric testing was applied to assess DEG enrichment in canonical pathways, using a p-value threshold of 0.05 to identify significant pathways. We also used Reactome to identify relevant pathways.
To detect subtle, coordinated expression changes not captured by individual gene significance thresholds, Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) was performed. Genes were first ranked by differential expression magnitude (combining log2 fold change and Wald statistic) between the two groups. Predefined gene sets were then evaluated for non-random enrichment at the extremes of this ranked list using a Kolmogorov-Smirnov-like statistic, enabling the identification of gene sets with consistent directional expression differences across biological states.

2.7.

## Western blotting
For the lysis procedure, cells and cardiac tissue samples were lysed using RIPA buffer (G2002, Servicebio, China). The protein concentration of the obtained cell/tissue lysates was determined to be 2 μg/μl, with a standard loading amount of 20 μg total protein per sample. The extracted proteins were separated by SDS-PAGE and then electrophoretically transferred onto a PVDF membrane (WGPVDF45, Servicebio, China). Membranes were incubated overnight at 4 °C with specific primary antibodies, followed by a 1-h incubation at room temperature (25 °C) with horseradish peroxidase-conjugated secondary antibodies (33101es60, Yeasen, China). Protein signals were visualized using an enhanced chemiluminescence (ECL) detection system (180–506, Tanon, China) and quantified via ImageJ software. Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH) was selected as the loading control for this study. Detailed information on the primary antibodies utilized is summarized in .

2.8.

## Real-time PCR
Total RNA was extracted from heart tissues and cells using the Trizol method. Subsequently, RNA samples were reverse-transcribed into cDNA with HiScript II Q RT SuperMix containing a gDNA wiper (R223-01, Vazyme, China) for quantitative PCR (qPCR) applications. Real-time qPCR assays were conducted on a LightCycler® 96 System (Roche, Switzerland) using ChamQ™ SYBR Color qPCR Master Mix (Q411-02, Vazyme, China). Gene expression data were normalized to the hypoxanthine–guanine phosphoribosyl transferase (HPRT), GAPDH, or β-actin housekeeping gene. Primer sequences used in this study are listed in .

2.9.

## Echocardiography
Transthoracic echocardiography was performed on mice using a Vivid 7 ultrasound system (GE, USA) to evaluate short-term and long-term cardiac damage on 3- and 21-days post-MI. Mice were anesthetized with 0.5 %–2 % isoflurane to maintain a heart rate of 450 ± 50 bpm and positioned supine on a heated platform. All measurements were conducted by an observer blinded to group assignments. Cardiac parameters were assessed at the mid-papillary level, including fractional shortening (FS%), ejection fraction (EF%), left ventricular internal dimension in systole (LVIDs), end-diastolic internal dimension (LVIDd), end-diastolic volume (LVEDV), and end-systolic volume (LVESV). LVEDV and LVESV were calculated using the biplane area-length method. EF% was determined via the formula: EF% = [(LVEDV-LVESV)/LVEDV] × 100 %. From the long-axis view at the papillary muscle level, 2D-guided M-mode tracings were obtained to measure LVIDs and LVIDd, with FS% calculated as: FS% = [(LVIDd-LVIDs)/LVIDd] × 100 %.

2.10.

## Immunohistochemistry staining
For immunohistochemistry, tissue sections were processed through deparaffinization, antigen retrieval (using citrate buffer), endogenous peroxidase inactivation, perimeter marking, and serum blocking. Primary antibodies were applied overnight at 4 °C, followed by room-temperature incubation with secondary antibodies. Stained sections were analyzed using bright-field microscopy and quantitative analysis was performed using ImageJ software.

2.11.

## Immunofluorescence staining
For immunofluorescence, heart tissue microarrays were dewaxed, rehydrated, and subjected to antigen retrieval with sodium citrate buffer. Cultured cells were washed with PBS, fixed in 4 % paraformaldehyde, and both tissues and cells were permeabilized with 0.1 % Triton X-100 and blocked with 3 % bovine serum. Samples were labeled with target-specific primary antibodies (mono- or multi-color) and imaged using a Zeiss confocal microscope (Germany). Quantitative analysis was performed by randomly selecting five fields per cardiac section and measuring fluorescence intensity with ImageJ software. All assessments were conducted by an investigator blinded to experimental group assignments.

2.12.

## Detection of ROS levels
Intracellular ROS levels were quantified using the DCFH-DA kit (S0033S, Beyotime, China). NRCMs or AC16 cells were seeded in 24-well plates and treated with experimental interventions when cell confluence reached 50 %. Cells were then incubated with 10 μM DCFH-DA at 37 °C for 30 min in the dark, followed by washing with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). Fluorescence imaging was performed using a TH4-200 fluorescent microscope (Olympus, Tokyo, Japan). For mitochondrial superoxide detection, MitoSox Red staining followed the manufacturer's protocol using the MitoSox Red Mitochondrial Superoxide Indicator (RM02822, Abclonal, China). Relative and mean ROS intensities were quantified in each group using ImageJ software.

2.13.

## Malondialdehyde (MDA) levels and superoxide dismutase (SOD) activity
Commercially sourced test kits (S0101S, S0131S, Beyotime, China) were applied to assess the levels of antioxidant enzymes and oxidative stress-related parameters based on the specified protocol. This assessment included the measurement of MDA content and SOD activity. All the assay operations were conducted in strict accordance with the instructions from the manufacturers.

2.14.

## Detection of myocardial enzymology
Three days after MI surgery, blood samples were collected from the abdominal aorta of each group. After a 30-min water bath at 37 °C, sera were centrifuged at 3000×g. Aliquots were transferred to 1.5 mL EP tubes and stored at −80 °C for later use. Myocardial enzyme indices, including lactic dehydrogenase (LDH) and creatine kinase isoenzyme (CK-MB), were measured using an automatic biochemical analyzer.

2.15.

## Transfection of recombinant adenovirus and adeno-associated virus
Recombinant adenovirus (Adv) and adeno-associated virus 9 (rAAV9) were used to modulate Nur77 expression in cultured NRCMs and NTRK2 or PGC-1α in mouse hearts, respectively. In NRCMs, we verified the successful overexpression of Nur77 using adenovirus ( ). A cardiac-specific chicken troponin T (cTnT) promoter was employed to drive tissue-specific overexpression of NTRK2 in cardiomyocytes of mouse hearts. Gene sequences for Nur77 and NTRK2 were obtained from GeneBank. Six-week-old male C57BL/6J wild-type mice were intravenously injected with rAAV9 at a dose of 3 × 1011 viral genomes (v.g.) per mouse two weeks prior to MI surgery to ensure transgene expression in cardiac tissue. We also demonstrated the successful overexpression of NTRK2 and Nur77 in mouse hearts based on the rAAV9 system ( ).

2.16.

## Plasmids and transfection
Human expression plasmids for Nur77 were procured from MiaoLing Plasmid Platform. For transient transfection, AC16 or 293T cells were transfected with the specified plasmids using the NEOFECT™ DNA transfection reagent following the manufacturer's instructions.

2.17.

## Immunoprecipitation/co-immunoprecipitation
Cell and tissue lysates (800 μg) were prepared and incubated overnight at 4 °C with anti-Nur77 antibody. Subsequently, 30 μL of protein G agarose beads (Cytiva) were added, and the mixture was incubated for an additional 4 h at 4 °C. After three washes with cold wash buffer, followed by three washes with lysis buffer (10 mM HEPES, pH 7.9; 10 mM KCl; 1.5 mM MgCl2; 50 mM NaF; 1 mM Na3VO4; 1 μM PMSF, supplemented with protease inhibitor cocktail), the immunoprecipitates were resuspended in 30 μL of loading buffer and subjected to Western blotting to examine total ubiquitin, ubiquitin (K48) modification, and binding to TRIM13.

2.18.

## Dual-luciferase reporter gene assay
The human NTRK2 promoter was cloned into the pGL3 promoter vector carrying a luciferase reporter gene. HEK293T cells or AC16 cells seeded in 24-well plates were co-transfected with the Nur77 overexpression plasmid (or empty vector), the luciferase reporter gene plasmid, and the TK-Renilla plasmid using SweTransDNA Transfection Reagent (Servicebio, G1805). After a 48-h incubation, the firefly/Renilla luciferase activity was measured by a dual-luciferase reporter system and a luminometer.

2.19.

## Histological analysis
Under anesthesia, hearts were harvested from each group, perfused with PBS, and then fixed in 4 % paraformaldehyde. After paraffin embedding, the heart tissue samples were sectioned into 5 μm-thick slices. These sections were used for two types of staining. HE staining was carried out to observe the general cardiac morphology, while Masson's trichrome staining was performed to evaluate the collagen content after MI. The fibrotic area from Masson's trichrome-stained sections was quantified using ImageJ software (National Institutes of Health, USA).

2.20.

## Molecular docking
We used AutoDock Vina 1.1.2 to perform molecular docking of TCS (PubChem CID: 5564) with Nur77 (PDB ID: 2QW4). PyMOL 2.4 preprocessed the protein. AutoDock Tools 1.5.6 generated PDBQT files. The Nur77 docking grid had dimensions of 58 Å × 92 Å × 98 Å, 1.00 Å spacing, and set coordinates, with other parameters default. The top 9 poses were output, and the best was the likely binding mode. PLIP and PyMOL 2.4 visualized results.
To explore Nur77-NTRK2 promoter binding, we used Nur77 (PDB ID: 2QW4). The Xiao Lab (http://biophy.hust.edu.cn/new/) created the NTRK2 promoter 3D-structure. PyMOL 2.4 preprocessed the protein. HDOCK SERVE (http://hdock.phys.hust.edu.cn/) did the protein-DNA docking, evaluated by three scores. The top-scoring model was selected, and PyMOL visualized interactions.

2.21.

## Statistical analysis
At least three independent cell experiments were performed to ensure the reliability of results. Normality of the data was assessed using the Shapiro-Wilk test. For statistical comparisons, one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was applied, followed by Bonferroni post hoc or Tukey's multiple comparison tests as appropriate. Data are presented as mean ± standard deviation (SD), and p values < 0.05 were considered statistically significant. All analyses were conducted using GraphPad Prism software (version 9.5.1).

3.

## Results
3.1.

## Long-term exposure to three doses of TCS exacerbated short- and long-term cardiac injury after MI in mice
To investigate the impact of long-term exposure to environmentally relevant concentrations of TCS on the prognosis of MI in mice, we administered TCS at three doses via oral administration for 8 weeks, followed by MI model induction. The experimental protocol was described in . The echocardiographic assessment conducted one day prior to Sham or MI surgery revealed that TCS exposure did not significantly deteriorate cardiac function in mice under physiological conditions ( ). Echocardiographic analysis performed on day 3 post-MI revealed that cardiac function in mice significantly deteriorated after MI, and chronic exposure to TCS at all three concentrations further exacerbated this dysfunction, manifested as significant declines in both left ventricular EF and fractional FS compared to the MI group treated without TCS ( A–B). Notably, even low-dose TCS exposure (1 mg/kg/day) induced a significant exacerbation of post-infarction cardiac dysfunction. Moreover, exposure to low, medium, and high doses of TCS further increased serum levels of post-infarction cardiac injury markers CK-MB and cTnT in a dose-dependent manner ( C–D). Following MI, the heart underwent an intense inflammatory response, while TCS exposure further exacerbated macrophage infiltration, as demonstrated by increased fluorescence intensity of the F4/80 marker ( E–G). Similarly, WB analysis revealed that levels of the neutrophil marker protein Ly6G were significantly elevated in the hearts of MI mice exposed to TCS, showing a consistent pattern of enhanced inflammatory cell accumulation ( H).
 To explore the long-term effects of TCS exposure on post-infarction mice, echocardiographic assessment of cardiac function was performed on day 21 after MI. Results showed that exposure to all three TCS concentrations further exacerbated long-term heart failure following MI ( I). Masson's Trichrome staining revealed that cardiac fibrosis in MI mice was significantly aggravated by TCS exposure, with enhanced collagen deposition in the infarcted myocardium ( J). Western blot analysis demonstrated that levels of α-SMA were further upregulated in the hearts of TCS-exposed MI mice ( K). Consistently, qRT-PCR confirmed significant upregulation of collagen-related genes Col1a1 and Col3a1 in the cardiac tissue of TCS-treated post-MI mice, aligning with the histological and protein expression data ( L–M). More importantly, TCS exposure significantly increased the 21-day cumulative mortality rate in male mice after MI (P for trend = 0.0435) ( ).
Subsequently, we further investigated the effects of TCS exposure on post-MI cardiac injury in female mice. As displayed in N, immunofluorescence results showed that on day 3 post-MI, there was significant neutrophil infiltration in the infarcted area of female mice, while TCS dose-dependently exacerbated such neutrophil infiltration, as evidenced by increased Ly6G fluorescence intensity, suggesting a further enhancement of inflammation. Echocardiographic results demonstrated that exposure to low-dose TCS led to a significant impairment of cardiac systolic function on day 3 post-MI, with medium and high doses of TCS further exacerbating cardiac dysfunction ( O). On day 21 post-MI in female mice, qRT-PCR results revealed that the cardiac fibrosis markers Col1a1 and Col3a1 were significantly upregulated, indicating enhanced cardiac remodeling ( P). Similarly, TCS exposure significantly increased the 21-day cumulative mortality rate in female mice after MI (P for trend = 0.0392) ( ). Additionally, echocardiographic findings revealed that TCS exposure significantly exacerbated cardiac function at 21 days post-MI ( ). These results demonstrated that environmentally relevant concentrations of TCS exposure significantly exacerbated the inflammatory response and cardiac dysfunction during the acute phase of MI in both male and female mice, while also worsening long-term ventricular remodeling and promoting heart failure.

3.2.

## TCS exposure exacerbated ferroptosis and mitochondrial dysfunction after ischemic myocardial injury both in mouse models and in NRCMs
To further investigate the biological processes altered by TCS exposure following MI, we performed RNA-seq sequencing ( ) and bioinformatics analysis. GO analysis revealed that TCS exposure significantly disrupted mitochondrial functions in post-MI hearts, specifically involving the electron transport chain and mitochondrial biogenesis ( ). Reactome pathway analysis consistently demonstrated perturbed OXPHOS and altered mitochondrial dynamics in ischemic hearts upon TCS exposure ( ). KEGG pathway enrichment identified ferroptosis and cellular senescence as the two most significantly dysregulated biological processes ( ). GSEA further confirmed that TCS exposure exacerbated cardiac cellular senescence while suppressing mitochondrial OXPHOS after MI ( ).
Building on bioinformatics results, we first experimentally validated whether TCS exposure further exacerbated post-MI mitochondrial dysfunction in the heart. qRT-PCR using tissue samples from sham-operated and day 3 post-MI mice demonstrated that TCS exposure exacerbated the downregulation of Mfn1 and Mfn2 (key genes regulating mitochondrial fusion) mRNA levels in post-infarction mouse hearts ( A). WB analysis, shown in B, corroborated this finding, showing that TCS exposure not only enhanced the MI-induced upregulation of DRP1 (a mitochondrial fission protein) but also further suppressed MFN2 protein expression, collectively indicating a significant disruption of mitochondrial dynamics. As displayed in C, qRT-PCR analysis revealed that TCS exposure further exacerbated the MI-induced downregulation of mRNA levels for mitochondrial OXPHOS-related genes, including mt-ATP6, mt-Cytb, mt-Nd1, mt-Co1, Cox4i1, Ndufs1, Ndufb8, Uqcrc2, and Sdhb in mouse hearts. In NRCMs, WB analysis showed that levels of mitochondrial OXPHOS-related proteins, including ATP5A, MTCO1, SDHB, and NFUFB8, decreased following hypoxic stimulation, while TCS exposure further suppressed their expression ( D). ROS, critical mediators of lipid peroxidation and ferroptosis promotion, were found to be markedly elevated in TCS-exposed NRCMs under hypoxic conditions, with both mitochondrial and intracellular ROS levels ( E).
 Additionally, TCS exposure further exacerbated MI-induced ferroptosis, manifested as upregulating ACSL4 protein levels and reducing GPX4 protein expression ( G). Immunohistochemical staining corroborated these results, showing a significant decrease in GPX4 expression in the hearts of TCS-exposed post-MI mice ( H). ROS-mediated lipid peroxidation has been reported to play a critical role in the process of ferroptosis [ ]. Here, as shown in I–J, we found that TCS exposure exacerbated the accumulation of malondialdehyde (MDA)—a lipid peroxidation product—in the heart following MI, while impairing the activity of the antioxidant enzyme superoxide dismutase (SOD). In NRCMs, TCS-exposed cells exhibited aggravated ferroptosis following hypoxic stimulation, with WB analysis confirming further increases in ACSL4 and decreases in GPX4 protein levels, consistent with in vivo observations ( K). As displayed in L, FerroOrange staining, a fluorescent method used to detect intracellular iron levels, showed that NRCMs exposed to TCS exhibited significantly stronger fluorescence intensity following hypoxia induction, indicating ferroptosis activation. These results suggested that TCS exposure significantly exacerbated MI-induced mitochondrial dysfunction and ferroptosis.
We also examined the effects of TCS exposure on mitochondrial damage and ferroptosis following MI in female mice. As shown in M, qRT-PCR results revealed that TCS exposure further downregulated the transcriptional levels of OXPHOS-related genes post-MI, including Sdhb, Ndufv1, and mt-Nd1. In N–O, Western blot results similarly confirmed that TCS exposure further reduced the levels of OXPHOS-related proteins post-MI, such as MTCO1, SDHB, and NDUFB8. Furthermore, the protein level of the ferroptosis-related marker ACSL4 was significantly upregulated in MI mice from the TCS exposure group, whereas the protein level of GPX4 was further decreased. These findings indicated that the exacerbation of MI injury induced by TCS exposure is not gender-specific, and mitochondrial dysfunction and ferroptosis may represent common mechanisms in both male and female mice.

3.3.

## TCS exposure exacerbated cardiomyocyte senescence after ischemic myocardial injury both in mouse models and in NRCMs
Building on the aforementioned bioinformatics results ( ), we further examined the effect of TCS exposure on post-infarction cellular senescence following MI. As displayed in A, qRT-PCR analysis revealed that TCS exposure significantly upregulated the mRNA levels of senescence markers P21 and P53 in post-MI mouse hearts. In B, WB analysis further confirmed that TCS exposure exacerbated post-infarction cellular senescence in mice, even at the low dose of 1 mg/kg/day, as evidenced by significant increases in the protein levels of senescence markers P16 and P21. Senescent myocardial cells secrete SASP factors, which induce senescence in neighboring cells, recruit inflammatory cells, and exacerbate fibrosis following MI [ ]. In C, qRT-PCR analysis revealed that TCS exposure significantly upregulated the mRNA levels of SASP-related genes in post-MI hearts, including Ccl2, Cxcl1, Cxcl3, Cxcl10, Edn3, Gdf15, Tgfb2, Il-1β, Il-6, and Tnf-α. Immunohistochemical staining showed that TCS exposure further increased the expression of γ-H2AX—a marker of DNA damage—in the hearts of post-MI mice ( D and E). Immunofluorescence co-staining with the myocardial cell marker cTnI revealed a significant increase in P21 expression specifically within cTnI-positive cardiomyocytes of TCS-exposed post-MI mice ( F and G), indicating TCS-induced enhancement of senescence in cardiomyocytes. In-vitro experiments using NRCMs revealed that TCS exposure further upregulated the mRNA levels of senescence markers P16, P21, and Glb1 in hypoxia-induced NRCMs, as determined by qRT-PCR ( H). WB analysis consistently confirmed increased protein levels of P16 and P21 in hypoxic NRCMs treated with TCS, aligning with the transcriptional data ( I). In J, qRT-PCR also demonstrated TCS-induced upregulation of SASP-related genes—including Edn3, Gdf15, and Tgfb2—in hypoxia-damaged cardiomyocytes. Immunofluorescence staining corroborated these findings, showing a significant increase in P16 expression specifically within TCS-exposed NRCMs ( K).
 Subsequently, we investigated the impact of TCS exposure on cardiac senescence in female mice post-MI. qRT-PCR results demonstrated that the transcriptional levels of SASP-related genes were significantly elevated in the hearts of female mice after MI, while TCS exposure further enhanced SASP levels, including those of Edn3, Tgfβ2, and Gdf15 ( L). Western blot results also confirmed that the level of P21 was significantly increased in post-MI female mice from the TCS exposure group ( M). These results indicated that TCS exposure significantly promoted MI-induced cardiomyocyte senescence in both male and female mice.
To determine whether TCS-exacerbated mitochondrial dysfunction and ROS burst served as the key drivers of ferroptosis and cellular senescence after MI, we employed Mito-TEMPO—a mitochondrial-targeted antioxidant—to scavenge mitochondrial ROS in the hearts of post-MI mice, followed by qRT-PCR analysis of heart tissues. As displayed in , consistent with previous findings, TCS exposure significantly upregulated the mRNA levels of senescence markers P21 and P53 in post-MI hearts. Notably, Mito-TEMPO treatment substantially attenuated this TCS-induced upregulation, restoring P21 and P53 expression to levels comparable with those in MI mice without TCS exposure. The TCS-induced upregulation of the ferroptosis marker ACSL4 mRNA level was similarly restored to levels comparable with those in Mito-TEMPO-treated post-MI mice without TCS exposure ( ). In , the myocardial fibrosis markers Col1a1 and Col3a1 also exhibited a similar pattern. Moreover, to explore nonspecific antioxidant effects, we performed a dual intervention in mice using rotenone as a mitochondrial ROS inducer ( ). As displayed in , the results showed that in MI mice exposed to TCS, rotenone further induced ferroptosis and senescence in the heart, which was characterized by increased ACSL4 and P21 protein levels and decreased GPX4 protein levels, while Mito-TEMPO inhibited these phenotypes. Most importantly, rotenone reversed the therapeutic effect of Mito-TEMPO. The mRNA levels of SASP-related factors including IL-1β, IL-6 and Gdf15 demonstrated similar patterns ( ). These results also confirmed that mitochondrial ROS was a key factor contributing to the exacerbation of ferroptosis and senescence in MI mice after TCS exposure.

3.4.

## TCS exposure in human cardiomyocytes exacerbated hypoxia-induced mitochondrial dysfunction-dependent ROS burst and promoted ferroptosis and senescence
To investigate the effects of TCS on human-derived cardiac myocyte cell lines, we further examined the impact of TCS on hypoxia-stimulated AC16 cells. As shown in A, JC-1 staining indicated that TCS further exacerbated hypoxia-induced mitochondrial membrane potential decline, manifested as a further decrease in the red/green fluorescence ratio, which suggested mitochondrial damage. WB results confirmed that TCS exposure further induced the downregulation of OXPHOS proteins caused by hypoxia, as the protein levels of ATP5A, MTCO1, SDHB, and NDUFB8 were further decreased ( B). Notably, TCS exposure potently promoted the production of mitochondrial and intracellular ROS in AC16 cells under hypoxic conditions ( C and D). qRT-PCR analysis confirmed that TCS further induced significant upregulation of mRNA levels for senescence markers, including P16, P21, P53, and Glb1 ( E). As shown in F, TCS exposure significantly increased the protein levels of P16 and P21 in AC16 cells after hypoxia treatment. The immunofluorescence results were consistent with these results, indicating that TCS treatment further enhanced the fluorescence intensity of P16 and P21 in hypoxia-stimulated cells ( G–I). The mRNA levels of hypoxia-induced SASP-related genes also significantly increased in the TCS-exposed group, including CXCL1, CXCL10, IL-1β, IL-6, MMP1, MMP3, GDF15, and EDN3 ( J).
 Moreover, TCS exposure significantly exacerbated hypoxia-induced ferroptosis in AC16 cells, manifested as increased ACSL4 protein levels and decreased GPX4 protein levels verified by WB ( K and L). Compared with hypoxic AC16 cells exposed to DMSO, MDA levels in hypoxic AC16 cells after TCS exposure were further increased ( M). In N, FerroOrange staining indicated that TCS exposure led to a further increase in iron ion accumulation in hypoxia-induced AC16 cells, manifested as enhanced fluorescence intensity, confirming the exacerbation of ferroptosis. Collectively, these results demonstrate that TCS significantly exacerbates hypoxic injury in human-derived cardiac myocytes, primarily by worsening mitochondrial dysfunction-induced oxidative stress to exacerbate cellular senescence and ferroptosis—findings that underscore its clinical relevance.

3.5.

## NTRK2 was identified as a pivotal downstream molecule mediating TCS-induced aggravation of ischemic myocardial injury
Previous researchers have shown that TCS downregulated the expression of NTRK2, which has been demonstrated to play a critical protective role following MI [ , , ]. To elucidate the potential mechanisms by which TCS induced the exacerbation of MI, we screened RNA-seq data ( ) and, combined with experimental validation, found that the mRNA level of NTRK2 was significantly downregulated in post-infarction hearts of the TCS-exposed group ( A). IHC results confirmed that compared to TCS-unexposed MI mice, the expression of NTRK2 in the hearts of mice post-MI exposed to three concentrations of TCS was significantly downregulated ( B). We used ANA-12, a potent selective NTRK2 receptor antagonist, to perform further experiments in AC16 cells and demonstrate the critical role of NTRK2 in TCS-induced poor prognosis of MI. Results showed that NTRK2 antagonism further exacerbated hypoxia-induced mitochondrial ROS production in TCS-exposed cells, manifested as a further enhancement of Mito-SOX fluorescence ( C). Furthermore, qRT-PCR and WB results confirmed that TCS increased the mRNA and protein levels of senescence markers P16 and P21 in hypoxia-treated AC16 cells, while NTRK2 antagonism further exacerbated cellular senescence ( D and E). Immunofluorescence results confirmed that γ-H2AX exhibited a similar expression trend, confirming that NTRK2 regulated the exacerbation of TCS-induced post-hypoxia senescence ( F and G). NTRK2 antagonism also exacerbated the elevation of TCS-induced post-hypoxic SASP factors in AC16 cells, including GDF15, TGFβ2, EDN3, and IL-1β ( H). In I–J, FerroOrange staining indicated that NTRK2 antagonism further exacerbated TCS-induced post-hypoxic ferroptosis in AC16 cells. qRT-PCR WB results confirmed that NTRK2 antagonism led to a further increase in the ferroptosis marker ACSL4 and a further decrease in GPX4 in TCS-induced post-hypoxic AC16 cells ( K and L).
 We further validated that NTRK2 antagonism exacerbated 10 mg/kg/day TCS-induced ferroptosis and senescence in the hearts of mice post-MI. Echocardiography revealed that NTRK2 antagonism further deteriorated cardiac function in TCS-exposed MI mice, manifested as decreased EF and FS ( M). WB results confirmed that NTRK2 antagonism intensified TCS-induced ferroptosis and cellular senescence in post-MI mouse hearts, evidenced by increased protein levels of P16, P21, and ACSL4, and decreased GPX4 protein expression ( N). Additionally, WB results confirmed that NTRK2 antagonism exacerbated TCS-induced ventricular remodeling in post-MI mice, as evidenced by further increased protein levels of α-SMA and Col3a1 ( O). These results suggested that NTRK2 may play a crucial role in the mechanism underlying the deterioration of MI prognosis induced by TCS exposure.

3.6.

## Nur77 served as a key mediator in TCS-induced downregulation of NTRK2 following ischemic myocardial injury
To elucidate the specific mechanisms by which TCS induced post-infarction downregulation of NTRK2 transcriptional levels, we hypothesized that TCS might lead to a reduction in the protein levels of upstream transcription factors governing NTRK2 expression. As shown in A, we used the SuperPred database to predict potential target proteins of TCS and used the KnockTF and FIMO JASPAR databases to predict upstream transcription factors of NTRK2, followed by identifying their intersection, and Nur77 was identified as a potential mediator. Molecular docking analysis revealed that Nur77 exhibited favourable binding affinity with TCS ( B). We found that TCS exposure did not alter Nur77 mRNA levels in the hearts of mice post-MI ( ). Thus, we further investigated changes in its protein levels. In AC16 cells, TCS exposure further reduced the protein level of Nur77 in hypoxia-treated cells ( C). Additionally, nuclear fractionation followed by WB analysis showed that TCS exposure decreased the nuclear Nur77 protein level in hypoxia-treated AC16 cells ( D). WB results further indicated that inhibiting the proteasomal pathway with MG132 significantly reversed TCS-induced post-hypoxic Nur77 downregulation, while inhibition of the lysosomal pathway with chloroquine had no effect, suggesting that ubiquitination may play a critical role in TCS-induced Nur77 degradation ( E). Further experiments demonstrated that TCS exposure significantly increased the total ubiquitination level of Nur77 in hypoxia-treated AC16 cells ( F) and in the hearts of post-MI mice ( G). Given that K48-linked ubiquitination is the major pathway for protein degradation [ ], we further examined the effect of TCS on K48-linked ubiquitination of Nur77. Results confirmed that TCS exposure also increased K48-linked ubiquitination of Nur77 after hypoxic injury in both in vivo and in vitro models ( H and I). In J, molecular docking analysis indicated that Nur77 exhibits favourable binding ability with NTRK2. qRT-PCR results showed that Nur77 knockout in mouse hearts significantly reduced NTRK2 mRNA levels ( K), whereas AAV9-mediated cardiomyocyte-specific overexpression of Nur77 significantly increased NTRK2 mRNA levels ( L). WB analysis in the hearts of Nur77 knockout mice confirmed that NTRK2 protein levels were significantly decreased compared with those in control mouse hearts ( M). Using adenoviruses or plasmids to overexpress Nur77 in NRCMs and human AC16 cells, qRT-PCR results showed that NTRK2 mRNA levels were significantly increased compared with control groups ( N–O). WB analysis confirmed that Nur77 overexpression elevated NTRK2 protein levels in NRCMs ( P). Dual-luciferase reporter gene assays were performed in 293T and AC16 cells, where cells were transfected with control or Nur77-expressing plasmids to assess the regulation of the firefly luciferase gene driven by the NTRK2 promoter. Results demonstrated that Nur77 significantly enhanced luciferase activity in both cell lines, confirming its direct transcriptional regulation of NTRK2 by binding to its promoter region ( Q–R). Subsequently, we further validated these findings in a TCS-induced post-MI deterioration mouse model. WB results showed that Nur77 knockout further reduced NTRK2 protein levels in mouse hearts, whereas AAV9-mediated cardiomyocyte-specific overexpression of Nur77 elevated NTRK2 protein levels ( S–T). As shown in U, immunofluorescence co-staining with the cardiomyocyte marker cTnI indicated that Nur77 knockout significantly reduced NTRK2 expression in cardiomyocytes of post-MI hearts from TCS-exposed mice, manifested as a notable decrease in red fluorescence intensity. Immunofluorescence staining in AC16 cells confirmed that TCS exposure led to a decrease in NTRK2 fluorescence intensity after hypoxia, while Nur77 overexpression restored NTRK2 fluorescence intensity ( V). These results confirmed that TCS exposure reduced NTRK2 expression by increasing K48-linked ubiquitination and degradation of Nur77, thereby inhibiting NTRK2 transcription.
 Previous studies have confirmed that the E3 ubiquitin ligase TRIM13 is a key molecule regulating the protein stability of Nur77, particularly through K48-linked ubiquitination [ , ]. Therefore, further validation was conducted, revealing that TCS exposure significantly upregulated the expression of Trim13 protein in AC16 cells after hypoxia ( ). Moreover, CoIP experiments demonstrated that the binding of TRIM13 to Nur77 was significantly increased in the hearts of MI mice exposed to TCS ( ). These results indicated that TRIM13 may be the key molecule through which TCS mediated the increased ubiquitination and degradation of Nur77.

3.7.

## Nur77/NTRK2 axis regulated senescence and ferroptosis after TCS-induced aggravation of ischemic myocardial injury
To investigate whether TCS exacerbated the prognosis of MI through the Nur77/NTRK2 cascade, we employed AAV9-NTRK2 adeno-associated virus for cardiomyocyte-specific NTRK2 overexpression and Nur77 knockout mice in further experiments. As shown in A–B, echocardiographic analysis revealed that cardiomyocyte-specific NTRK2 overexpression ameliorated 10 mg/kg/day TCS-induced post-3-day MI cardiac dysfunction. Conversely, genetic deletion of Nur77 exacerbated heart failure progression in response to TCS; however, this detrimental effect was reversed by concurrent NTRK2 overexpression. Notably, qRT-PCR revealed that the Nur77/NTRK2 axis regulates the transcription of OXPHOS-related genes following TCS-induced MI models, suggesting that mitochondrial function modulation remained a critical mechanism in this process ( ). Both qRT-PCR and WB analyses demonstrated that Nur77 knockout exacerbated TCS-induced post-MI cellular senescence, whereas cardiomyocyte-specific overexpression of NTRK2 attenuated this senescence phenotype, as evidenced by decreased expression of the senescence markers P16 and P21 ( C–D). The mRNA levels of SASP factors and the positive staining rate of Glb1 immunohistochemistry showed similar trends, suggesting that TCS regulates post-3-day MI cardiac senescence through the Nur77/NTRK2 axis three days after infarction ( E–F). Additionally, genetic deletion of Nur77 exacerbated TCS-induced ferroptosis 3 days after MI, as evidenced by further increased protein levels of ACSL4 and decreased GPX4; conversely, cardiomyocyte-specific NTRK2 overexpression mitigated ferroptosis in this context ( G).
 Further experiments were performed to investigate the regulatory role of the Nur77/NTRK2 axis in the long-term prognosis of TCS-induced MI, specifically focusing on the modulatory effects at 21 days post-MI. In H, echocardiographic analysis revealed that genetic deletion of Nur77 exacerbated TCS-induced heart failure at 21 days post-MI, whereas cardiomyocyte-specific NTRK2 overexpression improved cardiac function, as demonstrated by the recovery of EF and FS. qRT-PCR data confirmed that the Nur77/NTRK2 axis regulated long-term cellular senescence and ventricular remodeling following TCS-exposed MI ( I–J). Immunofluorescence results, including Col1a1 and α-SMA, were also consistent with prior findings, further corroborating the critical regulatory role of the Nur77/NTRK2 axis in TCS-mediated ventricular remodeling at 21 days post-MI ( K–N). These results indicated that TCS exposure regulated short- and long-term post-MI ischemic injury through Nur77 and its downstream target NTRK2.

3.8.

## TCS regulated PGC-1α expression via Nur77/NTRK2-mediated AKT/mTOR/YY1 signaling transduction
Since NTRK2 was not directly linked to mitochondrial function, we aimed to further investigate the key downstream mechanisms through which TCS modulates post-MI mitochondrial function via the Nur77/NTRK2 axis. qRT-PCR results showed that compared with MI mice without TCS exposure, exposure to three concentrations of TCS significantly reduced the mRNA levels of PGC-1α in mouse hearts ( A), which was consistent with the results in RNA-Seq data ( ). Immunofluorescence analysis revealed that in MI hearts of male mice exposed to 10 mg/kg/day TCS, the fluorescence intensity of PGC-1α was significantly decreased, and PGC-1α showed colocalization with the cardiomyocyte-specific marker cTnI ( B). Consistently, the protein levels of PGC-1α were significantly downregulated in MI hearts of female mice exposed to TCS ( ). As displayed in C, we performed correlation analysis on the mRNA levels of PGC-1α with senescence-related markers and mitochondrial OXPHOS-related genes in cardiac tissues from MI mice, including TCS-exposed MI mice, and results revealed that PGC-1α expression was significantly negatively correlated with senescence markers (P21, P53) and SASP factors (Gdf15, Tgfβ2), while it was significantly positively correlated with the mRNA levels of mitochondrial OXPHOS-related genes (Ndufv1, Ndufb8, mt-ND1, Ndufs1). Our further validation revealed that knockout of Nur77—an upstream transcription factor of NTRK2—significantly reduced PGC-1α mRNA levels in mouse hearts ( D). Plasmid-mediated overexpression of Nur77 in HEK293T and AC16 cells significantly increased PGC-1α mRNA levels ( E–F). Additionally, in Nur77-overexpressing AC16 cells and control cells, NTRK2 expression showed a significant positive correlation with PGC-1α expression ( G). The results of qRT-PCR demonstrated that the knockout of Nur77 significantly reduced the transcriptional level of PGC-1α in the hearts of MI mice after exposure to TCS, while the overexpression of NTRK2 in cardiomyocytes restored the mRNA level of PGC-1α ( H). These results demonstrated for the first time that Nur77 may regulate PGC-1α expression in cardiomyocytes via NTRK2.
 Previous studies have reported that NTRK2 regulates PGC-1α-mediated mitochondrial function in cardiomyocytes via the downstream AKT/mTOR/YY1 signaling [ , ]. Specifically, researchers have demonstrated that NTRK2 activates the AKT/mTOR pathway to modulate YY1 expression; as a transcription factor, YY1 directly drives PGC-1α transcription, thereby linking NTRK2 signaling to the regulation of mitochondrial function [ , ]. Therefore, we further validated whether TCS regulated PGC-1α expression via Nur77/NTRK2-mediated AKT/mTOR/YY1 signaling transduction, thereby influencing post-MI injury. In I, WB results demonstrated that in the MI mouse models exposed to TCS, Nur77 regulates the protein levels of p-AKT, YY1, and PGC-1α via downstream NTRK2. Specifically, Nur77 knockout suppressed the activation of the AKT pathway and reduced the protein levels of YY1 and PGC-1α, whereas NTRK2 overexpression reversed these phenotypic changes. We further utilized the mTOR pathway inhibitor rapamycin to investigate whether activation of the mTOR pathway is a critical step for Nur77-mediated upregulation of PGC-1α transcription. As shown in J, following Nur77 overexpression in AC16 cells, treatment with equal volumes of DMSO or rapamycin revealed that rapamycin-mediated mTOR inhibition significantly attenuated the Nur77-induced increase in PGC-1α mRNA levels. As displayed in K, following cardiomyocyte-specific overexpression of NTRK2 via AAV9 adeno-associated virus in Nur77-knockout MI mice exposed to TCS, LY294002 significantly suppressed mTOR pathway activation and the protein levels of downstream YY1 and PGC-1α compared to treatment with equal volumes of DMSO. These findings further confirmed that TCS regulated PGC-1α expression via Nur77/NTRK2-mediated AKT/mTOR/YY1 signaling transduction.
Finally, we investigated whether the inhibition of PGC-1α served as a key downstream target through which TCS, via Nur77/NTRK2-mediated mitochondrial function, exacerbated ischemic injury. As shown in L, the results confirmed that in Nur77-knockout MI mice exposed to TCS, following cardiomyocyte-specific overexpression of NTRK2 via AAV9 adeno-associated virus, inhibition of PGC-1α using SR18292 significantly exacerbated cellular senescence and ferroptosis, which was manifested by upregulated protein levels of P16, P21, and ACSL4, as well as downregulated GPX4 protein. Additionally, it worsened ventricular remodeling at day 21 post-MI, evident from increased expression of fibrotic proteins including col3a1 and α-SMA ( M). These results confirmed that TCS exposure exacerbated ischemic injury by deteriorating mitochondrial function, a process mediated by Nur77 degradation-induced transcriptional repression of NTRK2 and subsequent inhibition of downstream AKT/mTOR/YY1 signaling, ultimately resulting in reduced PGC-1α mRNA and protein levels.

3.9.

## Pharmacological activation of PGC-1α with ZLN005 mitigated TCS-induced exacerbation of post-MI injury
To explore potential translational therapeutics, we investigated the ameliorative effects of the PGC-1α activator ZLN005 on 10 mg/kg/day TCS-induced poor prognosis post-MI, aiming to identify candidate interventions for clinical translation. As shown in A, echocardiography results demonstrated that in TCS-exposed MI mice, the ZLN005 treatment group significantly improved cardiac function, as evidenced by increased EF and FS levels at day 3 post-MI. WB results demonstrated that ZLN005 treatment significantly increased the expression levels of OXPHOS-related proteins, including ATP5A, MTCO1, and SDHB ( B). qRT-PCR analysis similarly confirmed that ZLN005 treatment substantially upregulated the transcriptional levels of OXPHOS-related genes, such as mt-ATP6, mt-ND1, mt-Cytb, mt-Co1, Ndufb8, Uqcrc2, Cox4i1, Ndufv1, and Ndufs1 ( C). ZLN005 treatment also reduced the mRNA levels of TCS-induced post-MI senescence markers, including P16, P21, and Glb1 ( D), as well as the mRNA expression of SASP factors such as Gdf15, Edn3, Cxcl1, Cxcl3, Cxcl10, and IL-6 ( E). WB results confirmed that TCS-induced ferroptosis and cellular senescence after MI were ameliorated by ZLN005, as evidenced by decreased protein levels of P16, P21, and ACSL4, and increased GPX4 protein levels ( F). We further investigated the effect of ZLN005 on the long-term prognosis of TCS-exposed MI mice. As shown in G, results demonstrated that ZLN005 improved cardiac function and alleviated heart failure in mice on day 21 post-MI. qRT-PCR results confirmed that ZLN005 alleviated TCS-induced cellular senescence in post-MI mice on day 21, as demonstrated by downregulated mRNA levels of senescence markers P16 and P21 ( H). More importantly, as shown in I–J, results from both qRT-PCR and WB confirmed that ZLN005 attenuated ventricular remodeling in TCS-exposed mice with MI on day 21 post-MI, as indicated by the downregulation of fibrotic markers. Masson's trichrome staining further confirmed that cardiac fibrosis in TCS-exposed MI mice was attenuated by ZLN005 on day 21 post-MI ( K). To rule out the off-target effects of the ZLN005, the AAV9.cTnT.shPGC-1α virus was constructed and injected into mice via tail vein. After 2 weeks, specific knockdown of PGC-1α in cardiomyocytes was achieved ( ). Echocardiographic results showed that in MI mice exposed to TCS, ZLN005 treatment significantly improved cardiac function, while cardiomyocyte-specific knockdown of PGC-1α reversed this protective effect ( ). qPCR results indicated that ZLN005 treatment increased the mRNA levels of oxidative phosphorylation-related proteins in the heart, including Ndufb8 and Ndufv1, while knockdown of PGC-1α decreased their levels ( ). In addition, cardiomyocyte-specific knockdown of PGC-1α also significantly reversed the ameliorating effects of ZLN005 on ferroptosis and senescence ( ). These results confirmed that the pharmacological effects of ZLN005 on MI mice after TCS exposure were mainly dependent on PGC-1α, excluding off-target effects. Taken together, these findings indicated that ZLN005 held promise for improving cardiac dysfunction in TCS-exposed mice after MI, suggesting its potential for clinical translation.
 

4.

## Discussion
The poor prognosis following MI remains a critical clinical challenge. This study is the first to demonstrate that TCS, a ubiquitous environmental contaminant, exacerbates post-MI ventricular remodeling and cardiac dysfunction by driving cardiomyocyte ferroptosis and senescence. Mechanistically, TCS induces K48-linked ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation of the nuclear receptor Nur77, reducing transcription of NTRK2 and dampening the AKT/mTOR/YY1 signaling axis. This cascade downregulates PGC-1α, a key regulator of mitochondrial OXPHOS, triggering ROS burst. Excessive ROS promotes lipid peroxidation and DNA damage, synergistically activating ferroptosis and senescence to worsen acute injury and long-term remodeling. These findings establish TCS as a mitochondrial toxin that links environmental exposure to dysregulated cell injury pathways after MI. Importantly, pharmacological activation of PGC-1α with ZLN005 mitigates TCS-induced damage, highlighting a targeted strategy to counteract environmental pollutant-mediated adverse cardiac outcomes and underscoring the study's significance for improving post-MI prognosis.
MI, a cardiovascular disease with high global incidence and mortality, urgently requires solutions for short-term rescue of dying cardiomyocytes and long-term prevention of heart failure caused by ventricular remodeling [ ]. Studies have emphasized the significant impact of environmental factors on MI outcomes, including extreme temperature events and fine particulate matter [ , , ]. TCS, a broad-spectrum antimicrobial ingredient widely used in personal care products, cosmetics, toys, fabrics, and other household items, is one of the most frequently detected pollutants in human bodies and environmental matrices [ ].A recent population-based study has suggested that TCS exposure may increase the risk of coronary heart disease partially by elevating oxidative stress, while the specific mechanisms remain unclear [ ]. However, research on the impact of TCS exposure on MI prognosis remains scarce. In this study, we first demonstrated that long-term exposure to environmentally relevant concentrations of TCS exacerbates both short- and long-term myocardial injury following MI in mice. By establishing a link between this common environmental contaminant and one of the most prevalent cardiovascular diseases, the study holds important public health implications. An animal study conducted in 2023 using C57BL/6 mice (similar in age and strain to this study) administered TCS via gavage at 100 mg/kg and 200 mg/kg for 14 days, with free serum TCS concentrations of 54.8 ng/mL and 108.56 ng/mL, respectively [ ]. Given TCS has a metabolic half-life of 10–20 h in both male and female mice [ ], pharmacokinetic calculations derived plasma TCS concentrations of ∼0.55 ng/mL, 5.45 ng/mL, and 16.35 ng/mL for our study doses of 1, 10, and 30 mg/kg, respectively. Moreover, human studies show relevant serum TCS levels: in Chinese pregnant women, median serum TCS was 1.08 ng/mL (max 5.84 ng/mL) [ ]; in a Danish population, the 75th and 90th percentile serum TCS were 6.64 and 18.77 ng/mL (max 237.68 ng/mL) [ ]; and in a Swedish study, median blood TCS was 0.79 ng/mL (max 657.85 ng/mL) [ ]. These human TCS levels were comparable to our mouse plasma concentrations; even the 30 mg/kg group in this study had lower serum levels of TCS than those in humans after using mouthwash containing 0.03 % TCS [ ], confirming the environmental relevance of doses used in this study.
Notably, we found that even the lowest dose of TCS tested—1 mg/kg/day, equivalent to the daily human exposure via general environmental routes [ ]—significantly worsened post-MI prognosis in mice. Given the extremely high detection rates of TCS in human body fluids (Over 90 %) [ ], these findings underscore the necessity to recognize the potential risks of TCS exposure for MI outcomes, as its detrimental effects on post-infarction heart injury should not be underestimated.
Both ferroptosis and cellular senescence have been confirmed as critical drivers of myocardial injury and ventricular remodeling after MI. Cellular senescence is a stable state of cell cycle arrest induced by various intrinsic and extrinsic damages, including replicative stress, oncogenic activation, oxidative and genotoxic stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, irradiation, or chemotherapeutic drugs [ , ]. MI has been shown to significantly upregulate cellular senescence, manifested by increased expression of senescence markers and pronounced induction of the SASP [ ]. By secreting SASP factors, senescent cardiomyocytes induce senescence in neighboring cells such as fibroblasts to promote pathological remodeling post-MI. Studies have demonstrated that clearing senescent cells with senolytic agents or genetic knockout strategy ameliorates myocardial remodeling, cardiac dysfunction, and overall survival in mice post-MI [ , , ]. In this study, we found that TCS exposure further exacerbated cardiomyocyte senescence and the SASP following MI in mice, which explained the increased acute-phase inflammatory cell infiltration and aggravated long-term ventricular remodeling induced by TCS after MI, highlighting the pivotal role of senescence-related pathways in mediating TCS's detrimental effects on post-infarction cardiac repair. Ferroptosis, a recently recognized modality of regulated necrotic cell death, has been shown to exert a critical role in cardiovascular diseases—particularly MI [ ]. Ferroptosis can be activated by iron overload or inactivation of glutathione peroxidase 4 (GPX4), characterized by the accumulation of lipid hydroperoxides to lethal levels, and inhibiting ferroptosis represents a novel strategy to reduce myocardial cell death and improve cardiac conditions in heart diseases [ ]. Researchers have demonstrated that TCS induces hepatic iron overload, downregulates GPX4 expression, and promotes ferroptosis in the liver of mice [ ]. However, whether TCS triggers ferroptosis in the heart remains unclear. In this study, we demonstrated for the first time that TCS exacerbated lipid peroxidation in cardiomyocytes after MI in mice, which was manifested by the upregulation of MDA and the downregulation of the antioxidant system components. As a result, ferroptosis was aggravated, and the consequent loss of cardiomyocytes further exacerbated heart failure.
Mitochondrial OXPHOS is a core process of cellular energy metabolism and serves as the primary pathway for ROS production [ ]. ROS have been shown to be one of the key factors promoting the senescence of cardiomyocytes [ ]. Researchers have found that the inhibition of OXPHOS promotes the accumulation of ROS [ ]. A reduction in mitochondrial OXPHOS levels exhibits a causal relationship with decreased cell proliferation and premature senescence [ ]. TCS has been shown to inhibit OXPHOS in hepatocytes [ ], and we demonstrated in cardiomyocytes that TCS led to further inhibition of ischemia-induced OXPHOS and promoted ROS production in this study. Excessive ROS also induce lipid peroxidation, leading to ferroptosis and worsening the prognosis of MI [ , ]. Mito-TEMPO, a commonly used reagent for targeted scavenging of mitochondrial ROS, has been shown to inhibit ferroptosis and cellular senescence in multiple cell types [ , , ]. In this study, we found that treatment with Mito-TEMPO significantly eliminated the exacerbation of ferroptosis and cellular senescence after MI exposed to TCS. We further verified the temporal sequence among mitochondrial ROS, ferroptosis and cellular senescence in cardiomyocytes. As shown in , in cardiomyocytes exposed to TCS, partial mRNA levels of mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation, such as Ndufb8 and Cox4i1, were inhibited as early as 1 h after hypoxia, while mRNA levels of Ndufs1 and Uqcrc2 also significantly decreased at 3 h of hypoxia. This indicated that mitochondrial dysfunction was the earliest event during the early stage of hypoxic injury after TCS exposure. Mitochondrial dysfunction led to ROS burst [ ]. Consistent with expectations, ROS levels in cardiomyocytes increased but not significantly at 1 h of hypoxic injury after TCS exposure, while they significantly increased at 3 h, suggesting that mitochondrial dysfunction might be the driver of ROS burst ( ). More importantly, during hypoxia after TCS exposure, ferroptosis and senescence occurred at 6 h, i.e., after mitochondrial dysfunction and ROS burst, which was consistent with the results of our animal experiments ( ). In summary, our findings confirmed that mitochondrial dysfunction was the initiating factor during hypoxic injury of cardiomyocytes after TCS exposure, which subsequently led to ROS burst and ultimately resulted in the upregulation of ferroptosis and senescence ( ). Therefore, our findings demonstrated that TCS exposure significantly exacerbated both processes in vivo and in vitro, with a shared pathological basis: mitochondrial dysfunction-induced ROS burst.
Nur77, a stress-inducible nuclear transcription factor, plays a protective role in myocardial ischemic injury [ , ]. Researchers have demonstrated that Nur77 deficiency exacerbates cardiac fibrosis and cardiac dysfunction after MI by promoting endothelial-to-mesenchymal transition and its actions on monocytes and macrophages [ , ]. In this study, we demonstrated that Nur77 played a critical role in TCS-induced poor prognosis after MI, and its knockout led to exacerbated cardiomyocyte ferroptosis, cellular senescence, and ventricular remodeling, thus deteriorating cardiac function. Researchers have demonstrated that the upregulation of Nur77 induced by metformin alleviates myocardial ischemia/reperfusion-induced ferroptosis and improves cardiac function [ ]. Nur77 has been proven to ameliorate oxidative damage and cellular senescence in the cyclophosphamide-induced ovarian insufficiency model [ ], and alleviate cardiac fibrosis during aging [ ]. These findings indicate that Nur77 is closely associated with cellular senescence and ferroptosis. However, its involvement in environmental pollutant exposure remains unclear. Mechanistically, our study confirmed that TCS specifically downregulated its target gene NTRK2 by promoting ubiquitin-proteasomal degradation of Nur77. This revealed a highly selective post-translational regulation of transcription factors by TCS, explaining how TCS specifically suppressed cardioprotective pathways. Additionally, we first demonstrated that Nur77 regulated the transcription of PGC-1α through the NTRK2/mTOR/YY1 pathway, which explained its modulation of mitochondrial function. These findings were consistent with previous studies showing that knockout of Nur77 in β-cells reduced the expression of mitochondrial dehydrogenase subunits Idh3g and Sdhb, thereby inhibiting mitochondrial respiration [ ].
Ubiquitination, a pivotal post-translational modification, orchestrates protein degradation through the ubiquitin-proteasome system (UPS), with K48-linked ubiquitination being the most prevalent pathway for targeting proteins to the 26S proteasome for degradation [ , ]. MG132, a specific proteasomal inhibitor, blocks this degradation pathway by irreversibly binding to the proteasome's catalytic site, thereby preserving ubiquitinated proteins [ ]. Previous studies have identified the E3 ubiquitin ligase TRIM13 as a key mediator of Nur77 protein stability, primarily through K48-linked ubiquitination [ , ]. In the present study, we observed that TCS exposure significantly induced Nur77 degradation in cardiomyocytes, a process that was markedly attenuated by MG132 treatment, confirming the involvement of the proteasomal pathway. Mechanistically, TCS upregulated the expression of TRIM13, as evidenced by Western blotting in hypoxic AC16 cells, while CoIP assays revealed enhanced interaction between TRIM13 and Nur77 in the hearts of TCS-exposed mice post-MI. These findings established that TCS promoted TRIM13-mediated K48 ubiquitination of Nur77, leading to its proteasomal degradation. Our results bridged the gap between environmental exposure and post-translational modification and highlighted the critical role of ubiquitination dynamics in mediating environmental toxin-induced cardiac injury.
NTRK2, a neurotrophic factor receptor, has been increasingly recognized for its cardioprotective effects after MI [ , ]. Researchers have demonstrated that the inhibition of NTRK2/Akt/mTOR signaling pathway promotes cellular senescence in ovary [ ]. Similarly, NTRK2 agonist promotes functional recovery after traumatic brain injury through suppressing ferroptosis via the PI3K/Akt pathway [ ]. These findings are consistent with our experimental results, where NTRK2 inhibition further exacerbated TCS-induced post-MI ferroptosis and cellular senescence, promoting ventricular remodeling and heart failure. The mTOR activation has been demonstrated to upregulate PGC-1α transcription via direct binding of the transcription factor YY1 to the PGC-1α promoter region [ ]. Researchers have found that cardiac NTRK2-mediated signaling activates AKT/mTOR, promoting the upregulation of YY1 and PGC-1α expression in response to pathophysiological stress, which indicates that NTRK2/mTOR/YY1/PGC-1αsignaling plays a critical role in regulating cellular energetics in the cardiac stress response [ ]. In this study, we demonstrated that TCS led to further downregulation of NTRK2 expression after MI, whereas overexpression of NTRK2 in cardiomyocytes reversed the exacerbated MI injury. More importantly, our study identified Nur77 as a key transcriptional regulator of NTRK2 and showed that TCS disrupted NTRK2-mediated mTOR activation by targeting this regulatory node. PGC-1α, a key transcriptional coactivator, regulates cellular metabolism, energy balance, and mitochondrial biology [ ]. It centrally governs mitochondrial OXPHOS by promoting mitochondrial biogenesis, regulating electron transport chain complexes, and enhancing fatty acid oxidation—thereby enhancing OXPHOS capacity [ , ]. Interestingly, our study revealed that TCS exposure significantly reduced PGC-1α expression in post-MI cardiomyocytes. Further, Nur77 knockdown or overexpression significantly regulated PGC-1α transcription in mouse hearts, a process mediated by the mTOR pathway, which has not been previously reported. Notably, inhibiting PGC-1α abrogated the protective effects of NTRK2 overexpression against post-MI injury. Results showed that treatment with the PGC-1α activator ZLN005 showed significant efficacy in post-TCS-exposed MI mice, suggesting that PGC-1α represented a critical final target of TCS in this context. ZLN005, a well-characterized PGC-1α activator, confers protective effects in hepatic ischemia-reperfusion injury, Parkinson's disease, and diabetic cardiomyopathy through PGC-1α activation [ , , ]. It has also been reported to protect cardiomyocytes against high glucose-induced cytotoxicity by modulating SIRT1 expression and autophagy [ ]. While our PGC-1α knockdown rescue experiments demonstrated that ZLN005 improves TCS-aggravated myocardial infarction mainly via PGC-1α, the possibility of off-target effects cannot be entirely excluded.
Previous population studies indicated that the detection rate of TCS in female bodily fluid samples was relatively high. In a cohort study involving 2039 women, the detection rate of TCS in the urine of pregnant women was as high as 92.6 % [ ]. Exposure to TCS in women was shown to affect menstruation, reduce female fertility [ ], and might be associated with decreased infant birth weight [ ]. Given the demonstrated detrimental effects of TCS on women's health, it was necessary to explore the impact of TCS on myocardial injury in women after MI. The present study was the first to reveal that TCS exacerbated such post-MI myocardial injury by aggravating mitochondrial damage, ferroptosis, and senescence. Furthermore, our experiments confirmed that these toxic effects were detectable in both male and female C57BL/6J mice. Notably, in this study, we found that the cumulative post-MI mortality rate of female mice exposed to TCS was slightly lower than that of male mice, which might be attributed to the cardioprotective effects of estrogen in women [ ].
This study has limitations. Firstly, large-scale epidemiological cohort studies are essential to validate this correlation of TCS as a modifiable environmental risk factor for post-MI prognosis in humans. Given that only one mouse strain was employed to validate these observations, it remains unclear whether the observed effects are applicable to other mouse strains, warranting additional studies to address this question. Additionally, although the PGC-1α activator ZLN005 demonstrated efficacy in mitigating TCS-induced cardiac injury in preclinical mouse models, its safety profile, pharmacokinetic properties, and translational potential in humans remain undefined. Further studies are required to more thoroughly address these issues in the future.

5.

## Conclusions
TCS aggravated post-MI injury by disrupting the Nur77/NTRK2/PGC-1α signaling axis, thereby inducing mitochondrial dysfunction-driven ferroptosis and senescence in cardiomyocytes. Pharmacological activation of PGC-1α presented a promising therapeutic approach to mitigate TCS-mediated detrimental effects after MI.
