## Purpose
Despite advancements in 177Lu-based radiotherapy for cancer, its efficacy against refractory cold tumors remains limited. Targeted peptide-radionuclide conjugates (PRCs) combined with immunotherapy are emerging as promising theranostic strategies to maximize anti-tumor effectiveness.

## Methods
Cyclic peptide CEMJ4 was identified via phage selection, and further conjugated with DOTA and radiolabeled with 68Ga for diagnostic imaging and 177Lu for cancer therapy. The druggability was assessed by in vitro cell experiments, in vivo PET/CT imaging and biodistribution. Additionally, the feasibility of combining 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 with a hematopoietic progenitor kinase 1 inhibitor (HPK1i) was evaluated in B16F10 tumor-bearing mice, focusing on the anti-tumor immune response and tumor growth.

## Results
CEMJ4 exhibited high affinity for human erythropoietin-producing hepatocellular receptor A2 (EphA2; KD = 0.3 ± 0.2 µM), a therapeutic target overexpressed in several solid tumors. Radiolabeled 68Ga/177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 specifically bound to EphA2-expressing B16F10 cells and tumor models, effectively inhibiting tumor growth. Notably, 177Lu-induced T cell immunotoxicity was reversed by HPK1i, which modulated T cell dysfunction. Combining 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 with HPK1i significantly reduced tumor burden and increased tumor-infiltrating CD4+ T cells, CD8+ T cells, and M1 macrophages.

## Conclusion
This study identifies CEMJ4 as a promising peptide ligand for tumor-targeted radionuclide delivery and emphasizes the clinical potential of radionuclide therapy combined with immunotherapy in theranostics to enhance therapeutic precision and efficacy.

## Introduction
Radionuclide-based theranostics have emerged as a crucial strategy in cancer treatment, which integrates targeted therapeutics with diagnostics, systemically delivering targeted radiopharmaceuticals to tumor sites. However, numerous challenges persist. For instance, despite significant advancements in developing radiopharmaceuticals targeting sodium iodide symporter (NIS) [ ], somatostatin receptor type 2 (SSTR2) [ , ] and prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) [ , ], many therapeutic targets still lack corresponding targeted radiopharmaceuticals, necessitating further research and development. In addition, radionuclide therapy alone is often ineffective for certain refractory cold tumors, that lack T cell infiltration. While radionuclides can directly kill cancer cells and expose antigens to activate the immune system, T cells often struggle to overcome the tumors’ immunosuppressive environments. In some cases, radionuclide therapy may even induce both local and systemic immunosuppressive responses [ ]. Therefore, identifying novel targeted radionuclide-drug conjugates (RDCs) and developing effective combination therapy strategies for immune activation will enhance the applicability of radionuclide therapy in clinical cancer theranostics.
Erythropoietin-producing hepatocellular receptor A2 (EphA2) is a transmembrane glycoprotein belonging to the family of tyrosine kinase receptors [ ], which is abnormally overexpressed in numerous solid tumors [ ]. Due to its involvement in tumor occurrence, metastasis and drug resistance [ , ], EphA2 has been widely regarded as a promising target in clinical oncology for diagnosis and therapy. However, both antibody-drug conjugates (ADC) and small molecule drugs targeting EphA2 have not achieved satisfactory outcomes in clinical studies [ – ]. Recently, EphA2-targeting cyclic peptides have shown promising outcomes in preclinical and clinical studies when conjugated with cytotoxic payloads [ , ] or labeled with radionuclides, such as 68Ga and 177Lu [ – ]. In comparison to antibodies and small molecules, cyclic peptides exhibit superior specificity, tissue permeability and immunogenicity, leading to lower systemic exposure and favorable accumulation in target tissues [ ]. Moreover, cyclization improves the metabolic stability of linear peptides by reducing conformational flexibility [ ], while monocyclic peptides offer greater simplicity than bicyclic peptides, making them easier to synthesize, modify, and optimize structurally. Therefore, identifying EphA2-targeting monocyclic peptides holds significant potential as the ideal ligands for targeted RDCs.
Based on these considerations, we developed a monocyclic peptide ligand named CEMJ4 through phage selection, which exhibits a high affinity against human EphA2. We subsequently radiolabeled CEMJ4 with β+-emitting gallium-68 or β--emitting lutetium-177, a pair of radionuclides widely used in diagnostic imaging and therapy of prostate cancer [ , ] and neuroendocrine tumors [ , ]. ⁶⁸Ga/¹⁷⁷Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 specifically bound to the EphA2-expressing B16F10 cells and tumor models, effectively inhibiting tumor growth due to the DNA damage induced by the radionuclide 177Lu [ ]. Furthermore, our findings reveal that 177Lu can inhibit the activity of T cells infiltrated in tumor microenvironment (TME) through the p-AKT and p-ERK signaling pathways, a phenomenon that has not been widely studied so far. Surprisedly, this immunosuppression could be reversed by a hematopoietic progenitor kinase 1 (HPK1, also known as MAP4K1) inhibitor (HPK1i, Compound 5i [ ]), which promotes tumor immune surveillance and augments T-cell activity. Therefore, we explored a combination therapy of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 with HPK1i, potentially providing an alternative approach for reduced system toxicity and enhanced therapeutic effects in certain refractory cold tumors.

## Results

## Phage selection of cyclic peptides targeting the extracellular domain of human EphA2
In order to obtain cyclic peptides targeting EphA2, we performed three rounds of phage selection using a mixed library of double-bridged, monocyclic and linear peptides (Fig.  A). According to a previously reported method [ ], a peptide library consisting of 11 sub-libraries was constructed, including four 2-cysteine sub-libraries with the format (X)oC(X)pC(X)q (o + p + q = 3–5, 6, 7 or 8, where X denotes random amino acids and C denotes cysteine), six 4-cysteine sub-libraries with the format XC(X)lC(X)mC(X)nCX (l + m + n = 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 or 8) and a cysteine-free sub-library containing peptides of 5 to 14 amino acids in length (Fig.  B). The chemical reagent 2,6-Bis(bromomethyl)pyridine (BBP) was employed to crosslink cysteines to form monocyclic or double-bridged peptides from the aforementioned 2-cysteine and 4-cysteine sub-libraries, respectively. There were approximately 1010 individual transformants within the peptide library, and the diversity of the skeletal structure was enhanced by the variability of cysteine positions within the 2-cysteine and 4-cysteine sub-libraries.
After three rounds of selection under the highly stringent conditions that systematically reduced the amount of EphA2 (Figure ), we amplified the peptide-encoding DNA sequences from the phage genomes of samples collected at each round using a two-step PCR procedure [ ]. The PCR products of different samples were pooled and sequenced on the Illumina next-generation sequencing (NGS) platform and analyzed by MATLAB scripts [ ]. The abundant peptides were grouped into three different consensus families based on their sequence similarity. Interestingly, all three consensus families originated from the 2-cysteine sub-libraries, which formed monocyclic peptides upon the modification with BBP. They shared a conserved binding motif of (F/Y)XACPX2 − 3C (Fig.  C-D), which has not been reported in previously identified EphA2 targeting peptides [ ]. Furthermore, the abundance of the highly enriched peptides varied significantly across the three rounds of selection. For instance, the abundance of EMJ1 increased dramatically from 0.14% in the first round to 5.95% in the second round by 42.5-fold, and further rose to 13.4% and 19.7% in the third round under the 100 ng and 10 ng conditions, respectively. The enrichment trend of EMJ3 was similar to that of EMJ1. The peptides EMJ2 and EMJ4, however, despite showing significant enrichment between the first and second rounds, were not further enriched in the third round (Fig.  C-D).

 

## Affinity characterization of candidate peptides binding to EphA2
Following cyclizing the linear candidate peptides with BBP (chemical structures, purity and mass of the peptides are shown in Figure - , all compounds are > 95% pure by HPLC), the thiol groups of the two cysteine residues underwent nucleophilic substitution with BBP. Surface plasmon resonance (SPR) was then used to determine the kinetic rate constant and KD values of cyclic peptides binding to EphA2 (Fig.  A, Figure ). The SPR results confirmed the binding affinities we expected, based on the abundance and enrichment of the peptides in each round of selection. CEMJ1 and CEMJ2 (the BBP-cyclized forms of the EMJ1 and EMJ2 peptides), the two most abundant peptides in the third round, exhibited high affinities of 0.08 ± 0.01 µM and 0.11 ± 0.02 µM, respectively. As anticipated, the binding affinity of the less abundant CEMJ3 (the BBP-cyclized EMJ3 peptide, KD = 1.24 ± 0.03 µM) was more than 11-fold lower than that of CEMJ2. Surprisingly, despite being less abundant than CEMJ3 during the selection, CEMJ4 (the BBP-cyclized EMJ4 peptide) demonstrated a better affinity (KD = 0.3 ± 0.2 µM) than CEMJ3. Based on the affinity and dissociation properties of cyclic peptides observed in the SPR assay, CEMJ4 was selected for further investigation. To enable radionuclide labeling, CEMJ4 was conjugated with the 1,4,7,10-tetraaza-cyclododecane-1,4,7,10-tetraacetic acid (DOTA) (Fig.  B). The DOTA-CEMJ4 peptide showed a nanomolar affinity against EphA2 (KD = 0.16 ± 0.05 µM), while the KD of the linear form (DOTA-LEMJ4) was 105-fold weaker (KD = 16.8 µM) (Fig.  C-D).

 

In vitro and in vivo specificity evaluation of
68Ga/177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4
Subsequently, the DOTA-CEMJ4 was radiolabeled with 68Ga for diagnostic imaging and 177Lu for therapeutic applications [ ]. After purification, the radiochemical purity of both 68Ga and 177Lu-labeled CEMJ4 measured by radio-HPLC were over 99% (Figure ). After incubating in PBS or FBS at 37 °C for two hours, in vitro stability of 68Ga/177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 still exceeded 99%, while also maintaining a stable monomodal distribution in urine or blood samples collected from mice two hours post-injection (Figure ). The remarkable stability of 68Ga/177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 both in vitro and in vivo encouraged us to evaluate the druggability of CEMJ4 in tumor animal models.
To begin, we assessed the cellular uptake of 68Ga/177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 at various time points (5, 10, 20, 30, 60, 90, and 120 min) post-incubation. The results revealed that 68Ga/177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 specifically bound to EphA2-expressing B16F10 cells, with no detectable binding to EphA2-negative T cells. Furthermore, the uptake of the 68Ga/177Lu-labeled compound by B16F10 cells increased steadily over time (Fig.  A-B). When B16F10 cells were pre-blocked with an excess of non-radiolabeled peptide (DOTA-CEMJ4), the uptake of tracer (68Ga/177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4) was significantly reduced with a statistically significant difference observed between blocked and unblocked conditions (P < 0.0001) (Fig.  C), confirming the excellent target specificity of CEMJ4 in vitro. To further evaluate the cell binding affinity, competitive binding assays were conducted using varying concentrations of non-radiolabeled DOTA-CEMJ4. The results revealed the half-maximal inhibitory concentrations (IC50) of 314.1 nM for 68Ga-DOTA-CEMJ4 and 550.8 nM for 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 against B16F10 cells (Fig.  D), which closely correlate with the binding affinity of DOTA-CEMJ4 to human EphA2 measured by SPR (KD = 0.16 ± 0.05 µM, Fig.  A).

 

Given the high expression of EphA2 in various malignant tumors [ ], we first assessed EphA2 expression levels in several tumor cell lines (A2780, A375, LNCaP, MCF-7, SK-OV-3, B16F10) using western blotting prior to establishing the tumor models for this study. The results revealed that the six different tumor cell lines exhibited varying levels of EphA2 expression, with B16F10 and A375 cells showing the highest levels (Fig.  A). This finding was further confirmed by the micro-PET/CT imaging of mouse xenografts, which showed higher accumulation of 68Ga-DOTA-CEMJ4 in B16F10 (4.5% ID/g) and A375 tumor models (2.1% ID/g), proving the in vivo specificity of CEMJ4. Meanwhile, the non-tumor-binding peptides were rapidly cleared via renal pathways (Fig.  B).
Additionally, compared to 68Ga-DOTA-LEMJ4 (the linear form of EMJ4, 2.07 ± 0.61% ID/g), the cyclic counterpart exhibited higher tumor uptake (4.63 ± 0.42% ID/g) within 1 h after injection. Over time, the tumor uptake still maintained a high level at 2 and 4 h, indicating that the monocyclic conformation of CEMJ4 enhanced its ability to target tumors (Fig.  C-D). Further in vivo biodistribution studies of 68Ga-DOTA-LEMJ4/CEMJ4 confirmed the micro-PET/CT imaging results, with tumor radioactivity measured using a γ counter. At 4 h post-injection, 68Ga-DOTA-CEMJ4 maintained a tumor uptake of 6.06 ± 0.16% ID/g, while the maximum tumor uptake in the linear counterpart was only 1.49 ± 0.06% ID/g. Furthermore, non-target organs showed negligible uptake (≤ 1% ID/g) throughout all time points. Due to its renal excretion pathway, 68Ga-DOTA-CEMJ4 exhibited sustained high uptake in the kidneys, reaching 30 ± 3% ID/g at 2 h post-injection (Fig.  E-F).

 

Evaluation of the anti-tumor effect of
177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 in vitro
To analyze the anti-tumor activity of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 in vitro, we evaluated its effect on B16F10 cells at different concentrations using the CCK-8 assay. Compared to free 177Lu, 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 exhibited a stronger inhibitory effect on B16F10 cell viability (IC50 = 18.02 MBq/mL) (Fig.  A, Figure ). Next, B16F10 cells were treated with 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 at concentrations corresponding to 1/4, 1/2, and the full IC50 for 24 h, with RPMI 1640 medium used as a negative control. Afterwards, the cells were maintained in fresh, drug-free medium for 13 additional days. The biological effects of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 were then assessed in vitro, with results indicating the inhibition of cell colony formation (Fig.  B-C), as well as reduced migration and invasion abilities of B16F10 cells (Fig.  F-I). Additionally, γ-H2AX staining, a sensitive marker of DNA damage, along with apoptosis analysis, revealed a concentration-dependent increase in the apoptosis rate in B16F10 cells treated with 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 (Fig.  E and J). This was consistent with a significant increase in PI+/Annexin+ cells measured by flow cytometry (Fig.  D), suggesting that DNA damage induced by 177Lu led to late-stage apoptosis.

 

HPK1 inhibitor alleviates the inhibitory effect of
177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 on T cell activation in vitro
According to previous reports [ ], 177Lu-based therapy is a double-edged sword in terms of immune responses. To assess the immunomodulatory effect of radionuclide 177Lu, naïve splenic T cells were activated and then treated with varying doses of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 (1/4, 1/2, and full IC50, IC50 = 18.02 MBq/mL) for 48 h. Upon activation, naïve T cells proliferate by transmitting signals from the T cell receptor (TCR) to various downstream pathways, including the activation of MAP kinases and transcription factors. This process enables T cells to perform critical immune functions within the TME, such as secreting IL-2, IFN-γ, and other cytokines that contribute to anti-tumor responses [ ]. We observed a dose-dependent decrease in the relative expression of phosphorylated protein kinase B (AKT) and extracellular regulated kinase 1/2 (ERK 1/2) induced by 177Lu (Fig.  A, Figure A). Since HPK1 acts as a negative regulator of TCR signaling [ ], we employed an HPK1i (Compound 5i [ ]) to ameliorate T cell exhaustion and enhance anti-tumor immune response. As expected, the inhibition to T cells induced by 177Lu could be counteracted by HPK1i (Fig.  B, Figure B).
Compared to naïve T cells stimulated with anti-CD3/CD28 antibodies, those treated with anti-CD3/CD28 plus 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 showed reduced proliferation, as revealed by lower [3H]-thymidine incorporation, which was less than that observed in control naïve T cells (Fig.  C). It is promising to observe that this reduced proliferation caused by 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 can be partially reversed when combined with HPK1i. Parallel ELISA results revealed a profound reduction in IL-2 and IFN-γ production in the naïve T cells treated with 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 after anti-CD3/CD28 stimulation (Fig.  D-E), which could also be reversed by HPK1i. These results suggested that 177Lu may suppress T cell-mediated anti-tumor immune responses in a non-target-dependent manner, which can be partially alleviated by HPK1i.

 

HPK1 inhibitor synergizes with
177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 to enhance anti-tumor efficacy in vivo
To further confirm the anti-tumor activity of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 in vivo, we used a subcutaneous tumor model to evaluate its dose-response relationship in tumor suppression. A total of 20 tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into 4 groups (n = 5 per group): control (PBS), low dose (3.7 MBq), medium dose (7.4 MBq), and high dose (14.8 MBq). As shown in Fig.  A-B, treatment with the high dose of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 (14.8 MBq) significantly inhibited the growth of B16F10 cell xenografts, whereas the low and medium doses did not exhibit a notable effect.
Based on the finding that HPK1i can reverse 177Lu-induced T cell inhibition in vitro, we next evaluated whether combining 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 with HPK1i could achieve a synergistic anti-tumor effect in vivo. To mitigate potential adverse effects associated with radiation dose, a low dose (3.7 MBq) of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 was selected for this evaluation. C57Bl/6J mice bearing B16F10 xenografts were randomly assigned to receive either a single injection of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 alone, three injections of HPK1i alone (10 mg/kg), or a combination of both treatments. Compared to untreated control, neither the low-dose 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 nor HPK1i alone resulted in significant tumor growth inhibition. As anticipated, the combination therapy demonstrated a substantial reduction in tumor volume, with an average size of only 445.05 mm3, which was markedly smaller than that observed in the other groups (Fig.  C-D). The therapeutic efficacy was further supported by TUNEL (a marker of cell apoptosis), γ-H2AX (a marker of DNA damage), and Ki67 (a marker of cell proliferation) immunofluorescence staining of the tumors (Fig.  E). The results indicated that the combination therapy of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 and HPK1i significantly enhanced tumor cell apoptosis, increased the DNA damage of tumors, and inhibited tumor cell proliferation, thereby demonstrating an effective anti-tumor effect.
Subsequently, we further investigated the effects of the combination therapy on immune responses in vivo. Contrary to the inhibitory effects of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 on T cell activation observed in vitro, monotherapy with 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 in vivo actually increased the infiltration of CD3+/CD4+ and CD3+/CD8+ T cells within the TME, while showing no significant effect on F4/80+/CD86+ M1 and F4/80+/CD163+ M2 macrophages compared to the control group. Similar results were observed with HPK1i monotherapy. However, the combination therapy of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 and HPK1i not only further increased the infiltration of CD3+/CD4+, CD3+/CD8+ T cells, and M1 macrophage but also reduced the proportion of PD-1+CD8+ T cells (Fig.  F). Meanwhile, co-staining of M1 macrophage markers F4/80 and iNOS showed a more pronounced increase in the combination therapy (Figure ), indicating that the macrophages in TME were polarized towards the anti-tumor M1 phenotype.
Finally, we evaluated the safety of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 combined with HPK1i through body weight monitoring and H&E staining of major organs. After the combination therapy, no significant weight loss was observed in any group of mice (Figure A). Additionally, liver enzyme levels, specifically alanine aminotransferase (ALT), and kidney function indicators, including blood urea nitrogen (BUN) and creatinine (CREA), remained stable without significant changes. (Figure B-D). Furthermore, H&E staining of the liver, kidneys, heart, spleen, and lungs indicated no systemic toxicity associated with the combination therapy of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 and HPK1i (Fig. E).

 

## Discussion
Research over the past 25 years have demonstrated that EphA2 is a potential therapeutic target highly expressed in various malignant tumors, including prostate, ovarian, melanoma, and breast cancers [ – ]. These cancers pose a significant threat to human health and survival. Thus, new therapeutic strategies, including targeted radionuclide therapy (TRT) and immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) are being developed for more precise and personalized treatment.
As one of the most important forms of TRT, RDCs can accurately deliver radionuclides to tumor sites by utilizing targeted molecules, including antibodies, peptides or small molecules. In this study, we developed a peptide radionuclide conjugate (PRC) containing the EphA2-targeting cyclic peptide ligand CEMJ4, the chelator DOTA, and the payload 68Ga or 177Lu for diagnosis and treatment in certain refractory cold tumors. Compared to other targeted ligands, cyclic peptides offer superior tissue permeability and lower immunogenicity.
In fact, we successfully identified four EphA2-targeting cyclic peptides using a mixed phage displayed peptide library comprising double-bridged, monocyclic, and linear peptides. Interestingly, all four candidate peptides were exclusively derived from the 2-cysteine sub-libraries, forming monocyclic peptides when cyclized by BBP. This suggests that monocyclic peptides, with superior structural stability and affinity for the target protein, may be more efficiently enriched than the linear peptides. Additionally, the simpler cyclization conformation of monocyclic peptides enabled them to be multivalently displayed at the tip of each filamentous phage (theoretically 5 copies on each phage). In contrast, double-bridged peptides formed three distinct isomers when cyclized with BBP [ ], which led to most likely a monovalent display, thereby reducing their apparent affinities comparing with the multivalently displayed monocyclic peptides. Moreover, the conserved binding motif “ACP” of the four monocyclic peptides was different but homologous as the “AYP” motif of previously reported bicyclic peptides [ ], which employed the scaffold 1,3,5-triacryoyl-1,3,5-triazinane (TATA) for cyclization. The different cyclization reagents resulted in alterations to the enriched peptide skeleton, which subsequently caused discrepancies in the conserved binding motifs. In this study, we have also identified peptides containing the “AYP” motif, but with much lower relative abundances (< 0.7%).
Due to higher affinity and better binding kinetics, CEMJ1 and CEMJ4 were selected as the candidates and both conjugated to the chelator DOTA at C-terminus. Although CEMJ1 exhibited slightly higher affinity (0.08 ± 0.01 µM vs. 0.3 ± 0.2 µM), CEMJ4 demonstrated slower dissociation kinetics and clearer tumor visualization in preliminary PET/CT imaging. Subsequent biodistribution and therapeutic studies further confirmed CEMJ4’s superior in vivo performance, supporting its selection as the best candidate ligand of PRC.
Although TRT improves the precision of radiation therapy (RT), the moderate response rate of RT alone limits its widespread use [ ]. Over the past few decades, studies on RT have focused almost exclusively on its cytotoxic effects on tumors, ignoring the complex biological interactions within the TME that support tumor growth and progression. Recent studies have shown that low-dose TRT can trigger the release of inflammatory cytokines, thereby increasing immune cell infiltration and activation within TME [ , ]. This has led to numerous investigations into combined strategies of TRT and immunotherapy to maximize tumor eradication effectiveness. TRT has been gradually shown to synergistically increase anti-tumor effects when combined with ICIs [ , ], representative anti-tumor drugs in immunotherapy, including antibodies of programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) [ , ], programmed cell death ligand 1 (PD-L1) [ ], and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte antigen 4 (CTLA-4) [ ].
However, current evidence suggests that RT is a double-edged sword for immune responses, inducing immunosuppressive responses at certain doses [ ]. The tumor antigens generated after radiation exposure can vary significantly depending on cell types and radiation doses [ ], leading to different effects on immune activation. In addition, TRT has been shown to potentially upregulate PD-L1 expression [ ], a molecule associated with the inhibition of T cell activation. Particularly in mouse models, variations in drug dosage and administration sequence may lead to inconsistencies in immune activation results between preclinical and clinical studies [ , ]. Therefore, we need to explore new combination treatment strategies that can induce tumor cell death while simultaneously activating the immune system. Although the tumor growth inhibition (TGI) value in the combination therapy group of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 and HPK1i exceeded the additive effects of the two monotherapy groups (67.2% vs. 41.3%), the synergistic effects in vivo require further investigation in additional animal experiments with different dosing regimens.
HPK1 is a negative intracellular immune checkpoint, and inhibiting HPK1 promotes T cell accumulation within tumors and alleviates T cell exhaustion [ ], thereby enhancing anti-tumor immune responses. Since HPK1 is rarely expressed in B16F10 tumor and does not directly act on tumor cells, it does not influence tumor cell proliferation or apoptosis, nor does it induce DNA damage. Consequently, the greatest anti-tumor effects were observed in the group simultaneously treated with 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 and HPK1i. This suggests that combining HPK1i with 177Lu-labeled EphA2-targeting cyclic peptide is an effective strategy to enhance anti-tumor response. Further research to identify optimal treatment timing, sequencing, and doses is essential to maximize anti-tumor effects and minimize adverse reactions, ultimately providing a better treatment option for patients.

## Conclusion
In this study, CEMJ4, a EphA2 targeting cyclic peptide developed via phage display, was identified as a promising peptide ligand for tumor-targeted radionuclides delivery. CEMJ4 exhibited high affinity, excellent biological stability, target specificity and a favorable biodistribution profile, which was evaluated both in vitro and in vivo after being radiolabeled with 68Ga and 177Lu. In addition, we have demonstrated a novel and important synergy between 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 and HPK1i for the treatment of cold tumors with poor immune infiltration. These results underscore the clinical development potential of radionuclide therapy combined with immunotherapy in theranostics.

## Experimental section

## Cell lines and cell culture
The human breast cancer cell line (MCF-7), the human ovarian cancer cell lines (SK-OV-3, A2780), the human malignant melanoma cell line (A375), the human prostate cancer cell line (LNCaP) and the mouse melanoma cell line (B16F10) were provided by KeyGen Biotech (China). SK-OV-3 cells were cultured in McCoy’s 5 A medium containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS), while A375 cells were cultured in DMEM medium containing 10% FBS. MCF-7, LNCaP, A2780, B16F10 cells were cultured in RPMI 1640 medium containing 10% FBS. All the cells were cultivated at 37 °C under a humidified atmosphere with 5% CO2. All these cell culture reagents were purchased from KeyGen Biotech (China).

## Animal experiments
Five-week-old male C57BL/6J mice, five-week-old male and female BALB/c mice were purchased from Ziyuan Biotech (China). Tumor cells (1 × 106) were engrafted by subcutaneous injection into the right armpit of mice. The experiments were terminated when the tumor volume reached 1,000 mm3 or if body weight loss exceeded 20%. Tumor volumes were calculated using the formula: (width2 × length)/2. All animal care and experimental procedures were conducted in accordance with the Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals and were approved by the Institution Animal Care and Use Committee at Nanjing First Hospital, Nanjing Medical University (DWSY-24119575).

## Preparation of cyclic peptide library displayed on phage
Peptide libraries were designed and constructed as previously reported [ ]. For the first round of selection, E.coli TG1 glycerol stocks of eleven sub-libraries were separately cultivated in 0.25 L of 2YT medium containing tetracycline (10 µg/mL) overnight. The next day, the phages were isolated by centrifugation and precipitated by 25% volume of ice-cold buffer N (20% (w/v) PEG 6000 and 2.5 M NaCl) as previously described [ ]. The obtained phages comprising approximately 1014 t.u. were reduced with 1 mM of TCEP at room temperature (RT) for 30 min. Then, it was precipitated again by Buffer N and resuspended in 4.5 mL of degassed buffer R (pH 8.0, 20 mM NH4HCO3 and 5 mM EDTA). Subsequently, the reduced phages with free cystines were modified by 500 µL of 0.2 mM 2,6-Bis(bromomethyl)pyridine (BBP) at 30 °C for 1 h. After modification, the phages were precipitated and resuspended in 5 mL of buffer W2 (buffer W1 (pH 7.4, 10 mM Tris-Cl, 150 mM NaCl, 10 mM MgCl2, and 1 mM CaCl2) containing 1% (w/v) BSA and 0.1% (v/v) Tween 20) at RT for 30 min, which aims to block the phages to reduce non-specific binding.

## Bio-panning against human EphA2
The blocked beads immobilized with EphA2 were mixed with the blocked phages and incubated at RT for 1 h. Subsequently, the beads were thoroughly washed eight times with buffer W4 (buffer W1 containing 0.1% (v/v) Tween 20) and two times with buffer W1. After panning, the beads with captured phages were directly incubated with 5 mL of E. coli TG1 cells (OD600 = 0.4–0.8) for 30 min at 37 °C without shaking (inverted every 10 min). Then, the infected cells were plated on 15 cm 2YT plates containing tetracycline (10 µg/mL). The next day, bacterial cells were collected from the plates with 4 mL of 2YT medium containing 25% (v/v) glycerol, and stored at − 80 °C.

## Candidate peptide synthesis
All the linear candidate peptides were synthesized by GL Biochem (China) using solid phase peptide synthesis. Each linear peptide was dissolved in 50% (v/v) water/acetonitrile containing 0.1% trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) to a final concentration of 5 mM. Then, the diluted linear peptides at 0.5 mM were reacted with 1 mM of BBP in 20 to 50% (v/v) acetonitrile aqueous buffer containing 100 mM NH4HCO3 (pH 8.0) for 1 h at 30 °C. The reactions were quenched by preparative adding 2% (v/v) formic acid of the total volume. The reaction products were purified by high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) equipped with a C18 column (Hypersil™ PREP HS C18, 10 × 250 mm, 5 μm, Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). The mass of purified cyclic peptides was confirmed on a LCMS-2020 quadrupole mass spectrometer coupled with a Nexera LC-40 chromatography system (Shimadzu, Japan), which was equipped with a C18 column (Shim-pack GIST C18, 2.1 × 50 mm, 2 μm, Shimadzu, Japan). Finally, the purity of cyclic peptides was assessed by analytical HPLC equipped with a C18 column (Poroshell 120 EC-C18, 4.6 × 150 mm, 4 μm, Agilent, USA). All compounds are > 95% pure by HPLC. The solvent systems used in the above HPLC and LCMS experiments were 99.9% H2O/0.1% formic acid (FA) and 99.9% acetonitrile/0.1% FA. The mass of the peptides used in this study is listed in Table . After verification of purity and mass, the cyclic peptides were lyophilized and dissolved in DMSO for subsequent affinity assays.

## Radiosynthesis and stability assessment of radiolabeled peptides
Each peptide was dissolved in deionized water to reach a final concentration of 1 mg/mL. Fifteen microliters of each dissolved peptide was added respectively to 68GaCl3-HCl (4 mL, 148 MBq) or 177LuCl3-HCl (400 µL, 370 MBq) solution. One mL or 100 µL of 0.25 M NaOAc (pH 7.0) was then added to adjust the pH. The mixture was heated at 95 °C for 30 min, followed by purification of the radiolabeled products using a Sep-Pak C18 column (Waters, USA). Radionuclide 68Ga was obtained from 68Ge-68Ga generator (ITG, Germany). 177Lu was purchased from Isotopes Technologies Garching GmbH Company (ITG, Germany).
In vitro stability was assessed by incubating radiolabeled peptides in PBS or FBS (1 : 6, v/v) at 37 °C for 2 h. In vivo stability was evaluated in non-tumor-bearing mice by intravenous injection of 3.7 MBq/mouse of radiolabeled peptides into the tail vein. Two hours later, the mice were euthanized by decapitation, and blood or urine samples were collected. These samples were mixed with an equal volume of acetonitrile, centrifuged, and filtered. Then, all samples were analyzed by radio-HPLC (Shimadzu, Japan) equipped with a C18 column (5 μm, 250 × 10 mm, Waters Xbridge C18, USA) and a Flow-Count detector system (BioSan, USA). Analyses were performed using a linear gradient that began with 95% solvent A (0.1% TFA in water) and 5% solvent B (0.1% TFA in acetonitrile) for 2 min, and then increased to 35% solvent A and 65% solvent B for 15 min.

## Cell binding and internalization assays
B16F10 cells and T cells isolated from mice spleens were seeded in a 24-well plate at 5 × 105 cells per well, and cultured for 24 h. Subsequently, 50 µL (0.074 MBq) of 68Ga/177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 in RPMI 1640 medium was added in each well with or without an excess of non-labeled peptide (over 15-fold excess concentration of DOTA-CEMJ4 solution: 220 µM) and allowed to culture for 5, 10, 20, 30, 60, 90, and 120 min. After each incubation period, the supernatant was removed, and the cells were washed twice with PBS and then harvested with 1 M NaOH. The radioactivity in the cells was determined with a γ-counter (Perkin-Elmer, USA).
B16F10 cells were seeded in a 48-well plate at a density of 1 × 105 cells per well and cultured at 37 °C for 24 h. CEMJ4 in RPMI 1640 medium, was added to the plates at concentrations ranging from 1 × 10− 11 M to 1 × 10− 4 M. PBS solution was used as the control group. After incubation for 15 min, 50 µL (0.148 MBq) of 68Ga/177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 in RPMI 1640 medium was added to each well and the cells were cultured for an additional 2 h. The supernatant was removed, and the cells were washed twice with PBS, then harvested with 1 M NaOH. The radioactivity of the cells was determined with a γ-counter. Competition curves were fitted to a logistic function and the value of IC50 was calculated using GraphPad Prism. The IC50 represents the concentration of the competing ligand that displaces 50% of the specific binding of the radioligand.

## T cell toxicity assessment
Primary T cells were isolated from the spleens of C57/BL6J mice (6–8 weeks old) using anti-CD90.2 magnetic beads. Naïve CD4+ T cells were further purified by flow cytometric sorting based on CD4+CD25–CD44loCD62Lhi surface markers, respectively. The cells were stimulated with plate-bound anti-CD3 (1 µg/mL) and anti-CD28 (1 µg/mL) (Biolegend, USA), along with different concentrations of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 (1/4 of IC50, 1/2 of IC50, and IC50, IC50 = 18.02 MBq/mL) in 6-well plates (5 × 106 per well) for western blotting, and 4.5 MBq/mL of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 in replicate wells of 96-well plates (1 × 105 cells per well) for [3H]-thymidine incorporation assay and ELISA. T cell proliferation was measured based on [3H]-thymidine incorporation assay and the effects of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 (4.5 MBq/mL) on IL-2 and IFN-γ were assessed using ELISA Kit (eBioscience) according to the manufacturer’s protocols.
For [3H]-thymidine incorporation assay, 1 × 105 naïve T cells per well were seeded in 96-well plates pre-coated with or without anti-CD3/anti-CD28 and cultured in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum. Cells were treated with 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 (1/4 of IC50) and/or HPK1i (50 nM) for 72 h in triplicates. During the last 24 h of culture, [3H]-thymidine was added at a final concentration of 0.2 MBq/mL. Cells were then harvested, and incorporated radioactivity was quantified by liquid scintillation counting.

## PET/CT imaging and biodistribution studies
PET/CT imaging was performed in tumor-bearing mice implanted with six tumor cell lines (SK-OV-3, A2780, A375, LNCaP, MCF-7 and B16F10). Human tumor cell lines (SK-OV-3, A2780, A375, LNCaP and MCF-7) were subcutaneously implanted into four-five-week-old BALB/c mice, and the mouse melanoma cell line B16F10 was implanted into four-five-week-old C57BL/6J mice. Monitor tumor growth, and once the tumor size reaches approximately 300–500 mm3, 100 µL of labeled peptides (3.7 MBq) was injected via the tail vein with subsequent PET/CT imaging. PET/CT scans were performed at 1-, 2-, and 4-hour post-injection using an Inveon Micro PET/CT scanner (Siemens Medical Solutions, USA). The percentage of the injected dose per gram (% ID/g) of tumors was calculated from PET imaging data using Inveon Research WorkStation.
For normal mice injected with labeled peptides (1.85 MBq/100 µL) via the tail vein, the biodistribution study was conducted at 5, 15, 30, 60, 120 min post-injection (n = 3 per group). After euthanasia, blood, heart, liver, spleen, lung, kidney, stomach, intestine, pancreas, muscle, bone, and brain of the mice were collected, weighed, and analyzed for radioactivity using a γ-counter. The distribution of labeled peptides in each organ was expressed as % ID/g of tissue, with all radioactivity measurements corrected for decay. For biodistribution studies of labeled peptides, the B16F10 xenograft model was used (n = 3 per group), once the tumor size reaches approximately 300–500 mm3, mice was injected via the tail vein with labeled peptides (3.7 MBq/100 µL), the tumor and major organs were excised, harvested, weighed, and measured for radioactivity using a γ-counter at four hours post-injection. All intravenous injections were administered via the tail vein.

## Therapeutic effect evaluation
When tumor volume reached approximately 50–100 mm3, B16F10 tumor-bearing C57BL/6J mice were randomly divided into two experimental groups, each containing four subgroups: (1) a PBS control group and 3 dose groups of 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 with different doses (3.7 MBq, 7.4 MBq and 14.8 MBq); (2) a PBS control group, a HPK1i group (Compound 5i [ ] synthesized by Nanjing Deheng Medical Science and Technology Co., Ltd., chemical structure is shown in Figure , 10 mg/kg), a 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 group (3.7 MBq) and a combination group of HPK1i (10 mg/kg) and 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 (3.7 MBq) via tail vein injection. The 177Lu-DOTA-CEMJ4 were injected intravenously only once and the HPK1i was injected 3 times, every 2 days. After 2 weeks of treatment, the mice were euthanized and tumors were excised and weighed. All tumors were divided into two portions: one was prepared as a single-cell suspension for flow cytometric analysis; and the other was fixed with paraformaldehyde and embedded in paraffin blocks for histological analysis. Tumor volumes were measured with a digital caliper and calculated using the formula: (width2 × length)/2. The tumor growth inhibition (TGI) value was calculated using the formula: (1 - treated tumor volume/control tumor volume) × 100%.

## Statistics
All experiments were performed at least in triplicate, except for the affinity measurements of the linear EMJ4 peptide and the PET/CT imaging result in Fig.  B (n = 1). Data are presented as the mean ± standard deviation (SD). Data were analyzed and plotted using GraphPad Prism (version 8.0.1), and differences among more than two groups were analyzed by one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Dunnett’s test. Differences between two groups were analyzed by unpaired Student’s t-tests. A P value of < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.
