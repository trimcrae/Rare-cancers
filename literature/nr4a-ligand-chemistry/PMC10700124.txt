## Introduction
Inherited retinal dystrophies (IRDs) are a heterogeneous group of genetic disorders with varying phenotypes and severity, of which retinitis pigmentosa (RP) is the most common form. With the development of molecular diagnostics, more than 100 pathogenic genes associated with RP have been identified. In addition, most mutations are rod-specific, and only a few mutations cause retinal cone cell and retinal pigment epithelial degeneration [ ]. Mutations in the rhodopsin gene are the most common cause of autosomal dominant RP, occurring in 20–30% of cases. RP can be inherited in an autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, X-linked, or mitochondrial manner and can be syndromic or non-syndromic [ ]. The prevalence of RP is 1/7000−1/3000 worldwide and is approximately 1/4000 in China. Mutations lead to structural and functional abnormalities in different proteins, affecting specific signaling pathways and triggering specific types of cell death.
Gene mutations lead to structural and functional abnormalities in different proteins, affecting specific signaling pathways and triggering specific types of cell death. At the same time, all retinal neurons are progressively affected by oxidative stress and inflammation and other factors during disease progression. In addition, the subsequent microglia activation and gliosis trigger the secretion of molecules such as pro-inflammatory cytokines, chemokines, and trophic factors, further exacerbating the degenerative process. This leads to photoreceptor cell and retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) cell death and irreversible vision loss [ ]. It is widely believed that early, timely, and effective treatment can delay the progression of RP and reduce visual impairment. With increasing attention on RP and positive advances in various therapeutic areas, we have entered a new era in the diagnosis and treatment of RP [ ]. Neuroprotective agents (neurotrophins, anti-apoptotic agents and antioxidants), gene therapy, stem cell therapy, optogenetics, artificial retinas and photochemical switches have been extensively investigated, but their safety and efficacy remain to be improved. DNA-based gene therapy is limited due to the high genetic heterogeneity of RP, and there is a need to develop a therapeutic modality that can be applied to a broader patient population. The value of non-coding RNAs (ncRNAs) as therapeutic agents has received much attention in recent years. Although ncRNAs lack protein-coding capabilities, they have a powerful ability to regulate gene expression and are key effector molecules in biological processes. For this reason, we feel that ncRNAs are more proportional to the complexity of the organism than the content of the genome encoding proteins. Therefore, ncRNAs, as an RNA-based gene therapy, may be more adapted to the highly heterogeneous nature of RP and could become a suitable treatment for the highly heterogeneous nature of RP patients.
This article reviews the current role of ncRNAs in maintaining the normal physiological activity of retinal cells, describes the interactions between ncRNAs and various factors that promote RP progression, respectively, and analyzes how ncRNAs are involved in the regulation of specific signaling pathways, highlighting the important regulatory role of ncRNAs in RP. The purpose of this paper is to explore the potential of ncRNAs as diagnostic tools for RP and nonspecific gene therapy and to focus on the relationship between microRNAs (miRNAs), long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs) and RP.

## Physiopathology and clinical manifestations of retinitis pigmentosa
RP is characterized by hypotonic degeneration of photoreceptor cells and RPE, and the death of optic rod cells may be a direct result of genetic mutations. However, cone cell death may be caused by optic rod cell death rather than mutations in cone proteins [ ]. The loss of cone cells is devastating to vision. Hypotheses regarding the mechanism of cone cell death include the release of rod death toxicity factors, reduction of trophic factors, oxidative stress, and chronic inflammation etc [ ]. However, evidence suggests that the time between the onset of rod cells degeneration and the patient's legal blindness is usually decades long, so the release of toxic factors is considered unlikely.
Rod cells are responsible for the production of rod-derived cone viability factor (RdCVF), an essential trophic factor for cone survival. Genetic mutations lead to structural and functional abnormalities in specific proteins. Rod death triggers a variety of non-cell-autonomous processes, including unfolded protein responses, oxidative stress, and inflammatory/immune responses [ ]. These effects in turn cause a vicious cycle that promotes the death of photoreceptor cells and RPE. Although RPE cells are not directly involved in optical functions such as phototransduction, they play an essential role in supporting photoreceptor cells. This includes involvement in the visual cycle through recycling retinoids, reducing photooxidative stress by scavenging free radicals, providing nutrients and outer segments of phagocytosis photoreceptors, processing and recycling wastes, production, and secretion of growth factors. In addition, RPE is the main component of the outer blood-retinal barrier (BRB) [ ]; thus, it is certain that the absence of RPE will promote the progress of RP. Finally, retinal glial cells play an important role in supporting the structure and function of the retina. Activation of microglia and proliferation of glial cells promote the degeneration and remodeling of the retina [ ]. Therefore, the above structural and functional state of retinal cells influences the health of the retina and the progression of RP.
RP can also be syndromic or non-syndromic. Examples of syndromic RP are Usher syndrome and Bardet Biedl syndrome. Non-syndromic RP is much more common, accounting for 70−80% of all cases of RP [ ]. Non-syndromic RP usually begins with night blindness, followed by centripetal visual field loss, color vision abnormalities and finally complete loss of vision. Clinical examination includes loss of peripheral vision, loss of photoreceptor cells on optical coherence tomography (OCT) of the retina and diminished or absent functional response of retinal rods assessed by electroretinography (ERG) [ ]. Pigment deposits found on the exterior of the retina are called spicules and are the result of photoreceptor cell degeneration. The classic RP triad is pigment migration within the retina, optic nerve pallor and vascular decay. The early-onset RP subtype progresses more rapidly. It usually starts around the age of 10 years and becomes apparent and difficult to treat in the late 40s and early 50s [ ].

## Effects of ncRNA on normal retina
NcRNA is widely distributed in a variety of tissues and cells, it is bioconserved and cell-specific, and is involved in the regulation of a variety of biological processes including cell proliferation, differentiation, apoptosis and functional maintenance.
3.1

## Concept and classification of ncRNA ( , )
NcRNAs do not encode proteins but have certain enzymatic, structural, or regulatory functions. Although ribosomal RNA (RNA, RIBOSOMAL) and transfer RNA (RNA, TRANSFER) are also untranslated RNAs, they are not included in this paper. NcRNAs are a heterogeneous group of molecules that are divided into two different classes based on their length. According to 200 nucleotides (nt) [ ], ncRNAs are distinguished into lncRNAs [including lincRNA, circRNA and ceRNA, NATs/OS and small ncRNAs (including microRNA (miRNA) and piRNA). The most characterized ncRNAs are miRNAs, which are major players in the ceRNA network [ ]. MiRNAs are small, 18–25 nt long, ncRNAs generally function by binding to the 3′ untranslated region of mRNA, inhibiting translation and/or promoting mRNA degradation. Pre-miRNA double-stranded bodies are loaded into the Argonaute (AGO) protein. Once loaded, only one chain, called the guide chain, binds to the AGO protein to form the final miRNA-induced silencing complex (miRISC) in a Dicer/Drosha catalyzed mediated manner [ ]. MiRISC specifically recognizes mRNA and down-regulates gene expression through two post-transcriptional mechanisms: mRNA cleavage and translational repression [ ]. CircRNAs are covalently closed cyclic molecules without 5ʹor 3ʹ ends, formed by ‘back-splicing’ events. CircRNAs are classified into four categories by biogenesis: exonic circRNAs (EcircRNAs), exon-Intron circRNAs (EIciRNAs), intronic circRNAs (ciRNAs), and MecciRNAs localized to mitochondria [ ] A circRNA is a stable RNA molecule in various cells and tissues, and has been shown to act as a sponge for miRNAs [ ], interacting with proteins, regulating their activity and localization, such as RBPs [ , ], and are involved in regulating transcription and translation [ ]. Some circRNAs also have the potential to encode proteins [ ]. The ncRNAs with lengths of hundreds (200) nt or more are lncRNAs, According to the encoding gene and transcription direction, they can be divided into four categories: long intergenic noncoding RNAs (lincRNAs), intronic lncRNAs, sense lncRNAs and antisense lncRNAs [ ]. The most classic way for cytoplasmic lncRNAs to function is to act as miRNA sponges, forming a link in the ceRNA network [ ]. In addition, lncRNAs can also act as decoys and scaffolds, interact with proteins, RNA and DNA, and participate in epigenetic, alternative splicing, transcription, and post-transcriptional regulation [ , ]. NcRNAs play an important regulatory role in the pathophysiology of various diseases. Comparisons between RP models and wild-type models have shown altered expression profiles of ncRNAs that play an important role in retinal development, differentiation, disease, and prognosis [ , , , ]. This has been confirmed in several Dicer1 knockout or knock-in experiments in which animals exhibit some retinal structural and functional deficits.

3.2

## Effects of ncRNA on retinal neuronal cells
Photoreceptor cells are involved in visual functions such as phototransduction, and their normal structure and function are directly related to visual health. Some evidence suggests that ncRNAs are expressed in photoreceptor cells and are involved in regulating their function. By targeting LHX2, miR-124a is essential for cone cell maturation and survival, but it is only effective early in development and is not affected by miR-124a knockdown after differentiation [ ]. The regulatory feedback loop between miR cluster 143/145 and Nrl regulates the normal differentiation of rods [ ]. In zebrafish, miR-20b has been shown to inhibit photoreceptor proliferation, development, and promote apoptosis by targeting FGF2 and GRB2 through the MAPK/ERK pathway [ ]. FGF2 plays an important role in treating neuroretinal degeneration and protecting photoreceptors from oxidative damage [ ]. LncRNA TUG1 is required for normal photoreceptor development, and knockdown of lncRNA TUG1 can cause loss or shortening of the outer photoreceptor segment [ ], while miR-210 is essential for photoreceptor integrity and survival, and its absence leads to retinal degeneration [ ]. MiR-210 also regulates lipid metabolism by targeting acetyl coenzyme A synthase (ACS), thereby preventing neurodegeneration in the Drosophila retina [ ]. The direct role of miR-204 in the differentiation, maintenance, and function of photoreceptor cells has been well documented [ ]. Also a member of the miR-204/211, the function of miR-211 is only just beginning to be investigated. Although miR-211 is poorly expressed in adult retinal photoreceptors, the removal of miR-211 in the mouse compromises photoreceptor survival and function [ ]. MiR183 cluster (miR183C) miR-183/-96/-182 is highly universally expressed in photoreceptors and is a conserved sensory organ-specific miR-cluster [ ]. It is a key factor in the regulation of photoreceptors. It has rapid turnover ability by targeting voltage-dependent glutamate transporter Slc1a1, and its metabolism is higher than that of other cells [ ]. It plays an important regulatory role in photoreceptor differentiation [ , ], maturation [ ], synaptic connection [ , ], cone outer segments and function maintenance [ ], hair cell development and maintenance [ ], biomarker [ ], and circadian rhythm regulation [ ]. It also controls the normal distribution of retinal cells through the Rncr4 network [ ] and regulates retinal morphogenesis with PAX6 as the target [ ]. In addition, its downregulation may lead to photoreceptor death and retinal degeneration. MiR183c stimulus can protect photoreceptors in retinal degeneration models by targeting Caspase-2 [ ]. In recent years, research was not limited to the whole cluster, and the separate roles of the three parts were also investigated. MiR-183 is the most important component of miR183c, which maintains retinal function by targeting Rnf217 and cilia-related gene BBSome [ ]. Initially, Jin et al. did not observe retinal structural abnormalities through targeted deletion of miR-182, but visual function was not evaluated [ ]. In order to determine whether the regulatory effect of miR-182 can be compensated by miR-183/96, Xiang et al. subsequently created miR-183/96 double gene knockout (DKO) mice. Slc6a6 has been identified as a common target for miR-183 and miR-96, which is essential for cell maturation and maintenance of photoreceptors. Photoreceptor degeneration and decreased ERG response were observed in miR-183/96 DKO mice [ ]. They later created miR-183 knockout and miR-96 knockout models. MiR-183 knockout caused retinal dysfunction in mice, but did not show significant structural changes in the retina [ ]. Depletion of miR-96 delayed but did not arrest photoreceptor development in mice, considered essential for the maturation of photoreceptors [ ]. Although there are differences in expression levels in the retina, we believe that miR-183C supports and works together to maintain retinal structure and function.
As mentioned above, miR183c has important biological significance in all aspects of the retina, and its disorder can lead to serious structural and functional disorders of the retina. Therefore, it has great potential to become a biomarker and therapeutic target of retinopathy.
RPE cells are closely intertwined physically and functionally. The functional status of RPE directly affects photoreceptor health. A large body of experimental evidence suggests that ncRNAs play an important role in the development and function of RPE [ ]. For instance, let-7a/miR-125b [ ], miR-20b/106a, and miR-204/211 [ ] have a role in promoting the maturation and differentiation of RPE cells. Among them, miR-204/211 is the most abundant miRNA in RPE [ ], and microphthalmia-associated transcription factor (MITF) promotes differentiation of human RPE by regulating the expression of miR-204/211 [ ]. At the same time, lncRNA NEAT1 can change the ratio of (−) MITF through alternative splicing, so as to regulate the proliferation of RPE [ ]. LncRNA MEG3, lncRNA LINC00167 and lncRNA ZNF503-AS1 target miR-7-5p, miR-203a-3p and ZNF503, respectively, to regulate downstream protein expression and thus regulate RPE differentiation [ , , ]. One of the key functions of RPE is to phagocytize the outer segments of photoreceptor cells that fall off every day, which is essential for maintaining the balance of the retina and the function of photoreceptors [ ]. MiR-204 not only plays an important role in the development and maturation of RPE, but also regulates phagocytosis [ ]. Chen et al. proved that circNR3C1, as the sponge of miR-382-5p in RPE cells, targets PTEN to maintain the normal phenotype of RPE and promote RPE phagocytosis by blocking the AKT/mTOR signal pathway [ ]. In addition, it was found that circNR3C1 has multiple predicted binding sites for miRNAs, and whether it may target other miRNAs and signaling pathways has not yet been elucidated. The imbalance of ncRNAs mentioned above will lead to disorder of the structure and function of RPE cells.

3.3

## Effects of ncRNA on glial cells
Glial cells include microglia, Müller glial (MG) cells and astrocytes, and MG cells provide structural and metabolic support for neurons. Loss of MG cells will lead to retinal structure and function damage [ ]. MiR-7a has been shown to negatively regulate MG cell differentiation in vertebrate retinas by inhibiting the expression of Notch3 [ ]. MiR-9 is very important for the development, normal position and function maintenance of MG cells. Its deletion leads to an increased expression level of brevican, which results in pathological processes such as MG cell aggregation, migration, and retinal remodeling [ ]. MiR-29c inhibits RE-1 silencing transcription factor (REST) and promotes neuronal differentiation of late RPC at the expense of less MG cell production [ ]. All the above evidence suggests that ncRNA plays an important regulatory role in MG cells. The crosstalk between ncRNAs and the structure and function of microglia will be described in detail in the inflammatory section below.

3.4

## Extracellular vesicles as biomarkers
Extracellular vesicles (EVs) are vesicles bound by lipids secreted by almost all cell types and exist in all body fluids and the extracellular matrix. The internal components of EVs are usually composed of proteins and nucleic acids, especially miRNAs [ , ]. Experimental evidence has confirmed the significance of EVs as communication factors, therapeutics, and biomarkers. For example, EVs from RPE can affect the function and gene expression of rod and cone photoreceptors and microglia, and miR-21 in aged RPE exosomes can be transferred to co-cultured microglia to regulate their functional status [ ]. In addition, many studies have shown that stem cells transplanted during stem cell therapy are not integrated into the retina, and paracrine factor EV may be largely responsible for the efficacy [ ]. MiRNAs are tissue- and time-specific and highly conservative [ ]. Their expression is dysfunctional in many diseases and can be measured in different body fluids; thus, they are interesting biomarkers [ ]. Ahn et al. demonstrated that the increased level of EV miR-494-3p was a potential biomarker of mitochondrial dysfunction in RPE [ ]. Kakiuchi et al. also pointed out that circulating miR-96-5p, −1243p and 183-5p could be used as safe biomarkers of retinal toxicity [ ]. Vandermerwe et al. reviewed the role of EVs as biological markers, vectors (miRNAs) and therapeutics in the visual system [ ].

## Summary
As mentioned above, homeostasis of ncRNAs is essential for the maturation and survival of different retinal cells and for maintaining the normal structure and function of the retina. lists the targets and biological effects of ncRNAs in different retinal cells.

## NcRNAs and the pathogenesis of retinitis pigmentosa
Due to the asymmetry and heterogeneity of RP, it has been proven that the progression of RP is not only determined by the single factor of gene mutation. Most of the information on the mechanism of IRDs degeneration has been obtained from animal models that simulate the phenotype of photoreceptor cell degeneration, and understanding of the pathogenesis of RP is not comprehensive [ ]. However, more and more evidence has shown that apoptosis, oxidative stress, immune response/inflammation, and other factors are involved in the disease process, and we will discuss how ncRNAs alone participate in these important processes.
4.1

## Apoptosis
Apoptosis is the most intensively studied form of programmed cell death and is thought to be the primary mechanism of retinal degeneration [ ]. Apoptosis can be initiated in two ways: through mitochondria (intrinsic pathway) and death receptors on the cell surface (extrinsic pathway). Both pathways require activation of caspase [ ]. In addition to apoptosis, other forms of regulated cell death are also involved in cell loss in RP, including autophagy [ ], etc. We will highlight evidence for the role of ncRNAs in the apoptotic pathways. MiR-21-3p inhibition targets RGS4 to attenuate the lipopolysaccharide-induced inflammatory response and apoptosis in ARPE-19 cells [ ]. In vivo stem cell transplantation experiments in mice have shown that miR-21 maintains photoreceptor cell activity by targeting programmed cell death 4 (PDCD4) to counteract MNU damage [ ]. Silencing lncRNA-MEG3 reduces caspase3/7 activity, up-regulates the expression of anti-apoptotic protein (Bcl2) and down-regulates the expression of pro-apoptotic protein (Bax), thus preventing light-induced apoptosis in photoreceptor cells [ ]. Retinal cells in adult mammals do not regenerate spontaneously after death. Interestingly, MG cells in the adult zebrafish retina can dedifferentiate into neural precursor cells for retinal regeneration and eventual restoration of visual function. ASCL1 is considered to be a necessary transcription factor for MG dedifferentiation and retinal regeneration. ASCL1a-dependent induction of Lin-28 contributes to MG dedifferentiation by reducing the level of let-7 miRNA, let-7 can inhibit the expression of genes related to regeneration. The process was later found to respond in a Shh signaling dependent way [ , ]. In addition, Wohl et al. demonstrated that miR-124-9-9* enhances ASCL1-induced reprogramming through Rest signaling [ ]. Activation of neuroinflammatory pathways has been shown to be necessary to initiate regeneration of zebrafish retinal neurons (including photoreceptors) [ ]. For example, miR-18a regulates injury-induced photoreceptor regeneration by regulating inflammation [ ]. In recent years, the proliferative and neurogenic potential of miR-25, let-7 and miR-124 has been demonstrated in mammalian mouse MG cells [ , , ]. Of these, let-7 is the most widely studied with more precise evidence. In addition to MG cells, studies have proved that mature hRPE can also be transformed into neurons with photoreceptor properties through ASCL1 and miR-124 [ ]. The regeneration possibility of retinal cells described above may enable us to find other solutions for retinal degeneration. However, for the time being, delaying/stopping cell death may be a more viable option than death and regeneration, as it is effective in both the early and late stages of the disease.

4.2

## The role of ncRNA and oxidative stress in retinitis pigmentosa
Retinal cells are highly vulnerable to oxidative damage due to their high energy demand and active metabolism, as well as their continuous exposure to high oxygen levels and light-induced stress. During progressive photoreceptor degeneration in RP, oxygen utilization is reduced due to a decrease in the number of rod photoreceptors. The choroidal blood supply system lacks regulation and oxygen supply is not reduced. As a result, oxygen levels in the retina increase, which enhances the formation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and toxic free radicals. At the same time, antioxidant defense mechanisms are often negatively affected by aging and/or degenerative disease processes, leaving photoreceptor cells and RPE cells vulnerable to damage that accelerates retinal degeneration [ ]. Exposure to oxidative stress can alter the expression of miRNAs and long ncRNAs, which may be associated with the onset and progression of RP [ , , ].
Mini-αA can inhibit the activation of caspase-3, thus protecting RPE cells from apoptosis induced by H2O2 and preventing erroneous protein accumulation. Chen et al. suggested that the protective effect of Mini-αA may be mediated by miR-1246 [ ]. The activation of Sig1R can rescue cone cells by reducing oxidative stress and normalizing Nrf2 levels, which has become a new therapeutic target for retinal degenerative diseases [ ]. Recent experiments by Wang et al. have shown that Sig1R may play a protective role in the retina through the interaction with miR-214-3p, this finding is based on the authors' assumptions, and further studies are needed to clarify the relationship between Sig1R, Nrf2 and miR-214-3p [ ]. MiR-125b can protect RPE from oxidative damage by targeting the Nrf2/HIF-1 α signaling pathway [ ]. However, it has been reported that overexpression of miR-125b promotes dysglycemia in cells under oxidative stress by directly targeting hexosidase 2 (HK2) [ ]. Therefore, it remains to be seen whether miR-125b has a positive effect on the retina. MiR-17-3p inhibitors can achieve antioxidant stress protection by regulating the expression of manganese superoxide dismutase (MnSOD) and thioredoxin reductase-2 (TrxR2) [ ]. In the oxidative stress-induced RPE models, miR-23a and miR-374a can reduce the loss of RPE cells by activating the FAS-L/Fas pathway [ , ], and miR-27a/FOXO1 can regulate ROS accumulation and cell death [ ]. MiR-181 can reduce the atrophy and autophagy of RPE through the Bcl-2/Beclin1 autophagy pathway [ ]. Down-regulation of lncRNA PWRN2 was shown to reduce RPE cell death and mitochondrial damage [ ]. CircSPECC1 directly sponges miR-145-5p to regulate CDKN1A expression, resists oxidative stress injury and maintains lipid metabolism in RPE. This has been confirmed in vitro and in a mouse model of AMD, but it has not been validated in the RP model yet and further evidence is needed to confirm t the downstream regulatory pathway [ ]. Oxidative stress will lead to an imbalance in glucose metabolism, and the related dysregulation of lncRNA BDNF-AS and TUG1 will promote the apoptosis of RPE cells [ , ]. CircNNT and circZNF532 knockdown inhibit the high glucose-induced apoptosis of RPE [ , ]. All the above evidence confirms that ncRNAs reduce retinal cell death by participating in the regulation of oxidative stress.

4.3

## NcRNAs and inflammatory/immune responses in retinitis pigmentosa
Damage-associated molecular patterns released from apoptotic or necrotic photoreceptors trigger the release of MG cell inflammatory factors and cytokines, and activated MG cells increase CX3CL3 expression and secretion. This leads to the transformation of stationary microglia (M1) to an activated state (M2) and the secretion of inflammatory factors and complement C3. Stressed/damaged photoreceptors are also exposed to PS on the cell membrane surface, which is recognized by M2 phagocyte receptors. Autoimmune processes may also contribute to the pathogenesis of RP, leading to additional retinal degeneration [ ].
Microglia and MG cells play an important role in retinal immune defense, and activation of immune defenses in pathological states triggers an inflammatory response, which subsequently promotes the development of retinal disease. Finally, as the disease progresses, systemic inflammation is involved in disruption of the BRB. There is considerable evidence that inflammation is another potential mechanism for photoreceptor cell death [ , ]. To date, 25 miRNAs have been identified to be involved in the regulation of microglia polarization and inflammatory function, with miR-204 being the most prominent [ ]. As previously mentioned, miR-204 is present in high levels in the retina, miR-204 is essential for normal retinal structure and function, and there is evidence that it also plays a role in the inflammatory response to RP. When AAV-miR-204 was applied in a dominant RP model, microglia activation and apoptosis of photoreceptors were downregulated to protect retinal function. However, due to the limitations of the model, the reduction in PR death did not cause a significant increase in ONL thickness at the analysis stage. In view of the importance of miR-204 in ocular physiology and pathology, other models (long course of disease), carriers, and different doses and frequencies are worth investigating [ ]. Chemokine (C–C motif) ligand 2 (CCL2), an inflammatory chemokine secreted by Muller cells, has been shown to be upregulated in RP models [ ]. In retinal degeneration, miR-124 is localized from photoreceptor cells to MG cells and its expression is reduced. MiR-124 reduces inflammation and photoreceptor cell death by targeting CCL2 [ ]. Su et al. proved that the miR-9-5p/IDH3α axis in the ArRP model was associated with IDH3α mutations as a possible target for the prevention of immune response-induced inflammation [ ]. In addition to the regulation of retinal physiological functions, lncRNA NEAT1 regulates macrophage polarization through the miR-148a-3p/PTEN axis, thus playing a role in inflammation [ ].

4.4

## Summary
NcRNAs participate in the pathogenesis of RP through the above factors. In addition, phototoxicity and reduction of trophic factors also promote progress of the disease. In recent years, unfolded protein response (UPR) has attracted much attention as a factor in disease progression (misfolding and accumulation of rhodopsin in the endoplasmic reticulum) in P23H RHO mutant mice. The activation of other factors mentioned above may be caused by the continuous activation of UPR [ ]. There is no doubt that this pathway will be a promising therapeutic target, but unfortunately, no ncRNA associated with this pathway has been found to date. shows that different ncRNAs participate in RP regulation in different pathways.

## NcRNAs affect retinitis pigmentosa through multiple signaling pathways
Structural and functional impairment of specific proteins caused by gene mutations, among others, is associated with abnormal expression of signaling pathways and downstream genes involved in the development of RP.
5.1

## NF-κB signaling pathway
NF-κB is a widely expressed transcription factor that can be activated by inflammatory cytokines (tumor necrosis factor-α, TNF-α), physical and oxidative stress, affecting stress response, cell proliferation and apoptosis [ ]. Regulation of the NF-κB pathway in RP is essential to prevent excessive inflammatory responses [ ]. Under oxidative stress induction, increased expression of lncRNA CYLD-AS1 can act as a sponge for miR-134-5p, which is translocated from the cytoplasm to the nucleus together with Nrf2 to further promote nuclear transcription, thereby activating anti-oxidative stress factors and inhibiting the activation of NF-κB signaling, which ultimately regulates the inflammatory response. In addition, it can regulate the inflammatory response of other cells by regulating the secretion of exosomes [ ]. Recently, Kutty et al. found that both miR-146a and miR-146b-5p were expressed in human retinal pigment epithelial cells in the RD10 mouse model, and their expression was highly induced by pro-inflammatory cytokines [interferon-gamma (IFN-γ) + TNF-α + interleukin (IL)-1β]. Induction of miR-146a showed dependence on IL-1β, while induction of miR-146b-5p showed dependence on IFN-γ, possibly through the JAK/STAT pathway. These two miRNAs negatively regulated NF-κB by targeting IRAK1 [ ]. It was shown that MSC-EVs could regulate inflammatory responses through the miR-146a-Nr4a3 (nuclear receptor subfamily 4, group A, member 3) axis, and down-regulation of Nr4a3 significantly inhibited IL-1β-induced activation of the NF-κB signaling pathway [ ]. In addition, RAC1 has been shown to be a pro-apoptotic factor in the retina during photoinduced photoreceptor degeneration [ ]. ROS mediated by Rac1 (RAS-related C3 botulinum substrate 1) stimulate NF-κB phosphorylation and induce the release of TNF-α in retinal microglia cells. In addition, miR-124 directly regulates Rac1 expression [ ].

5.2

## Nrf2 signaling pathway
The transcription factor NF-E2 p45-related factor 2 (Nrf2) is thought to be involved in regulating homeostasis in vivo, with antioxidants as its primary function [ ]. MiR-141 activates Nrf2 signaling by targeting Keap1. High expression of miR-141 down-regulates Keap1, leading to destabilization, accumulation, and nuclear translocation of Nrf2, which results in transcription of multiple antioxidant response element (ARE) genes [heme oxygenase-1 (HO1), NAD(P)H quinone oxidoreductase 1 (NQO1), and γ-glutamyl cysteine ligase catalytic subunit (GCLC)] [ ]. MiR-626 [ ] and miR-125b also activate Nrf2 signaling by targeting Keap1, while miR-125b counteracts this effect by promoting Nrf2 silencing and inducing enhanced expression of HIF-1α degradation. Subsequent in vivo experiments confirmed that exogenous supplementation of miR-125b reversed the induction of outer retina thinning by sodium iodate [ ]. Overexpression of miR-601 significantly inhibited Cul3′-UTR activity, down-regulated Cul3 mRNA/protein expression, and led to activation of the Nrf2 signaling pathway and expression of downstream targets [ ]. Subretinal injection of miR-144-3p antagomir in SI mice enhanced the expression of antioxidant genes and reduced retinal oxidative stress and limited apoptosis [ ]. The above evidence suggests that ncRNAs may regulate the antioxidant stress capacity of RP through the Nrf2 signaling pathway.

5.3

## Other signaling pathways
Up-regulation of the mTOR pathway by conditional knockdown of the mTOR inhibitor in Pde6b mutation rods (RD10 mouse) improved rod and cone survival [ ]. Researchers found that extracellular stress activated the NF-κB signaling pathway, and reduced expression of miR-24 in the downstream led to an increase in CHI3L1 (Chitinase-3-like protein 1). CHI3L1 causes abnormal autophagy and dysfunction of RPE through activation of the AKT/mTOR and ERK pathways [ ]. Let-7a/miR-125b and miR-204/211 affect the proliferation and differentiation of retinal cells through the MAPK/ERK signaling pathway [ ]. The inflammatory cytokine mixture (IFN-γ + TNF-α + IL-1β) is activated through the JAK/STAT (Janus kinase/Signal transducer and activator of Jak/STAT transcription) signaling pathway, which increases miR155 and lncRNA BANCR expression in the hRPE [ , ]. In mice with photooxidation-induced retinal degeneration, miR-155 was expressed by glial cells in response to stress and subsequently transported to photoreceptors in an S–MeV-dependent manner, playing a positive role in reducing retinal inflammation [ ]. The lncRNA BANCR plays an important role mainly in tumorigenesis and development, as previously reviewed [ ]. LncRNA BANCR is overexpressed in patients with diabetic retinopathy and promotes apoptosis of retinal pigment epithelial cells [ ], but its role in RP inflammation is unclear.

## Clinical applications
NcRNAs have the following several advantages in the treatment of ocular diseases: 1) one ncRNA can target multiple sites, alleviating the problem of heterogeneity in gene therapy; 2) they have a higher safety profile compared to stem cell transplantation; and 3) subretinal administration does not affect changes in gene expression in other tissues and systems due to the BRB function of the retina. Nevertheless, miRNA-based therapeutics are still hampered by major issues such as off-target effects, immunogenicity, uncertain delivery platforms, and the dosage of miRNA [ ]. Although the therapeutic potential of ncRNA for RP is still in the exploratory stage, it has been shown to be a good potential target for the diagnosis, treatment and prognosis prediction of a variety of diseases. These include tumors and some retinal degenerative diseases (diabetic retinopathy, AMD, etc.), and a large number of clinical trials are actively underway. For example, Migdalska-Sge k et al. concluded in a set of controlled experiments that PPARδ and miR-17 may be markers for distinguishing tumor tissue and surgical margins, and that miR-17 may play a diagnostic role in the differentiation of tissue types in non-small cell lung cancer [ ]. In the hand, foot and mouth disease cohorts, hsa-miR-221 was found to be consistently and significantly down-regulated, demonstrating that hand, foot and mouth disease/infection could be diagnosed by miRNA circulating in the saliva of patients [ ]. MiR-122 promoted RV-induced lung disease in vivo by inhibiting its target SOCS1. High expression of miR-122 was associated with a poorer clinical prognosis. Studies have identified anti-miR-122 oligonucleotides as potential agents for the treatment of worsening capillary bronchitis and asthma [ ]. In a cohort study, Romeo et al. found that plasma miR-375 could be a promising prognostic marker in patients with advanced medullary thyroid carcinoma [ ]. In addition, miRNA-based delivery vectors have been reviewed [ ]. In response to the development of ncRNAs, research is constantly updated and developed, and viral reagents (e.g., the CRISPR-Cas system) or nanoparticle reagents are the most common delivery methods for ncRNA therapy [ ].

7

## Conclusion and perspectives
RP is an inherited blindness and there is a lack of effective therapeutic strategies to inhibit the progression of RP and protect patients’ vision. In recent years, ncRNA has been extensively studied as a signaling molecule involved in multisystem pathophysiological processes, making it a biomarker and a meaningful target for diagnosis, treatment and prognosis, attracting widespread attention. A growing body of evidence suggests that ncRNAs are closely associated with the maintenance of normal retinal function and the pathogenesis of RP. However, most of these studies have been limited to molecular cytology and animal experiments, and the exact mechanisms are not yet clear. There is now evidence that correction of abnormal ncRNA expression may be a promising non-gene-dependent therapeutic approach to slow down RP.
Currently, ncRNAs as therapeutics are included in research and development in various disciplines. The results of some existing studies have increased our confidence in exploring the clinical implications of ncRNAs for RP. More studies are in progress and we believe that the future of RP therapy will provide a new landscape.
