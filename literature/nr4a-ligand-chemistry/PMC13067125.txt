## Abstract
Hepatic fibrosis (HF), a common pathological consequence of chronic liver injury, is driven by the excessive proliferation and activation of hepatic stellate cells (aHSCs). Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis, the inflammatory form of metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD, formerly known as non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, NAFLD), serves as a key driver of the HF process. Although previous studies have found that lupeol can act as an FXR-agonist to ameliorate MASLD through the FXR-SHP pathway, its therapeutic potential is severely limited by drawbacks such as poor solubility, low stability, rapid degradation, insufficient targeting specificity, collectively constrain its therapeutic potential. To overcome these limitations, we developed the HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8—a dual-functional nano-delivery system combining CD44-targeting and pH-responsive drug release. It encapsulates the FXR-agonist lupeol in ZIF-8 framework and is surface-modified with hyaluronic acid to allow for active targeting of CD44-highly-expressing aHSCs. In the acidic-fibrotic microenvironment, ZIF-8 undergoes pH-responsive degradation, enabling precise release of both the lupeol and Zn2+. The released Zn2+ further disrupt glycolysis and facilitate the reversion of aHSCs to quiescent state, thereby synergistically enhancing the anti-fibrotic efficacy. The nanoparticles exhibited a uniform size (125.7 ± 0.16 nm), high stability, pH-dependent drug release and strong CD44-binding affinity, collectively improving lupeol's solubility and targeted delivery. Both in vitro and vivo experiments confirmed that HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 suppresses fibrotic markers through FXR-SHP activation. This study not only elucidates the anti-fibrotic mechanism of Lupeol but also presents an integrated targeted and microenvironment responsive strategy with considerable potential for clinical translation in the treatment of HF.
Keywords: Drug delivery, FXR-SHP, Hepatic fibrosis, CD44, ZIF-8

## Highlights
•The first to propose a novel research approach focusing on the mechanism by which lupeol prevents and treats hepatic fibrosis through the “FXR-SHP” signaling pathway. This holds significant original innovation for elucidating the “same treatment for different diseases” potential of lupeol.

## Introduction
Hepatic fibrosis (HF), as a type of chronic liver disease, lacks effective pharmacological treatments [ ]. Its core pathological driver is the aberrant activation of hepatic stellate cells (HSCs) [ ]. This process can be triggered by various factors, including viral infections, metabolic disorders, alcohol abuse, autoimmunity and bile acid accumulation. Among these, metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD, formerly known as non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, NAFLD) serves as a critical trigger. MASLD initiates with hepatocellular steatosis, which subsequently induces oxidative stress and hepatocyte injury. This process activates the NLRP3 inflammasome and promotes the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-6, IL-10 and TGF-β [ , , ], thereby fostering a chronic inflammatory microenvironment. In this context, the abnormal accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) serves not only as a key initiating factor driving the progression of HF but also as a crucial amplifying factor that promotes a vicious cycle among oxidative stress, inflammatory response, and fibrosis. Specifically, ROS first acts as an “initiating signal” to directly activate HSCs, the core effector cells in HF. Simultaneously, as an “effector molecule” and “inflammatory amplifier” ROS persistently exacerbates tissue damage and promotes the formation of an inflammation-fibrosis vicious cycle. Consequently, ROS occupies a central hub in the pathogenesis of HF, functioning both as an upstream trigger for HSC activation and as a critical bridge linking hepatocyte injury, inflammatory response, and abnormal deposition of extracellular matrix (ECM). It is noteworthy that redox imbalance induced by high-level oxidative stress triggered by ROS is not only closely associated with the progression of MASLD but also widely linked to the development of inflammation, aging, and various metabolic diseases [ ]. This cascade drives the progression to non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH), ultimately leading to hepatic fibrosis, cirrhosis and even hepatocellular carcinoma [ ]. During this process, key factors such as transforming growth factor-beta 1 (TGF-β1) drive the activation of quiescent hepatic stellate cells (qHSCs) into their activated state (aHSCs) [ , ]. These aHSCs exhibit high expression of alpha-smooth muscle actin (α-SMA) and type I collagen(Collagen1) [ ], accompanied by excessive secretion of ECM. Concurrently, the expression of the CD44 receptor on their surface is significantly upregulated. CD44 not only serves as a key biomarker for HSC activation but also emerges as an ideal molecular target for drug delivery. However, therapeutics aimed at aHSCs face multiple challenges, including insufficient targeting specificity, rapid in vivo metabolism, poor biocompatibility, and inadequate control over drug release [ , ]. Consequently, developing precise drug delivery strategies that target CD44 to intervene in HSC activation has become a highly promising therapeutic avenue for blocking the progression from MASLD to HF.
The Farnesoid X receptor (FXR) and its downstream target, the small heterodimer partner (SHP), form a pivotal signaling pathway within the core regulatory network of hepatic metabolic homeostasis. This pathway is integral to the regulation of bile acid, lipid, and glucose metabolism, and it also exhibits anti-fibrotic potential by suppressing the expression of pro-fibrogenic genes [ ], such as α-SMA, Collagen1, and CD44. CD44 serves as a critical molecular link between inflammation and fibrosis, promoting the activation of HSCs and the retention of inflammatory cells, thereby directly accelerating ECM deposition and driving fibrotic progression. Our prior research has confirmed that lupeol (a natural pentacyclic triterpenoid) effectively ameliorates hepatic steatosis and inflammation in MASLD by activating the FXR-SHP pathway [ ]. However, as MASLD represents an early stage of liver injury, it remains unclear whether the FXR-SHP pathway continues to exert a protective role during its progression to HF. Given that the FXR-SHP pathway inhibits key drivers of MASLD—lipotoxicity and inflammation, which are also central forces behind fibrotic advancement, we reasonably hypothesize that this pathway plays a critical regulatory function in the fibrotic stage as well. Mechanistically, activation of the FXR-SHP pathway may create a “low-inflammation” microenvironment, thereby reducing the impetus for CD44 expression [ ], while also potentially directly suppressing HSC activation and the consequent deposition of α-SMA and collagen1. Although direct evidence concerning the pathway's role in fibrosis remains relatively limited, strong indirect evidence supports our hypothesis. Previous studies have indicated that FXR agonists, such as obeticholic acid, can inhibit HSC activation and alleviate experimental liver fibrosis [ ]. Therefore, we propose the following hypothesis: activation of the FXR-SHP pathway constitutes a core protective mechanism spanning the entire continuum from MASLD to HF. This study aims to directly validate the therapeutic value of targeting the FXR-SHP pathway in HF. Successfully doing so would not only deepen our previous findings but also bridge a critical mechanistic gap between metabolic regulation and fibrosis suppression, providing a vital theoretical foundation for developing novel therapies for advanced liver disease.
Lupeol is a low-cost and easily obtainable pentacyclic triterpenoid active ingredient originating from Cichorium glandulosum Boiss. et Huet (CG), a traditional medicinal plant used in Xinjiang for liver protection. This plant has a long-standing medicinal history, and its hepatoprotective value is authoritatively confirmed by its inclusion in the Xinjiang Uyghur Pharmacopoeia. Studies have confirmed that it can effectively modulate the immune microenvironment, demonstrating potential therapeutic value in the treatment of metabolic diseases. Moreover, its anti-cancer effects have been extensively documented in the literature [ , ]. Building on evidence that lupeol alleviates MASLD by activating the FXR-SHP pathway as an FXR agonist, a key unanswered question is whether this protection persists during progression to HF. This research is therefore designed to systematically evaluate the mechanism and efficacy of lupeol and the FXR-SHP pathway during the fibrotic stage. Our research group has successfully obtained high-purity Lupeol from CG extract, with a content of up to 3.67% and a purity exceeding 95% [ ]. Despite lupeol's promising prospects, its inherent drawbacks as a drug candidate significantly hinder its clinical translation. As a pentacyclic triterpenoid, lupeol is highly hydrophobic and exhibits extremely poor aqueous solubility, which directly restricts its intestinal absorption and oral bioavailability. In terms of metabolic stability, the O-H bond at its C3 hydroxyl group and the carbon-carbon double bond on the isopropenyl side chain are susceptible to substitution and oxidation, leading to its rapid systemic clearance. More critically, this inherent hydrophobicity impedes effective circulation within the hydrophilic bloodstream and targeted delivery to the liver. Consequently, achieving effective therapeutic concentrations in the liver often necessitates high-dose administration, which significantly increases the risk of systemic toxicity. To address these challenges, we turned to a nanodrug delivery strategy [ , ]. Among various nanocarriers, zeolitic imidazolate framework-8 (ZIF-8) stands out due to its unique advantages: (1) High Drug Loading and Solubilization: ZIF-8 can be rapidly self-assembled under mild aqueous conditions, a process that is simple, reproducible, and allows for simultaneous encapsulation of lupeol. Its hydrophobic cavities exhibit high affinity for the hydrophobic structure of lupeol, and combined with electrostatic adsorption, enable highly efficient drug loading. This encapsulates lupeol in a nano-dispersed form, significantly improving its apparent solubility and absorption potential [ ]. (2) Smart Targeting and Release: ZIF-8 possesses excellent pH-responsive degradation properties. It remains stable under normal physiological conditions (pH 7.4) but rapidly dissociates in the weakly acidic microenvironment (pH 6.5–6.8) characteristic of fibrotic liver regions, enabling intelligent, targeted drug release at the disease site [ , , ]. (3) Improved Therapeutic Window: By enhancing hepatic accumulation and enabling targeted release, this strategy is expected to significantly reduce the required administered dose and minimize drug exposure in non-target tissues, thereby improving the therapeutic index and overall safety profile. (4) Synergistic Therapeutic Potential: The Zn2+ released upon the degradation of ZIF-8 have been reported to possess intrinsic anti-fibrotic activity and can be metabolized by the body [ ], demonstrating good biocompatibility. Therefore, beyond the controlled release of lupeol, this delivery system may exert a synergistic anti-fibrotic effect by combining the action of lupeol with the bioactivity of zinc ions. In comparison, traditional nanocarriers such as liposomes or polymeric micelles typically lack the sharp pH responsiveness of ZIF-8. Their drug release is often dominated by passive diffusion, which is less “intelligent”. Furthermore, their surfaces do not inherently provide a metal-organic interface as amenable to straightforward modification with targeting ligands like hyaluronic acid (HA) as ZIF-8 does. When contrasted with other metal-organic frameworks (MOFs), ZIF-8 achieves an optimal balance among biocompatibility, aqueous stability, and controlled degradability. Many other MOFs may suffer from instability under physiological conditions or require harsh synthesis parameters that are incompatible with efficient drug loading and biomedical applications [ , ]. In this experiment, for the synthesis of ZIF-8, zinc nitrate hexahydrate commonly used in the literature was not adopted; instead, zinc acetate anhydrous was selected. This choice is based on consideration of the drawbacks of the traditional method: zinc nitrate not only has higher toxicity and risks, is relatively less accessible, but its excessively fast reaction kinetics often leads to a wide particle size distribution in the product. The use of zinc acetate anhydrous effectively avoids these problems, enabling better control over the synthesis process and product quality.
The application of ZIF-8 in drug delivery for tumor therapy has been extensively explored, and in recent years, its use has also expanded to the treatment of HF. For instance, researchers have developed vitamin A-modified ZIF-8 lipid nanoparticles, leveraging the specific targeting ability of vitamin A toward aHSCs to achieve targeted delivery of anti-fibrotic drugs within the liver [ ]. This suggests that functionalizing ZIF-8 to confer active targeting capabilities is an effective strategy for enhancing the precision of HF treatment. To this end, this study focuses on the abundant zinc sites on the surface of ZIF-8, which can coordinate with carboxyl groups. By conjugating the high-affinity targeting ligand HA into the system, the surface functionalization of the nanoparticles was achieved to enhance their targeting recognition ability toward aHSCs. HA is a natural polysaccharide that can specifically bind to CD44 receptors [ , ], which can specifically bind to the over-expressed CD44 receptors on the surface of various tumor cells. As a result, HA has garnered significant attention in tumor-targeted applications for cancer therapy [ ]. However, CD44 is not only expressed in tumor cells but also present in other organs [ ]. Although CD44 is present at low levels in some normal tissues, its significant upregulation on aHSCs provides a targeting opportunity [ , , ]. Extensive studies have modified HA onto various nanocarriers for targeted delivery of drugs in cancer and liver fibrosis treatment [ ]. Within ZIF-8 systems, HA modification has also been reported for antibacterial and tumor-targeted therapies [ ], demonstrating the feasibility of HA@ZIF-8 composite systems in biological delivery. However, no studies have yet reported the use of HA-modified ZIF-8 for delivering lupeol to treat HF. To address this research gap, we constructed a “GPS-guided” nanocarrier that promotes specific recognition and receptor-mediated internalization into aHSCs (HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8), establishing a multi-level targeting strategy for precise delivery [ ]. This system not only retains the intrinsic pH-responsive drug release property of ZIF-8 but also enables active recognition and accumulation in aHSCs through specific HA-CD44 interactions, ultimately achieving cell-level precision in drug delivery. This unique physiological mechanism offers a strong rationale for using HA in targeted therapy of liver fibrosis [ ].
For the route of administration, we selected intravenous injection over oral delivery based on the following rationale: although oral administration is the most convenient method, ZIF-8 nanoparticles would rapidly degrade in the highly acidic environment of the stomach, leading to the destruction or premature release of lupeol before absorption, thereby failing to ensure sufficient drug delivery to the liver. Intravenous injection bypasses the gastrointestinal barrier, allowing intact nanoparticles to enter the systemic circulation directly. Subsequently, via the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect and active targeting, the nanoparticles can accumulate more efficiently in fibrotic regions of the liver. Intravenous administration prevented gastric degradation and enhanced liver accumulation.
In summary, building upon our previous findings in MASLD, this study innovatively extends the investigation of the FXR-SHP pathway to the field of HF. We designed and constructed a dual-targeting nano-delivery system-HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8, which combines CD44 targeting capability with pH-responsive drug release. Administered intravenously, this system aims to overcome the druggability challenges of lupeol and validate its efficacy and mechanism in combating hepatic fibrosis via activation of the hepatic FXR-SHP pathway. This work not only provides a feasible delivery strategy to facilitate the clinical translation of lupeol but also offers new scientific evidence for a deeper understanding of the role of the FXR-SHP pathway in HF.

## Materials and methods
2.1.

## Materials and reagents
Lupeol Certified Reference Standard (CRS) (with HPLC purity ≥98%; CAS: 545-47-1; Pubchem CID: 259846), Lupeol Reference Standard was identified by HPLC, 1H NMR and 13C NMR spectroscopy ( ) (with HPLC purity ≥95%), 2-Methimidazole (2-Mim), Nile Red (NR), Coumarin 6 (Cou6), and anhydrous zinc acetate (Zn (CH3COO2) 2) were purchased from Aladin Reagents (Shanghai, China). Hyaluronic acid (HA) was purchased from Sololebao Technology Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China), and methanol (99.5% purity) was purchased from Shanghai Lingfeng Chemical Reagents Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). These reagents were used without any further purification. The ultrapure water was prepared by using a microporous purification system (PERSEE, China). All other chemicals used were also of analytical reagent grade.

2.2.

## Preparation of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8
The loading scheme was optimized by comparing the loading efficiency and encapsulation efficiency under different process conditions [ ]. HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 was prepared using a room-temperature solution reaction method. Specifically, 10 mg of lupeol and 330 mg of 2-Mim were co-dissolved in 10 mL of methanol, while 92 mg of anhydrous zinc acetate was dissolved in 5 mL of deionized water. Subsequently, 5 mL of the aqueous solution was slowly added to 5 mL of the methanol solution at 25 °C, and the mixture was stirred for 24 h. The resulting product was centrifuged (12,000×g, 15 min) to remove unreacted reagents. The precipitate was washed three times with methanol via centrifugation (12,000×g, 15 min) to obtain Lupeol@ZIF-8. The supernatant from each centrifugation step was collected, and the absorbance at 243 nm was measured using a UV spectrophotometer (UV-2600, Shimadzu, Japan) to determine the lupeol content in Lupeol@ZIF-8, enabling the calculation of the drug loading and encapsulation efficiency. Next, 5 mg of HA was dissolved in 5 mL of deionized water, and 5 mg of the prepared Lupeol@ZIF-8 was dispersed in 1 mL of deionized water at room temperature. After standing for 20 min, the two solutions were mixed and stirred at room temperature for 24 h. The mixture was then centrifuged (12,000×g, 15 min) and washed three times. The final precipitate was freeze-dried to obtain HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8.

2.3.

## Sample characterization
HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 was prepared using a room-temperature solution reaction method, and its successful preparation was confirmed through Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR, Thermo Fisher Scientific Nicolet iS20, USA), Automatic Surface Area and Porosity Analyzer (BET, Micromeritics ASAP 2460, USA), and X-ray diffraction (XRD, Rigaku SmartLab SE, Japan). The size, polydispersity index (PDI), and zeta potential of the synthesized samples were measured using dynamic light scattering (DLS, Malvern Nano-ZS, UK). The morphology of the nanoparticles was characterized using transmission electron microscopy (TEM, HT7700, Japan). The UV absorbance of lupeol was measured at 243 nm using a UV spectrophotometer (UV-2600, Shimadzu, Japan). The drug loading content (DL) and encapsulation efficiency (EE) were calculated using the following equations:
EE(%)=drugmass/drugmasspackagedinthecarrier×100%DL(%)=drugmass/copolymermaterialmasspackagedinthecarrier×100%
2.4.

## pH-responsive release characteristics of Lupeol from HA/Lupeol @ ZIF-8
6.25 mg of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 was uniformly dispersed in 50 mL of phosphate buffer (pH 7.4, 6.5, and 5.8) containing 10% Tween 80. The centrifuge tube was then placed in a constant temperature shaker with a shaking frequency of 100×g for the drug release experiment. At each predetermined time point, 10 mL of the release medium was collected and centrifuged, and the supernatant was removed. An equal volume of fresh phosphate buffer containing 10% Tween 80 (10 mL) was added to each group to maintain the total volume and continue the drug release experiment. The absorbance of the supernatant at 243 nm was measured using a UV-visible spectrophotometer to determine the concentration of lupeol, representing the cumulative amount of released lupeol at each time point. Each experiment was performed in triplicate, and the average values were used for analysis. The release rate was calculated using the following formula:
Releaserate(%)=ReleaseofLupeol/TotalLupeolcontentinthecarriercomplexes×100%The concentration of Zn2+ in the samples at each time point was measured by ICP-MS: Following the same procedure, the centrifuged and filtered samples were appropriately diluted, and the Zn2+ concentration in the samples at each time point was measured using Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry (ICP-MS, Aglient 7850, USA), Each sample was tested in at least three replicates.

2.5.

## Stability experiments
HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 was uniformly dispersed in 50 mL of phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) and placed in a shaker at 100×g to evaluate the stability of the drug-loaded complex over 50 h. At 0, 10, 20, 30, 40, and 50 h, 10 mL of the solution was collected and immediately replaced with 10 mL of fresh phosphate buffer to maintain a constant volume. The average particle size and PDI of the drug complex were measured at 25 °C to analyze the stability trend of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 over time, providing insights into its potential stability under physiological conditions.

2.6.

## Cell culture
Rat hepatic stellate cell (HSC-T6), normal mouse hepatocyte (AML-12) and human hepatic stellate cell (LX-2) were provided by the Xinjiang Autonomous Region Institute of Pharmaceutical Research. DMEM medium was purchased from Gibco (Grand Island, USA). The complete medium was prepared by supplementing the base medium with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS, Zhejiang Tianhang Biotechnology Co., Ltd, Zhejiang, China) and 1% penicillin-streptomycin (Gibco, Grand Island, USA). This complete medium was used for cell resuscitation, passaging, and subsequent experiments.

2.7.

## Cell viability test and hemolysis assay
The cytotoxicity of different nanoparticle formulations was evaluated in vitro using the CCK-8 assay. LX-2 and HSC-T6 cells in the logarithmic growth phase were seeded into 96-well culture plates at a density of 1 × 104 cells per well and incubated at 37 °C with 5% CO2 for 24 h. After static adherence, the original culture medium was removed, and the cells were treated with free drugs (Lupeol, ZIF-8, Lupeol@ZIF-8 and HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8). The old medium was discarded, 100 μL of phosphate buffer was added to each well, followed by 200 μL of medium containing 10% (v/v) CCK-8 solution. The plates were incubated for 2 h at 37 °C, and the absorbance of the solution in each well was measured using a microplate reader. Three replicate wells were set up for each group, and the results of the cell proliferation experiments were expressed as mean ± standard deviation (SD).
cellviability(%)=(Asample−Ablankgroup)/(Acontrolgroup−Ablankgroup)×100To evaluate the hemocompatibility of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8, a red blood cell (RBC) hemolysis assay was performed. Briefly, 0.2 mL of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 nanoparticles at varying concentrations (10–100 μmol/L) was mixed with 1.8 mL of 2% (v/v) murine RBC suspension. Physiological saline and distilled water were used as negative and positive controls, respectively. The mixtures were incubated in a 37 °C water bath for 1 h, followed by centrifugation at 3, 000×g for 10 min. The sedimentation of RBCs was visually inspected and photographed. Subsequently, 200 μL of the supernatant was transferred to a 96-well plate, and the optical density (OD) was measured at 545 nm using a microplate reader. The hemolysis rate (HR) was calculated as follows:
Hemolysis(%)=(ODsample−ODnegativecontrol)/(ODpositivegroup−ODnegativecontrol)×100%
2.8.

## Live/dead cell staining
The Live/Dead Assay Kit (Beyotime) was used to evaluate the cell viability of HSC-T6 and LX-2 cells treated with Lupeol, Lupeol@ZIF-8 and HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8. First, cells were seeded into a 6-well plate at a density of 1.0 × 106 cells per well and cultured for 24 h. Subsequently, Lupeol, Lupeol@ZIF-8 and HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 (with a consistent lupeol concentration of 60 μM in all samples, as used in subsequent experiments) were co-cultured with the cells for 24 h. After incubation, the culture medium was discarded, and the cells were washed with PBS. Next, 60 μL of culture medium containing propidium iodide (PI, 5 μM) and Calcein-AM (2 μM) was added to each well and incubated for 30 min. Finally, the cells were observed under a fluorescence microscope (Olympus, Tokyo, Japan).

2.9.

## ROS levels analysis
The 2',7'-dichlorodihydrofluorescein diacetate (DCFH-DA, Solarbio) fluorescent probe was used to measure the intracellular ROS levels in cells treated with Lupeol, Lupeol@ZIF-8, and HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8. Specifically, cells were divided into the following groups: control (no treatment), Model (TGF-β1), Lupeol, Lupeol@ZIF-8 and HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8, and incubated for 6 h. After incubation, the cells were washed with phosphate buffer, and DCFH-DA solution was added, followed by incubation for 30 min. Finally, fluorescence images were captured using a fluorescence microscope (Olympus, Tokyo, Japan). Quantitative analysis was performed using flow cytometry (Beckman Coulter, USA).

2.10.

## Cell apoptosis assay
A DAPI Staining Solution (Beyotime Biotechnology, Shanghai, China) was used to perform the cell apoptosis shape assay on LX-2 cells according to the manufacturer's instructions. Cells were seeded into six-well plates at a density of 1.0 × 106 cells per well and cultured for 24 h. The next day, cells were treated with different media containing Lupeol, Lupeol@ZIF-8 and HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 (60 μM lupeol), with blank and model groups included as controls, and incubated for an additional 24 h. For qualitative analysis, DAPI solution was added 24 h after treatment, and fluorescence signals were recorded using a fluorescence microscope (Olympus, Tokyo, Japan).

2.11.

## Cell scratching experiment
6-well plates were seeded with HSC-T6 and AML-12 cells at a density of 1 × 106 cells per well. When the cells adhered to the bottom of the culture plates and formed a confluent monolayer, a straight cell-free zone was created in each well by uniformly scratching the cell layer using a 200 μL sterile pipette tip. The medium was then carefully aspirated, and each well was washed with 500 μL of phosphate buffer. Subsequently, 2 mL of the corresponding treatment medium containing Lupeol, Lupeol@ZIF-8 and HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 was added to each well, and the cells were incubated for 24 h. Finally, images of the scratched areas were captured using an inverted microscope (Leica, DMI1, China).

2.12.

## Glycolysis inhibition in aHSCs
In this therapeutic study, LX-2 cells were co-incubated under the following conditions: control group, TGF-β1-stimulated group, TGF-β1+Lupeol group, TGF-β1+Lupeol@ZIF-8 group and TGF-β1+HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 group. Lactate concentration, lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) activity, and adenosine triphosphate (ATP) content were measured using the lactate assay kit, LDH activity assay kit, and ATP assay kit, respectively, all provided by Sevier Biotechnology Co., Ltd (Wuhan, China) [ ].

2.13.

## Cellular uptake
Cou6-Labeled HA@ZIF-8 Preparation: 5 mg of ZIF-8 and 2 mg of Cou6 were dissolved in deionized water [ ], sonicated and mixed for 30 min. The mixture was then placed in a 4 °C shaker overnight. The next day, the mixture was centrifuged at 8,000×g for 10 min, and the process was repeated several times until the supernatant became clear and transparent. The resulting precipitate was lyophilized and stored for further use. Next, 5 mg of HA was dissolved in 5 mL of deionized water, and 5 mg of the prepared Cou6@ZIF-8 was dispersed in 1 mL of deionized water at room temperature. After standing for 20 min, the two solutions were mixed and stirred at room temperature for 24 h. The mixture was then centrifuged (8,000×g, 15 min) and washed three times. The final precipitate was freeze-dried to obtain HA/Cou6@ZIF-8.
Cellular Uptake Experiment: 1.0 × 106 cells/well HSC-T6 (CD44+), LX-2 (CD44+) and AML-12 (CD44−) were seeded in six-well plates and incubated for 24 h. After incubation, 10 μM Cou6-labeled solution was added to the cells and incubated for 6 h in the dark. Cell climbing slices were fixed using 4% paraformaldehyde, counterstained with DAPI, and visualized under a fluorescence microscope (Olympus, Tokyo, Japan) for imaging and analysis. To saturate the HA receptors on the cell surface, HA was added to pre-plated cell cultures at a final concentration of 1 mM and incubated for 2 h. The cells were then washed with phosphate buffer, and the old medium was replaced with the Cou6-labeled formulation. After 2 h of incubation, the cells were washed three times with phosphate buffer and fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 15 min. Following DAPI staining, the cells were visualized under a fluorescence microscope (Olympus, Tokyo, Japan). Quantitative analysis was performed using flow cytometry (Beckman Coulter, USA).
Cellular uptake mechanism: To investigate the cellular uptake mechanism, LX-2 cells were pre-incubated with endocytosis inhibitors including chlorpromazine (CPZ, 10 μM), amiloride (EIPA, 50 μM), and methyl-β-cyclodextrin (M-β-CD, 5 mM) for 30 min, followed by incubation with Cou6-labeled HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 (1.5 μM Cou6) for 2 h. The cells were visualized under a Confocal laser scanning microscopy (Nikon, Tokyo, Japan) (CLSM). Subsequently, the intracellular uptake efficiency of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 was quantitatively analyzed by flow cytometry.
Co-localization Assay: To investigate the intracellular trafficking of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8, a co-localization assay was performed. LX-2 cells were incubated with HA/Cou6@ZIF-8 (1.5 μM Cou6) for 2, 4, 12 h, followed by staining with LysoTracker Red (50 nM, 60 min) for lysosomal localization. After fixation with 4% paraformaldehyde, the cells were mounted with DAPI (1 μg/ml) containing medium for nuclear counter staining. fluorescence microscope was used to visualize the subcellular distribution of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8. The index of colocalization and linescanning profiles of fluorescent intensity of the LX-2 cells were calculated by Image J software.

2.14.

## Animals
Male C57BL/6J mice (20 ± 2 g) [Certification No: SCXK (Xin) 2020-0005] were obtained from the Animal Experiment Center of Xinjiang Medical University and housed under standard conditions (12-h light-dark cycle, 70% humidity, 25 °C). All animal experiments were conducted in strict compliance with the Animal Care and Use Regulations of Shihezi University (Approval No. A2025–711). After a one-week acclimatization period, the mice were randomly divided into two groups: 1) the control (CON) group (n = 6), fed a regular chow diet, and 2) the liver fibrosis group (n = 32), which received intraperitoneal injections of 10% CCl4 (10 mL/kg in olive oil solution) twice weekly [ ]. After 4 weeks, the liver fibrosis group was further randomized into four subgroups (n = 8), in addition to the normal group: 1) the Lupeol group (40 mg/kg); 2) the Lupeol@ZIF-8 group (40 mg/kg Lupeol); and 3) the HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 group (40 mg/kg Lupeol). The treatments were administered via tail vein injection. Throughout the experiment, mouse body weight and food intake were recorded regularly. Before the end of the experiment, all mice were fasted for 12 h. Following electrophoresis, proteins were transferred to PVDF membranes using a wet transfer system. The next day, the membrane was washed once in TBST and subsequently transferred to a sheep anti-rabbit/mouse secondary antibody solution diluted in specific proportions for 1 h at room temperature. ## Results and discussion
3.1.

## Synthesis of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8
The synthesis and decomposition mechanisms of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 are depicted in . HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 nanoparticles were synthesized using a room temperature solution reaction method. ZIF-8 is a typical metal-organic framework material, whose three-dimensional structure is formed through the coordination-driven self-assembly of Zn2+ ions with nitrogen atoms on 2-Mim ligands. The hydrophobic cavities of this structure exhibit high affinity for the hydrophobic backbone of the negatively charged lupeol, enabling efficient encapsulation of the drug within the positively charged ZIF-8 framework through multiple interactions such as electrostatic adsorption, hydrogen bonding, and van der Waals forces. Subsequently, the carboxyl groups on HA molecules coordinate with the exposed Zn2+ ions on the ZIF-8 surface, allowing stable modification of HA onto the nanoparticles. Ultimately, this process results in the construction of multifunctional HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 nanoparticles, which combine targeted delivery with pH-responsive drug release capabilities ( A). ZIF-8 maintains its structural integrity under physiological conditions, Under acidic conditions (pH < 7), protonation of 2-Mim disrupts the coordination between zinc ions and imidazole rings, leading to the gradual degradation of ZIF-8 and subsequent drug release ( B). The released Zn2+ enhances the cell's ability to scavenge free radicals, thereby reducing hepatocyte damage. After HA completes its targeted delivery, it can be degraded by endogenous hyaluronidase; meanwhile, both zinc ions (an essential trace element) and the pharmacologically active 2-Mim (which is primarily metabolized by the hepatic CYP450 enzyme system via hydroxylation and subsequent glucuronidation before being excreted by the kidneys) can be normally metabolized in the body. This favorable metabolic profile fundamentally prevents the risk of drug accumulation. It is noteworthy that both lupeol and Zn2+ possess antioxidant activity, and they can exert a synergistic effect, thus enhancing the anti-fibrotic efficacy. Furthermore, the presence of HA enables HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 to actively target aHSCs in the fibrotic liver by specifically binding to the over-expressed CD44 receptors on their surface, thereby enhancing cellular uptake. In contrast, Lupeol@ZIF-8, which lacks HA modification, fails to achieve such targeted delivery to aHSCs ( C). This system encapsulates hydrophobic lupeol within ZIF-8 and utilizes the targeting ability of HA to deliver the drug to the surface of aHSCs, achieving selective release under acidic conditions and ultimately demonstrating significant anti-fibrotic effects in a liver fibrosis mouse model.
 
3.2.

## Characterization of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8
In this study, HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 was synthesized using Room temperature solution reaction method [ ]. To maximize the pharmacological efficacy of the nanoparticles, the optimal preparation process was determined using a single-factor experimental method ( ). The finalized synthesis was carried out at 25 °C with a Zn2+/2-Mim molar ratio of 1:8 under stirring for 24 h. The drug loading capacity (DL) and encapsulation efficiency (EE) of Lupeol@ZIF-8 were quantified using UV-Visible spectrophotometry. Based on a standard curve of lupeol in methanol ( ), the system achieved a DL of 23.8% and an EE of 97.4% ( J, ). This exceptional drug-loading performance surpasses that of traditional drug delivery systems, highlighting ZIF-8 as a highly promising nanocarrier for co-delivery applications. TEM images revealed that all samples exhibited uniform polyhedral morphologies, while HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 exhibited a more spherical morphology after HA modification ( A, B, C). DLS measurements further confirmed the particle size and distribution: the average diameters of ZIF-8, Lupeol@ZIF-8 and HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 were measured as 72.81 ± 0.43 nm, 80.29 ± 1.03 nm, and 125.7 ± 0.16 nm, respectively, all displaying a narrow size distribution (PDI <0.4). The sizes of all are below 200 nm, which meets the size requirement for passing through the liver sinusoidal endothelial cell pores, allowing them to reach the HSCs. which is consistent with TEM observations ( D, E, F). A comparative visualization of the average particle sizes is provided in . The zeta potential analysis revealed surface charges of 15.8 ± 0.57 mV for ZIF-8, -7.06 ± 0.02 mV for Lupeol@ZIF-8, and -13.2 ± 0.64 mV for HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 ( G). The marked decrease in zeta potential following HA modification is attributed to the presence of negatively charged hydroxyl groups from lupeol and carboxylic acid groups from HA. UV-visible Spectrophotometer ( M) revealed a blue shift in the absorption spectrum of Lupeol@ZIF-8, confirming the successful encapsulation of lupeol. XRD patterns ( I) demonstrated that both Lupeol@ZIF-8 and HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 retained the characteristic diffraction peaks of ZIF-8. The standard ZIF-8 diffraction peaks were observed at 2θ = 12.86 and 18.22, while the synthesized ZIF-8 exhibited corresponding peaks at 2θ = 12.88 and 18.98. The slight rightward shift in the diffraction angles, attributable to differences in synthesis conditions, was not accompanied by changes in peak shape, intensity, or overall crystallinity, indicating that the crystalline structure of ZIF-8 remained unaltered after the loading of lupeol and HA. Furthermore, the successful incorporation of both lupeol and HA onto the ZIF-8 nanoparticles was qualitatively confirmed using FT-IR spectroscopy. As shown in H, the synthesized ZIF-8 nanoparticles exhibited characteristic FT-IR absorption peaks similar to those of pristine ZIF-8, including the stretching vibration of -NH at 3461.46 cm−1 and the Zn-N coordination bond at 420 cm−1. For Lupeol@ZIF-8, the appearance of a new peak at 2936.06 cm−1, corresponding to the -CH2OH group of lupeol, confirmed successful drug loading, verifying successful lupeol loading [ ]. Furthermore, the HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 nanoparticles displayed distinctive absorption peaks at 1032.59 cm−1 (C–O–C ether bond) and 1370.80 cm−1 (COO−). These characterization results collectively demonstrate: the successful synthesis of ZIF-8 carriers, the effective encapsulation of lupeol within the ZIF-8 framework, and the successful surface modification of Lupeol@ZIF-8 nanoparticles with HA. N2 adsorption-desorption isotherms studies reveal the specific Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) surface area (SBET) and pore size [ ]. The nitrogen adsorption-desorption isotherms ( K) indicated that both ZIF-8 and the composite material HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 loaded with HA and lupeol exhibited typical Type I isotherms, suggesting that the microporous structure of ZIF-8 remained intact after loading. Further pore size distribution analysis ( L) revealed that the primary pore size of both materials was concentrated around 2 nm, confirming the successful encapsulation of HA and lupeol within the pores of ZIF-8 without significant disruption of its crystal structure. The specific surface area and pore structure data showed that after loading, the specific surface area of the composite increased significantly from 350.34 m2/g to 1,531.28 m2/g, while the average pore size decreased from 2.13 nm to 1.68 nm. This change aligns with the structural modulation of ZIF-8 during the encapsulation process, indicating that the loading not only preserved the microporous nature of ZIF-8 but also further optimized its pore structure, potentially enhancing its performance in applications such as adsorption or sustained release. To evaluate the pH-responsive drug release profile of Lupeol@ZIF-8, in vitro release studies were carried out in Phosphate Buffered Saline (PBS) at pH 5.8, 6.5 and 7.4, simulating the acidic microenvironment of fibrotic tissues and the neutral physiological conditions, respectively. A standard curve of lupeol in PBS containing 10% Tween 80 was established following previously reported methods ( ). The results demonstrated that HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 exhibits a pronounced pH-dependent sustained release profile, with the cumulative release of lupeol at pH 5.8 being approximately four times greater than that under neutral conditions (pH 7.4). This indicates that the HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 system can effectively accelerate drug release in acidic microenvironments, thereby enhancing the targeted therapeutic efficacy against HF ( O), This phenomenon is attributed to the cleavage of coordination bonds between Zn2+ and 2-Mim under acidic conditions, which leads to the dissociation of the ZIF-8 structure and the subsequent release of lupeol and zinc ions. Based on this, the zinc ion release behavior of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 was investigated in this study. The cumulative release of zinc ions was continuously monitored for 48 h in PBS buffer at pH 7.4 and pH 5.8. At pH 7.4, the zinc ion release profile remained relatively flat, with a cumulative release of only (0.22 ± 0.10) μg/L after 48 h. In contrast, under pH 5.8 conditions, zinc ion release was significantly enhanced, reaching a cumulative amount of (0.81 ± 0.09) μg/mL, approximately 3.9 times higher than that at pH 7.4. This difference is primarily attributed to the pH-sensitive nature of the HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 nanocomposite, which undergoes structural dissociation in acidic environments, thereby promoting the release of zinc ions. In conclusion, this pH-responsive HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 nanocomposite significantly enhances its anti-hepatic fibrosis efficacy through the synergistic release mechanism of lupeol and zinc ions ( P). To further evaluate the material's stability under simulated in vivo conditions, DLS was used to monitor the particle size changes of Lupeol@ZIF-8 in PBS over 50 h. As shown in N, the particle size of Lupeol@ZIF-8 remained stable in PBS containing 10% Tween 80, with no significant alterations observed throughout the duration. The PDI remained consistently below 0.4, further confirming the high colloidal stability of the formulation. Schematic illustrations of the preparation process and the corresponding molecular model are presented in Q and R, respectively.
 
3.3.

## Biocompatibility study of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8
Under physiological conditions, HSCs remain in a quiescent state. However, stimulation by TGF-β1 induces HSC activation, leading to increased proliferation, upregulation of α-SMA, and excessive production of collagen I. These changes contribute to pathological ECM accumulation and promote the progression of HF. Furthermore, chronic liver injury exacerbates hepatocyte necrosis, which in turn intensifies the fibrogenic process. The HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 nanoparticle selectively targets aHSCs, effectively suppressing their activation and thereby interrupting the progression of HF ( A). To assess the anti-fibrotic efficacy of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 in vitro, we examined its cytotoxic effects on both quiescent and aHSCs (HSC-T6 and LX-2) using the CCK-8 assay. The results indicated that Lupeol exhibited negligible cytotoxicity toward quiescent HSC-T6 and LX-2 cells, even at concentrations as high as 140 μM, demonstrating favorable biocompatibility. In contrast, the same Lupeol concentrations significantly reduced the viability of activated HSC-T6 and LX-2 cells in a concentration-dependent manner, with cell viability dropping below 80% at 100 μM ( B and C). Based on comprehensive safety evaluations, a concentration of 60 μM was selected as optimal for subsequent experiments. Furthermore, treatment with 80 μM ZIF-8 alone maintained cell viability above 90% in aHSCs, confirming the low cytotoxicity of the carrier material. Compared to free Lupeol, treatment with an equivalent dose of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 resulted in a significantly stronger reduction in the viability of aHSCs ( D and E). This enhanced cytotoxic effect is likely attributable to improved cellular uptake mediated by CD44 receptor-targeted endocytosis of the HA-functionalized nanoparticles. These results indicate that HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 holds considerable promise for the treatment of HF, owing to its selective toxicity toward aHSCs. To further validate the toxicity of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 to HSCs, live/dead cell staining was performed using the Calcein-AM/PI kit. As shown in F and G, cells treated with Lupeol@ZIF-8 and HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 exhibited significantly enhanced red fluorescence compared to those treated with free Lupeol, indicating severe cell death. Quantitative analysis ( H and I) aligned with the cytotoxicity assay results, further confirming that HA modification enhanced cellular drug uptake. Additionally, hemolysis assays demonstrated that although the hemolysis rate of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 increased with concentration, it remained below 4% at all tested doses ( J), confirming the excellent hemocompatibility and in vitro safety profile of the material.

## Cellular uptake of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 in vitro
The experimental procedure for evaluating cellular uptake is illustrated in G. In the liver, the specific expression of CD44 receptors on hematopoietic stem cells ensures the targeted therapeutic efficacy of drug-loaded nanoparticles while minimizing their toxic side effects on other hepatic cells [ ]. Therefore, quantifying CD44 expression levels across different liver cells is essential for validating the targeting strategy and optimizing nanoparticle design. To explore whether HA modification enhances the targeted uptake of nanoparticles by aHSCs, we assessed the cellular internalization efficiency in HSC-T6 (CD44+), LX-2 (CD44+), and AML-12 cells (CD44−) using Cou6@ZIF-8 and HA/Cou6@ZIF-8 (1 mg/mL Cou6). Fluorescence microscopy observations demonstrated that the fluorescence intensity in HSC-T6 and LX-2 cells treated with HA-modified nanoparticles was significantly stronger than that in both the free Cou6 group and the non-targeted Cou6@ZIF-8 group, indicating enhanced cellular uptake attributable to the specific targeting ability of HA toward CD44 over-expressing HSCs ( A, C). Furthermore, pre-incubation of HSC-T6 and LX-2 cells with free HA solution markedly reduced the cellular uptake of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8, suggesting competition between soluble HA and the nanoparticles for binding to CD44 receptors. In contrast, no significant difference in uptake was observed between the control and HA-modified nanoparticle groups in CD44-negative AML-12 cells, further confirming the CD44-dependent targeting mechanism of the HA-functionalized system ( E). It is likely due to the presence of Kupffer cells in the normal liver that other non-parenchymal cells only express minimal amounts of CD44 [ ]. These results demonstrate that the cellular uptake of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 is primarily mediated by the over-expression of CD44 receptors on aHSCs, enabling targeted drug delivery while minimizing distribution in normal hepatocytes. Consistent with the imaging observations, flow cytometry analysis ( B, D, F) confirmed the CD44 receptor-mediated selective uptake of HA-modified ZIF-8 nanoparticles in HSCs and their reduced internalization in normal liver cells. The quantitative data further corroborate the earlier qualitative findings, reinforcing the efficiency and specificity of the active targeting mechanism.
 To investigate the cellular uptake mechanisms of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8, LX-2 cells were pre-treated with a panel of endocytic inhibitors: methyl-β-cyclodextrin (M-β-CD, inhibiting caveolae-mediated endocytosis), ethylisopropylamiloride (EIPA, inhibiting macropinocytosis), and chlorpromazine (CPZ, inhibiting clathrin-mediated endocytosis). The results revealed that both M-β-CD treatment and low-temperature incubation (4 °C, which suppresses energy-dependent cellular processes) significantly reduced the cellular uptake efficiency of the nanoparticles ( A and B). In contrast, neither EIPA nor CPZ treatment resulted in significant inhibition of nanoparticle internalization. These findings clearly indicate that the cellular uptake of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 occurs primarily through caveolae-mediated endocytosis and is dependent on energy-active transport mechanisms. It is worth noting that, as a cholesterol-depleting agent, M-β-CD not only inhibits caveolin-dependent endocytosis but may also non-specifically interfere with other membrane-related functions by altering membrane cholesterol content. Therefore, future studies could employ more specific inhibitors or caveolin-1-based gene knockdown techniques to further verify and clarify the cellular endocytic mechanism of this nanoparticle. Further co-localization studies using Lyso-Tracker Red, a lysosome-specific fluorescent probe, demonstrated that HA/Cou6@ZIF-8 nanoparticles were predominantly localized within lysosomes during the initial 2-4 h post-incubation. Significant overlap between the green fluorescence of Cou6 and the red fluorescence of Lyso-Tracker was observed in LX-2 cells after 4 h, consistent with clathrin-mediated endocytosis. However, when cells were incubated in fresh medium for 12 h, the green fluorescence dispersed throughout the cytoplasm, and lysosomal staining became markedly diminished. This observation indicates that the ZIF-8-based carrier effectively facilitated lysosomal escape, enabling the subsequent release of its cargo into the cytosol ( D). To semi-quantitatively evaluate the endosomal escape capability of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8, the colocalization index and fluorescence intensity line-scanning profiles of LX-2 cells were analyzed using Image J software, as presented in C, E. The lysosomal escape of HA/Cou6@ZIF-8 is mediated primarily by its pH-responsive degradation and the proton sponge effect induced by 2-Mim, which promotes lysosomal rupture and content release. This property establishes HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 as an ideal delivery carrier for hydrophobic drugs such as lupeol, enabling evasion of lysosomal degradation and significantly enhancing intracellular drug bioavailability.

## Distribution of NR-loaded ZIF-8 in normal and fibrotic mice
To validate the targeting capability of HA modification toward fibrotic liver, we investigated the biodistribution of NR-labeled Zeolitic imidazolate framework materials using in vivo imaging technology. A illustrates the experimental design of the mouse biodistribution study. Representative in vivo imaging results for normal and fibrotic mice are shown in B and C, respectively. In healthy mice, no significant differences in fluorescence signals were observed among the NR group, NR@ZIF-8 group and HA/NR@ZIF-8 group ( D). This can be attributed to the lack of effective endocytosis and selective uptake capabilities in qHSCs in normal liver tissue, leading to minimal accumulation of HA/NR@ZIF-8. In contrast, in fibrotic mice, while the fluorescence signal of the free NR group remained low, the NR@ZIF-8 group showed significantly higher fluorescence intensity compared to healthy mice, suggesting that ZIF-8 facilitates selective drug release within the acidic microenvironment of the fibrotic liver. Moreover, The HA/NR@ZIF-8 group exhibited even stronger fluorescence signals in the livers of fibrotic mice than the NR@ZIF-8 group ( E). This enhanced targeting effect is likely attributable to the activation and proliferation of HSCs in the fibrotic liver, which upregulates the expression of HA binding sites, thereby facilitating receptor-mediated endocytosis primarily through CD44. These results confirm that HA-modified ZIF-8 enables rapid and specific targeting of fibrotic liver tissue. At 24 h post tail vein injection, the fluorescence signals of all groups (NR, NR@ZIF-8, and HA/NR@ZIF-8) decreased gradually and accumulated in the bladder in both healthy and fibrotic mice, indicating effective metabolic clearance of ZIF-8. Based on in vivo imaging, which showed that the fluorescence signal peaked at 6 h after administration, this time point was selected for subsequent ex vivo organ imaging. The fluorescence distribution in excised organs further validated the selective accumulation of HA-modified nanoparticles in fibrotic liver, with signal intensity consistent with in vivo observations ( F, G, H, I). In summary, these findings demonstrate that the liver-targeting capability of HA@ZIF-8 significantly enhances the uptake efficiency of lupeol by aHSCs.
 To evaluate the pharmacokinetic properties of HA-modified nanoparticles, we systematically monitored blood concentration profiles of Lupeol following tail vein administration of nanoparticle formulations (Lupeol@ZIF-8 or HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8) in SD rats. Pharmacokinetic analysis ( and ) revealed that free lupeol was rapidly cleared from circulation, whereas encapsulation within ZIF-8 nanoparticles significantly prolonged its systemic circulation. Specifically, HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 exhibited a 2.70-fold increase in the area under the concentration-time curve (AUC) compared to free lupeol, This indicates that HA-mediated targeting protects the drug from degradation and non-specific clearance prior to reaching the target site. The apparent volume of distribution of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 was significantly reduced by 2.26 compared to free Lupeol, indicating a fundamental alteration in its in vivo distribution behavior. The drug predominantly accumulated in the liver tissue with reduced systemic distribution, thereby lowering the risk of off-target toxicity. In addition, the mean residence time (MRT) and plasma half-life (t1/2) of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 were approximately 4.36 times and 2.66 times longer, respectively, than those of the free drug, It is demonstrated that the markedly prolonged liver retention time of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 contributes to a controlled drug release pattern, an elongation of the therapeutic duration, and a consequent enhancement in therapeutic effectiveness. These results presented above reveal that the pharmacokinetics of Lupeol are markedly enhanced by HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8, owing to the system's efficient hepatic targeting and prolonged release properties. The improved pharmacokinetic profile can be attributed to the protective coating provided by HA modification, which enhances systemic circulation and delays clearance. which functions through two key mechanisms:
(1)Substantially reducing nanoparticle clearance by the reticuloendothelial system (RES).
(2)Prolonging blood circulation time to facilitate targeted delivery to aHSCs.
The superior pharmacokinetic profile, combined with active targeting capability, establishes HA-modified nanoparticles as an ideal drug delivery system for liver fibrosis therapy.

## An in vitro study of the therapeutic effects of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8
 A illustrates the schematic design of the in vitro experiments. Therefore, we further evaluated the effects of lupeol-loaded nanoparticles on TGF-β1 induced migration and contraction of HSCs. As shown in E, in the scratch assay, untreated control cells exhibited a marked healing trend after 24 h of culture, whereas the scratch width in TGF-β1 treated cells narrowed significantly, confirming that TGF-β1 stimulation promotes cell proliferation and migration. In contrast, aHSCs treated with lupeol showed no significant scratch closure compared to the control, indicating that lupeol effectively inhibits TGF-β1 induced migration. Notably, the HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 treatment group displayed the largest remaining scratch gap, demonstrating that HA modification leads to more effective suppression of cell migration. Furthermore, no significant inhibitory effect on cell migration was observed in normal hepatocytes, confirming the selective action of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 on aHSCs ( F). In summary, the scratch assay results demonstrate that HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 can significantly inhibit the activation and proliferation of HSCs and reduce ECM secretion ( G and H). It has been reported that aHSCs exhibit enhanced aerobic glycolysis during fibrogenesis, a metabolic pathway that provides the necessary energy and substrate support for their high biosynthetic and secretory functions [ ]. This study reveals that HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 can inhibit the glycolytic process in aHSCs ( L). Specifically, zinc ions released from ZIF-8 effectively suppress the activity of LDH, a key glycolytic enzyme. Experimental results show that LDH activity was significantly elevated in TGF-β1-induced aHSCs, whereas the ZIF-8 and HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 treatment groups exhibited only 74.98% and 75.70% of the induced group's level, respectively ( I). Additionally, lactate accumulation resulting from glycolytic activation is a critical factor promoting liver fibrosis progression. From the Lupeol, ZIF-8, to HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 treatment groups, intracellular lactate levels showed a progressively decreasing trend ( J), which was consistent with the ATP level measurements ( K). These findings further demonstrate that ZIF-8 can effectively suppress the functional activity of aHSCs by modulating energy metabolism pathways.
 In addition, we evaluated the expression levels of proteins related to inflammation and apoptosis, including IL-6, IL-10, Caspase3, Bax and Bcl-2. The changes among them are illustrated in L. To evaluate the effect of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 on apoptosis in HSCs, nuclear morphological changes were examined using DAPI staining. The results revealed that treatment of aHSCs with HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 induced characteristic apoptotic features, including nuclear condensation, enhanced fluorescence intensity, and the formation of granular chromatin fragments or crescent-shaped marginal aggregates ( A). During liver injury, ROS act as intracellular signaling mediators in the fibrotic process, activating HSCs to synthesize large amounts of collagen and abundant ECM [ ]. Therefore, reducing oxidative stress to inhibit the activation of HSCs represents a potential therapeutic strategy for HF. Using the DCFH-DA fluorescent probe, we detected intracellular ROS levels in LX-2 cells. The results demonstrated that TGF-β1 stimulation significantly increased ROS production compared to quiescent LX-2 cells, confirming elevated oxidative stress in aHSCs. Treatment with both free lupeol and Lupeol@ZIF-8 significantly reduced ROS levels relative to untreated aHSCs, although no significant difference was observed between these two groups. Notably, HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 treatment resulted in the most substantial reduction in ROS levels, highlighting the enhanced efficacy of the HA-modified formulation ( B). This is consistent with previous reports that lupeol can reduce oxidative stress during acute liver injury [ ]. Semi-quantitative experimental results further validated these findings ( C, D, E). In summary, HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 exerts anti-fibrotic effects by inducing apoptosis in HSCs and alleviating oxidative stress. Compared to the control group, the model group showed significantly increased expression of the pro-inflammatory cytokine IL-6 and the anti-apoptotic protein BCL-2 ( F, G, H), along with decreased expression of the anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10 and the pro-apoptotic proteins Caspase3 and Bax ( I, J, K). Treatment with HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 significantly inhibited the expression of the anti-apoptotic protein BCL-2 (p < 0.0001) and reversed the activation of the pro-apoptotic proteins Bax (p < 0.001) and Caspase3 (p < 0.01), forming a closed-loop regulatory mechanism that promotes apoptosis in diseased HSCs and suppresses inflammatory responses.
 
3.8.

## Biosafety assessment
The in vivo biosafety of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 was systematically evaluated through pathological analysis and serum biochemical parameter testing in mice. As shown in A–D, the levels of ALT, AST, BUN, and CRE in the serum showed no significant changes after treatment with Lupeol, ZIF-8, or HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8. Further histopathological analysis of major organs (heart, liver, spleen, lung, kidney) revealed no obvious structural abnormalities in the groups treated with Lupeol, ZIF-8, or HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 ( E). Taken together, these results indicate that Lupeol, ZIF-8, and HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 exhibit favorable systemic biosafety under the current experimental conditions.
 
3.9.

## The in vivo anti-fibrotic effects of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8
In vitro experiments demonstrated that HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 effectively suppressed HSC activation. Based on these findings, we subsequently evaluated its therapeutic efficacy in a carbon tetrachloride (CCl4) induced HF mouse model. In this study, the construction of a mouse model of HF and the corresponding therapeutic evaluation process are illustrated in A. HF was induced by injecting 10% CCl4 for eight consecutive weeks, and changes in the physiological functions of the mice were evaluated. During the experiment, due to CCl4 -induced hepatotoxicity, mice in the model group exhibited coarse fur, reduced activity, and decreased appetite compared to the normal group, along with significant body weight loss. These observations collectively reflected a systemic consumptive state induced by HF. In contrast, after therapeutic intervention, the overall health of the mice improved, with fur condition, mental state, and food intake essentially returning to normal levels ( B and C). At the end of the experiment, liver and spleen tissues were collected and weighed to calculate organ-to-body weight ratios to evaluate liver injury. Compared with the normal group, the model group exhibited a significant increase in both the liver index (P < 0.0001) and spleen index (P < 0.001), indicating the presence of hepatic inflammatory damage and successful establishment of the fibrosis model. Relative to the model group, all treatment groups showed a reduction in liver and spleen indices, suggesting amelioration of liver pathology. Notably, the HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 group demonstrated the most pronounced decrease in these indices, underscoring its superior efficacy in alleviating HF ( D and E). This provided strong initial evidence for the effectiveness of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 in alleviating fibrosis.
 In addition, CCl4 caused damage to healthy liver tissues, leading to elevated serum levels of ALT, AST, AKP and HYP (P < 0.0001) ( F, G, H). However, serum levels of ALT, AST, AKP and HYP in fibrotic mice showed a progressive decline following treatment with various formulations. Notably, HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 exhibited superior efficacy in mitigating CCl4 induced HF (p < 0.0001). The elevated HYP content further confirmed enhanced collagen deposition in CCl4 treated mice, underscoring the extent of fibrotic progression ( I). These results further demonstrate that HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 effectively attenuates liver injury and reduces collagen deposition, thereby significantly inhibiting the progression of HF.
This study systematically evaluated the therapeutic effects of different formulations on HF by regulating the FXR-SHP signaling pathway. The FXR-SHP pathway serves as a central signaling axis in bile acid metabolism and the regulation of HF. Its dysfunction is closely associated with HSCs activation, disordered lipid metabolism and fibrosis progression. Building upon previous research on the regulation of bile acid metabolism for the treatment of MASLD, this study further focuses on the regulatory mechanism of this pathway in the development and progression of HF, aiming to provide new experimental evidence for the treatment of HF. Compared with the blank control group (G1), the model group (G2) showed significantly increased expression levels of LXR (P < 0.001) and SREBP-1c (P < 0.05), while the expression of FXR (P < 0.05), BSEP, and SHP was markedly suppressed, with particularly pronounced down-regulation of SHP (P < 0.0001). This expression pattern indicates that the FXR-SHP signaling pathway is significantly inhibited during HF, leading to aberrant activation of downstream lipid synthesis genes (SREBP-1c, LXR) and the rate-limiting enzyme for bile acid synthesis CYP7A1, thereby exacerbating hepatic lipid accumulation, inflammatory responses, and fibrotic progression. After interventional treatment, the lupeol (G3) partially reversed these abnormal protein expressions. The Lupeol@ZIF-8 (G4), benefiting from the enhanced bioavailability provided by the ZIF-8 carrier, demonstrated superior improvement across all indicators compared to G3. Notably, the HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 group (G5) exhibited the most significant therapeutic effect: the expression of FXR (P < 0.001), SHP (P < 0.0001) and BSEP (P < 0.001) was restored to near-normal levels, showing highly significant differences compared to the model group. Meanwhile, the expression of CYP7A1, SREBP-1c and LXR was also significantly suppressed (P < 0.0001). These results indicate that HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 potently activates the FXR-SHP pathway, effectively ameliorating cholestasis and disordered lipid metabolism through SHP-mediated inhibition of CYP7A1 and BSEP-promoted bile acid excretion, thereby inhibiting HSCs activation and alleviating the progression of HF ( J, K and ).
In conclusion, the Lupeol formulation loaded onto ZIF-8 nanoparticles and modified with HA significantly enhances liver-targeted drug delivery efficiency and therapeutic efficacy. By efficiently modulating the FXR-SHP signaling pathway and reversing the aberrant expression of key molecules in HF, this strategy represents the most effective treatment approach identified in this study.

3.10.

## HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 ameliorates CCl4-induced hepatic fibrosis in mice
CCl4, a compound with significant hepatotoxicity, is activated by metabolic processes in the liver after entering the organ, leading to the formation of trichloromethyl radicals (CHCl3). These highly reactive free radicals disrupt the plasma membrane structure of hepatocytes and induce lipid peroxidation [ , ], which compromises the integrity of the hepatocyte membrane and ultimately triggers severe HF ( L).
 To determine the drug-induced toxic effects on vital organs, we performed histopathological assessments and quantified serum biomarkers of organ function ( B). From the results of morphological analysis ( A), the livers of control mice displayed a normal morphology, featuring a reddish hue, soft texture and smooth surface. In contrast, livers from the model group showed pronounced pathological alterations, such as increased stiffness, granular nodules on the surface, a dark grey discoloration and extensive fibrotic changes, which clearly distinguished them from the control group. Notably, the HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 treatment group exhibited the most substantial improvement in liver tissue repair, with smoother surfaces and macroscopic features closely resembling normal tissue among all treatment groups. Furthermore, HE staining results confirmed the recovery from liver injury. In control mice, hepatic lobules were well-structured, organized radially around the central vein, with hepatocyte cords arranged in an orderly manner. The model group, however, showed disrupted hepatic cord structures and localized inflammatory cell infiltration. Compared to the model group, all treatment groups (Lupeol, Lupeol@ZIF-8, and HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8) displayed reduced inflammatory cell infiltration and decreased necrosis of hepatocytes, with the HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 group showing the most notable histological improvement ( C).
Subsequently, collagen deposition in the liver was further assessed using Masson's trichrome and Sirius red staining. Minimal collagen fiber content was observed in healthy liver tissues. In contrast, the model group exhibited a substantial increase in collagen accumulation. Importantly, treatment with HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 significantly suppressed the formation of perivascular collagen fibers ( D, E, F). The results from Masson's trichrome and Sirius red staining were consistent with HYP content measurements, confirming that HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 treatment significantly reduced collagen deposition in fibrotic livers. α-SMA, a key marker of HSC activation, is closely associated with the severity of HF. CD44, a multifunctional cell surface glycoprotein, participates in numerous biological processes including cell proliferation, migration, inflammation and fibrosis. Immunohistochemical analysis revealed minimal collagen deposition in healthy liver tissue, whereas the model group exhibited widespread expression of α-SMA and CD44 in perivascular and septal areas. In contrast, CCl4 induced mice treated with HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 showed markedly reduced expression of both α-SMA and CD44, strongly suggesting that targeted inhibition of HSC activation can effectively halt the progression of HF ( G, H, I).
Finally, we examined the expression of fibrosis-associated proteins in mouse liver tissues by Western blot. The results indicated low expression levels of α-SMA and Collagen I in the control group, while a significant increase was observed in the model group. Notably, the HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 group showed the most substantial reduction in the expression of α-SMA and Collagen I, further confirming the superior efficacy of this formulation in suppressing fibrogenesis ( J and K).

4.

## Conclusions
In this study, we successfully synthesized HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 nanoparticles using a room-temperature solution-phase method. The porous structure of the ZIF-8 carrier effectively addresses three key challenges of lupeol: poor water solubility, Insufficient drug loading, low bioavailability and lack of targeting capability. It efficiently encapsulates the hydrophobic lupeol within its pores, significantly improving its solubility; during systemic circulation, the ZIF-8 framework protects the drug and enables precise delivery. Moreover, its responsive degradation property not only allows controlled drug release but also enables the synergistic release of Zn2+, which work together with lupeol to exert enhanced anti-fibrotic effects. while surface modification with HA conferred active targeting capability. The resulting nanoparticles exhibited a small uniform size (125.7 ± 0.16 nm), high drug encapsulation efficiency, excellent colloidal stability, pH-responsive drug release, and strong affinity for CD44 receptors. The HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 nanocomposite demonstrated favorable sustained-release characteristics in vitro and prolonged systemic circulation in vivo. HA modification successfully conferred the nanoparticles with the ability to target CD44 receptors on the surface of aHSCs, significantly enhancing cellular uptake efficiency. Further validation through HA pre-saturation experiments confirmed that this facilitative effect indeed originates from the specific binding between HA and CD44 receptors: when the receptors were pre-blocked with HA, nanoparticle uptake was markedly reduced, indicating that HA plays a critical role in mediating targeted uptake rather than non-specific interactions. This finding confirms the effectiveness of the HA modification strategy in enhancing the cellular targeting ability of the drug delivery system and provides experimental evidence for the design of receptor-mediated active targeting strategies. Both cellular uptake experiments and in vivo imaging confirmed the exceptional liver-targeting specificity of HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8, with preferential accumulation in CD44 over-expressing fibrotic livers and minimal distribution in normal hepatocytes. The in vivo anti-fibrotic studies demonstrated that HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8 mediates its potent anti-fibrotic activity via the FXR-SHP signaling pathway. Safety assessments indicated a hemolysis rate of less than 4%, supporting its high biocompatibility and safety as an intravenous drug delivery system. These findings establish a solid foundation for potential clinical translation.
In summary, this study successfully developed a dual-functional nanodrug delivery system, HA/Lupeol@ZIF-8, which integrates active targeting (HA) and pH-responsive drug release (ZIF-8). Compared to existing delivery systems such as liposomes [ ], this system demonstrates higher encapsulation and drug loading efficiencies, uniform particle size distribution, and meets the size requirements for hepatic sinusoidal permeability. The surface-modified HA enables active targeted delivery of the drug through specific recognition of CD44 receptors on the surface of aHSCs. Concurrently, the ZIF-8 carrier exhibits significant pH-responsive degradation in the acidic microenvironment simulating fibrosis, facilitating the controlled release of Lupeol. Both in vitro and in vivo experiments confirm that this system effectively improves the solubility and targeting of Lupeol, significantly enhances its accumulation in fibrotic liver regions, and synergistically enhances the inhibition of profibrotic protein expression and oxidative stress regulation, demonstrating excellent therapeutic potential. This study provides a novel strategy for overcoming the delivery challenges of hydrophobic natural drugs and achieving targeted therapy for HF. The value of this research lies in its dual contributions. Practically, it broadens the biomedical use of ZIF-8 and offers a new approach to treating HF. Theoretically, it clarifies the mechanistic link from metabolic regulation to fibrosis inhibition by verifying the FXR-SHP pathway, thereby deepening existing knowledge and providing a key rationale for novel late-stage liver disease therapeutics.
