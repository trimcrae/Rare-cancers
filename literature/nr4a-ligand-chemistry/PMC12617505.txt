## INTRODUCTION
Cardiovascular diseases remain a leading cause of global morbidity and mortality, with myocardial infarction (MI) particularly prevalent in aging population ( , ). Acute MI leads to substantial cardiomyocyte loss and activates resident cardiac fibroblasts (CFs), forming noncontractile scar tissue. This process exacerbates cardiac remodeling and ultimately contributes to heart failure. Furthermore, cardiac aging further impairs the capacity of hearts to repair through structural and functional changes ( ), underscoring the need for innovative therapeutic strategies that address both myocardial damage and age-related deficits.
Direct cardiac reprogramming represents a promising strategy to convert resident fibroblasts into induced cardiomyocytes (iCMs) to replenish the lost cardiomyocytes. Substantial advances have demonstrated that direct reprogramming repairs the injured heart by means of generating new cardiomyocytes, reducing fibrosis, and improving heart functions ( – ). The direct conversion is typically achieved by forced expression of a core set of lineage-specific transcription factors (TFs), such as myocyte enhancer factor 2C (Mef2c), Gata4, and T-box transcription factor 5 (Tbx5) (MGT) in mice, and additional factors in human fibroblasts ( – ). Recent studies have focused on removing transcriptional, epigenetic, and translational barriers to improve cardiac reprogramming efficiency, particularly when starting from neonatal or embryonic fibroblasts ( , ). However, translating these advances to adult and aged fibroblasts remains challenging because of unsolved molecular barriers ( – ). One such barrier is epigenetic modifications. For instance, PHF7 (PHD finger protein 7), a histone code reader, has been shown to activate adult cardiac reprogramming through interaction with the SWI/SNF (switch/sucrose nonfermentable) complex and further recruitment of cardiac TFs ( ). Despite these advances, a comprehensive understanding of the key molecular barriers that inhibit efficient cardiac reprogramming from adult and aged fibroblasts remains elusive.
Cellular senescence has emerged as a critical biological process under various physiological and pathological conditions, including tissue remodeling and aging ( – ). Senescence can be triggered by cellular stress responses in both mitotic and postmitotic cells and is characterized by persistent chromatin remodeling and transcriptional changes ( ). These include the up-regulation of secreted factors such as cytokines, chemokines, matrix proteases, and growth factors, collectively referred to as the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) ( ). Senescent cells can have a profound paracrine effect, contributing to chronic inflammation and age-related diseases, including cardiac pathologies ( , ). In the myocardium, senescent fibroblasts accumulate with aging and after MI, diversely contributing to tissue remodeling and regeneration ( , – ). Despite this, the impact of senescence on cardiac reprogramming has not been well explored.
In this study, we applied molecular and multiomics approaches to investigate iCM reprogramming in fibroblasts across different ages and identified cellular senescence as a critical molecular barrier to iCM fate conversion. We demonstrated that senescence induction in CFs substantially inhibits iCM generation. Through candidate-based screening, we identified orphan nuclear receptor subfamily 4 group A (NR4A) member 3 (Nr4a3, also known as Nor1) as a critical senescence-inducing factor that suppresses iCM reprogramming. Nr4a3 has been extensively studied in cardiometabolic disease where it exacerbates oxidative stress, apoptosis, and inflammation in cardiovascular disorders ( – ). Our mechanistic studies uniquely identified Nr4a3 as a dual-function hub that concurrently controls chromatin accessibility to activate SASP gene expression and suppress cardiac gene expression. In vivo, Nr4a3 knockdown further improves heart function following reprogramming after MI. Collectively, our study identified senescence as a critical reprogramming barrier and suggested that targeting Nr4a3 may alleviate senescence, reset epigenetic barriers, and enhance cardiac repair.

## RESULTS

## Cardiac reprogramming is less efficient in adult and aged fibroblasts compared to neonatal fibroblasts
While recent studies have begun to reveal that aging reduces fibroblast reprogramming into iCMs ( , ), the molecular barriers driving this resistance remain incompletely understood. To systematically address this gap, we isolated wild-type fibroblasts from different ages (neonatal, 1 to 3 days; adult, 2 to 3 months; aged, 18 to 24 months) and subjected them to our established MGT (Mef2c/Gata4/Tbx5) reprogramming protocol ( ) ( , ). Reprogramming efficiency was evaluated by cardiac-specific marker [cardiac muscle troponin T (cTnT) and α-actinin] expression. We found that reprogramming efficiency was significantly higher in neonatal CFs (~15%) compared to adult (<3%) and aged CFs (<2%) ( ). Notably, inclusion of Hand2 (MGTH cocktail) provided minimal improvement ( ), suggesting fundamental age-related barriers beyond TF optimization. These findings were consistent across immunocytochemistry (ICC) and fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) analyses. Reverse transcription quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) analysis demonstrated that adult and aged iCMs exhibited lower expression of cardiac genes (Tnni3 and Actc1) and ion channel genes (Kcna5 and Scn5a) but a higher level of fibrotic genes (Postn and Pdfgra), suggesting a repressive cardiac transcription state compared to neonatal iCMs (neo-iCMs) ( ).
 We also confirmed our findings using tail tip fibroblasts (TTFs) at different ages. Again, we found that both MGT and MGTH successfully induced iCMs in neonatal TTFs but rarely induced cTnT+ iCMs in adult and aged TTFs by flow cytometry and ICC. The results showed that reprogramming efficiency was significantly higher in neonatal TTFs (~12%) compared to adult (<3%) and aged CFs (<1%) ( ). In contrast to neonatal TTFs, adult and aged TTFs exhibited elevated expression of fibrotic genes, including Postn (~10-fold), and Col1a1 (approximately twofold). Correspondingly, iCMs derived from adult and aged TTFs displayed markedly lower cardiac gene expression (Actc1, Actn2, and Tnnt2), with levels ~10- and 100-fold lower, respectively, than those in neonatal TTF–derived iCMs ( ). Together, these results indicate that adult and aged fibroblasts are less reprogrammable compared with neonatal fibroblasts, suggesting that reprogramming barriers associated with advancing age inhibit iCM fate conversion.

## Transcriptomic and epigenetic landscape of iCMs derived from different ages
We next sought to investigate the transcriptomic and epigenetic landscape of MGT-transduced CFs across age groups using bulk RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) and assay for transposase-accessible chromatin using sequencing (ATAC-seq) (fig. S1, A to C). We performed joint analysis of these datasets and identified three distinct expression modules (M1 to M3) with each associated with specific biological processes ( ). The M1 module, which includes genes critical for cardiac muscle development and contraction such as Tnnt2 and Myh6, exhibited reduced expression and chromatin accessibility with age. While MGT transduction robustly activated these genes in neonatal CFs (neo-CFs), its effect was limited in adult and aged CFs ( ), indicating that aging may impair the fibroblast capacity to activate key cardiac genes during reprogramming. The M2 module consists of most down-regulated genes across all MGT-treated groups. Gene Ontology (GO) analysis linked these genes to chromosome segregation and cell cycle regulation, consistent with our previous findings on cell cycle arrest during reprogramming ( ) ( ).
 The M3 module included genes whose expression and chromatin accessibility increased with advancing ages. These genes were minimally responsive to MGT transduction and were involved in pathways including angiogenesis, cytokine production and secretion, and regulation of inflammatory response. Notably, fibrosis-related genes like Postn and Fap and inflammatory genes Cxcl14 and Il1α showed increased expression and more accessible chromatin states ( ). Pearson correlation analysis revealed a negative correlation between M1 and M3 modules (fig. S1, D and E), suggesting that these age-associated fibrotic and inflammatory pathways counteract the cardiac reprogramming process.

## Compromised reprogramming trajectory of aged iCMs at the single-cell level
Building on above observations, we aimed to further delineate the reprogramming trajectory of aged iCMs at a single-cell resolution. We and others have reconstructed a reprogramming route of murine iCMs that starts from induced fibroblasts to an intermediate state (pre-iCMs) and to a fully reprogrammed status ( , ). To examine how this trajectory might differ in aged cells, we performed single-cell RNA-seq (scRNA-seq) on iCMs on day 14, resulting in 3346 neonatal cells ( ) and 4896 aged cells for downstream analysis. Among 11 identified clusters, cluster 3 (C3) and cluster 5 (C5) represented reprogrammed iCM populations based on their high expression of cardiomyocyte-related genes (Tnnt3, Myl4, and Tnni3) and low expression of fibroblast genes (Postn, Tcf21, and Col1a2) ( and fig. S2, A to E). RNA velocity and CytoTRACE analyses that estimate cell potency and differentiation state ( , ) indicated that C5 cells were less reprogrammed pre-iCMs, while C3 cells were better reprogrammed iCMs ( ). Consistent with our previous findings, the aged group showed a significantly lower percentage of iCMs in C3 and C5 than the neonatal group.
We then investigated how iCMs from different age groups distribute along the pseudotemporal trajectory. Pseudotime analysis revealed a progressive reprogramming trajectory (S1 to S4), with aged pre-iCMs at early stages (S1) and fully reprogrammed neo-iCMs at later stages (S4) ( ). Along this trajectory, we identified the elevated expression of cardiac genes (Actc1 and Tnnt2) and involving pathways in sarcomere organization and cardiac muscle contraction. In contrast, aging-related pathways [inflammation response and extracellular matrix (ECM) organization] and involving genes (Mmp3 and Col3a1) were down-regulated ( ). Comparing pre-iCMs and fully reprogrammed iCMs across age groups revealed that iCMs derived from aged CFs display immature features, including reduced expression of genes involved in muscle contraction and myofibril assembly and increased expression of genes associated with ECM organization and collagen fibril assembly ( ).
We applied the SCENIC algorithm ( ) to further dissect the cis-regulatory elements (CREs) and the binding TFs in each state ( ). Within the key TFs relating to the cardiac gene regulatory network in neo-iCMs, we identified the enrichment of Tbx20 that has been reported to facilitate human iCM (hiCM) reprogramming and improve the contractility of iCMs ( ). Meox1 (mesenchyme homeobox 1), a master switch for cardiac fibrosis and suppressor of iCM conversion ( ), was enriched in aged iCMs controlling the expression of reported fibrotic genes including Jun, Mmp2, and Hsd11b1 ( ). These results revealed a delayed and less efficient reprogramming trajectory in aged iCMs, marked by reduced cardiac gene expression and an enhancement of age-associated fibrotic features.

## Induction of cellular senescence in aging CFs
On the basis of above observations, we next sought to investigate whether cellular senescence contributes to these age-associated changes. We performed RNA-seq to further analyze the global transcriptomic changes in starting CFs from different age groups. Using Mfuzz time clustering ( ), we identified several biological modules, with significant enrichment of genes and processes related to cellular senescence in aging CFs ( ). Specifically, we found that in aged CFs, pathways related to cytokine-mediated signaling, DNA repair regulation, inflammation, response to oxidative stress, and lysosome organization were up-regulated, all of which are hallmark features of cellular senescence ( , ).
 To validate the presence of senescence phenotype, we performed canonical senescence-associated β-galactosidase (SA-β-gal) staining, which detects senescent cells by increased β-galactosidase activity. As expected, SA-β-gal activity and phosphorylated H2AX (γ-H2AX) expression was significantly higher in aged (~30%) and adult CFs (~22%) compared to neo-CFs (~2%) ( and fig. S3A). Furthermore, the mRNA expression levels of well-known senescence markers, including Cdkn1a, Cdkn2a, and Cdkn2d, were significantly up-regulated by approximately twofold in aging CFs (fig. S3B). Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) analysis further confirmed the significant enrichment of senescence-related gene sets [SenMayo gene set ( )] in aged iCMs at both bulk and single-cell resolutions ( ).

## Cellular senescence inhibits iCM conversion
To investigate whether senescence affects iCM conversion, we exposed neo-CFs to known senescence-inducing stressors: extensive replication (REP) and γ-irradiation (IR) ( ). REP led to a gradual increase in the ratio of SA-β-gal+ cells, peaking at passage 6 (P6) (fig. S3, C and D), while a 20–gray (Gy) dose of IR maximized the number of senescent cells (fig. S3, E and F). Therefore, we applied P6-CFs and radiation dosage at 20 Gy (IR-CFs) for subsequent experiments. Both treatments led to elevated levels of senescence markers including an increased percentage of SA-β-gal+ cells and enhanced expression of Cdkn1a and p53 (fig. S3, G and H). We then rendered REP- and IR-treated cells for reprogramming. Both FACS analysis and immunostaining for cardiac markers (α-actinin and cTnT) showed that MGT transduction reached reprogramming efficiency around 15% using nonsenescent neo-CFs ( ). However, pretreatment of REP and IR to neo-CFs led to decreased iCM conversion rate at ~2% after 10 days of reprogramming ( ), closely resembling the low reprogramming efficiency in aged cells.
Senescence is also characterized by the secretion of SASP, which can alter the microenvironment ( ). To assess its impact on iCM reprogramming, we collected culture supernatant (sup) from aged CFs, P6-CFs, and IR-CFs and exposed nonsenescent neo-CFs for 6 days. We found that sup from senescent cells significantly increased SA-β-gal activity ( ). In addition, when conditioned medium from senescent CFs was applied to neo-iCMs during reprogramming (days 3 to 14), it significantly reduced reprogramming efficiency from 10% to about 2% in cTnT+ cells compared to control neo-CF medium ( ).

## Senolytic treatment enhances iCM reprogramming efficiency
Given the negative impact of senescence on reprogramming, we investigated whether senolytic treatment could restore iCM conversion. Aged CFs were treated with a combination of well-known senolytic reagents dasatinib and quercetin (D + Q) ( ) and subjected to reprogramming. We found that D + Q treatment significantly enhanced reprogramming efficiency (~6%) compared with the dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)–treated group (~2%) ( ). Furthermore, compared to the control group, the mRNA expression levels of cardiac genes, including Actc1, Tnni3, and Myh6, were significantly up-regulated by ~2- to 10-fold in the D + Q–treated group ( ). We also collected culture medium from aged CFs treated with either D + Q or DMSO and exposed nonsenescent CFs to the conditioned medium for 3 days (fig. S3I). The medium from D + Q–treated cells significantly reduced SA-β-gal activity ( ). When this conditioned medium was applied to neo-iCMs (fig. S3I), it significantly improved reprogramming efficiency compared to the medium from DMSO-treated CFs ( ). Together, these results confirm that cellular senescence inhibits cardiac reprogramming and that senolytic treatment can enhance iCM conversion efficiency.
 

## Identification of Nr4a3 as a reprogramming repressor
Given the observed senescence-associated inhibition of iCM conversion, we aimed to identify molecular drivers of this barrier. To do so, we curated a panel of TFs and epigenetic factors from comparisons across CFs and iCMs from advancing ages. GO analysis showed that these genes were mainly involved in cardiac development, fibrosis, inflammatory response, and aging ( ). We next used a loss-of-function approach to explore the function of 50 TFs and epigenomic factors in reprogramming using adult and aged CFs. For each candidate, a pool of short hairpin RNAs (shRNAs; two to four independent shRNA constructs targeting different regions within the gene) was used and knockdown efficiency was validated (fig. S4, A to F). Our screen results showed that knockdown of 19 of the 50 candidates increased reprogramming in both aged and adult CFs ( ). Deficiency of genes related to heart development (Eya4, Tfec, and Msx1), fibrosis (Fap and Tnxb), ossification (Smad9), aging, and other pathways (Foxc1, Tfdp1, and Kcnip3) significantly improved iCM generation (fig. S4, (G) to (L). Compared to controls, Nr4a3 knockdown produced the most marked increase in iCM generation, with a significant approximately threefold elevation in reprogramming efficiency (from ~1.8% in controls to ~5.8% in the Nr4a3 knockdown group), identifying it as a key reprogramming repressor.
 To further validate the role of Nr4a3 in regulating reprogramming, we examined its knockdown in several senescent CF models, including aged CFs, aged TTFs, IR-CFs, and REP-CFs. Multiple assays, including flow cytometry, ICC, and RT-qPCR analyses, showed that Nr4a3 knockdown consistently enhanced iCM reprogramming across senescent and aged fibroblast models ( and fig. S5A). Specifically, we observed increased expression of cardiac markers (e.g., Actc1 and cTnT) and decreased expression of fibroblast markers (fig. S5, B to D), further confirming the role of Nr4a3 as a critical repressor of iCM reprogramming.
To further assess the effect of Nr4a3 on reprogramming, we also investigated its overexpression in nonsenescent neo-CFs, which are typically more amenable to reprogramming. Overexpression of Nr4a3 (OE-Nr4a3) in neo-CFs significantly hindered iCM conversion, as evidenced by immunostaining and flow cytometry. The proportion of cTnT+ and α-actinin+ cells decreased from ~13% in controls to ~4% in the OE-Nr4a3 group ( ). RT-qPCR results also demonstrated a lower expression level of cardiac genes in the OE-Nr4a3 groups compared to the control groups ( ). Together, these results identify Nr4a3 as a potent iCM reprogramming repressor, with its knockdown markedly enhancing the generation of iCMs in both aged and senescent fibroblasts.

## Nr4a3 promotes cellular senescence in CFs
To further explore the association of Nr4a3 with cellular senescence, we investigated its expression at both transcriptomic and epigenomic levels. At the mRNA level, the expression of Nr4a3 increases with advancing ages ( ), which align with our earlier findings showing the enrichment of Nr4a3 regulon in aged iCMs ( ). We also observed a gradual increase in chromatin accessibility at the Nr4a3 promoter region across age groups ( ). In addition, Nr4a3 expression significantly increased in various senescent cells, including IR-CFs, REP-CFs, and CFs treated with conditional medium (fig. S5, E to G).
These observations led us to test whether Nr4a3 might actively promote cellular senescence. Ectopic Nr4a3 expression (OE-Nr4a3) in neo-CFs induced senescence phenotypes, such as increased SA-β-gal+ cells, along with increased γ-H2AX+ foci in the nuclei and reduced expression of proliferation marker genes ( , and fig. S5, H and I). Conversely, knocked down Nr4a3 in aged CFs reduced SA-β-gal activity, lowered γ-H2AX expression, and decreased the expression of Cdkn2a and Cdkn1a ( , and fig. S5, J and K). To further delineate the global impact of Nr4a3 depletion, we performed RNA-seq on senescent CFs (P3-CFs) treated with shNr4a3 or shNT. Differential gene expression analysis revealed that shNr4a3 treatment induced up-regulation of genes involved in chromatin remodeling (e.g., Smarcd1), muscle organ development (e.g., Ttn), and heart morphogenesis (e.g., Pitx2), as well as negative regulation of cytokine production (fig. S6, A and B). Conversely, down-regulated genes in the shNr4a3 group were enriched in fibrosis [e.g., regulation of supramolecular fiber organization (Pecam1), ossification (Mapk11), BMP signaling (Bmp4), and ECM organization] and inflammatory signatures [e.g., cytokine-mediated signaling pathway (Ccl2), cell chemotaxis (S100a14), and regulation of inflammatory response] (fig. S6C). GSEA analysis supported these findings, showing the enrichment of chromatin remodeling pathways and suppression of inflammatory response, cytokine binding, and extracellular matrix assembly in shNr4a3-treated CFs (fig. S6, D to G). Functionally, pre-reprogramming knockdown of Nr4a3 achieved ~80% silencing efficiency (fig. S7A) and significantly improved MGT-mediated conversion, as evidenced by the increased expression of cardiac and ion channel genes, decreased fibroblast markers, and enhanced iCM generation by immunostaining and flow cytometry (fig. S7, B to F). Collectively, these findings indicate that Nr4a3 promotes cellular senescence and establishes a fibrotic/inflammatory transcriptional state that limits the reprogramming capacity of aged fibroblasts.

## Nr4a3 reconstructs SASP expression to suppress iCM reprogramming
SASP is a hallmark of senescent cells and has been implicated in affecting cellular plasticity ( , ). Our RNA-seq analysis comparing CFs with or without Nr4a3 overexpression demonstrated global transcriptional changes induced by Nr4a3 ( ). Notably, Nr4a3 overexpression resulted in the elevated expression of SASP factors associated with cell chemotaxis (Cxcl5, Cxcl10, and Cxcl14), cytokine signaling pathways (Il1α, Cxcl1, and Crlf1) and ECM organization (Mmp11, Fap, and Tnxb) ( and fig. S8A). Intriguingly, the SASP factor profile induced by OE-Nr4a3 partially resembled that from aged CFs ( ).
 We next tested whether SASP activation by Nr4a3 could suppress reprogramming. The conditioned medium (sup) from OE-Nr4a3 CFs significantly impaired iCM reprogramming efficiency, reducing the number of cTnT+ cells to ~4% (fig. S8B and ). Overlap analysis of SASP factors induced by Nr4a3 and aging-associated SASP signatures revealed 38 shared factors ( and fig. S8C). Knockdown of individual SASP factors such as Cxcl14, Il1α, Fap, and Mmp3 rescued iCM reprogramming by reducing the inhibitory effects of Nr4a3 overexpression (fig. S8D and ).
Using publicly available Nr4a3 cleavage under targets and tagmentation (CUT&Tag) sequencing results ( ), we observed the significant enrichment of Nr4a3-bound DNA at the promoter region of Cxcl14, Il1α, Fap, and Mmp3 ( and fig. S8, E to G). In our multiomics analyses, Nr4a3 knockdown also led to reduced chromatin accessibility and H3K27ac modification at the promoters of these genes ( and fig. S8, H to J). To further confirm whether Cxcl14, Il1α, Fap, and Mmp3 are direct downstream targets of Nr4a3, we conducted Nr4a3 chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP)-qPCR and luciferase assays. Both assays demonstrated that Nr4a3 directly binds to the promoters of these four candidate genes and enhances their transcriptional activity ( , and fig. S8, K and L). These results suggest that Nr4a3 functions as an activator, driving the expression of these SASP genes.
 

## Blocking Nr4a3-induced SASP factors enhances iCM reprogramming potential
Given that SASP factors impede reprogramming, we tested whether inhibiting these factors could restore reprogramming efficiency. Specifically, we focused on C-X-C motif chemokine ligand 14 (Cxcl14), which was strongly up-regulated by Nr4a3 overexpression and available to the study. We next investigated the role of Cxcl14 in cellular senescence by silencing its expression with shRNA-mediated knockdown. Silencing Cxcl14 in P3-CFs reduced SA-β-gal activity and γ-H2AX expression (fig. S9, A to C), decreased p19 expression (fig. S9D), and down-regulated SASP factors, including Mmp3, Fap, Cxcl10, Serpin3n, and Cxcl1 (fig. S9E). 5-Ethynyl 2′-deoxyuridine staining and RT-qPCR analysis showed that proliferation was not affected (fig. S9, F and G). Furthermore, we found that adding Cxcl14 to neo-iCMs from day 3 of reprogramming reduced cTnT+ cells (fig. S9H). In addition, we added an anti-Cxcl14 neutralizing antibody to the culture medium of aged iCMs from days 3 to 10 of reprogramming, with isotype-matched Rat immunoglobulin G (IgG) as a control. FACS analysis revealed that blocking Cxcl14 significantly enhanced cardiac reprogramming ( ). Moreover, Nr4a3 overexpression reduced the proportion of cTnT+ and α-actinin+ cells from ~8% in controls to ~3%. Notably, Cxcl14 knockdown alleviated this suppression, restoring the percentage of cTnT+ and α-actinin+ cells to ~7% and thereby rescuing iCM conversion ( ). These results suggest that SASP blockade, particularly targeting Cxcl14, can restore reprogramming capacity in the presence of Nr4a3.

## Nr4a3 knockdown alters chromatin accessibility and epigenetic landscape
To further investigate the molecular mechanisms underlying the effects of Nr4a3, we conducted RNA-seq, ATAC-seq, and CUT&Tag profiling of histone modifications (H3K27ac and H3K27me3) in shNr4a3-iCMs and shNT-iCMs at day 7 of reprogramming ( ). Heatmap analysis of these multiomics datasets revealed clear separation between shNr4a3-iCMs and shNT-iCMs, indicating that Nr4a3 depletion leads to marked transcriptional and epigenomic changes (fig. S10, A to D).
 Chromatin accessibility analysis by ATAC-seq identified 970 differentially accessible regions (DARs), the majority of which were enriched in promoter regions (fig. S10, E to G). Among these DARs, 608 peaks were open, and 362 peaks were closed in shNr4a3-iCMs compared to shNT-iCMs (P value <0.05; |log2FC (log2 fold change)| > 0.5) ( ). DARs associated with genes in shNr4a3-iCMs were enriched in processes related to cardiac muscle tissue development, cell junction assembly, glycoprotein metabolism, and heart processes, while genes near closed DARs were enriched in pathways linked to ossification, angiogenesis regulation, ECM organization, and mitogen-activated protein kinase cascade activation ( ).
To identify the TFs driving these changes, we performed de novo motif analysis on the enriched peaks from shNr4a3-iCMs, which revealed an enrichment of TFs including TEAD, TBX5, MEF2C, and TBX20, all of which are implicated in heart regeneration ( ) ( , , ). In contrast, motifs related to inflammatory response and fibrosis, such as AP4, CEBPA, and FRA1 ( – ), were more prevalent in shNT-iCMs, reinforcing the idea that Nr4a3 depletion shifts the epigenetic landscape toward a regenerative phenotype.
RNA-seq analysis confirmed the transcriptional changes observed in chromatin accessibility. Upon Nr4a3 knockdown, genes involved in cardiac muscle development and regeneration, such as Myocd, were up-regulated, while genes associated with fibrosis and senescence, including Cxcl14, Il16, and Timp1, were significantly down-regulated ( ). These results indicate that Nr4a3 depletion remodels the chromatin landscape and drives a shift from a fibrotic/inflammatory state to a regenerative cardiac program.
Next, we focused on the histone modifications (H3K27ac and H3K27me3) that mark the epigenetic state of genes involved in cardiac development. In shNr4a3-iCMs, we observed that H3K27ac was enriched at the promoter regions of genes involved in muscle cell differentiation, striated muscle tissue development, and heart contraction ( and fig. S10, H and I). These genes lost the repressive H3K27me3 mark, indicating active transcription. Conversely, genes related to fibrosis and inflammation, such as those involved in wound healing and response to TGFβ (transforming growth factor–β), exhibited a loss of H3K27ac and a gain of H3K27me3 ( and fig. S10, H and I). Specifically, we annotated 2426 superenhancers (SEs) in shNr4a3-iCMs by ranking H3K27ac signal intensity (fig. S10J). GO analysis of SE-associated genes revealed strong enrichment for biological processes critical to cardiac function, including heart contraction, cell junction assembly, and sarcomere organization (fig. S10K). Notably, one of the identified SEs was located at the neuregulin 1 (Nrg1) locus, which is known to govern trabecular growth and cardiac gene programs through Nrg1/Erbb4 (Erb-B2 receptor tyrosine kinase 4) signaling ( – ). Intriguingly, knocking down Nr4a3 in iCMs resulted in the increased expression of genes associated with SEs, including Nrg1, Ttn, and Dlg2, as validated by RT-qPCR (fig. S10L). These findings further support the notion that Nr4a3 depletion facilitates the activation of cardiac regenerative programs and suppresses senescence-associated pathways. These findings are consistent with our ATAC-seq and H3K27ac joint analysis (fig. S10, M to P).

## Integrated multiomics analysis reveals a core set of genes involved in cardiac reprogramming
To further integrate the findings from RNA-seq, ATAC-seq, and CUT&Tag, we performed a joint analysis of 2227 up-regulated differentially expressed genes (DEGs), 3897 genes with increased chromatin accessibility, and 4484 genes marked by H3K27ac. This analysis identified a core set of 169 overlapping genes, including Myocd, that were consistently up-regulated across all datasets ( ). These genes were significantly enriched in biological processes related to cardiac development, cardiac muscle differentiation, and chromatin organization ( ). Conversely, we identified 93 genes that were simultaneously down-regulated in both expression and chromatin accessibility. These genes, involved in cartilage development (Bmp2 and Nfia) and ossification (Bmp2 and Zbtb16), are known to inhibit cardiac regeneration ( ) ( , , ). Together, these data suggest that Nr4a3 depletion not only activates cardiac genes but also represses those that inhibit cardiac regeneration.

## Nr4a3 suppresses the recruitment of reprogramming factors to activate Myocd expression
Myocardin (Myocd), a master regulator during heart development, has been reported to initiate iCM conversion together with other reprogramming factors ( , ). Given the enrichment of Myocd in our multiomics analysis, we hypothesized that Myocd plays a key role in mediating the effects of Nr4a3 depletion on cardiac reprogramming. Myocd expression was significantly lower in aged iCMs compared to neo-iCMs. Nr4a3 depletion led to a twofold increase in Myocd expression in aged iCMs ( and fig. S11A), suggesting that Nr4a3 represses Myocd during reprogramming.
To determine whether Myocd mediates the enhanced reprogramming observed upon Nr4a3 depletion, we knocked down Myocd in shNr4a3-iCMs. This significantly suppressed the enhanced reprogramming efficiency induced by Nr4a3 depletion; whereas Nr4a3 depletion alone increased the percentage of cTnT+ iCMs from ~4% in control cells to ~11%, knockdown of Myocd in shNr4a3-iCMs reduced this percentage to ~3% ( ). These data confirm that Myocd is a key downstream target of Nr4a3 in regulating iCM conversion. We next explored the molecular mechanism by which Nr4a3 regulates Myocd expression. Scanning the Myocd promoter [± 2 kb of the transcription start site (TSS)] on JASPAR website ( ), we identified several potential Nr4a3 binding motifs, some of which overlapped with MGT binding sites (fig. S11B). ChIP-qPCR using Flag-tagged Nr4a3-overexpressing fibroblasts confirmed that Nr4a3 directly binds to the Myocd promoter region, suggesting the direct regulation of Myocd ( ). Luciferase assay evaluating firefly luciferase expression driven by the Myocd promoter yielded similar results ( ). In addition, luciferase assays showed that Nr4a3 overexpression suppressed Myocd promoter activity in cells transfected with MGT (fig. S11, C and D), supporting the conclusion that Nr4a3 suppresses Myocd activation, thereby inhibiting iCM reprogramming.

## NR4A3 deficiency improves cardiac reprogramming in human senescent fibroblasts
To investigate the role of NR4A3 in human CF (HCF) reprogramming, we first analyzed its expression in HCFs across different developmental stages. Published data ( ) demonstrated a higher expression level of NR4A3 in adult HCFs compared with fetal HCFs ( ). We further observed a positive correlation between NR4A3 expression and cardiac SASP score in heart tissue from the GTEx (Genotype Tissue Expression) dataset ( ), suggesting that NR4A3 could be involved in regulating human fibroblast senescence ( ).
 Next, to model human fibroblast senescence, we used H9-derived fibroblasts (H9Fs), which reached a SA-β-gal+ percentage of up to 40% following REP ( ). RT-qPCR analysis confirmed an increase in NR4A3 expression in these senescent H9Fs ( ). We then introduced shRNA targeting NR4A3 together with MGT cocktail (human MEF2C, GATA4, and TBX5) into senescent H9Fs. The knockdown efficiency achieved was 90% as evaluated by RT-qPCR ( ). Both immunostaining and flow cytometry analysis revealed a marked improvement in hiCM reprogramming efficiency upon NR4A3 knockdown ( ). In addition, calcium transient analysis revealed that NR4A3 knockdown increased the proportion of hiCMs exhibiting rhythmic Ca2+ flux by nearly twofold compared to shNT controls ( ). NR4A3 knockdown significantly enhanced the number of heating hiCMs compared to control treatment ( and movie S1). Moreover, consistent results were observed when we combined shRNA targeting NR4A3 with the MGT133 cocktail (MGT and miR-133) in senescent H9Fs, further supporting the repressive role of NR4A3 in hiCM reprogramming (fig. S12, A and B).
We extended these findings to primary adult ventricular HCFs, which exhibited a higher degree of senescence with a SA-β-gal+ percentage of up to 60% ( ). Upon NR4A3 knockdown in these senescent HCFs combined with the MGT133 cocktail, we observed a fivefold increase in cTnT+ iCMs ( ). This was accompanied by up-regulation of cardiac genes and down-regulation of fibroblast signature genes at the mRNA level ( ). Similar to murine iCMs, MYOCD, a key cardiac transcriptional regulator, increased by twofold, while SASP markers, including FAP, CXCL14, MMP3, and IL1α, were significantly reduced following NR4A3 loss ( ). Together, these results indicate that knockdown of NR4A3 in human senescent fibroblasts remarkably enhances cardiac reprogramming.

## Knockdown of Nr4a3 improves in vivo reprogramming and heart function after MI
We next sought to evaluate the role of Nr4a3 in vivo. To trace the newly generated cardiomyocytes, we used a genetic lineage-tracing strategy to label the iCMs from CFs ( ). We used a mouse line in which the inducible Cre recombinase (MerCreMer) was inserted into the Postn genetic locus (PostnMerCreMer) and crossed it with an R26tdTomato reporter line. In the presence of tamoxifen, cells that express Postn (marker for activated fibroblasts) will permanently express tdTomato ( ). The tracing mice were subjected to MI and viral injection as previously described ( , ). Tamoxifen was administered to mice at 1, 3, and 5 days post-MI by oral gavage. Four weeks after MI, we found that ~90% of cells were tdTomato+ fibroblasts within the injured area, none of which expressed α-actinin in the control group ( ). Upon MGT treatment, tdTomato+ α-actinin+ cells were found at the border zone and infarct zone, suggesting induction of reprogramming iCMs in situ. We found that delivery of MGT and shNr4a3 viruses to injured hearts resulted in an increase in the absolute number of tdTomato+ α-actinin+ iCMs compared with control animals receiving MGT and shNT viruses ( ). We also ranked the quality of iCMs by sarcomere formation as previously described ( , ). Nr4a3 knockdown led to an increase in the percentage of iCMs exhibiting a well-organized sarcomere structure ( ).
 We next evaluated heart function by echocardiography to determine whether the improved generation of iCMs by shNr4a3 shown above contributes to functional restoration of the injured heart ( ). Baseline cardiac function, including left ventricular ejection fraction (EF) and fractional shortening (FS), was comparable among groups (fig. S13, A and B). At 3 days post-MI, no significant differences were observed across groups (fig. S13, C and D), indicating similar initial injury severity. Four weeks after MI, the EF of the left ventricle increased from ~29% [green fluorescent protein (GFP) group] to ~40% (MGT group) in the injured hearts. In the MGT-shNr4a3 group, the EF further improved to ~55% ( ). Consistently, the FS of the left ventricle greatly improved in the MGT-shNr4a3 group (~26%) compared to the control MGT-shNT group (~21%) ( ). We further quantified the scar area using Masson’s trichrome–stained sections from five layers of left ventricles in a double-blinded way. We identified a reduction of scar size in reprogramming mice compared with that of GFP controls and that Nr4a3 knockdown further decreased scar size ( ). Together, our study suggests that knockdown of Nr4a3 remarkably enhances in vivo cardiac reprogramming and contributes to functional improvement in the injured heart.

## DISCUSSION
In this study, we investigated the molecular mechanisms underlying the inefficient conversion of fibroblasts to iCMs with advancing ages. Through comprehensive profiling of the transcriptomic and epigenomic landscape of CFs and iCMs across different ages, we identified cellular senescence as a critical reprogramming barrier to iCM fate acquisition. Functional screening identified Nr4a3 as a key molecular switch that drives cellular senescence and inhibits iCM reprogramming. Specifically, Nr4a3 modulates SASP, which includes factors that actively suppress reprogramming. In addition, Nr4a3 impedes the recruitment of reprogramming factors and suppresses the activation of key cardiac transcriptional programs. We further demonstrated that Nr4a3 knockdown significantly improved iCM reprogramming in a murine model of MI (fig. S14).
Our study provided a comprehensive comparison of transcriptomic and epigenetic changes across distinct age groups in CFs and age-matched iCMs. We observed that adult and aged fibroblasts exhibit unique transcriptional and epigenetic signatures, including increased inflammatory signaling, activation of ossification pathways, and elevated cytokine production, all of which may impair fibroblast plasticity. These findings are consistent with previous work by Sun et al. ( ) who also reported that Epas1 (endothelial PAS domain protein 1)–mediated angiogenic pathways compete with and reduce cardiogenic reprogramming in a microRNA-based system. In line with these observations, we found that cellular senescence acts as a major barrier to the conversion of fibroblasts into iCMs, consistent with previous reports showing that cellular senescence in somatic cells can prevent efficient reprogramming into pluripotent stem cells or other differentiated cells ( – ).
Cellular senescence serves as a key biological mechanism that limits the proliferation of damaged cells. However, its chronic activation during aging restricts tissue repair and contributes to age-related diseases, including heart failure ( ). Senescence can further exacerbate these changes by secreting factors that form the SASP, which alters the microenvironment and further restricts the reprogramming potential of aged fibroblasts. Consistent with these findings, we demonstrated that removing senescent cells using senolytic D + Q treatment or conditioned medium from D + Q–treated cells could significantly enhance iCM reprogramming efficiency. Recent studies have suggested that transient senescence could promote heart repair in regenerative contexts, such as Agrin-mediated cardiac repair and neonatal heart regeneration ( – ). These findings indicate a crucial distinction between the reparative effects of transient senescence and the persistent, inhibitory effects of senescence on cell fate reprogramming in aged fibroblasts. Harnessing the reparative aspects of transient senescence while mitigating its chronic inhibitory effects in aging could provide a translational strategy for enhancing cardiac reprogramming and repair in different settings.
Nr4a3, a member of the NR4A subfamily of orphan nuclear receptors, has been implicated in the progression of human diseases including leukemia, atherosclerosis, and central nervous system diseases ( – ). Our results indicate that Nr4a3 expression is elevated in senescent and aged CFs. Forced expression of Nr4a3 exacerbated senescence features, including increased SA-β-gal activity, nuclear γ-H2AX staining, and a decrease in proliferation markers such as p21. Nr4a3 overexpression induced a robust SASP response, further fostering a fibroblast state that is resistant to reprogramming. The SASP is a highly heterogeneous mixture of cytokines, chemokines, growth factors, and matrix proteases, which together contribute to a persistent inflammatory microenvironment ( ). Our mechanistic investigations revealed that at least four critical SASP components, Cxcl14, Il1α, Fap, and Mmp3, are directly transactivated by Nr4a3. Notably, Cxcl14, which is induced by Nr4a3, was a key SASP factor whose inhibition significantly enhanced iCM conversion. This supports the growing body of evidence suggesting that mitigating inflammatory responses can improve reprogramming efficiency, especially in postnatal and adult fibroblasts ( , ). Our findings indicate the potential of targeting specific SASP factors, such as Cxcl14, to enhance cardiac regeneration and reprogramming in adult and aging hearts. The pro-inflammatory role of Nr4a3 has also been implicated in other contexts, such as in chondrocytes, where its overexpression increases Mmp3 and Mmp9 expression ( , ). This is consistent with our results, further reinforcing the importance of Nr4a3 in driving a chronic inflammatory state that inhibits reprogramming.
In addition to its role in cellular senescence, our study uncovered the epigenetic mechanisms by which Nr4a3 impedes reprogramming in aged fibroblasts. Our multiomics analysis revealed that Nr4a3 maintains a repressive chromatin state at key cardiac loci, preventing the binding of reprogramming factors. We observed that neo-CFs have a more accessible chromatin landscape and express key cardiac TFs, such as Myocd, which supports a permissive environment for iCM generation. In contrast, aged CFs exhibited reduced chromatin accessibility and decreased expression of these transcriptional factors, leading to a diminished reprogramming potential. These findings are in line with studies showing that preexisting chromatin accessibility is a key determinant of reprogramming efficiency ( ). For example, in induced hepatocyte reprogramming, chromatin regions that are accessible in the native hepatocyte genome allow reprogramming factors to bind and activate hepatic gene expression ( ).
We found that knockdown of Nr4a3 improved the recruitment of reprogramming factors; activation of cardiac transcriptional programs, including Myocd; and enrichment of cardiac-specific cis-regulatory elements. We focused on Myocd because it emerged as one of the most significantly up-regulated cardiac TFs in shNr4a3-iCMs. Myocd is a well-established master regulator of cardiac gene programs, coordinating the expression of structural and functional cardiac genes ( , ), making it a critical node in cardiogenic reprogramming. Together, these results suggest that Nr4a3 controls a shift in the chromatin landscape from a fibrotic, senescent state to a regenerative cardiac program. The dual function of Nr4a3 in both maintaining senescence and restricting reprogramming potential may extend to other aging contexts where senescence and repressive chromatin states limit regenerative responses.
In our model, echocardiography at day 3 and 7 post-MI served to exclude insufficient ligation and standardize injury. Clinically, echocardiography is more often applied during chronic stages to monitor remodeling and prognosis, but acute-phase imaging can also provide early assessment of infarct size and complications. Together, this underscores the complementary utility of echocardiography in both experimental and clinical contexts.
While our study provides mechanistic insights into the Nr4a3-mediated senescence barrier to cardiac reprogramming, several limitations should be noted. In this study, because of limited resources of primary age-equivalent HCFs, we used repeatedly passaged H9Fs and HCFs containing 40 to 60% senescent cells to evaluate the effect of NR4A3 on reprogramming. Further examination of cellular senescence and Nr4a3 expression in CFs from age-matched patients will be valuable.
In our mouse iCM experiments, reprogramming efficiency was primarily assessed using cardiac markers, and we did not observe robust functional maturation, such as spontaneous beating or calcium transients in aged fibroblast-derived iCMs despite successful molecular and structural reprogramming. In contrast, hiCMs treated with shNR4A3 exhibited moderate improvements in calcium transients and contraction when cocultured with human embryonic stem cell–derived cardiomyocytes. These observations suggest that additional maturation barriers, beyond Nr4a3 suppression, limit full functional acquisition in aged mouse fibroblasts and warrant future investigation.
While our study demonstrated the potential of targeting Nr4a3 in improving cardiac reprogramming, several limitations apply to our in vivo experiments. First, validation in more specific genetic models, such as tissue- or cell type–specific Nr4a3-deficient mice, will help clarify its role in senescence and MGT-mediated direct iCM reprogramming. Second, the functional benefits observed with Nr4a3 targeting likely arise from a combination of new iCM generation, remodeling of the infarct microenvironment, and systemic vascular and immune effects. Third, we used retroviral MGT as the baseline cocktail because it is the most established minimal factor set with broad cross-species applicability. Future studies using more selective viral vectors, such as modified adeno-associated virus serotype 5 with enhanced CF tropism ( ), could improve cell type specificity. Last, while our in vivo experiments were designed primarily to test whether the in vitro mechanistic findings could be translated into functional cardiac repair, combining Nr4a3 inhibition with human-effective cocktails will be essential to determine whether this approach represents an optimal strategy for enhancing cardiac reprogramming.
In summary, our results demonstrated that cellular senescence acts as a critical barrier to iCM reprogramming, particularly in aged CFs. We identified Nr4a3 as a key regulator of this process, and our results suggest that targeting senescence pathways, including Nr4a3 and SASP factors, can significantly improve reprogramming efficiency and cardiac repair. These findings offer insights into the role of aging in cellular reprogramming and highlight potential therapeutic strategies for improving tissue regeneration in age-related diseases, including heart failure.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

## Mouse strains and plasmids
All mice were maintained in specific pathogen–free facilities at the Medical Research Institute of Wuhan University. The experimental protocols followed the International Guiding Principles for Biomedical Involving Animals. All animal procedures were approved by the Animal Care and Ethical Committee of College of Medical Research Institute at Wuhan University (approval number: MRI2020-LAC68). Both male and female mice were used in this study. Wild-type C57BL/6 and CD1 mice were obtained from Beijing Vitalstar Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China). PostnMerCreMer mice were gifted from Hong Ma Lab from Zhejiang University. PostnMerCreMer mice were used to trace activated fibroblasts by crossing with Rosa26-loxp-stop-loxp (lsl)-tdTomato (R26tdTomato) mice ( ).
Retroviral pMXs-based vectors containing murine Gata4, Mef2c, Tbx5, Hand2, and polycistronic MGT were described previously ( , ). For gene overexpression, the lentiviral backbone construct pLenti-GFP was originally purchased from Addgene (no. 17448). Murine Nr4a3 was amplified from mouse heart cDNA and assembled into the pLenti-GFP backbone. All shRNAs were constructed in the pLKO.1 backbone lentivirus as previously described ( ). Cloning primers and oligos used are listed in table S1. All constructs were sequencing validated.

## Reprogramming fibroblasts into iCMs
Murine fibroblasts were seeded into 24-well plates at various densities depending on cell types (explant cultured TTFs, 2 × 104 per well; neo-CFs, 5 × 104 per well; adult/aged/senescent fibroblasts, 1 × 105 cells per well) 1 day before reprogramming. On day 0, murine fibroblasts were infected with retroviral MGT in iCM medium [10% fetal bovine serum in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium/M199 (4:1)] containing polybrene (4 μg/ml). Human fibroblasts were seeded at a density of 2 × 104 cells per well of a 24-well plate and were infected with retroviruses encoding hMGT and miR133 in iCM medium containing polybrene (4 μg/ml). For the shRNA knockdown and overexpression experiments, lentiviruses were added to cells on day 2. On day 3, cells were selected with puromycin (1 μg/ml) for 3 days. On day 6, puromycin was removed from iCM medium, and cells were cultured in regular iCM medium that was replaced every 2 to 3 days. During the reprogramming process, day 10 was selected for assessing cardiomyocyte-related protein expression, while day 14 was used to evaluate sarcomere assembly, which occurs slightly later than protein expression.

## Generation of senescent fibroblasts and SA-β-gal staining
Cellular senescence was induced by IR and REP. In general, freshly isolated neo-CFs were cultured overnight with fibroblast medium. On the next day, cells were exposed to IR at dosages ranging from 5 to 40 Gy in an irradiator (Rad Source RS2000ProX) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Immediately after irradiation, cells were replaced with fresh medium and cultured for 3 additional days. The percentage of SA-β-gal–positive cells was evaluated to confirm the successful induction of senescence.
To generate REP-senescent cells, neo-CFs were passaged by trypsin digestion every 3 to 4 days when they became 90% confluent. After each passage, the percentage of SA-β-gal–positive cells was determined to evaluate the degree of cell senescence.
SA-β-gal staining was performed using the SA-β-Gal Stain Kit (Cell Signaling, 9860) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, cells were washed in Dulbecco’s phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) and fixed with β-Gal Fixative buffer for 15 min at room temperature. Cells were then washed three times with PBS for 3 min each time and stained with Dyeing Working Solution containing an X-Gal Solution at 37°C overnight. An optical microscope was used to observe SA-β-gal–stained cells, and the percentages of SA-β-gal–positive cells were quantified using Image J.

## Mouse MI and in vivo delivery of virus
MI and in vivo reprogramming were performed in 8-week-old male C57BL/6 mice as we previously reported ( , ). Before surgery, mice were anesthetized with tribromoethanol (320 mg/kg) and 2-butanol (160 mg/kg). After exposing the heart, MI was induced by permanent ligation of the left anterior descending artery (LAD) with a 7-0 prolene suture. Sham-operated animals served as surgical controls and were subjected to the same procedures as the experimental animals with the exception that the LAD was not ligated. A pool of retrovirus MGT or GFP with lentivirus (shNr4a3 or shNT) was mixed in Dulbecco’s PBS with polybrene (8 μg/ml) before injection. Mixed viruses [retrovirus, 2 × 106 plaque-forming units (PFU); lentivirus, 5 × 106 PFU] were injected into two sites at the boundary area between the infarct zone and the border zone with 31-gauge insulin needles immediately after coronary artery occlusion. Mice were allowed to recover using a mouse ventilator and heating pad. At days 3 and 7 post-MI, echocardiography was conducted to exclude unsuccessfully ligated mice on the basis of EF values higher than 40%. Four weeks after MI, mice were weighted, subjected to echocardiography to evaluate heart functions, and euthanized for histology analysis.
Animals were randomly assigned to experimental groups before surgery. Inclusion criteria were successful LAD ligation with EF < 45% at day 3. Mice failing to meet this criterion were excluded from further analyses. All surgery operations, viral inoculation, and echocardiography were performed in a double-blinded manner.

## Quantification and statistical analysis
All experiments were repeated at least three times. All data were analyzed using Prism 8 (GraphPad). All results were expressed as the means ± SEM. An unpaired t test was used to determine the significance of differences between two groups. A one-way or two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used to determine the significance of experiments containing three or more conditions. In all cases, P < 0.05 was considered statistically significant (*P < 0.05; **P < 0.01; ***P < 0.001). All data are representative of multiple repeated experiments.
