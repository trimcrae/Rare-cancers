## Abstract

## Background
Sepsis induces systemic inflammation and multi-organ dysfunction, with the lungs frequently affected. Elevated in endothelial cells under hypoxic and inflammatory stressors, NDRG1’s contribution to septic pulmonary pathology remains largely uncharacterized. This study investigates whether NDRG1 exacerbates pulmonary inflammation through PKM2-dependent STAT3/NF-κB signaling.

## Methods
Mice underwent cecal ligation and perforation to model sepsis, with lung morphology examined by H&E staining. Transcriptional levels of VE-cadherin, ICAM-1, VCAM-1, and cytokines TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β were assessed by qPCR, while STAT3/NF-κB phosphorylation was determined by Western blot. NDRG1 localization in pulmonary endothelial cells was evaluated by immunofluorescence, and the NDRG1-PKM2-STAT3/NF-κB axis was further examined using Ndrg1 < sup>-/-</sup> mice and PKM2 modulators (shikonin; TEPP-46).

## Results
CLP-induced sepsis caused severe pulmonary hemorrhage, edema, and alveolar destruction, accompanied by increased ICAM-1, VCAM-1, and proinflammatory cytokines, as well as reduced VE-cadherin expression. NDRG1 was markedly upregulated in septic endothelia, while its deletion alleviated lung injury, inflammation, and cytokine release by inhibiting PKM2-dependent STAT3/NF-κB phosphorylation. PKM2 inhibition with shikonin reproduced, whereas activation with TEPP-46 abolished, these protective effects, confirming the NDRG1–PKM2–STAT3/NF-κB axis as a pivotal regulator of sepsis-induced pulmonary inflammation.

## Conclusion
By orchestrating downstream STAT3 and NF-κB signaling cascades through PKM2 dependency, NDRG1 contributes to the progression of septic lung injury via increased inflammatory responses and disruption of endothelial integrity. Intervening in this signaling route may offer a viable avenue for therapeutic management of lung injury linked to sepsis.

## Introduction
 Sepsis denotes a life-threatening syndrome characterized by whole-body inflammatory dysregulation triggered by microbial invasion, persists as the predominant cause of demise in ICU-managed individuals, with a global mortality rate exceeding 20% (Global Burden of Disease Study, 2017) [ – ]. While notable progress has been made in anti-infective strategies and organ support, sepsis-associated ALI and ARDS continue to be linked with high mortality rates—affecting over 60% of septic patients—highlights the insufficient understanding of endothelium-driven pathological mechanisms [ – ]. The pulmonary microvascular system, due to its distinct anatomical architecture characterized by dynamic interactions between alveolar epithelium and vascular endothelium, is a principal target for inflammatory mediators [ – ]. Pathological studies have demonstrated that disruption of the alveolar-capillary barrier—driven by neutrophil extracellular trap (NET) deposition and increased endothelial permeability—is closely associated with hypoxemia and fatal outcomes [ , ]. The route by which endothelial cells abandon vascular composure for an aberrant inflammatory profile remains only partially deciphered.
Emerging evidence indicates that N-Myc downstream-regulated gene 1 (NDRG1) functions as a critical regulator of endothelial inflammation [ ]. Although NDRG1 is highly expressed under hypoxic and inflammatory conditions, its dual roles in vascular biology remain controversial [ ]. Single-cell RNA sequencing has revealed markedly elevated NDRG1 mRNA expression in pulmonary endothelial cells compared to other vascular beds, suggesting a tissue-specific regulatory function. At the mechanistic stratum, NDRG1 exerts modulatory influence over the NF-κB axis through its molecular crosstalk with the nucleus-residing orphan receptor Nur77 [ , ] but whether this regulatory axis modulates sepsis-induced endothelial hyperactivation, particularly in the context of pulmonary microvascular barrier failure, has yet to be fully investigated (Figure. ). Additionally, clinical data suggest an association between NDRG1 genetic polymorphisms and ARDS susceptibility, yet preclinical validation of these associations in sepsis models remains limited.
 Nur77 expression is upshifted in ECs under inflammatory conditions, serving to negatively modulate NF-κB activity by upregulating IκBα. NDRG1 negatively regulates Nur77 expression, thereby reducing Nur77-mediated IκBα induction. As a result, IκBα levels decrease, leading to enhanced nuclear translocation and transcriptional activity of the NF-κB (p50/p65) complex. Triggering of NF-κB unleashes a transcriptional cascade encompassing cytokine surges, chemoattractant synthesis, endothelial tethering ligands, and thrombogenic mediators, all of which converge to amplify vascular perturbation in sepsis.
Alternative investigations have illuminated the indispensable contribution of PKM2-facilitated oxidative glycolytic metabolism within immune-activated macrophage populations [ , ]. In response to lipopolysaccharide stimulation, PKM2 engages STAT3-related transcriptional activity, resulting in the extracellular emergence of IL-1β and HMGB119. As a signature sentinel of the damage-associated molecular pattern ensemble, HMGB1 operates as a potent secondary alarm, amplifying cytokine outflow via engagement with Toll-like pattern sensors and RAGE conduits, thereby intensifying the pathophysiological momentum of sepsis [ – ]. Experimental studies have shown that inhibition of HMGB1 or blockade of PKM2 effectively attenuates sepsis-related inflammatory damage [ , ]. Our previous research, based on a sepsis model in mice established through cecal ligation and puncture, Elevated abundance of NDRG1 was discerned within vascular endothelial compartments of the lung, exhibiting a concordant rise alongside PKM2 enzymatic dynamics. Endothelial-specific knockout of NDRG1 significantly reduced PKM2 enzymatic activity along with the expression of interleukin-1β and interleukin-6, implying that NDRG1 may regulate downstream pro-inflammatory cascades through modulation of PKM2 activation.
Recognized for their transcriptional governance of inflammatory mediators, STAT3 and NF-κB collectively define a core signaling axis underpinning the cytokine storm observed in septic progression [ , , ]. Given the documented association between NDRG1 and NF-κB, along with evidence that PKM2 regulates STAT3 activation, we propose a mechanistic hypothesis: NDRG1 modulates pulmonary endothelial injury in sepsis by regulating PKM2 activation and thereby influencing the STAT3/NF-κB signaling axis. Elucidating this mechanism may provide novel insights into the pathogenesis of septic lung injury and establish a theoretical foundation for the development of targeted therapeutic interventions.

## Methods and materials

## Animals and interventional groups
Male C57BL/6 mice (SPF grade, 6–8 weeks old, 20–25 g) were obtained from Shanghai SLAC Laboratory Animal Co., Ltd. with the owner’s informed consent and housed in the SPF-grade animal facility at Fudan University under controlled environmental conditions (temperature 22 ± 2 °C, relative humidity 55 ± 10%, and a 12 h light/dark cycle).This study was approved by the Institutional Animal Welfare and Ethics Committee of the Laboratory Animal Center of Fudan University (Approval No. 2025-HSYY-159). A total of six groups were formed by random assignment, each consisting of four mice (n = 4): Sham, CLP, NDRG1-KO-Sham, NDRG1-KO-CLP, WT-CLP + PKM2 inhibitor (Shikonin), and NDRG1-KO-CLP + PKM2 agonist (TEPP-46). Sepsis was provoked via CLP surgery, with pulmonary tissue collected 48 h thereafter.

## CLP surgery and treatment
To model sepsis, the cecum was ligated and punctured while the animal was under anesthesia. Sham mice underwent laparotomy without ligation. Mice received subcutaneous saline for resuscitation and were monitored for sepsis symptoms. PKM2 inhibitors (Shikonin, 2 mg/kg) or agonists (TEPP-46, 10 mg/kg) were administered intraperitoneally at 0 h and 24 h post-CLP.

## Anesthesia​
All surgical procedures were performed under inhalational anesthesia.Mice were initially anesthetized in an induction chamber with 2–3% isoflurane delivered in oxygen (flow rate 1–2 L/min). After loss of consciousness, animals were transferred to a heated surgical platform and maintained on 1.5–2% isoflurane via a nose cone. Adequate anesthetic depth was confirmed by the absence of a withdrawal reflex to a toe pinch, ensuring a lack of sensation throughout the procedure. Mice were returned to their home cages only after full recovery of spontaneous activity.

## Tissue collection and processing
Lung samples were immersed in 4% paraformaldehyde for tissue staining or immediately cryopreserved for molecular evaluations. All samples were processed under sterile conditions.

## qPCR and Western blotting
RNA specimens were isolated via TRIzol reagent, subsequently converted into complementary DNA. Quantitative amplification was executed employing SYBR Green chemistry, with transcript abundance calibrated against GAPDH through the 2^−ΔΔCt algorithmic framework. Protein extracts were electrophoretically separated on SDS-containing gels and immunoblotted with primary antibodies directed against NDRG1, phospho-NF-κB, and GAPDH. Densitometry was performed using Image Lab software.

## Immunofluorescence and H&E staining
Paraffin-embedded lung Sect. (5 μm) were processed for antigen retrieval and stained with anti-NDRG1 and anti-CD31, followed by Alexa Fluor-labeled secondary antibodies. Nuclei were counterstained with DAPI. Confocal imaging was performed using the Zeiss LSM 900 microscope. For H&E, sections were stained and evaluated for lung pathology.

## Intraperitoneal injection
Drug solutions were prepared in saline and administered at a site 0.5 cm lateral to the lower abdominal midline. Mice were monitored for adverse effects post-injection.

## Statistical analysis
Numerical outcomes are presented as arithmetic mean accompanied by SEM. Intergroup variance was evaluated through dual-factor ANOVA, followed by Tukey-adjusted post hoc stratification. Kaplan–Meier curves were generated and compared using the log-rank approach; statistical relevance was inferred at thresholds below 0.05.

## Results

## Sepsis induces severe pulmonary inflammation and endothelial injury in mice
An acute lung injury model associated with sepsis was generated through cecal ligation and puncture (CLP). At 48 h post-surgery, lungs from CLP mice showed marked congestion, hemorrhagic spots, increased volume, and irregular margins (Fig.  B), while sham lungs appeared smooth and pink (Fig.  A). H&E staining revealed well-preserved alveolar structures in sham mice (Fig.  C), whereas CLP lungs exhibited alveolar collapse, thickened septa, intense inflammatory infiltration, and red blood cell extravasation—hallmarks of acute lung injury (Fig.  D).
 To confirm these changes at the molecular level, qPCR was performed. Notable increases in IL-8, TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β transcripts were detected in lungs following CLP intervention (Fig.  E). Unrestricted transcription of intercellular adhesion molecules (VCAM-1, ICAM-1) was observed within the endothelial lining, in contrast to the pronounced attenuation of VE-cadherin abundance (Fig.  F). Together, these findings confirm that CLP induces a robust inflammatory response and endothelial barrier disruption, establishing a reliable model for investigating septic lung injury.

## NDRG1 is significantly upregulated in septic lung injury and localizes to pulmonary endothelial cells
To elucidate the involvement of NDRG1 in lung impairment triggered by sepsis, its distribution pattern and transcriptional abundance were examined in mice subjected to CLP intervention. qPCR and Western blot showed significantly elevated NDRG1 mRNA and protein levels in CLP lungs compared to sham controls (Fig.  A–C). Dual immunofluorescence with CD31 revealed low baseline NDRG1 in sham lungs, while CLP lungs displayed strong NDRG1 signals co-localized with endothelial cells (Fig.  D). These results indicate that NDRG1 is transcriptionally upregulated and predominantly expressed in pulmonary endothelial cells during sepsis, supporting its potential role in endothelial-mediated lung injury.
 

## Loss of NDRG1 relieves alveolar structural compromise and cytokine surge in septic mouse model
To delineate the functional relevance of NDRG1 in sepsis-associated pulmonary insult, both gene-deficient (KO) and wild-type (WT) murine models underwent cecal ligation and puncture intervention. At 48 h, WT-CLP mice showed typical sepsis symptoms and severe lung damage, including congestion, hemorrhage, and exudation (Fig.  A). In contrast, NDRG1-KO mice displayed milder symptoms and better lung appearance. Histology revealed structural collapse, thickened septa, and inflammatory infiltration in WT lungs (Fig.  B), while NDRG1-KO lungs had preserved architecture and reduced inflammation (Fig.  C).
 Transcript quantification revealed attenuated signals of TNF-α, VCAM-1, IL-6, ICAM-1, IL-1β, and IL-8, alongside an elevation in VE-cadherin mRNA, within pulmonary tissues of NDRG1-deficient mice (Fig.  D–E). These data indicate that NDRG1 promotes pulmonary inflammation and endothelial damage during sepsis, while its deletion provides protective effects.

## NDRG1 promotes inflammatory lung injury via PKM2-Dependent signaling pathways
To determine whether NDRG1 mediates pulmonary inflammation through metabolic reprogramming, we assessed glycolytic enzyme expression in lung tissues from septic mice. qPCR revealed that CLP significantly upregulated PKM2, LDHA, and PDK, and downregulated PDHC in WT mice, indicating a metabolic shift toward glycolysis (Fig.  A). In contrast, NDRG1-KO mice showed reduced expression of glycolytic genes and increased PDHC, suggesting that NDRG1 deficiency suppresses glycolytic reprogramming.
 Given that PKM2 functions both in glycolysis and as a nuclear regulator of STAT3 and NF-κB, we hypothesized it is a downstream effector of NDRG1. To test this, four experimental groups were established: WT-CLP, WT-CLP + PKM2 inhibitor (Shikonin), NDRG1-KO-CLP, and NDRG1-KO-CLP + PKM2 activator (TEPP-46). H&E staining showed severe alveolar collapse, hemorrhage, and inflammation in WT-CLP lungs (Fig.  B), while Shikonin treatment improved alveolar architecture and reduced inflammation (Fig.  C). NDRG1-KO lungs showed only mild injury (Fig.  D), but TEPP-46 treatment restored the severe phenotype, with worsened hemorrhage and infiltration (Fig.  E).
At the molecular level, WT-CLP lungs exhibited elevated TNF-α, IL-8, VCAM-1, IL-6, ICAM-1, and IL-1β, along with decreased VE-cadherin expression (Fig.  F–G). Shikonin reversed these changes, reducing cytokine levels and restoring VE-cadherin. Conversely, TEPP-46 reversed the protective molecular profile in NDRG1-KO lungs, increasing inflammation and reducing VE-cadherin (Fig.  F–G).
Western blot further confirmed that phosphorylation of STAT3 and NF-κB was markedly increased in WT-CLP lungs and reduced by Shikonin (Fig.  H–I). In NDRG1-KO mice, STAT3 and NF-κB phosphorylation was low under baseline conditions but was restored by TEPP-46 (Fig.  J–K), indicating that PKM2 is required for NDRG1-mediated activation of these inflammatory pathways.
Together, these results demonstrate that NDRG1 exacerbates septic lung injury by enhancing PKM2 activity, which activates STAT3 and NF-κB signaling and promotes cytokine production and endothelial disruption. The reversal of protective effects by PKM2 activation in NDRG1-KO mice supports the conclusion that the proinflammatory function of NDRG1 is PKM2-dependent. By implicating the NDRG1–PKM2–STAT3/NF-κB axis in the pathogenesis of septic lung injury, these findings point to a potential therapeutic avenue.

## Discussion
Focusing on septic injury to the lung, we delineated the regulatory landscape, biological contribution, and intracellular signaling behavior of NDRG1, a gene responsive to stress perturbations and implicated in acute inflammatory damage. NDRG1 expression was markedly elevated in the lungs of septic mice and primarily detected in pulmonary endothelial cells. Knockout of NDRG1 effectively alleviated alveolar structural damage, reduced inflammatory cell infiltration and hemorrhage, and mitigated pathological lung injury. At the molecular level, NDRG1 deficiency markedly curtailed the expression of inflammatory effectors (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α, IL-8) and adhesion-related molecules (VCAM-1, ICAM-1), while restoring VE-cadherin expression [ ], highlighting the involvement of NDRG1 in compromising vascular barrier integrity during sepsis.
Mechanistically, we demonstrated that NDRG1 promotes inflammatory signaling by activating PKM2 and its downstream effectors STAT3 and NF-κB. Pharmacological inhibition of PKM2 using Shikonin in wild-type mice reversed the pro-inflammatory effects of NDRG1, whereas activation of PKM2 with TEPP-46 in NDRG1-deficient mice restored the inflammatory phenotype. These findings provide strong evidence that NDRG1 exerts its effects in a PKM2-dependent manner. Importantly, this study elucidates the NDRG1–PKM2–STAT3/NF-κB axis as a novel signaling pathway contributing to lung inflammation and endothelial damage in sepsis, offering mechanistic insights that extend beyond previous research focused solely on PKM2 expression or function.
NDRG1 has long been recognized for its involvement in cellular stress responses, autophagy, and metabolic regulation [ – ]. Recent studies have also suggested its role in inflammatory diseases; however, its function in septic lung injury remained unclear. Our findings establish NDRG1 as an upstream modulator of PKM2, driving the amplification of inflammatory responses and endothelial dysfunction. This expands the conceptual framework of NDRG1 in the context of immunometabolic regulation and inflammatory signaling.
Moreover, by employing in vivo pharmacological modulation of PKM2, we verified the functional dependency of NDRG1’s inflammatory effects on PKM2 activity. Compared to prior studies that centered on PKM2 alone, our work delineates a complete pathogenic pathway, advancing the understanding of metabolic-inflammation crosstalk in lung injury. These insights highlight the translational relevance of targeting the NDRG1–PKM2 signaling axis in clinical settings.
ALI and ARDS resulting from sepsis remain critical challenges in intensive care, with high mortality largely attributable to the lack of targeted therapeutic strategies [ , , ]. Our findings suggest that NDRG1 is a potent inflammatory driver and potential therapeutic target, particularly given its prominent expression in pulmonary endothelial cells and the protective effects observed upon its deletion. Additionally, PKM2 emerges as a key effector downstream of NDRG1, bridging metabolism and inflammatory signaling. Small-molecule PKM2 inhibitors such as Shikonin, already shown to possess anti-inflammatory potential in other disease models [ ], may hold promise for intervening in NDRG1-mediated inflammatory lung injury.
While the data offer valuable contributions, it is important to reflect on a number of unresolved limitations. First, our study focused on the acute phase (48 h) of sepsis, and it remains uncertain whether the NDRG1–PKM2 pathway is functionally relevant in the later immunosuppressive phase. Second, the molecular mechanisms through which NDRG1 activates PKM2—such as potential effects on post-translational modifications, conformational changes, or nuclear translocation—require further investigation. Third, our study did not address cell type-specific roles; whether NDRG1 regulates PKM2 in a cell-specific manner warrants additional functional validation. Finally, as STAT3 and NF-κB are convergent nodes of multiple signaling pathways, the precise contribution of PKM2 to their activation in this setting has yet to be conclusively delineated.
Collectively, our findings designate NDRG1 as a pivotal amplifier of inflammatory processes in septic pulmonary pathology, functioning via PKM2-driven engagement of the STAT3 and NF-κB cascades. Such outcomes unravel a previously concealed signaling dimension in septic lung derangement, positioning the NDRG1–PKM2 conduit as a feasible node for interventional exploitation against ALI and ARDS.

## Conclusion
Our study establishes the NDRG1-PKM2-STAT3/NF-κB axis as a pivotal driver and a novel therapeutic target in sepsis-induced acute lung injury. Using a CLP-induced sepsis model, we demonstrate that NDRG1, markedly upregulated in the pulmonary endothelium, acts as a critical inflammatory amplifier by activating the metabolic regulator PKM2. This activation, in turn, synergistically enhances the transcriptional activity of both STAT3 and NF-κB, thereby fueling a sustained inflammatory cascade, disrupting endothelial barrier integrity, and culminating in severe alveolar damage. The protective effects achieved through either genetic ablation of Ndrg1 or pharmacological inhibition of PKM2—marked by attenuated inflammatory signaling, reduced immune infiltration, and preserved lung architecture—conclusively validate this axis not merely as an associative pathway but as a functional and therapeutically targetable hub in septic pulmonary pathology.
The significance of this work is twofold. First, it bridges a critical knowledge gap by defining PKM2 as the essential molecular link through which NDRG1 exerts its pro-inflammatory effects, moving beyond correlation to mechanism. Second, it introduces a “metabo-inflammatory” paradigm in sepsis, positioning the NDRG1-PKM2 axis as a key node where cellular metabolism directly dictates the intensity of the inflammatory transcriptional response. From a translational perspective, directly targeting STAT3 or NF-κB is problematic as they are essential for normal immunity, and broad inhibition risks systemic immunosuppression. In contrast, targeting the upstream NDRG1-PKM2 axis offers a more selective strategy. It aims to precisely dampen the pathological inflammatory cascade activated in sepsis while sparing essential immune functions, thereby presenting a promising therapeutic avenue that may avoid global immunosuppression.
We acknowledge two primary limitations that also define clear directions for future research. First, while our in vivo data robustly establish the functional dependency, the precise molecular mechanism of NDRG1-mediated PKM2 activation (e.g., via direct interaction or post-translational modification) warrants further dissection in endothelial-specific cellular models. Second, the relative contribution of PKM2-dependent versus PKM2-independent signaling downstream of NDRG1 merits additional investigation. These questions, however, do not detract from the core finding but rather chart a roadmap for its deeper understanding.
In conclusion, we have elucidated a previously unrecognized signaling axis that is central to the pathogenesis of septic lung injury. Our findings not only provide a novel framework for understanding the dysregulated inflammation in ALI/ARDS but also concretely propose the NDRG1-PKM2 interface as a viable target for therapeutic development. Future efforts to develop specific inhibitors of this interaction and to validate its relevance in human sepsis samples are strongly justified by the compelling preclinical evidence presented here.
