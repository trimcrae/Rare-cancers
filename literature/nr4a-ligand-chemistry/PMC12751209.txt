## Abstract
During placenta development, trophoblasts dynamically adapt to hypoxic microenvironments to orchestrate physiological functions and morphogenesis. Early gestation establishes a transient hypoxic niche essential for trophoblast stem cell (TSC) proliferation and differentiation. This niche undergoes a transition to physiological reoxygenation following the remodeling of uterine spiral arteries during gestational weeks 10 to 12, establishing a spatiotemporal oxygen gradient critical for placental development. Disrupted hypoxia sensing—exemplified by deficient hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF) signaling—leads to placental maldevelopment, while sustained HIF activation drives preeclampsia-like pathology, underscoring the delicate equilibrium of oxygen-responsive mechanisms. Emerging evidence highlights hypoxia-centered signaling cascades, epigenetic reprogramming, and metabolic plasticity as pivotal regulators of trophoblast adaptation. Deciphering these molecular networks not only elucidates the pathogenesis of gestational complications but also holds potential for efficient therapies to restore placental homeostasis.
Keywords: Hypoxia, Trophoblast, Hypoxia-inducible factors, Metabolism, Preeclampsia

## Background
Human trophoblast subpopulations originate from human trophoblast stem cells (hTSCs) and differentiate into cytotrophoblasts (CTBs), syncytiotrophoblasts (STBs), and extravillous trophoblasts (EVTs) [ ]. During early gestation (< 10 weeks), CTBs form endovascular plugs that temporarily occlude maternal spiral arteries, creating a transient hypoxic microenvironment (pO₂ < 20 mmHg) essential for placental morphogenesis [ ]. As pregnancy progresses, these plugs dissolve, permitting maternal blood perfusion into the intervillous space and shifting trophoblasts from a hypoxic to an oxygen-rich environment (pO₂ ~ 40–60 mmHg), a transition critical for optimal placental function [ ].
Murine models with genetic ablation of Hif-1α and Hif-2α exhibit severe placental vascular defects, highlighting the indispensable and non-redundant roles of hypoxia-sensing pathways [ , ]. However, under chronic hypoxia, sustained HIF-1α activation promotes excessive trophoblast proliferation, impairs EVT invasion, and disrupts vascular remodeling, predisposing to pregnancy complications such as preeclampsia (PE) [ , ]. This duality underscores the physiological necessity of HIF-mediated angiogenesis while also illustrating the pathological consequences of its dysregulation. Before the establishment of functional maternofetal circulation, trophoblasts undergo extensive remodeling under a physiological low-oxygen environment, requiring precise coordination of proliferation, differentiation, and metabolic reprogramming. Emerging evidence highlights epigenetic regulation and metabolic reprogramming as critical factors in trophoblast adaptation to hypoxia, shaping cellular fate and function through distinct yet complementary mechanisms [ – ]. Understanding how oxygen tension orchestrates trophoblast invasion and vascular remodeling is critical for developing targeted therapeutic interventions.
Placental homeostasis is maintained through a dynamic equilibrium across interdependent regulatory axes: controlled invasion, which balances EVT-mediated physiological remodeling against tumor-like metastasis [ ]; oxygen sensing, which adapts to gestational stage-specific hypoxia [ ]; and immune tolerance, which suppresses maternal immune rejection while preserving antimicrobial defense [ ]. Perturbations in genomic stability, signaling networks, or epigenetic regulation can destabilize this equilibrium, emphasizing the need to delineate the molecular thresholds that distinguish physiological adaptation from pathological dysfunction in gestational disorders.
This review consolidates current advances in molecular mechanisms governing trophoblast hypoxic adaptation, their dysregulation in placenta development, and their translational implications for therapeutic targeting.

## Hypoxia microenvironment

## HIFs
The HIF family serves as the central transcriptional machinery governing cellular adaptation to oxygen deprivation. Functional HIF complexes comprise an oxygen-sensitive α subunit (HIF-1α, HIF-2α, or HIF-3α) and a constitutively expressed β subunit (HIF-1β) [ ]. While all α isoforms share conserved N-terminal basic helix-loop-helix (bHLH) and Per-Arnt-Sim (PAS) domains essential for dimerization and DNA binding, their functional divergence arises from C-terminal structural variations [ , ]. HIF-1α and HIF-2α possess transactivation domains (TADs) that recruit coactivators, such as p300/CREB-binding protein (p300/CBP) to drive hypoxia-responsive gene expression, whereas HIF-3α lacks TADs and acts as a dominant-negative regulator by competitively binding hypoxia-response elements (HREs) [ ].
In human trophoblasts, both HIF-1α and HIF-2α are expressed, but their temporal activation patterns reflect distinct roles. HIF-1α, ubiquitously expressed and rapidly induced under acute hypoxia (< 24 h), orchestrates early adaptive responses by upregulating glycolysis (glucose transporter 1, GLUT1; lactate dehydrogenase A, LDHA) and angiogenesis (vascular endothelial growth factor, VEGF) [ ]. In contrast, HIF-2α exhibits tissue-specific expression in the placenta and mediates sustained adaptation to chronic hypoxia (> 48 h) through erythropoietin (EPO) and vascular stabilization (VEGFA) [ ]. Murine models highlight their non-redundant functions: Hif-1a knockout mice die at embryonic day E10.5 with significant defects in placental development, vascular defects, and cardiac malformations [ ], while Hif-2α deficiency results in strain-dependent embryonic lethality or multi-organ dysfunction, accompanied by increased generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and linked to impaired systemic development [ , ].
HIF-1 functions as the master transcriptional responder to cellular oxygen availability, and its activity is primarily regulated at the protein level via oxygen-dependent post-translational hydroxylation. Under normoxic conditions, HIF-α subunits undergo rapid degradation through prolyl hydroxylation at conserved residues (Pro402 and Pro564 in HIF-1α), catalyzed by the prolyl hydroxylase domain (PHD) enzymes—PHD1 (EGLN2), PHD2 (EGLN1), and PHD3 (EGLN3). These hydroxylation events enable recognition by the von Hippel–Lindau (pVHL) E3 ubiquitin ligase complex, which targets HIF-α for proteasomal degradation [ – ]. Under hypoxic conditions, the enzymatic activity of PHDs is inhibited due to limited oxygen availability, resulting in the stabilization and accumulation of HIF-α proteins. Stabilized HIF-α translocates into the nucleus, dimerizes with constitutively expressed HIF-1β, binds HREs, and activates the transcription of hypoxia-responsive genes.
Concurrently, factor-inhibiting HIF (FIH) hydroxylates Asn803 in the C-terminal transactivation domain (C-TAD) of HIFα, blocking p300/CBP recruitment and transcriptional activation. These hydroxylation reactions are strictly oxygen-dependent, requiring molecular O2, α-ketoglutarate, and iron as cofactors—a biochemical link that directly couples HIF stability to cellular oxygen tension [ , ]. Placental EGLN1/PHD2 expression increases and peaks at 11–12 weeks, while HIF-1α remains elevated despite rising oxygen levels. The delayed PHD response might allow sustained HIF-1α activity by creating a hypoxia-permissive niche, possibly due to placental isoforms with reduced oxygen sensitivity [ ].

## Important axis and their potential roles in trophoblast behavior
While HIF activation initiates through canonical oxygen-sensing mechanisms—PHD/FIH inactivation enabling HIF-α/β dimerization and HRE-driven transcription—its biological impact is profoundly shaped by reciprocal regulation with TGF-β, mTOR, PI3K/AKT, and NOTCH pathways (Fig.  ).
 TGF-β exemplifies this bidirectional interplay: it stabilizes HIF-1α protein while HIF-1α transactivates TGF-β targets like plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) and connective tissue growth factor (CTGF), synergistically driving epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) and fibrotic remodeling [ , ]. Paradoxically, HIF-1α attenuates TGF-β signaling via Smad3 inhibition (through Smad7 or PP2A), creating a self-limiting loop to prevent excessive matrix deposition [ ]. This yin-yang relationship is context-dependent, as TGF-β can reciprocally suppress HIF targets in certain tissues—a regulatory dance co-opted in placental pathologies where dysregulated TGF-β/HIF crosstalk underlies aberrant trophoblast invasiveness [ ]. Extracellular matrix (ECM) dynamics complete this adaptive symphony, with HIF-inducible matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and lysyl oxidase (LOX) directing trophoblast invasion and vascular stabilization [ , ]. MMP-2/9-mediated basement membrane degradation enables extravillous trophoblast infiltration, while LOX crosslinks collagen fibrils to fortify nascent vasculature—a process hijacked in placenta accreta where dysregulated TGF-β1/MMP-2 synergy drives pathological invasiveness [ , ]. The conservation of LOX-mediated ECM remodeling across hypoxic tumors and placental tissues underscores the evolutionary optimization of HIF-driven survival mechanisms, while also revealing shared vulnerabilities exploitable for therapeutic intervention.
Metabolic integration emerges through mTORC1, which amplifies HIF-1α synthesis via STAT3-dependent transcription and 4E-BP1/S6K1-mediated translation [ ]. Additionally, AKT signaling contributes to HIF-1α stabilization by inhibiting its proteasomal degradation, thereby enhancing overall HIF-1α activity [ ]. In TSC2-deficient models, mTORC1 hyperactivity elevates HIF-1α activity tenfold, hyperactivating glycolytic enzymes like PDK1 to enforce Warburg metabolism [ ]. However, severe hypoxia triggers AMPK-mediated mTOR suppression, curbing HIF activity to prevent energetic collapse—a fail-safe mechanism critical for trophoblast survival [ ]. Simultaneously, HIF coordinates cellular survival strategies via bifunctional targets like BNIP3, which executes mitophagy to purge damaged mitochondria while paradoxically conferring apoptotic resistance [ ]. The fragility of this balance is evident in PE and intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR), where BNIP3 downregulation correlates with heightened trophoblast vulnerability to oxidative stress [ ].
Parallel interactions with mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) and Wnt pathways refine hypoxic responses. ERK1/2 phosphorylates HIF-1α to boost its transcriptional potency, synergizing with HIF in angiogenesis and stress adaptation [ ]. Wnt/β-catenin signaling intersects with HIF through shared targets like c-Myc and cyclin D1, supporting stemness and directional invasion [ ]. Notch completes this regulatory web: HIF-1α upregulates Notch1 and its ligand DLL4, enhancing MMP2/9-driven invasiveness, while Notch liberates HIF-1α from FIH-1 inhibition—a mutual reinforcement loop hijacked in PE placentas with excessive vascular remodeling [ , ]. Together, these multilayered signaling interactions do not merely regulate the magnitude of HIF activation—they actively sculpt its functional output. Once stabilized, HIF-1α orchestrates an extensive transcriptional program that executes hypoxia adaptation across vascular, metabolic, and structural dimensions of placental biology.

## Trophoblast remodeling

## Trophoblast stem cell dynamics: hypoxic regulation of stemness and differentiation
In mice, trophoblast stem cells (mTSCs) serve as the primary cellular reservoir during early placental development (embryonic day 6.5), characterized by expression of stemness markers Cdx2, Esrrβ, and Eomes, which support trophoblast expansion [ ]. Exposure to 2% oxygen together with fibroblast growth factor 4 (Fgf4) maximizes mTSC proliferation. Upon FGF4 withdrawal, mTSCs differentiate into trophoblast giant cells (TGCs), relieving proliferation constraints [ ]. Unlike TSCs, TGCs exhibit limited proliferative capacity but persist throughout placental lifespan, maintaining Cdx2 expression while losing Esrrβ/Eomes. This poised state enables their subsequent differentiation to meet developmental demands.
In contrast, human TSCs (hTSCs) lack the FGF4-dependent regulatory mechanism observed in mice. The hypoxic niche (2% O₂) sustains hTSC expansion rather than differentiation during early gestation—a critical adaptation for rapid trophoblast proliferation. Under hypoxia (2% O₂), reduced expression of the transcription factor GCM1 leads to downregulation of CDKN1C, resulting in loss of contact inhibition and enhanced self-renewal of hTSCs, but impaired differentiation into STBs and EVTs. Correspondingly, genes linked to EVT migration (ITGA1) and STB function (OVOL1, chorionic gonadotropin subunit genes) show diminished expression under 2% O₂ [ ]. Another stemness regulator, decorin, also safeguards the undifferentiated state of hTSCs; its downregulation induces premature differentiation and compromises placental morphogenesis [ ]. Paternally expressed gene 10 (PEG10), a retrotransposon-derived imprinted gene, dynamically responds to oxygen gradients at the maternal–fetal interface. Its expression remains low in early hypoxic phases but surges at gestational week 11 to 12, preceding placental vascularization (gestational week 12 to 13). Functional studies reveal PEG10’s indispensable role: PEG10-/- mice exhibit embryonic lethality from placentation failure [ , ], while 1% O₂ exposure in hTSC models downregulates PEG10 via impaired SMAD-binding element activation [ ]. This oxygen-sensitive regulation intersects with TGF-β signaling to modulate cytotrophoblast differentiation, potentially contributing to early-onset preeclampsia (EOPE) pathogenesis through disrupted syncytialization (Fig.  A).
 Importantly, while human and mouse TSCs share certain functional parallels, they differ substantially in proliferative mechanisms, differentiation cues, signaling responses, and transcriptional dependencies [ , ]. Recent studies show that transcription factors essential for human TSC proliferation and differentiation (e.g., DLX3, GCM1) are not critical in mice, underscoring species-specific functional divergence [ ]. Thus, caution is warranted when extrapolating findings across model systems.

## Syncytialization
Trophoblast syncytialization is a differentiation cascade where CTBs progressively fuse into multinucleated STBs to establish the placental barrier. The process initiates with cytoskeletal reorganization and cell cycle withdrawal, driven by suppression of Cyclin B/CDK1 complexes that enforce G2/M phase arrest—a prerequisite for fusion competence [ , ]. Concurrently, argininosuccinate synthase 1 (ASS1) expression sustains BeWo cell proliferation under standard culture conditions (20% O₂), whereas its hypoxia-induced depletion (3% O₂) may prime differentiation in vitro [ ]. Subsequent membrane reconstitution involves Syncytin-1 (ERVW-1) and Syncytin-2 (ERVFRD-1), endogenous retroviral envelope proteins that orchestrate calcium-dependent phosphatidylserine externalization and intercellular adhesion [ ]. Physiological oxygen levels (≈6% O₂) enhance Syncytin-1 and GCMa transcription, thereby promoting syncytialization [ ]. Terminal cytoplasmic fusion and organelle integration require endoplasmic reticulum stress (ERS) activating the IRE1α–XBP1 pathway, which upregulates autophagy mediators (LC3-II/Beclin-1) to clear misfolded proteins [ ]. Paradoxically, H/R induced excessive ERS elevates HtrA4 while suppressing Syncytin-2 and β-hCG, impairing syncytialization and precipitating EOPE [ ].
Oxygen tension exerts biphasic effects: physiological hypoxia (6% O₂) promotes differentiation, whereas extreme hypoxia (1% O₂) suppresses Syncytin-1, reduces hCG secretion [ , ], and reactivates stemness programs (TEA domain transcription factor 4, TEAD4) [ ]. Although HIF-2α levels naturally rise during syncytialization, excessive accumulation under 2% O₂ impairs trophoblast fusion. Notably, HIF-2α knockdown restores BeWo syncytialization by upregulating PIGF [ ] (Fig.  B).

## EVT differentiation, invasion and spiral arteries remodeling
The differentiation and invasive capacity of EVTs are orchestrated by a hypoxia-responsive regulatory network. EVT lineage commitment is initiated by NOTUM upregulation, which is transcriptionally activated by EPAS1 (HIF-2α). This process is antagonized by canonical WNT signaling, which suppresses TSC differentiation into EVTs [ ]. Concomitantly, estrogen-mediated downregulation of HIF-1α primes a pro-invasive transcriptional program under hypoxia condition (3% O2), marked by elevated expression of ITGA1 [ ].
Hypoxia exerts stage-specific effects on EVT invasiveness. In EVTs isolated from early gestation placentas (7–10 weeks) and in JAR cell models, 2% O₂ upregulates Lipocalin-2 (LCN2), which enhances MMP-9 activity and promotes invasion [ ]. These proteases degrade ECM components, with their activity further modulated by FGF2 (enhancing TIMP1) and CLDN4, FUT4 (enhancing MMP-9) [ , ]. Hypoxia-mimic-inducible PLAC8 activates CDC42/RAC1 GTPases in interstitial EVTs (iEVTs), synergizing with Angiopoietin-2/Tie2-integrin signaling to amplify JNK/c-JUN-driven MMP-2/9 transcription [ , ]. However, hypoxia also induces negative regulators: Sustained HIF-1α accumulation under hypoxia (< 2% O2) triggers SPOP-mediated PI3K/AKT/GSK3β pathway suppression, impairing EVT migration [ ]. SRC-3 (a coactivator of AKT/mTOR) is downregulated via HIF-1α, and PEDF is upregulated under 1% O2 conditions, promoting apoptosis. Clinical relevance emerges in PE where dysregulated HIF-1α-SRC-3 axis and elevated PEDF [ , ]. PE placentas also exhibit attenuated uPAR signaling—a HIF-1α target critical for EVT invasion [ ]. These findings underscore the delicate balance between pro- and anti-invasive hypoxia responses, disruption of which underlies placental maladaptation (Fig.  C).

## Epigenetic modifications in hypoxia adaptation

## DNA/RNA methylation
DNA methylation is a central epigenetic mechanism regulating gene expression through the addition of methyl groups to CpG islands, primarily within promoter regions [ ]. In the context of placental development, hypoxia acts as a dynamic regulator of DNA methylation, shaping trophoblast behavior and pregnancy outcomes through both gene activation and silencing.
In early placental development, hypoxia-induced nuclear export of ALKBH5 promotes demethylation of m6A-modified SMAD1/5 transcripts, enhancing their translation. This drives the expression of MMP9 and ITGA1, both crucial for trophoblast invasion [ ]. Conversely, in PE, hypermethylation of the PAX3 promoter, mediated by the DIAPH2-AS1/LSD1/DNMT1 axis, leads to reduced PAX3 expression and impaired EVT function, contributing to defective spiral artery remodeling [ ]. Importantly, hypoxia does not only affect the methylation of downstream targets, but also alters methylation within HIF genes themselves. In preterm PE placentas, CpG8 in the HIF-1α promoter is slightly hypomethylated, while cg22891070 in HIF-3α is significantly hypermethylated, linking DNA methylation to the dysregulation of oxygen-sensing machinery [ ].
As gestation progresses, maternal oxidative stress levels rise from mid- to late pregnancy, accompanied by enhanced placental antioxidant defenses [ ]. This shift is accompanied by global epigenetic remodeling, including changes in histone methylation patterns such as H3K9me1, H3K9me2, and H3K9me3 in trophoblasts [ ]. Genome-wide methylation profiling reveals that this hypoxia-to-oxidative-stress transition leads to differentially methylated regions (DMRs) in genes like ANKRD37, a HIF-responsive target, and PFKFB3, a key regulator of glycolytic flux—highlighting the intersection of oxygen sensing and metabolic control [ ]. Interestingly, not all methylation changes serve adaptive purposes. For example, hypomethylation of the MASPIN promoter in early pregnancy impairs EVT migration, underscoring how aberrant methylation may shift the balance from physiological adaptation to pathological dysfunction [ ]. From a translational perspective, DNA methylation patterns in cell-free DNA (cfDNA) offer promise as non-invasive early biomarkers of PE. DMRs predictive of PE can be detected as early as 12 weeks of gestation, and significant differences exist between early and late pregnancy methylation profiles. Predictive models based on cfDNA methylation have achieved 71.4% sensitivity and 100% specificity, emphasizing the diagnostic potential of methylation signatures [ ] (Fig.  A).
 

## Histone modification
Histone post-translational modifications—including acetylation, methylation, and lactylation—act as dynamic regulators of chromatin architecture, shaping transcriptional landscapes in response to environmental cues [ ]. In the placenta, oxygen availability is a major determinant of histone modification patterns, particularly at promoters of genes involved in inflammation, metabolism, and trophoblast differentiation.
Acute hypoxia transiently elevates global histone acetylation, largely due to increased acetyl-CoA production. In contrast, chronic hypoxia leads to global histone deacetylation, correlating with suppressed immune and growth signaling. Specifically, placental tissues under prolonged hypoxia show significant decreases in H3 acetylation at lysine residues K9, K14, K18, K27, and K56 [ ]. Reduced H3K27Ac and H3K9Ac at pro-inflammatory gene promoters (e.g., EGR1, cFOS, NUR77) is associated with HDAC1 recruitment and H3K9me3 enrichment, resulting in the epigenetic silencing of inflammatory pathways crucial for placental immune homeostasis [ ]. Hypoxia also influences trophoblast differentiation through HIF1α-mediated induction of MLL1, a histone H3K4 methyltransferase. MLL1 facilitates TEAD4 promoter methylation, thereby inhibiting Hippo signaling and suppressing cytotrophoblast syncytialization—a phenotype also observed in PE placentas [ ]. Beyond acetylation and methylation, hypoxia promotes histone lactylation: lactate, a glycolytic byproduct elevated under hypoxia, drives lactylation of fibrotic genes such as FN1 and SERPINE1, potentially contributing to fibrosis and placental maladaptation [ ].
Oxygen-sensitive histone-modifying enzymes further fine-tune this regulation. For instance, JMJD6, a Fe2⁺- and oxygen-dependent histone demethylase, exhibits reduced activity under hypoxia (3% O2), impairing its binding to the VHL promoter, leading to PE-like phenotypes in PHD2-inhibited mouse models [ ]. In parallel, PPARγ, a central regulator of lipid metabolism, recruits KMT5A to promote H4K20me deposition, thereby linking metabolic shifts with epigenetic responses to uteroplacental insufficiency (UPI)-induced hypoxia in mouse models [ ]. Recent studies demonstrate that overexpression of SETD1A and SMYD3 in human trophoblast cell lines enhances H3K4 methylation under severe hypoxia (1% O₂). These modifications likely contribute to hypoxia-driven epigenetic reprogramming and placental dysfunction, particularly in the pathogenesis of PE [ ] (Fig.  B).

## Non-coding RNAs
Non-coding RNAs (ncRNAs), including microRNAs (miRNAs), long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs), and circular RNAs (circRNAs), have emerged as pivotal modulators of placental adaptation to hypoxia. These transcripts regulate gene expression through post-transcriptional repression, chromatin remodeling, and scaffolding, and influence key placental processes such as trophoblast invasion, endothelial function, and metabolic homeostasis.
Among hypoxia-responsive miRNAs, miR-210 is the most extensively studied. It is upregulated by ~ 2.7-fold in PE placentas and regulates a broad spectrum of cellular programs, including mitochondrial function, cell cycle control, and apoptosis [ – ]. miR-150-5p inhibits EVT migration by targeting VEGF and MMP9 [ ], while extracellular vesicle–mediated transfer of exosomal miR-150-3p from hypoxic (1% O2) trophoblasts suppresses endothelial proliferation, migration, and angiogenesis by targeting CHPF [ ].
The competing endogenous RNA (ceRNA) hypothesis further explains how lncRNAs regulate hypoxia responses by sequestering miRNAs and indirectly controlling mRNA translation. Additionally, hypoxia-induced (0.5% O2) miR-218 represses FOXP1, which downregulates the lncRNA TUG1, thereby lifting suppression on TET3 and the DUSP family—an epigenetic cascade implicated in PE pathophysiology [ ]. Some lncRNAs and circRNAs exhibit dual or context-specific functions. MALAT1, for instance, protects against hypoxia-induced (2% O2) apoptosis by sponging miR-424 to maintain ERRγ and HSD17B1 expression, while also supporting trophoblast angiogenesis [ ]. circ_FURIN enhances trophoblast proliferation and invasion under hypoxia-mimicking condition by sponging miR-34a-5p and upregulating TFAP2A [ ].
Despite the growing body of evidence linking ncRNAs to hypoxia-driven placental remodeling, their direct mechanistic integration with canonical HIF-1α signaling remains limited. For example, miR-214-3p, upregulated through the HIF-1α/Twist1 axis, inhibits PlGF expression and trophoblast invasion [ ]. Similarly, miR-101-3p impairs proliferation by targeting WDR5, though direct transcriptional regulation by HIF-1α has not been established [ ]. Intriguingly, recent studies have identified urothelial carcinoma-associated 1 (UCA1) as a human-specific HIF-1α-regulated lncRNA that may bridge this mechanistic gap. UCA1 is upregulated in both placental tissues and maternal serum of PE patients and is transcriptionally activated by HIF-1α [ ]. Functionally, it supports trophoblast survival under hypoxia but disrupts maternal endothelial function, suggesting that UCA1 participates in a maladaptive, species-specific HIF-1α-driven regulatory loop underlying PE progression (Fig.  C).

## Metabolic reprogramming under hypoxia

## Glycolysis and glucose rewiring
Placental hypoxia in PE shifts trophoblast metabolism from oxidative phosphorylation to anaerobic glycolysis, as evidenced by increased lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) activity and PEPCK-mediated gluconeogenesis [ ]. In EOPE, transcriptomic profiling of 372 placentas reveals a homogeneous molecular signature dominated by hypoxia and HIF-1α activation. Glycolytic genes—particularly HK2—are upregulated and linked to suppression of autophagy [ ]. This Warburg-like phenotype limits oxygen dependence but compromises mitochondrial biogenesis and energy flexibility. Elevated glucose availability may in turn upregulate HIF-1α expression and influence angiogenic factor production in trophoblasts through the PKCβ/HIF-1α signaling axis, suggesting a potential feedback loop between hyperglycemia and hypoxia-driven metabolic reprogramming [ ]. Moreover, SORBS2, a hypoxia-induced RNA-binding protein, stabilizes HK2 mRNA to enhance trophoblast migration, positioning glycolytic enzymes as dual regulators of metabolism and motility [ ]. Excessive lactate production also impairs immune tolerance, as shown in recurrent pregnancy loss (RPL), where HIF-1α/SRC/LDHA signaling drives dysfunction—a phenotype reversible by monocarboxylate transporter 1 (MCT1) inhibition using AZD3965 in murine models [ ]. Concurrently, HIF activation rewires cellular metabolism by upregulating GLUT1 and LDHA, enforcing a glycolytic phenotype that prioritizes ATP yield over oxygen efficiency [ ]. This metabolic shift is further amplified by pyruvate dehydrogenase kinase 1 (PDK1)-mediated suppression of mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation, creating an oxygen-sparing state essential for cellular survival under hypoxia [ ]. Paradoxically, this conserved Warburg-like metabolism intersects with gestational pathology: diminished LDH activity in IUGR reflects failed metabolic adaptation, while fibroblast growth factor 15/19 (FGF15/19) leverages GLUT1 upregulation to counteract insulin resistance in gestational diabetes—a compensatory mechanism vulnerable to dysregulation [ ]. Interestingly, AMPK activation in PE models promotes GLUT3 translocation and inhibit gluconeogenesis, enhancing cell survival but reducing trophoblast invasiveness. AMPK knockout in mouse placentas leads to reduced glycogen deposition and trophoblast structural abnormalities, suggesting a novel AMPK–HIF axis that dynamically balances energy allocation between growth and invasion under hypoxia [ ] (Fig.  A).
 

## Glutamine metabolism
Decidual natural killer (dNK) cells rely on glutamine catabolism to maintain their activation state. Hypoxia disrupts α-ketoglutarate (α-KG) synthesis, impairing PHD2/EGLN1-mediated HIF-1α degradation, and locks dNK cells in a metabolically quiescent state. This suppresses secretion of trophoblast-supporting factors such as IGF-1 and GDF-15, ultimately impairing EVT invasion and vascular remodeling [ ]. Remarkably, α-KG supplementation restores dNK activity by reactivating α-KG–dependent histone demethylases (e.g., KDM6A), highlighting a potential therapeutic strategy to restore immune tolerance under hypoxia. Amino acid transport systems are crucial for maintaining metabolic and immune homeostasis at the maternal–fetal interface. Specifically, SNATs (SLC38 family), including SNAT1, SNAT2, and SNAT4, regulate the uptake of non-essential amino acids like glutamine into placental cells [ ]. Notably, decreased SLC38A4 expression is consistently observed in early-onset IUGR, regardless of its cause, indicating impaired amino acid transport may be a common metabolic bottleneck in placental insufficiency across hypoxic disorders [ ] (Fig.  B).

## Lipid metabolism
Trophoblasts under hypoxia exhibit a shift toward lipid storage rather than utilization. Simulated hypoxia via oxyquinoline in BeWo cells decreases mitochondrial mass and downregulates PPARGC1B, a key regulator of fatty acid oxidation, while concurrently upregulating BNIP3 and PDK1, indicating coordinated suppression of mitochondrial lipid utilization [ ]. Hypoxia (< 1% O2) promotes lipid droplet accumulation and PLIN2 expression, potentially as a cytoprotective mechanism against apoptosis [ ]. However, this adaptation may impair lipid transport to the fetus, especially during early human pregnancy when GPAT3 and AGPAT2 expression is upregulated to synthesize and store lipids for rapid placental growth [ ]. In contrast, hypoxia-induced lipid peroxidation generates reactive aldehydes (e.g., 4-oxononenal) that covalently modify eNOS, converting it into a superoxide-producing enzyme and intensifying oxidative stress in PE [ ]. In gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM), this is exacerbated by mitochondrial dysfunction, LOX-1 overexpression, and Lysophosphatidylcholine (LPC) accumulation [ ]. In PE placentas, downregulation of PPARγ and Nrf2 increases ferroptosis susceptibility. Pharmacological PPARγ activation (e.g., rosiglitazone) protects against ferroptosis via Nrf2/SREBP1 signaling [ ], while PLA2G6 deficiency amplifies GPX4 inhibition-induced cell death [ ] (Fig.  C).

## Therapeutic perspectives: targeting hypoxic nodes
Emerging preclinical evidence highlights the substantial therapeutic potential of modulating hypoxic signaling cascades in gestational disorders. Albers et al. were the first to demonstrate that placental, trophoblast-specific overexpression of Hif-1α in pregnant mice induces hypertension, proteinuria, IUGR, reduced placental weight, and structural placental abnormalities, accompanied by glomerular endotheliosis—all hallmarks of early-onset preeclampsia [ ]. Confirmation of placental Hif-1α overexpression as a causal factor leading to preeclampsia pathogenesis was provided by Sallais et al. using Phd2 placental, junctional zone-specific knockout mice, which display severe placental dysplasia, maternal hypertension, and proteinuria, hallmark attributes of early onset preeclampsia. These phenotypes can be reversed by the HIF inhibitor acriflavine, which restores placental architecture and fetal growth [ ]. In a mouse model of PE induced by angiotensin II receptor type 1 autoantibodies (AT1-AA), elevated adenosine levels contribute to HIF-1α overexpression in the placenta. Knockdown of placental HIF-1α inhibits adenosine accumulation and upregulates CD73 and ADORA2B in response to AT1-AA or LIGHT (TNFSF14) [ ]. In vitro studies demonstrate that activation of ADORA2B increases HIF-1α expression and induces CD73, ADORA2B, and FLT-1 expression in human placental explants, suggesting a positive feedback loop between adenosine signaling and HIF-1α that exacerbates placental dysfunction.
YC-1, a guanylate cyclase activator and HIF-1α inhibitor, significantly reduces the secretion of sFlt-1 and sENG from umbilical vein endothelial cells, primary trophoblasts, and placental explants derived from preterm PE patients [ ]. Additionally, YC-1 reverses TNFα-induced endothelial dysfunction, including suppression of VCAM-1 expression and monocyte adhesion, further implicating HIF-1α in PE-associated vascular pathology [ ]. Similarly, dysregulation of AMPK under hypoxic conditions contributes to uteroplacental vascular dysfunction. Pharmacological activation of AMPK with AICAR (200 mg/kg/day) in C57Bl/6 mice subjected to gestational hypoxia (from E14.5) partially rescues fetal growth restriction (P < 0.01) by enhancing uterine artery blood flow (P < 0.0001) [ ]. Conversely, inhibition of AMPK with Compound C (20 mg/kg/day) reduces uterine artery diameter and increases vasoconstriction under normoxic conditions, underscoring AMPK's essential role in maintaining gestational vascular homeostasis. Furthermore, the PI3K-Akt pathway plays a critical role in trophoblast survival. Pre-treatment with LY294002 or MK-2206, inhibitors of the PI3K/Akt survival pathway, attenuates the cytoprotective effect of relaxin, suggesting that this hormone exerts trophoblast-protective effects partly via PI3K-Akt signaling [ ].
Several agents target placental stress pathways. Puerarin alleviates PE-like symptoms in mice by suppressing CREB signaling and modulating the Hmox1 promoter, thereby inhibiting trophoblast ferroptosis [ ]. In hypoxic pregnant rats, the mitochondria-targeted antioxidant MitoQ prevents uterine arterial thickening and hypercontractility, improves vascular structure and reactivity, indicating its therapeutic potential against adverse changes in uterine artery structure and function during high-risk pregnancies [ ]. Klotho increases nuclear Nrf2, total Nrf2, SOD2, and NQO1 levels, thereby activating the antioxidant Nrf2/ARE signaling pathway. Silencing of Nrf2 abrogates the protective effects of Klotho overexpression on hypoxic human trophoblasts [ ]. Consistently, in vivo experiments reveal that Klotho overexpression suppresses oxidative damage and promotes cell proliferation in PE rat placental tissues. Collectively, these findings validate that Klotho activates the Nrf2/ARE signaling pathway to mitigate hypoxia-induced oxidative damage and restore normal cellular functions in human trophoblasts. ## Conclusion and perspectives
This study highlights the critical role of hypoxia-induced molecular mechanisms in trophoblast adaptation, emphasizing the importance of HIF related signaling, metabolic reprogramming, and epigenetic modifications in maintaining placental function. This article underscores the complex interplay of time, space, and oxygen dosage in regulating trophoblast behavior. While the first-trimester physiological low-oxygen environment (2–8% O₂) drives critical processes such as spiral artery remodeling and immune tolerance through HIF-1α/EPAS1-mediated pathways, persistent HIF-1α activation beyond 10 weeks has been causally linked to PE [ , , ]. In contrast, HIF-2α involvement in PE remains correlative and requires further investigation [ , ].
HIFs exhibit distinct isoforms in different tissues, and they may play divergent roles in various diseases, which should be understood and differentiated accordingly. Trophoblasts and tumor cells exhibit both similarities and differences in their behaviors, and research on tumor cells may offer insights for trophoblast studies. For instance, PPARγ is a key factor in trophoblast adaptation to acute hypoxia, regulating sustained activation of HIF-1α and lactate metabolism, mechanisms that are similar to the metabolic reprogramming seen in tumor cells [ ]. Studies have shown that Trop2, a surface antigen in trophoblast cells [ ], is overexpressed in many tumor types and has been targeted for anti-cancer therapies, showing significant efficacy in cervical cancer treatment [ ]. Similar Trop2-targeting strategies could be applied to treat hypoxia-associated placental pathologies.
In addition to molecular insights, careful consideration of experimental models is critical for advancing placental research. Commonly used in vitro models include choriocarcinoma-derived cell lines (e.g., BeWo, JEG3, JAR) and immortalized first-trimester extravillous trophoblast cell lines (e.g., HTR8/SVneo), which have provided valuable mechanistic insights. However, these transformed or cancer-derived cell lines exhibit significant limitations. Notably, transformed and cancer-derived cell lines often show altered signaling pathways, invasive properties, and epigenetic profiles that deviate from those of primary trophoblasts [ , ]. Other models, such as BMP4-treated hESCs, are also controversial. These cells are no longer pluripotent and do not fully differentiate into bona fide trophoblasts; for example, they exhibit significant demethylation of ELF5, a key trophoblast-specific transcription factor [ ]. Recently, three-dimensional (3D) trophoblast organoids have emerged as promising models. These organoids form multilayered spheroids with STB layer and CTB-like cells, structurally resembling placental villi. Functionally, they secrete key hormones such as hCG, progesterone, and PGF, and can generate EVT-like cells with invasive properties. These organoids provide a physiologically relevant and scalable platform to study placental development, maternal–fetal interactions, and environmental or pharmacological effects [ , ]. Similarly, analyses based on whole placental lysates lack cellular resolution. The placenta is a heterogeneous organ composed of multiple trophoblast subtypes—including cytotrophoblasts, syncytiotrophoblasts, and extravillous trophoblasts—as well as non-trophoblast cell populations such as endothelial, immune, and stromal cells. This complexity makes it difficult to attribute observed molecular changes to specific cell types. Therefore, molecular findings derived from placental lysates should be further validated at the cellular level to determine whether the molecule of interest directly affects the function of a particular cell type. In this context, single-cell transcriptomic and proteomic approaches offer powerful tools to resolve cell-type–specific expression patterns and functional relevance.
Future research should focus on developing targeted therapies that modulate hypoxic pathways, particularly targeting key nodes in these pathways, which may present promising therapeutic targets. In recent years, advances in multi-omics technologies—particularly spatially resolved approaches such as spatial transcriptomics, spatial proteomics, and spatial metabolomics—have provided new insights into the mechanisms of placental diseases [ , ]. Spatial transcriptomics enables the localization of gene expression across tissue architecture, shedding light on cell-type-specific transcriptional regulation [ , ]. Spatial metabolomics maps the distribution of small molecules and metabolic pathways within distinct placental regions, offering a snapshot of in situ biochemical activity [ ]. Spatial proteomics preserves tissue architecture and cellular context, thereby bridging the gap between transcriptional signals and actual cellular behavior [ , ]. This approach allows for a deeper understanding of the spatial microenvironment and functional heterogeneity within the placenta [ , ]. Integrating these complementary omics strategies—alongside advances in personalized medicine—will enhance our understanding of placental disorders and facilitate the development of more precise, individualized treatments [ , ].
