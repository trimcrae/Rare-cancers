## Background
Prostate cancer (PCa) faces significant global challenges, including rising incidence and drug resistance. Our research aims to identify novel protein targets for innovative treatments, evaluate potential adverse effects, and find compounds with strong binding affinity.

## Methods
We conducted a proteomic Mendelian randomization (MR) study to assess the causal relationship between plasma proteins and PCa risk. Plasma protein data were obtained from the UK Biobank Pharmaceutical Proteomics Project, which includes GWAS data for 2940 plasma proteins. The PCa GWAS data were sourced from the PRACTICAL Consortium me-ta-analysis, encompassing 79,148 patients and 61,106 controls. Plasma proteins with positive MR results were further analyzed for Bayesian colocalization, druggability, and stratification. We performed a phenome-wide association study (PheWAS) on first and second tier proteins and screened first tier proteins against TS Biochem’s library of 2070 anti- PCa com-pounds using molecular docking.

## Results
The MR study revealed five plasma proteins as protective factors and nine as risk factors for PCa (PFDR < 0.05). Subsequent analyses identified SCP2 (OR: 2.10, 95% CI:1.45–3.03, PFDR = 0.027) and C5 (OR: 1.23, 95% CI:1.10–1.37, PFDR = 0.037) as promising drug targets. Molecular docking showed beta-Amyrone exhibited strong binding energy with both SCP2 and C5 (binding energies of − 9.2 kcal/mol and − 8.9 kcal/mol, respectively), suggesting its potential as a dual-target inhibitor.

## Conclusion
Our study identified several protein biomarkers associated with PCa risk and screened potential compounds using molecular docking. These findings provide new insights and promising targets for PCa drug development, with beta-Amyrone showing strong potential as a dual-target inhibitor against both C5 and SCP2.

## Introduction
Prostate cancer (PCa), originating from the glandular epithelial cells of the prostate, predominantly manifests as adenocarcinoma (> 95% of cases). Its principal histological subtypes include acinar adenocarcinoma (characterized by glandular architecture) and ductal adenocarcinoma (less prevalent but more aggressive) [ , ]. Globally, PCa ranks as the second most common malignancy in males, with over 1.4 million new cases and 350,000 deaths reported in 2020 [ ]. Significant geographical disparities exist, with the highest incidence observed in developed regions such as North America and Europe, imposing substantial burdens on healthcare systems. Established risk factors encompass advanced age, ethnicity (notably African ancestry), obesity, smoking, family history, and germline mutations [ ]. While localized disease may be managed effectively through radical prostatectomy, radiotherapy, or hormonal therapy, therapeutic challenges persist due to molecular heterogeneity, intricate disease mechanisms, and treatment resistance [ ]. Androgen deprivation therapy (ADT), a cornerstone of PCa management, frequently leads to castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC) within five years in approximately 20–30% of patients. Notably, over 90% of CRPC patients present with bone metastases at diagnosis, exhibiting a median survival of 12–19 months despite docetaxel-based chemotherapy [ ]. This underscores the urgent need for novel therapeutic strategies to improve clinical outcomes.
Proteins, serving as pivotal mediators of genetic and phenotypic information, play dual roles in PCa management [ ]. On the one hand, some plasma proteins, such as prostate-specific antigen (PSA), serve as driving factors for non-malignant transformation. Due to their high sensitivity, they are of significant importance in the early diagnosis, prognostic evaluation, and treatment monitoring of prostate cancer [ ]. On the other hand, some proteins, such as matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), act as driving factors for tumor malignant transformation [ , ]. By participating in the growth, migration, invasion, and formation of the tumor microenvironment of cancer cells, they play a key role in the initiation, progression, and metastasis of prostate cancer [ ]. Therefore, systematically screening and validating these proteins, which are closely associated with disease-driving mechanisms and functional dysregulation, holds significant translational medical value for identifying potential therapeutic targets for prostate cancer.
Mendelian randomization (MR) has emerged as a powerful framework for drug repurposing and therapeutic target discovery [ , ]. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified protein quantitative trait loci (pQTLs)—single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) regulating protein abundance [ , ]. By leveraging these pQTLs as instrumental variables, MR and Bayesian colocalization analyses enable causal inference between protein targets and disease phenotypes while mitigating confounding biases inherent to observational studies [ , ]. Our study aims to: (1) identify novel protein targets for innovative prostate cancer therapies through proteome-wide Mendelian randomization, (2) validate these targets via Bayesian colocalization analysis, (3) evaluate their druggability and potential adverse effects, and (4) screen compounds with strong binding affinity through molecular docking. This systematic approach bridges genetic epidemiology with computational drug discovery, offering a robust pipeline for translating genomic insights into clinically actionable therapies.

## Materials and methods

## Study design
We performed analyses using summary-level GWAS data for European populations. All studies were approved by the appropriate Ethics review committees, and all participants signed informed consent forms. Figure  illustrates some of the main steps and methods of this study. First, we examined the association between gene-predicted plasma proteins and prostate cancer in two independent large-scale studies, applying FDR correction. Subsequently, we performed Bayesian colocalization analysis on plasma proteins corrected for association with prostate cancer. We further validated these proteins based on Finan et al.'s review of available drug target genes [ ]. We then stratified the reliability of the target proteins: first-tier proteins were identified as the most valuable, second-tier proteins as potential candidates, and third-tier proteins as having lesser potential. Concurrently, we conducted MR-PheWAS analysis on Tier 1 and Tier 2 plasma proteins using data from 2408 FinnGen R10 outcomes to explore the side effects of core proteins associated with prostate cancer. Finally, we conducted molecular docking-based compound screening on the most promising druggable plasma proteins in Tier 1, screening 2070 anti-prostate cancer compounds from TS Biochem and showcasing compounds with strong binding activity to the proteins. In our study, single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) were defined as instrumental variables (IVs), and all generated results are provided in the article and its supplementary materials. 

## Data sources
The plasma protein data were sourced from the UK Biobank Pharmaceutical Proteomics Project (UKB-PPP), a large-scale GWAS study comprising 2940 plasma proteins from 54,219 UK Biobank participants [ ]. For Mendelian randomization of drug targets, cis-pQTLs were selected as instrumental variables, and specific selection criteria were applied. These criteria are summarized as follows: 1. SNPs within ± 1 Mb of the gene region (cis-pQTLs); 2. Genome-wide significance thresholds were set at P < 5 × 10⁻⁸ to identify SNPs highly associated with plasma proteins; 3. The threshold for the linkage disequilibrium parameter (r2) was set at 0.001, with a genetic distance of 10,000 kb to ensure that the selected SNPs exhibit a very low degree of linkage disequilibrium; 4. F = R2 (n − k −1) / k (1 − R2), where R2 is the exposure variance calculated based on the dataset. The number of IVs is k, and the sample size is n. When the F statistic is < 10, it indicates weak instrumental variables, which are then excluded. These screening criteria ensure the reliability and accuracy of the selected instrumental variables, thereby enhancing the credibility of our study results.
We obtained pooled data on prostate cancer from the largest GWAS meta-analysis conducted by the PRACTICAL (Prostate Cancer Association Group to Investigate Cancer-Associated Alterations in the Genome) consortium. This data set included 79,148 prostate cancer cases of European ancestry and 61,106 controls. The mean age of prostate cancer patients in the PRACTICAL study was 66 years [ ].

## Proteome-wide MR analysis
In this study, we performed a two-sample Mendelian Randomization analysis with plasma protein as the exposure variable and prostate cancer as the outcome variable. The selection criteria for pQTLs were consistent with those previously outlined. Odds ratio (OR) and corresponding confidence interval (CI) for the associations between plasma protein and prostate cancer were estimated using Wald ratio methods or inverse-variance weighted (IVW) regression methods. To address multiple testing, we applied False Discovery Rate (FDR) methods in the analysis of plasma protein and prostate cancer. Subsequently, for plasma proteins with PFDR < 0.05, we performed reverse MR analysis using prostate cancer data as the exposure variable to investigate potential reverse causality. The MR analysis was conducted using the TwoSampleMR package in R software version 4.3.1.

## Bayesian colocalization analysis
Colocalization analysis evaluates the likelihood that known proteins share a common causal variable with prostate cancer. This analysis is based on a Bayesian model that assesses support for five exclusivity hypotheses: (1) no association with any trait; (2) association with trait 1 only; (3) association with trait 2 only; (4) association with both traits but different causal variations; (5) association with both traits and the same causal variation. Each hypothesis test (H0, H1, H2, H3, and H4) provides a posterior probability. We focused on variants with a high posterior probability of colocalization (PPH4 > 0.8) and considered genes with a joint posterior probability (PPH3 + PPH4) equal to or greater than 0.8 as proteins with colocalization possibility [ , ].

## Druggable protein testing and protein stratification
To assess the pharmacologic potential of identified proteins, we classified plasma proteins into those that are pharmaceutically actionable and those not yet considered pharmaceutically actionable, based on Finan et al.'s review of available drug target genes [ ]. Plasma proteins with PFDR < 0.05 in Mendelian randomization analysis were then categorized based on colocalization analysis results and their pharmaceutical actionability. Pharmaceutically actionable plasma proteins with strong evidence of colocalization (PPH4 > 0.8) were classified as Tier 1 targets; those with moderate evidence of colocalization (PPH3 + PPH4 > 0.8) were classified as Tier 2 targets. The remaining proteins were classified as Tier 3 targets, as their druggability may change with further research.

## Phenome-wide association study
PheWAS, also known as reverse GWAS, is a method for exploring associations between SNPs or phenotypes and a wide range of phenotypes spanning an entire phenome. This approach is particularly valuable for studying potential side effects associated with drug targets [ , ]. To further investigate the side effects of proteins associated with prostate cancer, we conducted a comprehensive phenotypic association Mendelian randomization (MR) study on primary and secondary target proteins. We used cis-pQTLs for proteins as exposure factors and disease summary statistics from a FinnGen biobank cohort with a sample size of up to 412,181 individuals (including 230,310 females and 181,871 males) as outcomes [ ]. MR analysis was performed using either the inverse variance weighted (IVW) method or the Wald ratio method, with results having Pfdr values below 0.05 considered statistically significant.

## Compound screening based on molecular docking
To further investigate potential inhibitors of the Tier 1 target proteins, we employed a molecular docking approach. Molecular docking facilitates the simulation of the recognition and docking process between molecules and protein receptors, identifying the optimal binding conformation and computing the binding energy [ ]. For this study, we obtained the structure files of 2070 small molecules from an anti-prostate cancer inhibitor library provided by Tao Shu (https://www.targetmol.cn/compound-library) and utilized the Autodock Vina platform for virtual screening to identify effective inhibitors [ ]. The specific methodology involved the following steps: (1) Retrieve the high-resolution structures of the Tier 1 target proteins from the Protein Data Bank (PDB), import them into PyMOL, remove solvent molecules and small organic molecules, and add hydrogen atoms and export the structures in PDBQT format. (2) Ligand preparation: The SDF structure files of the small molecules were downloaded, and hydrogen atoms were added, followed by energy minimization. The optimized structures were then exported in PDBQT format. (3) Molecular docking: A grid box was defined around the active site of the protein, with the protein treated as a rigid structure and the ligand as flexible. Batch docking was performed, and the corresponding binding energies were calculated.
Furthermore, we evaluated the rationality of the selected inhibitor library. In accordance with Lipinski’s rule of five, we calculated five key physicochemical properties (molecular weight (MW), octanol–water partition coefficient (ALogP), topological polar surface area (TPSA), number of hydrogen bond donors (HBD), and number of hydrogen bond acceptors (HBA)) for the 2070 compounds. These properties are instrumental during the drug discovery and development process, particularly in the early screening stages [ ]. The principles derived from this rule assist chemists in identifying potential drug candidates and optimizing their physicochemical properties to enhance their oral bioavailability [ ]. Additionally, we utilized the RDKit package to generate extended-connectivity fingerprints (ECFP6) for the molecules and computed the Tanimoto coefficients to assess the chemical similarity among these molecules.

## Results

## Prostate cancer-associated plasma proteins
In this study, a total of 1946 plasma proteins were included in the Mendelian randomization analysis, following stringent instrumental variable screening criteria. Detailed SNP information can be found in Supplementary 1. Notably, MR analysis (PFDR < 0.05) based on inverse variance weighted (IVW) or Wald ratio methods revealed that 14 plasma proteins are associated with PCa. WASHC3 (WASH complex subunit 3), MSMB (Beta-microseminoprotein), CDSN (Corneodesmosin), MICB_MICA (MHC class I polypeptide-related sequence B_A), and CEACAM21 (Carcinoembryonic antigen-related cell adhesion molecule 21) are identified as protective factors against PCa. The odds ratios and 95% confidence intervals for these associations were 0.708 (95% CI: 0.587–0.853), 0.726 (95% CI: 0.640–0.823), 0.906 (95% CI: 0.866–0.948), 0.935 (95% CI: 0.910–0.962), and 0.936 (95% CI: 0.904–0.970), respectively. Conversely, nine plasma proteins were identified as risk factors for prostate cancer: SERPINA3 (α-1 antichymotrypsin), CD164L2 (CD164 sialomucin-like 2 protein), GSTP1 (glutathione S-transferase P), SPINT2 (Kunitz-type protease inhibitor 2), C5 (complement C5), PLEKHO1 (pleckstrin homology domain-containing family O member 1), PRKAR2A (cAMP-dependent protein kinase type II-alpha regulatory subunit), ZBTB17 (zinc finger and BTB domain-containing protein 17), and SCP2 (non-specific lipid-transfer protein). The odds ratios and 95% confidence intervals for these associations were as follows: 1.093 (95% CI: 1.046–1.143), 1.104 (95% CI: 1.048–1.162), 1.149 (95% CI: 1.069–1.235), 1.218 (95% CI: 1.114–1.331), 1.228 (95% CI: 1.101–1.371), 1.686 (95% CI: 1.276–2.228), 1.876 (95% CI: 1.362–2.585), 1.890 (95% CI: 1.344–2.658), and 2.100 (95% CI: 1.457–3.027). For detailed information, please refer to Figs.  , , , and Supplementary 2. Among the 14 proteins associated with prostate cancer, after FDR multiple testing adjustment, we found that the development of PCa may lead to increased levels of WASHC3, indicating a reverse causal relationship. Therefore, we did not further analyze WASHC3 in subsequent studies. Whether it increases or decreases the risk of prostate cancer requires further investigation with more and larger datasets. 

## Bayesian colocalization analysis of PCa associated plasma proteins
To further explore the potential association between plasma proteins and prostate cancer, we conducted Bayesian colocalization analysis within ± 1 MB upstream and downstream of their respective genes. Strong support for Bayesian colocalization analysis was found for two proteins, C5 and SCP2 (PPH4 ≥ 0.8). Additionally, suggestive colocalization analysis results (PPH4 + PPH3 ≥ 0.8) were observed for eight plasma proteins: MSMB, MICB_MICA, CDSN, SPINT2, PRKAR2A, GSTP1, CEACAM21, and PLEKHO1. The colocalization results for the other proteins were negative. For further details, please refer to Fig.  and Supplementary 3.

## Druggability test and tiers of proteins
Based on pharmaceutical actionability screening results, seven proteins (C5, GSTP1, SCP2, MSMB, SPINT2, SERPINA3, CD164L2) were identified as potential drug targets, while six plasma proteins (CDSN, PRKAR2A, MICB_MICA, CEACAM21, PLEKHO1, ZBTB17) have not been recognized as druggable targets. Incorporating findings from Mendelian randomization studies, and based on Bayesian colocalization analysis results and the druggability of the proteins, we classified the proteins into three tiers: Tier 1 includes two pharmaceutically viable proteins with strong colocalization evidence (C5 and SCP2); Tier 2 includes three pharmaceutically viable proteins with suggestive Bayesian colocalization evidence (MSMB, SPINT2, GSTP1); and Tier 3 includes the remaining proteins, which have limited colocalization evidence or are not yet considered pharmaceutically viable. For more information, please see Fig.  and Supplementary 4.

## Phenome-wide association study of plasma proteins associated with prostate cancer
To assess the potential beneficial or side effects of prostate cancer-associated plasma proteins on other phenotypes, we performed a comprehensive phenotypic association analysis of primary and secondary target proteins against 2408 phenotypes across 46 categories in the FinnGen. After FDR correction, we found that SPINT2 was associated with two phenotypes with p-values less than 0.05, while C5, MSMB, and GSTP1 showed no apparent causal associations with any of the 2408 phenotypes in the database. Additionally, the SCP2 allele was excluded when matched to the phenotypic data. These phenotypes may represent therapeutic targets or potential adverse effects of the target proteins. Detailed data are provided in Fig.  and Supplementary 5. 

## Compound screening based on molecular docking
After analyzing the selected inhibitor library, we calculated the Tanimoto similarity for 2070 molecules, resulting in a value of 0.1759218 (Fig.  A), indicating low similarity between the molecules. Selecting molecular libraries with low similarity for virtual screening is advantageous for discovering inhibitors with new structures. The chemical diversity of the 2070 molecules was assessed using radar and scatter plot analysis of MW, ALogP, TPSA, HBD, and HBA, demonstrating that the compound library encompasses a wide range of compounds with diverse properties (Fig.  B, ). Retrieve the high-resolution structures of the Tier 1 target proteins SCP2 (PDB ID: 1qnd) and C5 (PDB ID: 1xwe) from the Protein Data Bank (PDB). Through molecular docking, we obtained the binding energies of each molecule with the target proteins and ranked the potential inhibitors based on their binding energy scores. Generally, the lower the binding energy (i.e., the more negative the value), the more stable the ligand–protein binding and the more likely the inhibitory effect will be significant. We selected the top five ligands with low binding energies and reasonable binding patterns as potential inhibitors for further study, with their binding energies shown in Fig.  A. The binding energies were all less than − 5, indicating a strong affinity between the molecules and the proteins. Notably, beta-Amyrone exhibited strong binding energy to both SCP2 and C5, suggesting its potential as an inhibitor for both proteins (Fig.  B). The chemical structures of the remaining molecules are shown in Fig.  C and D. We visualized the binding conformation of beta-Amyrone with SCP2 and C5 and analyzed the ligand–protein binding modes using Discovery Studio, revealing various interactions, including van der Waals forces, hydrogen bonds, and Pi-stacking (Fig.  E, F). The binding conformations of the remaining molecules with SCP2 are shown in Supplementary 6., and those with C5 are shown in Supplementary 7. 

## Discussion
In this study, we first conducted a proteome-wide Mendelian randomization analysis of prostate cancer, identifying genetically predictive levels for 14 blood proteins. Subsequently, we excluded WASHC3 proteins with reverse causality using reverse Mendelian randomization analysis. For the remaining 13 proteins, we performed Bayesian colocalization analysis, identifying 2 proteins with strong colocalization support and 9 proteins with suggestive colocalization support. Next, we evaluated the druggability of these proteins based on Finan et al.'s [ ] review of available drug targets 15, and ranked the drug targets accordingly. To further explore the potential safety concerns and additional therapeutic implications of these proteins, we performed MR-PheWAS analysis of the Tier 1 and Tier 2 targets against the FinnGen R10 database. Finally, to investigate potential effective inhibitory molecules for the most promising target proteins, we conducted molecular docking-based virtual screening using a library of 2,070 compounds against two druggable proteins with strong colocalization support.
The first-tier target proteins C5 and SCP2 demonstrate significant therapeutic potential in anti-prostate cancer drug development. Through a multidimensional validation strategy (including multiple-adjusted Mendelian randomization analysis, Bayesian colocalization, and molecular virtual screening), we confirmed the reliability of both as novel therapeutic targets. Sterol Carrier Protein 2 (SCP2), also known as the non-specific lipid transfer protein, is widely expressed in mammalian tissues [ ]. Studies indicate that SCP2 promotes prostate cancer progression by regulating cholesterol metabolic reprogramming and the androgen synthesis pathway [ , ]. Its molecular mechanisms involve activating the AKT signaling pathway to drive tumor proliferation and inducing tumor angiogenesis by mediating the binding of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) to VEGFR2 [ – ]. Notably, the SCP2 inhibitor itraconazole—an FDA-approved triazole antifungal drug—has shown anti-prostate cancer activity in preclinical studies, though its efficacy has not yet been validated through large-scale randomized controlled trials [ , ]. This study provides new genetic-level evidence for the therapeutic potential of SCP2 inhibitors, further supporting their feasibility as targeted therapies for prostate cancer (PCA). However, significant challenges remain in the clinical application of itraconazole: its property as a broad-spectrum CYP3A4 inhibitor may interfere with the metabolism of other anticancer drugs and induce antagonistic effects in combination therapies. Therefore, developing novel SCP2-selective analogs (retaining anti-angiogenic activity while avoiding CYP3A4 inhibition) has become a critical breakthrough direction [ ]. Based on this, we successfully identified drug-like small molecules with high binding affinity through computer virtual screening and molecular docking technology, laying a molecular foundation for the rational design of next-generation SCP2 inhibitors.
The complement system consists of over 50 proteins, receptors, and regulatory factors, which respond rapidly to signals by binding to specific domains to trigger reactions. It plays a key role in defending against pathogens and maintaining homeostasis, while also being involved in tumor initiation and progression [ ]. The complement system not only directly kills tumors by activating the immune system but also promotes tumor progression, metastasis, immune escape, and chronic inflammation through the abnormal expression of complement factors in the tumor microenvironment [ ]. Complement component C5 is a common target of the classical, alternative, and lectin pathways. Inhibitors of C5, such as Eculizumab and Ravulizumab, have been FDA-approved for the treatment of PNH and aHUS, by blocking the conversion of C5 into C5a and C5b [ , ]. It has been reported that C5 activation product C5a enhances the proliferation, invasion, and PD-L1 expression of castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC) through the C5aR-dependent pathway. This suggests that the C5a-C5aR system plays an important role in the promotion and immune evasion of CRPC, making it a promising therapeutic target for CRPC [ ]. Another study showed that PSA, as a complement-activating protease, promotes the formation of a cancer-favorable environment through complement activation (C5)-induced chronic inflammation [ , ]. This finding is consistent with our Mendelian randomization and Bayesian colocalization results. Further MR-PheWAS also validated the good safety profile of C5 inhibition. These findings provide strong evidence that C5 is an ideal and reliable clinical target for prostate cancer.
Virtual screening and molecular docking analysis revealed that β-Amyrone exhibits strong binding affinity with both prostate cancer-related proteins C5 and SCP2, suggesting its potential as an effective dual-target inhibitor acting on these two proteins, which highlights its promising application in anti-prostate cancer therapy. However, further research and comparison are needed to evaluate its clinical application prospects. β-Amyrone is a triterpenoid natural product that is widely found in plants, animals, and microorganisms [ , ]. It exhibits various biological activities and pharmacological effects, such as anti-inflammatory, anticancer, antibacterial, and antioxidant properties. It has been shown to inhibit the growth of MCF-7 breast cancer cells to some extent (IC50 value of 21.5 µg/mL) and enhance the therapeutic effect of other anticancer drugs, such as doxorubicin, on drug-resistant cancers by modulating P-glycoprotein (P-gp) activity, demonstrating its indirect anticancer potential [ – ]. In addition, studies have shown that the inhibitor of SCP2, itraconazole, also exhibits such anticancer mechanisms, which further supports the reliability of our computational virtual screening simulations. For C5, it is likely more about blocking its conversion to C5a and C5b to prevent CRPC proliferation and invasion. However, these specific mechanisms need more in vitro validation experiments to confirm.
In addition to SCP2 and C5, secondary targets such as MSMB, SPINT2, and GSTP1 are also considered to have potential as therapeutic targets for prostate cancer. These proteins were identified through Mendelian randomization multiple correction, colocalization analysis, and druggability screening. MSMB is a protein primarily secreted by luminal epithelial cells of the prostate gland and has significant causal association and colocalization support with prostate cancer. Observational and genetic epidemiological studies have shown a strong association between MSMB and prostate cancer risk [ , ]. In this study, we demonstrated that high expression of MSMB has a protective effect on prostate cancer development, and MR-PheWAS results showed no significant side effects, further supporting previous findings. However, druggability screening revealed low similarity of MSMB to approved drug targets, suggesting that more research is needed to explore its potential in prostate cancer treatment. SPINT2, also known as hepatocyte growth factor activator inhibitor type 2 (HAI-2), is a Kunitz-type serine protease inhibitor that plays a key role in various malignancies. Previous studies have shown that SPINT2 expression is significantly reduced in prostate tissue, possibly due to post-translational modifications [ ]. Although the specific role of SPINT2 in prostate cancer is not fully understood, our study identifies SPINT2 as a potential risk factor for prostate cancer. Additionally, according to the PheWAS findings, the use of SPINT2 inhibitors has shown potential benefits in the treatment of three diseases, including prostate cancer, suggesting its potential as a therapeutic target for prostate cancer. GSTP1 (glutathione S-transferase Pi 1) is an enzyme involved in cellular detoxification processes and plays different roles in various tumor types [ ]. It is considered a potential biomarker for prostate cancer [ ]. Although GSTP1 is widely believed to have a protective role in the prostate, methylation of the GSTP1 gene in prostate cancer tissue has been shown to inactivate it, thereby promoting tumor progression [ ]. Furthermore, the effects of GSTP1 may be influenced by ethnic and environmental factors [ ]. Our findings suggest that elevated plasma GSTP1 levels may contribute to prostate cancer in European populations, and the specific relationship between the two may be more complex than expected, requiring further exploration and validation.
This comprehensive proteome-wide Mendelian randomization and colocalization analysis, which integrates pQTL and prostate cancer GWAS data, further reinforces the causal relationship between plasma proteins and prostate cancer risk. The subsequent virtual screening based on molecular docking provides new insights into prostate cancer therapy. These methods offer significant advantages compared to traditional research approaches. Firstly, the MR design reduces potential confounding factors and reverse causality bias, providing more accurate causal effect estimates than traditional observational and proteomic studies. Secondly, the F-statistics for the instrumental variables (IVs) used were all greater than 10, indicating no weak instrumental variable bias. Thirdly, the proximity of the cis-pQTLs to their respective genes increases the level of evidence (cis-pQTL > trans-pQTL) and effectively reduces horizontal pleiotropy. Fourthly, multivariate analysis strategies, including reverse Mendelian randomization and Bayesian colocalization analysis, were employed to ensure the robustness and reliability of the results. Fifthly, virtual screening and molecular docking were performed on proteins with strong colocalization support and 2069 anti-prostate cancer inhibitors, reducing drug development costs. Finally, the side effects and other potential therapeutic effects of the identified targets were thoroughly explored using MR-PheWAS.
However, this study has some limitations. First, only cis-pQTL were selected as instrumental variables in this study, and the number of SNPs in most proteins did not exceed 3, which limited the application of some MR analysis strategies, such as Cochran Q test, MR Egger intercept test and MR-PRESSO test. Second, analyses based on pooled data cannot examine sex-or age-specific effects, nor can stratified analyses be performed. In addition, our data are based on European populations and the applicability of the results to other racial or ethnic groups remains to be verified. Finally, cell and animal experiments have not been conducted to investigate the causal relationship between these proteins and prostate cancer and the actual role of potential inhibitors. Future research should build on our findings to conduct basic experiments to further explore the specific mechanisms of prostate cancer resistance and targeted therapy.

## Conclusion
Our study utilized Mendelian randomization (MR) and Bayesian colocalization analysis to identify C5 and SCP2 as the most promising candidates for targeted prostate cancer therapy, while also recognizing MSMB, SPINT2, and GSTP1 as potential therapeutic targets. Through virtual screening and molecular docking, we discovered that β-Amyrone exhibits strong binding affinity with C5 and SCP2 (binding energies of − 9.2 kcal/mol and − 8.9 kcal/mol, respectively), suggesting its potential as a dual-target inhibitor for prostate cancer treatment. Despite certain limitations, we believe these proteins and inhibitors hold significant potential as drug targets for prostate cancer. Future research should further explore these targets and their potential anticancer activities, particularly in the mechanisms of cancer, pharmacodynamics, and drug uptake/efflux, and evaluate their impact on prostate cancer therapyen atoms and export.
