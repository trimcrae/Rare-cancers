To gain deeper insights into the cellular and molecular landscape underlying this process, we performed comprehensive single-cell RNA sequencing on primary ccRCC tumors and their matched venous tumor thrombi, aiming to systematically characterize the microenvironment of tumor thrombus formation. Our analysis reveals a distinct cellular composition within the tumor thrombus, marked by a significant enrichment of myeloid cells and a relative depletion of T/NK cells, establishing an immunosuppressive milieu at the tumor thrombus site. Notably, we identified a pro-tumorigenic tumor-associated macrophage subpopulation exhibiting M2-like polarization and pro-angiogenic/immunosuppressive characteristics that significantly expanding within the thrombus. Concomitantly, the CD8 + T cell compartment displayed transcriptional signatures indicative of functional exhaustion. Intercellular communication network analysis highlighted the upregulation of adhesion-promoting signaling pathways and context-dependent stromal-immune crosstalk within the thrombus. Furthermore, epithelial cells residing within the tumor thrombus exhibited transcriptional adaptations associated with enhanced migration and survival, alongside increased genomic instability. This study provides a valuable data resource for understanding and targeting the tumor thrombus niche.

## Introduction
Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC) is among the most common malignancies of the urinary system, with clear cell renal cell carcinoma (ccRCC) being the predominant histological subtype, accounting for approximately 70–80 % of all kidney cancer cases . Although early-stage RCC generally responds well to treatment, up to 20–30 % of patients experience local recurrence or distant metastasis at the time of diagnosis or following surgery, which substantially worsens their survival prognosis , . Tumor thrombus (TT), a unique and aggressive manifestation of RCC, involves the invasion of tumor cells into the renal vein, inferior vena cava, and even the right atrium, with an incidence rate of about 4–15 % . The presence of TT not only complicates surgical procedures and increases risks but also significantly elevates the likelihood of postoperative recurrence and metastasis, representing a critical factor affecting the prognosis of ccRCC patients , , , . A deeper understanding of the mechanisms underlying the development and progression of ccRCC with TT is crucial for improving clinical management of these patients.
Conventional bulk RNA sequencing provides an average gene expression profile of the tumor tissue, failing to capture the intricate cellular composition and intercellular interactions within the tumor , . The advent of single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) technology has enabled gene expression analysis at single-cell resolution, significantly advancing our understanding of the tumor microenvironment (TME) and tumor cell heterogeneity , , . The TME is a complex ecosystem comprising tumor cells, immune cells, endothelial cells, fibroblasts, and other cell types, whose interactions play a pivotal role in tumor growth, angiogenesis, immune evasion, and metastasis , . In the context of ccRCC with TT, the process by which tumor cells breach the vascular endothelial barrier, survive in the bloodstream, and eventually form distant metastases involves diverse cell types and intricate molecular mechanisms. Single-cell transcriptomics can precisely dissect the cellular composition, states, and functions in primary tumors, tumor thrombi, and potential metastatic sites, thereby revealing key cell populations and molecular pathways involved in thrombus formation , , .
To gain a more comprehensive understanding of the cellular and molecular characteristics of ccRCC with TT, this study employs scRNA-seq technology for in-depth analysis of primary ccRCC and tumor thrombus samples. By comparing the gene expression profiles of different cell types, we identified key cell populations, differentially expressed genes, and regulatory pathways associated with thrombus formation and progression. We also explored the interactions between tumor cells and other cell types in the TME to elucidate their dynamic changes within the thrombus microenvironment. This research decodes the cellular dynamics in ccRCC with TT, providing new insights and a theoretical foundation for elucidating the molecular mechanisms of thrombus.

## Results
2.1

## The multicellular ecosystem of RCC with TT
To investigate the cellular landscape of clear cell renal cell carcinoma with tumor thrombus (ccRCC-TT), we performed single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) on tissue samples collected from one treatment-naive patient ( A). The collected samples included: primary tumor tissue (PT, n = 1), adjacent normal kidney tissue (AN, n = 1), tumor thrombus tissue from the renal vein (TT, n = 1, labeled as " Tumor thrombus " in subsequent analyses), distal normal kidney tissue (DN, n = 1), and renal vein tissue (RV, n = 1). The presence of the tumor thrombus within the renal vein was confirmed by computed tomography angiography (CTA) imaging ( B). Single-cell suspensions were processed via a standard droplet-based protocol, yielding a gene-cell expression matrix after quality control and alignment ( C). A total of 76,142 cells were analyzed across all samples. Cell types were annotated based on the expression of canonical marker genes, enabling the identification of major cell populations, including B/Plasma cells, Endothelial cells, Epithelial cells, Fibroblasts, Myeloid cells, and T/NK cells ( D), with high expression of representative signature genes such as IGHG3, IGKC, ESM1, KDR, FCN3, COL3A1, CD163, GZMA ( F). Moreover, the distribution of immune and stromal cell populations varied substantially across tissues ( E) . 

2.2

## T cell dysfunction and cell state transition in ccRCC progression
T cells play an important role in tumor progression , . To further characterize the role of T cells in ccRCC progression, we performed a subclustering analysis of the T/NK cell population. This revealed four major cell types: NK cells, proliferating cells, CD4 + T cells, and CD8 + T cells ( ). All these subclusters were shared among each tissue type, but there is a certain tissue preference. In specific, CD8_c6 and CD8_cycling are predominantly distributed in the Primary tumor, whereas CD8_5 and CD8_12 are mainly found in the tumor thrombus ( ). Subsequent sub-clustering analyses delineated eleven distinct CD4+ T cell states ( ), and ten CD8+ T cell states ( A), each observed in multiple datasets. Among CD4+ T cells, we observed a range of differentiation states, including naive (Tn), regulatory (Treg), Tissue-resident memory (Trm), memory T cells (Tm). CD4 + T cell compositions differed substantially across tissues. In specific, CD4_FOXP3_Treg, CD4_CCR8_Treg, and CD4_TCF7_Tn cells were highly abundant in Primary tumor samples. The CD4_CAPG_Tm and CD4_IFNG_Trm subsets were nearly exclusive to tumor thrombus samples and rarely seen in Uninvolved normal samples. Moreover, CD4_CAPG_Tm was almost absent in Uninvolved normal tissues, while CD4_TCF7_Tn was seldom present in Distal normal samples ( ). 
Within the CD8+ T cell population, we identified naive (TN), Cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL), Exhaustion (Tex), and memory T cells (Tm) subsets. Additionally, we observed a CD8 + subset characterized by high expression of exhaustion-related markers, including CXCL13, LAG3, TNFRSF9, PDCD1 (PD-1), CTLA4, and CREM ( C, D and
 ) . Among these CD8 + states, CD8_CXCL3_Tex and CD8_PDCD1_Tex cells were highly abundant in samples other than Primary tumor samples. The CD8_LEF1_Tn subset was nearly exclusive in Tumor thrombus samples and rarely seen in Primary tumor samples ( F). To elucidate the differentiation trajectories of CD8 + T cells, we employed Monocle 3, a software to infer their developmental progression . This analysis revealed a bifurcating, tree-like trajectory originating from CD8 + TN cells, subsequently diverging into distinct T-cell populations defined by their local microenvironment ( G). These paths indicate divergent cell fates, supported by the expression kinetics of related gene signatures along the inferred pseudotime axis ( H). Differentially expressed gene (DEG) analysis identified RGS5, SPARC, and IGFBP7 as the top upregulated genes in tumor thrombus CD8 + T cells ( J, L and ). These genes were enriched in TGF beta signaling pathway and Ribosome. ( K).
Collectively, these data suggested that the T cell landscape undergoes significant remodeling during ccRCC progression, with a shift towards an exhausted phenotype in the primary tumor and the emergence of distinct naive-like populations in the tumor thrombus.

2.3

## Myeloid cell subpopulation shifts between primary renal cell carcinoma and tumor thrombus
Myeloid cells are highly heterogeneous immune cell populations and are key regulators in tumor progression , , . To shed light on the myeloid populations in UC, we performed a sub-clustering analysis and identified 11 clusters. These clusters exhibit unique transcriptional profiles as defined by key marker genes ( B). These clusters encompassed various myeloid subsets, including tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) expressing NR4A3 and SELENOP (Cluster 0), CD16-positive monocytes characterized by FCN1 expression (Cluster 3), and a cycling TAM population marked by MKI67 (Cluster 9) ( A). Comparative analysis of myeloid cell composition revealed significant shifts in the relative abundance of these subpopulations between primary and tumor thrombus tissues ( E). Specifically, we observed a significant increase in the proportion of Cluster 1 (TAM _SELENOP) within the tumor thrombus compared to the primary tumor. Conversely, the relative abundance of Cluster 4 (TAM _CXCL10) was decreased in the TT setting. 
Functional annotation of these myeloid cell clusters using gene signature scores ( C and ) indicated distinct functional orientations. For example, cluster TAM_SPP1 exhibited higher M2 polarization scores, while Cluster 0 (TAM_NR4A3) displayed elevated phagocytosis scores. Notably, comparison of myeloid cells derived from primary tumors and tumor thrombi revealed significant differences in the expression of genes associated with immune checkpoints and immune evasion, as well as angiogenesis and chemotactic cytokine signaling ( F). For instance, genes such as CSF1R and CD163 were significantly upregulated in TT myeloid cells, suggesting a potentially more immunosuppressive and pro-angiogenic role in the TT niche. GOBP pathway enrichment analysis ( G) further highlighted functional differences across myeloid cell clusters. Notably, the annotation indicated that pathways related to response to Hyperoxia and IL15 Signaling Pathway showed higher enrichment in clusters more prevalent in the tumor thrombus, while pathways such as regulation of Th1 cell differentiation were enriched in clusters more abundant in the primary tumor. Differential gene expression analysis between myeloid cells from primary and tumor thrombus ( H) identified a substantial number of differentially expressed genes. The volcano plot revealed that genes such as CD46 and MARCO were significantly upregulated in TT myeloid cells, and KEGG analysis shows that these upregulated genes are enriched in the WNT signaling pathway ( I), potentially contributing to the altered microenvironment at the TT site. Conversely, genes like SPP1 were predominantly expressed in myeloid cells associated with primary tumors ( H and ). These findings suggest a dynamic adaptation of the myeloid compartment during TT progression, potentially fostering an environment conducive to TT growth and immune evasion.

2.4

## Distinct epithelial cell subpopulations and increased copy number variation in tumor thrombus tissue
To investigate the epithelial cell compartment in ccRCC-TT, we analyzed the transcriptomic profiles of epithelial cells isolated from the primary tumor and tumor thrombus tissues. Unsupervised clustering identified 12 distinct epithelial cell subpopulations (Epi_c0 to Epi_c11) ( A), suggesting significant heterogeneity within this compartment. The distribution of these epithelial cell subclusters varied across different tissue origins ( B and C). We observed that Subcluster Epi_c1 and Epi_c5 was predominantly found in the tumor thrombus, while Subcluster Epi_c8 was more prevalent in the primary tumor. This suggests a potential selection or adaptation of specific epithelial cell states during the TT process. 
Analysis of KEGG pathway enrichment across these epithelial subclusters ( D) revealed distinct functional characteristics. For instance, Subcluster Epi_c1 and Epi_c5, enriched in the tumor thrombus, showed high enrichment scores for pathways related to focal adhesion. This indicates potential functional specialization of epithelial cells in different microenvironments.
Differential gene expression (DEG) analysis between epithelial cells from primary and tumor thrombus ( F) identified a significant number of differentially expressed genes. Epithelial cells in the tumor thrombus exhibited upregulation of genes associated with cell migration invasion (MMP7, CXCL1) , , while primary tumor epithelial cells showed higher expression of genes involved in cell cycle regulation or metabolism. Gene Ontology Biological Process (GOBP) enrichment analysis further supported these findings ( G), revealing that primary tumor epithelial cells were enriched in processes related to regulation of cell adhesion, whereas tumor thrombus epithelial cells showed enrichment in regulation of cell cycle G2M phase transition.
Based on the inferCNV analysis of copy number variations, we found that the patient exhibited a loss of chromosome 3 and a gain of chromosome 5 ( E). Moreover, the tumor thrombus showed higher levels of copy number variation and a greater variation score compared to the primary tumor ( H).
In summary, our analysis of epithelial cells demonstrates significant heterogeneity and distinct transcriptional and genomic profiles between primary and TT ccRCC tissues. The enrichment of specific epithelial subpopulations and the upregulation of genes associated with migration and invasion, alongside increased CNV, in the tumor thrombus suggest an adaptation of tumor cells to facilitate dissemination and survival in the TT niche.

2.5

## Stromal cell remodeling in ccRCC-TT
Stromal cells are crucial mediators of tumor progression, metastasis, and invasion , , . Comparative analysis of endothelial and fibroblast populations from primary tumors and tumor thrombi revealed distinct molecular signatures. Specifically, endothelial cells in the TT group demonstrated elevated MARCKSL1 and COL15A1 expression ( ). In addition, positive regulation of SMAD signaling was enriched in tumor thrombus, while antigen processing and presentation via MHC Class II was specifically enriched in primary tumors ( ). These findings suggest that TT endothelial cells may gain enhanced SMAD signaling capabilities, potentially promoting tumor cell migration and invasion. Conversely, primary tumor endothelium appears more actively involved in antigen presentation and immune surveillance.
Fibroblasts from TT sites displayed increased FOXC1 and POSTN expression ( ), with Focal adhesion assembly significantly enriched ( ), implying robust extracellular matrix remodeling and heightened cell motility within the TT microenvironment. This indicates that TT fibroblasts may support tumor dissemination by promoting focal adhesion dynamics and tissue invasion. Collectively, these data highlight the dynamic and context-dependent roles of stromal components in regulating tumor metastasis and immune modulation.

2.6

## Differential intercellular communication networks between primary renal cell carcinoma and tumor thrombus
To understand the potential role of intercellular communication in ccRCC-TT, we finally computationally inferred and compared cell-cell interaction networks in primary tumor and tumor thrombus tissues using CellChat (version 1.6.1) software . Our analysis revealed quantitative and qualitative differences in these networks between the two sites. In specific, we observed a higher number of inferred interactions in the tumor thrombus (n = 1217) compared to the primary tumor (n = 826) ( B, left panel). However, the average interaction strength was similar between the primary tumor (1.34) and the tumor thrombus (1.354) ( B, right panel). 
Analysis of the relative contribution of each major cell type to the overall interaction network ( C) indicated that CD86, CD45 and VWF were uniquely present in primary and TT sites, respectively, while tumorigenesis-associated pathways, such as NOTCH and TGFβ signaling, were observed in both tissues. We further investigated specific ligand-receptor pairs mediating communication between different cell types. Notably, the cell types involved that show a difference between primary and TT sites - e.g., VWF signaling, potentially involved in endothelial-tumor cell interactions, exhibited a higher communication probability in the tumor thrombus compared to the primary tumor. This suggests potential mechanisms facilitating tumor cell adhesion to the endothelium during intravasation. Examination of the distribution of incoming and outgoing interaction strengths for each cell type ( E) revealed that fibroblasts exhibited stronger outgoing interaction strength, potentially indicating increased signaling to other cell types within the TT niche. Focusing on the TGF-beta signaling pathway ( F), a known regulator of the TME, we observed a potentially stronger TGF-beta signaling axis between fibroblasts and myeloid in the tumor thrombus compared to the primary tumor. This may contribute to the fibrosis or immune suppression in the TT microenvironment.
Finally, we explored the potential communication between fibroblasts and specific TAM subpopulations ( G). The expression patterns of specific ligand-receptor pairs, such as Fibroblasts expressing CXCL12 and TAM_CXCL10 expressing its receptor ACKR3, showed differential expression levels between primary and TT sites. This suggests a potentially altered recruitment or polarization of specific TAM subsets by fibroblasts in the tumor thrombus. In summary, our analysis of cell-cell interactions reveals a distinct communication landscape in the tumor thrombus compared to primary renal cell carcinoma, characterized by a more dynamic and altered network in the TT niche.

## Discussion
Our comprehensive single-cell RNA sequencing analysis of primary ccRCC and its matched venous tumor thrombus provides a high-resolution view of the tumor microenvironment (TME) and reveals significant cellular heterogeneity and dynamic intercellular communication networks that may contribute to TT progression.
Within the T cell compartment, we observed a clear divergence in CD8 + T cell states between the primary tumor and the tumor thrombus. The primary tumor was enriched in CD8 + T cells exhibiting an exhausted phenotype (CD8_PDCD1_Tex), characterized by the expression of multiple inhibitory receptors such as PD-1, CTLA4, and LAG3. Conversely, the tumor thrombus showed a higher prevalence of CD8 + T cell subsets with potential memory and tissue-resident characteristics (CD8_LEF1_Tn and CD8_IL7R_Tm). Trajectory analysis further supported this observation, suggesting a differentiation pathway from naive CD8 + T cells towards distinct fates influenced by the local microenvironment. The upregulation of RGS5, SPARC, and IGFBP7 in TT CD8 + T cells, along with the enrichment of TGF beta signaling and Ribosome pathways, hints at potential mechanisms driving these phenotypic changes and their adaptation to the TT niche. Collectively, these data suggest a shift towards a more exhausted CD8 + T cell state within the primary tumor, potentially contributing to immune evasion, while the TT site harbors T cells with features of adaptation and survival in the vasculature.
The myeloid compartment underwent substantial remodeling during metastasis, resulting in the emergence of distinct subpopulations characterized by unique transcriptional profiles and functional properties. Notably, the tumor thrombus exhibited an increased proportion of a TAM subpopulation expressing SELENOP (TAM_SELENOP), while a CXCL10-expressing TAM population was less abundant. Functional annotation revealed that TT myeloid cells upregulated genes associated with immune checkpoint and evasion (e.g., CSF1R, CD163) and angiogenesis, suggesting a more immunosuppressive and pro-angiogenic role at the TT site. The enrichment of pathways related to response to Hyperoxia and IL15 Signaling in myeloid cells prevalent in the tumor thrombus further underscores their adaptation to the potentially distinct metabolic and cytokine milieu of the vasculature. The upregulation of CD46 and MARCO and the enrichment of the WNT signaling pathway in TT myeloid cells point towards potential mechanisms driving this altered microenvironment. These findings highlight a dynamic adaptation of the myeloid compartment, potentially fostering an environment conducive to TT growth and immune evasion within the tumor thrombus.
Analysis of the epithelial cell compartment revealed substantial heterogeneity and distinct transcriptional and genomic profiles between the primary and TT sites. 3p loss and 5q gain detected in our CNV analysis are consistent with previously reported driver alterations in ccRCC , , , . During the 1980s, cytogenetic and molecular genetic research revealed that deletion of the short arm of chromosome 3 (3p) was the most frequent genetic alteration observed in renal cell carcinoma , , , and VHL tumor suppressor gene was found to be located at position 3p25 on chromosome 3 , . The accumulation of HIF proteins stimulates angiogenesis and metabolic reprogramming, while activation of NF-κB signaling increases inflammatory responses . Impairment of the extracellular matrix and cell junctions facilitates tumor invasion and metastasis , , . And the upregulation of PI3K-AKT signaling further enhances tumor aggressiveness . Collectively, these changes driven by VHL deficiency play a central role in the initiation and progression of clear cell renal cell carcinoma. Specific epithelial cell subpopulations were enriched in either the primary tumor or the tumor thrombus, suggesting a selection or adaptation of certain tumor cell states during metastasis. The upregulation of genes associated with cell migration and invasion (MMP7, CXCL1) in TT epithelial cells, coupled with the increased enrichment of focal adhesion pathways, indicates a potential functional specialization facilitating extravasation and survival in the new environment. Furthermore, the tumor thrombus displayed higher levels of copy number variation compared to the primary tumor, suggesting ongoing genomic instability that may contribute to the acquisition of TT traits. In summary, our analysis of epithelial cells demonstrates significant heterogeneity and distinct transcriptional and genomic profiles between primary and TT ccRCC tissues, highlighting an adaptation of tumor cells to facilitate dissemination and survival in the TT niche.
Finally, our investigation into intercellular communication networks revealed a more active and rewired communication landscape within the tumor thrombus compared to the primary tumor. While the average interaction strength was similar, the increased number of inferred interactions in the TT site suggests a more complex interplay between different cell types. Notably, VWF signaling, potentially mediating endothelial-tumor cell interactions, showed higher communication probability in the tumor thrombus, hinting at mechanisms facilitating tumor cell adhesion during intravasation. The stronger outgoing interaction strength of fibroblasts in the TT niche and the potentially enhanced TGF-beta signaling between fibroblasts and myeloid cells suggest a role for stromal remodeling and immune suppression in the TT microenvironment. Differential expression of ligand-receptor pairs between fibroblasts and specific TAM subpopulations further indicates altered recruitment or polarization of immune cells at the TT site. Collectively, our analysis of cell-cell interactions reveals a distinct communication landscape in the tumor thrombus, characterized by a potentially more active and rewired network that may contribute to the establishment and progression of metastasis.
Although this study is limited by the inclusion of only a single case of clear cell renal cell carcinoma with venous tumor thrombus, and further validation in larger cohorts is warranted, our analysis nevertheless provides valuable insights and generates important hypotheses regarding the complex biology, as well as the cellular and molecular characteristics, of ccRCC-TT. We highlight several potential therapeutic targets within the TT niche, which may have important implications for preventing or treating metastatic disease. We believe these findings lay an important foundation for future research. Studies focusing on the functional validation of these identified cell populations and signaling pathways, ideally in larger patient cohorts and utilizing in vivo models, could facilitate a deeper mechanistic understanding and improved clinical management of ccRCC patients.

## Methods
4.1

## Human specimens
NAT, tumor, and thrombus specimens from a single treatment-naïve patient with clear cell renal cell carcinoma (ccRCC) (n = 1) were procured from Yantai Yuhuangding Hospital in Shandong, China. All experimental protocols received approval from the Institutional Research Ethics Committees (IRB) of both BGI and Yuhuangding Hospital, and informed consent was obtained from the enrolled patient for participation in the current study.

4.2

## scRNAseq
All tissues were processed immediately after dissection from the ccRCC patient. The collected tissues were washed three times with 1 × PBS to remove residual blood and then cut into small pieces in RPMI-1640 medium (Invitrogen). These tissue fragments were digested using the MACS Multi Tissue Dissociation Kit following the manufacturer’s instructions. The resulting cell suspension was filtered through a 40-μm cell strainer, and the dissociated cells were centrifuged at 400 × g for 15 min. The supernatant was discarded, and the cell pellets were washed twice with 1 × PBS, then re-suspended in PBS. Single-cell RNA-seq libraries were constructed using the MGI DNBelab C Series Reagent Kit according to the manufacturer’s protocols. The prepared library was then deep-sequenced on the DIPSEQ T1 sequencing platform.

4.3

## Raw data processing, quality control, and Seurat analysis
We employed the Seurat R package (version 5.1.0) to analyze the gene expression matrices. Cells with > 6000 or < 200 gene numbers or with over 50 % mitochondrial-derived UMI counts were considered low-quality cells and were removed. And DoubletFinder R package (version 2.0.4) was used to removal of doublets. The output- filtered expression matrix was then normalized with the function “NormalizeData”, identification of 2000 genes of high cell-to-cell variation by using the function “FindVariableFeatures”, reduce the dimensionality with principal component analysis (PCA), remove potential batch effect with harmony R package (version 1.2.0), and UMAP was performed on the top 50 PCs by using the function “RunUMAP”. The cluster-specific marker genes were identified by pair-wise comparisons of each cluster against all other clusters using the “FindAllMarkers” function in the Seurat package (thresh.use = 0.25, min.pct = 0.25, only.pos = TRUE).

4.4

## Tissue preference of clusters
To characterize the tissue distribution of different aspects of immune clusters, ratio of observed over expected cell numbers (Ro/e) were calculated and used to indicate preferences with Startrac R package (version 0.1.0) as described by Zhang et al. . In the analysis of cell clusters, a ratio of Ro/e > 1 indicates that the cells within a given cluster are more frequently observed in a specific tissue than would be expected by chance, suggesting an enrichment of this cluster in that tissue. Conversely, a ratio of Ro/e < 1 signifies that the cells of the cluster are less frequently observed in the tissue compared to random expectations, indicating a depletion of this cluster in the tissue.

4.5

## Signature score analysis
We utilized cell scores to evaluate the extent of expression of a specific predefined gene set by individual cells. To delineate T cell phenotypes, we employed the “AddModuleScore” function to calculate cell scores utilizing well-established signatures associated with cytotoxicity, exhaustion, naïveté, and residency.

4.6

## Pathway enrichment analysis
To compare signaling pathway enrichment between primary and TT sites, “FindMarkers” function was applied to identify cluster-specific differentially expressed genes between the primary and TT sites, we utilized the “fgsea” package (version 1.30.0) with default parameters. This analysis involved testing the enrichment of Gene Ontology (GO) and KEGG terms using a pre-ranked gene list based on fold changes. Additionally, to further investigate the heterogeneity of epithelial cells and tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), Gene Set Variation Analysis (GSVA, version 1.52.3) was performed, employing both KEGG and GO terms.

4.7

## Pseudotime trajectory analysis
To reconstruct the cellular differentiation trajectory of CD8 + T cell subsets, we employed Monocle 3 (version 1.3.1) . Specifically, we divided the clusters into large, well-separated partitions using the “cluster_cells” function and subsequently fitted a principal graph within each partition using the “learn_graph” function. The resulting principal graph was visualized on the UMAP plot as "skeleton lines," which represent the differentiation trajectories. Based on prior knowledge, we designated the naive T cell cluster, CD8_LEF1_Tn, as the root of the trajectory for CD8 + T cells.

4.8

## Single-cell copy number variation analysis
To investigate the heterogeneity of copy number alterations (CNAs) in epithelial cells at both the primary tumor and TT sites, we utilized the inferCNV R package to estimate CNV levels . Endothelial cells and fibroblasts were used as reference cell types. The analysis followed the procedure outlined in the official tutorial (https://github.com/broadinstitute/inferCNV). Specifically, the expression matrix was derived by extracting the reference cell and epithelial cell locations from the output files. Subsequently, average cell scores for each chromosome were computed individually to characterize the CNV patterns across the samples.
