## Abstract

## Background
High-grade glioma (HGG) cells reactivate neurodevelopmental programs regulated by ion channels to drive tumor progression. The activity of voltage-gated sodium channels (VGSCs) is fundamental to development, a target of blood-brain barrier (BBB)-permeable FDA-approved drugs, and aids tumor advancement in several cancers. However, the contribution of VGSC activity to HGG pathology remains unknown.

## Methods
Using single-cell and spatial transcriptomics, proteomics, and immunohistochemistry, we profiled the expression landscape of the VGSC family in patient tumors from two HGGs: adult glioblastoma and pediatric diffuse midline glioma (DMG). We further validated VGSC expression and function in HGG patient-derived cell lines using RNA, protein, and electrophysiological analyses, and assessed the anticancer efficacy of VGSC-modulating drugs in vitro through cell viability and invasion assays.

## Results
VGSCs α subunits targeted by different classes of VGSC-drugs are differentially expressed within DMG and glioblastoma. Overall, VGSCs that are sensitive to the neurotoxin, tetrodotoxin (TTX), and in normal physiology are expressed in the nervous system were upregulated by invasive HGG cells at the leading edge of DMG and glioblastoma tumors. Whereas the TTX-insensitive cardiac VGSC NaV1.5 was distinctly more abundant within the cellular tumor of the DMG microenvironment. VGSC-expressing HGG cells within both microenvironments receive oncogenic glutamatergic inputs from surrounding neurons. RNA, protein and electrophysiological analysis of patient-derived HGG cells supported our in vivo findings, where NaV1.5 plays a significant role in DMG cell lines, conducting TTX-insensitive transient and persistent sodium currents. Overall, VGSC-targeting drugs had limited anticancer efficacy; however, GS967 a persistent current blocker, significantly inhibited the invasiveness of a DMG cell line by ~ 33%.

## Conclusion
Inhibiting intrinsic VGSC persistent currents suppresses invasiveness in DMG subpopulations and may further hinder HGG progression by buffering oncogenic depolarizations from neuron-glioma synaptic activity. Therefore, VGSC-drugs targeting persistent sodium currents offer untapped therapeutic options for treating HGG.

## Introduction
High-grade gliomas (HGG) like glioblastoma and diffuse midline glioma (DMG) are aggressive and malignant brain cancers with abysmal prognoses [ ]. Adults with glioblastoma, typically succumb within five years [ ] and children with DMG, a pediatric HGG, typically do not survive beyond a year after diagnosis [ ]. Standard treatment interventions are ineffective and include radiotherapy and surgery, together with temozolomide chemotherapy for glioblastoma [ ]. A fundamental reason for treatment failure is a collective underestimation of how the intrinsic neural properties of HGG cells drive the disease [ ]. Derived from neural precursor cells, subpopulations of malignant HGG cells reactivate different neurodevelopmental mechanisms in response to paracrine, chemical, and electrical signals, driving tumor growth, invasion, and drug resistance [ ]. Neurons at the tumor’s leading edge are significant sources of signals, activating pathological bioelectrical mechanisms mediated by ion channels. In glioblastoma, the tumor’s leading edge is a high functional connectivity zone [ , ] where glioma cells and neurons form neurogliomal glutamatergic synapses through the reciprocal exchange of growth factors, glycoproteins and semaphorins [ ], proteins used to shape the developing nervous system; a reciprocity also observed in DMG [ ]. Presynaptic neurons release glutamate, depolarizing the postsynaptic HGG cell by activating ligand-gated ion channels, including Ca2+-permeable AMPA receptors, driving tumor growth and invasion [ ].
Other neurodevelopmental programs directed by ion channel activity are employed by heterogeneous multicellular tumor networks within glioblastoma and DMG, conferring distinct hallmarks. Subpopulations of HGG cells connect through neurite-like tumor microtubes, forming therapy-resistant cell networks; connections reminiscent of early brain development [ ]. In glioblastoma, these networks, linked by gap junctions, are controlled by pacemaker cell hubs that propagate Ca2+ waves through Ca2+-activated potassium channels, stimulating proliferative pathways [ ]. Another highly invasive glioblastoma cell network mimics immature neuron migration patterns, leading to tumor cell infiltration and colonization within the brain at a pace that positively correlates with the number of transient glutamatergic inputs [ ]. Indeed, the anticonvulsant AMPA receptor antagonist perampanel inhibits HGG growth and invasion in experimental models [ , ]. However, translating these insights into therapeutic advantages remains challenging due to safety concerns of the broad-acting drugs that have undesirable safety and target profiles [ ]. To address these barriers, our study focused on voltage-gated sodium channels (VGSCs), whose biology aligns with the maladaptive neural properties of HGG cells, to explore their functional role in HGG progression and their possible use as targets for pharmacological intervention.
VGSCs are hetero-oligomeric polypeptides that comprise a functional channel-forming α subunit associated with one or more modulatory β subunits (β1–4) [ ]. These transmembrane proteins selectively conduct sodium ions (Na+) into the cell, causing membrane depolarization, a mechanism fundamental to central nervous system (CNS) development [ ] and later, for neuronal physiology, muscle contraction, and hormone regulation [ ]. Mutations in VGSCs cause channelopathies and are targets of FDA-approved drugs [ ] that pass the blood-brain barrier (BBB), providing a substantial potential therapeutic resource for HGG [ ]. VGSCs are significantly upregulated in multiple organs, systemic and other CNS cancers, including gliomas, neuroblastomas, astrocytomas, and oligodendrogliomas [ – ], and have been implicated in aiding proliferation, migration, and invasion, where oncogenic behaviors are linked to the aberrant activity of specific α subunits, including tetrodotoxin (TTX)-insensitive NaV1.5, and TTX-sensitive NaV1.6 and NaV1.7 [ ]. Moreover, disruption of Na+ homeostasis within solid tumors is associated with more aggressive and metastatic behaviors [ ], a significant observation as glioblastoma tumors in patients exhibit significantly elevated Na+ concentrations compared to unaffected areas [ ]. Indeed, VGSC currents have been recorded from resected glioblastoma tissue [ ], and a positive correlation has been found between mutational burden in genes encoding proteins that regulate Na+ homeostasis, including VGSCs, and more aggressive forms of glioblastoma characterized by shorter survival times [ ].
However, the contribution of individual VGSC subunits to pediatric and adult HGG pathophysiology is poorly understood. Here, we used proteomics, single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq), spatial transcriptomics, and immunohistochemistry on patient-resected tumor tissue with in vitro analysis and electrophysiological experiments in patient-derived HGG cell lines, to examine the VGSC landscape of DMG and glioblastoma tumors and the anticancer efficacy of VGSC-modulating drugs on tumor growth and invasiveness. We found that VGSC-expressing HGG cells receive glutamatergic synaptic inputs from surrounding cells. Moreover, NaV1.5 plays a significant role in DMG cells by permeating persistent sodium currents that, if inhibited, significantly halt DMG invasiveness. Altogether, our results suggest that targeting persistent sodium currents therapeutically could slow the rapid progression of the disease.

## Methods

## Transcriptomic resources and analysis
Adult glioblastoma (glioblastoma) bulk RNA-Sequencing (RNA-seq) data (number of patients n = 151, with normal tissue and recurrent tumor samples removed from analysis; Supplementary Tables 1 and 2) were accessed from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) using TCGAbiolinks [ ] and summarized as gene-level counts. RNA-seq data from 75 pediatric primary HGG tumor samples (non-brainstem high-grade glioma NBS-HGG, n = 44; DMG, n = 31; Supplementary Tables 3 and 4) were obtained from the St. Jude Cloud [ , ] (https://www.stjude.cloud) and pre-processed and analyzed as detailed in Shard et al., 2025 [ ].
Four publicly available primary glioblastoma tumor scRNA-seq datasets (GSE131928, n = 20 [ ]; GSE173280, n = 10 [ ]; GSE182109, n = 10 [ ] and GSE141383, n = 9 [ ]) were downloaded and combined with our previously published scRNA-seq dataset (Ebert et al., 2020, n = 3 [ ], available upon request to G.A.G). For DMG scRNA-seq analysis, we used the following publicly available scRNA-seq datasets (GSE184357, n = 30 [ ]; GSE102130, n = 6 [ ]). From the GSE184357 [ ] dataset, we only included pediatric samples in our analysis. Pre-processing pipelines, including quality control filtering and normalization, were performed as we described previously [ ]. Furthermore, cell clusters in the scRNA-seq datasets were assigned cell-type identities based on the designations provided in the original publications. In the GSE141383 dataset [ ], where author-defined labels were not supplied, cell types were annotated by matching each cluster’s expression profile to curated glioma/glioblastoma reference signatures using CellKb (https://www.cellkb.com). Malignant cell states, characterized by expression of a set of marker genes were determined as described previously [ ]. Four FFPE glioblastoma patient samples used for spatial transcriptomics are detailed in Supplementary Tables 5 and were obtained from the South Australian Neurological Tumour Bank (SANTB), Australia. In addition, spatial transcriptomics data were also obtained from five DMG samples from a previously published study (GSE194329 [ ]). Pre-processing and quality control of spatial transcriptomics data from these tissues, as well as annotation of different tumor regions (leading edge, cellular tumour, microvascular proliferation, pseudopalisading cells), were performed as we described previously [ ]. Spatial expression maps of VGSC genes were generated using the SpatialFeaturePlot function in Seurat (v5). Spots were annotated as belonging to a tumor region if the mean region signature score in that spot exceeded the threshold value, defined as the average of the mean region signature score across all spots in the tissue plus one standard deviation. ## Cell surface proteomics and mass spectrometry
Primary tumor samples were analyzed using quadruplicate cell surface proteomics with an amino-oxy-biotin labeling technique as described before [ ].

## Cell culture
Patient-derived glioblastoma GBM6 (male) [ ], GBM39 (male) [ ] were obtained from Professor Paul Mischel Stanford University, USA and SB2 (male) [ ], RN1 (male) [ ], JK2 (male) [ ] and SJH1 (male) [ ] from Professor Bryan Day, QIMR Berghofer, Australia. DMG cell lines were obtained by Professor Michelle Monje, Stanford University, USA SU-DIPG36B (female) [ ], SU-DIPGXIII (female) [ ], and SU-DIPG38 (female) [ ]. All cells were cultured as previously described [ , ]. Cell lines were routinely PCR-tested (every 1–3 months) for mycoplasma by the Media Lab at the Harry Perkins Institute of Medical Research, Australia. Unless otherwise stated, cell lines were cultured as neurospheres grown on ultra-low attachment plates (Corning, CLS3262-20EA). Cell lines grown as monolayers were cultured on cultrex-treated plates (R&D Systems, 3432-001−01).

## Cell line mRNA extraction and reverse transcription
RNA was isolated from 1 × 106 cells using an RNEasy Midi Kit (Qiagen, 75144) according to the manufacturer’s instructions, which included DNase treatment to minimize genomic DNA contamination. RNA quantity and quality (260/280 ratios > 2) were measured using a Nanodrop ND-1000 Spectrophotometer (ThermoFisher Scientific; RRID: SCR_016517). For qPCR, 1 µg of RNA was reverse-transcribed using a QuantiTect Reverse Transcription Kit (Qiagen, 205311) according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

## qPCR
All qPCR assays used TaqMan™ Fast Advanced Master Mix (ThermoFisher Scientific, 4444556). qPCR Amplifications and ∆Ct values calculations were performed using the QuantStudio™ 7 Flex Real-Time PCR System (RRID: SCR_020245) and associated software. An initial screen of 32 reference genes (Supplementary Table 6) in glioblastoma cell lines was performed using a Fast 96-well TaqMan™ Array and Human Endogenous Controls Plates (ThermoFisher Scientific, 4396840) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. GAPDH was selected as the reference gene for VGSC transcript expression analysis, showing a mean Ct standard deviation of 0.9 (SFig. a). Expression analysis of VGSC genes in HGG patient-derived cell lines was performed using TaqMan™ Array Human Voltage-Gated Ion Channels Plates (ThermoFisher Scientific, 4414099; Supplementary Table 6), following the manufacturer’s protocol, analyzing at least three passages for each cell line, with data expressed as mean ± SEM.

## Cell viability and invasion assays
VGSC drugs used in this study are detailed in Supplementary Table 7 and were diluted to final concentrations of 10 µM or 50 µM. The effect of the drugs on cell proliferation was assessed as previously described [ ] using the ViaLight Plus Cell Proliferation and Cytotoxicity BioAssay Kit (Lonza, LT07-221) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Cell viability was calculated as a percentage of the vehicle control (100%). The IC50 values of oxcarbazepine administered to DIPG cells were determined from dose-response curves using a non-linear regression curve fit (GraphPad Prism 9.0, RRID: SCR_002798).
To measure invasive potential, GBM6 and DIPGXIII cells were seeded 5000 cells/well in 96-well ULA plates (Revvity, 6055330), centrifuged at 300 x g for three minutes, and incubated for three days to form neurospheres. 40 µL of media was removed from each well and the plate cooled on ice before 50 µL of pre-thawed Cultrex Spheroid Invasion Extracellular Matrix (In Vitro Technologies, 3500-096−03) was added to each well. Plates were centrifuged as before, drug treated (10 µM) and incubated at 37 °C for one hour. 100 µL of media was added before a final centrifugation to center the spheres for imaging. Plates were consecutively imaged every 12 hours for seven days using the IncuCyte S3 Live Cell Analysis System (Sartorius, RRID: SCR_023147). Cell invasion was measured with Incucyte 2022B Rev2 software (Sartorius) and normalized to vehicle controls. Proliferation and invasion analyses for each cell line were performed using at least three technical replicates, with data expressed as mean ± SEM.

## Sectioning of neurospheres and patient tissue
HGG cell line neurospheres were isolated by centrifugation at 1100 rpm for five minutes, washed in PBS (ThermoFisher, 10010049), and spun again. Neurospheres were fixed with 4% PFA (ThermoFisher, 28908) for five minutes, washed twice with PBS, and incubated with a 1:50 dilution of trypan blue (ThermoFisher, 15250061) in PBS for 10 minutes. Stained neurospheres were cryoprotected for at least 12 hours in 30% sucrose, embedded in O.C.T. compound (Agar Scientific, AGR1180), and frozen on dry ice. Neurospheres were cut into five µm sections using a −21 °C microtome Leica CM1860 cryostat (RRID: SCR_025772), mounted on Superfrost Plus slides (ThermoFisher Scientific, J1800AMNT), air-dried for 30 minutes, and stored at − 80 °C. Fresh-frozen glioblastoma and DMG tumor samples were obtained from the Sydney Brain Tumour Bank and Queensland Children’s Tumour Bank, respectively (Supplementary Table 5). Tissues were stored at −80 °C and thawed overnight at −20 °C before sectioning. Tumors were embedded in O.C.T. compound (Leica, 14020108926), sectioned at 10 μm, and processed as described for the neurospheres.

## Indirect Immunofluorescence
Samples were thawed at room temperature before processing. Sections were fixed for 10 minutes with 2% PFA in sodium acetate buffer (pH 6), followed by a five minute PBS wash to dissolve excess O.C.T. compound. To prevent non-specific staining, sections were blocked in 10% donkey serum (Jackson ImmunoResearch, 017-000−121) in PBS (pH 7.4) with 0.2% Triton (PBS-T) for 90 min. They were incubated overnight at 4 °C with primary antibodies (Supplementary Table 8). After PBS-T washing, sections were labeled for two hours at room temperature with fluorophore-conjugated secondary antibodies (Supplementary Table 9). Slides were washed with PBS, incubated in a 0.5% Sudan black (Sigma-Aldrich, 4197-25−5) solution for 10 minutes to eliminate lipofuscin autofluorescence, washed again, and then mounted using Fluoromount-G+/-DAPI (Invitrogen, 0100 − 20/0100-01). Specimens were visualized and scanned using either the Nikon A1Si or FLIM confocal microscope system.

## Image analysis
For DMG tissues marker co-expression was independently estimated using CellProfiler Image Analysis Software (RRID: SCR_007358). Nuclei were identified as primary objects in the blue channel of Nikon confocal microscope images, while OLIG2-positive cells were identified from among these as secondary objects in the 647-channel. NaV expression was identified as the primary object in the green channel. Cells co-localizing with these markers were counted as positive for that marker. The abundance of all OLIG2-positive cells, also positive for NaV was calculated as an overall percentage. Thresholds were initially calculated automatically using the Otsu two-class method and then adjusted empirically. For glioblastoma tissues, NaV/marker co-expression analysis was performed using the ImageJ (RRID: SCR_003070) cell counter plugin, with manual counting. Analyses were performed using at least three biological and technical replicates, with data expressed as mean ± SEM.

## Whole-cell patch clamp analysis
Whole-cell VGSC currents were recorded from HGG patient-derived cell lines using the Nanion Patchliner® (Nanion Technologies, Germany) as previously described [ ]. Voltage protocols were performed on individual cells from a holding potential of −120 mV, with 100 ms voltage steps in 5 mV increments injected every 2 s, ranging from − 120 mV to + 50 mV. Using this protocol, normalized peak transient currents were calculated by expressing the current measured at each voltage step as a percentage of the peak current. 3 µM TTX was administered to cells to delineate TTX-sensitive to insensitive VGSC currents. %INaP of the peak INaT was calculated from the activation protocol, as the mean current between 20 and 30 ms after the start of the 0 mV test pulse from − 120 mV and expressed INaP as a fraction of the maximum INaT.

## Statistical analysis
Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism 9.0 (RRID: SCR_002798), with a P-value less than 0.05 considered significant. Experimental replicates for each assay are detailed in their corresponding methodology section. Data were tested for normality using the Shapiro-Wilk test, and outliers were identified with Grubbs’ test. Depending on the normality of the data, statistical analysis used either parametric (Student’s t-test, One-way ANOVA with Dunnett’s or Tukey’s multiple comparison test, or Two-way ANOVA with Tukey’s multiple comparison test) or non-parametric (Mann-Whitney, Kruskal-Wallis with Dunn’s multiple comparison) tests. We used Kaplan–Meier curves with a log-rank test to assess the correlation between VGSC mRNA expression and patient survival.

## Results
To determine gene expression of the VGSC gene family in pediatric HGG and glioblastoma, we analyzed transcriptomic data obtained from primary tumors from the St Jude’s Children’s Hospital (DMG and NBS-HGG) and TCGA (glioblastoma, Fig.  a, c; values of log2 > 5 were considered as genes being expressed). Genes for pore-forming α subunits SCN4A (NaV1.4) and SCN10A (NaV1.8) were not expressed in pediatric and adult HGG datasets, whereas all β subunit genes were present (Fig.  a, c). Analysis of the St Jude’s dataset revealed that in NBS-HGG and DMG VGSC transcripts typically present in the CNS (SCN1A, NaV1.1; SCN2A, NaV1.2; SCN3A, NaV1.3; SCN8A, NaV1.6 and SCN9A, NaV1.7), heart (SCN5A, NaV1.5), and peripheral nervous system (SCN11A, NaV1.9) were expressed (Fig.  a). Transcript expression was comparable between NBS-HGG and DMG (Two-way ANOVA; SFig. a). In our surface proteomic analysis of two DMG patient tissues not all VGSC transcripts yielded protein expression at the cell surface. However, NaV1.2, NaV1.3, NaV1.5 and β1–4 were detected at DMG cell surface membranes (Fig.  b; for each sample, analysis of different heterogeneous regions is shown).
 Using the Xena genomics platform, we asked whether changes in VGSC transcript levels correlated with DMG patient survival. Analysis of the Puget et al. (2012) study [ ] that includes 51 patients shows that changes in transcript expression for nearly all VGSC genes do not influence survival, as only elevated levels of SCN1A (NaV1.1) mRNA expression correlated to worse overall patient survival (SFig. b; median survival high: 1.0 years vs. low: 2.0 years, log rank test P =.01). Therefore, changes in NaV1.1 expression correlating to poorer outcomes in a larger patient cohort suggests that our failure to detect NaV1.1 in our cell surface proteomic analysis was likely incidental to our two DMG samples, or a significant pool of the expressed protein resided intracellularly [ ]. Moreover, as NaV1.1 is abundant in inhibitory interneurons in the CNS [ ], we decided not to further pursue its role in HGG, as its inhibition may pose significant neurological problems for patients [ ].
We next focused on glioblastoma, where VGSC gene expression was similar to pediatric HGG (Fig.  c), albeit at significantly reduced levels (Two-way ANOVA; SFig. a, c), and SCN5A (NaV1.5) was poorly expressed (Fig.  c; SFig. c; SCN5A log2 gene expression Kruskal-Wallis with Dunn’s test: DMG vs. glioblastoma P <.0001 and NBS-HGG vs. glioblastoma P <.0001). We asked whether specific VGSC transcripts were upregulated for each glioblastoma molecular subtype defined as classic, proneural, mesenchymal. SCN2A (NaV1.2), SCN8A (NaV1.6), SCN1B (β1) and SCN2B (β2) were consistently expressed across all three molecular phenotypes and the remaining VGSC α subunit transcripts and SCN3B (β3) were upregulated in proneural tumors (SFig. c). Our surface proteomic analysis of glioblastoma primary tumors demonstrated that CNS α subunits were the most broadly expressed, with individual tumors showing variable α- and β- subunit expression and confirming a weak expression of NaV1.5 in one tumor (Fig.  d). Analysis of the TGCA glioblastoma dataset including 149 patients showed increased levels of SCN9A (NaV1.7) and SCN2B (β2) mRNA expression correlated to worse overall patient survival (SFig. d, e; SCN9A high: median survival 1.0 years vs. low: 1.2 years, log-rank test P =.02; SCN2B high: 1.1 years vs. low: 1.2 years, log-rank test P =.04).

## VGSCs are expressed by HGG cells
Since our RNA-seq and proteomic analysis used bulk tumor tissue, we used scRNA-seq analysis and immunohistochemistry to identify the malignant and nonmalignant cell populations within the tumor that expressed VGSCs subunits (Fig.  ). Single-cell analysis of two DMG datasets comprising 36 patients revealed that of the VGSCs subunits present in our proteomics analysis NaV1.2, NaV1.3, NaV1.5, β3 and β4 were predominantly expressed by tumor cells, with additional expression of NaV1.5 and β4 transcripts in endothelial cells (Fig.  a). Similarly, analysis of five glioblastoma scRNA-seq datasets comprising 52 patients showed that the α subunits present in our proteomic analysis with β3 and β4 highly expressed by tumor cells. Like DMG tumors, β1 and β2 were more strongly expressed by other somatic cell types, like oligodendrocytes (Fig. a, b). 
 As glioblastoma contains tumor cells in different transcriptional states, we explored whether there were any differences in VGSC subunit expression between states. We therefore annotated malignant cells according to their transcriptional state, as reported by Neftel et al. (2019) [ ]. These glioblastoma cell transcriptional states include neural precursor-like (NPC-like), oligodendrocyte precursor-like (OPC-like), astrocyte-like (AC-like), and mesenchymal-like (MES-like) states. We identified that VGSC subunits are enriched in NPC1/2- and OPC-like cells (SFig. a). 
We next concentrated our immunohistochemistry analysis on the targets of VGSC modulators, the ion-conducting α subunits. Immunohistochemistry analysis of three DMG tumors showed that co-expression with the glioma cell marker OLIG2 demonstrated that NaV1.2, NaV1.3, NaV1.5 were comparably expressed in tumor cells (Fig. c, d; P =.06, Kruskal-Wallis test). Moreover co-expression of these VGSC with Nestin, a marker for glioma cell populations that have demonstrated migratory and invasive phenotypes in in vivo HGG models [ – ], revealed that both NaV1.2 and NaV1.3 were less abundant in Nestin+ cells (NaV1.2 OLIG2+ vs. Nestin+ P <.0001; NaV1.3 OLIG2+ vs. Nestin+; P =.02; Mann-Whitney test) with NaV1.5 expression levels remaining consistent (NaV1.5 OLIG2+ vs. Nestin+; P =.9; Mann-Whitney test), showing the greatest co-localization with Nestin (Fig. c, d).
Regarding glioblastoma we focused our immunohistochemistry on several factors, including (i) the consistency of expression across tumor subtypes: NaV1.2 and NaV1.6; (ii) the oncofetal characteristics of cancer: NaV1.3 is upregulated during embryogenesis and NaV1.2 is upregulated during early postnatal development; and (iii) potential druggability: NaV1.7 is a specific target of BBB permissible pain drugs and NaV1.2, NaV1.3 and NaV1.6 targets for anticonvulsants.
Our RNA-seq and surface proteomic analysis used primary tumors; therefore, we included recurrent tumors in our immunohistochemistry analysis to ask whether VGSC expression changes in the secondary disease (Fig.  e-g). Overall, NaV1.2 was the most frequently expressed in OLIG2+ and Nestin+ cells (Two-way ANOVA; Fig.  g). NaV1.6 was significantly upregulated in primary tumor cells that were Nestin+ and although this observation was similar in recurrent tumors it was not statistically significant (Two-way ANOVA; Fig.  e-g). NaV1.3 was expressed at lower levels and showed no differential expression between OLIG2+ nor Nestin+ cells (Two-way ANOVA; Fig.  e-g). NaV1.7 was not detected in the primary or recurrent disease (Fig.  e-g). In summary, DMG tumors comprise fewer VGSC α subunit subtypes compared to glioblastoma, with NaV1.5, a channel targeted by cardiovascular drugs, being the α subunit most abundantly detected in Nestin+ DMG cells. Overall, in glioblastoma CNS-typical subtypes, including NaV1.2 and NaV1.6, likely play a more significant role in tumor pathology.

## VGSCs are upregulated at the leading edge of HGG tumors
Next, we asked where VGSCs are expressed in the DMG and glioblastoma tumor microenvironment using the spatial transcriptome of four glioblastoma tissues analyzed by the Visium CytAssist platform, and a spatially resolved public dataset of five DMG tissues [ ] (Figs. and ). We used gene expression markers identified by Puchalski et al., [ ] to define four tumor tissue microenvironments: (a) leading edge, (b) cellular tumor, (c) microvascular proliferation and (d) pseudopalisading cells around necrosis (Figs. a and a reproduced from [ ]) and used Manders’ overlap coefficient analysis to assess the degree of overlap of VGSC transcript expression (Figs. b and b, α subunits; Figs. c and c, β subunits) with the transcriptional signatures of the microenvironments (Figs. d and d). We only performed quantification where a region of interest was present in a tissue, which reduced the sample size for some regions; for example, only one DMG tumor tissue contained the leading edge. Nonetheless, our analysis revealed that VGSCs were broadly expressed across tissue regions in DMG tissues. However, individual VGSCs displayed distinct patterns of spatial enrichment in specific regions. CNS α subunit genes, including SCN2A (NaV1.2) and SCN3A (NaV1.3), were concentrated at the leading edge in DMG, with SCN3A additionally upregulated within the cellular tumor region (Fig. b, d). SCN5A (NaV1.5) was enriched within the cellular tumor region (Fig. b, d). β1–4 subunits concentrated at the leading edge (Fig. c, d), where β4 was broadly expressed throughout all microenvironments, and β2 and β3 showing additional expression within the cellular tumor. Comparatively, in glioblastoma, VGSC subunit transcripts were mainly concentrated at the leading edge (Fig. b-d). Overall, the concentration of VGSC subunits at the leading edge suggests that they play a significant role in invasion. Distinct patterns of individual α and β subunit localization in DMG, such as SCN5A (NaV1.5) within the cellular tumor, suggest that VGSCs play additional functional roles in DMG tumor maintenance and growth not employed in glioblastoma, potentially providing an opportunity to target individual VGSC subunits to combat the pediatric disease differentially.
 

## VGSC expressing HGG cells appose vGluT1 + synapses
In glioblastoma, the leading edge forms a zone of high functional connectivity, characterized by the active formation of glutamatergic neurogliomal synapses [ ]. Through these contacts, neuronal activity in this region causes depolarization of HGG cell membranes, driving tumor growth and invasiveness [ , ]. Therefore, by combining spatial transcriptomic and immunohistochemistry analysis, we next investigated whether VGSC α subunit-positive HGG cells receive glutamatergic synaptic inputs from neurons. We hypothesized that HGG membrane depolarization derived from glutamate binding to AMPA receptors on the cell surface (Ca2+ influx), would in turn initiate the opening of VGSCs (Na + influx), resulting in an amplification of membrane depolarization. Particularly as in glioblastoma VGSC and AMPA receptor transcripts (GRIA1-4) are enriched in the same cell populations, i.e., NPC- and OPC-like cells (SFig. a, b). In our analysis we used OLIG2 (OLIG2) and Nestin (NES) to define our HGG cells, a mature neuron marker, neurofilament protein H (NFH), and the presynaptic (vesicular glutamate transporter 1, VGLUT1) and postsynaptic (postsynaptic density protein 95, PSD95; SH3 and multiple ankyrin repeat domains 1, SHANK1) markers of glutamatergic synapses (Figs. e and e; tumor/neuronal marker maps reproduced from [ ]). Using Manders’ overlap coefficient analysis to assess our spatial data, we initially examined where our markers were located within the HGG microenvironment. In glioblastoma, the structural components of neurogliomal glutamatergic synapses were concentrated at the leading edge, and the tumor markers OLIG2 and NES within the cellular tumor (Fig. e, f). However, in DMG tumors synaptic markers were more evenly distributed between the leading edge and cellular tumor, with presynaptic (VGLUT1) and postsynaptic components (SHANK1) concentrated within the cellular tumor and to a lesser degree at the leading edge (Fig. e, f). Interestingly, like NES expression, NFH transcripts were more evenly distributed throughout the four microenvironments, perhaps reflecting the more diffuse nature of DMG that has a less defined leading edge compared to glioblastoma.
Unlike cardiovascular drugs, which can demonstrate higher efficacy for NaV1.5 [ , ], common neurological drugs do not discriminate between VGSC α subunit CNS subtypes. Therefore, we asked whether potentially differentially druggable VGSC NaV1.2+ and NaV1.5+ HGG cells along the leading edge in DMG received glutamatergic inputs from neighboring neurons. We chose NaV1.2 to represent CNS α subunits because our RNA-seq data showed that its transcript expression was consistent between NBS-HGG and DMG (SFig. a), and three molecular subtypes of glioblastoma (SFig. c), and NaV1.2 is substantially concentrated at the leading edge in DMG and glioblastoma (Figs. d and d). Using immunohistochemistry analysis on three DMG patient tumors, we showed that excitatory vGluT1+ synapses apposed NaV1.2 (Fig. g, h) and NaV1.5 (Fig. i, j) OLIG2+ cells. A limitation of our analysis was that we did not consistently identify presynaptic neurons represented by NFH/vGluT1+ co-localization (e.g. Figure j5); therefore, we confirmed the structural integrity of the synapses using SHANK1 and PSD95 as postsynaptic markers apposing vGluT1+ boutons on DMG OLIG2+ and Nestin+ cells respectively (Fig. k, l). Also, in support of our spatial maps vGluT1+ innervation varied considerably in NaV1.2/NaV1.5 OLIG2+ areas, dense in some regions (Fig. g-j) and sparse in others (SFig. b, d).
In primary and recurrent glioblastoma tumors, NaV1.2 OLIG2+ glioma cells were detected in areas of excitatory neuronal innervation (Fig.  g-j; SFig. e, h). However, like DMG, in glioblastoma we observed regions of dense NaV1.2+/vGluT1+ co-localization without NFH (e.g. primary tumor 2 SFig. e) and absent vGluT1+ synaptic innervation in neuron-glioma rich zones (e.g. recurrent tumor 2 SFig. f). Unlike DMG, however, postsynaptic structural components SHANK1 (OLIG2+) and PSD95 (Nestin+) tumor cells typically apposed vGluT1+ synapses co-localized with NFH (Fig.  k-n). In summary, VGSC blockers that target NaV1.5 in DMG and, generally, NaV1.2 in HGG may reduce glutamate-driven HGG proliferation by restricting depolarization of glioma cell membranes.

## Patient-derived HGG cell lines express functional VGSCs
Next, we investigated whether pharmacological modulation of VGSCs using clinically available drugs would block the innate oncogenic properties of HGG patient-derived cell lines. Therefore, to guide our choice of drugs for the assay, we first profiled VGSC subunit transcript expression using Taqman™ qPCR. Initially, we screened 32 reference genes using a TaqMan™ Express Endogenous Control Plate and confirmed that GAPDH expression was consistent in three HGG patient-derived cell lines (Ct mean ± SD, 19.8 ± 0.3; SFig. a). DMG patient-derived cell lines carrying either H3K27M histone mutations H3.3K27M or H3.1K27M expressed genes coding for all CNS α subunits, NaV1.5 and β1–4, where SCN2A (NaV1.2), SCN3A (NaV1.3) and SCN1B (β1) were most highly expressed (Fig. a). Immunocytochemistry analysis of sectioned DMG neurospheres (Fig. b) showed a heterogeneous staining of NaV1.2, NaV1.3 and NaV1.5, although NaV1.3 was undetected in DIPG36B (Fig. b5). Whole-cell patch clamp recordings (Fig. c, SFig e) confirmed that DIPGXIII and DIPG36B expressed functional TTX-sensitive VGSCs (i.e., NaV1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 1.6 and 1.7, black trace in Fig. c) but also TTX-insensitive channels (i.e., NaV1.5, 1.8, 1.9; red trace in Fig. c), which in DIPG36B a H3.1K27M carrying cell line comprised 13 ± 2% of the peak current TTX-sensitive current at 0 mV (mean ± SEM; n = 5) and in DIPGXIII a H3.3K27M carrying cell line comprised 23 ± 7% of the peak current TTX-sensitive current at 0 mV (mean ± SEM; n = 6; Fig. d). We surmised that NaV1.5 carried the TTX-insensitive current as SCN10A (NaV1.8) transcripts were undetected (SFig. b) and the fast inactivation channel kinetics of DMG VGSC currents are not typical of slowly inactivating NaV1.9 channels [ ]. Next, we performed a parallel analysis for glioblastoma patient-derived cell lines corresponding to three molecular subtypes. Although showing a degree of variability between cell lines, the RNA of CNS α subunits and β1–4 were expressed, where SCN8A (NaV1.6) was most consistently expressed across all five cell lines (Fig. e). For technical reasons, in our subsequent immunocytochemistry analysis, we focused on classical cells lines that form non-adherent tumorspheres readily, enriching our culture with glioma stem cells. Immunocytochemistry analysis of neurospheres confirmed the expression of CNS α subunits (e.g. NaV1.2, NaV1.3 and NaV1.6) in classical glioblastoma cell lines (Fig. g), including NaV1.7, an α subunit detected in our proteomic but not immunohistochemistry analysis of patient tumors (SFig. d). Whole-cell patch clamp of classical glioblastoma cell lines confirmed the expression of functional TTX-sensitive α subunits channels, which we have previously reported [ ] (Fig. f). Although qPCR analysis of a third classical cell line from a recurrent tumor (SB2) demonstrated that SCN5A (NaV1.5) was upregulated and translated in SB2 cells (SFig. c, d). Based on the transcript, protein, and functional expression of cell lines, we decided to test BBB-permeable drugs that modulate CNS α subunits and NaV1.5 (detailed in Supplementary Table 7).
 

## Targeting HGG cells with VGSC-modulating drugs
Positive changes in membrane potential cause VGSCs to transition between three conformational states: a closed resting state that transitions to an open activated state followed by a non-ion conducting inactivated state. Upon opening VGSCs give rise to a fast-inactivating “transient” current (INaT) and/or smaller a non-inactivating “persistent” current (INaP), which are likely gated by different mechanisms [ ]. VGSC drugs bind to different conformational states and can demonstrate different affinities to INaT or INaP (Supplementary Table 7). For example, Class Ic and Id antiarrhythmics, strongly block the persistent sodium current [ ]. Therefore, we confirmed that our cell lines carried INaP. A small INaP was observed in DMG and glioblastoma cell lines (mean ± SEM 2.3% ± 0.7, n = 13; SFig. f), and comparison between the pediatric and adult disease cells did not show significant differences in %INaP. (One-way ANOVA P =.7; SFig. f). However, upon TTX application in DMG cell lines, the percentage of INaP relative to the peak transient current increased significantly, showing a 167% and 300% increase in DIPGXIII and DIPG36B cells, respectively. This suggests that a substantial portion of endogenous persistent sodium currents in these cell lines are derived from TTX-insensitive channels, like NaV1.5, which are abundantly expressed in these cell lines (Fig. a).
We next asked whether modulating VGSC channel behavior using drugs influenced the proliferation of HGG cell lines. The VGSC drugs we chose to screen represented broad drug properties including: (i) drug class, (ii) the α subunit conformational state in which drug binding occurs, (iii) affinity for INaP or INaT, (iv) individual α subunit pharmacology (e.g., Ropivacaine is more efficacious towards NaV1.5 compared to NaV1.2 [ ]), and (v) where possible are FDA-approved and cross the BBB. We measured the effects of these drugs using the ViaLight™ Cell Proliferation assay, which measures cell proliferation by quantifying ATP levels, indicative of cell viability. Initially we incubated HGG cell lines with 50 µM of TTX, a concentration that would block the open pore of both CNS α subunits and NaV1.5. TTX, did not affect proliferation in adult and pediatric HGG patient-derived cell lines (Fig. a). However, because the resting membrane potential of cancer cells are depolarized (between − 50 mV and − 10mV [ ]) and potentially lie within ranges that VGSCs would be in the wrong conformational state for TTX blockade, we assayed VGSC modulators of several classes, which bind to different states of the channel, which possess higher affinities for VGSC subtypes and INaT or INaP (Supplementary Table 7).
 Generally, blockers of CNS α subunits and NaV1.5 INaT or INaP, and the activator Veratridine, failed to significantly inhibit HGG cell line proliferation at 10 and 50 µM (Fig. a; SFig. a); concentrations that lie within a clinically relevant range [ , ]. However, Oxcarbazepine, an anticonvulsant, inhibited the proliferation of DMG patient-derived cell lines with an IC50 of ~15 µM. An effect observed only when cells were cultured as monolayers (SFig. a, b). The effect of Oxcarbazepine was specific as its analogs Carbamazepine and Eslicarbazepine acetate showed no anti-proliferative activity (Fig. a; SFig. a). Oxcarbazepine is a prodrug metabolized by the liver to its pharmacologically active metabolite, 10-hydroxy-10,11-dihydro-carbamazepine, which directly binds to VGSCs. A dose of up to 50 µM of a racemic mixture of its two enantiomers did not affect DMG cell proliferation (SFig. c); therefore, coupled with the poor efficacy of its analogs, we concluded that the mechanism of Oxcarbazepine action was likely off-target.
GS967, an antiarrhythmic drug, is the only available tool that preferentially inhibits INaP and singularly targets VGSCs [ , ]. Generally, its administration showed limited efficacy in blocking HGG cell line proliferation. However, GS967 had varying effects on the proliferation of DMG cell lines. 50 µM GS967 increased DIPG38 proliferation by ~10%, but reduced DIPG36B and DIPGXIII proliferation by ~20–30% compared to vehicle treated controls (SFig. a). To circumvent these effects, we tested a clinically relevant dose of 10 µM but observed little change in DIPG38 proliferation and poor efficacy on the other HGG cell lines (Fig. a).
Since our scRNA-seq and spatial transcriptomics analysis revealed that VGSCs in glioblastoma and DMG are concentrated at the most invasive region of the tumor microenvironment, specifically at the leading edge, we next investigated the effects of 10 µM VGSC modulators on the invasiveness of HGG tumorspheres transplanted into Cultrex, an extracellular matrix solution. We selected HGG cell lines, DIPGXIII and GBM6, because they readily form neurospheres, demonstrate reliable invasive properties in culture, and their VGSC RNA expression and protein expression closely align with that observed in patients (Fig.  ; SFig. b-d). 3D invasion assays revealed that modulating VGSC activity failed to inhibit the invasiveness of the glioblastoma cell line GBM6 (Fig.  d). However, the invasive potential of DIPGXIII cells was more sensitive to VGSC-inhibiting drugs, a DMG cell line that carries a significant TTX-insensitive current (Fig.  a-d). Phenytoin and GS967 significantly inhibited the invasiveness of DIPGXIII (Phenytoin vs. vehicle P =.02; GS967 vs. vehicle P <.0001; One-way ANOVA, Dunnett’s multiple comparison test; Fig.  a, b), with GS967 significantly more efficacious compared to Phenytoin (Phenytoin vs. GS967 P =.03, t-test; Fig.  a, b).

## Discussion
Here, we demonstrate that VGSCs, which are relevant to normal CNS physiology, are also expressed in pediatric and adult HGG, with the cardiac α subunit NaV1.5 additionally enriched in DMG. In glioblastoma, VGSC and AMPA receptor transcripts are upregulated in NPC- and OPC-like cell populations, with VGSC expression concentrated at the tumor’s invasive leading edge: a microenvironment dense in glutamatergic synaptic inputs from neurons. In DMG, CNS VGSCs exhibit a similar expression pattern and innervation, except for NaV1.5, which is concentrated within the cellular tumor, also dense with glutamatergic innervation. Generally, VGSC-targeting drugs were ineffective at suppressing the intrinsic cancer properties of HGG cells except for GS967, which was efficacious in inhibiting the invasive capacity of DMG cells. Therefore, INaP-inhibiting drugs may not only mitigate the intrinsic invasiveness of HGG cells, but also buffer depolarizing neuronal innervation, helping to curb proliferation and invasion.

## Potent blockers of INap as anticancer agents
Consistent with our findings in patient samples, DMG cell lines exhibited higher endogenous NaV1.5 expression than glioblastoma cell lines, as determined by transcript expression, immunocytochemistry, and TTX insensitivity. Although %INaP was similar across all HGG cell lines, TTX application revealed that the proportion of persistent currents was significantly increased in our DMG lines, suggesting that they were largely mediated by TTX-insensitive NaV1.5, as supported by our qPCR and electrophysiology data. Ranolazine and GS967 are BBB-permissible antiarrhythmics that preferentially block INaP over INaT and inhibit NaV1.5, NaV1.7, and other CNS α subunits detected in our HGG cell lines [ , ]. Unlike GS967, which inhibited invasion in DMG cells, Ranolazine had no effect, perhaps reflecting its 130-fold lower potency towards INaP compared to GS967 [ , ]. Unlike the majority of VGSC drugs tested, such as Ranolazine [ ] and Phenytoin (with the latter possessing over 120 known protein targets [ ]), GS967 singularly targets VGSCs [ ]. This specificity suggests that small INaP-mediated changes in membrane potential may play a role in HGG metastasis, particularly in DMG. GS967 passes the BBB and is well-tolerated in rodents [ ], but its safety profile in patients is unknown [ ]. Nonetheless, the high selectivity of INaP-targeting drugs may offer a novel therapeutic strategy for hindering DMG invasiveness while minimizing toxicity linked to INaT inhibition.
The differential expression of VGSCs in the HGG microenvironment, such as the upregulation of NaV1.5 in DMG, provides opportunities to target both VGSC function (INaT vs. INaP) and individual α subunit isoforms. However, VGSC isoforms share similar voltage-dependent properties and structural homology, constraining the development of isoform-specific drugs [ ]. Further, broad-spectrum VGSC-targeting anticonvulsants, prescribed to brain tumor patients to treat disease-related epilepsy [ ], have demonstrated mixed clinical benefits in HGG. Our observation with Phenytoin, aligns with other cancer studies showing reduced invasiveness in vitro (DIPGXIII) [ ], but lacking efficacy in vivo [ – ]. Indeed, only one VGSC-targeting anticonvulsant, valproic acid, demonstrates dual antiepileptic and chemotherapeutic properties [ – ], though this is likely through VGSC-independent mechanisms [ – ], and its clinical benefit is inconsistent between patient cohorts, showing no benefit in a DMG clinical trial [ – ]. Therefore, isoform and functionally specific inhibitors would likely be therapeutically more advantageous for HGG than repurposing broad-spectrum VGSC-approved drugs.

## VGSC activity at the leading edge may increase invasiveness by amplifying excitatory neurotransmission
In DMG cells, GS967 significantly reduced invasion, suggesting that VGSC activity contributes to the invasive phenotype - an observation shared with other cancers, where increased INaP correlates with metastasis [ ]. Our use of patient-derived cell lines limited our ability to comprehensively evaluate invasion in additional cell lines, as these cell lines were inconsistent in forming invasive neurospheres. Still, future studies using newly developed brain organoid DMG models [ ] would likely overcome these inherent limitations, while recapitulating a more accurate tumor microenvironment [ ]. Moreover, in patient samples, abundant VGSC expression was detected in Nestin+ glioblastoma and DMG cells, which exhibit migratory and invasive phenotypes [ – , ]. Nestin as an accurate marker of glioma stem cells is debated, but a previous in vitro study showed that TTX-inhibition of endogenous VGSC currents in glioblastoma stem cells reduced their stemness marker levels and self-renewal capacity [ ], suggesting that VGSCs may play a role in sustaining the stem-like and invasive potential of these cells; a consideration also requiring further investigation.
Nonetheless, our spatial data showed that VGSCs concentrate in areas of high glutamatergic neuronal innervation, particularly at the invasive leading edge in HGG. In glioblastoma, VGSC transcripts were upregulated alongside AMPA receptor genes by NPC-like and OPC-like cells, including cellular signatures that mark highly invasive cells that are electrotonically unconnected to the central tumor [ ]. These cells mimic neurite migration patterns, processes regulated by VGSCs during neural progenitor development [ ], and regulated by transient glutamatergic synapses that increase invasion speed [ ]. HGG cell membrane depolarization drives HGG progression, and in normal physiology, INaP enhances neuronal intrinsic excitability, by boosting membrane depolarization, particularly in response to neurotransmitters such as glutamate [ , ]. Although our colocalization analysis suggests that similar mechanisms occur in HGG, these need to be confirmed electrophysiologically. Despite this limitation, our current data indicate that the inhibition of INaP may provide a novel approach in mitigating the highly invasive behavior of HGG cells.

## The role of VGSCs in development supports HGG pathology
Knockdown of SCN9A (NaV1.7) in glioblastoma cell lines disrupts gene expression pathways related to neural development, cell proliferation, and tumor invasion, suggesting that VGSC activity in HGG reactivates developmental processes to drive tumor growth [ , ]. Indeed, mice lacking TTX-sensitive α subunits, or β subunits, exhibit perinatal lethality or severe CNS/PNS defects [ – ]. Although our glioblastoma scRNA-seq analysis revealed increased SCN9A expression in progenitor-like cells, our proteomic and immunohistochemistry analyses indicated low NaV1.7 expression in glioblastoma patient tumors, despite its detection in cell lines. Furthermore, increased SCN9A expression correlated with poorer patient survival. We observed similar results in the DMG dataset, where increased SCN1A expression predicted worse clinical outcomes; however, protein expression at the cell surface was undetected in our proteomic analysis. Discrepancies between disease models and patient data may be due to methodology, including cell surface protein detection, tumor heterogeneity [ ], variations in tissue integrity (e.g., postmortem DMG), or a potential RNA regulatory function of VGSCs in HGG.
In DMG, VGSC expression was more uniform across the four microenvironments compared to glioblastoma, which based on our single-cell data, cannot be wholly attributed to the nonmalignant cell populations. It remains unclear whether these spatial differences reflect intrinsic cell lineage differences or extrinsic influences such as trophic factors. An interplay between these factors may exist, as during development, growth factor stimulation of neurite extension is significantly accelerated by spontaneous VGSC activity [ ]. In tumors, trophic factors such as BDNF (e.g., in DMG), NGF, and astrocyte-derived GDNF may increase VGSC expression by either directly binding to their promoters or through intermediate signaling pathways [ ]. Additionally, spatial gradients of growth factors within other microenvironments, such as EGF and VEGF in the perivascular niche, and the more diffusely distributed IGF, may differentially regulate VGSC subtypes. However, further study is needed to verify these possible regulatory mechanisms.

## Conclusions
Our findings reveal that VGSC activity does not intrinsically promote HGG proliferation, but increased INaP may promote invasiveness in DMG. Moreover, given their role in development and expression within the invasive microenvironment under strong glutamatergic synaptic drive, VGSC activity may facilitate maladaptive neurodevelopmental mechanisms that progress HGG. Therefore, our work demonstrates that potent INaP inhibitors like GS967 may represent effective adjunct therapies for HGG by suppressing multifactorial mechanisms responsible for tumor invasiveness.
