## Background
Meningioma is a common primary intracranial tumor with high recurrence and metastasis rate. Delineating the pathological ecosystem at the brain-tumor interface (BTI) of meningioma is critical for understanding the mechanisms of tumor metastasis and developing effective new therapies.

## Methods
To identify biomarkers of early metastasis and discover potential therapeutic targets, we integrated single-cell transcriptome datasets of meningioma, and identified the cell populations and molecular signatures uniquely present at the BTI.

## Results
A specific BTI-enriched tumor cell population with a pro-EMT (epithelial mesenchymal transition) characteristics was associated with invasion and metastasis, and ANXA2 and COL5A1 were detected as the biomarkers for these BTI-enriched tumor cells. Additionally, we characterized the BTI-specific immunosuppressive microenvironment composed of SPP1+ tumor-associated macrophages, as well as specific endothelial cells (ACKR1high) and pericytes (THY1high) promoting the highly malignant invasive state of angiogenesis.

## Conclusions
Collectively, BTI in meningioma is a metastatic and immunosuppressive zone. We have discovered potential biomarkers that help detect early metastasis and recurrence of meningioma.

## Introduction
Meningioma is a common primary intracranial tumor, accounting for approximately 35% of all cases [ ]. Currently, the incidence rate of meningioma shows an upward trend year by year and increases with age [ ]. Low-grade meningioma (benign) is classified as WHO (World Health Organization) grade l, accounting for approximately 80.1%. High-grade meningiomas (malignant) are classified as WHO grade II and III, accounting for 18.3% and 1.5%, respectively [ ]. At present, the main treatment strategy for meningioma is maximum surgical resection combined with radiotherapy. Most patients with benign meningiomas can be cured due to limited tumor growth. However, high-grade meningiomas growing in an infiltrative manner are highly invasive, and patients with brain invasion and extracranial metastasis have much worse prognosis [ , ].
When meningiomas proliferate in a highly infiltrative manner, the demarcation between the tumor tissue and the surrounding normal brain tissue tends to blur, making it more challenging to distinguish the two. Moreover, the presence of residual tumor cells at the surgical margin is a primary cause of recurrence [ , ]. Thus, accurately characterizing and differentiating the brain-tumor interface (BTI) in meningioma holds immense significance. Previous solid tumor-related studies have shown that the tumor core and tumor margin possessed unique microenvironments [ ], such as different tumor cell characteristics, stromal cell features and immune microenvironments. For example, in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma, it was reported that the tumor leading edge might mediate tumor invasion and metastasis [ , ]. In colorectal cancer, patients with high microsatellite instability often exhibit a disorganized tumor-stroma interface accompanied by high-level immune cell infiltration. This condition was also associated with the efficacy of immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) therapy [ ]. In gastric cancer, it has been found that the tumor-stroma interface (TSI) was of a unique microenvironment, which was rich in pro-angiogenic endothelial cells and pro-metastasis macrophages [ ]. In liver cancer, regions between the tumor and adjacent non-tumor tissues were characterized by a hypoxic microenvironment, an exacerbated inflammatory response, and pronounced local immunosuppression [ ]. In brain solid tumors, the BTI also has specific gene expression signatures and a complex microenvironment. For instance, in gliomas, a distinct subset of microglial cells, characterized by high-level expression of chemotactic and pro-inflammatory genes, has been identified at the TSI [ ]. In the BTI of medulloblastomas, an increase in endothelial VCAM1 (vascular cell adhesion molecule 1) expression and micro-vessel density was found, along with a significant upregulation of tumor proliferation and enrichment of cancer cells with high stemness [ ].
However, little work has been done on the BTI of meningioma. Existing study on BTI in meningioma only focused on the MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging) features, which showed that extensive peritumoral brain edema (PTBE) could predict brain invasion of meningioma [ ]. The scarcity of research on the BTI of meningioma is likely linked to the formidable challenges in precisely obtaining samples that accurately capture the tumor boundaries.
In this study, we integrated scRNA-seq (single-cell RNA sequencing) datasets of meningioma-related samples, encompassing the BTI, from multiple sources. By leveraging high-resolution single-cell transcriptomics, we comprehensively characterized the unique pathological ecosystem in the invasive region of the meningioma BTI. Our analysis offers valuable biological insights into elucidating the invasion mechanism, which is of great significance for identifying potential therapeutic targets for meningioma.

## Materials and methods

## Human specimen datasets
The analyzed data in this study consists of 3 datasets, which were downloaded from the NCBI GEO (Gene Expression Omnibus) database (GSE206647 and GSE183655) and NCBI SRA (Sequence Read Archive) database (PRJNA826269). Ultimately, we integrated scRNA-seq data from 33 samples of 23 patients. These 33 samples included 11 normal meninges (N), 2 brain-tumor interfaces (BTI) and 20 tumor cores (TC), of which 10 were low-grade and 10 were high-grade meningioma. The clinical information is summarized in Supplementary Table 1.

## Single-cell sequencing data processing
scRNA-seq FASTQ files were processed with human reference genome GRCh38-3.0.0 by the 10X Genomics Cell Ranger software (v6.0.0) using default parameter settings. R package Seurat (v4.3.0) was used for downstream processing. According to the specific characteristics of each scRNA-seq dataset, the data quality control criteria were adjusted accordingly, such that > 90% high-quality single-cells were retained for downstream analysis. Expression matrices of the 33 samples were merged into a single data object. We used harmony (v1.0.3; lambda = 1, theta = 2) to merge the datasets and remove batch effects. After performing principal component analysis and clustering (FindClusters), UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection) visualization was performed in Seurat with the first 20 principal components based on the top 2000 highly variable genes. For the combined dataset, the clustering resolution was set to 0.7.
Marker genes for each defined cluster were identified using the FindAllMarkers function in Seurat. For each cluster, the genes expressed in more than 25% of cells with at least a 0.25 log2(fold-change) were considered. In order to ensure the repeatability of the cell populations between samples in three datasets, we removed the cell populations with strong sample bias. The initial combined dataset contained 195,452 cells. Cell populations with more than 60% of cells came from only a single sample, or with fewer than 300 cells were removed. Finally, twenty cell clusters were annotated as 9 distinct major cell types according to the top 20 marker genes of each initial cluster. For the myeloid immune cells and stromal cells, the clustering resolution was set to 0.2 to delineate the subpopulations. Cell numbers of each cluster and cell type per sample are summarized in Supplementary Table 2. Differentially expressed genes of the clusters and cell types are summarized in Supplementary Table 3.

## Copy number variation (CNV) analysis
InferCNV (v1.8.1) was used to analyze the scRNA-seq data to identify evidence of large-scale chromosomal copy number changes in the somatic cells, such as the increase or loss of entire chromosome(s) or large segments of chromosome. InferCNV inferred chromosomal variation by exploring the intensity of gene expression at different locations in the tumorous cell genome compared to a set of normal cells as reference. We selected T cells, myeloid cells, endothelial cells, and pericytes as normal references.

## Odds ratio analysis
To characterize the tissue distribution per cell type, odds ratios (ORs) were calculated and used to evaluate bias. The more inclined value of OR > 1.5 is considered as biased for a tissue type, and OR < 0.5 is considered otherwise. Analysis details were as follows: for each combination of cell type i and tissue j, a 2 by 2 contingency table was constructed, which contained the number of cells of cell type i in tissue j, the number of cells of cell type i in the other tissues, the number of cells of the other cell types in tissue j, the number of cells of the other cell types in the other tissues. Fisher’s exact test was applied on this contingency table to obtain OR and the corresponding p-value. P-values were adjusted using the BH (Benjamini-Hochberg) method implemented in the R function p.adjust. We found that all ORs > 1.5 or ORs < 0.5 had adjusted p-values < 1e−10. Hence, a higher OR with a value > 1.5 indicated that cell type i was more preferred to distribute in tissue j, and a lower OR with a value < 0.5 indicated that cell type i was preferred not to distribute in tissue j.

## Pathway enrichment analysis
ClusterProfiler (v4.7.1) was used to perform Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway and Gene Ontology (GO) function enrichment analyses for a gene list. The gene list included the differentially expressed genes (DEGs). GSVA (v1.42.0, Gene Set Variation Analysis), an unsupervised gene set enrichment analysis tool, was used to assess the performance of gene sets for pathway enrichment at the sample level. After obtaining the GSVA score matrix, we used limma (v3.54.2) for differential pathway analysis. Meanwhile, we employed fgsea (v1.20.0) to perform GSEA (Gene Set Enrichment Analysis). This enabled us to compute the enrichment scores of pertinent pathways and determine their statistical significance.

## Gene set scoring
The CellCycleScoring function from the Seurat package was used to calculate the cell cycle scores for each cell. Based on the expression of classical marker genes for the G2/M and S phases, the computed scores for the S phase and G2/M phase were saved in the metadata, along with the predicted classification of cells in the G2/M, S, or G1 phase. Cell cycle scoring was performed on all tumor cells. The AddModuleScore function in the Seurat package calculates the scores of predefined gene sets in each cell, representing the activity or enrichment of these gene sets in the cells. Reference gene sets were downloaded from the CancerSEA database. Using the AddModuleScore function, cell cycle, proliferation, metastasis, invasion, EMT (epithelial-mesenchymal transition), angiogenesis and apoptosis scores were calculated for specific cell subsets. Macrophage M1 and M2 scores were also calculated to determine the functional characteristics of the macrophages.

## Cell fate trajectory analysis
To explore the developmental trajectories of relevant single-cells, Monocle (v2.20.0) was used to perform cell fate trajectory analyses. Normalized single-cell gene expression data for selected cells were loaded into Monocle2. Genes with differential expression (adjusted P value < 0.05) between clusters were used to order cells. We inferred the cell fate trajectory by performing dimension reduction and cell ordering with the default parameters. Next, we transformed the gene expression levels across the trajectory pseudotime using z-score normalization and applied smoothing to the data. Finally, we visualized the genes with correlated and anti-correlated expression profiles to the predicted pseudotime. CytoTRACE (v.0.3.3) was used to determine developmental potential of relevant tumor cells. The top 100 genes correlated or anti-correlated with CytoTRACE values were chosen as markers for immaturity and differentiation, respectively. The result values between 0 and 1 represent the prediction order of the relative differentiation state of cells (0: higher differentiation; 1: lower differentiation).

## Tumor signaling analysis
PROGENy (1.20.0) was used to analyze tumor signaling in the scRNA-seq data. PROGENy was able to infer the pathway activity of 14 signaling pathways (androgen, estrogen, EGFR, hypoxia, YAK-STAT, MAPK, NFκB, PI3K, p53, TGFβ, TNFα, Trail, VEGF, and WNT) from gene expression data. By default, pathway activity inference is based on the gene set of the first 100 most responsive genes after the perturbation of corresponding pathway, which we refer to as pathway footprint genes. Each footprint gene is assigned a weight that represents the intensity and direction in which the pathway perturbation is modulated. The pathway score was calculated by the weighted sum of the product of expression and footprint gene weights.

## Predictive analysis of transcription factor activity
We used DoRothEA (1.6.0) to predict transcription factor (TF) activity. DoRothEA contains the built-in interactions between TFs and their target genes. A set of TFs together with their corresponding target genes was defined as a regulon. The credibility of the interaction between TF and the target is divided into five levels, A-E, according to the amount of supporting evidence, with A being the most credible and E being the least confident. For rigorousness purpose, we only considered TF and target gene interactions with credibility from A to C. DoRothEA inferred TF activity from expression of its direct target genes.

## Survival analysis
The gene expression and clinical data were downloaded from UCSC Xena (https://xena.ucsc.edu/). Survminer (v0.4.9) was used to plot the overall survival (OS) curves.

## Cell–cell interaction analysis
We used iTALK (v0.1.0) for ligand-receptor interaction analysis across major cell types in BTI sites. iTALK can be used to visualize the complexity, diversity and dynamics of cell–cell communication in a wide range of biological processes. Communication types can be divided into growth factors, cytokines, checkpoints, and others.

## Immunofluorescence staining
Tissues were collected from the patients pathologically diagnosed with meningioma at Sun Yat-Sen University Cancer Hospital for OCT-embedding and immunofluorescence staining. The samples included tissues from tumorous and normal meninge regions. The OCT-embedded tissues were placed on a cryostat chuck and frozen to the optimal cutting temperature, which is typically around −20 °C. Using a cryostat microtome, sections of the tissues are cut at a thickness of 6 µm. Sections were fixed with acetone for 3 min and washed with PBS for three times. They were then treated with 0.5% Triton-X for 15 min to increase membrane permeability. Non-specific adhesion sites were blocked with 5% solution of bovine serum albumin (BSA) for 1 h at room temperature. The tissues were incubated with the first antibody, including N-cadherin (CST, 13116 T), COL5A1 (CST, 86903 T), KI67 (CST, 9129 T) and ANXA2 (Abcam, ab178677), respectively, at 4 °C overnight. Next, the sections were washed with PBS for three times and incubated with Goat Anti-Rabbit IgG H&L (Alexa Fluor® 488) (Abcam, #Ab150077) at room temperature for 1 h to label the first antibody green. The sections were then incubated with the second antibody, E-cadherin (CST, 14472 T) or Vimentin (CST, 3390 T) at 4 °C overnight, washed with PBS for three times, and incubated with Goat Anti-Mouse IgG H&L (Alexa Fluor® 647) (Abcam, #Ab150115) at room temperature for 1 h to label the second antibody red. After washing three times with PBS, the slides were covered with an anti-fluorescence quenching agent to seal the coverslip, and finally imaged using confocal microscopy (Nikon, NIS-Elements AR).

## Quantization and data analysis
Statistics are calculated using Wilcoxon’s rank sum test. P-values less than 0.05 were considered significant and were marked as follows: p < 0.05 (*), p < 0.01 (**) and p < 0.001 (***).

## Results

## Delineation of cells in meningioma tumor core and BTI
The large-scale integration of scRNA-seq data from multiple patients enabled high-resolution mapping of cellular landscape for meningioma. By integrating the scRNA-seq data of 18 tumor samples we recently published [ ], with another two public meningioma datasets, we formulated a combined single-cell transcriptome dataset comprising 33 meningioma-related samples, encompassing 11 normal meninge, 20 tumor core and 2 BTI tissues (Fig.  A and Supplementary Table 1). 
After data quality control and batch effect removal (Materials and methods), scRNA-seq data for 195,452 cells were kept for downstream analyses (Supplementary Fig. 1A–C). The included scRNA-seq datasets were divided into three groups according to the surgical site: normal meninge (N), tumor core (TC) and BTI (Supplementary Fig. 1B). Based on the highly variable genes among the analyzed cell and clustering, and by inferring the total CNVs per cell cluster, we were able to identify the tumor cell populations (Supplementary Fig. 1D–E). According to cell type definition in previous studies, we further divided the tumor cell population [ , ]. The cells were classified into 9 major cell types according to the canonical marker genes (Fig.  B–C). These major cell clusters were annotated as meningeal/meningioma cell (MGC; CRABP2, KRT10 and KRT18), cycling meningioma cell (cMGC; MKI67 and TOP2A), myeloid cell, T cell, B cell, endothelial cell, mural cell, fibroblast and doublet (Fig.  B, , Supplementary Tables 2, 3). Meningeal cells from normal meninge tissues did not form a distinct cluster but were dispersed among the MGC cluster (Fig.  D, Supplementary Fig. 1B).
Next, we found the cMGCs from BTI and TC were mapped to distinct areas in the UMAP space, rather than being mixed together like the MGCs (Fig.  D). This potentially implied that the cMGCs from BTI and TC were transcriptomically distinct. We used the OR [ ] analysis to compare the abundance of the 9 defined cell types across the sample groups, describing the organizational distribution of the major cell types (Materials and methods). The MGCs showed little difference in distribution between TC and BTI; however, the cMGC was predominantly enriched in BTI (Fig.  E). Myeloid, T and B cells exhibited a higher degree of abundance in normal and TC tissues, while fibroblasts, endothelial cells, and mural cells were primarily identified in the normal samples (Fig.  E). Subsequently, we also compared the abundance of all cell types among the three sample groups. Interestingly, we found that the proportion of cMGC significantly increased from the inner TC to outer BTI (Fig.  F).

## Characterization of major tumor cell populations
The integrated dataset included 4808, 53,979 and 11,855 MGCs and cMGCs from the normal, TC and BTI tissue samples, respectively (Fig.  A). The MGCs from the normal group were more transcriptomically distinct from those from the TC and BTI, therefore were referred as normal meningeal cells (Supplementary Fig. 2A). In addition, the cMGCs were rarely identified in the normal group. As the focus of our study is on the metastasis of meningioma, we hereafter only focused on the 65,834 tumorous MGCs and cMGCs from the BTI and TC samples (Fig.  A). 
The DEG analysis between MGCs and cMGCs showed that cMGCs highly expressed genes related to tumor malignant proliferation and poor prognosis, such as TOP2A, H2AFZ, UBE2C and UBE2S [ – ] (Fig.  B). Further, the cMGCs were predicted to have significantly higher differentiation potential and cellular stemness (Fig.  C).
The cell cycle is a critical process for proper cell division and is central to cancer biology. As one of the hallmarks of cancer, uncontrolled cell proliferation is a primary driver of tumor progression [ ]. We subsequently confirmed that the active cell cycle functional scores and the proportion of the cMGCs mapped to the G2/M and S phases of cell cycle were significantly higher than the MGCs (Fig.  D, Supplementary Fig. 2B). These results potentially implied that the regulation of cell cycle in the cMGCs is disrupted, and cells continue to divide uncontrollably, resulting in greater proliferation functional scores (Fig.  E).
Furthermore, we compared the functional characteristics between the tumorous cMGCs and MGCs from the TC and BTI samples. The GSVA and GSEA results revealed that the cMGCs exhibited enriched expression of genes related to multiple tumor malignancy development pathways, such as EMT, MYC targets, G2/M checkpoint and E2F targets (Fig.  F, Supplementary Fig. 2C). Likewise, the KEGG pathway enrichment analysis demonstrated that the MGCs presented a high level of enrichment in gene expression related to ‘proteoglycans in cancer’ and ‘protein processing in the endoplasmic reticulum’, which are typical pathways commonly implicated in malignant tumors [ , ]. Conversely, the cMGCs demonstrated an enrichment of gene expression pertaining to the ‘spliceosome’, ‘cell cycle’, ‘DNA replication’, ‘oxidative phosphorylation’, and ‘HIF-1 signaling pathway’, all of which are closely linked to the malignant proliferation of tumors (Supplementary Fig. 2D). Also, in comparison to the MGCs, the cMGCs manifested enhanced metastatic and invasive capabilities, with much higher expression in genes like ANXA2 [ – ], HIF1A [ – ], SOX4 [ , ] and COL5A1 [ , ] (Fig.  G, , Supplementary Fig. 2E). Metastasis and invasion served as a driving force behind the malignant transformation of solid tumors and accounted for the majority of cancer-related deaths. Overall, the delineated cMGC cluster was a group of tumor cells with high proliferation potential and in a low differentiation state, featuring more metastatic and invasive characteristics of tumorous cells.

## Differences of cMGCs at the TC and BTI sites
We sought to compare the differences of cMGCs at the TC and BTI sites. The defined cMGCs were highly enriched at the BTI site, in comparison to TC (Fig.  A). Vimentin is highly expressed in all subtypes of meningiomas and is a commonly used marker for meningioma cells [ , ]. Our immunostaining experiments on frozen tumor tissues from the BTI and TC showed that the BTI sites had stronger and more concentrated expression of KI67 for cell proliferation (Supplementary Fig. 3A). According to the previously published TC and BTI marker genes, we found that cMGCs from the BTI group (cMGC_BTI) in our study consistently had higher expression of LAMC2, TGFB1 and ITGA5 [ ] (Fig.  B). Further, we identified the DEGs between cMGC_BTI and cMGC_TC (the cMGCs from TC). cMGC_BTI showed higher expression in genes including TPM1, NNMT, COL5A1, ANXA2, and CXCL14 (Fig.  C). GSVA pathway enrichment analysis on the DEGs indicated that cMGC_BTI was of higher expression of genes mapped to pathways such as MYC targets, EMT, oxidative phosphorylation, and hypoxia. In contrast, the upregulated genes in cMGC_TC were highly enriched in pathways related to interferon and inflammatory responses, suggesting a strong immune-active microenvironment (Fig.  D). Further, GO enrichment analysis revealed that the cMGC_BTI had significant upregulation of pathways related to fibroblast migration, epithelial cell migration, endothelial cell migration, migration of cells involved in angiogenesis, as well as tissue migration (Fig.  E). Also, we found that the cMGC_BTI exhibited significantly higher metastasis, invasion, and EMT functional scores than the cMGC_TC. (Fig.  F). Collectively, cMGCs at the BTI site had stronger proliferative capability and were potentially inducing the migration of other cell types (e.g., fibroblast, epithelial cell and endothelial cell). 
The activation of EMT [ , ] is a critical process in cancer cell metastasis. We speculate that this phenomenon is likely attributed to the EMT phenotype transformation of cMGCs at the BTI site. This transformation may render the invasion and metastasis more active at the BTI compared to the TC. The characteristics of EMT include the loss of epithelial markers, such as cytokeratin and CDH1 (E-cadherin), and the upregulation of mesenchymal markers, such as CDH2 (N-cadherin), VIM (Vimentin), and fibronectin. Various transcription factors such as TWIST, SNAIL, SNAIL2, NF-κB, ZEB1, SIP1, and TCF3 can promote EMT. We found that, in comparison to cMGC_TC, the cMGC_BTI had much lower expression of CDH1, KRT19, and KRT18, while CDH2 was much higher expressed (Fig.  G). Moreover, the immunofluorescence staining results validated that the BTI tissues with enriched cMGCs were of higher CDH2 (N-cadherin) expression and lower CDH1 (E-cadherin) expression (Fig.  H, Supplementary Fig. 3B).
EMT is a staged process in which epithelial cells lose polarity and adhesion capabilities, transforming into an invasive and metastatic cell phenotype. This transition is regulated by various signaling pathways, including TGFβ, WNT, and Notch signaling pathways [ ]. The tumor-associated pathway activity score analysis suggested that the pro-EMT characteristics in cMGC_BTI may be modulated by the TGFβ and WNT pathways (F g.  I). Additionally, the cMGC_BTI showed higher RNA expression of the EMT-related transcription factors TWIST1, SNAIL2, and TCF3 than the cMGC_TC (Fig.  G). We also predicted transcription factor activity and observed higher expression of ASCL1, TEAD1, HHEX, and TFAP4 in the cMGC_BTI (Supplementary Fig. 3C). Previous studies have shown that ASCL1 can regulate the expression of WNT11, which plays a role in the cell proliferation and EMT of human small cell lung cancer [ ]. TEAD1 inhibition can significantly reduce migration and EMT both in vitro and in vivo [ – ]. HHEX has also been implicated in tumorigenesis [ – ]. Additionally, TFAP4 has been reported to promote the invasion and metastasis of hepatocellular carcinoma by inducing EMT and regulating MMP-9 expression through the activation of the PI3K/AKT signaling pathway [ ]. Overall, these findings support the pro-EMT characteristics for cMGCs at the BTI.

## Developmental trajectory of cMGCs from TC to BTI
Next, we aimed to delineate the differentiation trajectory of cMGCs at the TC and BTI sites. Compared to cMGC_TC, the cMGC_BTI was mapped to the later stage of differentiation, which were located towards the late end of pseudotime trajectory (Fig.  A, ). We identified the top 20 genes most closely related to the pseudotime trajectory. Among these, most genes showed increased expression along the trajectory, while CLU (Clusterin) was downregulated (Fig.  C). This was consistent with our previous study in which we discovered CLU as a tumor suppressor [ ]. 
By plotting gene expression along the pseudotime scale, we found that ANXA2 and COL5A1 were the most representative genes positively correlated to the trajectory (Fig.  D, Supplementary Fig. 3D). The expression levels of ANXA2 and COL5A1 genes at the cMGC_BTI were significantly higher than cMGC_TC (Supplementary Fig. 3E). ANXA2 and COL5A1 were also associated with metastasis and invasion characteristics (Fig.  H). Immunofluorescence staining also confirmed that tumor cells at BTI had higher expression of ANXA2 and COL5A1 than TC (Fig.  E, , Supplementary Fig. 3F). Collectively, these results suggest that cMGC_BTI potentially developed from cMGC_TC, representing a type of tumor cells invaded from the tumor core to the brain-tumor interface.
In addition, we found that ANXA2 and COL5A1 were highly expressed in a variety of tumors, and the high expression of these two genes was associated with worse prognosis (Fig.  A–C). We also found that the expression level of COL5A1 were significantly increased in the intracranial implantation metastasis (IIM) tumor cells than the meningioma in situ (MIS) (Fig.  D), suggesting COL5A1 as a potential molecular marker of IIM. In cMGCs, the expression levels of ANXA2 and COL5A1 were positively correlated (Fig.  E), which suggests that there was a synergistic effect between these two genes, and they are functionally related. These two genes may jointly participate in the invasion or metastasis of tumor cells. Overall, we propose that ANXA2 and COL5A1 could serve as molecular markers of malignant borderline in meningiomas to predict and measure early metastasis. 

## Characterization of myeloid and stromal cells
The tumor microenvironment (TME) plays a crucial role in the occurrence, development, and metastasis of tumors. Different TMEs have distinct cellular compositions and interactions. Besides the tumor cells, we also focused on the immune cells and stromal cells from the BTI samples.
The immune infiltration at the BTI site was dominated by myeloid cells, and the existence of lymphoid cells was very low (Fig.  F). We re-clustered the myeloid immune cells into 12 subgroups, including subtypes of monocytes, macrophages, cancer-associated macrophages-like cells (CAMLs) and mast cells (Fig.  A, Supplementary Fig. 4A, B, Supplementary Tables 2, 3). CAML is a myeloid-tumor characteristic cell population, featuring co-expression of myeloid (LYZ, CD68, CD14 and MRC1) and tumor genes (KRT18) [ ]. 
Comparing to the normal and TC samples, we found that the BTI samples were predominantly enriched with Macro_SPP1 cells which was of typical M2 tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) phenotype, such as phagocytosis (Fig.  B, ). M2 TAMs play an anti-inflammatory and tumor cell growth promoting role in the immune microenvironment [ ].
Meanwhile, we also re-clustered the stromal cells (endothelial, mural cell and fibroblasts) into 8 subgroups. The stromal cells subtypes included endothelial cells (EC), smooth muscle cells (SMC), pericytes (PC) and fibroblasts (Fib) (Fig.  E, Supplementary Fig. 4C, D, Supplementary Tables 2, 3). We found that the stromal cells at the BTI site were mainly composed of EC_ACKR1 and PC_THY1 cells (Fig.  F). EC_ACKR1 cells at the BTI sites showed significantly higher levels of angiogenesis functional scores but a lower tendency towards apoptosis (Fig.  G). ACKR1high EC cells have been reported to activate the VEGFR pathway, facilitate angiogenesis and contribute to the inflammatory microenvironment in tumors [ ].
We also found that PC_THY1 at the BTI sites was highly enriched for gene expressions associated with pathways such as angiogenesis, tumor vascular stability, growth factors, extracellular matrix (ECM) organization, cell matrix adhesion, and response to TGF-β (Fig.  H). In tumor tissues, cells with high-level of THY1 expression were considered to be mesenchymal-stem-cell-like (MSC-like) cells [ ]. Further, in a study of spatial transcriptomics of recurrent glioma samples, a rare THY1+ region located in the perivascular niche was discovered, which strongly expressed EMT and stem cell characteristic genes, and co-localized with macrophages [ ]. These collectively support that PC_THY1 might play a critical role in the malignant progression of meningioma.
Collectively, our analyses suggest BTI sites of meningioma were potentially of immunosuppressive microenvironment, characterized by a low degree of immune cell infiltration and a predominance of myeloid immune cells, specifically Macro_SPP1. EC_ACKR1 and PC_THY1 might play roles in regulating tumor angiogenesis, participating in tumor immune regulation, and promoting tumor cell metastasis. These specific macrophages and stromal cells were enriched at the BTI, thus implying potential therapeutic targets at the tumor boundary. Further, BTI was characterized by a microenvironment of enhanced oxidative phosphorylation, enhanced glycolysis, hypoxia, and immunosuppression (F g.  I).

## Working model of the invasion zone at the BTI site
The tumor boundary is the frontier where tumor cells, immune cells and stromal cells interact within the TME. The cellular composition, cell–cell interactions, and molecular network regulation at the tumor boundary have a profound impact on the TME remodeling. To assess the microenvironment of meningioma BTI as a whole, we analyzed the interactions among the major cell subpopulations (Fig.  J, Supplementary Fig. 4E). We found that signaling between cMGCs and other cell types was mainly mediated through ligand-receptor pairs such as VIM-CD44, TIMP1-CD63, FN1-ITGB1, CTGF-ITGA5, and COL5A1-ITGB1, thereby promoting tumor cell proliferation, angiogenesis and inducing EMT [ ]. PC_THY1, EC_ACKR1 and cMGC promote angiogenesis through ligand-receptor pairs such as CTGF-ITGA5/LRP1, VEGFA-ITGB1 and PGF-FLT1. SPP1-CD44 activated relevant signaling pathways to promote the migration, invasion, and adhesion of tumor cells. Collectively, our data suggest that brain-tumor interface is an invasive zone promoting meningioma tumor progression (Fig.  ). In the working model, tumor cells at the BTI are highly invasive and metastatic (ANXA2highCOL5A1high cMGC). They potentially recruit SPP1+ macrophages with M2 phenotype and interact with MSC-like pericytes and endothelial cells, potentially promoting EMT, immune evasion, and angiogenesis, and ultimately facilitating the malignant progression of the tumor. 

## Discussion
The cellular characteristics and molecular events occurring at the BTI are fundamental for unraveling the mechanisms underlying the invasion and metastasis of meningioma. Tumor boundary is the zone where tumor cells invade the para-tumor tissues and come into direct contact with other cells [ ]. It is the most active zone for tumor cell infiltration and invasion [ , ]. The blurry boundary between meningioma tumor and normal tissues makes it difficult to completely resect the tumor through surgery, which is also one of the main causes of recurrence and metastasis after surgery [ ]. In radiomics, the presence of peritumoral edema shown by meningiomas on MRI indicates their malignant invasion [ , ]. Currently, a great deal of research attempts to distinguish the tumor boundary by using different methods, such as developing algorithms [ ] for spatial analysis or using aptamer-assisted fluorescence-guided surgery (AptaFGS) [ ]. In this study, we utilized scRNA-seq datasets to comprehensively analyze the different functional cell types and molecular signatures of distinct human meningioma-related tissues, including normal meninge, tumor core, and brain tumor interface samples. We identified the BTI-related molecular markers at the transcriptome level, offering new perspectives for understanding the malignant transformation of meningiomas and for potential targeted therapies.
We further characterized the invasion zone in meningiomas at the BTI [ , , , ]. The key feature was the enrichment of specific tumor cell subtype (cMGCs) at the BTI with EMT phenotype, potentially promoting meningioma cell invasion and metastasis. During the process of EMT, the polarity of cells is lost, the cytoskeleton is rearranged, and cell adhesion is altered, enabling cells to acquire invasive and migratory abilities [ ]. ANXA2 and COL5A1 were identified as the molecular markers of these specific tumor cells at the malignant borderline of meningioma.
ANXA2 and COL5A1 were rarely studied in tumor invasion. In pan-cancer, ANXA2 and COL5A1 have been regarded as biomarkers closely related to the malignant phenotype of tumors and poor prognosis [ , ]. ANXA2 is highly expressed in a variety of solid tumors and is a widely reported oncogene [ , ]. ANXA2 initiates EMT via the Twist/Snail pathway in nasopharyngeal carcinoma (NPC) to promote cancer progression and therapeutic resistance [ ]. ANXA2/TTK complex activates the Akt/mTOR signaling pathway and accelerates EMT, thereby promoting esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (ESCC) invasion and metastasis. The ANXA2/TTK/Akt/mTOR axis is a potential therapeutic target for ESCC [ ]. ANXA2 is also a biomarker closely associated with malignant phenotype and poor prognosis of glioma, and plays an important role in immune response, inflammatory activity and immunosuppression [ ]. ANXA2-EGFR pro-inflammatory signaling potentiates EMT-induced fibrotic stress in idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis [ ].
Collagen is one of the most abundant proteins in animals. Besides serving as a structural component of tissues, collagen can also regulate cell behavior which plays a role in cell adhesion. New research findings have revealed the roles of collagen in tumor dormancy and immune evasion, and its interactions with cancer cell metabolism [ ]. Collagen could also serve as a prognostic marker for cancer patients, and therapeutic strategies targeting the collagen extracellular matrix (ECM) have the potential to prevent tumor progression and metastasis. Overexpression of COL5A1 promotes tumor progression and metastasis [ , , ]. COL5A1 is associated with the invasiveness of meningiomas. The expression level of COL5A1 increased significantly with meningioma grade, and the down-regulation of COL5A1 negatively regulate the migration and proliferation of meningioma cells [ – ].
In the present study, we have discovered that ANXA2 and COL5A1 could potentially serve as biomarkers for the malignant invasion zone of the meningioma tumor. The invasive ability of cMGCs from TC to BTI gradually increases, with increasingly high expression of ANXA2 and COL5A1. Further, these cells were found enriched at the BTI site, potentially facilitating cancer metastasis and invasion.
Although ANXA2 and COL5A1 are also expressed in normal tissues and perform important physiological functions [ – ], their abnormally high expression, functional remodeling capacity and spatial expression bias in tumor are key drivers of tumor progression, making them potential biomarkers. The expression differences between normal and pathological states are the core prerequisites for converting them into biomarkers or therapeutic targets. The physiological functions of ANXA2 and COL5A1 in normal tissues may lead to off-target effects of targeted therapy, suggesting the need to develop tumor-specific strategies [ , ].
In addition, we also found that the invasion zone of meningioma has a unique local tumor ecosystem, with complex interactions among cellular components. This indicates that the invasion zone is a complex system with multi-directional interactions. The immune cells at BTI were mainly SPP1+ TAMs. A study analyzed TAMs from large amounts of RNA-seq data showed that patients with more TAMs of high SPP1 expression had a poorer prognosis [ ]. Another study identified SPP1+ TAM have more M2 features and found SPP1+ TAM has the function of promoting tumor metastasis. Further, SPP1+ TAM is not present in the primary tumor, but is significantly enriched at the metastatic sites and in lymph nodes, and is often identified in patients with metastasis [ ]. Thus, we speculate that the SPP1+ macrophage (Macro_SPP1) enriched in the BTI samples could promote the metastasis of meningioma tumor cells.
We also hypothesized that the cell–cell interactions and the mechanisms promoting invasion/metastasis in the invasive BTI zone. VIM-CD44, TIMP1-CD63, FN1-ITGB1, SPP1-CD44, CTGF-ITGA5, VEGFA-ITGB1 and COL5A1-ITGB1 between cMGCs, Macro_SPP1, EC_ACKR1 and PC_THY1 may be the potential ligand-receptor pairs mediating the mechanism of meningioma malignant transformation. The ligand-receptor pairs in-between the cMGCs themselves, as well as between cMGCs and Macro_SPP1 could promote tumor cell proliferation, induce EMT, and accelerate tumor progression. The CD44-VIM ligand can activate signaling pathways such as PI3K/Akt and MAPK which act on downstream transcription factors such as Snail, Slug and Twist, thereby promoting the expression of EMT-related genes and inducing the occurrence of EMT [ ]. TIMP1-CD63 can mediate the EMT-like transition of normal cells together by enhancing the signal transduction activity of β1 integrin [ ]. Activation of the FN1-ITGB1 ligand pair can upregulate the expression of EMT-related transcription factors, thereby inducing EMT and enhancing the invasion and metastasis capabilities of tumor cells [ , ]. SPP1 secreted by macrophages can bind to the CD44 receptor on the surface of tumor cells, in turn participate in the EMT process and enhance the stemness of tumor cells. As a result, tumor cells become more invasive and drug-resistant, facilitating tumor progression and metastasis [ , ].
The ligand-receptor pairs between cMGCs and stromal cells could also promote the EMT and tumor angiogenesis. CTGF-ITGA5 can induce the proliferative signaling pathway and promote the survival and proliferation of tumor cells [ ]. CTGF and VEGF work synergistically during the process of tumor angiogenesis. VEGF mainly promotes the proliferation and migration of endothelial cells, while CTGF plays a role in the formation and stabilization of vascular lumens [ – ]. The interaction of VEGFA-TGB1 can mediate angiogenesis, EMT and inflammatory regulation [ ]. COL5A1-ITGB1 is implicated in the process of tumor angiogenesis, facilitating the formation of tumor blood vessels by modulating the interaction between vascular endothelial cells and the extracellular matrix [ ].
Although there were important discoveries revealed by our study, it also has limitations. The sample size for BTI in this study is limited, and micro-fluidics derived single-cell transcriptome data is insufficient in analyzing spatial-specific features. We consider combining the scRNA-seq with spatial transcriptomics and genomic sequencing in future studies to improve this. Investigating the spatial information of gene expression and chromosomal structural alterations will contribute to a better understanding of meningioma and potential new therapeutic targets.

## Conclusion
We systematically examined the transcriptional profile of cells at the BTI site in meningioma. Our analysis results provide preliminary insights for filling the knowledge gaps in the cellular and molecular characteristics of malignance events at the BTI of meningioma. The findings regarding this tumor invasion region offer potential functional insights for prognostic prediction and propose possible therapeutic targets for meningiomas.
