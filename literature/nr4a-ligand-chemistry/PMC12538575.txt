In G. duodenalis, its intriguing set of minimal “core” protein kinases and the highly expanded Giardia-specific Never-in-Mitosis-A related kinases (Neks) emerge as a novel druggable space. We propose this kinome as an understudied and underutilised space to explore novel antigiardial targets. Lastly, we have conducted a high-throughput screening of 430 compounds, covering 53 kinase targets and 51 chemical scaffolds, identifying 83/430 antigiardial kinase inhibitors of which 33 true positives could be validated in a subset subjected to drug-susceptibility testing, highlighting intriguing spaces for further development and molecular probes to further explore kinase regulatory pathways in this parasite.

## Introduction
Protein kinases (PKs) are essential enzymes which regulate protein phosphorylation, a post-translation modification (PTM) central to nearly all eukaryotic cell processes, including metabolism, division and signal transduction ( ; ; ). Dysregulation of PKs and their pathways are often associated with human diseases ( ; ; ; ). To date, there are 231 small molecule PK inhibitors that have been approved by the FDA, with a corresponding druggable PK target ( ); PK-targeting small molecule screens have been performed for over 20 years ( ; ; ). The core structure of a PK domain has been conserved across all domains of life, defined by their five β-strands and an α-helix in the N-termini lobe, and six α-helices in the C-termini lobe ( ; ; ; ; ; ). Linking PK structure and function is central to developing selective inhibitors ( ; ; ). Further, bona fide PKs are characterised by a “catalytic triad” of protein residues, consisting of an ATP-binding lysine, substrate-interacting aspartate, and another magnesium-binding aspartate ( ); all three residues are required (by the current nomenclature) to perform phosphorylation ( ; ). Additional PK/substrate sequence motifs and co-occurring protein domains divide hundreds of these orthologous core enzymes into eight major groups: the AGC kinases, which contain PKs A, G, and C, the casein kinases 1 (CK1); the sterile 20 kinases (STE); the tyrosine kinases (TK) and the tyrosine kinase-like (TKL), the guanylate cyclases (GYC), the calcium/calmodulin-dependent kinases (CAMK) and the group of cyclin-dependent kinases, mitogen-activated kinases, glycogen synthase kinases and casein kinases (CMGC), a “core” set of PKs ( ; ; ; ).
Many parasitic PKs are validated druggable targets ( ; ; ; ; ; ; ; ). The push to explore PKs in parasites largely coincided with the launch of many protist genome sequencing projects and the availability of sequence databases ( ; ), which supported efforts to identify novel antiparasitic chemotherapeutics. In this vein, the first genome of Giardia duodenalis assemblage A WB genome was published in 2007 ( ; ) with a 93 % chromosomally mapped genome a few years later ( ). In 2011, Manning and colleagues ( ) performed the first comprehensive analysis of the G. duodenalis kinome, discovering a minimal core kinome relative to higher eukaryotes, highlighting an intriguing druggable space in a parasite where drug-discovery efforts have traditionally been underserved.
Giardia duodenalis (syn Giardia intestinalis, Giardia lamblia) is a microaerophilic gastrointestinal parasite causing giardiasis and burdens which disproportionately affect children from lower socioeconomic tiers, which in turn causes developmental and nutritional disorders ( ; ; ; ; ). Further, giardiasis has been associated with lifelong post-infectious disorders, such as irritable bowel syndrome and chronic fatigue ( ). In the two-stage life cycle of G. duodenalis, chemotherapeutic treatments mainly target the virulent trophozoite stage ( ), and remain the gold standard in clearing existing infections, given limited development on vaccine candidates ( ). The infective cyst stage also poses public health challenges, as these cysts are resilient to traditional water disinfection strategies ( ; ; ).
The call for new antigiardial chemotherapeutics is neither new nor unexpected. For the past seven decades, the primary drug has been the broad-spectrum anti-microaerophilic/-anaerobic nitroheterocyclic antibiotic metronidazole ( ; ). Whilst nitroheterocyclics selectively target the microaerophilic metabolism of G. duodenalis, they also target the anaerobic gut microbiome, causing dysbiosis of the microflora and bacterial infection sequelae ( ). Moreover, due in part to continued and non-compliant usage, treatment failure attributed to drug resistance is common ( ; ; ). Common adverse effects of metronidazole include nausea and vomiting, and whilst rare, severe toxic effects can include transient leukopenia, neutropenia and neurotoxicities ( ; ). Therefore, with an unmet need to develop new chemotherapeutics with novel mechanisms, the understudied kinome of G. duodenalis represents an underutilised opportunity to exploit a variety of parasite-essential signalling and regulatory pathways to break infection and transmission cycles.
In humans, the core kinome is estimated to include ∼500 PKs, covering eight major groups ( ). In comparison, the first annotation of G. duodenalis PKs ( ) estimated 80 core PKs in G. duodenalis, one of the smallest known core kinome in any eukaryote at the time of its publication ( ). Intriguingly, they highlighted a large, expanded group more than twice the size of the core kinome (198/278) ( ). This was proposed as an expanded, highly divergent, single PK group, based on sequence homology of a few to the Never-in-Mitosis-A (NIMA)-related kinases (Neks) orthologs, but primarily based on in-paralogs within sub-species ( ). Further, this kinome hosts one of the largest proportions of pseudokinases relative to the size of its complete kinome ( ; ). These PKs lack the complete catalytic triad and are considered catalytically inactive ( ; ; ). Consequently, based on a limited number of publications ( ; ; ; ; ; ; , ), nearly all of these Giardia-specific Neks have no known functions, no known substrates, no sequence motifs – more than meeting definitions used in model species of ‘understudied’ PKs ( ). Notably, most of these Neks share little sequence homology with Neks in higher eukaryotes, as well as limited homology within the parasite ( , ), such that they could represent parasite-unique targets that could exploit divergent chemical scaffolds or substituents for Giardia-specific chemotherapeutics.
We credit the rationale that both core and expanded branches of the kinome in G. duodenalis represent interesting drug-discovery spaces: the minimal core kinome containing essential PKs with little biological redundancy and the highly divergent Nek kinome containing Giardia-specific PKs ( ). However, these spaces have remained mostly unexplored over the last decade, and herein, we have taken several steps to review, update current annotations and incentivise PK research in this parasite. Firstly, we propose a reannotation of PKs in the most recent genome of G. duodenalis ( ), noting that the initial annotation of this unique kinome in 2011 was built upon a fragmented genome ( ; ). To facilitate this, we look to new bioinformatic tools at both the protein sequence and structural level ( ; ), specifically noting the state-of-the-art AlphaFold2 protein structure prediction algorithm ( ; ). Secondly, PKs are demonstrated druggable enzymes primarily as targets in tumours ( ), partly due to the well-conserved PK domain structure throughout evolution ( ). Thus, we probe the core kinome in G. duodenalis with designed small molecule PK inhibitors from these screens to discover novel antigiardial compounds, thus highlighting novel druggable opportunities within existing molecular spaces.
Herein, we stringently re-annotated a portion of the new G. duodenalis assemblage A kinome, highlighting new sequence- and structure-based annotations that should be adjusted in silico based on the newer, high-quality genome assembly of G. duodenalis. Further, we performed a high-throughput screen of a curated library of small molecule PK inhibitors and identified molecular probes to probe G. duodenalis PK regulation. Crucially, we shed light on the “Nek kinome”, providing a revised perspective on these likely Giardia-specific PKs, including a suggestion for their annotation, providing an incentive to functionally validate the biological roles of these PKs and pseudokinases.

## Materials and methods
2.1

## Query and classification of kinase-like proteins in the G. duodenalis genome
InterProScan v5.61 ( ) was used to extract PK domain boundaries (under the superfamily accession for the PK domain IPR011009) from the predicted protein sequences from the G. duodenalis genome (WBC6 2019 ( )). Identified PK domain boundaries were queried against a curated list of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) using HMMsearch ( ); the complete list of HMMs was retrieved from the Conserved Domain Database (CDD ( )), from (for a confident set of defined and clustered pseudokinases) and (for a set of clustered prokaryotic PKs) (Supplementary Data S1). HMMbuild generated pseudokinase and prokaryotic PK HMMs based on their respective sequence alignments. Each identified PK domain was assigned numerical suffixes based on the location of the PK domain in the full-length protein; 293 PK domains were retrieved ( , ). The HMM file used is provided in Supplementary Data S1. 
All 293 PK domain boundaries were queried on AlphaFold2 and AlphaFold3 for predicted protein structures ( ; ). Domains were aligned to representative mammalian PK crystal structures cyclin dependent kinase 16, B-Raf kinase and phosphatidylinositol-4-phosphate 5 kinase (PDB: 3MTL and 5G6V, 5CSW and 9BFB, and 5E3S) using TM-align to generate TM-scores normalised to representative structures (max TM-scores to each template was taken as the representative value) ( ). All folded PK domains were manually verified using ChimeraX-1.8 ( ); structures with unique/lack of secondary structures were noted, and those which did not resemble the canonical PK domain were excluded from further analyses ( ) ( A).
Identified PK domains were filtered for more stringent, “confident” HMM assignments based on either of the following two criteria: 1) the difference between the best HMM domain match score and the second best HMM domain match score must be greater than 50 (ΔHMMscore >50), or, 2) the best HMM domain match and second best HMM domain match belong to the same “group” of PKs (e.g. best match CDKL5 is from the CMGC group, and second best match CDKL2 is also from the CMGC group). Confident atypical PKs (aPKs) were assigned if HMM assignments were not part of the “PKc” group from CDD (CDD: cd00180). A total of 64 “confident” core HMM assignments were made (discussed below), 9 were aPKs, and 55 were part of the core kinome ( C).
2.1.1

## In silico query of kinase-domain containing proteins in G. duodenalis
For all identified PK and pseudokinase domains, common PK domain motifs and key catalytic residues ( ) were identified with XSTREME (version 5.5.5) with default settings ( ) ( ). Those PK domains with no XSTREME-identified catalytic triads were manually queried using a structural-based sequence alignment through AlphaFold-generated structures ( ; ). Briefly, structures were individually aligned to the crystal structure PDB: 5CSW, and Seed Extension (SE) was used to extract pairwise alignments ( ). Pairwise alignments were then aligned using MAFFT (--merge option) to create a structural-based, transitive multiple sequence alignment of PK domains ( ). Those PK domains with no XSTREME-identified catalytic triads were then manually identified based on this alignment ( ). InterProScan v5.61 was used to identify co-occurring protein domains ( ) ( D). All PK domain-containing proteins were queried on the DeepTMHMM web server to predict the presence of transmembrane domains ( ).

2.1.2

## Structural phylogeny of kinase-domain containing proteins
A structural phylogenetic tree was generated for the complete set of initial 284 PK domains (excluding aPKs), and another tree was generated for the “confident” 55 core PK domains using FoldTree ( ). Predicted PK domain structures (provided in Supplementary Data S2) were grouped based on previous HMM assignments ( ) and visualised using ggtree in RStudio ( ).

2.1.3

## Classification and evidence of ncPK expression throughout G. duodenalis lifecycle
The non-core protein kinases (ncPKs) were sub-classified by the presence of the catalytic triad and co-occurring domains. Evidence of ncPK expression in the trophozoite stage of G. duodenalis (WB-1B isolate ( )) was identified from a previous mass spectrometry dataset ( ). Peptides with pep scores <0.01 were mapped to the new G. duodenalis proteome (UniProt UP000001548) and used as a proxy for ncPK expression evidence in the trophozoite stage during WB growth. Previously identified significant (previously annotated) Neks during the G. duodenalis lifecycle ( ) were cross-referenced to this in silico ncPK dataset.

2.2

## Cultivation of parasitic protists in vitro
Giardia duodenalis trophozoites (WB-1B) were maintained in 10 mL of TYI-S-33 supplemented with 10 % heat-inactivated bovine serum in flat-sided tubes as previously described, with subculturing performed twice a week ( ).

2.3

## Compounds and compound libraries
The SelleckChem PK inhibitor (n = 423) and the epigenetic inhibitor (n = 7) libraries were obtained through Compounds Australia, Griffith University. 10 mM stock solutions of compounds were made in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO, Cell Signalling Technology) and stored indefinitely at – 20 °C. Compounds Australia provided the SelleckChem compounds and their PK targets. Torkinib (PP242) was sourced from Sapphire Biosciences (#13643-1 MG), and stocks made in 10 mM DMSO were stored indefinitely at – 20 °C.

2.4

## Cell viability assays with CellTiter-Glo
2.4.1

## High-throughput screening of SelleckChem inhibitor libraries against G. duodenalis trophozoites
Single replicate, single dose (10 μM) Cell viability assays were performed in a 384-well plate (Greiner Bio-One Microplate: 781098) format against G. duodenalis trophozoites in incubation volumes of 25 μLs ( ; ). Briefly, 500 trophozoites were seeded into 384-well plates and incubated anaerobically for 48 h (BD GasPak EZ: 260683) before the addition of 10 μL of CellTiter-Glo reagent to each well; firefly luminescence was measured on microplate readers with the emission spectra set to 580-80 nm.

2.4.2

## Drug-susceptibility testing of PK inhibitors against G. duodenalis
Drug-susceptibility testing of a subset of compounds (n = 57) was performed once in technical duplicates or quadruplicates in a 10-point serial dilution series from 30 μM in 384-well plate formats as above. For torkinib, drug-susceptibility testing was performed in technical triplicates in biological quadruplicates (n = 4) at an 8-point serial dilution series from 5 μM in 96-well plate formats (Corning NBS Microplate: CLS3995) ( ; Briefly, 2000 trophozoites were seeded into 96-well plates in 50 μL incubation volumes and incubated anaerobically as above for 48 h before the addition of 30 μL of CellTiter-Glo reagent to each well and measured on microplate readers as above.

2.4.3

## Normalisation and data processing of CellTiter-Glo assays
Luminescent readouts were normalised to in-plate controls for a percentage viability. Within each 384-well microplate, the median ATP-luminescence values of at least eight wells containing 20 μM metronidazole were used as a positive control of complete inhibition of trophozoite growth (‘kill value’), and the median ATP-luminescence values of at least 16 wells containing 0.5 % DMSO were used as a negative control of uninhibited trophozoite growth (‘live value’). The kill value was subtracted from each luminescent value in each plate before these values were divided by the dynamic range (live value – kill value) to obtain a normalised luminescent value for each well, which were then used as a proxy for percentage viability of trophozoites ( ).
The median normalised trophozoite viability value for the overall screen was calculated to be 90.16 % based on 462 wells containing 0.5 % DMSO. This was used to establish a dynamic cut-off for trophozoite viability using the median absolute deviation method (2x MAD); compounds which reduced trophozoite viability below “Median – 2x MAD” ( ) were considered “hits”. Single-dose screening data were visualised with ggplot2 in RStudio ( ).
For drug-susceptibility assays, normalised parasite viability was visualised on GraphPad Prism 10 and dose-response profiles were fitted with the log[compound] vs normalised response, variable slope model with robust regression fitting, and curve constraints at – 25 % as a minima and 125 % as a maxima as previously described ( ).

2.5

## Clustering of chemical analogues across PK inhibitor libraries
Available SMILES were provided by SelleckChem (through Compounds Australia). Open Babel (v3.1.0) was used to convert the retrieved SMILES to 2D mol2 structures, and a pairwise comparison between 430 unique, small molecules was performed using the FP2 path-based fingerprint format to generate Tanimoto coefficients ( ; ). The output identity matrix of Tanimoto coefficients was clustered using the cluster_matrix function with the ward.D2 method with the RStudio package “textshape”. Using a Tanimoto coefficient cut-off at 0.7, the hierarchical clustered matrix guided the manual assignment of compound clusters.
2.5.1

## Compound set enrichment analysis of identified chemical clusters for antigiardial activity
Enrichment analyses of identified chemical structure clusters (n = 51, from 430 unique compounds) were performed with multiple two-sided Fisher’s Exact Tests and p-values adjusted with the Benjamini-Hochberg method; adjusted p-values <0.05 were used as cut-offs. Within each cluster, targets were assigned based on a concatenation of PKs from each cluster member.

## Results
3.1

## Re-annotation of the G. duodenalis PK domains with HMMs
Since the initial annotations of the highly divergent G. duodenalis kinome by Manning and colleagues in 2011 ( ), a chromosomal-scale genome assembly was made available for the reference G. duodenalis assemblage A in 2020 ( ), providing better gene models and hence more accurate predicted protein sequences. For example, the number of genes almost halved in this new contiguous genome compared to the previous assembly ( ; ), prompting investigations into possible erroneous kinase-coding genes in the previous genome but also the possibility of new kinase-coding genes in this new genome; we show in this work 31 deprecated kinase-coding genes in the old genome, and six new kinase-coding genes in this new assembly ( ). Further, recent advances in accurate protein structure prediction algorithms ( ; ; ; ) allowed for high-throughput structural comparisons with resolved crystal structures of PKs. Together, these provided the incentive to re-explore and re-annotate this protist’s intriguing kinome, particularly to attempt to resolve the low-confidence PK assignments in this parasite. Within this new G. duodenalis proteome (UniProt UP000001548), we took a stringent HMM- and structure-based approach to confidently identifying PK domains.
Initially, we used a sequence-based HMM approach which catalogued a combined 293 PK domains (284 PK domains and 9 aPK domains) within the most recent proteome of G. duodenalis assemblage A, strain WB ( , ). Here, 31 previous PK sequences in the 2011 study were not found in this new proteome ( ); these were classified previously as ‘Nek-GL4’ or ‘Nek-Unclassified’ ( ). Further, whilst pilot sequence-based phylogenetic and HMM analyses for these PK domains confirmed the expanded Nek-kinome as demonstrated previously ( ; ), we also observed a novel category of numerous PK domains annotated as “PknB” (protein kinase B ( ; ; )) or “Ciliate-A5” ( ). These annotations clustered ambiguously in the initial phylogeny with Neks ( A, ) rather than in discrete novel subgroups. These results agree with Manning and colleagues’ previous hypothesis that Neks are derived from early-branching eukaryotes or prokaryotes, as the PknB group are bacterial eukaryotic-type PKs ( ; ; ). However, these may also be novel eukaryotic-emerged PKs based on annotations to the Ciliate-A5 group, where the HMM models were constructed from protist-specific PKs ( ). Together, these could indicate that these G. duodenalis PKs occurred at the midpoint of evolution between bacterial and eukaryotic PKs but were nonetheless highly divergent from either of these groups. Hereinafter, we broadly group these ambiguously annotated PKs (with low confidence assignments, see Methods 2.1) which matched to these HMMs as “non-core protein kinases” (ncPKs), and the remaining were designated as the “core” kinome as previously published ( ). 
Given that these equivocal annotations were based only on sequence homology, we stringently filtered initial HMM results (Methods 2.1) and used a structure-guided approach as an orthogonal method to annotate the core kinome ( ; ). Our combined approach was used to conservatively filter for a set of “confident” PK domains, which are more likely to resemble the canonical PK domain based on predictive structures and with accurate HMM assignments. Here, we excluded 29 PK domains as their predicted protein structures either lacked confidence or did not closely resemble the conserved PK domain ( B). Based on ΔHMMscores >50 or a broad group match of best and second-best HMM assignments (See Methods 2.1), we identified 9 confident aPK domains and 55 core PK domains ( C).
3.1.1

## Validation of 55 core PKs in the G. duodenalis WBC6 predicted proteome
Using our stringent HMM-assigned, structure- and phylogeny-supported core kinome, we assigned 55 PK domains into four major groups: 24 belonging to the CMGC, 7 to the STE, 7 to the AGC and 5 to the AMPK-like group, with the remaining 12 classified as others ( B– ). Within this list of 55 core PKs, 49 concurred with the previous annotations proposed in 2011 ( ); those with UniProt identifiers: A8B7V0, E2RTY0, A8BQY4, A8BLF9, A8B8B7 and A0A644FBG0 did not concur with previous annotations ( ). However, we note that the previous annotation for five of these PK domains was also noted to have weak homology based on their classification searches and were likely tentative assignments at the time ( ); only A8B7V0 was previously unannotated and herein annotated as part of the CMGC group. Further, when we cross-referenced the remaining 23 non-confident core PKs (a total of 78 core PKs annotated herein) with previous annotation notes, we found that a majority of these again were tentative assignments in 2011, where sequence homology was noted as “too divergent” to known relative PK domains in higher eukaryotes (based on high E-values in either BLASTP and HMM search methods). Some were also noted to contain large inserts, which we confirmed through predicted PK structures and noted similar instances of inserts with the PK domain in other parasitic protists, such as Plasmodium falciparum ( ).
Ten of the 55 core PKs identified contained unusual predicted secondary structures (such as additional α-helices, β-strands and disordered inserts), which we believe confounded the generated structural phylogeny ( B– ). Nonetheless, we also noted that seven of these 55 core PKs contained one or more accessory domains ( ), which are common in PKs, affecting their functionality and cellular localisation. Two annotated “AMPK-alpha” PKs (A8BIZ4 and A8BU43) contained a C-termini KA1 domain, which is a typical accessory domain of orthologous PKs in higher eukaryotes ( ; ; ; ). Two contained C-termini armadillo (ARM)-like repeat folds (A8BGL6 and A8BQY4), which are proposed to mediate protein-protein interactions as with other “repeat-like” folds ( ). One labelled as a Rho-associated coiled-coil containing kinase (ROCK) contained a C-termini pleckstrin homology (PH) domain (A8B1S2) ( ). PH domains are known for low-affinity binding to phosphoinositides ( ). One contained an N-termini CRIB domain (A8B738) and was classified here and previously as a p21-activated kinase (PAK) ( ). Finally, we noted another PK (A8BKE0) with an unusual insert that bisected the PK domain; this was annotated as a papain-like cysteine peptidase domain with an E-value at ∼10−6 ( ). However, the pLDDT scores (as a measure of prediction confidence on a protein structure) within this insert were low (data not shown). Hence, we believe this is a PK domain split by an insert rather than another protein domain.
Our stringent annotation has further condensed the core kinome in G. duodenalis assemblage A to an even smaller complement, from 78 to 55 PKs. The 23 PK discrepancy between ours and the previous annotations is not an explicit disagreement; whilst we still classify these as core PKs, we could not confidently subclassify these into the 7 core PK families, as these have secondary HMM assignments which were not confidently mapped (Methods 2.1).

3.1.2

## An abundance of ncPKs in the G. duodenalis WBC6 predicted proteome
When the initial structural-phylogeny of 284 PK domains was constructed, we observed several instances of ambiguous clustering of domains assigned Nek, PknB and Ciliate-A5 ( A). We have termed these “ncPKs” (non-core protein kinases). While Nek annotations were expected, the presence of two additional novel HMM assignments, which notably also did not have strict phylogenetic clusters between each other, brought into question the reliability of homology-based models and the downstream complication of extrapolating both relationships and function through assigning PK annotations (i.e. assignments to a specific PK group). Based on their unique and divergent sequences, we believe previous attempts at classifying ncPKs through HMM assignments and BLASTP searches either did not include enough species and/or diverse PK groups outside the model opisthokonts. We hypothesised that these ncPKs comprised a larger supergroup of Giardia-specific PK domains (currently without an HMM profile), which led to the ambiguous clustering of these ncPKs in G. duodenalis. Such an expansion of a family of kinases was also noted in other parasitic protists, namely the apicomplexan-specific FIKK kinases ( ; ). In total, we identified 177 ncPK domains in the new G. duodenalis proteome (reinforced by AlphaFold predicted structure validation, i.e. folds like a predicted PK domain), four of which are novel as they were missing in the previous genome assembly ( ). We noted that most of these 177 ncPKs contained disordered amino acid inserts (mainly at the PK activation loop but also in the N- and C-lobes, D).
Subsequently, we devised a sequence/structure/functionality-driven classification of all ncPKs in the G. duodenalis WBC6 (assemblage A) proteome ( D). The previous nomenclature of this group was based on a combination of sequence similarity within the PK domains and three-way inter-assemblage orthology between GS, WB and P15 strains of G. duodenalis ( ). We believe this was problematic given the complexity of the Giardia species assemblage ( ; ; ), and the inevitability of taxonomic revisions and renewed genome assemblies. We therefore suggest an alternative nomenclature which highlights functional information. This proposed system is intended to classify ncPKs with confident PK features related to functionality in G. duodenalis. More specifically, our nomenclature explicitly highlights the presence of key residues and domains, which we hypothesise are evidence of functional roles rather than relying solely on sequence similarities between PK domains ( D).
Firstly, the nomenclature captured the presence of the canonical ‘KDD’ triad, which confers catalytic activity. Protein kinases which lack these three residues, by the current nomenclature, are termed pseudokinases ( ; ). As such, the first two categories of ncPKs were termed ncPKDDs (ncPKs containing the catalytic triad), whereas PKs without the ‘KDD’ triad were herein termed ncPsKs (non-core pseudokinases). We defined 58 ncPKDDs and 119 ncPsKs. A second categorical annotation was added based on the existence of co-occurring domains. Within this category, 111/177 ncPK domains also contained co-occurring C-termini ANKs and/or RING/U-box domains ( ), which are broadly considered facilitators/mediators of protein-protein interactions (PPIs) ( ; ; ), often mediating substrate-catalysis. Consequently, ncPKs with co-occurring PPI domains were given the prefix “Int-“ (“Interacting”). Based on these four categories, we now highlight 26 ncPKDDs and 40 ncPsKs, which lacked additional domains, compared to 32 Int-ncPKDDs and 79 Int-ncPsKs ( D– ).
Considering the high proportion of ncPKs detected and structurally predicted in silico, we further explored proteomic evidence of these ncPKs at the protein level. Based on a previous mass spectrometry dataset, we detected the expression of 131/177 of these ncPKs in the trophozoite stage of G. duodenalis WB assemblage A; notably, 31 of these ncPKs ( ) were differentially expressed in the parasite’s lifecycle ( ). 

3.2

## High-throughput phenotypic screening of SelleckChem PK inhibitors
While the extent of the ncPKs in G. duodenalis is abundant, our further reduced annotation of its core kinome highlighted the lack of redundancy, which may prove to be druggable (i.e. minimal core kinome susceptible to small molecule-mediated perturbations). To probe this minimal core kinome of G. duodenalis, we used the SelleckChem curated library of small molecule inhibitors (n = 430 small molecule inhibitors mainly targeting the mammalian core PKs) to perform a high-throughput screen against G. duodenalis trophozoite viability (Promega: CellTiter-Glo). Within this small library, we sub-classified 126 compounds into 51 “chemotypes” clusters based on the presence of at least two chemical analogues. We noted some limitations in this method, as most of the resultant clusters were small, these were unlikely to achieve enrichment significance (discussed below); only three clusters contained a maximum of five chemical analogues per cluster, whilst most clusters had only two analogues per cluster ( ). In this single-dose (10 μM) single-replicate screen, we selected a ‘hit’ cut-off at 47.3 % trophozoite viability relative to uninhibited growth (trophozoites grown in 0.5 % DMSO). This cut-off was selected based on twice the median absolute deviation (MAD) below the median trophozoite viability (median viability: 90.16 %, from n = 462 wells containing 0.5 % DMSO) ( Aa). Using this cut-off, 83 compounds were deemed antigiardial hits at 10 μM, consequently indicating a hit rate of 19.3 % ( ). Within these 83 compounds, 22 compounds were sub-classified into 14 chemotype clusters ( ), and six clusters harboured more than one active antigiardial analogue ( Aa). Among these 83 hits, we noted some inhibitors which canonically target mammalian tyrosine kinases (TKs) which theoretically should not be present in G. duodenalis ( ). However, there were almost no overlap or shared chemotypes between these TK-targeting antigiardial hits ( ). 
With an unexpectedly high hit-rate of ∼20 % relative to previous antigiardial high- and medium-throughput screening studies ( ; ), we performed multiple Fisher’s Exact Tests on identified chemotype clusters to determine whether inhibitors with a particular chemical scaffold (pharmacophores) were enriched for antigiardial activity. In total, 51 tests were performed, and when adjusted for false-discovery at 5 % with the Benjamini-Hochberg method, no clusters were significantly enriched for antigiardial activity ( ). This was likely due to the lack of sample size within each cluster, i.e. the high prevalence of small cluster sizes ( ). Nonetheless, clusters 2, 16, 17, 25, 27 and 28 contained a higher proportion of active to inactive analogues (>66 %), whereby targeted screens with more chemical analogues could be more conclusive about the potential of the scaffold for targeting G. duodenalis.
Five of these six clusters with a high proportion of antigiardial analogues were reported to target the aPKs ( Aa), particularly mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR) kinase. These PKs share structural similarities to a canonical PK domain but have notable divergences in their underlying sequence ( ). Our review of this protist’s kinome revealed nine confident aPKs ( ). The highest ratio of active:inactive chemical analogues in this single-dose screen (four antigiardials and one inactive) were those compounds from cluster 16 which were proposed to target the ATP-binding site of the mTOR PK ( ; ; ). Consequently, we selected the representative compound torkinib for dose-dependent drug-susceptibility testing to determine its potency. This confirmed the activity of torkinib at > 5 μM doses ( Ab). However, these in vitro IC50 values were not as potent as other antigiardials in other studies, with only an average IC50 value of 2.23 μM relative to other sub-micromolar antigiardial candidates ( ; ; ), possibly alluding to some key structural differences despite moderate sequence similarity of the PK domain to other mammalian TOR kinases ( ).
Lastly, we validated our single-dose screen via drug-susceptibility testing of a subset of 56 PK inhibitors (47 antigiardial compounds and 9 inactive compounds in the single-dose screen). We confirmed the antigiardial activity of 33 compounds based on quantified IC50 values < 10 μM and confirmed the inactivity of the 9 inactive compounds based on IC50 values > 10 μM; 14 compounds which were active in the initial screen at a 10 μM dose were validated as false positives with IC50 values > 10 μM ( B). In this dose-response validation, four compounds from clusters 2, 27 and 28 were active, particularly AZD8055 (cluster 2) and apitolisib (cluster 27), which had demonstrated sub-micromolar IC50 values ( B). Again, both these compounds target the mTOR PK, with apitolisib also demonstrating activity against the lipid kinase phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K) ( ). Other notable compounds with sub-micromolar IC50 values did not cluster within any chemotypes in our analysis, these were the glycogen synthase kinase 3β inhibitor AR-A014418, the glycogen synthase kinase 3 and cyclin dependent kinase inhibitor R547, the Janus kinase inhibitor AZ960, and the PI3K/mTOR inhibitor omipalisib.

## Discussion
In the 15 years since Giardia’s first assembled genome ( ) and the 10 years since its kinome’s original annotation ( ), its PK complement has become no less intriguing. Specifically, neither improved sequence models nor the addition of structural tools have resolved conclusive homologies to existing PK groups/families for 177 G. duodenalis ncPKs annotated herein. Furthermore, of the previous complement of core PKs (PKs which belong to the eight canonical eukaryote PK groups), we emphasise that only 55 of these have confident assignments, where a further 23 do not have sufficient homology (based on our models) to attribute them to one of these eight core groups. Based on this, we have opted for a conservative nomenclature, particularly for the ncPKs in G. duodenalis, which likely contain bona fide Neks but also more divergent PKs which clustered in our analysis with the Nek, PknB and Ciliate-A5 PKs. This highlights the presence or absence of functional features (catalytic triad) and additional domains. In the case of the former, this more explicitly highlights the larger proportion of what should be considered pseudokinases in the G. duodenalis kinome, which may not play a role in enzymatic phosphorylation but in the broader signalling cascade which are likely localisation-dependent ( ), such as scaffolding of protein complexes or interactors to other proteins ( ). While we believe this adds clarity to the ncPKs in this parasite, we note that these 177 ncPKs likely do not belong to a single PK group (that is, all Neks are now ncPKs). Instead, functional characterisation of these ncPKs should be prioritised to further sub-classify within this supergroup of ncPKs, deriving function-driven and cellular localisation niches rather than homology-based PK annotations in this parasite.
Our work did not significantly change the number or broader family classifications of previous annotations, except for a change in nomenclature for potentially Giardia-specific ncPKs ( ). Primarily, based on our annotations, none of the initial 293 sequence-identified PK domains were classified as tyrosine kinase (TK) or tyrosine kinase-like (TKL) proteins, concurring with previous observations that some parasitic protists completely lack this group of kinases, and TKs/TKLs may have evolved later throughout eukaryotic life ( ; ; ; ). However, phosphorylated tyrosine (pY) residues exist in G. duodenalis, suggesting that TKs evolved from earlier in eukaryotic life from other PKs to gain TK activity ( ; ). However, which of these G. duodenalis PKs (or other G. duodenalis enzymes) harbour TK functions to perform phosphorylation on tyrosine residues is still unknown. Similar to the TKs, exemplars from the TKL group are not found in early eukaryotes such as G. duodenalis ( , ; ; ; ; ; ); our models concur with these observations as we found no additional evidence of these TKLs within G. duodenalis in our annotation.
Our data here, along with others ( ; ), demonstrated success in perturbing PI3K/mTOR pathways as an approach for yielding potent antiparasitic properties and may hold potential for drug development as an antiparasitic target ( A). Elsewhere, genetic approaches to perturb the PI3K/mTOR pathway have demonstrated their essentiality in parasite growth, with the same phenocopy observed after perturbation with small molecule inhibitors ( ; ). In G. duodenalis, whilst small molecule perturbation of the orthologous PI3K/GTOR (in G. duodenalis, mTOR is termed ‘GTOR’ for Giardia target of rapamycin) has been studied, genetic methods to demonstrate this phenocopy has not yet been shown ( ; ). If available genetic methods ( ; ; ; ) can demonstrate the essentiality of this PI3K/GTOR pathway, it should incentivise PI3K/GTOR-driven drug-design of inhibitors as novel antigiardials. Furthermore, we observed higher-than-expected inhibitory doses of compounds such as torkinib against G. duodenalis compared to its potent activity in mammalian counterparts ( ; ). This, along with GTOR’s sequence divergence from human and yeast mTOR ( ), suggests GTOR may have minute differences within the PK domain. These differences may be exploited with small molecule inhibitors, providing an incentive for refining GTOR-specific scaffolds, which could provide efficacy and selectivity against the parasite.
Tyrosine kinases primarily relay extracellular signals into the cell, where more than half of the current defined TKs are classified as receptor-TKs ( ). Paradoxically, TKs are absent (by current definition) in early-branching eukaryotes and even in some plants, even though these cells are constantly exposed to dynamic and external stimuli to which they must adapt for survival ( ; ; ; ; ; ). For example, in G. duodenalis, the parasite must constantly adapt to changing physiological conditions throughout the host’s gastrointestinal tract ( ). Despite the absence of TK annotations in the G. duodenalis kinome, we discovered TK inhibitors which were active in the initial high-throughput screen against G. duodenalis (e.g. EGFR PK inhibitors), but by current convention, such inhibitors should not be active. Interestingly, this phenomenon has been previously observed in another kinase unique to apicomplexan parasites (FIKK kinase), where some TK inhibitors were shown to bind with high affinity to inhibit FIKK kinase function ( ). Further, tyrosine phosphorylation events have been demonstrated in G. duodenalis ( ; ) and are present in other early eukaryotes as well ( ; ; ). These observations, along with our findings of seemingly specific TK inhibitors with antigiardial activity, further credit the presence of these signalling pathways in early eukaryotes, which may still be evolving into the canonical TK protein structure by current nomenclature. Hence, TK inhibitors may target the earlier features of a typical TK to exert its antigiardial properties. Therefore, studies identifying the ancestral or early diverging TKs sequences in G. duodenalis could streamline SAR studies, which may guide the development of early eukaryotic TK inhibitors with specific antigiardial activity.
Genetically validated drug targets are more attractive from a drug-discovery perspective, as it is more likely to guarantee a phenotype from small molecule-directed perturbation. Our work here provides an interesting prospect in developing novel inhibitors against potentially Giardia-specific ncPKs. Given the size and sequence diversity of these ncPKs ( ), sequence homology and structural identity to other empirically-validated PKs are insufficient in informing the true (biochemical and genetic) function(s) of these ncPKs; only few ncPKs were probed for their function, where some were correlated to regulation of microtubule dynamics ( , ). However, implementing functional genetic tools from model eukaryotes to G. duodenalis to probe the biological functions of ncPKs is not straightforward, as these parasites are less genetically tractable and significantly divergent from the closest model eukaryotes (e.g. yeast). Hence, from a global health (macro) perspective, we propose prioritising the characterisation and druggability of ncPKs, which are implicated in stage-transition from G. duodenalis trophozoites to its infective cyst form ( ), under the assumption that inhibiting such ncPK function would block host-to-host transmission.
For example, based on this we could propose the prioritisation of one ncPKDD (A8BUD9) and three Int-ncPsKs (A8BFA4, A8BP90 and A8BJ13), which are implicated in stage-transition from G. duodenalis trophozoites to their infective cyst form, given their significant up-regulation in encysting cells at the protein level ( ( )). If biological consequences of these proteins can be proven through reverse genetic methods (i.e. perturbation of these proteins leads to an inability for trophozoites to encyst), numerous target-based drug discovery/design methods could be employed in lieu of viability screens against these genetically validated targets. In all four cases, crystallographic methods to resolve protein structures are also key in informing rational drug design ( ). Against a catalytically capable ncPKDD, discovering/designing orthosteric inhibitors (i.e. antagonists) at the ATP-binding site is common, as they directly inhibit enzymatic function ( ). However, orthosteric inhibitors towards pseudokinases (the three Int-ncPsKs above) do not guarantee a biological effect; drugging pseudokinases commonly involve perturbing the interaction of the pseudokinase domain with interacting protein or nucleic acid partners ( ; ). Therefore, targeted inhibitors against pseudokinases often require an in-depth analysis of their interactome (scaffolding partners) before designing allosteric inhibitors to perturb these scaffolds ( ), leading to a phenotypic effect. Whilst more rigorous, drugging these Int-ncPsKs often leads to higher selectivity towards the pathway/organism of interest, particularly for inhibitors which bind at the interface between proteins ( ; ; ).
Overall, our work here shows the potential of PK inhibitors as attractive antigiardials but also highlights the gaps for in silico annotation methods, which should only be empirically validated by functional characterisation, especially in non-model organisms such as G. duodenalis. When functional and genetically validated PK targets can be shown, a shift to target-based or rational drug development of antigiardial PK inhibitors may offer better selectivity and potency for new chemotherapeutic options.
