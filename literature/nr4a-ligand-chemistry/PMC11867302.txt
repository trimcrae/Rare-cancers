## Summary

## Background
COVID-19 continues to show long-term impacts on our health. Limited effective immune-mediated antiviral drugs have been launched.

## Methods
We conducted a Mendelian randomization (MR) and colocalization analysis using 26,597 single-cell expression quantitative trait loci (sc-eQTL) to proxy effects of expressions of 16,597 genes in 14 peripheral blood immune cells and tested them against four COVID-19 outcomes from COVID-19 Genetic Housing Initiative GWAS meta-analysis Round 7. We also carried out additional validations including colocalization, linkage disequilibrium check and host–pathogen interactome predictions. We integrated MR findings with clinical trial evidence from several drug gene related databases to identify drugs with repurposing potential. Finally, we developed a tier system and identified immune-cell-based prioritized drug targets for COVID-19.

## Findings
We identified 132 putative causal genes in 14 immune cells (343 MR associations) for COVID-19, with 58 genes that were not reported previously. 145 (73%) gene-COVID-19 pairs showed effects on COVID-19 in only one immune cell type, which implied widespread immune-cell specific effects. For pathway analyses, we found the putative causal genes were enriched in natural killer (NK) recruiting cells but de-enriched in NK cells. Using a deep learning model, we found 107 (81%) of the putative causal genes (41 novel genes) were predicted to interact with SARS-COV-2 proteins. Integrating the above evidence with drug trial information, we developed a tier system and prioritized 37 drug targets for COVID-19.

## Interpretation
Our study showcased the central role of immune-mediated regulatory mechanisms for COVID-19 and prioritized drug targets that might inform interventions for viral infectious diseases.

## Research in context

## Evidence before this study
We searched for key terms in PubMed for publications prior to August 1, 2024, using the following terms: (“COVID-19" or “SARS-CoV-2”) AND (“single-cell eQTL” or “protein” or “transcript”) AND (“Genome-wide association study” or “single-cell Mendelian randomization” or “causal genes”). Our search identified multiple studies that have pinpointed genes or proteins associated with COVID-19. However, there is a notable lack of studies that integrate single-cell eQTLs with Mendelian randomization to systematically investigate the potential causal relationships between immune-related genes and COVID-19 in European populations.

## Added value of this study
We systematically evaluated the immune-mediated genes and mechanisms for COVID-19 by combining single-cell eQTL MR and colocalization with pathway and protein–protein interaction information. We identified 343 MR associations for 132 genes in 14 immune-cell types related to four types of COVID-19 outcomes, with 58 genes that, to the best of our knowledge, were not reported previously. NCR3, a gene with robust MR evidence on severe COVID-19, interacted with SARS-COV-2 proteins, and its targeted drug is currently under clinical trials for cancer treatment. We further developed a tier system to prioritize COVID-19 drug targets and identified 37 promising drug target genes that showed multiple layers of evidence.

## Implications of all the available evidence
Our study identifies immune-mediated causal genes and mechanisms for COVID-19 and provides a tier system to prioritize immune-related drug targets for COVID-19.

## Introduction
The coronavirus disease (COVID-19) pandemic has resulted in nearly 7 million deaths during the past four years (https://covid19.who.int/). , Although vaccination against COVID-19 reduces the risk of severe COVID-19 disease and death, the continual emergence of SARS-CoV-2 variants with increased transmission and immune evasion has led to breakthrough infections in vaccinated individuals. , 
Until now, over 700 agents with anti-SARS-CoV-2 mechanisms have been investigated in preclinical and/or clinical studies, with more than 20 anti-SARS-CoV-2 drugs proposed to reduce the risk of severe COVID-19. However, the emergence of drug safety issues, drug resistance, , and limited treatment effectiveness , have made it imperative to search for new anti-coronavirus drug candidates that are highly specific to one cell type and widely applicable to many people.
Given the strong connections between the immune system and COVID-19, , , , previous studies have extensively investigated the use of immunomodulators, such as systemic corticosteroids, anticoagulants, and cytokine antagonists, to mitigate the dysfunctional responses in patients with COVID-19. , , , , , Immune cells exhibit diverse states during different stages of infection. In the early stage, the virus enters the body through endothelial cells expressing targets like OAS1 and ACE2. This alters the expression of genes like IFNAR2, affecting cell interactions and downstream gene expression. Consequently, a cascade effect occurs, leading to reduced functionality of natural killer (NK) cells and monocytes/macrophages, as well as coagulation disorders. However, the exact cause of immune cell responses, e.g. NK cell dysfunction, on COVID-19 remains unclear. It is urgently needed to uncover the immune-mediated mechanism and additional drug targets for COVID-19.
Recent studies have shown the value of human genetic evidence in increasing the success rate of drug trials. Existing genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified a whole set of genomic loci that are associated with COVID-19. , , , However, the causality of these genes is less clear. Some post-GWAS approaches, such as Mendelian randomization (MR), can estimate the putative causal effects of expression levels of genes and proteins on COVID-19, which have prioritized drug targets, such as OAS1, IL6R, in a cost-effective manner. , However, most of the existing studies have used expression data measured in bulk tissues, which may mask the cell-type specific effect of genes and proteins on COVID-19. Advancements in single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) technology have facilitated the examination of gene expression patterns at the individual cell level. Recent genetic studies have mapped genetic variants with single-cell expression data and identified a list of cell-type specific expression quantitative trait loci (eQTL, which is a genetic variant robustly associated with expression levels of a gene in a certain cell type). Single cell eQTL mapping has proven to be a valuable tool in identifying causal genes and understanding their involvement in disease pathogenesis, including immune diseases , and COVID-19 in the Japanese population. , However, few studies have integrated single-cell eQTLs with MR to systematically investigate the putative causal relationships between immune-related genes and COVID-19 in other ancestries.
In this study, we conducted a single-cell eQTL MR and colocalization analysis to investigate the putative causal effects of gene expressions from 14 immune cell types on risk of COVID-19 severities. Functional enrichment analysis and predictions of host-pathogen protein interactions were further applied to identify immune-mediated causal genes and networks involved in the pathophysiology of COVID-19. Integrating genetic evidence with clinical trial information, we developed a tier system to prioritize immune-mediated drug targets for COVID-19.

## Methods

## Ethics
This study did not require ethical approval as all analysis was based on publicly available aggregated statistics without access to individual data. The included GWAS studies obtained informed consent from the study participants and were approved by the relevant local ethics committees.

## Instrument selection and validation
In this study, the genetic variants associated with plasma eQTLs were used as genetic instruments for the Mendelian randomization (MR) analysis. We started the instrument selection process by accessing the cis-eQTL data from OneK1K data. We selected all conditionally independent cis-eQTLs that were associated with eGenes at a p-value <0.005. To ensure accuracy, we removed highly correlated SNPs by performing linkage disequilibrium (LD) clumping with a clumping r2 cutoff of 0.01. The LD reference panel selected was ‘EUR’. We estimated the strength of the genetic predictors of each instrument using F-statistics. If any eQTLs had F-statistics lower than the widely used threshold of 10, we considered them to have limited power (potentially causing weak instrument bias ) and removed these from the MR and follow-up analyses. Weak instrument selection and validation are provided in .

## Outcome selection
In the two-sample MR study, GWAS summary statistics of outcome were obtained from the COVID-19 Host Genetic Initiative (HGI) (Round 7, https://www.covid19hg.org/results/r7/) and Long Covid GWAS summary statistics. Data were from European ancestry populations. The sample composition of the four COVID-19 (also shown in ) phenotypes and four types of Long Covid were listed in . Ethical approval was not required for this study since the original GWAS previously received appropriate ethics and institutional review board approval. The number of SNPs for each group of COVID-19 and Long Covid are provided in .
 

## Single-cell eQTL MR analysis
During the discovery MR analysis, we aimed to estimate the potential causal effects of eGenes at the single cell level on COVID-19 in individuals of European ancestry. To ensure that the genetic signals in the cis-acting region were accurately represented and to increase power, we conducted one of two sets of analyses depending on the number of instruments selected from the exposure. For eGenes with only one eQTL, we utilized the Wald ratio analysis to estimate the effects between eGenes and outcome group. For eGenes with two or more eQTLs, we used the conditional independent eQTLs as genetic instruments and applied a generalized inverse variance weighted (gIVW) approach that considered the correlation between nearby eQTLs. The MR estimates were obtained using the TwoSampleMR R package (github.com/MRCIEU/TwoSampleMR) and were subjected to two thresholds to select MR estimates with good genetic evidence. A Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) of 0.05 was applied to select the best MR estimates with robust signals. MR analysis was performed according to the STROBE-MR checklist. , 
For the definition of novelty of our MR findings, we conducted a comprehensive search of COVID-19 related genes in 10 databases as well as twenty-five COVID-19 associated MR research ( , , , ). , , , , , , , , 

## Directionality test using MR steiger filtering
A drug that alters disease risk should in theory influence both gene expression and protein levels. To determine the direction of effect of our MR findings, we conducted a directionality test using Steiger filtering. This allowed us to test the directionality of the eQTL-disease associations for all candidate instruments. Any eQTLs flagged by the Steiger filtering as explaining more of the variance in the outcome than the exposure was removed from subsequent MR and follow-up analyses ( , , ).

## Genetic colocalization and LD check analyses
To improve the reliability of MR signals, we conducted three sensitivity analyses for candidate MR signals. Firstly, we used Cochran's Q test for gIVW results to estimate the potential heterogeneity of MR estimates across each eQTL. The results showed that all the gIVW results had limited evidence of heterogeneity (Q-value>0.05). Therefore, we could use these eGenes for the follow-up analyses. The heterogeneity results are listed in the .
To distinguish putative causative MR signals from gene–disease pairs confounded by LD, the results that passed the MR corrected p-value (Bonferroni corrected threshold, False discovery rate, FDR) filtering (<0.05) were evaluated using genetic colocalization analysis. For more robust colocalization evidence, we applied two sets of colocalization analyses. First, we developed an approximate colocalization analysis called LD check, which estimated the LD r2 between each eQTL against all variants with GWAS (p < 1 × 10−4) in the region associated with the disease outcomes. Evidence for approximate colocalization was based on an R2 of 0.7 and above between the eQTL and any of the outcome variants.
We then performed genetic colocalization analysis using the ‘coloc’ R package, following conventional methods. To ensure the inclusion of all eQTLs near the phenotype, we expanded the eQTL screening range of each phenotype in the exposure to a range of 100 KB upstream and downstream. A colocalization probability (PP.H4/(PP.H3 + PP.H4) ≥70%) in this analysis indicated a likely colocalization of the two genetic association signals within the test region ( ). If any one method passed the test, it could be identified as having a co-localization relationship or a causal effect. Causal genes were confirmed as unreported genes via searches conducted in 10 databases. , , , , , , , , 
Additional methodological details as well as the results of colocalization analysis for multiple causal variants, tissue-specific Mendelian randomization, MR sensitivity analyses, differential gene analysis (DEG), entropy analysis and mediation analysis are provided in .

## Gene ontology and pathway enrichment analysis
Metascape (version 3.5) is a comprehensive bioinformatics tool that integrates over forty databases. It offers several features such as enrichment analysis of biological pathways, structural analysis of protein interaction networks, and gene annotation capabilities. Cytoscape (version 3.1), is an open-source software platform that allows for the visualization of complex networks and integration with any type of attribute data. In this study, we utilized Cytoscape and Metascape online tools (http://metascape.org/) to analyze the functional enrichment of all identified causal genes (min overlap = 3, p -value cutoff = 0.05, and min enrichment = 1.5). We also used Metascape's combined (all) option for Protein–protein interaction (PPI) analysis (min network size = 3) and visualized the Pathway enrichment results together with PPI results using the ggplot2 R package (R package, ggplot2, v3.4)) and Cytoscape software respectively. The MCODE algorithm was applied to the network to identify neighborhoods where proteins were densely connected. GO enrichment analysis was applied to each MCODE network to extract “biological meanings” from the network component, where the three terms with the smallest p-values were retained.
Enrichment of MR results was conducted via function phyper () with all MR results, all significant MR results, significant MR results in each cell type and MR results in each cell type as values.

## Host–pathogen interactome predictions
We used a computational method called ‘DeepHPI’ to predict the host–pathogen network with our putative unreported causal genes and SARS-COV-2 proteins. We used convolutional neural network (CNN) models for the host–pathogen interactions (HPI) prediction process. We also used String (https://string-db.org/) to explore potential relationship with our unreported causal genes and previously identified SARS-COV-2 causal genes. These results were then integrated into a single diagram ( , ).

## Drug targets prioritization
Most drugs target small-molecule proteins. To identify potential drug targets, we used 132 causal genes from four outcome groups. We integrated our MR findings with clinical trial evidence from OpenTargets, DrugBank, and Drug Gene Interaction database (https://www.dgidb.org/) to identify drugs with repurposing opportunities. The selection of prioritized genes was based on drug targets with current clinical indications for inflammation, respiratory disorders, viral infection and top symptoms from COVID-19 as well as long-COVID.

## Results

## Summary of instrument selection for immune-cell specific gene expressions
In this study, we aimed to select valid instruments using a three-step selection process. We extracted 26,597 independent cis-acting eQTLs associated with expression levels of 16,597 genes in 14 different immune cells (single-cell gene expression data measured in 982 participants from the OneK1K cohort ), and used them as candidate genetic instruments for this study ( ). Given the complexity of single-cell sequencing data, the expression level of a gene in a specific immune-cell type was noted as a gene expression profile of that gene in this study ( ).
For the instrument selection, we removed highly correlated genetic variants by performing linkage disequilibrium (LD) clumping with a LD r2 cutoff of 0.01. The Europeans from the 1000 Genome project were used as the LD reference panel. After clumping, a total 17,959 independent cis–eQTLs for expression levels of 16,597 genes in 14 cell types were selected ( a, ). Second, we calculated F-statistics for each eQTL and excluded 20 eQTLs with F-statistics<10 from further analysis, since they may suffer from potential weak instrument bias. Third, we used Steiger filtering to verify the directionality of the instruments. This approach tested whether an eQTL explained more variance for the exposure (gene expression profile of a gene) than the outcome (COVID-19). All of our cis-eQTLs showed right directionality and were kept as valid instruments ( ). In summary, 17,939 eQTLs for expression levels of 16,597 genes in 14 immune cells had been selected as valid instruments for the MR analysis.
 Our instruments and eGenes (in which eGene means a gene with one or more eQTLs) were consistently enriched in CD4 naïve and central memory T cells before and after instrument selection (CD4 NC; a and b), where CD4 SOX4 and plasma cells showed the smallest number of instruments and eGenes.

## Identification of putative causal genes for COVID-19 infection
We then estimated the putative causal effects of gene expression profiles in 14 immune cell types on COVID-19 outcomes using two-sample MR. Four types of COVID-19 outcome data were extracted from the COVID-19 Genetic Housing Initiative GWAS meta-analysis Round 7 (https://www.covid19hg.org/results/r7/), including “very severe respiratory confirmed COVID-19 vs. population” (noted as “A2”, N case = 13,769, N control = 1,072,442); “Hospitalized COVID-19 vs. population” (“B2”, N case = 32,519, N control = 2,062,805); “COVID-19 vs. population” (“C2”, N case = 122,616, N control = 2,475,240) and “hospitalized COVID-19 vs. not hospitalized COVID-19” (“B1”, N case = 16,512, N control = 71,321) groups (see “Outcome selection” in ). For COVID-19 outcomes, only GWAS summary statistics of participants of European ancestry were used. For “hospitalized COVID-19 vs. not hospitalized COVID-19” (B1), GWAS summary statistics of the European only data were not publicly available, we therefore used data from an admixed population instead, the proportion of which were European was 81% in this data ( ).
After harmonizing the exposure data (gene expression profiles of tested genes) and outcome data (COVID-19 outcomes), 14,249–14,485 MR estimates of gene expressions on COVID-19 were calculated for the four types of COVID-19 outcomes respectively ( a). Following false discovery rate (FDR) correction, a total of 144, 3, 191 and 40 MR associations (which means association of one gene expression profile against one type of COVID-19 outcome with MR and colocalization evidence, see ) remained for A2 (“very severe respiratory confirmed COVID-19 vs. population”), B1 (“hospitalized COVID-19 vs. not hospitalized COVID-19”), B2 (“Hospitalized COVID-19 vs. population”) and C2 (“COVID-19 vs. population”) respectively ( a). In order to examine heterogeneity in the MR results, we conducted a Cochran's Q test for 16 MR associations estimated by two or more instruments, which found little evidence to support heterogeneity among instruments (Q-value>0.05). To distinguish causality from confounding by linkage disequilibrium (LD), we applied two colocalization approaches, LD check, genetic colocalization, Susie and SharePro colocalization. These analyses showed that 343 of the 378 (91%) MR associations showed colocalization evidence (defined as colocalization probability ≥70% for colocalization or r2 ≥ 0.7 for LD check; Susie and SharePro colocalization probability>0.9; a, ). These 343 MR associations were related to expressions of 132 genes in 14 immune cell types.
 We conducted a comprehensive search of COVID-19 related genes in 10 databases as well as twenty-five COVID-19 associated MR research ( , , , ). , , , , , , , , We found that 58 of the 132 identified genes were not reported before for COVID-19 ( a, ).
When comparing MR associations between four COVID-19 outcomes, we found 54 genes showed MR and colocalization evidence in two or more COVID-19 outcomes. One gene, NBEAL2, showed evidence in four COVID-19 outcomes, 12 genes showed evidence in three outcomes, where 41 genes showed evidence in two outcomes ( b). As an example, the NBEAL2 gene, showed robust MR and colocalization evidence in all four types of COVID-19 outcomes ( c–e). These 54 genes showed associations with two or more COVID-19 outcomes were considered as the replicable genes ( ) connected with COVID-19. Mendelian randomization on Long COVID is provided in .

## Immune-cell type specific effects of gene expressions on COVID-19 outcomes
We further estimated the cell-type specificity of the 132 putative causal genes for COVID-19, which included 343 MR associations (200 gene-COVID-19 pairs) in total. Of them, 145 of the 200 (73%) pairs only showed effects on COVID-19 in one specific immune cell type ( a). Similarly, 30 (56%) of the 54 replicable genes (with robust MR evidence in two or more COVID-19 outcomes) showed cell-type specific effects in only one immune cell type ( b). In contrast, seven genes showed MR associations in five or more immune cell types, for example TOMM7 expressions in 13 immune cell types were associated with COVID-19. This implied a generalized immune-mediated effect of these genes on COVID-19 ( b).
 The cell-type specific MR analysis provided some evidence to support CD4 NC cell as an enriched cell type for COVID-19. However, we also observed that CD4 NC had the largest number of eGenes ( a and b). We then estimated the ratio between number of identified causal genes versus number of eGenes for each of the 14 immune cell types ( c). The results showed that the COVID-19-associated genes were enriched in three immune cell types: natural killer (NK) recruiting, plasma and CD4 SOX4 cells, after adjusting the influence of eGenes' number. In contrast, the figure was de-enriched in some other cell types like NK cells. By using another enrichment method via function ‘phyper’, compared with all the significant MR results and significant cell type MR results, as shown in , the figure was consistent with that of from c.

## Enrichment of the COVID-19 associated genes in immune-viral related mechanisms
To explore the immune-mediated mechanisms that were enriched by the identified COVID-19 associated genes, we performed a set of functional enrichment analysis including ontology clusters, pathway analysis, and gene cluster analysis. Among the 132 genes associated with COVID-19, 119 genes could be converted into Entrez gene ID and were included in the enrichment analysis. The results showed that the 119 genes were enriched in pathways such as interferon signalling, cytokine signalling in immune system and interferon alpha/beta signaling ( , “MyList_MCODE_ALL” part), consistent with findings reported in a previous study. The enrichment results could be classified into six types of biological pathways: virus response pathways, immune-inflammatory pathways, cellular process pathways, metabolic process pathways, response to stimulus and disease pathways ( a, ). We then performed protein–protein interaction (PPI) network analysis for the identified causal genes. Among the 119 genes included in this analysis, 15 of their encoded proteins showed little evidence of interaction with any other proteins, leaving 104 protein coding genes for the PPI analysis. 34 of the 104 gene-coding proteins (33%) were further clustered into four modules using Metascape ( b).
 

## Validation and repurposing of immune-related drug target for COVID-19
In light of identifying potential immune-related drug targets for COVID-19, we conducted two analyses. First of all, we searched for drugs that target the 132 identified genes in PubChem (https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/) and in existing drug–gene interaction databases, including OpenTargets, DrugBank and Drug-gene interaction database. This search identified 32 candidate drugs that target 24 of the identified genes ( ). The main indications of the 32 drugs were chronic cough, various types of pain (neuropathic, musculoskeletal, and inflammatory), tumour and lipid metabolism abnormalities, which were symptoms that occurred in patients with COVID-19 infection and post-COVID sequalae. , 10 of the 24 identified genes were unreported genes that were not reported in any previous studies and 90% of them showed an effect on the severe COVID-19 (“very severe and hospitalized COVID-19 cases” in A2 and B2 groups) and eight genes only showed MR associations in one specific cell type.
 

## Evidence triangulation to prioritize drug targets for COVID-19
Combining the above evidence obtained from this study, the following criteria were used to prioritize promising drug target genes for COVID-19 ( ): (i) the gene showed MR and colocalization to support it as a putative causal gene for COVID-19 and was not reported by previous studies (therefore it has to be an unreported gene, and the gene will get an extra score if it is also a replicable gene); (ii) the unreported gene was enriched in related pathways or included in the four clustered modules; (iii) the gene showed predicted interaction with SARS-COV-2 proteins and/or associations with existing host proteins for COVID-19; (iv) the gene is a druggable gene (either as a target for an existing drug or as a druggable target). For the 58 unreported genes with MR and colocalization evidence to support their causality, we scored and ranked them into three tiers based on the above criteria. 18 genes were ranked as Tier 1 drug targets (overall score ≥1). 23 genes, including 19 protein-coding genes and 4 non-coding genes, were ranked as Tier 2 targets (overall score between 0.35 and 1) and the remaining targets were ranked as Tier 3 targets ( ). Except 4 non-coding genes, the remaining 37 Tier 1 and Tier 2 genes were treated as promising drug targets genes for COVID-19 ( ). Of them, NCR3 was ranked as the top candidate gene with highest score of 2.1, which showed good evidence from all tested criteria. Similarly, EHMT2, RHEB and HSPA1B also showed good evidence from multiple criteria (overall score between 1.85 and 1.95). Additional findings of results are provided in .
 

## Discussion
In this study, we systematically evaluated the immune-mediated genes and mechanisms for COVID-19 by combining single-cell eQTL MR , and colocalization with pathway and protein–protein interaction information. Through MR and colocalization analyses, we identified 343 MR associations for 132 genes in 14 immune-cell types related to four types of COVID-19 outcomes, with 58 genes that we believe have not been reported before. The MR associations were enriched in some specific immune cell types such as NK recruiting, plasma and CD4 SOX4 cells, but de-enriched in other cell types such as NK cells. Of note, 145 gene-COVID-19 pairs (73%) showed causal effects in one specific immune cell type. We also found two pathways, brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) signalling and primary ovarian insufficiency, that were enriched by the genes we identified. By using a deep learning model, we observed 107 causal genes (81%) that were predicted to interact with SARS-COV-2 proteins. We further developed a tier system to prioritize COVID-19 drug targets and identified 37 promising drug target genes that showed multiple layers of evidence like functional enrichment, interactions with SARS-COV-2 proteins and druggability. Collectively, our study identifies immune-mediated causal genes and mechanisms for COVID-19 and provides a tier system to prioritize immune-related drug targets for COVID-19.
Our study provided a comprehensive demonstration of cell-type specificity for the prioritized COVID-19 associated genes. The MR results revealed that 73% of the identified gene-COVID-19 pairs only showed effects on one specific immune cell type. For instance, the MR associations of NBEAL2 and ZNF311 were exclusively identified in CD4 NC cells. Recent studies have showed that NBEAL2 acts as a causal gene for gray platelet syndrome , , and bovine pigmentation disease, and also regulates the expression of CTLA-4 in conventional T cells and thereby influences the number and status of effector T cells. In conjunction with the pathway enrichment findings and the tissue-specific MR results, it is hypothesized that this gene might impact the inflammatory responses of immune cells through the regulation of granulocyte degranulation. Additionally, considering recent discussions on coagulation issues in COVID-19, 
NBEAL2 could also play a role in platelet function by influencing the differentiation of myeloid cells. In summary, our study illustrated that cell-type specificity is commonplace for causal genes influencing COVID-19 risk.
The cell type enrichment analysis suggested that the identified genes for COVID-19 were de-enriched in NK cells but were enriched in NK R cells. NK cells in patients with severe COVID-19 exhibit significant phenotypic and functional changes, such as a decrease in number and impaired function, , , which might partly explain the de-enrichment of NK cells we observed. In addition, our study suggested that most of the causal genes identified in NK recruiting cells were associated with hospitalized COVID-19 cases (B2) and severe COVID-19 cases (A2). Although the role of NK cells in COVID-19 has been summarized before, few studies have linked NK recruiting cells with COVID-19. The distinguished enrichment pattern between NK and NK recruiting cells may suggest that the causal genes may specifically activate NK recruiting cells to fight against SARS-COV-2 infected cells by recruiting other immune cells via cytokine productions, rather than other NK cell functions. Additional functional investigations are warranted for specific immune cell types, such as NK recruiting cells.
Our mediation results (see ) detected a few cell-type eGenes (48 MR associations, accounting for 12%) that could be mediated by smoking (5 MR results) or obesity (48 MR results, BMI was utilized as an indicator of obesity) in their impact on the development of COVID-19 (2–13% mediation proportions), which are consistent with conclusions drawn in previous literature identifies smoking as a significant risk factor for the progression and exacerbation of COVID-19. , 
More and more research has indicated that COVID-19 infection could lead to damage in multiple systems of the human body. , It is noted that the genes identified in the present study and their enriched pathways were also involved in various systems. For instance, the BDNF signalling pathway (including FRS3) is known to play an important role in a variety of neurological diseases, and recent studies have also found that serum BDNF levels were decreased in individuals with COVID-19, , which suggests a potential mechanism for immune-cell mediated neurological pathways on COVID-19 infection. MSH5, has been linked to male azoospermia and female primary ovarian failure and is proposed to influence the development and function of the reproductive system by participating in DNA mismatch repair during meiosis. Given the observed disruption of the HPG axis in patients with COVID-19 affecting spermatogenesis and epididymis, our identification of those immune-cell derived reproductive-related genes and pathways might offer some insight into this impairment.
Recent studies have prioritized drug targets for complex diseases using human genetics tools. , , However, there is still a huge gap between causal genes and promising drug targets for COVID-19. Therefore, we developed a tier system to prioritize drug targets from the 132 COVID-19 associated genes, which combined four pieces of evidence from multiple independent data sources. This approach prioritized 37 genes as the most promising drug targets for COVID-19 (Tier 1 and Tier 2 genes). Among them, NCR3, got the highest score, which showed causal effects on three COVID-19 outcomes (very severe respiratory confirmed COVID-19 vs. population; Hospitalized COVID-19 vs. population; and COVID-19 vs. population). This gene showed effects in two cell types, CD4 SOX9 and Mono NC. Combining this evidence, NCR3 could be considered as a druggable target with cell-specificity upon different types of COVID-19 severity. The drug targeting of NCR3 is under clinical trial investigation for tumour and tumour metastasis. , This finding aligned with our pathway enrichment results, which suggested that NCR3 is related to leukocyte activation and proliferation. Furthermore, emerging studies suggest that COVID-19, particularly severe COVID-19, may serve as a biomarker for tumour formation. , We hypothesized that this could be a contributing factor to tumourigenesis, as several of our causal genes were known to be associated with tumours, such as FUBP1,

RHEB and NOTCH4. 
Our study has several limitations. Due to the lack of gene expression data before and after cell activation, we are unable to understand the dynamic causal pattern of our identified genes on COVID-19, which may identify additional causal genes and mechanisms for COVID-19. Second, it is worth noting that due to the smaller number of cases in the Long COVID dataset and COVID-19 outcome B1 (hospitalized COVID-19 vs. not hospitalized COVID-19), we had limited statistical power to identify causal genes in these groups compared with other COVID-19 outcomes. Third, the COVID-19 genome-wide association study data is related to prevalent COVID-19 cases. Thus, the new targets identified in this study might be better suited for COVID-19 prevention. However, it is fully possible that a target for disease prevention can also be a target for disease treatment, such as OAS1 for COVID-19. Therefore, the causal genes identified in this study still require further experimental confirmation and population-based verification to determine their specificity for COVID-19 treatment.
In conclusion, we comprehensively examined the immune-mediated genes and mechanisms across 14 immune-cell types for COVID-19. The tier system we developed supports identification of 37 genes as promising drug targets for COVID-19. Future studies are warranted to understand the causal mechanisms, optimal targets, efficacy and safety of these targets and their potential benefits on preventing and treating COVID-19.
