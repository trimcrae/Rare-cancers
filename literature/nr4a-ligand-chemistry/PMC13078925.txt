https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-nc-nd/4.0/)Gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) tightly regulates the synthesis and the release of gonadotropins, follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH)
and luteinizing hormone (LH), while the intracellular molecular mechanisms following GnRH signal for the regulation of transcription remain
incompletely understood. In this study, we used primary culture of rat anterior pituitary cells to investigate the role of NR4A
transcription factors (NR4A1, NR4A2, and NR4A3) in GnRH regulation of gonadotropin secretion. GnRH agonist stimulation rapidly and
transiently increased Nr4a1, Nr4a2, and Nr4a3 expression within one hour, accompanied by
a time-dependent increase in Fshb mRNA levels and the secretion of both FSH and LH. The knockdown of each
Nr4a gene using siRNA significantly reduced Fshb expression under GnRH stimulation.
Nr4a1 knockdown caused the most pronounced decrease in FSH secretion. Although Lhb and
Cga mRNA levels were largely unaffected, LH secretion was consistently reduced following NR4A knockdown. These findings
suggest that NR4A transcription factors act downstream of GnRH signaling to promote Fshb transcription and facilitate
gonadotropin secretion, thereby modulating GnRH-dependent control of FSH and LH secretion.

The neuroendocrine pathway relies on the gonadotropin-releasing hormone
(GnRH) secreted by the hypothalamus. GnRH acts on gonadotropes of the anterior pituitary gland to stimulate the synthesis and release of two key
reproductive hormones: follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) and luteinizing hormone (LH). Both gonadotropins are heterodimeric glycoproteins
comprising a common α-subunit (CGA) and hormone-specific β-subunits (FSHβ and LHβ) [ ]. Distinct
transcription factors tightly regulate the expression of each subunit gene [ ].
Although the gonadotrope secretes FSH and LH, their secretion patterns and transcriptional regulation vary each other. LH is released in a
pulsatile manner in response to intermittent GnRH stimulation, whereas FSH secretion is rather sustained, constitutive, and primarily regulated
at the transcriptional level of the Fshb gene [ ]. These different secretory dynamics
reflect the specialized physiological roles of each hormone in processes such as follicular development and ovulation, indicating that distinct
regulatory pathways govern FSH and LH secretion [ , ].
The NR4A family, comprising NR4A1 (Nur77), NR4A2 (Nurr1), and NR4A3 (Nor1), consists of orphan nuclear receptors that are rapidly and
transiently induced in response to extracellular stimuli [ ]. These transcription factors share structural
similarities and can act independently or cooperatively to regulate the expression of their target genes [ , ]. Defining their roles in the pituitary gonadotropes will improve our understanding of the
transcriptional networks underlying reproductive endocrine regulation.
In our recent work using the mouse-derived gonadotrope cell line LβT2, we demonstrated that GnRH stimulation induces immediate early gene
expression of Nr4a3 and that NR4A3 suppresses GnRH-dependent Fshb gene expression [ ]. These findings suggested a functional link between NR4A3 expression and FSH secretion. Several reports have also shown
that GnRH stimulation rapidly and transiently upregulates not only Nr4a3 but also Nr4a1 and
Nr4a2 in gonadotropes [ , , , , , ]. We have also observed dynamic Nr4a expression patterns in the rat anterior pituitary, which parallels
the LH surge [ , ]. In addition, other groups have shown that
in the immature mouse gonadotrope cell line αT3-1, GnRH-induced Nr4a1 expression contributes to derepressing
Fshb transcription [ ]. Overall, these findings raise questions regarding the role of
synchronously expressed NR4As in GnRH-responsive Fshb expression and gonadotropin secretion. However, the individual
contributions of NR4A1, NR4A2, and NR4A3 to Fshb, Lhb, and Cga expression, as well as to
gonadotropin secretion, remain unclear.
Although cell lines such as LβT2 and αT3-1 are widely used in pituitary endocrinology, their capacity for endogenous hormone secretion is
limited [ , ]. To address these limitations, the present study
used primary cultures of rat anterior pituitary cells, which better represent physiological conditions, to investigate the responses of
Nr4a1, Nr4a2, and Nr4a3 toward GnRH stimulation and define their individual functional
roles in regulating gonadotropin gene expression and hormone secretion.

## Materials and Methods

## Animals
Female Wistar-Imamichi rats (200–300 g) were purchased from SLC Japan (Shizuoka, Japan). Rats were housed under controlled conditions (23 ±
3°C and 14-h/10-h light–dark cycle, respectively, with lights on at 0500 h) with free access to standard laboratory chow and tap water. All
procedures were approved by the Animal Care and Use Committee of the Kitasato University School of Veterinary Medicine (approval no.:
24–015).

## Primary pituitary cell cultures
Adult female rats were euthanized by decapitation under isoflurane anesthesia, and the anterior pituitary glands were promptly and
aseptically collected. Primary cultures of anterior pituitary cells were prepared as previously described [ ]. Briefly, anterior pituitaries were minced into approximately 0.5 mm fragments and incubated at 37°C for 40 min in trypsin
solution containing 0.25% trypsin (Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc., Waltham, MA, USA), 10 mM EDTA (Kanto chemical, Co., Inc., Tokyo, Japan)
and 20 mM HEPES (Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc.) in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM; Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc.). Following
enzymatic digestion, the fragments were gently dissociated into single cells by pipetting. The resulting single-cell suspension was plated
in 96-well plates at a density of 2.5 × 104 cells per well and cultured in DMEM supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (Thermo
Fisher Scientific Inc.) and 1% MEM nonessential amino acids (Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc.) at 37°C in a humidified atmosphere of 5%
CO2 for 48 h. After the initial incubation, the culture medium was replaced with fresh medium containing either vehicle or
10−8 M GnRH agonist (GnRHa, Des-Gly10 [Pro9]-GnRH ethylamide; Intervet K.K., Tokyo, Japan). Supernatants were collected at one,
three, and six hours after treatment and stored at –30°C. Cells were harvested using ISOGEN (Nippon Gene, Tokyo, Japan), and stored at –80°C
until analysis. For the zero-hour time point, cells were collected immediately before medium replacement.

## Transfection with siRNA
Gene-specific 27mer siRNA duplexes targeting Nr4a1 (siNr4a1, #SR500720B), Nr4a2 (siNr4a2, #SR513154C),
and Nr4a3 (siNr4a3, #SR500165A) and a universal scrambled negative control siRNA duplex (siCont, #SR30004) were purchased
from OriGene Technologies (Rockville, MD, USA). Nr4a family genes were knocked down by forward transfection with
Lipofectamine RNAiMAX reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Inc.). For each well of a 96-well plate, 20 μl of Opti-MEM (Thermo Fisher
Scientific Inc.) containing 10 pmol of either siNr4a1, siNr4a2, siNr4a3, or siCont and 1 μl of Lipofectamine RNAiMAX was added. Dispersed
anterior pituitary cells were then seeded directly onto the transfection mixture at a density of 2.5 × 104 cells per 100 μl per
well. After 48 h, the culture medium was replaced with fresh medium containing either vehicle or GnRHa (10−8 M). The culture
supernatants and cells were collected three hours after treatment and stored for subsequent analyses.

## Reverse-transcription quantitative PCR (qPCR)
Total RNA was extracted from pituitary cells using ISOGEN and reverse transcribed into cDNA using a High-Capacity cDNA Reverse
Transcription Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc.). The primer sequences used for qPCR assays are listed in . qPCR was performed on a StepOnePlus Real-Time PCR system (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA) using THUNDERBIRD SYBR
qPCR Mix (TOYOBO, Osaka, Japan) according to the manufacturer's instructions. The cycle threshold (Ct) values were set to a ΔRn of 0.5, and
relative gene expression was quantified using the ΔΔCT method, with ribosomal protein L19 (Rpl19) as the internal reference gene.

## Measurement of FSH and LH concentrations
Primary antibodies against FSH (anti-rFSH-S11, rabbit polyclonal, 1:12.5, AFP-C0972881, RRID:AB_2687903) and LH (anti-LH, rabbit
polyclonal, 1:40, AFP-240580, RRID:AB_2784499), as well as purified rat FSH (rFSH-I-9, AFP-5178B) and LH (rLH-I-9, AFP-11538B), and
reference standards for FSH (rFSH-RP-2, AFP-4621B) and LH (rLH-RP-3, AFP-7187B), were obtained from the National Hormone and Peptide Program
(Harbor-UCLA Medical Center, Torrance, CA, USA). Homemade anti-rabbit gamma-globulin goat serum, used as a secondary antibody for the
immunoassays, was generated in our laboratory and purified to the IgG fraction using Ab Spin Trap columns (GE Healthcare, Chicago, IL,
USA).
The concentrations of FSH and LH in the culture supernatants were measured using a time-resolved fluorometric immunoassay (DELFIA system;
PerkinElmer, Branchburg, NJ, USA). Briefly, 96-well immune plates (DELFIA® Microtitration Plates; PerkinElmer) were first coated with
anti-rabbit gamma globulin, followed by incubation with either anti-rFSH-S11 (1:5,000 dilution) or anti-LH (1:40,000 dilution). For the FSH
assay, diluted culture supernatants and FSH standards were incubated overnight at 23°C. For the LH assay, diluted samples and LH standards
were incubated overnight at 4°C. Biotinylated rat FSH and LH were prepared using biotin-LC-LC-NHS (Tokyo Chemical Industry, Tokyo, Japan).
After washing the plates, appropriately diluted biotinylated FSH and LH were added and incubated at room temperature for two hours and one
hour, respectively. For signal amplification using the tyramide signal amplification system, horseradish peroxidase-conjugated streptavidin
(Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc.) was diluted 1:100,000 and incubated for 30 min at room temperature. This was followed by a 30-min incubation
with 50 μM biotinyl tyramide (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) in 0.1 M sodium borate buffer (pH 8.5, Kanto chemical, Co., Inc.)
containing 0.0015% hydrogen peroxide (Kanto chemical, Co., Inc.) and 50 μg/ml 4-iodophenol (Sigma-Aldrich). After thorough washing,
europium-labeled streptavidin (PerkinElmer) was added at a 1:1,000 dilution and incubated for 30 min at room temperature. Finally, the
plates were treated with DELFIA Enhancement Solution (PerkinElmer), and the specific fluorescence was measured using a Multilabel Reader
2030 ARVO X4 (PerkinElmer) at 340 nm excitation and 613 nm emission. The intra- and inter-assay coefficients of variation were 11.2% and
9.7%, respectively, for the FSH assay, and 6.4% and 1.6%, respectively, for the LH assay.

## Statistical analysis
Data are presented as mean ± standard error of the mean (SEM). Statistical comparisons were performed using one-way analysis of variance,
followed by the Tukey-Kramer test, and P < 0.05 was considered statistically significant. All statistical analyses were performed using
the JMP Student Edition 18.2.2 (JMP Statistical Discovery LLC, Cary, NC, USA).

## Results

## Induction of gonadotropin secretion and transient expression of NR4A family genes following GnRH stimulation
First, we investigated the effects of a gonadotropin-releasing hormone agonist (GnRHa) on gonadotropin secretion and subunit gene
expression in primary rat pituitary cells. Following GnRHa stimulation, the LH and FSH concentrations in the culture supernatant
significantly increased in a time-dependent manner, with a marked increase observed after three hours of treatment ( ). Gene expression analysis revealed significant upregulation of Fshb mRNA at three hours post-stimulation. In
contrast, Lhb expression remained unchanged ( ). Cga expression increased
modestly after six hours. Simultaneously, the expression of the NR4A transcription factors—Nr4a1, Nr4a2,
and Nr4a3—was rapidly and significantly increased within one hour of GnRHa treatment, followed by a subsequent decline,
indicating a transient response pattern ( ).

## Suppression of gonadotropin subunit gene expression by Nr4a gene knockdown
To investigate the functional roles of NR4As in the regulation of gonadotropin subunit genes, we performed individual siRNA-mediated
knockdowns of each gene. Knockdown of Nr4a1 resulted in approximately 75% reduction in its expression, regardless of GnRHa
stimulation, and significantly suppressed Fshb expression under stimulated conditions, with a slight decrease in both
Lhb and Cga expression ( ). Similarly, Nr4a2 knockdown led to a 34–48% decrease in its expression, accompanied by a significant reduction in
Fshb ( ). Although Lhb expression remained unchanged,
Cga expression decreased moderately. In the case of Nr4a3, its knockdown (58–60% reduction)
significantly attenuated GnRHa-induced Fshb expression and led to a decrease in Lhb and
Cga expression ( ).

## The involvement of NR4As in GnRH-dependent gonadotropin secretion
Nr4a1 knockdown significantly reduced GnRHa-induced FSH secretion, whereas knockdown of Nr4a2 or
Nr4a3 had no significant effect on FSH secretion levels ( ). In contrast, LH secretion in response to GnRHa was significantly suppressed by knockdown of any of the three Nr4a
genes ( ).

## Discussion
Using primary anterior pituitary cultures, we demonstrated that three NR4A family transcription factors were rapidly induced by GnRH
stimulation and promoted Fshb expression and LH secretion. These results indicate that NR4A-mediated transcriptional
regulation contributes to the physiological control of GnRH on FSH and LH secretion dynamics in mature pituitary gonadotropes. To date, most
studies have focused on the role of individual NR4A factor, particularly NR4A1, in regulating gonadotropin gene expression. However, a
comprehensive analysis of all the NR4As in the context of gonadotropin synthesis and release has not yet been conducted. To our knowledge, our
results provide the first evidence that all three NR4A factors regulate gonadotropin gene expression and GnRH-dependent hormone secretion.
Among the gonadotropin genes, Fshb expression was significantly induced by GnRH stimulation and was suppressed following the
knockdown of any individual NR4A factor. This strongly suggests that all three NR4As contribute to GnRH-induced activation of
Fshb transcription. In contrast, the effects on Lhb and Cga expression were less
pronounced than those on Fshb, indicating that the NR4As may act selectively on Fshb gene promoter. The
possibility that NR4A1 specifically promotes Fshb expression has been proposed previously [ , , , ].
Unexpectedly, NR4A3 enhanced Fshb expression in primary pituitary cells, in contrast to our previous findings using the LβT2
gonadotrope cell line [ ], in which NR4A3 suppresses Fshb expression. The underlying
cause of this discrepancy remains unclear. There are several plausible explanations for these findings. It may be due to indirect effects
resulting from gene knockdown in non-gonadotropic cells, since primary pituitary cultures comprise a heterogeneous population of cell types.
Another possibility is that LβT2 cells do not fully reflect the physiological characteristics of mature pituitary gonadotropes. Indeed, Bliss
et al. have reported that the expression pattern of NR4A1 in LβT2 cells may not accurately mirror that of normal gonadotropes [ ], suggesting the importance of selecting appropriate cellular models for elucidating NR4A family
functions.
Notably, although Lhb mRNA expression was not substantially altered by Nr4a knockdown, LH secretion was
significantly reduced. This suggests that the NR4A family is involved in the GnRH-regulated secretion. To our knowledge, NR4As have not been
previously implicated in hormone exocytosis. LH secretion is highly dependent on the physiological pattern of pulsatility or a surge in GnRH
release [ ], and is primarily regulated by calcium-dependent pathways [ ]. These pathways involve a biphasic calcium response: an initial transient phase mediated by IP3-induced mobilization,
followed by a sustained phase via PKC activation [ , ]. Our
current observations suggest that NR4As contribute to the latter phase by regulating genes involved in these signaling pathways.
In contrast, FSH is generally secreted constitutively [ , ],
and its levels are closely linked to Fshb transcription [ ]. Thus, the NR4A family
may serve as key transcriptional regulators that support this constitutive secretion. Considering the timeline of increased
Fshb expression and FSH secretion following GnRH agonist stimulation, the impact of Nr4a knockdown on
constitutive secretion may be more apparent beyond the 3-h time point examined in our study. This might explain the modest reduction in FSH
secretion.
Fshb transcriptional activity is primarily governed by a partial cAMP response element (CRE)/activator protein 1 (AP1)
element in the proximal Fshb promoter, which serves as a key binding site for c-Fos, c-Jun, and CREB [ , ]. Notably, this element is estimated to account for 30–50% of the total GnRH
response [ ]. Furthermore, c-Fos-deficient mice exhibit an infertile phenotype similar to that observed
in FSHB knockout mice [ , ]. Conversely, although
reproductive phenotypes have not been fully characterized in NR4A1 or NR4A3 knockout mice, fertility appears to be preserved [ , ]. However, because NR4A2 knockout mice die shortly after
birth due to suckling defects, their reproductive capacity remains unknown [ ]. Together with our data
showing only partial suppression of Fshb, these findings suggest that NR4A factors may play a supportive rather than an
essential role in FSH regulation.
Members of the NR4A family share common structural domains and DNA response elements, and their expression and effects are often coordinated
[ , , ]. Our findings
support this hypothesis, indicating that NR4A1 shows greater induction and functional effects, followed by NR4A3 and NR4A2. Notably, although
Nr4a3 mRNA levels were lower than those of Nr4a1/Nr4a2 in our qPCR analysis, Nr4a3
knockdown significantly attenuated GnRHa-induced Fshb expression and reduced GnRHa-induced LH secretion. These results
suggest that NR4A3 could have a relatively high functional impact compared with those of NR4A1/NR4A2. However, these apparent differences and
the relative contributions of each factor should be interpreted with caution. First, NR4A factors are expressed not only in gonadotropes but
also in other anterior pituitary cell types [ , , ], which limits the validity of directly comparing absolute expression levels across heterogeneous
primary pituitary cultures. In addition, because our qPCR analysis was not based on absolute quantification, direct comparisons across
different genes should be made cautiously. The variable knockdown efficiency observed in this study (approximately 40–70%) further complicates
the quantitative assessment of each factor’s contribution. To more precisely define the roles of individual NR4A, experimental models of
gonadotrope-specific NR4A suppression or deletion are required.
Whether these three NR4A proteins act cooperatively or compensate for each other in gonadotropin regulation remains an important question.
Multiple studies and reviews have proposed the possibility of heterodimeric complex formation among NR4As [ , , , ,
 ]. Furthermore, the development of a severe myeloid/lymphoid-lineage phenotype in NR4A1/NR4A3
double-knockout mice [ , ], a phenotype absent in
single-knockout mice, supports the importance of cooperative NR4A function. However, co-transfection of siRNAs targeting two or three
Nr4a genes consistently decreased cell viability in our system. Under these conditions, hormonal responses showed
substantial variability, and we were unable to obtain reproducible, interpretable double/triple knockdown data for inclusion. Future
investigations using high-efficiency viral delivery systems or cell type-specific knockout models are required to elucidate these interactions
and their physiological significance.
In conclusion, our study revealed that the NR4A family comprises transcription factors that promote GnRH-dependent Fshb
expression and LH secretion in rat pituitary cells. Furthermore, all three NR4As appeared to participate in GnRH-responsive gonadotropin
secretion, suggesting the existence of interdependent regulatory mechanisms. Future studies should determine whether these factors form
complexes with one another or other transcriptional co-regulators, identify their direct binding sites within target gene promoters, and
clarify their physiological significance in reproductive function in vivo.
