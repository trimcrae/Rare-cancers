## Abstract
Vaccine adjuvants are pivotal in novel vaccine development, enhancing immunization and modulating immune responses. This study prepared liposomes loaded with simvastatin and ginsenoside Rh2 (LIPO-SIM-Rh2) via high-speed shearing, rotary evaporation, and high-pressure homogenization. Using C57BL/6J mice, six groups were established to explore its immune-enhancing effects and mechanisms. Following two-dose immunization at 0 and 21 days, IgG antibody titers (ELISA), immune cell responses (flow cytometry), and safety/function of antigen-presenting cells (pathological sections, immunofluorescence staining) were detected. Results showed LIPO-SIM-Rh2 had a stable dispersion and a near-100 % encapsulation efficiency. Compared with other groups, it significantly increased anti-OVA specific IgG titer, promoted cellular immune response, up-regulated MHC-I molecule antigen presentation pathway, activated macrophages, promoted dendritic cell homing, and caused no obvious damage or inflammation to major organs. In conclusion, LIPO-SIM-Rh2 adjuvant has a feasible preparation method and strong immune-enhancing potential for vaccines.
Keywords: Adjuvant, Vaccine, Liposome, Simvastatin, Ginsenoside Rh2, Cellular immunities

## Introduction
Vaccines remain the most effective tool for the prevention and control of infectious diseases [ , ]. However, the suboptimal immunogenicity of some candidate vaccines restricts the use of vaccines in the prevention of diseases caused by more complex pathogens (such as the human immunodeficiency virus, malaria) [ , , , , ] or other infectious diseases (such as adult tuberculosis, cancer vaccines, etc.) [ , ]. In recent years, the AS01 adjuvant has played a pivotal role in the successful development of the herpes zoster vaccine and other vaccines including malaria candidates. By significantly augmenting cellular immune responses, it has profoundly inspired research and innovation in vaccines relying on novel adjuvants [ , ]. On the other hand, the hepatitis B vaccine incorporating the CpG adjuvant has optimized the immunization schedule, reducing the number of doses from three to two [ , ]. This indicates that the new adjuvant holds great potential for enhancing approved vaccine products.
However, globally, only a limited set of eight human vaccine adjuvants are currently available, namely aluminum salts, MF59, AS01, AS03, AS04, CpG1018, Matrix-M, and AF03 [ , , ]. Evidently, this is far from sufficient to cater to the diverse requirements of a wide array of vaccine types and the substantial demand for vaccines. Furthermore, given that adjuvants are not applicable or suitable for all vaccines, it is of utmost importance to develop a greater number of novel adjuvants. In fact, although there is a wide array of substances exhibiting adjuvant effects, the number of adjuvant candidates with good druggability is relatively small.
Simvastatin is a commonly and extensively utilized lipid-lowering drug, predominantly applied in the treatment of hypercholesterolemia and mixed hyperlipidemia. Its mechanism for reducing blood lipids lies in the inhibition of the activity of 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl coenzyme A (HMG-CoA) reductase, thereby decreasing the synthesis of cholesterol in the liver. The typical starting dosage is 10 mg per day [ , ]. As a medication that has been extensively and globally administered for over three decades, simvastatin has accumulated an extensive volume of safety data. Furthermore, the preparation process of simvastatin is stable and cost-efficient, making it a highly promising candidate for vaccine adjuvants [ , , ]. Regarding the adjuvant potential of simvastatin, it is only in recent years that research has confirmed that it can delay the retention time of antigens by inhibiting the mevalonate pathway, and enhance antigen presentation and T cell activation. The titer of the induced IgG antibody is 703 times higher than that induced by the antigen alone and 53 times higher than that of the aluminum adjuvant control group [ ]. However, simvastatin is insoluble in water. In the study, it was dissolved in DMSO before injection [ ], a practice that is clearly not suitable for the scenarios of human vaccine injection. Therefore, it may be a worthwhile exploration to consider using liposome (LIPO) technology to encapsulate simvastatin (SIM) to form the LIPO-SIM adjuvant.
Ginsenoside Rh2, a natural active component derived from ginseng, is water-insoluble, like simvastatin. It exhibits excellent safety and can be mass-produced via biotransformation or hydrolysis processes [ , ]. Previous studies have shown that Rh2-loaded liposomes serve as excellent adjuvant candidates, capable of enhancing the neutralizing antibody titer of foot-and-mouth disease vaccine, elevating cellular immune responses, and promoting splenic cell proliferation [ ].
Thus, we proposed preparing the LIPO-SIM-Rh2 bimolecular nano-adjuvant by co-encapsulating both compounds into liposomes, aiming to evaluate its immune-enhancing effect on antigens and explore its potential as a vaccine adjuvant. The research framework is outlined in .

## Materials and methods
2.1.

## Materials
Simvastatin was procured from MedChemExpress (New Jersey, USA), while 20(S)-Ginsenoside Rh2 (with a purity of ≥98 %) was obtained from Macklin (Shanghai, China). DOPC and DSPE-mPEG, which were components of theLIPO skeleton, were sourced from GLPBOP (New Jersey, USA), and cholesterol was purchased from J&K scientific (Beijing, China). The 10 kDa ultrafiltration concentration tubes were provided by Millipore (Billerica, Massachusetts, USA). 200-mesh formvar-coated copper grids were purchased from Zhongjing Keyi (Beijing, China). Uranyl acetate was purchased from TED PELLA (California, USA). RPMI 1640 medium and fetal bovine serum (FBS) were furnished by Gibco (New York, USA). Ovalbumin (OVA) and bovine serum albumin (BSA) were supplied by Solarbio Science & Technology (Beijing, China). The TSA Fluorescence Three Staining Kit and TSA Fluorescence Single Staining Kit were supplied by CIGBIO Biomed Co., Ltd. (Hangzhou, China). DAPI was obtained from Beyotime Biotechnology (Shanghai, China). Goat anti-mouse IgG conjugated with horseradish peroxidase (HRP) was furnished by Transgen Biotech (Beijing, China). In this study, the antibodies employed for flow cytometry detection are as follows. BioLegend (San Diego, USA) was the provider of CD3-APC-CY7, CD8-FITC, NK1.1-PE, MHC-I-SIINFEKL-PE, CD127-PE-CY7, and KLRG1-APC. CD4-Percp-cy5.5 was acquired from BD Pharmingen (San Jose, USA).

2.2.

## Preparation and characterization of liposomes
To prepare the oil phase of liposomes, DOPC, CHOL, DSPE-mPEG, simvastatin, and ginsenoside Rh2 were dissolved in absolute ethanol at mass ratios of 50:22:0.5:50:50 for LIPO-SIM-Rh2 (DOPC: CHOL: DSPE-mPEG: simvastatin: ginsenoside Rh2), 50:22:0.5:100 for LIPO-SIM (DOPC: CHOL: DSPE-mPEG: simvastatin), and 50:22:0.5:100 for LIPO-Rh2 (DOPC: CHOL: DSPE-mPEG: ginsenoside Rh2). A high-speed shearing emulsifier (Fluke FA25, USA) was then operated at 10,000 rpm to generate the shearing environment. Under this condition, the oil phase was slowly introduced into the PBS solution. After that, the resulting primary LIPO emulsion was transferred to rotary evaporator. It was rotated at a speed of 75 rpm for 12 min to evaporate the ethanol. Finally, the emulsion underwent homogenization procedures in a high-pressure nanofluidizer (Nanogenizer 30K, USA). Specifically, it was homogenized twice at 10,000 psi and then twice at 18,000 psi, leading to the successful preparation of LIPO-SIM-Rh2.
At a temperature of (25 ± 0.5 °C), the average particle size, Zeta potential and polymer dispersity index (PDI) were determined using DLS (Bettersize, China). The average encapsulation efficiency % (EE %) was measured via the ultrafiltration centrifugation method and analyzed by HPLC (Thermo Scientific, Rockford, USA), following the procedures detailed in a prior study [ ].
15 μL of the LIPO-SIM-Rh2 suspension was pipetted onto a TEM grid and allowed to adsorb for 10 min. Excess liquid was blotted away, and the grid was left to air-dry slightly. Subsequently, 20 μL of 2 % uranyl acetate staining solution was pipetted onto the grid and left to stand for 5 min. Excess staining solution was removed, and the grid was air-dried under incandescent light. High-resolution images were observed and acquired using a JEOL JEM-1400 Flash transmission electron microscope (JEOL, Japan) equipped with a high-sensitivity sCMOS camera, operating at an accelerating voltage of 80 kV.

2.3.

## Ethics statement and animal experimental design
All experimental procedures related to animals were carried out in compliance with the ethical guidelines set by the Animal Ethics Committee of Ganzhou Polytechnic College. The approval number for this study was GZPT202401, and the approval was granted on Jan. 21, 2024.
In the present work, female C57BL/6J mice, at an age of 8–10 weeks and with a body weight ranging from 20 to 22 g, were utilized. These mice were sourced from GemPharmatech Co., Ltd located in Nanjing, China. Subsequently, they were housed in cages equipped with individual ventilation mechanisms, ensuring they had free access to both food and water. The animal experiments were partitioned into six groups, each comprising four animals. These groups were designated as the LIPO-SIM-Rh2 + OVA group, the LIPO-SIM + OVA group, the LIPO-Rh2 + OVA group, the LIPO-Blank + OVA group, the OVA protein control group, and the PBS blank group. Prior to immunization, a small quantity of blood was drawn from the tail veins of all animals to prepare serum. The immunization protocol involved two injections, administered at day 0 and day 21 respectively, and samples were collected on the 28th day. The injection volume was set at 0.2 mL, and the injection dosage for each administration was as follows: the OVA content was 100 μg. The LIPO-SIM-Rh2 group contained 50 μg of SIM and 50 μg of Rh2. The LIPO-SIM group contained 100 μg of SIM, the LIPO-Rh2 group contained 100 μg of Rh2, and the volume of LIPO-Blank was equivalent to that of LIPO-SIM- Rh2.

2.4.

## Assessment of immune responses
IgG antibody titers were measured by ELISA and the ELISA protocol was in accordance with the methodology described by Xia et al. [ ]. The features of immune cell responses were assessed via flow cytometry (Thermo Fisher Scientific, MA, USA). The percentages of T cells (CD3+), CD4+ T cells (CD3+CD4+), CD8+ T cells (CD3+CD8+), the MHC-I molecular pathway (MHC-I-SIINFEKL+), memory precursor effector CD8+ T cells (MPECs, CD3+CD8+CD127highKLRG1low/-), and short-lived effector CD8+ T cells (SLECs, CD3+CD8+CD127lowKLRG1high) within spleen cells were determined.
The principal organs, including the heart, liver, kidney, lung, spleen, and local lymph nodes, were immersed in 4 % paraformaldehyde for fixation. Subsequently, pathological sections were fabricated, and hematoxylin-eosin (HE) staining was utilized to evaluate the potential of LIPO-Rh2 to trigger organ inflammation or toxicity. Moreover, regarding the tissue sections of the local lymph nodes, immunofluorescence staining was carried out to explore the functions of macrophages and dendritic cells (DCs). In accordance with the manufacturer's guidelines, the TSA (tyramide signal amplification) Fluorescence Three Staining Kit and TSA Fluorescence Single Staining Kit were applied to examine macrophage activation (anti-CD80 (TSA 520), anti-CD86 (TSA 570), anti-CD206 (TSA 690), sourced from Santa Cruz Biotechnology, USA) and DC homing (anti-CD11c (TSA 520), obtained from Proteintech Group, Inc., Wuhan, China). Subsequently, the slides were imaged using a fluorescence microscope.

2.5.

## Statistical analysis
Data were presented in the form of mean ± standard deviation (SD). To evaluate the significance of the differences between the two groups, Student's t-test was performed with the aid of GraphPad Prism 8.0 software (GraphPad Software Inc., located in San Diego, CA, USA). In every analysis, statistical significance was determined when the p-value was less than 0.05.

## Result
3.1.

## LIPO-SIM-Rh2 was a stable liposome
Dynamic light scattering (DLS) was employed to analyze the particle size distribution and characterize the properties of LIPO-SIM-Rh2. A showed the appearance of LIPO-SIM-Rh2 after preparation, presenting a translucent state. B–D displayed the appearances of LIPO-SIM-Rh2, LIPO-SIM, and LIPO-Rh2 before high-pressure homogenization and rotary evaporation experiments, which exhibited remarkable insoluble small particles. As illustrated in E, the measured ζ-average value was (119.34 ± 1.58 nm). Under the transmission electron microscope, they exhibited a spherical shape with certain size variations, which was consistent with the trend of particle size distribution detected by DLS ( F). In terms of stability, the unchanged translucent appearance, the relatively low polydispersity index (PDI) value of (0.133 ± 0.02), and the relatively high zeta potential of (−12.67 ± 0.635) mV collectively suggested that LIPO-SIM-Rh2 could be considered to some extent a nanoparticle with stable dispersion properties ( G–H). Additionally, neither simvastatin nor ginsenoside Rh2 was detected in the ultrafiltrate. Furthermore, no precipitation of solid substances was observed either before or after the ultrafiltration process. This observation could imply that the encapsulation efficiency of simvastatin and Rh2 was close to 100 %.
 
3.2.

## LIPO-SIM-Rh2 significantly enhanced specific immune responses
When compared with the specific IgG titers against OVA elicited by LIPO-SIM and LIPO-Rh2, those induced by LIPO-SIM-Rh2 showed a significant increase. Moreover, the humoral immune response level triggered by LIPO-SIM was significantly higher than that induced by LIPO-Rh2 and LIPO-Blank. Additionally, the LIPO-SIM-Rh2 adjuvant significantly promoted cellular immune responses, as demonstrated by the proportion of CD3+T cells ( B). As illustrated in D, it was highly likely that up-regulation of the MHC-I molecular antigen presentation pathway contributed to this process. E showed that the proportions of MPEC and SLEC cells in memory T cells induced by the LIPO-SIM-Rh2 adjuvant were relatively low, possibly because memory cells had not fully formed between the immunization start and sacrifice on day 28.
 
3.3.

## LIPO-SIM-Rh2 activated macrophage and promoted the homing of DCs
We next examined macrophage activation and dendritic cell (DC) homing in the local lymph nodes. As shown in A, LIPO-SIM-Rh2 induced activation of M1 (CD80+/CD86+) and M2 (CD206+) macrophages, with CD80+ cells demonstrating the most significant activation. Analysis of DC homing (CD11c+) in local lymph nodes ( B) revealed that LIPO-SIM-Rh2 promoted DC homing, which might represent a critical mechanism underlying the augmented specific immune responses.
 
3.4.

## LIPO-SIM-Rh2 was an adjuvant with a low safety risk
For the safety evaluation of LIPO-SIM-Rh2, major organs (heart, liver, spleen, lung, kidney, and local lymph nodes) of mice were subjected to pathological analysis using the maximum cross-sectional sectioning method. No significant damage or inflammation was observed in these vital organs ( ). The local lymph nodes in theLIPO-SIM-Rh2 immunized group displayed slight nuclear loss and cytoplasmic staining, potentially indicating neutrophil accumulation.

## Discussion
Upon reviewing previous studies [ , ], we propose simvastatin and ginsenoside Rh2 as potential adjuvant candidates, as these compounds exhibit properties such as enhancing vaccine immunogenicity, low cost, and ease of large-scale production. Additionally, adjuvants exert their effects by enhancing the body's immune response to vaccines, a process involving complex multi-dimensional mechanisms. Different adjuvants often differ in their efficacy to potentiate immune responses. In this study, simvastatin acts as an adjuvant through metabolic pathways, whereas ginsenoside Rh2 enhances immunity by promoting splenocyte proliferation [ , ]. Notably, combining adjuvants with distinct action pathways to leverage synergistic advantages represents a valuable research direction, with prior studies [ ] demonstrating the benefits of combinatorial adjuvants. Furthermore, both compounds are nearly water-insoluble, indicating that encapsulation within liposomes could serve as a viable delivery strategy [ , ]. Given their lipophilic nature, unsaturated fatty acid-based DOPC is used as the liposomal membrane component to optimize encapsulation efficiency and local release at the injection site [ , ].
As shown in , the experimental results, including dynamic light scattering (DLS) detection, visual inspection of appearance, and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) imaging, collectively suggested that LIPO-SIM-Rh2 could be considered to some extent a nanoparticle with stable dispersion properties. Although the results based on DLS and appearance remained unchanged over a period of up to 48 weeks, indicating that LIPO-SIM-Rh2 existed stably, the combined TEM detection confirmed that there were round particles of different sizes in LIPO-SIM-Rh2. In this case, the dynamic light scattering (DLS) method might produce scattering signals dominated by larger particles, masking the presence of smaller particle populations. Taking transmission electron microscopy images at each time point would enable a more accurate evaluation of particle size distribution, which was a factor that must be considered and studied in the next step of the research. The average encapsulation efficiency was determined by ultrafiltration centrifugation. After conducting ultrafiltration, simvastatin and Rh2 were not detected in the ultrafiltrate, and no solid substances precipitated either prior to or following the ultrafiltration process. Based on these observations, it could be reasonably hypothesized that the encapsulation efficiency of LIPO-SIM-Rh2 might approach 100 %. These results demonstrated that LIPO-SIM-Rh2 fulfilled the stability criteria for its application as an adjuvant.
In order to verify the potential of LIPO-SIM-Rh2 to enhance immune responses and its mechanism of action, we conducted animal immunization using the model antigen OVA. There were several significant advantages of using the model antigen OVA as an antigen to assess the potential and mechanism of the adjuvant. Firstly, OVA, as an antigen with relatively weak immunogenicity, provided an opportunity for us to explore whether the adjuvant had a potent immune enhancement effect. Secondly, by detecting the details of peptides presented by MHC molecules (MHC-I-SIINFEKL+) on the cell surface, we were able to study the cross-presentation reaction within the OVA-based immune response.
During the evaluation of in vivo animal experiments, we found that LIPO-SIM-Rh2, as an extremely potent vaccine adjuvant, could elicit robust humoral and cellular immune responses, especially the cellular immune responses mediated by CD3+ T cells (p < 0.001) and CD8+ T cells (p < 0.05), by enhancing the MHC-I molecular delivery pathway. On the other hand, macrophages in local lymph nodes were activated, and the homing effect of DC cells was enhanced. In summary, the LIPO-SIM-Rh2 adjuvant might induce cellular immune responses dominated by CD8+ T cells by enhancing the MHC-I molecular delivery pathway, activating macrophages and enhancing the homing effect of DC cells. Notably, flow cytometry analysis showed that the proportions of memory-related T cells, namely MPECs and SLECs, in spleen cells in the LIPO-SIM-Rh2 group were lower than those in the control group. This might be due to the short time from immunization to sacrifice, as the relevant T cells had not differentiated into memory T cells.
No obvious damage or inflammation was detected in these vital organs by examining HE-stained pathological sections. On the other hand, simvastatin was a clinically established cholesterol-lowering drug with a long history of use, renowned for its excellent safety profile. When expanding its application to the field of vaccine adjuvants, the impact on cholesterol homeostasis in healthy individuals or those with lipid metabolism disorders was likely negligible, given the extremely low dosage (50 μg/dose) and limited administration frequency (2 doses).
The objective of this study was to probe into the potential and mechanism of action of the LIPO-SIM-Rh2 adjuvant preliminarily. In this work, only the model antigen OVA was used for evaluation. In subsequent studies, experiments related to viruses or bacteria should be carried out after immunization with the viral or bacterial vaccines. On the other hand, the research methods of this paper could have been further improved. The research logic was to screen LIPO-SIM-Rh2 for its significant enhancement of antibody responses through antibody level assays, and then study the characteristics of immune responses induced by it at the cellular level. However, if the characteristics of immune responses induced by LIPO-SIM and LIPO-Rh2 at the cellular level had been added as controls, the conclusions of this paper would have been more convincing, and they should have been included in the design of subsequent mechanistic studies.
In conclusions, LIPO-SIM-Rh2 adjuvant, which was fabricated using a hybrid approach combining ethanol injection and high-pressure homogenization, showed a stable distribution and consistently maintained a high encapsulation efficiency during long-term storage for up to 48 weeks. It was capable of enhancing cellular immune responses by enhancing the MHC-I molecular delivery pathway, activating macrophages and augmenting the DC homing effect.
