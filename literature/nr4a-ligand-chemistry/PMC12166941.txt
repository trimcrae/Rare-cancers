## Highlights
•Single-cell and single-nuclei atlas uncovered WAT landscape in high-BMI patients.
•Novel subsets of immune cells and mature adipocytes predicted poor prognosis of BC.
•PVMs, APOD+γδ T cells, and FKBP5+ adipocytes dominanted in BC-related WAT.
•PVMs elevated c-Maf to mediate cell chats, while APOD+γδ T cells secreted EGFs.
•Crosstalk between innate immunity and ASCs contributed to BC progression.
Keywords: Breast cancer, High BMI, Adipose microenvironment, scRNA-seq, snRNA-seq

## Abstract

## Objectives
High body mass index (BMI), encompassing overweight and obesity, is a well-established risk factor for developing breast cancer (BC). The underlying mechanisms linking elevated BMI to increased BC risk involve metabolic reprogramming and chronic inflammatory microenvironments regulated by cellular networks within breast white adipose tissue (WAT). However, the complicated landscape and specific cell chat leading to BC-related adipose microenvironment remained unclear.

## Materials and methods
We unveiled a comprehensive cell atlas by employing single-cell (N = 27) and single-nuclei (N = 6) transcriptomics to address dynamic changes of immune and stromal cell components within WAT in high BMI population. Bulk RNA-seq data sets were used for validation.

## Results
Characteristics of adipose-infiltrating tissue-resident macrophages (PVMs), APOD+γδ T cells, and mature FKBP5+ adipocytes in breast cancer women with high BMI were revealed, in terms of transcriptional genes, metabolism features, developmental trajectories and gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA). PVMs upregulated c-Maf combined with its co-activator CREB1 to increase TCA cycles. APOD+γδ T cells were found to elevate intracellular lipid metabolism, leading to poor clinical prognosis. Mature FKBP5+adipocytes served as an advanced adipogenesis mediator to promote tumor aggressiveness. In-depth analysis of cell-cell interactions uncovered a remodeling trend towards metabolic dysfunction and chronic inflammation in WAT with weight gain via EGF, CXCL, and CCL signalings.

## Conclusion
These results provided a novel understanding of detailed and unbiased cellular landscape of WAT in breast cancer with high BMI from single-cell atlas perspective, uncovering interplays between breast adipose-infiltrating immune cells and stromal cells that promote progression of BC under high BMI conditions.

## Introduction
Overweight and obesity have become one of the major health issues in developed countries and regions [ ]. For adults, if BMI, defined by the index of weight in kilograms divided by the square of one’s height in meters, is greater than or equal to 25 can be considered as overweight, or 30 as obesity [ ]. According to recent report via World Health Organization (WHO), the prevalence of high BMI (BMI≥25 kg/m2) among children and adolescents tripled since 1975 [ ]. Whereas the impact of high BMI on diabetes and heart disease is well-known [ , ], a growing evidence from clinical and preclinical studies indicates that high BMI represented an independence risk factor of cancer [ , ]. BC, one of the most common cancer and the second leading cause of cancer death in developed countries, is more likely to occur in high BMI condition [ , ]. The association between high BMI and the odds of BC development occurs among most races, regardless of menstrual status [ ].
Despite notable progress in breast cancer treatment [ , ], therapeutic resistance and disease recurrence remain major concerns. Investigating the etiological mechanisms of breast cancer could be key to developing more effective treatment strategies. Regarding to the internal mechanisms and causes, there are various perspectives, the mainly two of which are metabolic reprograms and chronic inflammatory microenvironments [ , , ]. High BMI usually accompanies with dysregulated metabolism pathways both systemically and locally within the adipose tissue [ ]. Overweight and obesity involve sustained abundance of energy and nutrition which satisficed the demand of uncontrolled tumor proliferation [ ]. On the other hand, chronic inflammation, a classic and prevalent stimuli within microenvironment to promote tumor progression, is recently recognized to be caused by high BMI [ , , ]. Regardless of both hypothesis, the common link is inseparable from adipose tissue (AT) [ ].
AT can be divided into WAT, beige AT, and brown adipose tissue (BAT) [ ]. A higher amount of WAT is usually observed in individuals with high BMI [ ]. As well-known, breasts are mainly composed of glands and WAT [ ]. It comprises heterogeneous cell population, mostly mature white adipocytes, but also contains other stromal vascular fraction cells (SVFs), including immune cells, adipocyte progenitor cells (pre-adipocytes), adipose-derived stem cells (ADSCs), and endothelial cells [ , , ]. Specifically, type 1 immune cells and innate cells may contribute to expansion of pro-inflammatory macrophages in AT [ , ]. Recent studies have provided broad and deep understanding of human SVFs to demonstrate cellular heterogeneity and tissue homeostasis using single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) or single-nuclei RNA sequencing (snRNA-seq) [ , , ]. However, due to the unique composition of AT, it is hard to uncover the interaction of mature adipocytes and immune cells. What’s more, there is a pressing need to understand the complicated cellular landscape and specific cell chat from the perspective of both metabolic pathways and chronic inflammatory manners.
To address these outstanding questions, we mapped distinct cell types of immune cells and other adipocyte-associated populations using single-cell atlas. Overall, we approximately conducted scRNA-seq and snRNA-seq, to reveal uncharacterized cell population. They constituted unique subsets and functional involvement of adipose-resident macrophages, γδ T cells, and mature adipocytes. Furthermore, analysis of ligand-receptor and signaling networks unleashed distinct high BMI associated metabolic reprograms and chronic inflammation within WAT to promote tumorigenesis and progression. In conclusion, our high-dimension transcriptomic atlas and systemically investigation provides a novel insight into possible plasticity and heterogeneity of cellular composition in WAT.

## Result

## Immune cells and adipose-associated stromal cells led to metabolic dysfunction and chronic inflammation in WAT microenvironment of high BMI BC patients
To comprehensively explore the unbiased cellular composition of WAT in BC patients with normal BMI (17kg/m2<BMI<25 kg/m2, Group N) and high BMI (Group O), which might potentially lead to the different incidence and prognosis of BC, we constructed a single-cell combined with single-nuclei transcriptional atlas of human WAT samples from published datasets [ , ] ( A and Supplementary Table S1).
 Employing t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t-SNE), we segregated all cells derived from scRNA-seq data into 30 clusters of 8 main cell types based on featured genes, including immune cells, epithelial cells, endothelial cells, smooth muscle cells, fibroblasts, ADSCs, pre-adipocytes, and mature adipocytes ( B-C). Validation through snRNA-seq data confirmed the presence of these cell populations ( D-E). Notably, unlike scRNA-seq, a high abundance of mature adipocytes and reduced presence of fibroblasts were observed in snRNA-seq.
Interestingly, in Group O, both metabolism and the chronic inflammatory scores exhibited elevation compared to Group N, aligning with previous studies [ , ] ( F-G). It substantiated the heightened metabolic reprogramming and the chronic inflammation in BC-bearing women with high BMI. Through gene set scoring analysis at single-cell and single-nuclei levels, we identified prevalent metabolic alterations roughly across most cell types, while inflammatory responses were predominantly observed in immune cells (Supplementary Figure S1A-D). To elucidate the specific cellular constituents driving the remodeling of AT microenvironment, we observed that innate immune cells significantly activated in Group O ( H). Surprisingly, collagen construction, associated with extracellular matrix formation, was increased in Group O, correlating with poor clinical prognosis [ ] ( I). Furthermore, collagen fiber responses were primarily activated in ADSCs, pre-adipocytes, and mature adipocytes, collectively categorized into adipocyte-associated stromal cells (ASCs) (Supplementary Figure S1E-F). Therefore, we postulated that innate immune cells and ASCs played pivotal roles in remodeling BC-related WAT microenvironment under high BMI conditions.

## Single-cell sequencing revealed unique innate immunity involving macrophages and γδ T cells in high BMI populations
Initially, scRNA-seq data was employed to clarify diversity of the immune system in WAT. 25,578 immune cells were clustered based on differential expression genes and visualized. The clustering analysis revealed distinct clusters which were annotated into 12 subsets based on featured genes, including B cells, CD4+
T cells, CD8+
T cells, conventional dendritic cells (cDCs), γδ T cells, macrophages, mast cells, myeloid dendritic cells (mDCs), monocyte-like myeloid-derived suppressor cells (M-MDSCs), monocytes, natural killer (NK) cells, and other undefined cells ( A and Supplementary Figure S2A).
 Identified clusters encompassed cells from both Group N and Group O samples, indicating that each identified cell type was associated with a common cell lineage rather than derived from a single patient sample ( B-C and Supplementary Figure S2B). Remarkably, proportions of γδ T cells, macrophages, M-MDSCs, cDCs, and mDCs were significantly increased in Group O. In contrast, mast cells, CD4+T cells, and CD8+
T cells remarkedly decreased in Group O. Other clusters remained unchanged ( D). Subsequently, we analyzed two sets of bulk RNA-seq public data (WAT: GSE205668, BC: PMID36812344). We evaluated proportions of each immune component and found that those increased immune populations were significantly enriched in both WAT and BC of Group O ( E and Supplementary Figure S2C). Furthermore, Pearson’s correlation analysis revealed a prominent positive correlation in the WAT and intra-tumoral abundance of macrophages, γδ T cells, and other immune cells with BMI ( F-G and Supplementary Figure S2D-F).
We then assessed gene set scores of metabolic reprogram and inflammation pathways in immune cells to clarify particular subsets ( H-K). Interestingly, macrophages and γδ T cells displayed activation response to hypoxia, a classification of pathological status in BC, and were involved in inflammatory response. Additionally, macrophages transcriptionally harbored PPAR signaling pathway and hormone metabolic process. While these results suggested that diverse immune components, especially macrophages and γδ T cells, underwent changes with weight gain in women, it remained crucial to identify heterogeneous subclusters that might directly contribute to metabolic reprograms and chronic inflammation.

## Diverse macrophages subsets contributed to human WAT microenvironment construction in high BMI patients
Given the predominant presence of macrophages within myeloid cells in WAT and their remarkable accumulation [ , ], we delved deeper into understanding which subcluster might play a crucial role in remodeling BC-related WAT microenvironment of high BMI women. Previous studies suggested that macrophages consisted of heterogenetic subclusters classified into distinct groups by their cytokine and chemokines secretion, lipid metabolism, and transcriptional factor (TF) expressions [ , ]. In this study, we reclustered macrophages into 10 subclusters which could be functionally classified into 6 subtypes to elucidate the different compositions between Group N and Group O ( A and Supplementary Figure S3A-B): an immature subcluster expressing FCGR3B and TREM1 (subcluster 5), monocyte-like macrophages (MLMs) identified by FCN1 and FCGR3A (subcluster 2 and 4), type 1 macrophages (M1) based on expressions of IL1B, GCLM, C3, and SAA2 (subcluster 6, 7 and 8), chemokine-secreted macrophages via CXCL5 and CCL2 (subcluster 0), traditional lipid-associated macrophages (LAMs) characterized by APOC1 and TREM2 (subcluster 3), and notably, tissue-resident macrophages(subcluster 1) based on LYVE1 and FOLR2, named perivascular macrophages (PVMs), as recently reported [ ] ( B and Supplementary Figure S3C).
 Proportions of subclusters in each group were then calculated. Remarkably, MLMs, LAMs, and PVMs showed a substantial accumulation in the Group O, while M1 and chemokine-secreted macrophages decreased ( C). Consistent with our findings, the analysis of bulk RNA-seq data also demonstrated higher abundances of PVMs, MLMs, and LAMs in Group O ( D and Supplementary Figure S3D). These observations suggested that high BMI-related factors had compounded effects on shaping varied subclusters of macrophages.
To further explore the potential trajectories of macrophages and their developmental branches, monocle pseudotime analysis decoded that both subcluster 0 and subcluster 5 shared a naïve phenotype ( E and Supplementary Figure S3E-F). Part of subcluster 0 cells developed a trajectory with branch 1 that led to an isolated cell state. The rest of subcluster 0 and subcluster 5 navigated into 3 alternative developmental fates, which were composed of different functional clusters. M1 traveled to branch 4 mostly, while MLMs ran parallel to both branch 2 and 3. LAMs and PVMs were equally contributed to 3 differential states (state1, 7, and 8).
To understand the metabolic landscape of macrophages in WAT, we computed the scores of all 76 active metabolic pathways in 10 subclusters ( F). Surprisingly, LAMs exhibited the highest metabolism diversities and activities among all. However, PVMs presented lower activities in metabolism pathways. Additionally, glucose metabolism, including ketone bodies synthesis and degradation, O-glycan biosynthesis, glutamine metabolism, and arginine metabolism were mostly enriched in M1. We further computed scores of other gene sets related to macrophage properties and found that PVMs had significantly higher scores for cell-cycle pathway, proliferation, and cytokine production, indicating it retained self-renewal ability to expand in situ and potential cytokine production ability (Supplementary Figure S3G). Given that PVMs had limited influence on metabolism reprogramming in WAT, we decided to further investigate its biological features and association with clinical outcomes.

## LYVE1+PVMs represented a subtype of tissue-resident macrophages associated with poor clinical outcomes in BC
A previous study demonstrated that presence of PVMs (LYVE1+FOLR2+MHCII−macrophages) existed since embryo development and exhibited residency in organs [ ]. However, further features of functions, transcriptional features, intercellular metabolism remained unknown. PVMs upregulated genes including LYVE1, FOLR2, MRC1, and KLF4, distinguishing them from other subclusters ( A). GSEA revealed that PVMs displayed the tissue-resident nature, growth factors secretion and early response to estrogen, while downregulating signatures associated with cellular transformation, fatty acid binding and regulation of cell killing ( B).
 Macrophages were widely reported to polarize towards M2 via PPARγ, especially in AT [ , ]. However, according to our GSEA results, PPARγ signaling pathway was hardly activated in PVMs ( B). This prompted us to investigate whether other TFs played pivotal roles in promoting the polarization of PVMs. Combining these findings with previous research [ ], we surprisingly found that c-Maf (coded by MAF) and its co-activator CREB1 were highly expressed in PVMs rather than other traditional TFs associated with macrophage polarization ( C). Min Liu, et al. reported that c-Maf programmed M2 macrophages as a checkpoint in non-small cell lung cancer patients by increasing TCA cycles via upregulating isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH) [ ]. We then computed scores of metabolism-related gene sets in PVMs and other subclusters. Notably, IDH genes and Acetyl-CoA metabolic process increased compared to others, except for other lipid-associated metabolism pathways ( D and Supplementary Figure S4A-B). Furthermore, we found that LYVE1 was highly co-expressed with FOLR2, a member of folate receptor known to bind hyaluronan (HA). This complex was reported to interact with CD44 to promote cell growth and invasion [ ]. Here, we distinguished M2 macrophages into 2 specific subtypes, LAMs as well as PVMs, and figured out that only the latter subtype were programmed by c-MAF in WAT.
Survival analysis using TCGA cohort confirmed that PVMs were associated with an unfavorable prognosis in BC patients ( E-H). Collectively, these data suggested a tissue-residency nature, unique ligand-receptor crosstalk, and crucial intracellular metabolism of PVMs in high BMI-induced BC, contributing to poor prognosis.

## Enriched APOD+γδ T cells in high BMI patients exhibited elevated intracellular lipid metabolism
Though diversities of γδ T cells in thymus and peripheral situs were explored previously [ ], the heterogeneity of subclusters and biological function of γδ T cells in WAT remained unknown. To uncover the transcriptional maps of adipose-infiltrating γδ T cells, 1248 total γδ T cells were classified into 8 clusters ( A), the abundance of which varied between Group N and Group O ( B and Figure S5A-B).
 Some previously described subsets [ ] were identified in our analysis as expected ( C). For instance, subcluster 3 was characterized as γδT1 cells (IFNG, TNF), subcluster 7 represented γδT17 cells (MAF, KIR3DX1), and subcluster 6 stood for naïve cells (TCF7, IL32, and CD2). Subcluster 2, a highly cytotoxic subtype named NKG2A+γδ T cells (KLRC1, SELL, and YY1), decreased in Group O. Meanwhile, subcluster 0, designated as APOD+γδ T cells (APOD), a novel-defined subtype in our study, accumulated in WAT of high BMI BC patients. Apolipoprotein D, a component of transporter of several small hydrophobic molecules associated with cholesterol metabolism encoded by APOD, was linked to this subcluster. Subsequently, we confirmed the proportion of APOD+γδ T cells using bulk RNA-seq public data, indicating that APOD+γδ T cells increased in both WAT and BC of Group O ( D and Supplementary Figure S5C).
To further evaluate transcriptional properties of γδ T cells, we characterized the expressions of several groups of genes ( E). Notably, γδT1 cells displayed not only cytotoxicity (GZMA, GNLY, IFNG, and TBX21), but also genes associated with residency (NR4A1, NR4A3, CD69, ITGAE, and RUNX3). In addition, γδT17 showed upregulation of TFs related to pro-inflammatory cytokines production, including MAF, ZEB2, and IKZF2. Interestingly, NKG2A+γδ T cells exhibited abundant co-stimulatory gene expressions (TNFRSF9, ICOS, TNFRSF14, and JAML). To our surprise, APOD+γδ T cells rarely upregulated cytotoxicity nor exhaustion features, but showed elevated expressions of lipid metabolism-associated genes (ADIPOQ, AQP7, FABP3, and SLC27A2). Previous research revealed that genes associated with lipid storage and response to lipoproteins promote tumor progression and metastasis [ ]. Notably, APOD+γδ T cells displayed marked upregulation of genes linked to advanced stages of BC compared to other subclusters ( F). Moreover, BC patients with higher infiltration of APOD+γδ T cells exhibited lower overall survival rates ( G).
Across all subclusters of adipose-infiltrating γδ T cells, we performed unsupervised cell trajectory analysis using Monocle2 to delineate the evolutionary dynamics ( H). Two major differentiation paths of γδ T cells originating from stem-like cells (subcluster 6). Moreover, 6 distinct cell states were defined (Supplementary Figure S5D-E). Interestingly, γδT1 and γδT17 cells mainly navigated through branch 1 spanned state 5 to state 7. Meanwhile, novel APOD+γδ T cells routed from state 4 and branched towards state 1, indicating their unique developmental trend of gene expression compared to traditional functional γδ T cells. To further recapitulate the transcriptomic characteristics of two trajectories, we investigated the gene expressions across cell states (Supplementary Figure S5F). The origin state (state 4) was enriched of ribosome synthesis gene sets, demonstrating higher protein translation. The branch 1 increased gene expressions of residency along with proliferation (CNOT6L, HSP90AA1, RUNX3, PLCG2, and CD81). On the other hand, the branch 2 resembled genes of cell migration (IL2RG, CTSW, CFL1, KLRF1, IGFBP7, LSP1, CMC1, and MYOM2). In conclusion, these data provided a novel landscape of adipose-infiltrating γδ T cells and outlined the development trajectories of subclusters from transcriptional perspective for the first time.

## Single-nuclei sequencing uncovered FKBP5+adipocytes as an advanced adipogenesis mediator to promote tumor aggressiveness
Because snRNA-seq was proven effective in capturing transcriptional information of mature adipocytes, we further conducted an analysis of the nuclei transcriptome in ASCs ( A). In comparison to Group N, high BMI patients exhibited an increased proportion of pre-adipocytes, while the accumulation of mature adipocytes showed no difference ( B). It was well-known that ADSCs typically progressed to pre-adipocytes and then matured towards mature adipocytes [ ]. Our monocle pseudotime analysis confirmed previous findings, which proved the reliability of classification from another perspective ( C).
 While prior studies highlighted the distinction between white adipocytes and brown adipocytes [ ], little was known about the heterogeneity within mature white adipocytes. Therefore, further reclustering of mature adipocytes was performed to unveil their diversities ( D-E). Among all subtypes, mature adipocytes in WAT of high BMI patients were predominantly composed of subcluster 1, characterized by expressions of FKBP5 and YJU2, termed mature FKBP5+adipocytes ( F and Supplementary Figure S6A-B). We subsequently validated their increased abundance in WAT and BC of high BMI patients using bulk RNA-seq datasets separately ( G and Supplementary Figure S6C)
Given the evolving recognition of high BMI as a risk factor for both BC incidence and unfavorable prognosis, our investigation delved into the potential role of mature FKBP5+adipocytes in tumor progression. GSEA showed that mature FKBP5+adipocytes served as an advanced adipogenesis mediator ( H), which explained the late differentiation state to some extent (Supplementary Figure S6D). What’s more, it directly upregulated tumor aggressiveness ( I) and let to poor patient outcome ( J). In summary, our analysis revealed the diverse and significant functions of mature adipocytes in the context of weight gain.

## Outgoing communication patterns sent by innate immune cells and ASCs resulted in BC progression
Next, to decipher the crosstalk between ASCs and immune cells within the WAT microenvironment, we utilized an overall information flow of each signaling pathway ( A). In Group O, cell recruitment pathways were significantly upregulated, such as CXCL and CCL. Meanwhile, tissue-residency signaling including ITGB2 and CD99 was enriched. Besides, metabolic imbalance in cholesterol metabolism pathways (SAA) occurred in high BMI populations. Notably, interactions related to myeloid cells (CSF) as well as inflammatory interleukin (IL1) strongly boosted, suggesting the potential connection between innate immune cells and ASCs in WAT.
 Further exploring revealed 3 cell patterns of major secreting cells ( B). Pattern 1 mainly consisted of LAMs, PVMs, M-MDSC, γδT17, and APOD+γδ T cells, which were immunosuppression subsets. Pattern 2 simply contained ASCs which remodeled extracellular collagen and matrix. As for Pattern 3, mDCs, M1, cDCs, and MLMs served as mediators. As for the detailed communication patterns, Pattern 1 was enriched with negatively regulated factors, while Pattern 2 was responsible for collagen formation and adipogenesis ( C), confirming our previous observation (Supplementary Figure S1E-F). Additionally, upregulated ligand and antigen presentation signals dominated in Pattern 3.
We then analyzed cell-cell communications for specific mechanisms. We complied innate immune cells accumulated in Group O to detect crosstalk between them and with ASCs ( D and Supplementary Figure S7A). As expected, lipid-associated cells like LAMs preserved strongly interaction with ASCs. What's more, it seemed that ASCs also established highly connections with APOD+γδ T cells. Further signaling pathway filter uncovered that strong EGF signals were sent from APOD+γδ T cells other than γδT17 cells. In the meantime, ASCs were able to capture EGF signals via EGFR to enhance cell activity and function ( E and Supplementary Figure S7B). In contrast, PVMs served as a bridge between classical immunosuppressive cells, including MLMs and M-MDSCs ( F). These interaction network even strengthened in Group O (Supplementary Figure S7C). Specifically, PVMs mediated and influenced both CXCL and CCL signaling pathways to attract more MLMs, which were even boosted in the circumstance of high BMI ( G-H and Supplementary Figure S7D-E), shaping immunosuppressive microenvironments to promote breast WAT-mediated tumor progression. Combined, these data demonstrated a complex mixture of crosstalk among PVMs, LAMs, APOD+γδ T cells, and ASCs that regulated metabolic pathway and inflammatory signaling within human WAT with weight gain in women.

## Discussion
Obesity is a global health issue and has been positively associated with an increased risk of breast cancer. AT plays a pivotal role in chronic inflammation and metabolic disorder. In the current study, to characterize diverse the cell atlas in WAT of women with normal BMI and high BMI, we conducted single-cell transcriptional data analysis involving 25,578 immune cells from scRNA-seq and 17,429 ASCs from snRNA-seq of human breast WAT. Our findings revealed significant alterations in the major cellular components of BC-related WAT microenvironments in high BMI patients, including some undefined subclusters. Through the combined expressions of feature genes, metabolic pathways, GSEA and cell-cell interactions, we unveiled the complex effects contributing to tumorigenesis, progression and unfavorable prognosis of BC in WAT.
Our analysis initially unleashed the unique adipose-infiltrating immune cell profile. On the one hand, previous research emphasized the role of regulatory T cells (Tregs), within the realm of adaptive immunity, in shaping systemic metabolism [ , ]. On the other hand, more studies highlighted the significance of innate immune cells in governing stromal construction, nutrition, lipid storage and thermogenesis [ , ]. For instance, AT consisted of enriched innate lymphoid cells (ILCs), which could be distinguished into diverse subtypes. ILC1 expressed IFN-γ [ ], while ILC3 produced IL-17 [ ], both promoting chronic inflammation and implicating other cells in WAT [ , ]. Furthermore, invariant natural killer T (iNKT) cells were reported to utilize non-redundant pathways to preserve metabolic integrity via IL-10 or IFN-γ [ ]. Notably, we revealed an accumulation of macrophages and γδ T cells in human breast WAT of women with high BMI. Given the close association of these cell types with metabolic reprogramming and chronic inflammation in WAT, we decided to delve deeper into the features and functions of subclusters.
It was widely recognized that myeloid cells played a pivotal role in AT among populations with high BMI [ ]. Within this context, adipose-associated macrophages (AAMs), were acknowledged as the most relevant myeloid cells in AT [ ]. Based on our transcriptional analysis of cellular functions, AAMs can be roughly categorized into several subclusters, including M1, type 2 macrophages (M2), MLMs, and other subtypes [ , , ]. M1 macrophages predominantly relied on glycolysis to produce pro-inflammatory cytokines, whereas M2 macrophages utilize oxidative metabolism to support cancer development through biological molecules, such as IL-4, Arg-1 [ ]. Further trajectory investigation into M2 macrophages revealed that they consisted of two major subclusters, LAMs and PVMs. LAMs, expressing APOC1 and TREM2, were reported to be correlated with unfavorable prognosis in multiple diseases by enhancing lipid-associated metabolic pathways and promoting crosstalk with ASCs and cancer cells [ , , ].
Notably, LYVE1 and FOLR2 distinguished PVMs among macrophage subclusters. Originating from embryonic development, PVMs were known to reside in organs [ ] and maintain cardiovascular physiology through hyaluronan-mediated manners [ , ]. As for BC, PVMs were considered to form a collaborative CCR5-dependent perivascular niche that influences chemotherapy responses, although the underlying mechanism remained unknown [ ]. Here, we uncovered that PVMs were capable of secreting more growth factors independently, such asEGFs, thereby enhancing tumor growth. What’s more, cell communication networks revealed that PVMs significantly upregulated CCL and CXCL pathways instead of interactions with ASCs, attracting M-MDSC to shape immunosuppressive WAT microenvironments. This finding provided an explanation for the formation of CCR5-dependent perivascular niche to some extent.
Previous studies explored the landscape and cellular features of circulating γδ T cells [ , ], whereas we first elucidated adipose-infiltrating γδ T cells. We analyzed a total of 1248 γδ T cells then categorized them into eight subclusters. Traditional pro-inflammatory γδT17 cells were known to maintain adipose homeostasis and thermogenesis by producing IL-17 [ , ]. Our finding revealed that APOD+γδ T cells were accumulated in human WAT of high BMI populations for the first time. Further analysis indicated that APOD+γδ T cells displayed highly expression of lipid-associated genes, suggesting a unique developmental trajectory. APOD, encoding a component of transporter of several small hydrophobic molecules associated with cholesterol metabolism, activated intracellular to enhance lipid transportation and response, to promote BC progression. Interestingly, APOD+γδ T cells were classified into the same cell pattern as γδT17 cells. However, only APOD+γδ T cells directed EGF signaling pathways towards ASCs rather than γδT17 cells, consequently boosting adipogenesis and lipid storage, which separated APOD+γδ T cells apart from other γδ T cells.
Mature adipocytes and their stromal cells constituted the major composition of human breasts, with adipocyte-derived lipids, cytokines, and exosomes mediating cancer progression [ , , ]. As weight increased, the content and biogenesis of adipocyte-derived exosomes underwent changes [ ]. Recognizing that scRNA-seq weakly capturing transcriptional expressions of mature adipocytes, we conducted snRNA-seq datasets to illustrate WAT remodeling in high BMI populations through heterogeneity ASCs. ASCs were characterized into ADSCs, pre-adipocytes, and mature adipocytes. The proportion of mature adipocytes showed no difference, but pre-adipocytes increased with weight gaining, indicating a significant mobilization of the developmental state of ADSCs under high BMI conditions. Uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP) plots of mature adipocytes showed that mature FKBP5+adipocytes were enriched in Group O. GSEA suggested that mature FKBP5+adipocytes mediated poor prognosis of BC by elevating adipogenesis.
Systemic breast cancer treatment has been well developed in recent years, particularly in early-stage screening and intervention strategies [ , ]. While conventional therapies, such as surgery, radiotherapy, and chemotherapy, remain integral, innovative approaches including engineered cell therapies and other strategies are gaining momentum. Notably, engineered adipocytes implantation has emerged as a potential modality for local immunomodulation and metabolic intervention in the tumor microenvironment [ ]. Given the established association between high BMI and increased breast cancer risk, high BMI-related biological mechanisms are critical and underexploited for translational exploration. In this study, we addressed the technical barriers in AT research by integrating two complementary single cell transcriptomic platforms, scRNA-seq and snRNA-seq, allowing the concurrent analysis of immune and adipocyte populations at both live-cell and nuclear levels. This strategy enabled us to uncover cellular interactions with high resolution and physiological relevance. Through that, we identified novel proinflammatory immune subsets, including APOD⁺γδ T cells and PVMs, which exhibit functional crosstalk with ASCs. These findings suggest that high BMI-induced changes in innate immune components may play a previously underrecognized role in shaping the tumor-promoting environment. From a immunotherapy standpoint, these immune cell types represent promising targets for localized therapeutic intervention aimed at delaying tumor progression and potentially enhancing the response to conventional therapies.
While our integrative approach provides valuable insight into the immunometabolic interface in breast cancer, further mechanistic and preclinical validation is still needed. For instance, determining whether PVMs directly mediate tumorigenesis through ligand-receptor interactions could inform combination strategies with immunotherapy. Taken together, our findings provide adipose-targeted interventions with potential applicability in high BMI populations.
In summary, our research uncovered a comprehensive single-cell atlas of immune cells and stromal cellular populations in human WAT across normal and high BMI conditions. We shed light on previously un recognized cell subtype and cell communication alterations in shaping high BMI-associated WAT microenvironments. This work would lay a foundation for identifying potential therapeutic targets aimed at mitigating systemic metabolic dysfunction and chronic inflammation following weight gain.

## Materials and methods

## Single-cell and single-nuclear RNA-seq data obtaining and quality controlling
Public single-cell RNA-seq (scRNA-seq) and single-nuclear RNA-seq (snRNA-seq) data of WAT from women suffered from cancer resection was obtained from GSE195665. Public snRNA-seq data of WAT from HD was obtained from GSE176171. Women with a BMI below 25 were classified as Group N, while females with a BMI above 25 were classified as Group O. Several criteria were applied to filter the cells: those with fewer than 800 unique molecular identifiers (UMIs), expressing <300 genes, or having a mitochondrial count ratio greater than 0.2 were excluded. Additionally, cells with a complexity metric called log10GenesPerUMI (logarithm base 10 of the number of genes per UMI) lower than 0.8 were removed. Genes expressed by fewer than 10 cells were also filtered out. To detect and eliminate doublets, we utilized DoubletFinder R package (v2.0).

## Across-sample integration
Before integrating the samples, we applied various functions with default settings to account for technical and biological variations. These functions included NormalizeData, CellCycleScoring, and SCTransform, which were used to normalize the data and remove irrelevant sources of variation, such as those originating from mitochondrial expression. Subsequently, we utilized the RunHarmony function from the Harmony R package (v1.0) with the group.by.vars parameter set to "sample" and the project.dim parameter set to FALSE. All other parameters were kept at their default values.

## General clustering
To identify clusters and reduce dimensionality, we generated a t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) plot using 40 principal components (PCs). Subsequently, all cells were clustered using the FindNeighbors and FindClusters functions in Seurat. For scRNA-seq, initial clusters were further categorized into immune cells, epithelial cells, endothelial cells, smooth muscle cells, fibroblasts, adipocyte-derived stromal cells, pre-adipocytes, or mature adipocytes based on the expression of specific marker genes. For snRNA-seq, initial clusters were further categorized into immune cells, epithelial cells, endothelial cells, smooth muscle cells, adipocyte-derived stromal cells, pre-adipocytes, or adipocytes based on the expression of specific marker genes. Prior to expression value presentation and differential analysis, we preprocessed the raw expression matrix using the NormalizeData function in Seurat.

## General immune cell analysis
For precise identification, we performed reclustering of immune cells using 40 principal components (PCs). To determine marker genes for each cell type, we utilized the FindAllMarkers function with an average log2-fold-change (logFC) threshold of 0.25.
Additionally, we conducted multiple iterations of reclustering within macrophages and gd T cells using 40 PCs and resolution parameters ranging from 0.4 to 0.6. This allowed us to identify distinct subsets of cells with differential expression (DE) profiles. For each subset, we identified marker genes using the FindAllMarkers function with a logFC threshold of 0.25.

## Bulk RNA-seq data processing
In this study, public bulk RNA-seq datasets GSE205668 and PMID36812344 was further analyzed in the form of z-score. To evaluate the abundance of specific immune cell subsets in each sample, we calculated the average expression level of signature genes of corresponding immune cell subsets. The signature genes for each subset can be found in Supplementary Table S2. We then assessed the correlation between the abundance of these target immune cell subsets and the BMI of each patient using the Pearson correlation coefficient (PCC).

## Gene set analysis
The R package irGSEA (v2.1.5) was used to perform gene set scoring analysis on the gene sets retrieved from the MSigDB repository and literature [ ] (Supplementary Table S3). Differential signature scores between groups were assessed using a two-sided Wilcoxon rank-sum test. The R package scMetabolism (v0.2.1) was used to compute scores of active metabolic pathways.
LogFC was used as a ranking metric for preranked gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA), which was performed using the GSEABase R package (v1.52.1) based on the MSigDB repository. Gene sets with FDR < 0.05 were considered significant.

## Trajectory inference analysis
To determine pseudotime trajectories computationally, we utilized the Monocle R package (v2.22.0) in macrophages, gd T cells, adipocyte-derived stromal cells, pre-adipocytes, and mature adipocytes. For each analysis, we employed DDRTree-based dimension reduction on the differentially expressed genes (DEGs) within each cluster. Pseudotime values and transcriptional states were then calculated using the orderCells function, with the default parameters. The slingshot algorithm was used for a separate trajectory inference analysis of mature adipocytes.

## Survival analysis based on TCGA data
The TCGA BRCA dataset was used to calculate the prognostic value of LYVE1+PVMs. The gene expression data and clinical information of BRCA patients were downloaded from UCSC Xena (http://xena.ucsc.edu/). The abundance of LYVE1+PVMs was estimated as average expression value of marker genes of LYVE1+PVMs multiplied by fraction of total macrophages per sample as inferred by CIBERSORTx. To determine the significance of differences in OS, DFI, PFI, or DSS between samples stratified by abundance of LYVE1+PVMs, Kaplan-Meier analysis and log-rank tests were perfomed using the survminer v0.4.9 and survival v3.3 R packages.
To calculate the prognostic value of APOD+γδ T cells and mature FKBP5+adipocytes, we utilized online analysis platform Kaplan-Meier Plotter (https://kmplot.com/analysis/index.php?p=service&cancer=breast_rnaseq_gse96058). The abundance of APOD+γδ T cells was estimated as average expression value of TRDC and APOD. That of mature FKBP5+adipocytes were estimated as average expression value of ADIPOQ and FKBP5.

## Cell-to-cell communication analysis
Potential ligand receptor (LR) interactions were identified using the CellChat R package (v1.6.1). Firstly, we generated separate cellchat objects for ADSCs, pre-adipocytes, mature FKBP5+adipocytes, M1, LAMs, MLMs, PVMs, cDCs, mDCs, M-MDSCs, APOD+γδ T cells, and γδT17 cells. The overall information flow of each signaling pathway was compared using rankNet function with the option measure = "weight". The outgoing communication pattern of secreting cells was identified using identifyCommunicationPatterns function with the parameter k set to 3. The network centrality scores of each signaling pathway was calculated with netAnalysis_computeCentrality function and visualized with netAnalysis_signalingRole_network function. Specifically, CellChat used measures in weighted-directed networks, including out-degree, in-degree, flow betweenesss and information centrality, to respectively identify dominant senders, receivers, mediators and influencers for the intercellular communications [ ]. Subsequently, we proceeded with comparison analysis in order to infer differentially enriched ligand receptor interaction in Group O, results were visualized using the netVisual_diffInteraction, netVisual_heatmap, and netVisual_bubble functions.

## Statistical analysis
Marker genes of subclusters and DEGs between groups were identified using the MAST test with Bonferroni FDR correction. Results with an adjusted p < 0.05 and logFC > 0.25 were considered significant. Gene set enrichments were analyzed using preranked GSEA, and results with p < 0.05 and q < 0.05 were considered significant. To compare between the two groups, unpaired two-sided Wilcoxon rank-sum tests were employed. The significance of differences in OS, DFI, PFI, and DSS between samples stratified by abundance was determined by log-rank tests. The significance of correlation analyses based on TCGA data was determined by Pearson correlation test. In the last 3 types of tests, results with p < 0.05 were considered significant.
