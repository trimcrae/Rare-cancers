## Abstract
 The ligand-activated
transcription factors Nur77, Nurr1, and NOR-1
forming the NR4A family of nuclear receptors are considered as potential
targets in various pathologies, including neurodegeneration and cancer.
However, chemical tools for pharmacological NR4A modulation as a prerequisite
for target validation are rare. Recent findings suggest that NR4As
bind fatty acid metabolites and fatty acid mimetic (FAM) drugs, opening
new opportunities for NR4A modulator development. We have explored
the chemical space of FAM NR4A ligands by using fragment screening,
in silico analysis, and systematic structure–activity relationship
evaluation. From a chemically diverse library of 92 fragments, we
identified 11 new FAM NR4A agonist and inverse agonist scaffolds.
Structural optimization of the most active FAM fragment yielded NR4A
agonists with submicromolar potency and binding affinity, demonstrating
remarkable potential of FAM as NR4A-modulating tools and drugs.

## Introduction
The
three NR4A receptors Nur77 (NR4A1), nuclear receptor-related
1 (Nurr1, NR4A2), and NOR-1 (NR4A3) are among the least studied members
of the nuclear receptor family of ligand-activated transcription factors. − Albeit having constitutive, ligand-independent transcriptional activity,
all three orphan receptors were shown to respond to ligands, thus
offering access to pharmacological modulation. − NR4A receptors hold promising
therapeutic potential, e.g., in cancer , and neurodegenerative
diseases, but ligands are rare, especially
for NOR-1. Proposed roles of Nur77 in cancer and Nurr1 in neuroprotection have inspired
the search for direct modulators, which has yielded several validated
ligand scaffolds,, , but only few potent modulators. Binding of fatty acids and prostanoids, as well as synthetic
ligands like oxaprozin (A, ), vidofludimus (B), and the de novo design C( ) derived from B, interestingly suggested that NR4A receptors interact with fatty
acid mimetics (FAMs), which are also known as ligands of several other
nuclear receptors. 
 FAMs have attractive drug-like
chemical features and might offer new
opportunities in ligand development
for NR4A receptors, which are urgently needed to capture their therapeutic
potential. To explore FAMs as putative NR4A ligands systematically,
we have established a custom focused library of 92 chemically diverse
fragment-like FAM and screened it for modulation of NR4A receptors
in vitro. Several FAM fragments were identified as selective or pan-NR4A
agonists or inverse agonists. These comprehensive screening data provided
valuable insights for FAM-based NR4A ligand development and enabled
the extraction of chemical features relevant for activity. 4-Benzyloxybenzoic
acid, which is a frequent FAM scaffold, emerged as the most active
fragment hit and was systematically studied in terms of structure–activity
relationship (SAR) and optimization potential to yield potent NR4A
agonists.

## Results and Discussion

## Custom FAM Fragment Screening Library
To explore the
activity of FAM on NR4A receptors systematically, we assembled a custom
screening set of carboxylic acid containing fragment structures. From
5850 commercially available carboxylic acid fragments (MW 150–300
g/mol), we selected 92 using a diversity picker to enable screening
in 96-well format with four controls ( ). The screening set was chemically diverse
as illustrated by low Tanimoto similarity computed on Morgan fingerprints ( a) and contained
70 unique scaffolds. Distribution of molecular weight, SlogP, topological
polar surface area (TPSA), and sp3-hybridized carbon (Csp3) confirmed
favorable fragment-like features ( b).
 

## Fragment Screening
In the initial screening, the FAM
fragment set was tested in Gal4-hybrid reporter gene assays for modulation
of Nur77, Nurr1, and NOR-1 at 100 μM in three independent repeats.
These cellular (transiently transfected HEK293T cells) test systems employ chimeric receptors composed of the ligand-binding
domain (LBD) of the respective human nuclear receptor and the DNA-binding
domain of the yeast protein Gal4 to control the reporter (firefly
luciferase) activity. Constitutively expressed renilla luciferase
is used to normalize for transfection efficiency and monitor test
compound toxicity.
The NR4A receptors exhibit high constitutive
activity, which is also reflected by
the corresponding Gal4-hybrid receptors, and can thus be activated
(agonists) or repressed (inverse agonists). Both types of ligands
are relevant for the development of chemical tools and may hold therapeutic
potential. Therefore, we considered fragments causing reduced reporter
activity (≤70%, inverse agonists) or enhanced reporter activity
(≥125%, agonists) on at least one NR4A as primary hits for
validation. Based on these criteria, 26 fragments were further evaluated.
Twelve fragments (2–5, 17, 20, 25, 35, 41, 44, 45, and 64) were dropped
after retesting at 100 and 200 μM on the respective receptor
for a lack of significant and dose-dependent effects (Figure S1).
We then used differential scanning
fluorimetry (DSF) with the Nurr1
LBD and the remaining 14 fragments as an orthogonal readout providing
potential insights into binding ( ). The validated NR4A ligands B( ) and simvastatin were used as references and lowered thermal stability of
the Nurr1 LBD (simvastatin: ΔTm =
−3.5 °C; B: ΔTm = −0.94 °C). This effect has also been observed
for other nuclear receptor–ligand interactions and may be due to the destabilization of the high-affinity
Nurr1 homodimer. Despite higher cellular
potency of B compared to simvastatin, , the latter caused a stronger ΔTm, indicating that the correlation between ΔTm and cellular EC50 value was weak, which has
also been observed for other members of this protein family. , Addition of coactivator peptides that are recruited upon ligand
binding can improve DSF experiments on nuclear receptors, but suitable coregulators are lacking for NR4A.
Thus, the DSF results of B and simvastatin support suitability
of DSF to indicate ligand binding to Nurr1, but the results must be
treated with care and considered as a secondary readout. DSF experiments
with the fragment screening hits revealed destabilization of the Nurr1
LBD with moderate but statistically significant ΔTm by 11 fragments supporting their direct interaction
with the Nurr1 LBD.
 Full dose–response profiling of these fragments
for NR4A
modulation revealed agonistic (11, 16, 22, 53, 78, and 91)
and inverse agonistic (18, 19, 28, 30, and 58) activities with up to single-digit
micromolar potency for some fragments ( ). Interestingly, the screening hits revealed
diverse NR4A preference profiles, indicating that selective modulators
of the NR4A receptors may be obtainable despite their high similarity.
 The focused fragment screening demonstrated that NR4A
receptors
can be targeted with FAM-type ligands with considerable chemical diversity
as obvious from low pairwise Tanimoto similarity computed on Morgan
fingerprints (mean ± SD = 0.25 ± 0.07) of the 11 validated
hits. Compared to the entire screening set ( ), the validated hits were characterized
by high lipophilicity, low H-bond donor (HBD) and acceptor (HBA) counts,
low TPSA, and a low fraction of Csp3, suggesting a preference for
hydrophobic aromatic scaffolds. The identified ligands included both
agonists and inverse agonists, indicating potential for bidirectional
NR4A modulation by FAM, with agonists tending to have a more linear
geometry than the inverse agonists.
 

## SAR of Benzyloxybenzoic Acid as FAM Nurr1 Ligand
Benzyloxybenzoic
acid (22) emerging as potent fragment hit is a highly
privileged FAM scaffold and appeared
suitable to probe the SAR of FAM NR4A ligands further. Based on the
need for Nurr1 ligands and our interest in this receptor, we focused
SAR evaluation on Nurr1 ( ). The regioisomer 3-benzyloxybenzoic acid (102) exhibited a similar activity as 22, while the corresponding
ortho-isomer was inactive at 100 μM (not shown), prompting us
to analyze the SAR for 3- and 4-substituted benzoic acid in parallel
in a combinatorial approach.
 Replacement of the benzyl motif
by 2-, 3-, and 4-chlorobenzyl residues
(93–95, 103–105) informed about structural preferences for Nurr1 agonism.
Potency with the 4-benzoic acid (93–95) series was highest with a 2-chlorobenzyl substituent while it increased
in the 3-benzoic acid (103–105) series
from 2-chlorobenzyl over 3-chlorobenzyl to 4-chlorobenzyl substitution.
The 3- and 4-chlorobenzyloxy derivatives of 3-benzoic acid (104, 105) exhibited submicromolar potency and
favorable activation efficacy, suggesting preference for 3- and 4-substitution
on the lipophilic backbone and a 3-benzoic acid headgroup.
Evaluation
of alternative substituents on the favored 3- and 4-positions
of the benzyl motif suggested different structural preferences in
both regions. Among the 3- and 4-fluorobenzyl derivatives of the 4-benzoic
acid series (96, 97), the 3-fluorobenzyl
substituent (96) gained activity compared to the chloro
analog 94, while the 4-fluoro (97) and 4-chloro
(95) derivatives were equipotent, suggesting that chlorine
might not be favored in 3-position. Indeed, trifluoromethyl (98, 108) and phenyl (109) as alternative
3-substituents enhanced activation efficacy (108) and
potency (109).
The fluorobenzyl derivatives of
the 3-benzoic acid series (106, 107) were
substantially less active than
the corresponding chloro analogs (104, 105), indicating that steric contributions of the chloro substituents
rather than electrostatic effects were relevant for activity. This
hypothesis was further supported by the enhanced activity of the 4-tert-butylbenzyl analogs (100, 110) among which 3-benzoic acid derivative 110 was remarkably
potent (EC50 = 0.12 μM). Based on the favorable effects
of 2-chloro (93, 103) and 4-chloro (95, 105) substitution, we evaluated the 2,4-dichloro
analogs 101 and 111, among which the 3-benzoic
acid derivative 111 also exhibited strong potency.
The preliminary SAR evaluation of benzyloxybenzoic acids as NR4A
ligands thus indicated that FAMs can be tuned to remarkable potency
on this understudied protein family, opening new opportunities for
the development of NR4A modulators as tools and drugs. 108 emerged from the benzyloxybenzoic acids for its high activation
efficacy, while 110 and 111 exhibited superior
potency. Orthogonal characterization by isothermal titration calorimetry
(ITC) confirmed binding to the Nurr1 LBD and revealed affinities consistent
with the EC50 values ( a). Binding of 108, 110, and 111 according to ITC involved enthalpic and entropic
contributions, suggesting that FAMs as NR4A ligands form directed
interactions with the protein and exploit the hydrophobic effect. 
 Since the benzyloxybenzoic acid
is a highly privileged FAM scaffold suggesting
interaction with various FAM targets
and thus low selectivity, we also tested the activity of 108, 110, and 111 on other fatty acid and
lipid-activated nuclear receptors. 108 and 111 slightly activated PPARγ and 108 exhibited substantial
RXRα agonism, while 110 showed no other activity
at 1 μM corresponding to >30-fold selectivity for NR4A receptors
( b). These
favorable results on affinity, potency, and selectivity of 110 further highlight the great potential of FAM as NR4A modulators,
with 110 and 111 emerging as remarkably
potent NR4A agonists despite their small (MW < 300 g/mol) and simple
structures.
Evaluation of 108, 110, and 111 on Nur77 and NOR-1 ( b) interestingly revealed a preference of 108 for Nurr1, opening a potential access to selective Nurr1
agonists. 110 and 111, in contrast, exhibited
high agonist
potency on all three NR4A receptors (110: EC50(Nur77) = 0.14 ± 0.07 μM (163 ± 4% activation); EC50(NOR-1) = 0.07 ± 0.02 μM (185 ± 5% activation); 111: EC50(Nur77) = 0.04 ± 0.01 μM (211
± 8% activation); EC50(NOR-1) = 0.15 ± 0.09 μM
(221 ± 3% activation)). Both 110 and 111 also activated full-length human Nurr1 in a more physiological setting
on the three response elements NBRE (monomer), NurRE (homodimer),
and DR5 (RXR heterodimer) with nanomolar potencies (110: EC50: 13 ± 3 nM (NBRE), 10 ± 5 nM (NurRE),
and 12 ± 6 nM (DR5); 111: EC50: 43 ±
13 nM (NBRE), 22 ± 10 nM (NurRE), and 21 ± 9 nM (DR5); d).

## Conclusion
Multiple endogenous fatty acid metabolites
function as crucial
signaling molecules, for example, in inflammatory processes and thus
interact with various receptors and other proteins. Recent findings
such as prostaglandin binding to Nurr1 and NOR-1 indicate that the NR4A family
of nuclear receptors like several other ligand-activated transcription
factors has fatty acid-sensing roles.
This offers new attractive access to NR4A modulator development for
which potent ligands are still rare despite the high therapeutic potential
of NR4A modulation. FAMs addressing natural fatty acid-binding sites
in proteins have favorable properties as obvious from a number of
successful FAM drugs. 
Here, we
sought to gain insights into the SAR of FAMs as NR4A ligands
by focused FAM fragment screening. Apart from sharing the carboxylic
acid motif, the screening set was chemically diverse to broadly explore
the chemical space of potential FAM NR4A modulators. While not all
screened fragments fully comply with the structural features of FAMs,
especially in terms of lipophilicity (cf. b), the strong enrichment of lipophilic fragments
as hits (cf. ) underscores the preference of NR4A receptors for FAM ligands. All
validated hits exhibiting NR4A agonism or inverse agonism, as well
as the optimized descendants 110 and 111, are characterized by hydrophobic aromatic scaffolds typical for
FAMs.
The hit scaffolds retrieved from the FAM screening are
a valuable
collection of leads for optimization towards potent and selective
NR4A modulators. Importantly, several fragment hits as well as 110 and 111 also activated NOR-1 for which very
few direct ligands have been reported so far, thus confirming druggability
of this orphan receptor , and offering access to potent
NOR-1 modulators.
The optimized descendants of 22 (e.g., 110 and 111) provide a remarkably
simple scaffold endowed
with considerable NR4A agonist potency. Despite lacking favorable
drug likeness, these compounds highlight the potential of FAMs to
obtain potent NR4A-modulating tools and possibly drugs. Moreover,
profiling of 110 and 111 on related nuclear
receptors indicated that FAM ligands selectively targeting NR4A receptors
can be obtained despite the strong preference of several other nuclear
receptors for FAMs and the privileged scaffold of 22 and
descendants. It should be noted, however, that the potential interaction
with FAM-binding proteins outside the nuclear receptor family was
not evaluated and that other off-targets must be considered.
Compared to the Nurr1 agonist B( ) and derivatives, the agonistic screening hits and the optimized
descendants of 22 exhibited weaker NR4A activation efficacy.
While the desirable level of efficacy for potential NR4A targeting
drugs remains to be established, strong activation is particularly
desirable for chemical tools to study the effects of NR4A modulation,
underlining the need for further optimization of all FAM screening
hits.
Overall, our results validate NR4A receptors as new FAM-binding
targets and indicate the great potential of FAMs to obtain potent
NR4A modulators. These insights and the FAM screening hits as leads
contribute to advancing NR4A ligand development towards high-quality
chemical tools and possibly drugs.

## Biological Characterization

## Reporter
Gene Assays
NR4A modulation was determined
in reporter gene assays in transiently
transfected HEK293T cells (German Collection of Microorganisms and
Cell Culture GmbH, DSMZ). Modulation of Nur77 (NR4A1), Nurr1 (NR4A2),
and NOR-1 (NR4A3) was determined in Gal4-hybrid reporter gene assays
using pFR-Luc (Stratagene, La Jolla, CA, USA; reporter), pRL-SV40
(Promega, Madison, WI, USA; internal control), and the hybrid receptor
plasmids pFA-CMV-hNur77-LBD, pFA-CMV-hNurr1-LBD, or pFA-CMV-hNOR-1-LBD. Modulation of full-length human Nurr1 (NR4A2) was studied using
the reporter plasmids pFR-Luc-NBRE, pFR-Luc-NurRE or pFR-Luc-DR5, the full-length human nuclear receptor Nurr1 encoded by pcDNA3.1-hNurr1-NE
(Addgene plasmid #102363), the full length human RXR encoded by pSG5-hRXR
and pRL-SV40. HEK293T cells were cultured in Dulbecco’s modified
Eagle’s medium, high glucose supplemented with 10% fetal calf
serum, sodium pyruvate (1 mM), penicillin (100 U/mL), and streptomycin
(100 μg/mL) at 37 °C and 5% CO2 and seeded in
96-well plates (3 × 104 cells/well). After 24 h, the
medium was changed to Opti-MEM without supplements, and the cells
were transiently transfected using Lipofectamine LTX reagent (Invitrogen)
according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Five hours after
transfection, the cells were incubated with the test compounds in
Opti-MEM supplemented with penicillin (100 U/mL), streptomycin (100
μg/mL), and 0.1% DMSO for 16 h before luciferase activity was
measured using the Dual-Glo Luciferase Assay System (Promega) according
to the manufacturer’s protocol on a Tecan Spark luminometer
(Tecan Deutschland GmbH, Germany). Firefly luminescence was divided
by Renilla luminescence and multiplied by 1000, resulting in relative
light units (RLU) to normalize for transfection efficiency and cell
growth. Fold activation was obtained by dividing the mean RLU of a
sample by the mean RLU of the untreated control. All samples were
tested in at least three biologically independent experiments in duplicates.
For dose–response curve fitting and calculation of EC50 values, the equation “[Agonist] versus response (three parameters)”
was used in GraphPad Prism (version 7.00, GraphPad Software, La Jolla,
CA, USA). Compound B( ) (3
μM) served as the positive control NR4A agonist. Selectivity
profiling was performed with identical procedures using the corresponding
pFA-CMV-NR-LBD clones (reference ligands in parentheses): pFA-CMV-THRα-LBD (1 μM triiodothyronine), pFA-CMV-RARα-LBD (1 μM all-trans retinoic acid), pFA-CMV-PPARα-LBD (1 μM GW7647), pFA-CMV-PPARγ-LBD (1 μM pioglitazone), pFA-CMV-PPARδ-LBD (1 μM L165,041), pFA-CMV-VDR-LBD (1 μM calcitriol), pFA-CMV-FXR-LBD (1 μM GW4064), and pFA-CMV-hRXRα-LBD (1 μM bexarotene).

## Recombinant
Expression and Purification of Nurr1 LBD Protein
The Nurr1
LBD (aa 362–598), subcloned into pNIC28-Bsa4,
was expressed with an N-terminal His6-tag in Escherichia coli BL21(DE3)-R3-pRARE2, cultured in
TB. Expression was induced with 0.5 mM IPTG at 18 °C overnight.
The protein was purified by Ni2+-affinity chromatography,
followed by TEV treatment to remove the histidine tag. The cleaved
protein was further purified by reverse Ni2+-affinity chromatography
and size exclusion chromatography.

## Differential Scanning Fluorimetry
Experiments were
conducted on an QuantStudio 1 (Applied Biosystems, Waltham, CA, USA)
following a published protocol. A total
of 2 μM recombinant Nurr1 LBD protein in buffer (20 mM Tris
pH 7.5, 100 mM NaCl) supplemented with SYPRO Orange dye (1:1000 dilution)
was tested with compounds (simvastatin, B, 1, 11, 16, 18, 19, 22, 28, 30, 53, 58, 60, 65, 78, 91) solubilized with 1% DMSO at a final concentration
of 100 μM or with untreated control (1% DMSO) in 71 cycles (1
°C/cycle). B( ) and simvastatin served as reference ligands. Each compound was
tested in three independent experiments in technical duplicates. Amplification
plots were analyzed by using a Boltzmann fit to obtain melting points
(Tm). ΔTm corresponds to ΔTm = Tm (compound) – Tm (untreated).

## Isothermal Titration Calorimetry
ITC experiments for
ligand binding to Nurr1 were conducted on an Affinity ITC instrument
(TA Instruments, New Castle, DE) at 25 °C with a stirring rate
of 75 rpm. Nurr1 LBD protein (10–20 μM) in buffer (20
mM Tris pH 7.5, 100 mM NaCl, 5% glycerol) containing 3% or 4% DMSO
was titrated with the test compounds (100 μM in the same buffer
containing 3% or 4% DMSO) in 21–26 injections (1 × 1 μL
and 20 × 4 to 25 × 4 μL) with an injection interval
of 150–180 s. As control experiments, the test compounds were
titrated to the buffer, and the buffer was titrated to the Nurr1 LBD
protein under otherwise identical conditions. The heat rates of the
compound-Nurr1 LBD titrations were analyzed using NanoAnalyze software
(TA Instruments, New Castle, DE) with an independent binding model.

## Computational Procedures

## Compilation of the Fragment Screening Library
Carboxylic
acid containing molecules annotated in Reaxys (www.reaxys.com) were filtered for
commercial availability from preferred vendors (Sigma-Aldrich, Enamine,
BLDpharm, Biomol, abcr), molecular weight (MW 150–300 g/mol),
and undesired elements (B, P, any metal, second COOH group, NH2 group). The resulting 5850 molecules were imported in KNIME
(v4.3.2, KNIME AG, Zurich, Switzerland), Morgan fingerprints were computed using RDKit software (v4.2.0),
and the RDKit diversity picker (random seed: 74) was used to select
92 molecules to form the chemically diverse fragment screening set.

## Descriptor and Fingerprint Calculation
Molecular descriptors
and fingerprints were calculated in KNIME (v4.3.2) using RDKit software
(v4.2.0).
