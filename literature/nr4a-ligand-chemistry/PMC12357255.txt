## Highlights
•single-cell RNA-seq identifies NR4A3high MCs enriched in metastatic OC.
•NR4A3 drives CXCL16/IL-8 secretion via JAK2/STAT6 to polarize M2 macrophages.
•Targeting NR4A3 in MCs reverses immunosuppression and inhibits OC progression.
Keywords: Mast cells, Ovarian cancer, M2 macrophages, Metastasis, NR4A3

## Abstract
Tumor-associated mast cells in the tumor microenvironment play a critical and complex role in the progression of tumor malignancy. However, the key molecules that control mast cell activation and target the biological function of ovarian cancer (OC) cells are still not fully understood. In this study, we performed scRNA-seq on cells isolated from six cases of epithelial OC tissues (three cases of primary tumor and three of metastatic tumor), and we identified three mast cell subtypes, among which the proportion of the second group of mast cell subsets specifically expressing NR4A3 was significantly higher in the metastatic tissue than in the primary tissue, suggesting that NR4A3 expression of MC may be related to the metastasis and prognosis of OC. In vitro, the biological functions of constructed NR4A3high bone-marrow-derived mast cells, such as degranulation response, showed a significant decline, but their secretion of high levels of CXCL16 and IL-8 promoted the polarization of macrophages to M2 through the STAT6 pathway, thus promoting the migration and invasion of OC. In ovarian tumor models in mice with mast cell deficiency (c-Kit W-sh/ W-sh), adoptive transfer of NR4A3high mast cells can not only promote subcutaneous tumor growth, but also promote intraperitoneal tumor cell colonization, decrease the ratio of CD8+ T cells, and increase the ratio of M2 macrophages. These results indicate that NR4A3 can drive mast cells to release more CXCL16 and IL-8 and induce macrophage M2 polarization through STAT6 signaling pathway, thereby mediating the metastasis of ovarian cancer.

## Introduction
Ovarian cancer (OC) is the third most common gynecological malignancy worldwide and has the highest mortality rate[ ]. Due to the lack of effective screening methods, 60–90 % of patients are diagnosed at an advanced stage. In recent years, many studies have found that the tumor microenvironment plays a crucial role in regulating the malignant progression of ovarian cancer [ , , ].
The tumor microenvironment (TME) refers to the internal and external environment in which tumor cells exist. The TME consists of the blood and lymphoid vessels, nerves, fibroblasts, extracellular matrix (ECM) proteins, endothelial cells, pericytes, and immune cells [ ]. Studies have shown that these dynamic interactions among cells play a crucial regulatory role in the evolution of OC [ ]. The tumor immune microenvironment is a key regulatory system: The heterogeneous immune cell populations infiltrating within it can significantly affect the phenotypic transformation and biological behavior of tumor cells through complex molecular interaction mechanisms [ ]. Mast cells are a type of innate immune cells in the tumor stroma; by releasing various biochemical factors and cell signaling molecules, they influence multiple aspects of tumor biology events, including cell proliferation, angiogenesis, invasiveness, and metastasis, thus playing a crucial regulatory role in the tumor microenvironment [ , ]. Nevertheless, their role is two-sided, promoting or inhibiting the development of tumors in different situations. Some studies have found that mast cells can also establish crosstalk with other infiltrating tumor cells in the TME, thereby influencing local immunosuppression or anti-tumor immunity [ , , , ]. However, the effect of mast cell and macrophage interactions in the tumor microenvironment on the malignant progression of ovarian cancer is unclear.
Nuclear receptors are a class of widely distributed transcription factors in living organisms. They can bind to different types of ligands as needed, including steroids, thyroid hormones, and lipid molecules [ ]. Furthermore, some nuclear receptors are referred to as orphan receptors because they have not yet been found to bind to specific ligands, but they still play essential roles within cells [ ]. NR4A3 (NOR-1) is one of three members of the NR4A subfamily (NR4A1–NR4A3). NR4A receptors are immediate-early genes, whose expression is rapidly induced in response to a plethora of stimuli, including stress, prostaglandins, growth factors, inflammatory signals, antipsychotic drugs, and fatty acids [ ]. Hyein 38[ ] found that antigen stimulation of bone-marrow-derived mast cells increased the expression levels of Nr4a3 (NOR1), Nr4a1 (Nur77), and Nr4a2 (Nurr1) transcription factors, suggesting that the NR4A family may be important in mast cell function by regulating gene transcription. The nuclear receptor 4a3 can negatively influence the transcription and protein levels of mast cell tryptase, as well as mast cell responses to allergens [ ]. Current research has shown that the dual knockout of PRDM1 and NR4A3 enables phenotypic conversion of CAR T cells to combat tumor-infiltrating CAR T cell depletion and improve anti-tumor response [ ]. In cervical cancer, the expression of BTN3A1 on tumor cells induced by IFN-γ can promote the up-regulation of NR4A2/3 expression in TFs by Vδ2 T cells, thereby affecting the activation and expression of their exhaust- related molecules in the TME [ ].Taken together, these reports suggest that NR4A3 plays an essential role in immune cells, and it can impact tumor development by affecting a series of genes related to cell proliferation and survival. However, there is limited research on the relationship between NR4A3 and the immune microenvironment in ovarian cancer.
Therefore, we carried out single-cell RNA sequencing on human ovarian tumor tissues, including primary tissues and metastatic tissues. We found that a group of mast cells that mainly expressed NR4A3 had an increased proportion in metastatic tissues. The results suggest that mast cells overexpressing NR4A3 can secrete CXCL16 and IL-8 to induce macrophage M2 polarization through the JAK2/STAT6 pathway and promote the migration and invasion of ovarian cancer cells.

## Materials and methods

## Single-cell analysis of clinical data and cell lines
According to cases with the diagnosis of ovarian cancer and uterine fibroid, we carried out single-cell RNA sequencing on human primary and metastatic tumor tissues, from January 2013 to December 2020, at Nanjing First Hospital, Nanjing Medical University. These specimens were fixed in formalin and embedded in paraffin. The ovarian cancer tumor tissues were gently dissociated using the Singleron PythoN automated tissue dissociator. The cell viability standard was viable cell rate >85 % (detected by trypan blue staining). Next, cells were captured using the SCOPE-chip microfluidic chip technology, with a capture efficiency of >50 % and a dual-cell rate control of <5 %. The library construction was completed using the GEXSCOPE series of kits: mRNA reverse transcription (template conversion efficiency >30 %), cDNA amplification (optimized by 18 PCR cycles), and library construction (average fragment size ∼350 bp). The quality control indicators were as follows: original data Q30 >85 % (Illumina platform standard), valid data volume >50,000 reads/cell. At the cellular level: median gene number >1500 (in mammalian cells), mitochondrial gene proportion <20 %, UMIs saturation >40 %. Analysis level: The contour coefficient of cell clustering was >0.7, and the kBET after batch effect correction was >0.8. Finally, cell annotation, pseudo-temporal analysis, cell communication, clonal evolution, and other analyses were conducted using the SynEcoSys database, Palantir algorithm, sCircle immunoassay module, etc. Informed consent was obtained from all patients or their family members. This study complied with the requirements of the Ethics Committee of Nanjing First Hospital, Nanjing Medical University. The ethics number is KY20240822-KS-06.
The mouse ovarian cancer cell lines ID-8 and OVHM and the murine monocytic cell line RAW 264.7 were obtained from the Type Culture Collection of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (Shanghai, China). ID-8, OVHM, and RAW 264.7 cells were cultured in DMEM (KeyGENE BioTECH) supplemented with 10 % fetal bovine serum (FBS, ExCellBio) and 1 % penicillin–streptomycin (Gibco). Cells were maintained in a humidified 5 % CO2 environment at 37 °C.

## Bioinformatic analyses of clinical data
Raw gene expression data for ovarian cancer (GSE26193) were retrieved from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) database and the TCGA database. The Kaplan–Meier Plotter online analysis tool (https://kmplot.com/analysis/) was used to perform Kaplan–Meier analyses of patients with ovarian cancer from the GSE26193 and TCGA (GEO Accession viewer (nih.gov), The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA)).

## Tissue immunofluorescence and immunohistochemistry
Human and murine tissues were fixed overnight in 4 % paraformaldehyde, embedded in paraffin, and serially sectioned at 5-mm intervals. Endogenous peroxidase activity was blocked for 10 min using 3 % hydrogen peroxide. The slides were submerged in 10 mM citrate buffer and microwaved for 15 min to achieve antigen retrieval. Nonspecific binding was prevented for 1 h using 10 % normal goat serum at room temperature. Tissue sections were incubated with primary antibodies overnight at 4 °C. After being washed, the slices were incubated for 1 h at room temperature with Alexa Fluor-conjugated secondary antibody diluted in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS).
For IHC, slices were treated after antigen retrieval with 5 % normal goat serum for 10 min. Slides were incubated with various primary antibodies at 4 °C for 12 h, and then incubate them with the appropriate secondary antibodies at room temperature for 1 hour. Hematoxylin was used as a counterstain after incubating sections with diaminobenzidine.

## Extraction of bone-marrow-derived mast cells
Bone marrow cells obtained from femora and tibiae of 10-week-old mice were cultured in 1640 supplemented with 10 % FBS, 60 μg/ml penicillin, 50 μg/ml streptomyces sulfate, 2 mM l-glutamine, and 10 ng/ml recombinant IL-3. The cell concentration was maintained at 0.5–1 × 106 cells /ml, and the medium was changed weekly [ ].

## Cell transfection
The full-length NR4A3 and GFP genes were inserted into the GV502 vector to construct an NR4A3 overexpression lentivirus (LV-NR4A3). GFP lentivirus (LV-NC) was used as the negative control. These lentiviruses were provided by GENECHEM (Shanghai, China). Bone-marrow-derived mast cells were transfected with LV-NR4A3 or negative control LV-NC and were subsequently treated with puromycin (2 μg/mL) for 3 weeks to generate stably transfected cells.

## RNA extraction and real-time quantitative polymerase chain reaction
Total RNA was extracted with TRIzol reagent (Vazyme Biotech) according to the manufacturer’s protocol, dissolved in RNA-free ddH2O, and stored at –80 °C. Then complementary DNA was synthesized from total RNA using Superscript III transcriptase (Vazyme Biotech). RT-qPCR was performed using SYBR green (Vazyme Biotech) on a real-time detector (Q5) to determine the mRNA expression levels of genes of interest. β-ACTIN mRNA expression was used to normalize the expression of target genes in each sample. Relative mRNA expression levels were calculated using the 2−ΔΔCt method. All primers were synthesized by GENEray Biotechnology (Shanghai, China). The primers used for qRT-PCR are listed in Supplemental Table S1.

## Protein extraction and western blotting
The cells were lysed in cold RIPA buffer (KeyGene Biotech) with a protease inhibitor cocktail and a phosphatase inhibitor cocktail (KeyGene Biotech), and the protein concentration was measured using the BCA method (KeyGene Biotech). A total of 10–30 μg of protein per well was loaded on 10 % or 15 % sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) and transferred to PVDF membranes (Millipore, USA). The membranes were rinsed with 1 × TBST, blocked with 5 % nonfat milk in 1 × TBST for 3 h at room temperature. The blots were probed overnight at 4 °C with the primary antibodies. The membranes were then washed three times for 10 min with 1 × TBST, followed by incubation with the appropriate horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated secondary antibodies at room temperature for 2 h. Finally, the membranes were rinsed five times with 1 × TBST for 7 min each. The signal was visualized using an ECL detection reagent (New Cell and Molecular Biotech, Suzhou, China) and a chemiluminescence device. The antibodies used for Western blotting are listed in Supplemental Table S2.

## Cell proliferation assay
Cell proliferation was determined using the Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK8, APExBIO). Each well of a 96-well culture plate was seeded with approximately 1 × 103 target cells and maintained at 37 °C overnight. CCK-8 detection solution (10 μl) was added to each well and incubated at 37 °C for 2 h. The absorbance at 450 nm was measured using a microplate reader.

## Colony-formation assay
A total of 1000 cells were seeded into six-well plates with three repetitions. After 14 days of cultivation, the colonies were washed with PBS, fixed in 4 % paraformaldehyde for 15 min, and stained with crystal violet solution for 30 min. The numbers of colonies were counted using ImageJ software.

## Transwell assay
For cell migration detection, cells were plated in the Transwell chamber of a 24-well plate in serum-free medium. Then the cells were added to the upper chamber of a Transwell assay insert (Corning, USA), and 600 µL of conditioned medium was added to the lower chamber. After incubation at 37 °C for 24 h, the cells remaining in the upper chamber were removed using cotton swabs. The upper chamber was fixed in 4 % paraformaldehyde for 15 min, stained with crystal violet solution for 30 min, and washed twice with PBS. Cells in three microscopic fields were counted and photographed. For the invasion assay, Transwell chambers were coated with Matrigel (1:8 dilution; Corning, USA) and left at 37 °C for 12 h. The cells were seeded into the upper chamber with serum-free medium. Then conditional medium was added to the lower chamber. After 24 h, the invasive ability was evaluated as described above for the cell migration assay.

## Wound-healing assay
Cells were seeded in a six-well plate. When the cell fusion rate reached 80–90 %, a linear wound was created using a 200-µL pipette tip. The cells were washed with PBS three times to remove the scratched cells, and then serum-free culture medium was added. Cell migration was monitored in a 37 °C incubator for 24 h. The injury area was measured at the beginning and end of the experiment using ImageJ software. The cell migration rate was calculated as (initial width – final width)/initial width.

## Preparation of conditional medium
A sample of 5 × 106 mast cells were collected, culture medium was changed, and the culture was continued for 48 h. Then the conditioned medium was centrifuged for 10 min at 3000 rpm to remove cell debris, and after 0.45 μm aseptic filtration it was stored at –20 °C for subsequent experiments.

## ELISA assay
The supernatants from LV-NC and LV-NR4A3 mast cells were collected after incubation in serum-free 1640 RPMI for 24 h. The medium was centrifuged to remove the cellular debris and frozen at –80 °C until it was subjected to ELISA to assay the levels of LTA4 and histamine. The LTA4 and histamine concentrations were determined using a commercial ELISA kit (CLOUD-CLONE CORP) according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

## Mouse assay
First, subcutaneous tumor models were established in 5-week-old female C57/BL6J mice and they were randomly divided into three groups, with six mice in each group. They were further divided into the mouse ovarian cancer cell line ID8 injected alone, the control mast cell +ID8 group, and the NR4A3 overexpressed mast cell +ID8 group. After the tumor was visible to the naked eye and formed, the transplanted tumor was measured with a vernier caliper as having a short diameter (W) and a long diameter (L), the volume of the tumor was calculated according to the formula V (cm3) =L × W2/2. The size of the tumor was measured and recorded every other week using a vernier caliper. After 8 weeks of tumor grafting, the tumor tissue was collected and the volume and weight of the tumor were calculated. All animal experiments were approved by the Animal Nursing Committee of Nanjing First Hospital, Nanjing Medical University.
Next, we used mast-cell-deficient mice (c-Kit W-sh/ W-sh) and randomly divided the mice into three groups, with five mice in each group, namely the ID8 group, the ID8+LV-NC group, and the ID8+LV-NR4A3 group. Intraperitoneal injection of ovarian cancer cells and mast cells was carried out. To evaluate intraperitoneal metastasis of ovarian tumors, the mice were sacrificed 4 weeks later. The ascites in the abdominal cavity of the mice was aspirated and the volume of ascites was recorded. The ascites were collected and centrifuged, and red blood cells were lysed to prepare a single-cell suspension. The ratio of CD4+/CD8+
T cells and M1/M2 macrophages in the ascites was detected by flow cytometry, and fluorescent antibody staining was performed.

## Flow cytometry
The collected ascites was passed through 70–μm pores and the red blood cells were lysed in RBC lysis buffer (Beyotime) for 3 min. Cell labeling was performed using fluorescently coupled antibodies against mouse CD3, CD4, CD8, F4/80, CD86, and CD163. Flow cytometry was performed on a DxFLEX flow cytometer (Beckman) and the data were subsequently analyzed using FlowJo V10. M0 macrophages extracted from RAW264.7 cells were washed, suspended, and stained with anti-CD206 antibodies. The cells were analyzed by flow cytometry. The antibodies used for flow cytometry are listed in Supplemental Table S3.

## Statistical analyses
Each experiment was performed in three independent replications. All data were expressed as mean ± SD, represented by error bars. The statistical significance was calculated by means of a two-tailed Student's t-test for two groups. A parametric or nonparametric ANOVA test was used for multiple-comparison experiments. Survival curves were estimated using the Kaplan–Meier method and compared using the log-rank test. P value < 0.05 was considered to be significant. All analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism 8 software (GraphPad Software).

## Results

## The level of expression of NR4A3 in mast cells is related to the prognosis of ovarian cancer
To clarify the mast cell heterogeneity in ovarian cancer, we performed scRNA-seq to profile six samples (three primary tumor tissues and three metastatic tumor tissues; A). After quality control filtering, a total of 67,489 cells were retained. Then we used uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP) to catalog them into 10 main cell clusters annotated with canonical marker genes, including epithelial cells, fibroblasts, T cells, B cells, mononuclear phagocytes, endothelial cells, mast cells, neutrophils, Plasmacytoid dendritic cells, and mesothelial cells ( B and C). The mast cell cluster can be further divided into three subpopulations containing mast cell-1, mast cell-2, and mast cell-3 ( B). The expression of NR4A3 in the first group of mast cells decreased significantly (log2FC = −3.4492235). The second group of mast cells was significantly up-regulated (log2FC = 3.1440969), while the third group of mast cells did not express NR4A3. The mast cell-2 expressed NR4A3 specifically ( E). Moreover, the proportion of mast cell-2 and the expression of NR4A3 in mast cells were significantly higher in metastatic tumors, suggesting that mast cells with high expression of NR4A3 may be related to the metastasis and prognosis of ovarian cancer ( E). By using database GSE178913, we further demonstrated that the expression of EOC in the metastatic tissues was higher than that in the primary tumors (Supplementary Fig. S1A). Next, we integrated mast cell differential genes and the GSE26193 database, and 19 genes related to prognosis were screened by univariate analysis. Among these 19 genes, NR4A3 showed the most significant correlation with prognosis (Supplementary Fig. S1B). The survival analysis of cases extracted from the GSE26193 and TCGA manifested that NR4A3 expression was associated positively with poor prognosis in patients with ovarian cancer (Supplementary Figs. S1C, 1D). To confirm the clinical significance of NR4A3 in mast cells of ovarian cancer, clinical samples were collected for immunofluorescence. The immunofluorescence results demonstrated that the number of mast cells expressing NR4A3 in ovarian tumor tissues was significantly increased compared with that in normal tissues ( F). Fisher’s exact test of NR4A3 expression in mast cells and clinicopathological characteristics revealed that NR4A3 expression in mast cells was associated with distant metastasis status and grade but not with lymph node metastasis status ( ).
 

## NR4A3high mast cells decrease degranulation ability and generate more CXCL16 and IL-8
Next, we used the lentiviral vector LV-NR4A3 to overexpress NR4A3 in the bone marrow-derived mast cells. As shown in , . NR4A3high mast cells secreted less mast cell-associated protease and more CXCL16 and IL-8A, we successfully constructed NR4A3- overexpression mast cells and reconfirmed by qRT-PCR and Western blotting. Then we investigated whether overexpression of NR4A3 in mast cells affects mast cell self-degranulation response. qRT-PCR results showed that the mRNA expression level of tryptase alpha/beta 1(TPSAB1) in mast cells with high NR4A3 expression was relatively decreased ( B). Afterward, we used Compound 48/80 as an inducer of mast cell degranulation to activate mast cells. The supernatant of mast cells was collected, and the content of histamine and leukotriene released after degranulation was detected by ELISA. The results showed that the NR4A3high mast cells released histamine and leukotriene mediators significantly decreased, further indicating that NR4A3 overexpression in mast cells can reduce the ability of degranulation ( C and D). To understand the mechanism by which NR4A3high mast cells in the microenvironment promote the metastasis of ovarian cancer cells, we collected LV-NC and LV-NR4A3 mast cells for RNA sequencing. Sequencing showed that 472 genes were up-regulated and 102 genes were down-regulated in LV-NR4A3 mast cells as compared with LV-NC cells. The NR4A3high mast cells can down-regulate the expression of Tpsab1 and Mcpt8 genes as compared with the control group ( E). Moreover, these differential genes are mainly related to necroptosis, cell cycle, and other pathways ( F). By transcriptome sequencing, we found that CXCL16 and IL-8 were significantly up-regulated in NR4A3high mast cells, which were further confirmed by qRT-PCR and Western blotting ( G, H, and I).
 

## NR4A3high mast cells can accelerate M2 macrophage polarization by secreting CXCL16 and IL-8
In our previous single-cell sequencing analysis, we found that mast cells and macrophages interact more closely in the microenvironment of metastatic ovarian cancer tumors than in that of primary tumors ( A). Studies have reported that mast cells recruit tumor-related macrophages by secreting CSF2, CCL3, and IL-6 after activation, and promote angiogenesis and lead to malignant progression of tumors [ ]. Therefore, in order to explore the relationship between mast cells and macrophages, LV-NR4A3 and LV-NC mast cells were co-cultured with mouse monocytes/macrophages RAW 264.7, respectively ( B). We observed that there was no significant difference in the morphology of macrophages after co-culture with the two types of mast cells, but co-culture with LV-NR4A3 mast cells seemed to promote the proliferation of raw cells ( C and D).
 Macrophages are classified into types M1 and M2 according to their functions and levels of inflammatory secretion [ ]. M1 macrophages are thought to have anti-tumor effects, while M2 macrophages, commonly known as tumor-associated macrophages (TAMS), promote tumor development through angiogenesis, immunosuppression, hypoxia induction, tumor cell proliferation, and metastasis. The expression levels of the macrophage M1/M2 activation markers iNOS, TNF-α, CD206, and CD163 were detected by qPCR. The results showed that, as compared with macrophages co-cultured with mast cells in the LV-NC group, mast cells with overexpression of LV-NR4A3 increased the levels of M2 polarization markers such as CD163 and CD206 after co-culture with macrophages and promoted the polarization of macrophages in the M2 direction ( E). Consistent with this result, we also demonstrated again that LV-NR4A3 mast cells could promote M2 polarization of macrophages, using flow cytometry ( F).
Other studies have shown that CXCL16 can promote the polarization of macrophages toward M2 [ ].To investigate how LV-NR4A3 mast cells promote macrophage M2 polarization, we assumed that NR4A3high mast cells secreting CXCL16 and IL-8 induce macrophages to undergo M2 polarization. So we added CXCL16 and IL-8 receptor antagonist or inhibitors ML339 and Reparixin during co-culture. The results indicated that the mean fluorescence intensity of the M2 marker CD206 was indeed reduced after the use of inhibitors ML339 and Reparixin ( G). Western blotting found that the phosphorylation level of STAT6 in LV-NR4A3 co-cultured macrophages was significantly increased as compared with that in LV-NC co-cultured macrophages ( H). Meanwhile, TGF-β levels increased significantly, while pro-inflammatory factor IL-6 levels decreased. Meanwhile, we detected the expression levels of IL-4 and IL-13. LV-NR4A3 co-cultured macrophages did not stimulate its expression. After the use of ML339 and Reparixin, these effects can be eliminated significantly, with ML339 alone having a better suppressive effect. However, there was no significant difference in the levels of IL-4 and IL-13 after the use of the inhibitor, indicating that NR4A3high mast cells activate the STAT6 pathway of macrophages through CXCL16 and IL-8 to induce M2 polarization. Next, IL-4 and IL-13 were used to induce M2 polarization of macrophages and then co-cultured with mast cells to investigate whether mast cells with high NR4A3 expression promoted M2 polarization of macrophages and had a synergistic effect with IL-4 and IL-13. The results showed that the use of IL-4 and IL-13 did not further increase the proportion of cells with M2 polarization in the presence of NR4A3-OE, indicating that there was no synergistic effect between the two to promote M2 polarization in macrophages (Supplementary Figs. S2A–S2C).

## M2 macrophages induced by NR4A3high mast cells significantly promote the migration and invasion of ovarian cancer cells in vitro
In order to investigate the role of NRA43 in mast cells in ovarian cancer progression, the mast cells were co-cultured with ovarian cancer cell lines ID8 and OVHM. We observed that the high expression of NR4A3 in tumor-associated mast cells does not directly promote the proliferation, migration, and invasion of ovarian cancer cells (Supplementary Figs. S3A–3E). To investigate whether M2 macrophages induced by NR4A3high mast cells are associated with the malignant phenotype of ovarian cancer cells, macrophages co-cultured with LV-NR4A3 and LV-NC mast cells were indirectly co-cultured with ovarian cancer cells (Supplementary Fig. S4A). The results of cck8 and colony formation showed that macrophages pretreated with NR4A3high mast cells did not significantly promote the proliferation of ovarian cancer cells (Supplementary Figs. S4B and 4C).
Subsequently, we observed that M2 macrophages induced by NR4A3high mast cells promote the migration of ovarian cancer cells through wound healing assay ( B). In addition, Transwell experiments demonstrated that M2 macrophages induced by NR4A3high mast cells significantly promote the migration and invasion of ovarian cancer cells compared with control mast cells retreated macrophages ( C and D). Taken together, these results suggest that NR4A3high mast cells promote the migration and invasion of ovarian cancer by inducing M2 polarization of macrophages. To study the effect of M2 type macrophages induced by NR4A3high mast cells on ovarian cancer cells, we performed follow-up experiments using macrophages pretreated with the inhibitor ML339 and Reparixin ( A). It was observed that the malignant phenotype can be reduced to some extent after blocking macrophage M2 polarization using ML339 and Reparixin ( B–D).
 

## NR4A3high mast cells promote ovarian cancer metastasis in vivo and induce immunosuppression
To evaluate the in vivo role of mast cells with high NR4A3 expression in ovarian cancer, we first established a subcutaneous tumor model. Compared with control group, mice injected with ID8+LV-NR4A3 mast cells had larger subcutaneous tumors ( A–C). Immunohistochemical results showed that the expression of CD206 in the group with high NR4A3 expression was significantly higher than that in the control group ( D). To exclude the influence of mast cells in mice, we used mast-cell-deficient mice. ID8 cells were injected into the abdominal cavity of mice with or without two types of mast cells (at a ratio of 4:1), and subsequent changes were monitored. After 1 month, the volume of ascites was measured and differences in T cells and macrophages were analyzed using flow cytometry. As shown in E, compared with mice injected with ID8 alone, ID8+LV-NR4A3 mice had significantly more metastases on the abdominal wall, while ID8+LV-NC mice had significantly fewer metastases. Moreover, the volume of ascites collected by us also showed the same results: The mice in the ID8+LV-NR4A3 group had the most severe ascites, followed by the mice in the ID8 group, and the last were the mice in the ID8+LV-NC group ( F). The results revealed that compared to ID8+NC mast cell mice, the proportion of CD4+T cells and CD8+T cells in ascites from the ID8+OE mast cell group was significantly reduced, indicating impaired anti-tumor immune effects ( G). Additionally, there was a notable increase in M2 macrophage content, further supporting our previous findings that mast cells expressing high levels of NR4A3 can indeed promote M2 polarization in macrophages and exert pro-tumorigenic functions ( G).
 These findings indicate that NR4A3high mast cells enhance omental metastasis in ovarian cancer by secreting CXCL16 and IL-8 to induce M2 macrophages through the STAT6 signaling pathway ( ).
 

## Discussion
Ovarian cancer is one of the gynecological malignancies with the highest mortality rate, and most patients are diagnosed with intraperitoneal metastasis at a late stage. Ovarian cancer mainly spreads directly to adjacent organs through pelvic dissemination rather than through lymphatic or blood channels [ ]. In recent years, the tumor microenvironment has played a key role in the metastasis mechanism of ovarian cancer and is gradually being considered a therapeutic target for ovarian cancer patients [ ]. Advanced ovarian cancer is often accompanied by ascites, which is mainly characterized by the immunosuppressive microenvironment and rich in various inflammatory cytokines and immunosuppressive cells, which help tumors evade immune clearance by inhibiting effector T cell function and promoting immune tolerance [ , ]. In fact, various host immune cells not only shape the tumor microenvironment, but also participate in multiple stages of metastasis. Therefore, in order to improve clinical outcomes for patients with ovarian cancer, it is critical to understand the immune events associated with tumor development and progression [ ].
Mast cells are mainly found in connective tissues [ ], although the phenotype and function of mast cells in promoting allergic reaction, anti-infection, tissue repair, and angiogenesis have been studied extensively [ , ]. However, as part of the immune microenvironment, mast cells also play an undeniable role in the regulation of tumor cells. Many studies have shown that NR4A3 expression is enhanced in acinar cell carcinoma (ACC) [ , ]. However, NR4A3 may act as a tumor suppressor gene in hematologic and solid tumors of breast cancer, bladder cancer, and non-small cell lung cancer, inhibiting tumor development and metastasis and promoting good prognosis [ , , , , ]. In this study, we observed that after NR4A3high mast cells were activated in the face of exogenous stimuli, their own degranulation reactions, such as the release of histamine, leukotriene, and other mediators, were significantly reduced, suggesting that the expression level of NR4A3 in mast cells greatly affected the biological function of MC. The ovarian cancer microenvironment may have an effect on tumor cells as well as other immune cells.
Importantly, the complexity of the tumor microenvironment is particularly evident in the interactions between various cells. Moritz F. Eissmann et al. discovered that specific deletion of mast cells can reduce the number of tumor-associated macrophages, inhibiting tumor cell growth and angiogenesis in gastric cancer [ ]. Jammal MP et al. [ ] found that in contrast to primary ovarian cancer, the proportion of M2 macrophages and mast cells in malignant ovarian tumors is significantly increased. The proportion of M2 macrophages was correlated positively with histological grade, disease stage, and prognosis [ ]. Nonetheless, the interaction between mast cells and macrophages in the ovarian cancer microenvironment has not been studied thoroughly at present. In our study, the single-cell sequencing results showed that the interaction between mast cells and macrophages was significantly enhanced in the tissues with ovarian cancer metastasis, suggesting that we should explore further the important influence of functional crosstalk between the two types of immune cells on the occurrence and development of tumors.
Chemokines are a large family that mediate many biological behaviors, including chemotaxis, tumor growth, and angiogenesis. The CXC chemokine subfamily is closely related to tumor growth regulation and metastasis [ , ]. As a member of the family, the CXCL16 axis is expressed primarily in immune cells and plays an important role in innate and adaptive immune responses against infections and cancer [ ]. Studies have shown that CXCR6 and CXCL16 are abnormally expressed in a variety of cancer tissues and play crucial roles in regulating the development and metastasis of cancers as inflammatory chemokines [ , ]. According to previous studies, IL-8 is associated with osteolytic bone metastasis induced by lung cancer cells [ ].
To elucidate the role of NR4A3 in MCs and its influence on OC progression, we observed in this study that NR4A3high mast cells can induce macrophage M2 polarization through the secretion of chemokine CXCL16 and IL-8, promote the metastasis of ovarian cancer, and lead to poor prognosis. At the same time, we found that TGF-β factor was increased in M2 macrophages induced by NR4A3high mast cells, and inflammatory factors such as TNF-α and IL-6 were significantly decreased. NR4A3high mast cells co-cultured with macrophages can promote the polarization of macrophages from M0 to M2 by activating the JAK2/STAT6 pathway. In addition, we used mast-cell-deficient mice to construct a peritoneal metastasis model of ovarian cancer to explore the interaction between mast cells and macrophages in vivo. Consistent with the previous results, we found that NR4A3high MCs could mediate the M2 type transformation of macrophages, reduce the proportion of CD8+ T cells, lead to immunosuppression, and promote the metastasis and progression of ovarian cancer. Therefore, targeted inhibition of mast cell NR4A3 expression in TME may be a promising treatment for ovarian cancer, but the functional crosstalk between NR4A3 and other immune cells needs to be explored in further experiments.

## Conclusion
In conclusion, our findings demonstrate that interactions between NR4A3high mast cells and M2 macrophages promote metastasis in ovarian cancer. NR4A3 induces mast cells to secrete elevated levels of the chemokines CXCL16 and IL-8. These chemokines, in turn, drive M2 macrophage polarization via the STAT6 pathway. Critically, pharmacological inhibition of the chemokine receptors CXCR6 (ligand: CXCL16) and CXCR1/2 (ligands: IL-8/CXCL8) effectively suppresses the NR4A3high mast-cell-induced M2 macrophage phenotype and thereby inhibits ovarian cancer metastasis.
