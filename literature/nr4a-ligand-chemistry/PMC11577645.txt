## Background
Heat stress (HS) can affect the physiology and metabolism of animals. HS-induced intestinal inflammation in pigs is a common disease, causing severe diarrhea, that can result in substantial economic losses to the pig industry, but the molecular mechanisms and pathogenicity of this disease are not fully understood. The objective of this study was to identify the differentially expressed genes (DEGs) and long noncoding RNAs (DELs) related to inflammation in the colon tissues of pigs under constant (1, 7, and 14 days) HS.

## Results
LncRNA and targeted gene interaction networks were constructed. GO annotation and KEGG pathway analyses were subsequently performed to determine the functions of the DEGs and DELs. The results revealed 57, 212, and 54 DEGs and 87, 79, and 55 DELs in the CON/H01, CON/H07, and CON/H14 groups, respectively. KRT85, CLDN1, S100A12, TM7SF2, CCN1, NR4A1, and several lncRNAs may be involved in regulating the development of intestinal inflammation. GO analysis indicated that the DEGs and DELs were enriched in a series of biological processes involved in the innate immune response, RAGE receptor binding, and positive regulation of the ERK1 and ERK2 cascades. KEGG pathways related to inflammation, such as the tight junction (TJ) and MAPK signaling pathways, were enriched in DEGs and DELs.

## Conclusions
This study have expanded the knowledge about colon inflammation-related genes and lncRNA biology in pigs under HS; analyzed the the lncRNA‒mRNA interaction for HS-induced intestinal inflammation. These results may provide some references for our understanding of the molecular mechanism of the intestinal response to HS in pig.

## Background
Various factors, such as temperature change, overtiredness, starvation, new feed, and infection, may precipitate gastrointestinal disorders, thus inducing inflammation in animals [ ]. With global warming, heat stress (HS) has become an essential pathogenic factor that affects animal growth. HS can substantially affect the physiological functions, productivity, and health of animals [ , ]. For example, these changes can decrease the respiration rate, vasodilatation, milk yield, and fertility in dairy cows [ ]. HS severely affects feed intake, egg production, and quality and increases mortality in chickens [ ]. HS can significantly induce an imbalance between the intestinal microecosystem and immune system and can cause mucosal barrier imbalance, intestinal ulcers, and inflammatory cell infiltration, leading to inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) in pigs [ , ]. These problems may result in serious economic losses for the pig industry, with annual economic losses close to $1 billion in the United States [ ].
Previous studies have shown that HS activates Toll-like receptors (TLRs), which can damage the intestinal epithelium [ ]. TLR4-mediated responses include the initiation of the nuclear factor-κB (NF-κB) and mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) signaling pathways. NF-κB can induce inflammatory mediators and suppress immune responses, causing IBD and other autoimmune diseases. The MAPK signaling pathway induces inflammation and the expression of tight junction (TJ) proteins [ – ]. Other evidence suggests that inhibiting the NF-κB and MAPK pathways is vital for treating intestinal inflammation [ ]. Unfortunately, the pathogenesis of HS-induced intestinal inflammation is still unclear [ ]. Therefore, in these studies, we identified signaling pathways and analyzed genes to obtain additional information about the pathogenic mechanism of intestinal inflammation.
Long noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs) are critical components of regulatory gene networks. Studies have shown that lncRNAs play essential roles in the pathogenesis of IBD [ , ]. However, there is insufficient information on the specific lncRNAs that are involved in heat stress-induced intestinal inflammation. High-throughput sequencing and transcriptome biology analyses reveal the presence and quantity of lncRNAs in a biological sample under specific conditions [ ]. In this study, we obtained colon samples from HS pigs. We performed transcriptome sequencing of these tissues to identify the differentially expressed genes (DEGs), lncRNAs (DELs), and potential lncRNA‒mRNA coexpression networks. Our findings may contribute to the development of novel diagnostic and therapeutic targets.

## Materials and methods

## Experimental animals and groups
Eighteen crossbred boars (Duroc ♂ × Local Luchuan pig ♀) were purchased from a farm in Zhanjiang City of Guangdong Province and were 2 months old (15 ± 1 kg). The pigs were randomly allocated to a control group (CON; n = 9) or a HS group (n = 9). These pigs were housed in two animal rooms at the Animal Hospital of Guangdong Ocean University, Zhanjiang. Each room was equipped with an independent humidity and temperature control system. In the trial periods, the photoperiod was maintained under 12:12 h (light: dark). The pigs were fed a balanced pig feed three times (morning, afternoon, and evening) a day with about 6 h interval, and allowed them to drink freely. The air temperature of the CON group was 20 ± 2 °C, while that of the HS group was 35 ± 1 °C. All the groups were housed at approximately 80% relative humidity. The study was approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of Guangdong Ocean University, China (IACUC No. GDOU-LAE-2020-007). The animal experiments strictly followed the ARRIVE guidelines (Animal Research: Reporting of In Vivo Experiments) [ ].

## Sample collection
The diarrhea and fecal status of pigs were observed and recorded daily. The Diarrhea Index was calculated based on the score in Table [ ]. Pig colonies were collected on the 1st, 7th, and 14th days under HS and CON conditions (3 in each group). Pigs were euthanized by electric shock fainting and venous bleeding. Separation of colon tissue through abdominal surgery. Sampling locations were as consistent as possible. Each intestinal segment cut open after separation of fat and mesentery, and the luminal contents were removed and washed with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS, pH = 7.2). Subsequently, The colon tissue was placed into sterile tubes and stored at -80 °C for molecular analysis.

## Total RNA extraction
Colon samples were collected and grouped. Total RNA was extracted using TRIzol reagent (TransGen Biotech, Beijing, China). The RNA purity was determined using a NanoDrop 1000 spectrophotometer (NanoDrop Technologies, Wilmington, DE, USA). RNA integrity was assessed using electrophoresis (1% agarose gel). The RNA was transcribed into a reverse transcription kit (TransGen Biotech, Beijing, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions for gene expression analysis.

## Sequence data analysis and annotation
The library was constructed using Ribo-off rRNA Depletion Kit (Vazyme, Nanjing, China). After the construction of the library was completed, a Qubit TM dsDNA HS Assay Kit and a Qubit 3.0 Fluorometer (Termo Scientifc, MA, USA) were used for quantification. Subsequently, the inserted fragments of the library were detected by the Qsep400 high-throughput analysis system (BiOptic., Jiangsu, China). Then, the sequencing library was generated on an Illumina sequencing platform (http://www.biomarker.com.cn/technology-services/illumina) according to sequencing by synthesis (SBS) technology (Biomarker Technologies, Beijing, China). The raw data were subjected to adapter removal, low-quality read removal, and rRNA read removal using the FastQC (v.0.10.1) and NGSQC (v. 2.3.3) Toolkit [ , ], after which we generated clean reads for analysis. These clean reads were subsequently mapped to the pig (Sus_scrofa.Sscrofa11.1_99.genome.fa; GCA_000003025.6) genome by HISAT2 (v. 2.2.1) with default parameters [ ]. The alignment was performed with StringTie (v. 2.1.6) [ ]. The coding potential calculator (CPC) (v. 0.9-r2) [ ], the coding-noncoding index (CNCI) (v. 1.0) [ ], the protein families database (Pfam) (v. 30.0) [ ], and the coding potential assessment tool (CPAT) (v. 2.0.0) were subsequently used to filter transcripts with coding potential [ ]. The differential expression analysis based on negative binomial distribution was performed by the DESeq2 (v. 1.44.0) package in R (v. 4.4) [ ]. The fold Change (FC) and false discovery rate (FDR) were obtained by correcting the difference significance P-value in the process of differential expression gene detection. Poisson model and empirical Bayesian method with discrete degree are used to reduce the excessive dispersion across transcripts. The screening criterion for DEGs was defined as a difference of |log2FC| ≥ 1 and a P < 0.05 significance level.

## GO and KEGG enrichment analyses
The functional properties of the DEGs and DELs were predicted based on Gene Ontology (GO) (http://www.geneontology.org/) enrichment via the Biological Process (BP), Cellular Component (CC), and Molecular Function (MF) categories using ClusterProfiler package (v. 3.19) [ ]. Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway enrichment analysis was subsequently performed for the DEGs and DELs [ ]. The GO terms and pathways were considered differentially enriched (P < 0.05).

## LncRNA-mRNA coexpression network
The construction of a lncRNA‒mRNA coexpression network revealed the correlation between lncRNAs and mRNAs to investigate key lncRNAs and their functions in inflammation using Cytoscape (v. 3.10.2)(https://cytoscape.org/download.html) [ ]. The coexpression network was selected using the following absolute values: Pearson correlation coefficient (|PCC|) > 0.99 and P < 0.05. The lncRNA‒mRNA pairs were filtered via GO and KEGG analyses based on the analysis results [ , ].

## qRT‑PCR validation
qRT‒PCR was performed on a CFX Connect real-time system (Bio-Rad Hercules, CA, USA). The primers used are shown in additional Table . The relative quantification of lncRNAs and mRNAs was normalized to that of β-actin using the 2−ΔΔCt method.

## Statistical analysis
All the statistical analyses were performed using SPSS (v. 19.0) software and Graph Pad Prism (v. 9.5.1) software for the plotting of results. The data are presented as the mean ± SEM and were analyzed by Student’s t test. P < 0.05 was considered to indicate statistical significance.

## Quality analysis of RNA-seq data
Twelve cDNA libraries constituting three biological replicates were constructed from the four treatment groups and sequenced using an Illumina high-throughput sequencing platform to explore the molecular mechanism of HS colic inflammation in pigs. The twelve samples were named CON (CON1, CON2, and CON3), H01 (H011, H012 and H013), H07 (H071, H072 and H073), and H14 (H141, H142, and H143). The RNA-seq results are summarized in Table . For the twelve samples, 282.44 Gb of clean data were obtained; the Q20 was above 98.74%, while the Q30% was not less than 92.92% after quality control. To assess the quality of our assembly, clean reads from all the samples were subjected to alignment with the reference genome of pigs. The HISAT2 alignment percentage of all the mapped reads ranged from 79.53 to 94.98%, as shown in Table . Cuffmerge was used to merge all the files from individual samples to obtain a combined file. Assembled transcripts with noncoding potential class codes such as “i”, “x”, “u”, “o”, and “e” were filtered out, and the remaining categories were classified as mRNAs. Filtered low confidence single exon transcripts in transcript splicing results, transcripts (exon ≥ 2) were selected for screening of the number of exons in lncRNAs. Transcripts with a length greater than 200 nt and FPKM ≥ 0.1 were selected. Screened out transcripts that overlap with the database annotation exon region by Cuffmerge. To differentiate lncRNAs from protein-coding transcript units, four analytic tools, CNCI, CPC, Pfam, and CPAT were used.
As shown in Fig.  a, a total of 19,090 accurately expressed lncRNA transcripts were identified. The studied lncRNAs were divided into lincRNAs, antisense lncRNAs, intronic lncRNAs, and sense lncRNAs, which accounted for 35.6%, 17.9%, 44.1%, and 2.4%, respectively, of the total lncRNAs (Fig.  b). The transcript length, exon length, and ORF length of the mRNAs and lncRNAs are shown in Fig. . Most lncRNA lengths are between 400 and 3000, whereas the lengths of mRNAs are evenly distributed, ranging from 200 to > 3000. The lengths of the lncRNAs and mRNAs clearly diverged according to the differences in transcript length distribution according to the composite analysis. Unlike those of mRNAs, the ORF lengths of all lncRNAs were between 50 and 150 bp. Correlation analysis based on gene expression data can aid in identifying clusters among samples. As shown in Fig.  c, the larger the correlation coefficient between samples was, the better the sample clustering was. A circos plot was generated to show the location and expression of the identified mRNAs and lncRNAs in the pig genome (Fig. ).

## Discussion
Multiple molecules mediate the mechanisms underlying HS-induced colic inflammation in pigs. Numerous studies have evaluated the expression of markers such as mRNAs, proteins, or components of signaling pathways [ ]. However, until recently, the relationship between inflammation and lncRNAs in HS pigs has remained unclear. Here, we aimed to identify the expression profiles of mRNAs and lncRNAs involved in HS-induced colic inflammation, explore the functions and interactions of mRNAs and lncRNAs via transcriptome sequencing and analysis, and provide a novel theoretical basis for the diagnosis and treatment of colic inflammation.
The present study identified 57, 212 and 54 DEGs in the CON/H01, CON/H07, and CON/H14 groups, respectively. Among the DEGs were several genes involved in the inflammatory response. KRT85, CLDN1, S100A12 and TM7SF2 exhibited high expression in the CON/H01 group. The RT‒qPCR results showed increased expression of these genes in colon tissue samples from H01 pigs compared with those from CON pigs. KRT is the main component of intermediate filaments in epithelial cells, and KRTs have been implicated in the processes of epithelial integrity, cell death, and the response to inflammation in the colonic epithelium [ , ]. KRT85 and KRT8 belong to the keratin gene family, which are type II (KRT basic type). A previous study revealed a significant abundance of KRT8 in intestinal epithelial cells in IBD patients. KRTs 8 and 18 are keratin interaction pairs. In vitro, both KRT8 and KRT18 in the HS model caused intestinal cells to detach from the surface. KRT18 displaces the TJ proteins ZO-1 and claudin-4 and can directly interfere with epithelial barrier function [ – ]. In addition, the expression of KRT18 in colorectal cancer tissues was significantly greater than that in normal tissues, and high KRT18 expression was associated with deep tumor invasion and lymph node metastasis [ ]. CLDN proteins determine the barrier features of TJs, and CLDN1 is one of the 27 members of the CLDN family [ ]. CLDN1 expression was upregulated in the CON/H01 group. Research has shown that after 5 days of heat stress, CDLN1 expression is upregulated in the jejuna of birds fed a standard diet. This upregulated expression is likely an adaptation strategy to avoid intestinal leakage [ ]. However, several studies have shown increased expression of CLDN1 in primary colorectal cancer (CRC) patients. Furthermore, aberrant CLDN1 expression can lead to alterations in TJ structure and function or cell signaling pathway dysregulation. Therefore, CLDN1 could be clinically beneficial for CRC treatment [ – ]. The S100A12 belongs to the S100 family of genes and has been implicated in immune regulation. Research has shown that S100A12 can bind to and activate RAGE. These interactions induce signal transduction through the transmembrane domain and cause cytoplasmic phosphorylation, leading to increased NF-κB activation and inflammatory response initiation [ – ]. Additionally, the activation of TLR4 signaling by S100A12 induces cognate upregulation of proinflammatory cytokines, including IL-1β, IL-6, and IL-8 [ ]. The TM7SF2 encodes an endoplasmic reticulum (ER) enzyme; therefore, overexpression of this protein impairs the physiological functions of the ER. Severe ER dysfunction eventually causes cell death by activating NF-κB [ ]. The DEGs were functionally annotated based on GO terms and KEGG pathway information. Among the DEGs, most were related to the innate immune response in the MF category, the extracellular space and brush border membrane in the CC category, RAGE receptor binding in the MF category, and the PPAR signaling pathway. The PPAR signaling pathway was more prevalently and aberrantly activated in tumor epithelial cells than in normal cells. PPARs belong to subfamily 1 of the nuclear hormone receptor superfamily of transcription factors and, upon ligand binding, regulate the expression of both target genes [ , ]. Several studies have shown that growth factor-induced MAPK activation plays a role in regulating PPAR function. PPARs are expressed mainly in the colonic epithelium, and their activation represses NF-κB signaling and plays a vital role in regulating inflammation in various diseases [ , ]. Therefore, according to the transcriptome analysis, we considered that the mucosal inflammatory response occurred on the first day in the HS pigs.
Our previous research revealed noticeable pathological changes in pigs in the HS group on days 7 and 14. In HS animals, blood vessels are dilated to maximize peripheral heat loss. Therefore, the blood supply to the intestine is reduced, decreasing the intestinal oxygen supply and damage and triggering localized inflammation [ ]. The present study revealed that CCN1 and NR4A1 were significantly expressed in the CON/H07 groups. CCN1 is an angiogenic matricellular protein highly expressed at inflammatory sites. Studies have shown that the expression of CCN1 was increased in mice with experimental colitis [ ]. CCN1 protein expression is upregulated in intestinal tissue subjected to ischemia in pigs, and this upregulation is more concentrated in the mucosal area of the intestine. CCN1 induces macrophage gene expression by activating NF-κB in a β2- and syndecan-4-dependent manner and supports macrophage adhesion through the integrin leukocyte-specific integrin receptor αMβ2 [ , ]. NR4A1 is an orphan nuclear receptor that plays a critical role in many cellular processes in response to diverse stimuli [ ]. Previous studies have revealed extensive interactions between NR4A1 and the NF-κB signaling pathway. The IKKi promoter contains a functional NR4A binding site. It is activated by all three NR4A (NR4A1, NR4A2, and NR4A3) receptors according to transient transfection assays. The expression of NR4A potentiates the induction of inflammatory gene expression by the ligands TLR3 and TLR4 [ ]. For the CON/H07 and CON/H14 groups, GO analysis revealed that the enriched BP terms included ERK1/2 cascades, the CC terms included the extracellular space and cell surface, and KEGG functional enrichment analysis of cellular processes was performed for TJs. ERK1/2 belongs to the MAPK family and transmits extracellular signals to intracellular targets. Proteomic studies have shown that HS activates the NF-κB and MAPK signaling pathways and results in damage to the intestinal epithelium. Furthermore, the TLR4/NF-κB signaling pathway was also activated in intestinal tissue. NF-κB is an effector molecule of the TLR4 signaling pathway that causes local intestinal mucosal epithelial cell apoptosis and damage to tissues and organs [ , , ]. The intestinal mucosal barrier comprises intact intestinal epithelial cells and junctional complexes, which include TJ proteins. TJ proteins, including the peripheral membrane ZO protein family and the transmembrane occludin and claudins, play crucial roles in intestinal immune function. Research has shown that the expression of P-ERK, ZO-1, and claudin-4 decreases in HS-induced IPEC-J2 cells. The expression of the TJ proteins ZO-1 and claudin-4 was downregulated, but the expression of phosphorylated ERK1/2 was significantly upregulated in the colon of HS pigs. Therefore, as the expression of TJ proteins decreases, intestinal permeability increases, and intestinal barrier function is impaired by HS [ , ]. Our previous studies revealed that, compared with those in the CON group, epithelial cell sloughing, vasodilation, and mucosal hyperemia occurred in the colonic intestinal epithelium of HS pigs on day 7, and the crypt depth became shallower by day 14 [ ].
LncRNAs play a significant role in the diagnosis and treatment of diseases and have received an increasing amount of attention [ ]. In the present study, the expression levels of lncRNAs in colon tissues significantly differed between control and HS pigs. According to the analysis of the lncRNA‒mRNA interaction network and our validated results, the lncRNA MSTRG.47709.5 regulated RAB (a Ras-related protein in the brain) 30 in the CON/H01 and CON/H14 groups; MSTRG.50167.1 regulated RAB11FIP2 in the CON/H01 and CON/H07 groups; and MSTRG.28207.43 regulated RAB27B in the CON/H14 group. The RAB GTPase gene family is a central mediator of cell compartmentalization, and members of this gene family play essential roles in specifying transport pathways in the intracellular membrane trafficking system. RAB GTPases regulate intracellular trafficking via vesicle budding, motility, docking, and fusion through interactions with effectors and regulate immunity and inflammation [ – ]. RAB11A belong to the RAB11 subfamily. Studies have reported that intestine-specific loss of RAB11A results in enterocyte microvillus atrophy and the mislocalization of apical proteins, increasing early mortality. Furthermore, mouse intestine-specific inactivation of RAB11A also causes NF-κB activation and inflammatory cytokine production [ – ]. RAB11FIP2 is a RAB11 family-interacting protein that can interact with myosin 5B (MYO5B), which alters the expression of myosin 5B to affect the apical membrane trafficking mechanism in epithelial cells. Both germline and intestinal-targeted knockout of MYO5B in mice led to deadly diarrhea due to inadequate presentation of apical water and sodium transporters [ – ]. The expression levels of the lncRNAs CASC11 and RAB11FIP2 were positively correlated and reciprocally regulated in CRC, and RAB11FIP2 was found to be a mutual target of both miR-381-3p and miR-646. These proteins bind to the RAB11FIP2 3’UTR and inhibit the translation of RAB11FIP2. CASC11 adsorbs miR-646, and miR-381-3p targets the RAB11FIP2 in the cytoplasm of CRC cells. Several sets of data suggest that serum exosomal RAB27A mRNA mRNA expression increases in lung cancer patients and that lncRNA-RP11-510M2.10 may downregulate RAB27A expression by adsorbing transcription factors or acting as a precursor of miRNAs [ , ]. Our research revealed that the expression of MSTRG.47709.5, MSTRG.50167.1, and MSTRG.28207.43 was significantly increased in HS pigs, suggesting that these genes potentially regulate RAB genes. However, further experimental studies are needed to confirm these findings.
Zinc finger protein (ZNF) genes encode the most prominent family of regulatory proteins with zinc ion-binding finger-like domains. These ZNF proteins can interact with RNA, DNA, or proteins to participate in various cellular processes. Many studies have indicated the vital role of ZNF proteins or genes in tumors, and ZNF-regulated lncRNAs might serve as diagnostic markers [ ]. The lncRNA 604/ZNF326/EMT signaling axis may play an important role in CRC progression. In the nucleus, ZNF326 acts as an oncoprotein in cancer and can bind to 604, thereby promoting epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT). Therefore, 604 could promote CRC metastasis by regulating ZNF326 in the nucleus [ ]. In addition, the lncRNA SNHG15 binds and inhibits the activity of endogenous miR-335-3p, thereby leading to increased ZNF326 expression. ZNF326 inhibited cisplatin-induced cell apoptosis; thus, developing resistance to drugs, especially cisplatin (CDDP), has compounded challenges in treating osteosarcoma [ , ]. Our analysis revealed the upregulation of MSTRG (Table and ). The expression of 50167.1 was related to ZNF (300, 322 and 445) in the CON/H01 and CON/H07 groups. In the CON/H07 group, MSTRG.46195.4 was related to ZNF827, and MSTRG.19891.1 was related to ZNF (12, 300, 592 and 800). HS-induced intestinal inflammation might be affected by lncRNA regulation in ZNF.
The expression of other lncRNAs was also found to be significantly increased. MSTRG.30039.11 (in CON/H01) targets GFPT1 (glutamine-fructose-6-phosphate transaminase 1); MSTRG.13202.5 (in CON/H01 and CON/H07) targets CD47; MSTRG.8273.18 (in CON/H07) targets UBE2O (ubiquitin-conjugating enzyme E2O); and MSTRG.34871.3 (in CON/H14) targets STAT2 (signal transducer and activator of transcription 2). The potential targets of these genes are associated with inflammation and cancer. GFPT1 is involved in a vital glucose metabolism pathway. Glucose metabolism remodeling is an essential hallmark of cancer. Research has shown that the lncRNA ELFN1-AS1 facilitates the progression of esophageal cancer (ESCA) by acting as a competing endogenous RNA (ceRNA) to promote GFPT1 expression by sequestering miR-183-3p [ , ]. In atherosclerosis, apoptotic cells are rapidly cleared by macrophages, which recognize phagocytic ‘eat me’ signals on apoptotic cells; however, this signaling can be blocked by the antiphagocytic CD47 molecule, resulting in constant necrotic cell accumulation in the necrotic core and aggravating the inflammatory response. The lncRNA MIAT acts as a miRNA sponge to modulate the expression of the antiphagocytic molecule CD47 positively by sequestering miR-149-5p [ ]. UBE2O is a large E2-ubiquitin conjugate that has both E2 and E3 activities, and UBE2O can promote BC cell proliferation and EMT via the UBE2O/AMPKα2/mTORC1-MYC positive feedback loop. The lncRNA HCG18 indirectly facilitates UBE2O expression by competitively adsorbing miR-103a-3p, thus activating the UBE2O/mTORC1 axis in BC cells. In addition, hypoxia-inducible factor 1α (HIF-1α), a downstream target of mTORC1, can transcriptionally promote HCG18 expression, forming a positive feedback loop in BC [ ]. STAT2 was discovered to be a key transcriptional activator of type I interferon (IFN) signaling, and the associated gene products are immunomodulatory, proinflammatory, and anti-inflammatory. Recent research has shown that DSS stimulation disrupts intestinal barrier function by activating STAT2. Polysaccharide (RAMPtp)-Induced Expression of the novel lncRNA ITSN1-OT1 blocks the nuclear importation of phosphorylated STAT2 to protect intestinal barrier function furthermore, the lncRNA linc00513 promotes the IFN pathway by modulating the critical transcription factors STAT1 and STAT2 in systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) [ – ]. Along with further investigations, these results show that lncRNAs may be therapeutic targets for advanced disease.
There were certain limitations in this study. First, a power calculations did not be carried out to determine sufficiency of animal numbers. Second, the sense or antisense lncRNA did not be analysis and discussion, and lack of discussion on antisense lncRNA and mRNA correlation. Third, some pathways were enriched with only one or two genes. Subsequent experiments will further verify these pathways and genes.

## Conclusions
The present study identified numerous DEGs and DELs in the CON/H01, CON/H07, and CON/H14 groups. Several of genes included KRT85, CLDN1, S100A12, TM7SF2, CCN1 and NR4A1 were significantly expressed, and they were found to be involved in inflammation and cancer. It was also found that some novel lncRNAs (MSTRG.47709.5, MSTRG.50167.1, MSTRG.28207.43, MSTRG.30039.11, MSTRG.13202.5, MSTRG.8273.18 and MSTRG.34871.3) were significantly upregulated in HS-induced intestinal inflammation for pigs. They were may be related to the occurrence and development of inflammation. Further research is still essential to explain the molecular mechanism of dysregulated lncRNAs.
