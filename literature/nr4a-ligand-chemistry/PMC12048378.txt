This study aims to address the diagnostic and therapeutic challenges of bladder cancer (BC) by using Mendelian randomization (MR) with a large sample size from multiple centers to identify the plasma proteins which are causally related to the pathogenesis of BC. Followed by merging nine plasma protein datasets from six studies, a total of 5538 plasma proteins and three BC datasets (ieu-b-4874, ukb-b-8193, FinnGen_R11_C3_ BLADDER_EXALL) were used to perform proteome‑wide MR to estimate the contribution of plasma proteins to BC, separately. To ensure the robustness of the results, Veen intersection operation on MR results revealed that 14 meaningful candidate pathogenic plasma proteins (ANKRD27, BIN1, FAHD1, IL17RB, MRPL21, PPT1, PSCA, SLC16A3, SLURP1, SPON2, TACSTD2, TMEM87B, YWHAB) were obtain from three datasets. Then, we validated these proteins through various methods, including meta-analysis, reverse MR, Bayesian co-localization analysis and summary-data-based MR (SMR), and pathogenic plasma proteins were divided into three layers according to the validation confidence. We then performed single-cell transcriptome analysis (Registration number: GSE222315), which showed that 13/14 candidate plasma proteins were expressed and 12 proteins were differentially expressed in at least one cell type. Finally, protein–protein interactions (PPI) analysis and druggability evaluation were performed to explore the relationship between the interaction of plasma protein markers and existing cancer drug targets. Summarily, our research uncovered 14 plasma protein biomarkers linked to BC risk, offering novel perspectives on the etiology and potential targets for developing screening biomarkers and therapeutic drugs for BC.

## Introduction
Cancer is a major social, public health, and economic problem in the twenty-first century. According to recent statistics, bladder cancer (BC) was the ninth most common cancer worldwide with 613,791 new cases and the thirteenth leading cause of cancer-related deaths with 220,349 fatalities in 2022 [ , ]. The incidence and mortality rates continue to rise globally [ , ]. BC poses a considerable clinical challenge due to the lack of early specific clinical symptoms and the high recurrence rate [ ]. In recent years, targeted therapy and immunotherapy have made some progress, but the prognosis of BC patients is still not ideal, mainly due to the difficulty of early diagnosis and the lack of effective therapeutic targets [ , ]. Therefore, there is an urgent need for additional evidence on non-invasive biomarkers for early BC diagnosis and the identification of new therapeutic targets [ ].
For a long time, a large number of studies have focused on the identification of urinary and blood markers of BC, but their specificity and sensitivity have been insufficient for clinical application due to the influence of too many confounding factors [ – ]. In this context, plasma proteins that leakage or excretory in the blood as a result of hyperactive metabolism of tumor tissue or precancerous lesions, provide a window for such exploratory [ ] and serve as a reliable source of biomarkers and proprietary drug targets [ , ]. Plasma proteins can mediate signal transduction, transport, repair, growth, and immune defense, and also serve as biomarkers for various cancers, facilitating early diagnosis, prognostic assessment, and treatment monitoring [ , ]. Currently, several studies have shown that several plasma proteins are associated with BC risk [ , ]. However, these studies had only a small number of proteins, small sample sizes, and single analysis methods, which limited the opportunity to gain insight into the causal role of proteins in BC risk.
Mendelian randomization (MR) analysis based on multi-omics data is an emerging tool that integrates data from different biological fields and facilitates cross-validation between different omics, which can provide strong evidence for the discovery of potential biomarkers and therapeutic targets. In recent years, multiple large-scale proteomic studies have uncovered protein quantitative trait locus (pQTLs) data for over 5000 plasma proteins [ – ], providing a valuable data resource for systematically elucidating the causal effects between plasma proteins and BC risk. The analysis of plasma protein pQTLs combined with BC genome-wide association studies (GWAS) data can bridge the gap between genomic and phenotypic information [ ], allowing precise identification of pathogenic plasma proteins associated with BC and their associated genetic variants, thereby revealing the genetic mechanisms of gene expression regulation. There have been studies demonstrating that MR analysis of plasma proteomics provides important insights in etiological studies and drug target prioritization in a wide range of diseases such as Alzheimer’s disease [ ], IgA nephropathy [ ], idiopathic fibrosis [ ], and a variety of neoplasms [ – ].
Therefore, MR analysis based on multi-omics data provides a methodology for exploring plasma protein markers of BC. In this study, we systematically integrated human plasma proteomics and genomics data from six studies comprising nine datasets to conduct a proteome-wide MR analysis, identifying 14 plasma protein biomarkers associated with BC risk. Additionally, we analyzed the expression patterns of the corresponding protein-coding genes in specific cell types. Finally, we assessed protein–protein interactions (PPIs) and potential drug targets to provide novel insights into BC screening biomarkers and therapeutic targets.

## Methods

## Study design
The overall design of this study is shown in Fig.  . In the discovery stage, nine large-scale proteomic data sets from six studies were included and integrated, and then the data was used for MR analysis with the three BC datasets. Through intersection operation and meta-analysis, potential pathogenic plasma proteins of BC were identified. In the validation phase, the BC-associated plasma proteins were supported by Bayesian co-localization analysis, SMR and HEIDI test. Subsequently, single-cell RNA-seq analysis of BC tissue was used to examine the expression changes of candidate pathogenic plasma proteins in specific cell types. Finally, the therapeutic potential of candidate proteins was explored by PPI and drug target evaluation. 

## Study population and datasets
In this study, we included data from three large-scale studies of BC datasets: ieu-b-4874, ukb-b-8193 from the MRC IEU openGWAS database (https://gwas.mrcieu.ac.uk/) [ , ]; FinnGen_R11_C3_ BLADDER_EXALL from the FinnGen database (https://www.finngen.fi/en) [ ]. Of these, the ieu-b-4874 dataset has 1279 BC patients and 372,016 normal controls of European ancestry; the ukb-b-8193 data from the UKBB [ ] database, stored in the openGWAS database, has 1101 cancer cases and 461,832 normal controls of European ancestry; FinnGen_ R11_C3_BLADDER_EXALL dataset contains 2574 cases and 345,118 controls of European ancestry [ ]. Detailed information on populations, year, number of cases and controls, genotyping platform, and other information can be found in Supplementary file 1: Table S1.

## Proteomic datasets and genetic instruments selection
In the discovery stage, summary data on genetic associations of exposure factors were obtained from nine different large-scale plasma proteomic datasets of six studies (Pietzner (4775 proteins); Ferkingstad (4719 proteins); Sun_1 (2995 proteins); Sun_2 (2995 proteins); Suhre (1224 proteins); Eldjarn_iceland (4274 proteins); Eldjarn_AF (1228 proteins); Eldjarn_SA (622 proteins); Eldjarn_BI (2305 proteins)) [ – ]. These studies have collectively measured genetic data for thousands of plasma proteins using different platforms such as SomaLogic, Olink, and SomaScan V4, as detailed in Supplementary file 1: Table S2. The proteomic data were harmonized by remapping protein IDs to corresponding gene symbols, ensuring uniform processing and gene annotation. Instrumental variables were then selected using the following criteria: exclusion of SNPs within the MHC region (25.5–34.0 Mb); The ratio of variability (R2) for duplicate SNPs was calculated and the SNPs with the max R2 were selected; removal of chained unbalanced SNPs using LD clustering, (r2 threshold = 0.01, Window size = 1 Mb, P threshold = 5 × 10–8); Remove heterogeneous IVs (Radial MR P < 0.05); Remove weak IVs (F-statistic < 10); Each SNP was tested using PhenoScanner to exclude the influence of confounding factors. Finally, 24,091 pQTLs for 5538 plasma proteins (2774 cis-pQTLs, 21,317 trans-pQTLs) were selected for this study (Supplementary file 1: Table S3).

## Proteome-wide MR and reverse MR analysis
The pQTLs data for 5538 plasma proteins, which were collated after the above steps, were used as genetic instrumental variables. The BC datasets from three large-scale studies, ieu-b-4874, ukb-b-8193, and FinnGen_R11_C3_BLADDER_EXALL, were used as the outcome variables, respectively. The “TwoSampleMR” package [ ] was used for proteome-wide MR analysis and the Steiger test was used for orientation verification and selection of positive SNPs. For proteins with only one SNP, the Wald ratio method was used to estimate the change in BC risk with a one-standard deviation increase in protein level; for proteins with SNPs, the inverse variance weighting (IVW) method was used to estimate the MR effect. Cochran’Q testing was used to assess the heterogeneity of the genetic instrumental variables [ ], and the MR-Egger intercept was used as an indicator to evaluate directional pleiotropy. The intersection Veen operation was performed on the results of the MR analyses, and the plasma proteins with P < 0.05 among the three results were selected as candidate plasma protein markers and drug targets. Meanwhile, we performed meta-analysis of the three outcomes for each candidate plasma protein, combining the OR effects. Additionally, reverse MR was used to assess whether there was a reverse causal relationship between the candidate proteins and BC. In reverse MR, the BC dataset ieu-b-4874 was used as the exposure file, whereas the cis-eQTLs data of candidate plasma proteins were obtained as the outcome file by downloading from the eQTLGen database (Supplementary file1: Table S2). A wider P-value threshold of 5E−4 was used as a criterion for pQTLs screening, and other steps were consistent with proteome-wide MR.

## Colocalization analysis
Colocalization analyses assume that there is only one causal SNP for the exposing factor and outcome, which excludes the confounding effects and linkage disequilibrium. To evaluate whether the relationship between protein expression and BC risk were caused by a shared causal SNP, we analyzed plasma proteins significantly associated with BC by Bayesian co-localization and passed all sensitivity tests via the “coloc” R package [ ]. And BC dataset ieu-b-4874 and candidate plasma proteins corresponding to cis-eQTLs data were used for co-localization analysis. For each protein, we chose to include cis-eQTLs within ± 500 kb of the genome encoding the protein. When a protein corresponded to more than one cis-eQTLs, colocalization analysis was performed separately for each cis-eQTL, and the cis-eQTL with the highest significance of colocalization was identified for label. Consistent with previous studies, the default parameters for colocalization were used, namely P1 = 1 × 10−4 (prior probability of SNP association with corresponding plasma protein), P2 = 1 × 10−4 (prior probability of SNP association with BC), and P12 = 1 × 10−5 (prior probability of SNP association with protein and BC) [ , ]. Posterior probabilities were evaluated for every locus (SNP) to obtain five hypotheses (H0-H4). Since H4 means a shared causal SNP for BC and plasma proteins, posterior probability for hypothesis H4 exceeding 80% is considered strong evidence of colocalization. Finally, we used the “LocusCompare” R package [ ] for visualizing the regional results of co-localization.

## SMR analysis and HEIDI test
SMR analysis provides evidence for causal inference by using summary data to assess the relationship between genetic variation and disease risk, and SMR analysis is used to verify the causal relationship between plasma proteins and BC in this study [ ]. The HEIDI test was used to distinguish associations between proteins and BC due to genetic covariance rather than causality [ , ]. In order to be consistent with the discovery phase, pQTLs of protein-coding genes in whole blood plasma proteins were obtained from the GTEx database (https://www.gtexportal.org/) for subsequent analysis for 10 of the 14 identified potential therapeutic targets (Supplementary file 1: Table S2). SMR software (SMR v1.3.1) was used for SMR analysis and HEIDI test [ ]. P < 0.05 was the cut-off value for SMR, and could be considered a causal relationship. In contrast, the P > 0.05 of the HEIDI tests indicated that the correlations of the plasma markers with BC was not driven by a linkage imbalance of SNPS. After identifying the plasma markers of BC, we conducted a wide review of the literature related to these plasma proteins, and identified the proteins not reported with BC as new plasma protein markers for BC.

## Single-cell RNA-seq analysis
To further validate the differential expression of plasma protein markers in BC tissues and adjacent tissues, we downloaded a single-cell RNA sequencing data set from the GEO database (Registration number: GSE222315) [ ] to evaluate the enriched expression of candidate plasma proteins in specific cell types in BC tumor tissues. We first performed data preprocessing and merging of raw single-cell RNA-seq data using the “Seurat” software package [ ]. The processing steps included: (1) selecting genes that should be expressed in at least three cells; (2) Select cells with more than 200 genes and less than 8000 genes detected; (3) Less than 25% of the cells with excessive mitochondrial content were removed, and (4) 2000 highly variable genes were selected. The RNA TPM was then normalized and scaled using NormalizeData and ScaleData functions. After determining that there was no obvious mixed intersection between cell groups when dims = 10 and resultions = 0.1, we comprehensive searched for markers of different cells to classified and labeled. To test whether the expression of identified genes encoding proteins that are pathogenic for BC is altered in specific cell types of BC tumor tissue, we performed the differential analysis based on the Wilcoxon rank-sum test to compare the gene expression levels of plasma protein markers between each cell type and other cell types.
We performed the differential analysis based on the Wilcoxon rank-sum test to compare the gene expression levels of plasma protein markers between each cell type and other cell types. Genes with | (Log2 FC (fold change)) |> 0.5 and P < 0.05 were identified as differentially expressed genes in a cell type. Furthermore, the expression differences between candidate plasma proteins and different cells of BC were demonstrated.

## PPI analysis and druggability assessment
To explore the interactions of 14 plasma protein markers and their possible associations, we constructed a PPI network via the STRING database (https://string-db.org/) with an interaction confidence level of 0.4. And then, by using DGIdb (https://www.dgidb.org/) [ ], ChEMBL (https://www.ebi.ac.uk/chembl/)) [ ], and DrugBank (The database https://go.drugbank.com/) [ ] was searched for protein-drug interactions. We further assessed whether the plasma markers could serve as potential drug targets. To achieve this, we used a database to retrieve drug information related to the plasma markers and collated the main information from the above three databases. Drug names and information on the process of drug development against the identified proteins were recorded.

## Results

## Proteome‑wide MR analysis identified 14 plasma protein biomarkers for BC
Proteome‑wide MR Analysis was performed on the pretreated 5538 plasma proteins from the nine data sets (exposure) with the BC data sets from three different sources (outcomes), and the results are shown in supplementary file 1: Table S4-6. Of the results, 170 BC pathogenic proteins were identified in the ieu-b-4874 dataset (Fig.  a); 197 pathogenic plasma proteins were identified in the FinnGen dataset (Fig.  b); and 144 pathogenic plasma proteins were identified in the ukb-d-C67 dataset (Fig.  c). Finally, the results of the three data sets were intersected, and a total of 14 candidate BC pathogenic plasma proteins were obtained (Fig.  d). The results of MR analysis of these 14 candidate pathogenic plasma proteins are shown in supplementary file 1: Table S7. Among them, elevated levels of nine proteins (ANKRD27, IL17RB, MRPL21, PPT1, PSCA, SLC16A3, SLURP1, TACSTD2 and TNFRSF19) and decreased levels of 5 proteins (BIN1, FAHD1, SPON2, TMEM87B, YWHAB) were associated with BC risk increase. These associations were generally consistent across various analytical approaches, such as weighted models, MR-Egger and weighted medians. These associations were generally consistent across, including weighted models, except for the simple model. Additionally, these proteins exhibited neither significant heterogeneity nor pleiotropy (Cochran’Q_P value > 0.05, pleiotropy P value > 0.05). 
Then, we conducted a meta-analysis of the MR results for the 14 identified BC pathogenic plasma proteins, and presented the OR values and 95% confidence intervals (95% CI) in the form of forest plots (Fig.  ). In the meta-analysis of these three MR results, all 14 proteins demonstrated significant associations. The odds ratios (95% CI) for BC per standard deviation increase in protein levels were 1.040 (1.018–1.062) for ANKRD27, 0.970 (0.956–0.983) for BIN1, 0.983 (0.972–0.993) for FAHD1, 1.005 (1.002–1.008) for IL17RB, 1.008 (1.004–1.013) for MRPL21, 1.020 (1.008–1.032) for PPT1, 1.008 (1.006–1.009) for PSCA, 1.046 (1.021–1.070) for SLC16A3, 1.015 (1.009–1.021) for SLURP1, 0.986 (0.977–0.994) for SPON2, 1.005 (1.002–1.008) for TACSTD2, 0.994 (0.990–0.998) for TMEM87B, 1.015 (1.007–1.023) for TNFRSF19 and 0.990 (0.984–0.996) YWHAB (Fig.  ). These proteins were all strongly associated with BC risk in a consistent direction, that is, higher levels of ANKRD27, IL17RB, MRPL21, PPT1, PSCA, SLC16A3, SLURP1, TACSTD2 and TNFRSF19 were associated with increased BC risk in all three outcomes. The other 5 proteins (BIN1, FAHD1, SPON2, TMEM87B, YWHAB) were negatively associated with BC risk. 

## Reverse MR and colocalization analysis supported the causality of MR results
Although we performed Steiger filtering during proteome‑wide MR, there could still be potential causal inversion, so we performed reverse MR for directionality testing. We performed a reverse MR analysis using ieu-b-4874 (exposure) and cis-eQTLs of candidate plasma pathogenic proteins from the eQTLGen database (outcome). Among them, PSCA and SLURP1 were not analyzed due to the lack of relevant data, and the remaining 12 plasma proteins (ANKRD27, BIN1, FAHD1, IL17RB, MRPL21, PPT1, SLC16A3, SPON2, TACSTD2, TMEM87B, TNFRSF19 and YWHAB) are shown in supplementary file 1: Table S8. All P values were greater than 0.05, which did not show reverse causality.
The same data for reverse MR were used for colocalization analysis, so the proteins PSCA and SLURP1 lacked complete summary data for the colocalization analysis. All other 12 proteins (ANKRD27, BIN1, FAHD1, IL17RB, MRPL21, PPT1, SLC16A3, SPON2, TACSTD2, TMEM87B and TNFRSF19) were supported by colocalization analysis with a PP4 score greater than 80% (Table  ), and detailed data are shown in supplementary file 1: Table S9. This suggests that there is most likely a common causal variation between candidate plasma protein cis-eQTLs and BC risk. Specifically, at least one SNP was highly associated between each plasma protein and BC (supplementary file 2: Figures S1-S12). It can be concluded that these highly correlated SNPs are the common cause of the corresponding plasma proteins and BC, or it can be considered that SNPs affect the biological process of BC by affecting the expression of plasma proteins. 

## SMR analysis and HEIDI tests
To further validate the MR results and to make the results consistent with the discovery phase, we selected the SNPs of whole blood proteins from GTEx database as the exposure files for SMR analysis. Among them, PSCA, SLURP1, SPON2, and TNFRSF19 lacked relevant data and could not be analyzed by SMR. SMR and HEIDI tests were performed on the remaining 10 proteins (ANKRD27, BIN1, FAHD1, IL17RB, MRPL21, PPT1, SLC16A3, TACSTD2 and TMEM87B) with complete summary level data. Of them, four proteins (ANKRD27, FAHD1, MRPL21, and TACSTD2) passed the SMR test (P < 0.05); Nine proteins (ANKRD27, BIN1, FAHD1, IL17RB, MRPL21, PPT1, SLC16A3, TACSTD2 and TMEM87B) passed HEIDI test (P > 0.05) (Table  , Supplementary file 1: Table S10). SMR site maps and HEIDI effect maps for the 10 proteins are shown in supplementary file 2: Figures S13-S22. The locus map showed that these 10 proteins were all associated with BC risk. Additionally, among the 9 proteins tested by HEIDI, elevated ANKRD27, IL17RB, MRPL21, PPT1 and TACSTD2 were linked to a higher risk of BC; reduced TMEM87B, YWHAB, BIN1, and FAHD1 are associated with an increased risk of BC. This is consistent with the direction of effect of our discovery phase proteins on BC.
According to the evidence presented, we categorized these plasma proteins into three layers. Four proteins (ANKRD27, FAHD1, MRPL21, and TACSTD2) successfully met all criteria and were designated as Tier 1. Eight proteins (ANKRD27, BIN1, FAHD1, IL17RB, PPT1, PSCA, SLC16A3, SLURP1, SPON2, TACSTD2, TMEM87B, TNFRSF19, and YWHAB) were classified as Tier 2 with one or two of colocalization/SMR/HEIDI analysis failed or lack of data. Two proteins (PSCA, SLURP1) failed in all three tests and were classified as Tier 3. Of course, stratification is mainly to show the reliability of the results, and most of the data that did not pass the validation in this study were mainly due to the lack of validation data, which cannot completely exclude the proteins that failed the test. Additionally, five proteins (ANKRD27, BIN1, MRPL21, SLURP1, and TMEM87B) that had not previously been linked to BC at gene polymorphism, mRNA, or protein level were identified as novel markers.

## Cell‑type specificity expression in the BC tissue
To explore the difference of expression of 14 pathogenic plasma proteins in specific cells of bladder cancer tissue and normal tissue, we conducted a single-cell RNA-seq analysis using public database data. The cells were clustered into nine groups and further categorized into eight cell types (epithelial, B cell, macrophage, monocyte, fibroblast, tissue stem cell, T cell, and endothelial cell) (Fig.  a) based on the specific markers for each cell type (Table  , Supplementary file 1: Table S11, Supplementary file 2: Fig. 23a-d). Thirteen of the 14 protein-coding genes had expression data in BC tissues except for SLURP1. Figure  b illustrates the single-cell expression of these 13 proteins across each cell type. Figure  c shows the differences in the specific enrichment of 13 protein-coding genes in different cell types in bladder tumor tissues versus normal tissues. Figure  d shows 12 genes with average differences in at least one cell type (Log2FC > 0.5, P < 0.05). Among the 13 genes expressed by single-cell analysis (Table  , Supplementary file 1: Table S12), PSCA was highly expressed in tissue stem cells, endothelial cells, fibroblasts, macrophages and T cells, and was also higher in tumor tissues than in adjacent tissues. TACSTD2 was enriched in tissue stem cells, fibroblasts and macrophages. TNFRSF19 was highly expressed in epithelial cells, while MRPL21 and ANKRD27 were mainly enriched in epithelial cells. On the contrary, BIN1 and SPON2 were down-regulated in a variety of cells. In conclusion, the expression of pathogenic plasma proteins was changed in BC tissues. Additionally, for genes and pathogenic plasma proteins enriched in cells of each tumor tissue, the expression differences between cancer and paracancerous can be seen in Supplementary file 2: Fig. 23e-g. Among the 9 proteins with elevated gene expression associated with increased risk of BC, except for SLURP1, the expression of other 8 proteins were increased in tumor tissues. And for the 5 proteins with decreased gene expression associated with increased risk of BC, the expression of BIN1, FAHD1, SPON2, and YWHAB was decreased in tumor tissues, except for TMEM87B. 

## Protein–Protein interactions and druggability for potential therapeutic targets
PPI analysis showed that there were limited interactions among the identified potential causal proteins, only YWHAB was identified with BIN1 and ANKRD27; PSCA was associated with TACSTD2 and SLURP1; MRPL21 and PPT1; and the interaction between IL17RB and TNFRSF19 (Supplementary file 2: Figure S24). Of which, they are involved in the regulation of endocytosis and transport; regulation of cell proliferation and immunity; functional interaction between mitochondria and lysosomes; and immune regulation, respectively. In the drug evaluation, we found that seven of all 14 proteins (BIN1, IL17RB, PPT1, PSCA, SLC16A3, TACSTD2, and TNFRSF19) have become drug targets (Supplementary file 1: Table S13). There are many drugs that target BIN1 or affect BIN1 expression. Among the identified drugs, docetaxel monotherapy is used to treat locally advanced or metastatic breast cancer after chemotherapy failure. Doxorubicin and cyclophosphamide are employed as adjuvant therapies for operable node-positive breast cancer. Asciminib is indicated for treating adult patients with Philadelphia chromosome-positive chronic myeloid leukemia (Ph + CML) in the chronic phase who have previously received more than two tyrosine kinase inhibitors. Additionally, BIN1 has been associated with tumors, attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) in children, and oral contraceptives for pregnancy prevention. Tetrodotoxin, a drug targeting PPT1, is used for managing chronic and breakthrough pain in advanced cancer patients, as well as for treating opioid dependence. Podofilox For treatment of external genital warts. MK-4721 targeting PSCA was investigated for treatment in prostate cancer. Streptozocin targeting SLC16A3 have been developed to treat of malignant neoplasms of pancreas. Sacituzumab, targeting TACSTD2, is indicated by the FDA, Health Canada, and EMA for adult patients with unresectable locally advanced or metastatic triple-negative breast cancer who have undergone two or more prior therapies for metastatic disease. Linvoseltamab targeting TNFRSF19 is investigating and no related studies.

## Discussion
Extensive research has explored urinary and blood biomarkers for BC, yet their clinical utility remains limited by inadequate specificity and sensitivity attributed to confounding factors [ , ].In this study, we conducted a comprehensive exploration and analysis of the causal relationship between 5538 plasma proteins and BC risk. Fourteen protein markers were identified by proteome-wide MR Analysis. Among them, nine proteins (ANKRD27, IL17RB, MRPL21, PPT1, PSCA, SLC16A3, SLURP1, TACSTD2 and TNFRSF19) were associated with BC risk increase, while the other 5 proteins (BIN1, FAHD1, SPON2, TMEM87B, YWHAB) were negatively associated with BC risk. Bayesian co-localization highlighted the causal effects of 10 protein biomarkers, 9 of which were validated by SMR and HEIDI tests. Overall, we found 3 proteins (ANKRD27, FAHD1, and TACSTD2) with the most convincing evidence (layer 1), 9 proteins (BIN1, IL17RB, MRPL21, PPT1, SLC16A3, SPON2, TMEM87B, TNFRSF19, YWHAB) with convincing evidence (layer 2) and 2 proteins (PSCA, SLURP1) with less convincing evidence (layer 3). Among these identified markers, six proteins (ANKRD27, BIN1, MRPL21, SLURP1, SPON2, and TMEM87B) were novel plasma protein markers associated with BC. We further validated the differential expression of the genes encoding the identified plasma protein markers in multiple cell subsets. Seven of these proteins (BIN1, IL17RB, PPT1, PSCA, SLC16A3, TACSTD2, and TNFRSF19) have been found to be the targets of drug development, and the drugs based on these targets may be re-used as the treatment of BC.
Our analysis identified 9 candidate plasma protein markers with previously reported evidence of association with BC based on the evidences from earlier genetic or experimental studies, including FAHD1, IL-17RB, PPT1, PSCA, SLC16A3, SPON2, TACSTD2, TNFRSF19, and YWHAB. Two of them (FAHD1, TACSTD2) were prioritized as the most convincing evidence (Tier 1). A genome-wide meta-analysis by Galesloot TE et al. identified FAHD1 as associated with non-muscle invasive BC recurrence and progression [ ]. Altered expression of IL-17 cytokine family ligands and receptors was associated with infiltration of inflammatory cells, and immunoreactivity of IL-17RB as well as infiltrating neutrophils was reduced in BC compared to patients with cystitis (p < 0.05) [ ]. Genetic polymorphisms in PSCA are strongly associated with BC risk [ – ], with rs2294008 (C/T) in particular being more prominent in Asians [ , ]. Additionally, both mRNA and protein expression of PSCA is increased in BC, and this alteration increases and promotes the progression of BC [ , ]. The mRNA level of SLC16A3 is up-regulated in BC, and BC patients with high SLC16A3 mRNA expression have poor OS [ ]. As a complement to the mechanism, Chengjun Li et al. found that SLC16A3 may affect the immune infiltration of BC by regulating BC metabolism and m6A methylation, ultimately leading to the progression of BC [ ]. SPON2 is a member of the extracellular matrix protein family that has been validated as a broad-spectrum tumor marker for various tumors (primarily prostate cancer) and a variety of non-tumorigenic diseases [ ]. And in BC, SPON2 is closely associated with immunity and long-term prognosis [ ]. Human trophoblast cell surface antigen 2 (TROP2) belongs to the tumor-associated calcium signaling gene family and is a metabolite of TACSTD2, and TACSTD2/TROP2 is widely expressed in advanced urothelial carcinoma, independent of FGFR3 alterations or PD-L1 expression, and thus is a suitable target for ADC therapy in most advanced urothelial carcinomas [ – ]. In patients undergoing radical cystectomy, TNFRSF19 expression is associated with pathological stage and poor prognosis, suggesting that TNFRSF19 may serve as a novel molecular marker to aid in identifying and selecting patients for this procedure [ ]. Ching-Chyuan Su et al. performed LC–MS/MS analysis of metastatic BC in women and showed that PPT1 is a novel biomarker for BC [ ]. In the study by Nord H et al., a comprehensive analysis of DNA copy number changes and gene expression also identified YWHAB as a novel marker associated with high grade and recurrence of Ta stage BC [ ]. In conclusion, most of the pathogenic plasma proteins identified in this study are related to the diagnosis and prognosis of BC, and they are important targets to guide the treatment of BC.
Importantly, we also identified five novel BC candidate proteins biomarker, including ANKRD27, BIN1, MRPL21, SLURP1, and TMEM87B. Among them, ANKRD27 and MRPL21 were the most compelling evidence (tier 1).Of these proteins, ANKRD27 was originally identified as a protein containing an N-terminal VPS9 (vacuolar protein sorting 9) domain with guanine nucleotide exchange factor activity against the small GTPase Rab21, and was identified to be significantly associated with OS in BC patients [ ]. BIN1 is a tumor suppressor that binds to and inhibits the oncogenic MYC transcription factor, thereby regulating tumor progression and a series of signals in the microenvironment to regulate cancer development [ ]. BIN1 has gradually become an early diagnostic or prognostic marker for a variety of cancers and inflammatory diseases [ , ]. MRPL21 is upregulated in rectal cancer, esophageal squamous cell carcinoma and AML [ – ]. Mechanistically, MRPL21 promotes tumor cell proliferation and progression through TP53 mutation-induced apoptotic resistance [ ]. SLURP1 has been implicated in pancreatic cancer, prostate cancer, and esophageal squamous cell carcinoma and is a potential biomarker [ – ]. Nicotine intake leads to decreased expression of SLURP1, which functions as tumor suppressor [ , ]. According to bioinformatics analysis, the new gene TMEM87B may interact with HPV18 E6 and may be a potential target for cervical carcinogenesis [ ]. In conclusion, although the roles of these proteins in BC have not been studied so far, they play important roles in tumors and are potential biomarkers for BC.
The strength of this study lies in the fact that we comprehensively investigated the relationship between 5538 plasma protein biomarkers and the tumorigenesis risk of BC. A large number of samples were included in both the discovery and validation phases, and a full range of calculations were performed. The design advantages of this study are its large sample size, wide proteome coverage, and strict control of confusion bias risk and reverse causality. Meanwhile, the consistency of the results of reverse MR, co-localization analysis, SMR and HEIDI tests indicated the robustness of screening plasma protein markers. This study also categorized candidate plasma proteins into 3 tiers based on the level of evidence of the study, and also identified versus new BC markers according to whether or not they had been reported in BC-related studies. The results of single-cell RNA-seq analysis, PPIs, and pharmacological evaluation provide insight into the expression and potential interactions of candidate proteins in BC tissues and further identify available drug targets. Although some proteins (e.g., ANKRD27 and FAHD1, etc.) lacked drug information, these proteins are still worthwhile as a promising new therapeutic target for BC.
However, we must also recognize some limitations of this study. First, the subjects of this study were limited to European population, so further research is needed to validate these findings before they can be generalized to other ethnic groups. Nonetheless, some plasma protein biomarkers have been reported to be associated with BC, suggesting that there may be some close link between genetics and protein expression. Secondly, the association between plasma protein levels and BC was investigated at the single-cell RNA-seq level but not at the protein level. In addition, due to the lack of summary data, some proteins, such as PSCA and SLURP1, were not supported by evidence from colocalization analysis, SMR and HEIDI tests, but this does not completely rule out the importance of these proteins. Finally, the instrumental variables associated with pathogenic plasma proteins may be influenced by factors other than genetics.
In conclusion, our study identified 14 plasma protein biomarkers associated with BC risk, offering new insights into the etiology and potential plasma biomarkers of bladder cancer, and also providing new targets for the development of therapeutic drugs for bladder cancer.
