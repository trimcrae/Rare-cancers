## Introduction
Peripheral artery disease (PAD) is a disorder characterized by the narrowing or occlusion of major arteries in the lower extremities. It is primarily caused by systemic atherosclerosis (AS). It affects approximately 20% of elderly individuals, and its prevalence is rising with the aging of the population and increasing rates of comorbidities, such as obesity, diabetes, and hypertension [ , ]. The main manifestation of PAD varies with ischemia severity. It ranges from early symptoms like coldness, pain, and intermittent claudication to advanced critical limb ischemia (CLI), which can cause ulcers, gangrene, and severe limb damage. CLI is particularly concerning, with over 50% of patients succumbing within five years of diagnosis, a high rate of amputation, and an elevated risk of major adverse cardiovascular events [ , ]. In patients with refractory PAD, invasive interventions, such as endovascular procedures or bypass operation, may be required. Despite these approaches, middle- and long-term outcomes remain suboptimal because of the multifactorial nature of PAD, which includes microvascular dysfunction. Moreover, postsurgical outcomes are concerning, with amputation rates and six-month mortality exceeding 20% in certain populations [ ]. Therefore, these challenges emphasize the importance of advancing our understanding of PAD pathophysiology and developing highly effective therapeutic strategies.
The core pathological mechanism of PAD is the lumen stenosis or occlusion of major arteries in the lower extremities. This situation subsequently leads to a chronic progressive ischemic and hypoxic condition in the lower leg and foot. Therefore, when discussing the therapeutic strategy for PAD, we need to pay attention simultaneously to the changes in the microenvironments of major arteries and distal limb tissues. For PAD in the early stage, the focus of treatment should be on the microenvironment of the plaques in the major artery, with the goal of suppressing and stabilizing them. However, once major arteries are occluded, attention should also be given to improve the microenvironment of the lower leg and foot to protect muscles and nerves against ischemia while continuing antiatherosclerotic therapy. Additionally, promoting neovascularization, specifically arteriogenesis in the proximal limb and angiogenesis in the distal limb, is crucial for restoring blood flow and preserving overall limb viability. Nonetheless, clinical trials have demonstrated that angiogenesis alone is insufficient to achieve consistent benefit in PAD/CLI, underscoring the need for integrative strategies that also address metabolic, immune, and stromal dysregulation.
In the setting of ischemia and hypoxia, profound alterations in cellular energy metabolism occur, resulting in the accumulation of lactate. It was first documented in 1979 that the surface pH of the gastrocnemius muscle in patients with CLI progressively decreases, reaching approximately 6.85 in gangrenous tissues [ ]. Subsequent metabolomic studies have further corroborated this finding, revealing remarkable lactate and alanine buildup in muscle samples from patients with CLI, alongside a marked reduction in the pyruvate-to-lactate ratio [ ]. These metabolic alterations highlight lactate accumulation as a hallmark of ischemic tissues in PAD. Beyond being a byproduct of anaerobic metabolism, lactate acts as an important signaling molecule in the ischemic microenvironment, triggering adaptive responses, such as neovascularization, to mitigate ischemic damage. Neovascularization, comprising angiogenesis, vasculogenesis, and arteriogenesis, is a critical compensatory mechanism in PAD and a key target for therapeutic development. Recent advances in metabolomics have deepened our understanding of lactate's role in promoting vascular repair, offering new insights into its potential as a therapeutic modulator of neovascularization.
Lactylation has recently garnered considerable attention due to its novel function of protein posttranslational modification via the utilization of lactate as a substrate. Lactylation can directly regulate gene expression and play an important part in regulating life processes and cellular functions, such as energy metabolism, macrophage polarization, and tumor progression [ , , ]. Notably, lactylation has also been deeply implicated in the regulation of neovascularization [ ]. The discovery of lactylation has led scientists to recognize the important participation of lactate in regulating cell functions beyond its direct effects and is expected to provide new strategies and methods for treating PAD. Herein, we review the recent advances in the knowledge of lactate metabolism and lactylation modification associated with PAD, with a focus on the mechanisms underlying their effects on the regulation of the functional states of different vascular component cells, including endothelial cells (ECs), vascular smooth muscle cells (VSMCs), and macrophages.

## Lactate metabolism
2.1

## Lactate production
Lactate is predominantly produced through glycolysis and glutamine metabolism, with glycolysis being the primary pathway. In glycolysis, glucose is transported into cells via glucose transporters (GLUTs) and is subsequently metabolized into pyruvate through a series of enzymatic steps. This pathway involves three irreversible rate-limiting reactions, which are catalyzed by hexokinase (HK), phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK1), and pyruvate kinase (PK). Under hypoxic conditions or in cells performing aerobic glycolysis, such as ECs and astrocytes, pyruvate is converted into lactate by the enzyme lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) [ , ]. Glutamine metabolism is another source of lactate in cancer cells. Glutamine enters cells via alanine-serine-cysteine transporter 2 and is metabolized into glutamate and α-ketoglutarate. The latter enters the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, where it contributes to the production of key intermediates, such as oxaloacetate, malate, nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD) phosphate (NADPH), and pyruvate. Pyruvate is then converted into lactate, completing the metabolic pathway [ ].
Lactate degradation occurs through two main pathways. First, lactate produced in cells can be oxidized back into pyruvate, which then enters mitochondria. Here, it is irreversibly converted into acetyl-CoA by pyruvate dehydrogenase, entering the TCA cycle to provide energy. Alternatively, lactate released into the bloodstream can be transported to the liver and kidneys, where it is converted into glucose via gluconeogenesis. Additionally, lactate produced by skeletal muscle is released into the bloodstream and similarly transported to the liver, where it is reconverted into glucose. This process, known as the Cori or lactic acid cycle, allows lactate to be recycled into glucose and returned to the circulation [ ]. 2.2

## Lactate transport
Lactate shuttling refers to the transport of lactate in cells or between cells and various tissues and organs. Intracellular lactate shuttling primarily occurs between the cytoplasm and mitochondria, as well as between the cytoplasm and peroxisomes. Intercellular lactate transport is frequently observed between glycolytic and oxidative cells. Additionally, lactate transfer among various organs, including skeletal muscle, the heart, the brain, the liver, and kidneys, plays a crucial role in maintaining lactate homeostasis. This dynamic shuttling enables lactate to serve multiple biological functions, including acting as an energy substrate, a gluconeogenic precursor, and a signaling molecule [ , ].
The transmembrane transport of lactate is mediated by monocarboxylate transporters (MCTs). MCT1 has a high affinity for lactate and is primarily responsible for the import of extracellular lactate into the cell. It allows extracellular lactate to enter the intracellular TCA cycle and supply energy. Under hypoxic conditions, MCT1 can also conversely export intracellular lactate to the extracellular region [ ]. Therefore, MCT1-mediated lactate transport is bidirectional, with the direction of transport determined by the lactate concentration gradient across the cell membrane.
G protein-coupled receptor 81 (GPR81), also known as hydroxycarboxylic acid receptor 1, serves as a specific receptor for L-lactate and is widely expressed across various cell types. Research has shown that GPR81 plays an important role in regulating numerous physiological processes, including energy metabolism, angiogenesis, inflammatory responses, and lipolysis [ , , , ]. Given the diverse functions of GPR81, lactate likely contributes to the regulation of these processes through this receptor. However, the contribution of the lactate/GPR81 signaling axis to the pathology and pathophysiology of PAD remains poorly understood.

## Lactylation
3.1

## Discovery of lactylation
Lactylation was first described in 2019, when researchers identified histone lysine lactylation using liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS) techniques [ ]. Zhang et al. [ ] demonstrated that lactate can serve as a substrate for histone lysine residues. Under the influence of lactyltransferase, lactate is transferred to these residues, resulting in histone lactylation, a process referred to as enzymatic lactylation. Subsequently, Gaffney et al. [ ] uncovered an alternative mechanism of lactylation, in which the glycolytic byproduct methylglyoxal forms a conjugate with glutathione (GSH) via the enzyme glyoxalase 1 (GLO1). This reaction produces lactyl-GSH, which is then hydrolyzed by GLO2, releasing free GSH and D-lactate. Lactate is subsequently transferred to protein lysine residues, a process known as nonenzymatic lactylation [ , ].
While histones are the most studied targets, lactylation can also occur on nonhistone proteins. For instance, under hypoxic conditions with elevated lactate levels, the transcription factor Yin Yang 1 (YY1) is lactylated at lysine 183. This modification enhances YY1's ability to transactivate fibroblast growth factor 2 (FGF2), thereby promoting angiogenesis [ ]. Additionally, intracellular lactate facilitates the lactylation of apolipoprotein C2 (APOC2) at lysine 70, which in turn stimulates extracellular lipolysis. This mechanism results in the release of free fatty acids, accumulation of regulatory T cells, induction of resistance to immunotherapy, and promotion of metastasis [ ]. However, lactylation modifications are not confined to specific proteins or cellular locations. Remarkably, lactylation modifications are widely distributed in various cellular compartments, including the cell membrane, nucleus, cytoplasm, mitochondria, and endoplasmic reticulum, and affect multiple biological functions [ ].

3.2

## Regulation of lactylation
Lactylation is a reversible and dynamic posttranslational modification regulated by writers (modifying enzymes), erasers (demodifying enzymes), and readers (modification-binding proteins). Writers are enzymes responsible for adding lactyl groups to target proteins, whereas erasers remove these modifications, returning the proteins to their unmodified state. Reader proteins specifically recognize and bind to lactylated residues, thereby influencing gene expression and various cellular processes. Together, these three classes of proteins cooperate to regulate the dynamic modulation of lactylation. Recent research has predominantly focused on the writers and erasers involved in lactylation. Known writers include p300/CREB-binding protein (CBP), general control nondepressible 5 (GCN5), histone acetyltransferase 1 (HBO1), lysine acetyltransferase 8 (KAT8), and mitochondrial alanyl-transfer RNA (tRNA) synthetase (AARS2) [ , , , , ]. Histone deacetylases 1–3 (HDAC 1–3) and sirtuins 1–3 (SIRT 1–3) are proteins that have been demonstrated to function as erasers [ ]. Brahma-related gene 1 (Brg1), a newly discovered histone lactylation reader, has just recently been reported to play a key role in the reprogramming of induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) [ ]. Notably, these lactylation-related enzymes are also involved in catalyzing other types of acylation modifications, such as acetylation and butyrylation. Therefore, further investigation is needed to determine whether competition or crosstalk exists between these modifications.

## Hypoxia, metabolic dysregulation, and lactate accumulation in PAD: clinical and experimental evidence
PAD is fundamentally characterized by complex arterial pathologies that result in vascular stenosis or occlusion, ultimately leading to distal limb ischemia and hypoxia. These pathophysiological changes manifest clinically as intermittent claudication, rest pain, and CLI in advanced cases. Although the full spectrum of PAD pathogenesis remains incompletely understood, vascular lesions and ischemic myopathy have emerged as central contributors. The pathological features of vascular lesions in PAD include pathological intimal thickening, fibroatheroma, fibrocalcific lesions, restenosis, chronic luminal thrombi, and medial calcifications [ ]. These lesions severely compromise vascular function, reduce perfusion, and induce tissue ischemia and oxygen deprivation in the distal limb. Skeletal muscle, which is composed of myocytes, adipocytes, fibroblasts, neural cells, and stromal vascular components, is a primary tissue affected by PAD. Among the above cells, myocytes are the most susceptible to ischemia and hypoxia, playing a pivotal role in altering the local microenvironment and ultimately determining limb function. Several studies have demonstrated that patients with PAD exhibit reduced calf muscle mass, increased intramuscular fat infiltration, and fibrosis. Muscle biopsies have further revealed mitochondrial dysfunction, which correlates strongly with functional impairment, mobility loss, and increased mortality [ , ]. Mitochondria are the powerhouse of the cell, and their dysfunction leads to a cellular energy crisis, triggering metabolic disturbances and biochemical imbalances that influence cell survival under ischemic conditions [ ]. The role of lactate in skeletal muscle under physiological and pathological conditions has been thoroughly reviewed by Bartoloni et al. [ ] and is therefore not the primary focus here. Instead, this section highlights the broad metabolic abnormalities in PAD, particularly those involving lactate metabolism under ischemic stress.
Direct evidence of tissue hypoxia in PAD includes decreased skin temperature and transcutaneous oxygen tension (TcPO2). In patients with CLI, skin temperatures have been reported to be as low as 27.4 °C (24.0–31.1 °C) [ ]. Moreover, TcPO2 levels are predictive of ulcer healing: levels below 20 mmHg are associated with poor healing and a high risk of amputation, whereas TcPO2 levels above 40 mmHg are correlated with complete healing [ ]. Another indicator of anaerobic metabolism is tissue pH. Early studies have demonstrated that muscle pH progressively decreases with disease severity, from 7.27 ± 0.4 in patients with claudication to 7.17 ± 0.02 in those with rest pain, and to as low as 6.84 ± 0.03 in those with ischemic gangrene [ ]. Studies using hindlimb ischemia (HLI) animal models have also confirmed an increase in anaerobic metabolism following arterial occlusion, demonstrating remarkable reductions in intracellular and extracellular pH (intracellular pH (pHi): from 7.00 ± 0.03 to 6.60 ± 0.05; extracellular pH (pHe): from 7.30 ± 0.01 to 6.36 ± 0.05), and a six-fold increase in lactate concentration compared with preischemic levels [ ].
Lactate metabolism in PAD has also been assessed by using microdialysis techniques. Lactate levels in ischemic limbs in patients with CLI undergoing successful revascularization were found to be significantly elevated before surgery compared with those in controls (mean, 3.68 ± 1.61 mmol/L vs. 1.54 ± 1.36 mmol/L, P = 0.01). Within 24 h postrevascularization, lactate levels initially spiked (6.58 ± 1.56 mmol/L, P = 0.002) but subsequently declined to control levels (1.71 ± 1.69 mmol/L). By contrast, patients who ultimately required major amputation experienced a transient decrease in lactate immediately postprocedure (<1.74 mmol/L) that was followed by a return to ischemic preoperative levels within 12 h [ ]. Lundberg et al. [ ] further demonstrated that in patients with CLI, muscle lactate levels were higher during limb elevation, a maneuver that reduces blood flow, than when the limbs were in the horizontal position (3.3 mmol/L (1.2–5.0 mmol/L) vs. 2.5 mmol/L (1.1–4.3 mmol/L), P = 0.04). Interestingly, they also noted that the degree of lactate accumulation varied among patients. In some individuals, lactate levels remained unchanged with limb elevation, possibly as a result of impaired glycolytic capacity from chronic ischemia or reaching a metabolic ceiling beyond which further lactate production was impossible [ ]. Alterations in lactate-associated metabolic and ischemic indicators in PAD and HLI models are summarized in [ , , , , , ]. 
These findings collectively suggest substantial lactate accumulation in PAD, particularly in CLI, that correlates with disease severity. Notably, in patients who achieved successful surgical revascularization, the restoration of blood flow led to symptomatic improvement and lactate level normalization. Conversely, in cases of revascularization failure, lactate levels rapidly rebounded to preoperative ischemic levels, often resulting in limb loss [ ]. These results underscore the importance of revascularization in determining clinical outcomes, a cornerstone of PAD management. However, not all patients are eligible for bypass surgery or endovascular interventions due to diffuse vascular disease or severe microangiopathy. Even among those undergoing successful procedures, restenosis remains a major challenge, adversely affecting long-term outcomes.
In this context, neovascularization, a compensatory response to ischemia through the formation of new capillaries or expansion of collateral arteries, represents a promising therapeutic alternative, particularly for patients unsuitable for surgical intervention. Emerging evidence suggests that lactylation, a novel posttranslational modification derived from lactate metabolism, may play a regulatory role in angiogenesis. Therefore, revisiting the interplay among lactate metabolism, lactylation, and neovascularization in PAD may open new avenues for therapeutic intervention. By targeting these molecular processes, we may enhance endogenous vascular regeneration and improve outcomes in patients with PAD.

## Neovascularization in PAD
5.1

## Three types of neovascularization
As PAD progresses, major arteries are occluded by atherosclerotic plaques or thrombosis, obstructing blood flow to the lower limb and causing hemodynamic changes that lead to chronic or acute hypoxia in distal tissues. In response, neovascularization, comprising angiogenesis, arteriogenesis, and vasculogenesis, is an important compensatory mechanism to restore blood supply and alleviate ischemia. illustrates this process following lower limb artery occlusion. Neovascularization occurs through different mechanisms, each regulated by distinct physiological stimuli. When a major artery is severely stenosed or occluded, increased fluid shear stress stimulates arteriogenesis in the collateral vessels of the proximal limb [ ]. By contrast, vasculogenesis and angiogenesis are primarily driven by local ischemia in distal limb tissues [ ]. Given the distinct pathological characteristics of the proximal and distal limbs, a nuanced understanding of these regions is essential for optimizing therapeutic strategies aimed at enhancing neovascularization and improving tissue perfusion in PAD. 

5.2

## Angiogenesis
Angiogenesis is the process by which new blood vessels form from existing capillaries or postcapillary venules. It involves EC proliferation, differentiation, and migration. It can occur via sprouting or nonsprouting mechanisms [ ]. Angiogenesis requires the coordination of three distinct EC subtypes, namely, tip (with migratory properties), stalk (with proliferative properties), and phalanx (involved in vascular maturation) cells. It begins when ECs, stimulated by growth factors, activate and degrade the local microvascular basement membrane. These cells then differentiate into tip and stalk cells. Tip cells sense growth factor signals and guide vessel growth through pseudopodia, whereas stalk cells proliferate to elongate blood vessel sprouts. The sprouts fuse to form lumens, which are stabilized by pericytes (PCs), eventually establishing a mature and functional microvasculature [ ]. In PAD, ischemia-induced hypoxia primarily triggers angiogenesis via hypoxia-inducible factor-1α (HIF-1α), which upregulates proangiogenic factors [ ]. Therefore, angiogenesis-promoting therapies aim to enhance new vessel formation, restore blood perfusion, and prevent the loss of limb function.

5.3

## Arteriogenesis
Arteriogenesis is a key adaptive mechanism in PAD, enabling the restoration of blood flow after major artery occlusion, primarily through the remodeling of collateral arteries [ ]. Within 2–8 weeks of femoral artery occlusion, the popliteal artery markedly dilates to function as a collateral vessel, and the vessel walls gradually thicken [ ]. This process is driven by a pressure drop downstream of the occlusion that increases blood flow into the collateral arteries and creates a pressure gradient that promotes arteriogenesis, which is characterized by lumen enlargement [ ]. In the early stages of ischemia, increased shear stress in the collateral arteries triggers the release of nitric oxide (NO) induced by endothelial NO synthase (eNOS), leading to the vasodilation of the collateral arteries [ ]. Concurrently, abrupt changes in fluid shear stress promote the expression of chemokines and adhesion molecules in ECs, facilitating the recruitment of circulating monocytes. These monocytes traverse the endothelial layer, enter the intima, and differentiate into macrophages [ ].
The infiltrated macrophages release various growth factors, including transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β), epidermal growth factor (EGF), FGFs, and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α) [ , , , ]. These factors induce the phenotypic transition of VSMCs from a quiescent, contractile state into a proliferative, migratory synthetic phenotype. In the later stage of remodeling, VSMCs revert into their contractile phenotype [ ]. In addition, growth factors and cytokines secreted by macrophages activate proteolytic systems that facilitate extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling, further promoting the phenotypic transformation of VSMCs [ ]. Notably, the extent and effectiveness of arteriogenesis in collateral arteries vary among patients with PAD. This variation may help explain the differences in symptom severity across individuals [ ].

5.4

## Vasculogenesis
Vasculogenesis is the process by which endothelial progenitor cells (EPCs) differentiate into ECs, thereby forming new blood vessels or incorporating into the EC layer [ ]. While vasculogenesis primarily occurs during embryonic development, it can be reactivated in adulthood under pathological conditions, such as ischemia and hypoxia. In these situations, growth factors like vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), stromal-derived factor 1 (SDF-1), FGFs, and angiopoietin-1 (Ang-1) stimulate the mobilization of EPCs from the bone marrow to ischemic regions, where they contribute to vascular repair and regeneration [ , , ]. In addition to these growth factors, granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (CSF) and granulocyte-macrophage CSF (GM-CSF) have been shown to enhance EPC mobilization [ ]. For example, GM-CSF plasmid administration has been found to increase circulating EPC levels and capillary density [ ].
EPCs contribute to vasculogenesis through two mechanisms: first, by differentiating into ECs and integrating into the walls of newly formed blood vessels [ ], and second, by modifying the perivascular microenvironment to promote endothelial repair and regeneration via paracrine factors, including VEGF, NO, and matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) [ ]. However, EPC mobilization is often impaired in patients with PAD, likely due to a decline in EPC number and function associated with risk factors, such as smoking and diabetes [ ]. Based on the evidence, the contribution of EPC to neovascularization in PAD is generally limited.

6

## Vascular component cells and PAD
Neovascularization is a highly complex and finely regulated biological process involving multiple cells and tissue components, including ECs, PCs, VSMCs, ECM, and immune cells. These elements play critical roles in different stages of neovascularization, contributing to tissue repair and vascular remodeling. In the pathological context of PAD, the function and phenotype of vascular component cells are drastically altered to adapt to the ischemic and hypoxic environment, ultimately aiming to restore blood flow and determine the outcome of PAD.
6.1

## ECs
Under normoxic conditions, ECs are quiescent, maintaining tight junctions through molecules, such as VE-cadherin and claudins, to preserve their monolayer structure, which is supported by PCs and forms the vascular basement membrane. Under hypoxic conditions, proline hydroxylase 2 (PHD2), an oxygen sensor on ECs, inhibits the degradation of HIF-1α, promoting its translocation to the nucleus. This activation upregulates HIF-1α-regulated genes, driving angiogenesis to alleviate tissue hypoxia [ ]. Besides hypoxia, EC function is influenced by various factors during angiogenesis, including proangiogenic signaling, metabolic changes, and epigenetic modifications. VEGF is the primary proangiogenic factor that binds to its receptor, stimulating EC proliferation, differentiation, migration, and increasing vascular permeability [ ]. Several signaling pathways, including the tumor protein p53, Hippo/Yes-associated protein (YAP)/transcriptional coactivator with PDZ-binding motif (TAZ), TGF-β, wingless/integrated (Wnt), and Notch receptor signaling pathways, play essential roles in angiogenesis [ , , , , ]. However, their effects vary depending on the specific pathological microenvironment.
Interestingly, ECs are highly dependent on glycolysis even in the quiescent state because this metabolic pathway supplies more than 85% of adenosine triphosphate (ATP) [ ]. In glycolytic metabolism, glucose is converted into lactate. The upregulation of key glycolytic enzymes, such as LDH-A and 6-phosphofructo-2-kinase/fructose-2,6-bisphosphatase (PFKFB3), promotes angiogenesis, whereas the downregulation of these enzymes exert opposite effects [ , ]. In addition, other metabolic pathways may also have an effect on angiogenesis. For example, amino acid and lipid metabolism also play a regulatory role in angiogenesis. Glutamine can be taken up by ECs and converted into glutamate under the action of glutaminase (GLS) for aerobic oxidation, whereas GLS knockout greatly suppresses angiogenesis [ ]. VEGF receptor and delta-like ligand 4/Notch signaling can upregulate the expression of endothelial cytoplasmic fatty acid binding protein 4 (FABP4). When the FABP4 gene is knocked out, the proliferation and migration of ECs are substantially suppressed, and numerous ECs become apoptotic [ ]. These findings highlight the critical role of metabolism in EC activation and angiogenesis.
Antiangiogenic therapies, such as bevacizumab and tyrosine kinase inhibitors, are important for tumor treatment. By contrast, PAD requires the promotion of angiogenesis and formation of mature vessels. Thus far, however, effective drugs for PAD remain lacking. Although preclinical studies on angiogenesis have shown promising results in animal models, their findings have not been confirmed in clinical trials. Therefore, a pressing need exists for the further elucidation of the mechanism of EC-mediated angiogenesis in PAD and development of new therapeutic strategies.

6.2

## VSMCs
VSMCs mainly constitute the media of blood vessels and regulate the tension and diameter of blood vessels through a contractile mechanism. They are highly plastic and can transition from a static contraction phenotype to a synthetic phenotype characterized by high proliferation, phagocytosis, and migration abilities [ ]. In the context of PAD, VSMCs play a critical role in arteriogenesis, especially in collateral arteries, to alleviate ischemia following major artery occlusion [ ]. In a nonpathological state, VSMCs maintain a quiescent contractile phenotype with minimal proliferative activity [ ]. However, under conditions of chronic inflammation, chemical stimulation, or physical injury, VSMCs undergo a phenotypic switch to a synthetic state, characterized by enhanced proliferative capacity and increased secretion of proinflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNF-α, C–C motif chemokine 2, and monocyte chemoattractant protein 1), ECM proteins (e.g., type I collagen), and MMPs [ , , , , ]. This transition is accompanied with a decrease in contractile markers, such as alpha smooth muscle actin (α-SMA) and smooth muscle protein 22, and an increase in synthetic markers like osteopontin [ ].
The phenotypic switching of VSMCs is crucial for the adaptation to ischemia and regulation of the microenvironment following arterial occlusion. Under normal conditions, contractile VSMCs maintain vascular tone and blood flow that are crucial for collateral vessel function. Conversely, synthetic VSMCs are involved in the growth and remodeling of the vascular media and formation of stable collateral arteries. The phenotypic switching of VSMCs relies on the coordinated action of various signaling molecules, enabling the extensive remodeling of collateral arteries to restore blood flow effectively to downstream ischemic tissues [ ]. The myocardin, serum response factor CC(A/T)6GG (CArG) complex, is a key regulator of VSMC phenotypic switching. When stabilized, it maintains the contractile phenotype; however, its disruption results in the downregulation of contractile markers and the induction of the synthetic phenotype [ ]. Integrins, TGF-β, and insulin-like growth factor (IGF) influence the stability of this complex via different pathways, thus maintaining the contractile phenotype [ ].
Growth factors, including FGFs, platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF), and EGF, activate the rat sarcoma (Ras)/rapidly accelerated fibrosarcoma (Raf)/mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) kinase (MEK)/extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK) signaling pathway, suppressing contractile phenotype-associated gene expression and promoting the synthetic phenotype [ , , ]. The coadministration of VEGF/PDGF-BB or FGF-2/PDGF-BB has been shown to stimulate collateral artery formation in ischemic tissues in vivo [ , ]. Hemodynamic changes following major artery occlusion also influence VSMC phenotypic switching. Increased blood flow velocity and shear stress in the collateral arteries activate the eNOS/NO signaling pathway, promoting vasodilation and the conversion of VSMCs into the synthetic phenotype. This change increases collateral artery diameter and thickens the vessel wall, ultimately reducing shear stress. In eNOS knockout mice, arteriogenesis is highly impaired [ ], underscoring the essential role of the eNOS/NO signaling pathway in synthetic VSMC-mediated arteriogenesis.

6.3

## Macrophages
Macrophages are other vascular component cells with an important role in angiogenesis and arteriogenesis. In the mouse HLI model, macrophages are the most prominent immune cells responding to ischemia, with infiltration peaking 72 h after femoral artery ligation [ , ]. Their roles in neovascularization include secreting growth factors and chemokines that induce vessel sprouting, stimulate EC and VSMC proliferation, and remodel the ECM. Additionally, macrophages secrete PDGFs that recruit PCs and other perivascular cells to promote vascular maturation [ ]. The critical role of macrophages is further supported by studies showing that the reduction in tyrosine kinase with immunoglobulin-like and EGF-like domains 2 (TIE2)-expressing macrophages impairs arteriogenesis [ ].
Macrophages exhibit high plasticity, with their functions and roles in angiogenesis and arteriogenesis varying on the basis of their polarization state. They are broadly categorized into M1 and M2 phenotypes on the basis of their surface markers, functions, and cytokine profiles. M1 macrophages, characterized by increased aerobic glycolysis and lactate efflux, secrete proinflammatory cytokines, such as TNF-α, interleukin-6 (IL-6), and inducible NOS (iNOS), that promote inflammation. Under lipopolysaccharide and interferon-gamma (IFN-γ) stimulation, macrophages are polarized toward M1, and the expression levels of VEGF, FGFs, and MMP-9 are markedly elevated [ ]. Although M1 macrophages have been traditionally thought to inhibit angiogenesis, their role may vary depending on the specific pathological context. By contrast, M2 macrophages, which release anti-inflammatory cytokines, such as IL-10, arginase-1, and IL-13, promote tissue repair and neovascularization [ ]. M2 macrophages can be further divided into subtypes (M2a, M2b, M2c, and M2d), each with distinct functions in neovascularization [ ]. In summary, M2a, M2c, and M2d macrophages promote angiogenesis and vascular maturation by secreting proangiogenic factors, including VEGF, FGFs, and MMPs. In vitro coculture experiments have shown that macrophage-derived PDGFs enhance the proliferation of VSMCs and downregulate the expression of contractile markers, such as α-SMA, whereas IL-6 released by macrophages induces the VSMC secretion of MMP-1 [ ]. M2b macrophages, which secrete TGF-β1, participate in arteriogenesis by promoting the transition of VSMCs to a contractile phenotype [ ]. [ , , , , , , , , ] shows the phenotypes of macrophages and their respective roles in neovascularization.
Macrophages, therefore, play a critical role in the pathophysiology of PAD by modulating the ischemic microenvironment and influencing vascular remodeling. However, the exact mechanisms by which they interact with other angiogenic cells to affect PAD outcomes remain unclear. Further studies are needed to understand their role in PAD and their potential as therapeutic targets to improve neovascularization and tissue repair.

## Lactate and lactylation in vascular component cells
7.1

## Lactate and lactylation in ECs
ECs exhibit a marked metabolic reliance on glycolysis, which generates over 85% of ATP under quiescent and angiogenic conditions [ ]. Glycolysis produces large amounts of lactate, which in turn stimulate VEGF production, indirectly enhancing the proliferation and migration of ECs [ ]. Notably, lactate-induced VEGF secretion is associated with a reduction in NAD levels, leading to the decreased posttranslational glycosylation of VEGF, thus potentiating its proangiogenic activity [ ]. Lactate also promotes angiogenesis through multiple pathways, including HIF-1α-dependent and -independent mechanisms. In the HIF-dependent pathway, lactate inhibits the PHD-mediated degradation of HIF-1α, stabilizing HIF-1α and inducing VEGF expression to foster angiogenesis [ ]. Lactate binds to N-Myc downstream regulatory gene 3 (NDRG3) in an HIF-independent manner, facilitating its binding to c-Raf, thereby activating the Raf/ERK signaling pathway to support angiogenesis [ ]. Lactate can also promote angiogenesis through a reactive oxygen species (ROS)-dependent mechanism by activating the nuclear factor κB (NF-κB)/IL-8 pathway [ ]. Additionally, lactate elevates the levels of growth arrest specific 6 (Gas6), Ang-1, and possibly VEGF that bind to receptor tyrosine kinases, such as AXL receptor tyrosine kinase (Axl), TIE2, and VEGFR-2, further promoting angiogenesis [ ]. In severe systemic inflammation (sepsis), lactate diminishes the expression of key endothelial junction proteins, including vascular endothelial cadherin (VE-cadherin), claudin-5, and zonula occludens-1 (ZO-1), via heat shock protein A12B (HSPA12B) and GPR81-dependent pathways, leading to increased vascular permeability [ ]. These findings underscore VEGF's dual role, promoting angiogenesis while enhancing vascular permeability.
Building on the role of lactate in regulating angiogenesis and EC function, lactylation, mediated by lactate, also plays an important role in the progression of PAD and ischemia. Fan et al. [ ] demonstrated that lactate accumulation following hypoxia and myocardial infarction (MI) promotes Snail nuclear translocation and lactylation. This process activates the TGF-β/SMAD family member 2 (Smad2) signaling pathway, leading to endothelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EndMT), which exacerbates cardiac fibrosis and heart dysfunction. By contrast, the exercise-induced lactylation of methyl-CpG binding proteins 2 (MeCP2) suppresses the ETS-related gene (Erg)/MAPK pathway and reduces the expression of adhesion molecules (vascular cell adhesion molecule-1 and intercellular adhesion molecule-1), monocyte chemoattractant protein-1, and proinflammatory cytokines (IL-1β and IL-6), as well as eNOS, in ECs. This exercise-induced lactylation modulates endothelial function and inhibits the progression of AS [ ]. Additionally, lipid peroxides can induce EndMT via the lactylation of histone H3 lysine 18 (H3K18), activating Snail through aerobic glycolysis in ECs, further contributing to endothelial dysfunction and AS progression [ ]. Taken together, these findings suggest that lactate and lactylation exert divergent effects on EC function and AS progression, offering novel therapeutic potential for PAD treatment. However, given the distinct pathophysiology of early- and late-stage PAD, therapeutic strategies must be stage-specific. The aim of treatment in early-stage PAD is to prevent the progression of AS and arterial obstruction, whereas that in the late stage is to promote neovascularization to alleviate ischemia and prevent amputation. Therefore, additional attention should be paid to the treatment of PAD by targeting lactate metabolism and lactylation. presents an overview of lactate and lactylation modification in ECs in PAD. 

7.2

## Lactate and lactylation in VSMCs
Similar to ECs, VSMCs also exhibit increased glycolytic activity under normoxic conditions, resulting in elevated lactate production [ ]. Although glycolysis accounts for only approximately 30% of ATP production, its byproduct, L-lactate, plays a crucial role in VSMC function. Lactate not only enhances mitochondrial reserve capacity, but also plays a crucial role in the phenotypic switching of VSMCs [ ]. The knockdown of the key glycolytic enzyme LDH markedly inhibits the proliferation and migration of VSMCs, highlighting the importance of lactate in these processes [ ]. Moreover, lactate production in VSMCs is modulated by various factors. For instance, the κ-opioid receptor agonist U50488H reduces lactate production by downregulating PFKFB3, thereby inhibiting VSMC calcification [ ]. Oxidized low-density lipoprotein accelerates AS progression by enhancing PKM2-dependent glycolysis, further implicating lactate in atherosclerotic progression [ ].
The lactate-mediated phenotypic switching of VSMCs has been widely demonstrated. Yang et al. [ ] showed that in contrast to the vehicle control, VSMCs cultured in a glucose-free and lactate-rich medium transitioned to a synthetic phenotype. This phenotypic switch was characterized by the elevated expression levels of synthetic VSMC markers, enhanced proliferation and migration, and the reduced expression of contractile markers. These findings suggest that lactate promotes the shift from a contractile to a synthetic phenotype in VSMCs. High glucose concentrations similarly induce phenotypic switching by increasing lactate production and activating the GPR81 signaling pathway [ ]. Additionally, the downregulation of miR-23b has been shown to mediate phenotypic switching via targeting Smad3 [ ], further supporting lactate's central role in the phenotypic modulation of VSMCs.
Meanwhile, lactylation also plays a crucial role in VSMC-associated AS. TNF receptor-associated protein 1 (TRAP1), a mitochondrial chaperone, increases lactate production by promoting glycolysis and facilitates the lactylation of HDAC3, activating the transcription of the senescence-associated secretion phenotype (SASP). This phenomenon exacerbates VSMC senescence and accelerates AS development [ ]. Similarly, the lactylation of SRY-related HMG-box 10 (Sox10) has been shown to induce vascular inflammation and intimal hyperplasia by promoting the transdifferentiation of VSMCs into macrophage-like cells. The suppression of the phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K)/protein kinase B (Akt)/Sox10 signaling pathway by regulator of G protein signaling 5 (RGS5) reduces vascular inflammation and intimal hyperplasia [ ]. Furthermore, nuclear receptor subfamily 4 group A member 3 (NR4A3) promotes lactate production by binding to the promoters of aldolase A (ALDOA) and PFK liver type (PFKL), leading to histone H3K18 lactylation. This process upregulates Phospho1, a gene involved in bone mineralization, contributing to calcium deposition in the media of the aorta [ ]. Collectively, these findings underscore the pivotal roles of lactate accumulation and lactylation in the pathophysiology of PAD. The lactate-induced phenotypic switching and transdifferentiation of VSMCs into macrophage-like cells contribute to AS and vascular calcification. These mechanisms provide valuable insights into the metabolic disturbances driving the progression of vascular calcification and AS, which are hallmarks of PAD. gives an overview of lactate and lactylation modification in VSMCs in PAD. 

7.3

## Lactate and lactylation in macrophages
Lactate plays a crucial role in modulating macrophage-mediated immune and inflammatory responses, particularly in the repair processes following PAD and tissue ischemia [ ]. This role is closely linked to its polarization toward different phenotypes. High glycolytic activity and lactate production are key characteristics of proinflammatory macrophages, establishing a direct connection among metabolism, cytokine production, and macrophage infiltration. In tumor models, lactate has been shown to bind to the GPR81 receptor on the surfaces of macrophages. Upon activation, GPR81 suppresses Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4)-induced inflammation and promotes the polarization of macrophages toward the anti-inflammatory M2 phenotype [ ]. This effect is mediated by the lactate-induced upregulation of HIF-1α and HIF-2α levels [ , ]. In HLI models, lactate secreted by ECs enters macrophages via MCT1 and promotes M2 polarization, which eventually induces angiogenesis and restores blood perfusion after ischemia [ ]. These findings suggest that lactate can alleviate limb ischemia by promoting angiogenesis via the regulation of macrophage polarization. However, whether lactate produced by glycolysis within macrophages or transported from the extracellular environment primarily influences macrophage polarization remains unclear.
Lactylation has emerged as a critical regulator of disease progression, particularly by modulating macrophage function. This modification has been demonstrated in various disease models, including bacterial infection, inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), septic shock, Alzheimer's disease (AD), tumors, and AS. Despite its important role, lactylation has been underexplored in the context of PAD or HLI models. Therefore, we also review the existing literature on lactylation modification in other disease models to explore its potential relevance in PAD and HLI. Zhao and co-workers [ ] were the first to report that lactate accumulation during bacterial infection leads to the M1-to-M2 macrophage polarization shift via H3K18 lactylation, highlighting the effect of lactylation on macrophage polarization in immune responses. Similarly, in IBD, the activation of TLR signaling enhances the PI3K/Akt pathway through B-cell adapter for PI3K (BCAP), promoting glycolysis and lactate accumulation. This phenomenon, in turn, drives histone lactylation and supports the transition of macrophages from an inflammatory to a reparative phenotype [ ]. Increased H3K18 lactylation has also been observed in patients with septic shock, suggesting its potential as a biomarker for severe systematic inflammation [ ]. Additionally, in the context of AD, the lactylation of histone H4K12 has been shown to contribute to microglial activation and AD progression through a glycolysis-histone lactylation-PKM2 positive feedback loop [ ]. Notably, recent studies have revealed the role of macrophage H3K18 lactylation in the HLI model, where it predicts changes in gene expression during muscle regeneration after ischemia [ ]. Beyond histones, the lactylation of nonhistone proteins has been linked to disease processes. For example, the exercise-induced lactylation of the nonhistone protein MeCP2 promotes M2 macrophage polarization, which inhibits AS [ ]. Despite these findings, the role of lactate and lactylation modification in PAD progression and neovascularization in the HLI model, particularly in relation to macrophage function, remains poorly understood and warrants further investigation. shows a schematic of lactate and lactylation modifications in macrophages.

## Potential therapeutic agents and targets acting on lactate metabolism pathways
Lactate, as the end product of glycolysis, plays a multifaceted role in metabolic and signaling pathways, including its generation, transport, and downstream molecular effects. Key molecular targets in these processes include LDHA, HK, pyruvate dehydrogenase kinase (PDK), MCTs, GPR81, and lactate-derived posttranslational modifications, such as the lactylation of malate dehydrogenase 2 (MDH2). The pharmacological modulation of these targets offers potential for therapeutic intervention by altering lactate dynamics and its associated signaling effects.
Current pharmacological strategies aimed at reducing lactate production primarily focus on the inhibition of LDHA, HK, and PDK. Among these strategies, LDHA inhibitors are the most extensively studied and include agents, such as vitamin C, stiripentol, galloflavin, and FX-11 [ , , , ]. These agents function by inhibiting LDHA to reduce lactate generation and have demonstrated therapeutic effects in cancer, acute kidney injury, and other disease models. The glycolysis inhibitor 2-deoxyglucose (2-DG) competes with glucose at the level of HK, thereby suppressing glycolytic flux and lactate production [ ]. Dichloroacetate (DCA), another notable agent, inhibits PDK and facilitates pyruvate entry into mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation, reducing lactate buildup. Notably, DCA and 2-DG exhibit synergistic effects in tumor suppression [ ].
Other agents act by disrupting lactate transport. AZD3965, primarily targeting MCT1 with partial activity against MCT2, inhibits lactate efflux, thereby causing intracellular acidification and promoting tumor cell apoptosis. This compound has completed phase I clinical trials [ ]. Other inhibitors of the MCT family include emodin, AR-C155858, VB124, syrosingopine, and lonidamine and target one or more members of the MCT family to modulate lactate dynamics [ , , , , ]. GPR81 is a specific receptor for lactate. Its antagonist, 3-hydroxybutyrate, inhibits lactate binding to GPR81 and has shown neuroprotective effects by mitigating lactate-mediated ischemic brain injury [ ].
Most of these agents have been investigated in oncology, where enhanced glycolysis and lactate production support tumor survival and immune evasion. In cancer therapy, glycolytic inhibition or lactate transport blockade can restrict energy supply or induce lethal acidosis, ultimately leading to tumor cell death. However, the therapeutic goal in ischemic diseases, such as PAD, differs fundamentally from that in cancers. Instead of promoting cytotoxicity, the aim in ischemic disease therapy is often to preserve tissue viability. In early or compensatory stages of PAD, moderate lactate accumulation appears to exert protective effects by promoting angiogenesis, arteriogenesis, and macrophage polarization toward a reparative M2 phenotype. By contrast, during advanced stages, such as CLI, excess lactate contributes to local acidosis, cellular apoptosis, and tissue necrosis. Therefore, the timing and context of lactate modulation are critical, and stage-specific strategies must be considered to optimize therapeutic outcomes.
Adding further complexity, lactate also functions as a precursor to lactylation, a recently identified posttranslational modification that directly influences gene expression in response to metabolic stress. Lactylation exhibits context- and target-specific effects in various ischemic disease models. For example, it exacerbates ferroptosis in hepatic ischemia through the modification of PCK2, whereas in cerebral ischemia, the lactylation of MeCP2 inhibits proapoptotic gene transcription and promotes neuronal survival [ , ]. Even within a single disease, such as myocardial ischemia, lactylated proteins execute divergent roles: the lactylation of Serpina3k (SA3K) supports cardioprotection via enhanced fibroblast signaling, whereas that of MDH2 aggravates injury [ , ]. The regulation of protein lactylation is primarily indirect, achieved through the manipulation of upstream lactate metabolism. By inhibiting LDHA or blocking lactate transport, lactate availability, and thus lactylation potential, can be reduced. However, this approach lacks specificity and may disrupt essential metabolic functions. With the continued advancement of epigenetic and metabolic research, enzymes that are directly responsible for adding or removing lactyl groups, such as putative lactyltransferases and delactylases, are expected to be identified. These enzymes may serve as novel and precise drug targets for fine-tuning lactylation without broadly impairing energy homeostasis or redox balance. Representative pharmacological agents targeting lactate metabolic pathways are summarized in [ , , , , , , , , , , , , , ]. 
Despite the therapeutic promise of targeting lactate metabolism and lactylation, potential adverse effects must be carefully considered. LDHA inhibition, while effective in reducing lactate, can lead to the intracellular accumulation of pyruvate and reduced NAD (NADH). This metabolic imbalance may impair oxidized NAD (NAD+) regeneration, disrupt glycolytic flux, and compromise energy production, potentially harming nontarget tissues. In some contexts, such as breast cancer, LDHA inhibition has paradoxically been associated with increased ECM remodeling and metastasis [ ]. Similar risks apply to other classes of agents targeting lactate metabolism, underscoring the need for precise control over therapeutic timing and molecular specificity.

## Conclusion and prospects
PAD is a complex vascular disorder marked by progressive arterial narrowing, chronic ischemia, endothelial dysfunction, and maladaptive vascular remodeling. Despite advances in understanding its molecular and cellular basis, its current therapies remain insufficient, especially in addressing its dynamic, stage-dependent progression from early stenosis to CLI. This situation underscores the urgent need for precision therapies tailored to the distinct pathological phases of PAD.
Recent evidence highlights a central role for lactate metabolism and lactate-mediated signaling in PAD pathophysiology. Beyond its classical role as a glycolytic byproduct, lactate acts as a key metabolic and signaling intermediate. It promotes endothelial proliferation and angiogenesis via the HIF-1α/VEGF axis, modulates VSMC phenotypes, and influences immune polarization toward reparative M2 macrophages, thereby coordinating vascular remodeling and ischemic repair. While these processes may be compensatory in early disease, excessive lactate accumulation in advanced PAD exacerbates acidosis, tissue necrosis, and apoptosis.
Lactylation, a novel lactate-derived posttranslational modification, directly regulates gene expression under metabolic stress. Its effects are context- and substrate-specific, offering cytoprotection in cerebral ischemia but aggravating injury via proferroptotic or profibrotic pathways in other tissues. Notably, its role in PAD remains largely unexplored. Current pharmacological approaches mainly target lactylation indirectly by modulating lactate metabolism. Identifying specific lactyltransferases and delactylases may enable precise epigenetic interventions.
Therapeutics targeting lactate pathways, including LDHA, HK2, PDK, MCTs, GPR81, and lactylated proteins like MDH2, show promise in cancer and ischemia models. However, their application in PAD requires caution because global lactate inhibition risks disrupting beneficial adaptive responses and cellular homeostasis. For instance, LDHA inhibition may impair NAD+ regeneration and promote fibrosis, undermining tissue recovery. Therefore, targeting lactate metabolism and lactylation in PAD demands precise timing, stage consideration, and cellular context. Future research should delineate the temporal dynamics and cell-specific roles of lactate and lactylation, map lactylated substrates, explore epigenetic crosstalk, and develop selective modulators.
Therapeutic angiogenesis remains an important and intensively studied direction in PAD/CLI, reflecting the critical role of neovascularization in ischemic tissue repair. Nevertheless, despite extensive clinical efforts, angiogenesis-based interventions alone have not produced consistent or substantial clinical benefit. This underscores that while angiogenesis is necessary, it is not sufficient to resolve the multifactorial pathology of PAD, which also involves profound metabolic, immune, and stromal dysregulation.
In summary, lactate metabolism and lactylation represent promising targets in PAD therapy. Integrating these pathways within broad metabolic, vascular, and immune frameworks offers potential for stage-specific, mechanism-driven, precision medicine approaches. Advancing this field will require coordinated basic, translational, and clinical research to transform metabolic insights into effective PAD treatments.
