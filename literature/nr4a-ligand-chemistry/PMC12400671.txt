Conventional treatments such as surgery, radiation therapy, and chemotherapy often face limitations, including significant side effects and the development of drug resistance. Consequently, there is an urgent need to identify novel, effective natural compounds with anti-tumor properties. L-mimosine, a non-protein amino acid derived from the seeds of Leucaena leucocephala and Mimosa pudica, has demonstrated cytotoxic activity against various cancers, including sarcoma, melanoma, breast cancer, and lung cancer. However, its precise molecular mechanisms remain poorly understood. To elucidate these mechanisms, a network pharmacology approach was employed, complemented by molecular docking, molecular dynamics simulations, and in vitro experiments to evaluate the impact of L-mimosine on breast cancer cell proliferation. Breast cancer-associated targets and L-mimosine-interacting genes were retrieved from multiple databases. Pharmacokinetic and toxicity properties were assessed through ADMET analysis. Protein–protein interaction networks of overlapping genes were constructed, identifying key hub genes such as SRC, MAPK8, PRKACA, and AKT1. Among these, AKT1 demonstrated the highest docking scores in molecular docking studies, and molecular simulations confirmed the stability of L-mimosine binding to AKT1. In vitro assays validated that L-mimosine inhibits breast cancer cell proliferation by inducing DNA damage and apoptosis in a dose-dependent manner. Furthermore, L-mimosine was found to arrest MCF-7 cells in the S phase of the cell cycle, as analyzed through cell cycle assays. This research highlights L-mimosine’s potential as a promising anticancer agent against breast cancer by promoting apoptosis and interfering with cancer cell cycle progression.

## Introduction
Breast cancer stands as the most common malignant tumour among women globally, contributing to up to 36% of all cancer cases. In 2018, an estimated 2.089 million women were diagnosed with breast cancer [ ]. Its prevalence has been rising throughout various locations, particularly in emerging nations [ ]. An estimated 685,000 women died from breast cancer in 2020, corresponding to 16% or 1 in every 6 cancer deaths in women [ ] and The global burden continued to increase, with 2.3 million new cases and 670,000 deaths reported in 2022[ ]. Breast cancer is categorized into four subtypes based on molecular and histological features such as the expression of hormone receptors [estrogen receptor (ER+) or progesterone receptor (PR +), and human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2+)]. These subtypes are: (i) Luminal A (HR+/HER2−), (ii) Luminal B (HR+/HER2+), (iii) HER2+, and (iv) Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC: ER−, PR−, HER2−) [ ]. TNBC, which constitutes about 15% [ , ]. One of the key challenges in breast cancer diagnosis and treatment is its heterogeneous nature, which dictates therapeutic options [ ]. Therefore, there is an urgent need to explore novel multitargeted strategies for effective breast cancer treatment [ ].
Medicinal plants are considered renewable sources of bioactive compounds that can be utilized in treating various ailments, including cancer [ – ]. Phytochemicals play a crucial role in carcinogenesis initiation, development, and progression. They can suppress or reverse early stages of cancer, inhibit the invasive potential of premalignant cells, and regulate cell proliferation and apoptosis signalling pathways in transformed [ , ] More than half of all pharmaceuticals, including over 80% of licensed chemotherapeutic drugs, are based on bioactive natural compound [ , ].
L-mimosine is a non-protein amino acid found predominantly in the plant species Leucaena leucocephala and Mimosa pudic a. L-mimosine exhibits antibacterial, anticancer, antioxidant, and iron-chelating properties [ , ]. L-mimosine exhibits diverse biological effects, including tyrosinase and thyroxine decarboxylase inhibition, cell death induction, cell cycle arrest, disruption of DNA replication and deoxyribonucleotide metabolism, and inhibition of serine hydroxymethyltransferase transcription. Structurally resembling tyrosine, it holds significant potential for therapeutic applications [ , ]. Due to its structural similarity to deferiprone (DFP), mimosine is considered an excellent metal chelator [ ]. L-mimosine has shown anticancer effectiveness against a few cancers, such as lung, breast, melanoma, and sarcoma. Sarcoma (35,813), melanoma (331,722), breast cancer (2,296,840), and lung cancer (2,480,675) are among the new incidents that have been reported [ – ]. However, the mechanistic pathway for L-mimosine’s regulation of breast cancer remains unclear, suggesting the need for a network pharmacology approach to understand its regulatory mechanisms against breast cancer better.
This study demonstrated that L-mimosine inhibits cell proliferation, induces apoptosis, causes DNA damage, and arrests the cell cycle in MCF-7 cells at higher concentrations. To uncover the underlying molecular mechanisms, a network pharmacology approach was employed, revealing AKT1 as a potential target. Molecular docking, binding free energy calculations, and molecular dynamics (MD) simulations further validated L-mimosine as a promising anti-AKT1 agent. Similarly, network pharmacology has been used to identify compounds like kaempferol and apigenin, in triple-negative breast cancer and colorectal cancer [ – ].

## Materials and methods

## Drug-likeness, pharmacokinetics and toxicity assessment of L-mimosine
Assessing the pharmacokinetics of lead molecules is essential in drug discovery, as it examines how a drug is absorbed, distributed, metabolized, excreted and toxicity (ADMET) in the body and is key to determining a compound's potential as a therapeutic agent. We assessed the drug-likeness potential of L-mimosine using the pkCSM web server to mitigate the risk of drug failure across various pharmacokinetic characteristics [ ]. The pKCSM server provides an accessible platform for quickly predicting a range of pharmacokinetic and toxicity properties of small molecules, enabling early evaluation before experimental testing. The SMILES (Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry Specification) data of L-mimosine were sourced from PubChem and utilized to predict the pharmacokinetic and toxicity parameters [ ].

## L-mimosine targeting breast cancer-related gene mapping
To elucidate the molecular targets of L-mimosine in breast cancer, we employed a multi-database approach. The 3D protein structure (PubChem ID: 440,473) and SMILE (SDF) structure of L-mimosine were retrieved from PubChem (https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/). Potential drug targets were identified using PharmMapper, a pharmacophore mapping tool (https://www.lilab-ecust.cn/pharmmapper); ChEMBL, a resource for bioactive molecule data (https://www.ebi.ac.uk/chembl/); and SwissTargetPrediction, which predicts biological targets based on chemical similarity (http://www.swisstargetprediction.ch/result). These targets were validated via the UniProt database for matching Gene IDs (https://www.uniprot.org/).
Breast cancer-related genes were identified using DisGeNET, a database of gene-disease associations (https://www.disgenet.org/search), and GeneCards, which provides detailed gene-related information (https://www.genecards.org/). The resulting gene lists were merged, duplicates removed, and compared with L-mimosine targets using Venny 2.0 (https://bioinfogp.cnb.csic.es/tools/venny/index2.0.2.html). This alignment identified potential L-mimosine targets associated with breast cancer.

## Construction of Protein–Protein Interaction (PPI) network and hub genes acquisition
To identify intersecting key genes, Protein–Protein Interaction (PPI) analysis was performed using the STRING database (https://string-db.org/) with parameters restricted to"Homo sapiens"and a confidence score of 0.900. The resulting PPI network was imported into the CytoHubba plugin of Cytoscape v3.10.1 for hub gene identification [ , ]. Hub genes were ranked based on 12 scoring methods, including local methods such as Maximal Clique Centrality (MCC), Density of Maximum Neighbourhood Component (DMNC), Maximum Neighbourhood Component (MNC), and Degree, and global methods like EPC, Bottleneck, Eccentricity, Closeness, Radiality, Betweenness, and Stress [ ]. Shortlisted target genes were further processed for their molecular and functional enrichment analysis to identify them as a potential anticancer drug target.

## GO and KEGG pathway enrichment analysis
Potential gene targets were analyzed using ShinyGO v0.78 (http://bioinformatics.sdstate.edu/go/), a platform for Gene Ontology (GO) and KEGG pathway enrichment analysis. Enriched GO terms were identified across Biological Process (BP), Molecular Function (MF), and Cellular Component (CC) categories, providing functional insights into the gene targets. KEGG pathway analysis highlighted gene annotations and signaling pathways relevant to cellular mechanisms. The top 20 enriched pathways were further analyzed for detailed GO insights. A significance threshold of P < 0.05 was applied to ensure robust results.

## Molecular Docking and Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulation methodology
L-mimosine (PubChem ID: 440,473) was retrieved from the PubChem database. Ligand preparation was carried out using the LigPrep wizard in Schrödinger Maestro, with geometry optimization and partial atomic charge calculations performed using the OPLS4 force field [ , ]. To ensure stability, hydrogen atoms were added, and energy minimization was conducted at physiological pH (7.2 ± 0.2). Key target proteins identified from the cytoHubba analysis, including SRC (PDB ID: 2BDF), AKT1 (PDB ID: 3 CQW), MAPK8 (PDB ID: 3ELJ), and PRKACA (PDB ID: 8X5L), were selected for molecular docking. The 3D structures of these proteins were obtained from the RCSB Protein Data Bank, and protein preparation was conducted using Schrödinger Maestro’s Protein Preparation Wizard [ ]. Docking grids were generated at specific coordinates using the Glide tool, with a ligand binding grid of 11 Å in diameter. The docking protocol was set to ‘Extra Precision (XP)’ mode to achieve accurate binding predictions. Docking scores were recorded as negative values in kcal/mol, indicating binding affinities between the ligand and proteins. The resulting ligand–protein interactions, including hydrogen bonding and hydrophobic interactions, were analyzed using BIOVIA Discovery Studio Visualizer. Complexes with the highest docking scores were selected for MD simulations.
MD simulations were carried out using the Desmond package (Schrödinger Maestro Release 2021–03) to assess the structural stability of the L-mimosine-protein complexes. The prepared docked complexes were used as starting structures for 100 ns simulations. Each system was enclosed in an orthorhombic box with periodic boundary conditions, ensuring a buffer distance of 10 Å. The OPLS4 force field was applied for parameterization, and Na +/Cl- ions were introduced to maintain electrostatic neutrality. Energy minimization was conducted using the steepest descent algorithm, followed by equilibration under an NPT (constant number of atoms, pressure, and temperature) ensemble. The production phase was subsequently carried out for 100 ns, capturing the dynamic behavior and stability of the ligand–protein complexes [ ].

## Cell line procurement and reagent preparation
The human breast cancer MCF-7 cell line was purchased from national center for cell sciences (NCCS), Pune, Maharashtra, India. MCF-7 cells were cultured in MEM media supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1X Penstrep (Penecilium streptomycine/antibiotic solution (Gibco, Life technologies, USA). The cells were kept at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere containing 5% CO2. Throughout the study, exponentially developing cells were used.

## Cell proliferation assay
To evaluate the IC50 of the L-Mimosine the breast cancer cell line (MCF-7) was seeded in 96 well plate. L-mimosine was procured from Koa hoale seeds (Sigma Aldrich). L- mimosine dissolve in Distilled water with 10–15 min to sonicator at a concentration of 1 mg/ml as a stock solution. The inhibitory concentration value was determined using working concentrations of 1, 10, 25, 50, 100, 250, 500, 700, 1000 µg/ml. After 24 h of cell adhesion, the cells were treated in triplicate with sequential amounts of L-mimosine. After 24 h of treatment, the medium removes and 100 µl methylthiazol tetrazolium (MTT) (5 mg/10 ml) was added to each well and incubated at 37 °C for 3 h in the dark; the solution was then removed, and each well was replaced with 100 µl of DMSO (dimethyl sulfoxide). Optical density (OD) was measured with ELISA reader (Epoch BioTek) at an absorbance wavelength of 570 nm.

## Wound healing assay
MCF-7 cells were grown up to 90% confluency in 6-well plates. A wound was gently formed in the centre of the wells with the help of a sterile 200 μl micro-tip, the floating cells were then removed by washing the wells with phosphate buffered saline (PBS, Gibco, Life technologies, USA). Cells were treated for 24 h with L-mimosine (200 μg/ml, 250ug/ml, 300 µg/ml and 350 μg/ml). After a 24-h exposure, images of the wound were captured using a camera under an inverted microscope (Zeiss, Germany). The wound area for 0 and 24 h was evaluated using Image J software.

## Comet assay
Approximately 4 × 104 MCF-7 cells seeded into 6 well plate and treated with L-mimosine concentration 200 µg, 250 µg, 300 µg and 350 µg and incubate for 24 h. Comet assay was performed as described previously [ ]. Briefly, the cells were suspended in low melting point agarose (LMPA, Himedia,) in PBS at a final concentration of 1.0% at 37 °C and loaded onto a slide pre-coated with a thin layer of 2% agarose. The slides were then immersed in the lysis buffer at 4 °C for overnight. Next Day, slides were then allowed to incubate in a horizontal gel electrophoresis unit containing 0.3 m NaOH and 1 mm Na2 EDTA for 20 min. Thereafter, electrophoresis was performed for the next 30 min at 300 mA at 4 °C followed by staining with 1 µg/ml ethidium bromide and analysed under a fluorescence microscope with Rhodamine filter attached. The proportion of the cells with damage (i.e., those displaying tailing versus all the cells) was scored.

## Morphological observation of nuclear alterations
MCF-7 cells were cultured on poly-L-lysine-coated coverslips in multi-well plates and treated with L-mimosine (200, 250, 300, and 350 µg/ml) for 24 h. Post-treatment, cells were washed with PBS, stained with Hoechst 33,342 (100 ng/ml) in the dark for 15–20 min, and washed again with PBS. They were then incubated with propidium iodide (PI) for 5–10 min. Stained cells were visualized using a Zeiss fluorescence microscope to evaluate treatment effects.

## Apoptosis assay
The apoptotic effects of L-mimosine on MCF-7 cells were analysed using an apoptosis analysis kit (ab14155, Abcam). MCF-7 cells (2 × 105) were seeded in a 12-well plate and incubated for 24 h at 37 °C in a 5% CO₂ atmosphere for attachment. Cells were treated with L-mimosine (200, 250, and 350 µg/ml) in a 10% FBS medium for 24 h, with control cells maintained without L-mimosine. Post-treatment, cells were harvested, washed with PBS, and resuspended in 1X binding buffer. Cells were stained with 5 µl Annexin V-PE for 5 min in the dark. Apoptosis was assessed using a MacsQuant 10 flow cytometer (Miltenyi Biotech) with excitation/emission at 488/578 nm, quantifying the percentage of apoptotic cells to evaluate L-mimosine's impact on apoptosis induction.

## Cell Cycle analysis
The effects of L-mimosine on the cell cycle of MCF-7 cells were assessed using a cell cycle analysis kit (ab287852, Abcam) following the manufacturer’s protocol. MCF-7 cells (2 × 105) were seeded in a 12-well plate, incubated for 24 h for attachment, and synchronized in a 0.1% FBS medium for another 24 h. Cells were then treated with varying concentrations of L-mimosine in a 10% FBS medium for 24 h, while controls were maintained without L-mimosine. Post-treatment, cells were harvested, washed with cold PBS, fixed in 70% ethanol, and stained with the provided staining solution. Cell cycle distribution was analysed using a MacsQuant 10 flow cytometer (Miltenyi Biotech) at 488 nm excitation to determine the percentage of cells in each phase, elucidating L-mimosine's effects on cell cycle progression.

## Statistical analysis
The results are presented as mean ± SEM. Statistical analysis was conducted using GraphPad Prism version 8.0. A one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used to compare the means of multiple groups. A P-value of ≤ 0.05 was considered statistically significant, indicating that the observed differences were unlikely to have occurred by chance.

## Results

## Drug-likeness, pharmacokinetics and toxicity assessment of L-mimosine
To optimize the therapeutic potential of phytochemicals in cancer treatment, applying drug-likeness filters is crucial. These filters enhance the likelihood of a compound's success in clinical trials, positioning it as a promising therapeutic candidate. Evaluating ligands by predicting their Pharmacokinetic and Toxicity properties using ADMET (Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion, and Toxicity) parameters significantly accelerates the drug discovery process. In the present study, we have predicted the pharmacokinetic and toxicological profiles of L-mimosine through the PkCSM server and highlighted its properties as a potential therapeutic agent (Table  ). The calculated dataset for L-mimosine aligns well with the recommended parameters for optimal therapeutic efficacy, demonstrating its favorable druggability profile. Notably, L-mimosine adheres to the Lipinski Rule of Five (Table  ), which indicates its suitability for oral administration and enhances its prospects as a promising candidate for cancer therapy.
 
The compound exhibits low water solubility (−2.89 log mol/L), indicating limited solubility in aqueous environments, which may affect formulation strategies. Intestinal absorption in humans is moderate (45.69%), suggesting partial absorption within the gastrointestinal tract. L-mimosine acts as a P-glycoprotein substrate, meaning it is likely subject to efflux by this transporter, potentially reducing bioavailability. However, it is not a P-glycoprotein II inhibitor.
The fraction unbound (Fu) in humans is 0.524, meaning approximately 52.4% of the compound remains unbound in plasma and available for pharmacological action. Its CNS permeability is poor (−3.521 log PS), indicating limited ability to cross the blood–brain barrier, thus reducing the likelihood of central nervous system penetration.
L-mimosine is neither a substrate nor an inhibitor of key cytochrome P450 enzymes, including CYP3 A4, CYP2 C19, and CYP2D6, suggesting a low potential for drug-drug interactions via these metabolic pathways. The total clearance is 0.429 log ml/min/kg, reflecting a relatively slow rate of elimination from the body.
In terms of toxicity, L-mimosine does not exhibit AMES toxicity, indicating that it is not mutagenic. It is also not an inhibitor of hERG I channels, implying a low risk of cardiotoxicity. The compound's oral rat acute toxicity (LD50) is 2.166 mol/kg, which quantifies the lethal dose in preclinical animal models. Additionally, it is non-hepatotoxic and demonstrates low toxicity toward T. pyriformis (0.27 log µg/L), a model organism commonly used in toxicity assays. In summary, L-mimosine presents a favorable pharmacokinetic and toxicological profile with moderate absorption, minimal risk of drug-drug interactions, and low toxicity, making it a promising candidate for further development in cancer therapy.

## Determination of L-mimosine targets and breast cancer associated gene
The L-mimosine targets were predicted using the Pharmapper, ChEMBL and Swiss databases. Pharmmaper has identified a total of 270, ChEMBL has identified a total of 150 and Swiss database has identified a total of 100 target genes. After removing redundancies and duplicates between these databases a total 479 target genes were identified. To identify the breast cancer associated genes we used GeneCards and DisGeNET databases and a total of 14,387 genes breast cancer associated genes were retrieved. After retrieval, total of 426 intersection genes were identified (Fig.  ) that might regulate the progression of breast cancer and were further used to construct the PPI network. 

## Protein- protein network construction and hub gene expression analysis
The protein–protein interaction (PPI) network was constructed using the STRING database, which predicts potential interactions among the 426 targeted genes at the protein level. The resulting network comprised 425 nodes and 720 edges, with an average node degree of 3.39 and a local clustering coefficient of 0.443. PPI enrichment analysis revealed a highly significant p-value (< 1.0e-16), indicating strong functional connectivity among the proteins. In this network, each node represents an individual protein, while each edge indicates an interaction between two proteins, where a higher degree value reflects a more prominent role of the corresponding protein in the network. To ensure high-confidence interactions, a filtering criterion of a combined score > 0.900 was applied, resulting in the identification of 236 core target genes, characterized by superior network topology parameters.
The visualization and analysis of the network were conducted using Cytoscape software. To identify the hub genes, the network was imported into the cytoHubba plug-in of Cytoscape (Fig.  ). Using this tool, 19 genes were identified as hub genes (Table  ), as they were present in at least two of the cytoHubba ranking methods. Among these, the Serine-Threonine Kinase (SRC) gene emerged as the most prominent hub, appearing in eight different cytoHubba approaches. Additionally, the genes AKT1, MAPK8, and PRKACA were identified in seven approaches; BCL2L1 appeared in six approaches; Bax was detected in five methods; and GRB2, BCL-2, BID, and HSP90 AA1 were each found in four methods. Furthermore, EGFR, RHOA, HK1, PDE4D, HRAS, and ITGB2 were identified in three methods, while APRT and RAF were present in two methods. 

## Go and KEGG pathway analysis-
To verify the existence of the hub gene in several biological pathways, we also carried out GO and module analyses to identify the biological mechanisms, cellular constituents, and molecular functions associated with the potential genes (Fig.  ). Initially, a review of the biological processes involving 19 selected genes reveals that these processes include protein localization to the membrane, negative regulation of the apoptotic process, negative regulation of programmed cell death, response to endogenous stimuli, protein localization to mitochondria, response to compounds containing oxygen, and regulation of intracellular signal transduction. Hub genes relate to dendritic development cones in the cellular component. Dendritic terminal, cytoplasmic side of the membrane of the ruffle membrane, outer side of the membrane of the mitochondria and organelles, focal adhesion, and anchoring junction are all components of the BH3 family protein complex. Regarding the molecular function category, hub genes play a role in ATPase binding that affects protein kinase activity, transferase activity, adenyl nucleotide binding, BH3 domain binding, death domain binding, and Rho GDP-dissociation inhibiting binding. Lastly, the 19-hub gene KEGG pathway involves several cancer pathways, EGFR tyrosine kinase, ErbB signalling pathway, sphingolipid signalling system, neutrophil signalling pathway, Ras signalling pathway, and other processes related to cell death. These pathways work together in intricate networks, and when they are dysregulated, the growth of breast cancer cells can become uncontrolled. Gaining insight into these processes is essential for creating specialized treatments that block elements of these pathways in order to regulate tumor growth.

## MD simulation test
Docking scores primarily predict the binding affinity at a single conformation, but they don't provide information on the stability of the complex. MD simulations can help assess the stability of the docked complex by observing how the ligand and receptor interact over time under physiological conditions. This can reveal if the complex remains stable or if the ligand dissociates, providing insights into the robustness of the binding. In the current research, the complex of AKT1 with its standard inhibitor MK-2206 and selected compound L mimosine was forwarded for MD simulation.
The results of the MD simulation were analyzed based on 3 main perspectives including RMSD in Å, bond formation pattern of ligands, and deviation of the RMSD graph. From an RMSD perspective, both MK-2206 and L mimosine gave an almost similar result which was between 13–14 Å during 100 ns but L mimosine consistently bound with the target pocket while MK-2206 left the pocket between 20–28 ns (Fig.  ). After 28 ns MK-2206 showed more instability in a pocket during the whole journey of 100 ns. So even with the same RMSD number, L mimosine gave better stability and binding with the target. From a deviation perspective, L mimosine deviates between the standard and acceptable range of 1–3 Å indicating its stability while MK-2206 reaches double the value after 40 ns showing its movement in the pocket. From a bond formation pattern perspective, MK-2206 consistently continues its bonding with GLU:278 and breaks its bond at 35 ns with GLU:341 and TYR:315. It also forms a new bond with PHE: 442 from 25 ns, GLU: 234 from 40 ns, and PHE:161 from 40 ns (Fig.  ). L mimosine is very strongly stable with GLU: 314, HIS: 354, and GLU: 355 during the whole simulation of 100 ns, the pattern of this bond formation depicted the movement of ligands in the binding pocket. From all three perspectives, L mimosine bound very well with greater stability with a target for desired inhibition.

## L-mimosine inhibits cell proliferation in MCF-7 cells
Abnormal cellular proliferation is the hallmark of cancer cells, and controlling this growth could be an effective anticancer strategy. To evaluate the anticancer activity of L-mimosine (0–1000 µg/ml), MCF-7 cells were exposed to varying concentrations of the compound for 24 h. Following this exposure, an MTT cellular proliferation assay was performed to determine percent cell viability and calculate the IC50 value. The results showed a gradual decrease in the percent viability of MCF-7 cells as the concentration of L-mimosine increased (Fig.  ). Specifically, 50% cell death (IC50) of MCF-7 cells was observed at a concentration of 188 µg/ml (Fig.  .) This indicates that L-mimosine effectively inhibits the proliferation of MCF-7 cells, highlighting its potential as an anticancer agent. 

## L-mimosine Inhbits the cell migration on MCF-7 Cell Line
The impact of L-mimosine on the migratory ability of MCF-7 cells was evaluated using a wound-healing assay. In this assay, MCF-7 cells were exposed to various concentrations of L-mimosine for 24 h. After this period, the extent of wound repair was assessed and compared between the control and treated cells. As illustrated in (Fig.  ), cells treated with L-mimosine showed a reduced rate of wound healing, with this effect becoming more pronounced at higher concentrations of the compound. In contrast, the control cells exhibited nearly complete wound repair after 24 h. These findings indicate that L-mimosine inhibits the migration ability of MCF-7 cells in a dose-dependent manner. 

## L-mimosine induced DNA-strand breaks on MCF-7 cell Line
If the proliferation of cancer cells is impacted by drug treatment, DNA damage could be a likely cause. To investigate the DNA damage induced by L-mimosine, a single-cell gel electrophoresis (comet assay) was performed, which enables the detection of DNA fragmentation resulting from various types of DNA damage. MCF-7 cells were incubated with different concentrations of L-mimosine for 24 h, and DNA damage, such as the percentage of tail DNA, was evaluated. Our comet assay data revealed that treatment with L-mimosine significantly induced DNA tail formation in a dose-dependent manner compared to the control MCF-7 cells. As shown in Fig.  , the percentage of tail DNA increased significantly, reaching up to 25% Tail DNA, compared to the control. MCF-7 cells that had been incubated with 200, 250, 300, and 350 µg/ml of L-mimosine showed comet with the formation of more tail content than head content, indicating much more extensive DNA damage by single- strand breaks. 

## Nuclear staining
MCF-7 cells, morphological alterations were examined using Hoechst 33,342 nuclear staining and propidium Iodide (PI) (Fig.  ). Following 24 h of treatment with L-mimosine at concentrations of 200 µg/mL, 250 µg/mL, 300 µg/mL, and 350 µg/mL, the treated cells exhibited a spherical morphology, with many cells displaying intact membranes. In contrast, the control cells displayed normal development, with clear cellular structures observed under the inverted light microscope. Hoechst staining further revealed hallmark apoptotic features, including nuclear condensation, DNA fragmentation, and the presence of perinuclear apoptotic bodies. ## Apoptosis analysis
The results of the apoptosis study using L-Mimosine at varying concentrations (200 µg/ml, 250 µg/ml, 350 µg/ml) compared to the control. The control group, which was not treated with L-Mimosine, showed baseline levels of apoptosis, serving as the reference for evaluating the effects of the drug. treatment with 200 µg/ml of L-Mimosine led to a slight increase in apoptosis compared to the control, indicating the initial impact of the drug on promoting cell death. At this concentration, a more noticeable increase in apoptosis was observed compared to the 200 µg/ml group, demonstrating a dose-dependent effect of L-Mimosine in inducing apoptosis. The highest concentration of L-Mimosine (350 µg/ml) resulted in a significant increase in apoptosis (Fig.  ). This indicates a strong dose-dependent response, with higher concentrations of the drug leading to greater levels of cell death. These results suggest that L-Mimosine induces apoptosis in a dose-dependent manner, with higher concentrations promoting more pronounced cell death. 

## Cell cycle analysis
MCF-7 cells were subjected to flow cytometry analysis following treatment with L-mimosine at concentrations of 200, 250, and 350 µg/mL for 24 h. The results demonstrated a significant, dose-dependent accumulation of cells in the S phase, indicating S phase arrest. Specifically, the percentage of cells in the S phase increased to 13%, 17.7%, and 23.26% at 200, 250, and 350 µg/mL of L-mimosine, respectively, compared to 10.27% in the untreated control (Fig.  ). These findings collectively suggest that L-mimosine effectively inhibits cell proliferation by inducing S phase cell cycle arrest and may also promote apoptosis in MCF-7 cells. 

## Discussion
Breast cancer is highly diverse and heterogeneous, leading to varied clinical presentations and outcomes for individual patients [ ]. Advanced stages of breast cancer can metastasize throughout the body via blood vessels and lymphatics, often resulting in cancer-related mortality [ ]. By 2050, an estimated 3.2 million new cases of female breast cancer are expected to be diagnosed annually worldwide [ ]. Therapeutic backbones vary based on the clinical tumor subtype and include endocrine therapy, anti-HER2 targeting, and chemotherapy [ ]. In contemporary breast cancer therapy, a multidisciplinary approach is utilized, leading to significant reductions in mortality due to improved treatment protocols. Current clinical efforts are directed towards developing combinations of immunotherapies that can convert non-responders into responders [ ]. However, several critical challenges persist [ , ]. These include severe side effects, the presence of difficult-to-treat tumour subtypes, intratumor heterogeneity, and the currently incurable nature of metastatic cancer. One significant challenge in developing breast cancer treatments is creating medications that minimize adverse effects [ , ].
Tribal knowledge and the conventional Ayurvedic and Siddha medical systems both mention the usage of numerous therapeutic plants for a variety of clinical conditions. Research indicates that natural compounds obtained from herbal plants can be employed as efficient substitutes [ , ]. Bioactive dietary componds like kampherol and apigenin inhibit triple negative breast cancer by targeting SIRTs [ ]. Natural compound like 6-glucan, andrographolide, silibinin, mimosine and trans-anethole showed synergistic effects with paclitaxel (PTX) against metastatic breast cancer, [ , ]. The role of CDK6 in cancer progression has been investigated, identifying Ellagic Acid as a potent CDK6 inhibitor [ ]. Similarly, the potential of Myricetin as a kinase inhibitor, specifically targeting MARK4, has been explored in breast and lung cancer cell lines, highlighting its promising therapeutic implications [ ]. Additionally, MSID000025, a compound derived from fungal secondary metabolites, has been demonstrated as a promising CDK4/6 inhibitor candidate in a breast cancer cell line [ ]. Furthermore, CMNPD11585 and CMNPD2744, derived from marine natural products, have been identified as potential CDK4/6 inhibitors. In silico studies have demonstrated their strong binding affinity and stability, while in vitro experiments have confirmed their effective cytotoxicity against MCF7 breast cancer [ ]. Synethesized novel novel 3-N-aryl substituted-2-heteroarylchromones as inhibitors of MARK4, a crucial target in breast cancer treatment [ ]. The phytochemical analysis of Senecio laetus Edgew, aerial fractions revealed bioactive compounds with antioxidant and antiproliferative potential against MCF-7 breast cancer cells, indicating their therapeutic significance [ ]. The anti-proliferative effects of alkaloid-rich extracts from Senna didymobotrya leaves on MCF-7 breast cancer cells, both independently and in combination with laser irradiation [ ].
In this study, a network pharmacology approach was employed to construct a compound-disease-target-pathway network to identify potential targets of L-mimosine for breast cancer treatment via the regulation of multiple pathways [ ]. Target identification for breast cancer and L-mimosine was performed using multiple databases, identifying a total of 426 targets through compound-target-disease network analysis via Venn diagram.
The STRING tool, which predicts potential gene interactions at the protein level, was used to analyze the 426 identified genes [ ]. The study narrowed down to 19 key genes based on their presence in at least two CytoHubba methods, with SRC, AKT1, MAPK8, and PRKACA being the top four. The network and sub-networks identified by this topological analytic technique can provide new insights into critical regulatory networks and potential protein therapeutic targets.
Following target identification, targets were annotated in cellular processes, biological regulation, response to stimuli, and signalling within the Biological Process category. Module analysis and GO analysis of these 19 genes highlighted their roles in the Ras signalling pathway, chemical carcinogenesis activity, and apoptosis pathways. Subsequently, the four most frequent targets (SRC, AKT1, MAPK8, PRKACA) were analyzed for docking with L-mimosine. Molecular docking confirmed the accuracy of the interactions between L-mimosine and these targets, with AKT1 showing a particularly strong affinity. SRC family kinases (SFKs) play a crucial role in breast cancer progression, with SRC serving as a key proto-oncogene involved in various aspects of breast cancer development, including tumor growth, metastasis, drug resistance, and stemness regulation [ , ]. Study highlights the involvement of the PI3 K/Akt and MAPK/ERK1/2 signaling pathways in IGF1-induced VEGF-C upregulation, emphasizing their crucial roles in promoting lymphatic metastasis in breast cancer [ , , ]. Increased PRKACA expression correlated with clinical resistance in breast cancer samples. High PRKACA levels indicated intrinsic resistance in some HER2-positive cancers. Targeting PRKACA and its downstream effectors could have helped prevent the emergence of resistance [ – ]. These four hub genes are playing crucial role in breast cancer progression and inhibit apoptosis in breast cancer.
To further validate the docking study, MD simulations were conducted for 100 ns to assess the interaction efficacy and complex stability between L-mimosine and AKT1. The simulation results indicated promising outcomes, with RMSD values within a reasonable range during complex formation, confirming the stable and optimal protein–ligand interaction. Growth factors like epidermal growth factor (EGF) can activate AKT signalling, which in turn trigger progression [ ]. Downstream signalling cascades that promote cell survival and proliferation in a variety of cell types, including mammary epithelial cells [ ].Overexpression and hyperactivation of the serine/threonine protein kinase B (AKT) pathway are common in breast cancer progression [ ]. Targeting AKT represents an attractive treatment option for many breast cancer subtypes, especially those resistant to conventional treatments [ ]. The human AKT kinase family comprises three distinct genes (AKT1, AKT2, and AKT3) located on chromosomes 14, 19, and 1, respectively [ , ]. AKT1 knockdown inhibits tumor growth by blocking cell-cycle progression and/or promoting apoptosis in breast cancer models [ ]. Overexpression and/or constitutive activation of AKT1 in mammary epithelial cells inhibit pro-apoptotic signals and activate survival signals, supporting tumorigenesis [ , ]. Transgenic mouse studies suggest AKT1's significant role in the initiation, development, and progression of breast tumours [ ]. Hyperactivated AKT1 pathways are strongly correlated with breast cancer initiation and development [ , ]. AKT1 depletion leads to the upregulation of specific genes, many of which are downregulated in breast tumours with high AKT1 levels and upregulated in tumours with no detectable AKT expression [ ].
To complement bioinformatics analysis, which may not fully capture the effects of L-mimosine on breast cancer, we conducted biological experiments to assess its impact on MCF-7 cells. A series of assays were performed, including a cell proliferation assay to measure growth inhibition, a comet assay to assess DNA damage, and a scratch assay to evaluate cell migration. Additionally, nuclear staining and apoptosis assays were conducted to confirm apoptosis induction by L-mimosine, while cell cycle analysis was performed to investigate potential cell cycle arrest. These results confirm that L-mimosine inhibits cell proliferation and promotes apoptosis in a dose-dependent manner.
This study employed a network pharmacology approach to identify potential targets of L-mimosine for breast cancer therapy. Multiple targets were implicated in various pathways contributing to breast cancer development and progression [ , ]. The identified targets and pathways are crucial for the anticancer potential of L-mimosine against breast cancer. The interactions between L-mimosine and these targets demonstrated stability and acceptable binding affinities and anti-proliferation activity of L-mimosne, indicating its potential efficacy as an anti-breast cancer agent. SRC, AKT1, PRKACA, and MAPK8 are the potential hub genes that the in silico research found to stimulate proliferation and prevent apoptosis in breast cancer cells. L-mimosine's antiproliferative effects on breast cancer cells were consistently demonstrated through in vitro cell viability experiments. Using the comet assay, apoptosis analysis, and nuclear staining with Hoechst and PI staining, apoptosis induction was further verified, demonstrating a concentration-dependent shift of breast cancer cells toward apoptosis. These data show that the in vitro and in silico outcomes are consistent with one another.
This research offers an alternative strategy for targeting breast cancer through L-mimosine. The primary limitation of this study is the need for further molecular experiments and in vivo validation to strengthen the findings. While in silico and in vitro analyses, including cell proliferation, DNA damage, apoptosis, and cell cycle studies, highlight the multitargeted potential of L-mimosine in breast cancer, additional research is necessary. Furthermore, since this study is limited to cellular experiments, validating the identified hub genes through expression analysis and additional experimental verification is essential for strengthening the findings.

## Conclusion
This study used in vitro studies, molecular docking, network pharmacology, and molecular dynamics simulations to investigate the molecular processes of L-mimosine in breast cancer. GO and KEGG analysis revealed important pathways associated with apoptosis, cancer signalling, and EGFR tyrosine kinase resistance, out of the 426 possible targets we found. Docking and MD simulations revealed that AKT1 was the most promising target, while SRC, AKT1, MAPK8, and PRKACA were the top hub genes. The dose-dependent inhibition of MCF-7 cells by L-mimosine was validated by in vitro studies. More in vivo research is necessary to confirm L-mimosiane's anticancer processes, as these results suggest that it may be a multitarget therapeutic agent for breast cancer.
