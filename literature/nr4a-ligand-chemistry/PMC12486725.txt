## Background
Autoantibodies against envelope (Env) protein encoded by human endogenous retrovirus group K (HERV-K) are prevalent in rheumatoid arthritis (RA) and systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), but it remains unclear which proviruses are responsible for this autoantigen. It also remains poorly understood how the transcription of HERV-K loci is regulated in cells that can produce Env.

## Results
We aligned our neutrophil RNA sequencing data to the new telomere-to-telomere reference genome and found uniquely mapping transcripts from HERV-K101, K102, K104, K108, K109, K117 and ERVK5, of which only K102, K108, and K109 encode an intact Env. Expression of K102 and K108 were higher in SLE than in healthy donors or RA (padj < 0.05). Transcripts from these proviruses increased in response to interferon-α in monocytes and neutrophils from RA patients and healthy donors, but not in SLE, presumably because they have chronically elevated type I interferons in vivo. Indeed, HERV-K expression was significantly higher in SLE patients with high type I interferon gene signature. Tumor necrosis factor-α and other cytokines and TLR ligands also induced HERV-K102 and K108 transcripts. Interferon-α also increased detectable Env protein in monocytes, macrophages, and neutrophils from RA patients. Among the genes for epigenetic silencers of HERV-K, only TRIM28 was significantly decreased in SLE patients with high interferons (padj = 0.00024).

## Conclusions
Our data establish a role for interferons in maintaining increased HERV-K expression in SLE and suggest that interferons or other cytokines can upregulate HERV-K to similar levels in RA. A transient increase may also accompany normal immune responses, suggesting that endogenous retroviruses may have been co-opted for efficient immune responses.

## Background
In 1976 it was reported that an antigen from a C-type retrovirus was present in kidneys and spleen from a patient with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) [ , ]. Although these results were contested [ ], numerous reports of serum antibodies against retroviral reverse transcriptase [ ] and other retroviral antigens [ – ] were published over the following two decades. However, these studies remained imprecise and controversial [ ] until the cloning of several endogenous retroviruses [ – ] and the sequencing of the human genome [ ] revealed that as much as 8.3% of our genome consists of sequences referred to as endogenous retroviruses [ ].

Endogenous retroviruses are reverse-transcribed cDNA copies of the RNA genomes of exogenous retroviruses that infected the germline of our ancestors [ , ]. Following the initial infection, reverse transcription, and DNA repair, many of them likely were full-length 9.2 kb proviruses with the classical LTR-gag-pro-pol-env-LTR structure. However, over evolutionary time, the vast majority of them have been compromised by deletions, mutations, new insertions, and other genetic events [ ]. Over 95% are now present in our genome only as ‘solo-LTRs’ [ – ] generated by the annealing of the two long-terminal repeats (LTRs), which were initially identical to each other (being reverse-transcribed from the same single LTR in the circular RNA genome of the exogenous retrovirus), followed by the excision of the loop formed between them. Approximately 300,000 solo-LTRs now constitute parts of promoter and enhancer regions of many of our genes.

The numerous genetic alterations that have incapacitated our endogenous retroviruses [ ], such that none of them are infectious anymore [ ], have left only a few with intact open reading frames (ORFs) for proteins that retain any of their original functionalities. The most recently integrated [ ] members of the Human Mouse Mammary Tumor Virus-Like 2 (HML-2) subfamily of the human endogenous retrovirus group K (HERV-K) family [ ] are the most intact. These young HML-2 proviruses are human-specific and still capable of producing at least some of the retroviral proteins [ ], some even produce viral particles [ , ]. These near-intact proviruses lost their virulence very recently, as was demonstrated by the fully infectious retrovirus produced by a synthetic consensus sequence [ ]. This “Phoenix” virus infects cells through heparan sulfate-containing surface receptors [ ] and reverse-transcribes its RNA genome and integrates it into the genome of the host cell, generating proviruses that are very similar to the existing young HML-2 loci [ ]. Another indication of recent infectivity is the insertional polymorphism of HERV-K113 [ , ] and HERV-K115 [ ], which exist in the genome of approximately 30% and 15% of modern humans, respectively, suggesting that they infected our ancestors after our species had become more geographically dispersed [ ]. There is also a seemingly intact HML-2 provirus on chromosome Xq21.33 [ ] in approximately 2% of people of recent African descent.
Endogenous retroviruses, particularly those that retain any functionality, are effectively silenced by DNA methylation [ ] and other epigenetic mechanisms [ , ] in healthy individuals, but many can be transcriptionally activated under certain circumstances, e.g., during early embryonic development [ ], in cancer [ , ], and in HIV-infected individuals [ – ]. Transcripts from endogenous retrovirus loci are also present at elevated levels in patients with rheumatoid arthritis (RA) [ , , ] and systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) [ , ].
The early work on retroviral antigens in patients with autoimmune conditions has been repeated more recently with more molecularly defined tools, which revealed that patients with SLE or RA indeed have autoantibodies that recognize group antigen (Gag) and envelope (Env) proteins derived from HERV-K/HML-2 loci [ – ]. We have replicated these findings with recombinant Env protein as antigen [ , ] and find that anti-Env autoantibodies are higher in patients with active disease, in smokers, and in RA in patients with seropositive disease. Early papers also detected HERV-K transcripts in RA leukocytes [ ], synovial fluid [ , ], and synovial tissue [ ], but could not resolve which genomic loci they originated from. Using RNA sequencing, we determined that at least 8 HERV-K/HML-2 loci are expressed at increased levels in RA [ ] and SLE [ ], but that only two of them have an intact env ORF, namely HERV-K102 and K108. Env protein encoded by these proviruses is present in a subset of neutrophils and a few monocytes isolated from RA patients [ ], but minimally, at best, in other immune cell lineages.
Here, we first re-examine the expression of HERV-K proviruses in SLE, RA, and healthy donor (HC) neutrophils by RNA sequencing and alignment with the newest telomere-to-telomere (T2T) genome sequence and then asked why HERV-K102 and K108 transcripts are basally elevated in SLE patients compared to RA or healthy donors, using type I interferons, cytokines, and TLR ligands. We measured K102 and K108 transcripts by quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction (qPCR), used immunofluorescence microscopy to detect Env protein, RNA sequencing to illuminate gene expression, and tested HERV-K102 transcriptional control with a luciferase reporter driven by the 5’ long-terminal repeat (LTR) of HERV-K102. Our hypothesis is that myeloid cells in SLE express increased levels of HERV-K because of the elevated type I interferons in this disease. In contrast, myeloid cells from RA patients express K102 and K108 at levels that are similar to healthy controls but respond more vigorously to cytokines or TLR ligands to reach the same levels as in SLE. During a normal immune response, myeloid cells from healthy donors also increase K102 and K108 expression, but, at least ex vivo, this is less pronounced and more transient than in RA cells.
The purpose of our study was to determine how dynamic HERV-K expression is in immune cells from patients with autoimmune conditions compared to healthy donors and to build a basis for further exploration of their epigenetic and transcriptional control and possible contribution to antiviral or autoimmune disorders.

## Results

## HERV-K provirus expression by RNAseq using the T2T genome
Using RNA sequencing and aligning reads to the recently released fuller version of the human genome termed T2T, the expression of HERV-K proviruses in neutrophils from SLE (n = 35), RA (n = 15), and age- and sex-matched HC (n = 20) confirmed that HERV-K102 and K108 are expressed at higher levels in SLE patients [ ] based on uniquely mapping reads. Using this more complete reference genome, we also find increased expression, albeit at very low read counts, of HERV-K101 (0–37 normalized uniquely mapping read counts in the individual patients), K104 (0–7.8 normalized read counts), K109 (0–29 normalized read counts), K117 (0–18 normalized read counts), and ERVK5 (0–12 normalized read counts) (Fig. A), compared to K102 (86–2496) and K108 (0–142). Nevertheless, K109 has intact env ORFs and may contribute to ~ 1% of any detectable Env protein if all HERV-K transcripts with an intact env are translated equally. HERV-K109 is a type 2 provirus, like HERV-K108, and their Env proteins only differ by 12 residues of 699. Figure B shows the ORFs of the seven detected HERV-K/HML-2 proviruses. 

## HERV-K102 expression is induced by type I interferon in monocytes and neutrophils
Transcripts from the HERV-K102 provirus on chromosome 1q22.1 can be detected by RNA sequencing and qPCR in immune cells from SLE [ ] and RA patients [ ]. This type 1 provirus has an intact env ORF lacking a 292-bp sequence, which is uniquely present in type 2 proviruses, and which encodes a very long (~ 100aa) leader sequence. For this reason, it is not clear that K102-encoded Env can be transported to the plasma membrane and, hence, it may not be the Env that we have detected by flow cytometry in intact (unpermeabilized) neutrophils from these patients [ ]. To gain more insight into the transcriptional regulation of this locus, we used qPCR and RNA isolated first from peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC) from RA patients, and subsequently from various immune cell lineages from patients with RA or SLE, or healthy donors. RNA quality was verified by absorbance at 260nm, 280nm, agarose gel electrophoresis, and a mock-reverse-transcribed sample was included as a negative control to exclude any contamination by genomic DNA. Unique forward and reverse primers [ ] were ordered for HERV-K102 transcript isoforms that contain env (Fig. A) and their selectivity for K102 was validated by Sanger sequencing of the 199-bp band from 7 SLE patients, which, except for a few individual single-nucleotide polymorphisms, matched K102 in the T2T reference genome. We used 18S RNA for normalization, as it was more stable under our conditions of stimulation than GAPDH and IPO8 (not shown). Nevertheless, normalizing our data against GAPDH gave very similar results. 
Activating the T cells in the PBMC cultures with anti-CD3 plus anti-CD28 did not induce HERV-K102 expression (Supplemental Fig. S1A), but a small effect was observed after the addition of 500 U/ml of interferon α (IFNα)(Supplemental Fig. S1A). To ask if the effect of IFNα in RA PBMC was modest because only a subset of immune cells responded, we purified monocytes, B cells, CD8 T cells, and (separately) neutrophils from RA and SLE patients, as well as from heathy donors, and tested them individually for HERV-K102 expression after treatment with IFNα. In purified CD8 T cells, HERV-K102 expression increased less than two-fold (Supplemental Fig. S1B), while, as a positive control, they reacted with a more than 60-fold induction of the IFNα-responsive gene IFI6 (Supplemental Fig. S1C).
IFNα induced a rapid, but transient, 2.5-fold increase in K102 transcript levels in healthy monocytes (Fig. B) and a 4.5-fold increase in RA monocytes (Fig. C). As a positive control, expression of IFI6 increased in a time-dependent manner after the addition of IFNα (Fig. D). In SLE monocytes, HERV-K102 expression was basally sevenfold higher compared to healthy and RA monocytes (Fig. E), and it did not increase after the addition of IFNα, but instead slowly declined (Fig. E). This is likely related to the already high levels of type I interferons in the majority of SLE patients (see Fig. ). For better visualization of these differences, Fig. F shows the time-course for healthy, RA, and SLE monocytes together and calculated as fold increase over the untreated healthy control. The effect of IFNα was dose-dependent with an EC50 of 0.1 ng/ml and reaching a plateau at 1 ng/ml both for HERV-K102 and IFI6 induction (Supplemental Fig. S1D and E).
Since monocytes remain viable for many days in cell culture, we also stimulated RA monocytes with IFNα for longer time-courses and observed that HERV-K102 expression was maintained at the elevated level for up to 2 days after the initial rapid increase (Fig. G). These data are statistically significant (p < 0.0001). In healthy donor monocytes the early rise was much smaller with HERV-K102 expression returning to basal levels by 8 h (Fig. G). This difference in magnitude of response was also seen in the expression of IFI6 (Fig. H). B cells stimulated with IFNα did not show any statistically significant change in HERV-K102 expression but became significant at the 48 h timepoint (Fig. I), while they responded much more robustly by induction of IFI6 (Fig. J), p < 0.0001.
IFNα also induced a rapid, but transient, tenfold increase in HERV-K102 expression in healthy donor neutrophils (Fig. A) and an increase in IFI6 expression (Fig. B), while the RA neutrophils reached the same tenfold activation at 2 h but then continued to rise up to 25-fold by 6 h (Fig. C). Similarly, the induction of IFI6 was more than 5 times higher in RA neutrophils than in healthy control neutrophils (Fig. D). 

## Effects of other cytokines and TLR ligands on HERV-K102 expression
Having found that IFNα induced an increase in HERV-K102 transcripts in both monocytes and neutrophils, we tested a range of other cytokines. As shown in Fig. E, IL-8 did not induce HERV-K102 transcripts, while the ligands for TLR4 (lipopolysaccharide; LPS) and TLR7 (imiquimod), as well as the cytokines IL-1β, IL-3, IL-6, IL-10, IL-22 (Fig. F), IL-4, IL-33, IFNγ, and GM-CSF (Fig. G) induced a statistically significant 2–fourfold increase in HERV-K102 transcripts. The effects of IL-3, IL-4, and IL-22 were small, while IL-8, IL-17, and IL-18 (Fig. F) did not have a significant effect. PAM3, an agonist for TLR1/2, caused a threefold increase in K102 transcripts, while the TLR5 agonist flagellin and the TLR2/6 agonist FSL-1 only had modest effects and C5a had none (data not shown). The thyroid hormone thyroxin induced a twofold increase in HERV-K102 transcripts peaking at 6 h (Supplemental Fig. S1G), while estradiol and progesterone did not have any significant effect (data not shown). In monocytes from healthy donors, all these TLR ligands and cytokines had a minimal effect on HERV-K102 expression (Supplemental Fig. S1J).

## Expression of HERV-K108/109 is similarly induced by interferon and cytokines
We also quantified transcripts from the type 2 provirus HERV-K108 using a forward primer located in the 292-bp region that is missing in K102 and other type 1 HML-2 proviruses (Fig. A). The leader sequence encoded by this 292-bp piece allows K108-encoded Env to be glycosylated in the Golgi and then transported to the cell surface. These primers also amplify transcripts from the type 2 proviruses HERV-K104 and K109 (Fig. A) both of which are expressed at considerably lower levels by RNA sequencing in neutrophils: K104 in 1 of 20 HC (average 0.05 ± 0.20 normalized read counts), 2 of 35 SLE (0.32 ± 1.41 normalized read counts), and 3 of 15 RA (0.20 ± 0.45 normalized read counts) patients. These values are below cut-offs typically considered in RNA sequencing. The expression of K109 was > 0 in 3 HC (0.26 ± 0.84), 12 SLE (2.28 ± 5.29), and 1 RA (0.22 ± 0.87) compared to the 24.35 ± 25.40, 49.66 ± 47.98, and 26.73 ± 25.0 normalized read counts for K108, thus contributing no more than 1.3% in HC, 5.24% in SLE, 1.6% in RA of the signal obtained with these primers. 
In agreement with our previous paper [ ], the expression of K108 relative to the house-keeping 18S RNA was much lower than K102, even considering that HERV-K104 and K109 transcripts may have contributed to the signal in a some patients. In RA neutrophils, K108 transcripts increased in response to IFNα and TNFα over 6 h (Fig. B). For comparison, K102 transcripts increased with a faster time-course and to higher levels than K108 in response to TNFα (Fig. C). Interestingly, TNFα also induced K108 in SLE neutrophils (Fig. D), as well as K102 (Supplemental Fig. S1F). Compared to K102, K108 transcripts also increased in response to many cytokines (Fig. E and F), but their relative potencies were somewhat different: LPS was less active on K108 than K102 (1.5 versus 3.5-fold) and IFNγ induced K102 fourfold but not K108, while IL-6 had a larger impact on K108 than on K102 expression (3.5 versus 2.5-fold). Healthy donor monocytes responded very little to these stimuli (Supplemental Fig. S1K).

## Induction of detectable Env in myeloid cells by IFNα
To evaluate if the increased HERV-K transcripts in interferon-treated monocytes and neutrophils are also translated into increased amounts of the Env protein, we stained untreated or IFNα-treated RA monocytes with a specific anti-Env mAb (Kenv6) plus a fluorescently labeled secondary antibody. This antibody recognizes the pre-fusion conformation of Env (manuscript submitted). These experiments showed that the majority of untreated monocytes lacked detectable Env protein (Fig. A), while a subset of the treated monocytes displayed a specific staining of Env in a granular pattern (Fig. B). The secondary antibody alone did not stain the cells (Fig. J). Quantitation of monocytes with any detectable Env staining showed that less than 10% of the untreated RA monocytes were positive, while this increased to an average of 70%, with a broad range between different fields of view, after 4 h of IFNα treatment (Fig. C). This difference was statistically significant (p < 0.001). 
Monocytes from RA patients maintained in cell culture for 3 days differentiated into adherent macrophage-like cells with a much larger diameter and a more rounded nucleus. These cells were more frequently Env-positive (Fig. D), but much more strikingly so after 4 h of IFNα treatment (Fig. E). This difference was statistically significant (Fig. F). A closer inspection of images of RA macrophage treated with IFNα for 4 h and stained with anti-Env revealed a number of small dots along the plasma membrane of the cell (white arrows in Supplemental Fig. S2A), perhaps representing virions or partial virions of HERV-K.
Similarly, immunofluorescence staining of neutrophils for Env revealed that untreated RA neutrophils had detectable Env in a portion of the cells (Fig. G), as we have reported before using flow cytometry [ ], but that IFNα-treated neutrophils contained clearly more Env (Fig. F) in a majority of the cells (Fig. I). Flow cytometry of non-permeabilized IFNα-treated neutrophils with the Kenv6 mAb showed a much smaller percentage of positive cells (~ 1%), indicating that the increased Env largely remained intracellular. Healthy donor monocytes or neutrophils did not contain any detectable Env (not shown).
Neutrophils from untreated SLE patients contained Env with a very similar distribution to IFNα-treated RA neutrophils (Supplemental Fig. S2B), while healthy donor neutrophils were devoid of any detectable Env (Supplemental Fig. S2B, right hand panels). The secondary antibody alone did not stain anything (Supplemental Fig. S2B, lower panels).

## Expression of HERV-K silencing genes and relevant transcription factors
To gain more insight into the mechanisms leading to the higher basal expression of HERV-K102 and K108 in SLE neutrophils and monocytes compared to healthy cells, as well as its response to cytokines, we examined the expression of known epigenetic and transcriptional regulators of HERV-K transcription [ , ] (Fig. A). The expression of the 3 DNA methyl transferases DNMT1, DNMT3A and DNMT3B were unchanged in RA and SLE neutrophils compared to neutrophils from healthy donors (Fig. B). In contrast, one of the genes in the ‘human silencing hub’ (HUSH) epigenetic modifier complex [ – ], TRIM28, was downregulated in a statistically significantly (padj = 0.00024) manner in SLE neutrophils (Fig. B), particularly in those with high interferon (Supplemental Fig. S3), but not in RA, while the other components SETDB1, PPHLN1, and TASOR, as well as ZNF93 were similar in all three groups (Fig. B). MPP8 transcripts were not detected in neutrophils. The low TRIM28 expression may be why HERV-K102/K108 expression was basally elevated in SLE myeloid cells compared to RA and healthy cells. 
In the absence of full epigenetic repression, transcription factors that are predicted to bind to motifs in the 5’LTR [ ] include p53, NF-κB, YY1, c-MYC, NR4 (only in K102), OCT, AP-1 (i.e. FOS and JUN), SP1, as well as the nuclear hormone receptors for estrogen, progesterone, glucocorticoids, and thyroxin (Fig. C). Transcripts for TP53, NFKB1, NFKB2, REL, RELA, RELB, YY1, MYC, NR4A1 (also known as Nur77), NR4A2, NR4A3, FOS, JUN, ESR1, ESR2, NRC31, NRC32, THRA, THRB, were unaltered between the three groups, while JUNB was lower in SLE neutrophils than in HC neutrophils (padj = 0.015).
The 5’LTR is also predicted to contain two interferon response elements (ISRE), including binding motifs for STAT1 and IRF family members [ ]. The genes for STAT1 and STAT2 were much upregulated in SLE neutrophils (padj = 9.72 × 10–5 and padj = 1.0 × 10–4, respectively) compared to healthy neutrophils. IRF3 was downregulated in SLE neutrophils (padj = 0.012), while IRF5 and IRF7 were upregulated (both padj = 0.0002)(Fig. D).
All these data point to weakened epigenetic silencing of the HERV-K102 5’LTR in SLE neutrophils, but not in RA neutrophils, compared to healthy neutrophils. However, the differences we observe in HERV-K102 induction in RA neutrophils compared to healthy neutrophils cannot be readily explained by differences in expression of epigenetic silencers or transcription factors.

## HERV-K promoter 5’LTR reporter gene induction
Next, we constructed a luciferase reporter gene under the control of the entire 1,110-bp 5’LTR of HERV-K102 (Fig. A). This reporter construct was first tested in 293HEK cells, which showed measurable luciferase activity above the negative control (not shown). When introduced into the macrophage-like THP-1 cell line and stimulated after 18 h with IFNα for 4 h, firefly luciferase activity was measurable and increased in the IFNα-treated samples compared to the untreated ones (Fig. A). Next, we truncated the 5’LTR construct to start from nucleotides 301 or 601, respectively. The LTRΔ300-luc construct responded to IFNα similarly to the full-length 5’LTR-luc reporter (Fig. B), but the Δ600 construct was not responsive (Fig. C), suggesting that the IFN-responsive portion of the 5’LTR was located between nucleotides 300 and 600, which is where the predicted IFN-response elements are located. 

## HERV-K provirus expression is higher in SLE patient with a type I interferon gene signature
To ask if the induction of HERV-K expression by IFNα in cell culture is relevant in patients with autoimmune disease, we segregated the SLE patients included in our RNAseq data set (n = 35) into those who have an interferon gene signature indicative of elevated IFNα or lack such a signature. The average expression of 111 interferon induced genes (ISGs) was calculated per sample and SLE samples were considered having high interferon signatures if the average expression of ISGs was higher than the 90th percentile of ISG expression in healthy controls, which was the case in 27 of the 35 patients. As shown in Fig. D-J, the average expression of HERV-K101, K102, K108, K117 and ERVK5 were all higher in ISG-high SLE patient than in patients with ISG expression in the healthy control range, but the difference was only statistically significant for HERV-K102 and HERV-K108. These data support the notion that type I interferons upregulate the expression of many HERV-K proviruses, which all have highly homologous 5’LTR sequences, also in vivo in SLE patients with elevated interferons. It has been suggested [ , ] that HERV-K transcripts or proteins, such as the reverse transcriptase (pol), can drive type I interferon production. Our present data indicate the reverse relationship: type I interferons induce HERV-K expression in vitro and in vivo. The two directions of causality are not mutually exclusive.

## Discussion
The presence of IgG autoantibodies against the Env protein encoded by HERV-K in patients with autoimmune disorders implies that this protein has been present and that it elicited an immune response typically not seen in healthy individuals. Our finding that neutrophils from SLE [ ] and RA [ ] patients express HERV-K102 and, to a lesser extent, HERV-K108 and a few patients also K109, makes these three proviruses the most likely sources of Env protein. Indeed, mass spectrometry of anti-Env immunoprecipitates identified several peptides that match all three proviruses and cover both N-terminal (from aa 120) and C-terminal regions of them (Supplemental Fig. S4). In addition, one peptide was unique to K102, one unique to K108, and two unique to K109 (Supplemental Fig. S4) supporting the notion that all three proviruses contribute to Env production. We also note that using the new T2T human reference genome enabled us to detect K109 expression in SLE neutrophils, but that it is expressed at 20-fold lower levels than K108 and 100-fold lower than K102 (Fig. ), with read counts > 0 in only 12 of 35 SLE patients. Several additional HERV-K proviruses are transcriptionally active in SLE and RA but cannot be the source of Env protein since their transcripts, even if appropriately spliced, cannot be translated into full-length Env due to premature stop-codons or deletion of the entire env sequence. Identifying HERV-K102 and K108 as the proviruses responsible for producing the majority of the Env protein that we have detected by flow cytometry [ ] or, in the present paper by immunofluorescence microscopy (Fig. ), enables us to study their transcriptional regulation. This, in turn, will eventually help elucidate why immunogenic Env protein is produced.
Our data are in agreement with the well-recognized silencing of HERV-K expression by a DNA methylation-dependent mechanism in healthy individuals [ , , – ], likely also in immune cells. The induction of HERV-K transcripts by type I interferons and TNFα, as well as other cytokines and TLR ligands, is a new finding with potential consequences for the immune system. It is tempting to speculate that HERV-K expression may serve as an amplification mechanism in the defense against exogenous viruses. This notion is supported by a role of HERV-K encoded Np9 (a splice variant of type 1 Env) in suppressing Epstein-Barr virus [ ], which also transactivates HERV-K [ ]. Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) also activates HERV-K expression [ , , , ], at least in part through transcriptional stimulation by the HIV Tat protein [ , ]. In addition, HERV-K encoded Gag interferes with HIV Gag function [ ] and T cells specific for HERV-K encoded proteins are important in controlling HIV [ , ].
Immunity against HERV-K Env is found in several cancers, including breast cancer [ , ] and lung cancer [ ]. In the latter, the immune response against HERV-K102 Env is instrumental in the clinical efficacy of pembrolizumab (anti-PD-1) and survival of lung cancer patients [ ]. It is also intriguing that HERV-K102 expression is increased in breast cancer [ ] as well as in RA [ ] and SLE [ , ], all female-dominated conditions. Env protein encoded by HERV-K102 is induced by the combination of estradiol and progesterone in breast cancer cells [ ] and then readily recognized by anti-Env antibodies affinity-purified from RA sera [ ]. However, estrogen and progesterone failed to induce HERV-K102 expression in RA monocytes (not shown), perhaps because they had already acted in the female patients (75% of patients). Anti-Env antibodies and Env-specific T cells are also present in breast cancer patients [ , ]. Hence, HERV-K102 encoded Env and an immune response against it are shared features of these conditions, prompting questions about a deeper connection between this malignancy and autoimmunity. Is it, for example, possible that an immune response against HERV-K expressing malignant or premalignant cells later prompts immunity against non-malignant innate immune cells expressing Env?
A paper by Chuong and co-workers [ ] reported that several LTRs contain an interferon-responsive element, in agreement with our findings. However, this study did not include the human-specific 5’ LTRs of K102, K108, of K109, but instead focused on solo-LTRs of the MER family and the regulation of interferon γ-activated genes located close to MER LTRs. Perhaps interferon responsive LTRs are more widespread beyond the 5’LTRs of the young HML-2 HERV-K group studied in our paper.
Our study has some caveats. First, the young HML-2 loci are so similar (except for deleted parts) that it is challenging to construct PCR primers that are completely specific for any individual provirus. While our primers for K102 were highly specific among the set of HERV-K transcripts detected by RNAseq in neutrophils, our primers for HERV-K108 also match HERV-K104 and K109, two highly homologous and recently acquired type 2 HERV-K transcripts. While the former has an incomplete env ORF with an early stop codon, K109 may contribute to a small extent to the production of type 2 Env in up to 20% of SLE patient neutrophils.
Second, although our LTR-luciferase reporter responded to IFNα (Fig. ) and actinomycin D completely prevented any induction of K102 expression (data not shown), we cannot categorically conclude that the effect of IFNα we observe is mediated solely by stimulation of HERV-K transcription. However, we did not see any increase in HERV-K102 transcripts in RA monocytes treated with NMDI14, a specific inhibitor of NMD, arguing against reduced mRNA degradation by NMD as a central mechanism.

## Conclusions
In conclusion, our data support the hypothesis that several young HERV-K proviruses, notably K102, K108, and K109, are expressed at elevated levels in SLE myeloid cells because their transcription is stimulated by the chronically increased type I interferons in the majority of patients. In contrast, myeloid cells from RA patients require cytokine or TLR stimulation to reach the same elevated levels of HERV-K transcripts. In addition, this induction is longer lasting and, in monocytes, of higher magnitude than in healthy donors. Nevertheless, even healthy immune responses may be accompanied by upregulation of HERV-K transcripts and perhaps the encoded proteins, such as Env. Given that endogenous retroviruses have been present in our genome for tens of millions of years, it would not be surprising if the immune system had evolved beneficial uses for them, such as in the defense against exogenous viruses.

## Methods

## Human subjects
Venous blood was obtained for RNAseq from de-identified SLE patients (n = 35), RA patients (n = 17), and healthy volunteers (n = 20) and for RNA isolation and immunofluorescence experiments from SLE, RA, and healthy controls (approximately n = 15 each) through the University of Washington Rheumatology Biorepository with approval of the Institutional Review Board (STUDY00006196). Informed written consent was obtained from all participants according to the Declaration of Helsinki.

## Isolation of monocytes and neutrophils
Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC) and neutrophils were isolated from freshly drawn venous blood by gradient centrifugation on PolymorphPrep (CosmoBio, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Cells were washed and suspended in Dulbecco’s phosphate-buffered saline at 107/ml. Monocytes, B cells, and CD8-postive T cells were purified from the PBMC fraction by negative selection using the EasySep Human monocyte enrichment kit without CD16 depletion (cat#19,058), EasySep Human B cell enrichment kit (cat# 19,054), and EasySep Human CD8 enrichment kit (cat# 19,053) from Stem Cell Technologies. The purity of the resulting cell preparations was verified by flow cytometry and consistently exceeded 95%.

## Stimulation of PBMCs, monocytes, B cells, CD8-positive T cells and neutrophils
Freshly isolated PBMCs, monocytes, B cells, CD8-positive T cells or neutrophils were seeded in 24-well or 48-well plates suspended in RPMI 1640 media (Gibco cat# 11,875,093), supplemented with 10% Fetal Bovine Serum (FBS; Atlanta Biologicals cat# S11150H) and 1 × Antibiotic/Antifungal (Cytiva Cat# SV30079.01). Cells were then stimulated with 500U/mL of human recombinant interferon alpha 2b (IFNα−2b; abcam cat# ab187208) either alone or in combination with Human T-Activator CD3/CD28 Dynabeads (Gibco cat# 11131D) at a bead to cell ratio of one to one, 1–30 µM 5-aza-deoxycytidine (5-aza-dC; Sigma-Aldrich cat #A3656) either alone or in combination with 500 U/ml of IFNα−2b (abcam cat# ab187208), 20 ng/ml IFN-γ (Invivogen cat# rcyec-hifng), 100 ng/ml lipopolysaccharide (LPS; Invitrogen eBioscience cat# 501,122,025), 10 µg/ml imiqumod (Invivogen cat# R837), 8 ng/ml tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α; R&D Systems cat# 210-TA/CF), 100 ng/ml human granulocyte–macrophage colony stimulating Factor (GM-CSF; PeproTech Cat# 300–3), 10 ng/ml IL-1β (R&D systems cat# 20-1LB-010), 10 ng/ml IL-3 (R&D systems cat# 203IL010CF), 10 ng/ml IL-4 (PeproTech cat#200–04), 10 ng/ml IL-6 (abcam cat# ab259381), 100 ng/ml IL-8 (eBioscience cat# 14–8089-63), 100 ng/ml IL-10 (R&D systems cat# 1064ILB010), 60 ng/ml IL-17A (R&D systems cat# 79-55I-L010CF), 100 ng/ml IL-18 (R&D systems cat# 91-24I-L010CF), 100 ng/ml IL-22 (R&D systems cat# 782IL010CF), or 50 ng/ml IL-33 (R&D systems cat# 3625IL010CF). An unstimulated control was also included at 0h. Cells were then incubated at 37 °C, with 5% CO2 for 2 to 48 h. After each time points, cells were harvested and suspended in 300 µl 1 × DNA/RNA shield (Zymo Research Cat# R1200-25). Supernatants were stored at −20°C until RNA isolation was carried out.

## RNA isolation and quantitative polymerase chain reaction (PCR) analysis
RNA was isolated from patient or healthy donor immune cells using the Quick RNA whole blood kit (Zymo Research Cat# R1201), samples were treated with DNase I before proceeding to cDNA synthesis. RNA concentration and purity (260/280) were measured using the Thermo Scientific NanoDrop 1000. cDNA was synthesized using the QuantiTect Reverse Transcription Kit (Qiagen Cat# 205,311), with the genomic DNA removal step that was performed according to the manufacture’s protocol. PCR was then carried out using a high-fidelity DNA polymerase with 18S primers, and primers for HERV-K102 [ ] were forward: 5'-AGA AAA GGG CCT CCA CGG AGA TG-3’ and reverse: 5'-ATC CTG GTG CTC TCC CTA GG-3’ (amplicon 199 bp). Primers for HERV-K108 were forward: 5’-GTA TGC TGC TTG CAG CCT TGA TGA T-3’and reverse: 5’-GTG ACA TCC CGC TTA CCA TG-3’ (amplicon 381 bp). This primer pair amplifies additional type 2 proviruses, including HERV-K104 and K109. LINE-1 forward 5’-ACC AAA AGT AGA TAA AAC CAC AAA GA-3’, reverse 5’-GAA CTG CGT TCC TTT GGA GG-3’ (amplicon 102 bp), IFI6 forward 5’-TGC TAC CTG CTG CTC TTC A-3’, reverse 5’-TCA GGG CCT TCC AGA ACC-3’ (amplicon 97 bp), IFNγ forward 5’-GAG TGT GGA GAC CAT CAA GGA AG-3’, reverse 5’-TGC TTT GCG TTG GAC ATT CAA GTC-3’, 18S forward 5’-CTC AAC ACG GGA AAC CTC AC-3’, reverse 5’-CGC TCC ACC AAC TAA GAA CG-3’. Amplification was performed on the QuantStudio™ 3 Real-Time PCR System, 96-well, 0.2 ml (Applied Biosytems catalog# A28137), using the PowerTrack™ SYBR Green Master Mix for qPCR (Applied Biosystems catalog #A46109). Gene expression was normalized to the reference gene 18S.

## RNA sequencing
RNA sequencing was performed on neutrophils as described before [ , ], but with an expanded number of patient samples: SLE (n = 35), RA (n = 15), and HC (n = 20). Sequencing depth was to > 50 million reads and the reads were 101 bp, paired-ended, and stranded. Our RNA seq data have been deposited at GEO as GSE261866.

## Bioinformatics of HERV-K proviruses
Read quality was analyzed with FASTQC (http://www.bioinformatics.babraham.ac.uk/projects/). Reads were then aligned to the human T2T reference genome with STAR v2.7.11b [ ] allowing multimapping (STAR parameters: outFilterMultimapNmax set to 100 and winAnchorMultimap set to 100) to capture reads that align to transposable elements and therefore potentially multiple locations, as well as uniquely mapping reads to understand which loci truly are transcribed. Quantification was with TElocal v1.1.1 l [ ], which was developed for quantifying reads at the locus level. TElocal uses transposable element annotations from RepeatMasker [ ]. Reads were normalized and differential testing was performed using DESeq2 v1.38.3 [ ] controlling for sex, age, comorbidities, and DMARD use. We relied on unique mapping to determine which proviruses are expressed and unique reads with the distributed percent of multimapping reads for quantitation. To better understand the protein-coding potential of each detected HERV-K locus, the genomic sequences of the full loci were downloaded and assembled (they are typically annotated by RepeatMasker as several separate pieces) and then translated into amino acid sequence using Expasy Translate (https://web.expasy.org/translate/) for BLAST searches, alignments, and mass spectrometry analysis. Each locus was also compared to the genomic review of the HML-2 subfamily of HERV-K by Subramanian and co-workers [ ] to verify type, locations of stop codons, and other relevant features.

## Antibodies and other reagents
The following primary antibodies were used: anti-Env mAbs Kenv1 and Kenv6, which recognize a linear epitope in the distal portion of the N-terminal (extracellular) region of Env and a conformational epitope in the N-terminus of Env, respectively (obtainable from Saphire lab). Secondary antibodies were: Goat anti-mouse IgG secondary Ab conjugated to AF647 (#A-21235) or AF488 (#A-11001) (Invitrogen). Non-specific binding of antibodies to Fc FcγRII was prevented by including anti-human CD32 (#MAB1300SP, Fisher Scientific, USA) and normal goat serum (ThermoFisher, #50062Z). Paraformaldehyde was from ThermoFisher Scientific, Triton X-100 from Millipore-Sigma, Germany, and BlockAid solution from ThermoFisher Scientific. DNA staining was with 4',6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) or Hoechst 33,342, both from ThermoFisher.

## Immunofluorescence microscopy
Freshly isolated neutrophils were cultured for 30 min at 37 °C on 96-well glass bottom plates (Cellvis #P96-1-N) pre-coated with poly-L-lysine (Millipore-Sigma # P4707). Macrophages were generated by leaving monocytes on the plates for 3 days at 37°C. The adherent cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde solution for 20 min, followed by two washes with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). Cells were permeabilized with 0.1% Triton X-100 in PBS for 10 min followed by three washes with PBS. To prevent nonspecific binding, neutrophils were blocked using 10% normal mouse and/or goat serum in BlockAid solution with anti-human FcγRII antibody overnight at 4 °C. Neutrophils were then incubated with a 1:50 dilution of mouse anti-Env mAb Kenv6, followed by three washes with PBS. The cells were subsequently stained with goat anti-mouse IgG secondary Ab conjugated with Alexa Fluor 647 (red). Negative controls consisted of cells stained without primary antibody or with only secondary antibodies. DNA was stained with either DAPI or Hoechst33342. Images were captured at 40 X, 60 X, or 100 X magnification using a Keyence BZ-X800 Fluorescence microscope and analyzed using BZ-X800 Analyzer software.

## Immunoprecipitation and mass spectrometry
Neutrophils were lysed by mixing 107 cells in 500 µl of ice-cold lysis buffer (25 mM Tris/HCl, pH 7.4, 150 mM NaCl, 1% TritonX-100, and 0.05% SDS, and 1xHalt protease and phosphatase inhibitor cocktail (Thermo Fisher Scientific, #78,440) with vigorous vortexing and clarified by centrifugation for 15 min at 4°C. Lysates were preincubated on ice for 1 h with anti-mouse IgG-coupled beads (protein G agarose beads, Thermo Fisher Scientific, #20,398) with agitation, centrifugated for 5 min, and the supernatant transferred to a new tube. In parallel, anti-Env mAbs Kenv1 or Kenv6 were incubated with anti-mouse IgG-coupled beads, which then were washed once in lysis buffer, mixed with the lysates, and incubated on ice for 1 h. The beads were then washed 5 times in lysis buffer, once in lysis buffer with 500 mM NaCl, once with lysis buffer with no SDS, and once with distilled water. After another three times washes of 20mM ammonium bicarbonate, on-beads trypsin digestion was performed at 37 °C for 5 h followed by reduction and alkylation. A C18 column (Thermo Fisher Scientific, #89,873) was used to purify and concentrate peptides. LC–MS/MS was performed at the University of Washington’s Proteomics Resource on Orbitrap Exploris 480.

## 5’LTR luciferase reporter experiments
Nucleotides 1–1,110 of the 5’LTR of HERV-K102 (GenBank: JN675014.1) were cloned into the dual luciferase vector pCMV-rLuc (kindly provided by Julie Crudele, University of Washington) so that the ATG codon that starts the group antigen (Gag) translation in the HERV-K102 transcript was 17 codons before, and in-frame with, the firefly luciferase gene. The Δ300 and Δ600 versions of the 5’LTR luciferase constructs lacked the 300 or 600 first nucleotides, respectively. The macrophage-like cell line THP-1 was transfected with this 5’LTR-luc plasmid by Amaxa 4D-Nucleofector X system (Lonza) and 24 h later treated with or without 500U/ml of IFNα for 4 h. Cells were harvested in 1xPassive Lysis Buffer (Promega). Firefly and renilla luciferase were measured using dual-luciferase reporter assay system (Promega, #E1910) on Promega GloMax Discover microplate reader.

## Statistical analyses
The statistical significance of the data sets from patient samples was calculated using ANOVA and the paired t-test. For RNA sequencing data, p-values were adjusted for multiple comparisons. A p-value < 0.05 was considered statistically significance; values < 0.05 are denoted with *, < 0.01 with **, < 0.005 with ***, and < 0.001 with ****. GraphPad Prism 10.4.1 was used for all statistical analyses.
