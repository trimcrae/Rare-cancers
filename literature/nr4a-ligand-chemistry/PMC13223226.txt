## Introduction
The intestinal epithelium serves as a critical interface between the host and the external environment, playing essential roles in nutrient absorption, immune defense, and barrier integrity . This rapidly renewing tissue is maintained by intestinal stem cells (ISCs), which reside at the crypt base and continuously support epithelial turnover through self-renewal and multipotent differentiation . ISCs not only sustain daily epithelial turnover but also drive regenerative responses following injury, making them vital for intestinal health . The functional integrity of ISCs is therefore fundamental to intestinal homeostasis and repair following weaning-induced intestinal injury.
Weaning represents a critical developmental stage in early life, characterized by dramatic dietary, environmental, and psychological shifts . In piglets, this period often induces severe intestinal dysfunction, including villus atrophy, barrier disruption, and impaired nutrient absorption . Accumulating evidence indicates that ISC dysfunction lies at the core of these pathological changes, manifesting as reduced stem cell numbers, suppressed proliferation, and aberrant differentiation . Concurrently, weaning triggers significant alterations in gut microbiota composition, such as decline in beneficial taxa and expansion of potential pathogens . Recent advances have highlighted that the gut microbiota exerts profound influence on ISC behavior through various mechanisms, including metabolite secretion, immune modulation, and niche remodeling , . Understanding how microbial signals modulate ISC function offers promising insights into novel strategies for mitigating intestinal injury during the vulnerable weaning period.
This review aims to summarize research from the last 5–10 years on the roles of ISCs in intestinal health, the impact of weaning stress on ISC function, and the regulatory mechanisms exerted by the gut microbiota. On this basis, we discuss the potential mechanisms linking weaning-induced microbial shifts to ISC dysfunction and, importantly, analyze promising nutritional intervention strategies at different stages. By integrating evidence from model organisms and pigs, this review seeks to provide a mechanistic framework for developing novel interventions aimed at preserving intestinal homeostasis during this vulnerable transition.

## Functions of ISCs and regulating mechanisms
The maintenance of intestinal epithelial homeostasis relies on the rapid and continuous turnover of cellular lineages, a process fueled by a resident population of multipotent ISCs . These cells are critical for preserving the structural integrity and absorptive capacity of the gut, possessing the ability to self-renew while orchestrating the generation of differentiated cells. This dynamic process is governed by a sophisticated network of intrinsic molecular programs and extrinsic signals derived from the surrounding niche . Before exploring how external stressors, such as weaning, disrupt these systems, it is essential to elucidate the fundamental biological functions of ISCs and the regulatory mechanisms that sustain their physiological activity.

## Functions of ISCs in intestinal health
The upper half of Fig. illustrates the differentiation lineage of ISCs and their localization within the crypt, while providing an overview of the ISC niche. Residing predominantly at the base of the crypts, ISCs are pivotal for maintaining epithelial homeostasis, ensuring tissue integrity, and orchestrating regeneration following injury. Based on their proliferative status and functional characteristics, ISCs are broadly categorized into two distinct populations. The first comprises actively cycling Lgr5⁺ stem cells located at the crypt base. These cells exhibit robust self-renewal capacity and multipotency, serving as the primary workhorses for constitutive epithelial replacement , . The second population consists of relatively quiescent stem cells, historically identified by their position at the “+4” cell position and markers such as Bmi1 or Hopx. These cells function as reserve stem cells, remaining largely quiescent under homeostasis but capable of rapid activation upon injury. They can dedifferentiate into active Lgr5⁺ ISCs or directly give rise to progenitor cells, thereby providing a crucial backup mechanism for epithelial repair , . This hierarchical organization ensures both daily maintenance and resilience against damage. 
The differentiation fate of ISCs is precisely regulated to generate the diverse epithelial cells, and thereby determines the structural and functional complexity of the gut. ISCs give rise to two primary lineages: the absorptive and the secretory lineages. The absorptive lineage primarily produces enterocytes, responsible for nutrient absorption, and microfold cells, which sample luminal antigens. The secretory lineage generates several specialized cell types: goblet cells that secrete mucins to form the protective mucus layer; Paneth cells that release antimicrobial peptides essential for innate host defense; enteroendocrine cells (EECs) that secrete hormones regulating metabolism and gut function; and tuft cells involved in chemosensation and immune modulation , . The balance between these differentiation pathways is critical for gut health, directly governing barrier integrity, immune surveillance, and digestive efficiency. For instance, Wnt ligands and antimicrobial peptides secreted by Paneth cells are vital for maintaining the ISC niche, whereas a reduction in goblet cells leads to a thinned mucus layer, increasing susceptibility to microbial invasion and inflammation , .
The regenerative potential of ISCs is indispensable for recovering from intestinal damage caused by injuries such as weaning, ischemia, radiation, chemical injury, inflammation, or infection. Following injury, Lgr5⁺ ISCs intensify their proliferative activity to replenish the lost epithelial cells. Concurrently, the quiescent reserve stem cells are mobilized, often undergoing dedifferentiation to augment the pool of active ISCs and accelerate mucosal healing , . Conversely, dysfunction in ISCs, manifested as an imbalance between proliferation and differentiation, loss of stemness, or impaired regenerative responses, can severely compromise intestinal barrier function. Such dysfunction is increasingly recognized as a key contributor to the pathogenesis of chronic inflammatory conditions like inflammatory bowel disease, and may even contribute to cancer by allowing the accumulation of oncogenic mutations , . Therefore, ISCs are considered guardians of intestinal health, whose proper function is essential to both physiology and pathology.

## Core regulatory roles of cellular signaling pathways
The lower half of Fig. briefly depicts the major signaling pathways regulating ISC function, including the Wnt, bone morphogenetic protein (BMP), Notch, and epidermal growth factor (EGF) signaling pathways, as well as their interactions. The Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway stands as the principal regulator of ISC self-renewal and maintenance. The binding of Wnt ligands (e.g., Wnt3 from Paneth cells) to Frizzled receptors stabilizes cytoplasmic β-catenin, allowing its nuclear translocation. Inside the nucleus, β-catenin forms complexes with TCF/LEF transcription factors to activate target genes such as Lgr5 and Myc, thereby driving ISC proliferation , . Recent studies further elucidate upstream regulatory mechanisms; for example, the nuclear receptor Nur77 promotes Paneth cell differentiation and niche stability by inhibiting Dact1, an antagonist of Wnt signaling, thereby potentiating the Wnt pathway . Furthermore, microbial metabolites, including propionate and lactate, can activate Wnt signaling via GPR43 and GPR81 receptors, respectively. This activation has been shown to reverse the downregulation of ISC markers like Olfm4 and Lgr5 in colitis models, highlighting a direct link between gut microbiota and ISC fate , .
The Notch pathway plays a decisive role in directing ISC lineage commitment. Engagement of Notch receptors with ligands on adjacent differentiated cells triggers proteolytic cleavage, releasing the Notch intracellular domain, which translocates to the nucleus and activates target genes like Hes1. Hes1, in turn, represses the transcription of pro-secretory genes, thereby inhibiting differentiation into secretory lineages and preserving the stem/progenitor cell pool , . This pathway can be modulated by immune signals; for instance, IL-13 has been demonstrated to promote ISC self-renewal via Notch activation, and upregulation of the Notch ligand Jagged1 enhances the viability and growth of intestinal organoids .
In contrast to the proliferative drives of Wnt and Notch pathways, the BMP pathway acts as a key negative regulator, curbing excessive ISC expansion to maintain tissue homeostasis. BMP signaling activity exhibits a gradient along the crypt-villus axis, with high levels in the villus compartment. BMP ligands bind to BMP receptors, leading to the phosphorylation and activation of Smads (Smad1, 5, and 8) and form a complex with Smad4. The complex then translocates into the nucleus and regulates target gene expression . BMPs inhibit Wnt target genes via Smad-dependent transcription, while their antagonists, such as Noggin and Gremlins, are locally expressed at the crypt base to spatially restrict BMP activity, thus preserving the ISC niche , . For example, Gremlin2 secreted by smooth muscle cells effectively neutralizes BMP signaling, creating a permissive microenvironment for ISC self-renewal .
The EGF pathway is another critical mitogenic signal for ISCs. EGF signaling does not directly determine cell fate but rather provides a pro-survival signal that supports the expansion of all proliferative cells, including stem and progenitor cells. EGF binding to its receptor (EGFR) forms receptor dimers that initiate intracellular signaling cascades, including Ras/Raf/MEK/ERK, JAK/STAT, and PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathways , , thereby promoting cell cycle progression. Intriguingly, a recent study reported that L-glutamate directly binds to and activates EGFR, thereby initiating the EGFR-MEK-ERK-mTFB2 signaling axis, which ultimately promotes mitochondrial biogenesis to provide energy support for ISC proliferation .
The Wnt, BMP, Notch, and EGF signaling pathways form a sophisticated network that coordinately regulates ISC fate. Among them, the Wnt pathway plays a pivot role in maintaining ISC self-renewal and proliferation by activating the β-catenin/TCF cascade. The Notch pathway functions synergistically with Wnt, promoting cells to adopt an absorptive cell fate or remain proliferative by suppressing secretory lineage differentiation genes through Hes1. In contrast, the BMP pathway inhibits proliferation and promotes differentiation. It engages in an antagonistic interaction with Wnt signaling, thereby strictly delineating the proliferative zone at the crypt base from the differentiation zone in the villus. The EGF pathway provides universal support for cell proliferation via pathways like MEK/ERK and augments the output of other pathways, such as Wnt. The dynamic equilibrium among these pathways ensures ISC homeostasis.

## Indirect regulation by immune cells and cytokines
The immune system exerts profound and indirect effects on ISC dynamics through cytokine secretion. IL-22, produced by immune cells such as γδ T cells and group 3 innate lymphoid cells (ILC3s), is a potent stimulator of ISC proliferation and regeneration. Inulin supplementation promotes IL-22 production by γδ T cells in the colonic lamina propria. IL-22 acts on ISCs to enhance their proliferative activity (without increasing their number). In γδ T cell- or IL-22-deficient mice, the inulin-induced epithelial remodeling is completely abrogated . Similarly, astragalus polysaccharides induce IL-22 secretion by enriching beneficial Lactobacillus species, thereby reversing age-related or stress-induced ISC regeneration deficits . A previous study indicates that IL-22 may promote the regeneration of ISCs by inducing STAT3 phosphorylation in intestinal epithelial cells, thereby activating downstream proliferation signaling . The phosphorylated STAT3 increases the number of Paneth cells and enhances their secretion of critical stem cell maintenance factors such as EGF, Wnt3, and Notch ligands, thereby indirectly providing essential growth signals for stem cell proliferation . Another study showed that IL-13 released by type 2 innate lymphoid cells (ILC2s) can promote ISC proliferation and self-renewal by activating the Notch and Wnt signaling pathways .
Macrophages are critical mediators of the gut microbiota-host interaction , including in regulating ISC proliferation and differentiation. Studies have shown that prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) produced by PGE2+ macrophages could promote ISC self-renewal by activating the Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway via EP1/EP4 receptors . Meanwhile, CD206+ macrophages secrete Wnt6/Wnt9a to activate the Wnt/β-catenin pathway in mesenchymal niche cells, thereby stimulating their proliferation. Supported by Wnt/β-catenin signaling and supporting factors such as CXCL12 and IL-7 secreted by MNCs, Lgr5+ stem cells differentiate into Paneth cells, thereby maintaining intestinal barrier function .

## Support of stromal cells and niche microenvironment
Non-epithelial cellular components of the niche provide essential structural and signaling support for the ISCs. Paneth cells, located among ISCs in the small intestine, are a key source of niche signals, secreting Wnt3, EGF, and antimicrobial peptides. Genetic ablation of Nur77 leads to Paneth cell dysfunction, resulting in reduced secretion of critical factors like Wnt3 and DLL4, and a consequent decline in Lgr5+ ISC numbers . Microbial metabolites can also target Paneth cells; lactate, for instance, can stimulate Paneth cells to secrete Wnt3, thereby promoting ISC proliferation .
The ISC niche is a complex, multi-layered mesenchymal structure comprising diverse populations of mesenchymal and smooth muscle cells. PDGFRα+ intestinal stromal lineage originating from postnatal LTβR+ perivascular stromal progenitors can produce pro-differentiation factors, including BMPs, retinoic acid, and laminins . The ablation of PDGFRα in the LTβR stromal lineage induced a significant increase in Olfm4+ cells, Lgr5+ ISCs, and EdU+ proliferating epithelial cells. Intestinal subepithelial myofibroblasts accumulate at the crypt–villus junction and secrete factors such as BMP5, establishing a BMP signaling source that promotes epithelial cell differentiation . Smooth muscle cells—including those of the muscularis mucosae (MM) and the superficial muscularis propria—secrete BMP antagonists (e.g., Grem2, Nog) and R-spondin (RSPO) family factors . Specifically, the MM expresses Grem2 and RSPO3, while the superficial musculature propria secretes Nog and Chrdl1. Together, they maintain a low-BMP environment at the crypt base to support ISC stemness. Trophocytes, as central supportive cells for ISCs, highly express potent RSPO2 and Grem1 . Through the dynamic balance of the BMP signaling gradient and the Wnt signaling network, these cells achieve precise regulation of ISC proliferation and differentiation during postnatal maturation.

## Regulation of ISCs by gut microbiota
While the genetic programs regulate the fundamental renewal of the intestinal epithelium, the gut microbiota serves as a pivotal external regulator . The resident microbial community constantly communicates with the host epithelium, modulating ISC proliferation and differentiation to maintain mucosal homeostasis and facilitate tissue regeneration after injury . Current research indicates that this complex interaction is orchestrated through specific molecular mechanisms, including the modulation of host immune responses and the secretion of bioactive compounds . Of these mechanisms, the production of functional small molecules represents the most direct link between gut microbial activity and stem cell fate. Figure illustrates the specific mechanisms by which gut microbiota regulates the proliferation and differentiation of ISCs through the production of short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), tryptophan metabolites, bile acids, and other metabolites. 

## Regulation of ISCs by short-chain fatty acids
SCFAs—mainly acetate, propionate, and butyrate—are produced by gut microbes during the fermentation of dietary fiber, mucus, or other exogenous substrates. Beyond serving as energy sources for intestinal epithelial cells, SCFAs help maintain intestinal homeostasis and guide ISC fate via multiple mechanisms. Interestingly, different SCFAs exhibit distinct and sometimes concentration-dependent effects on ISC behavior, which may have implications for intestinal repair under stress conditions such as weaning.
Acetate, derived from bacteria such as Akkermansia, enhances ISC proliferation. Administration of Akkermansia muciniphila to ETEC-infected mice significantly alleviated ETEC-induced body weight loss, intestinal villus shortening, and crypt deepening, while increasing VH and density . Furthermore, it upregulates the expression of ISC markers Lgr5, Wnt3, active β-catenin, and its downstream target genes (c-Myc, CyclinD1). Further investigation revealed that acetate is produced by Akkermansia. muciniphila can activate G protein-coupled receptors Gpr41/43 on Paneth cells, stimulating the production of Wnt ligands . This subsequently activates the Wnt signaling pathway, leading to an increase in Lgr5+ ISCs within the crypts of the small intestine and colon. Acetate treatment in organoids enhances the expression of Lgr5 and Olfm4, promotes ISC proliferation, and results in enlarged organoids , . Furthermore, erythritol-fed mice exhibited a 2.25-fold increase in fecal acetate levels, upregulation of Lgr5 expression, and an elevated number of proliferating stem cells . Additionally, the differentiation rate of Lgr5+ ISCs into villus cells accelerated, indicating improved differentiation efficiency.
Propionate exhibits a synergistic effect with acetate in promoting ISC proliferation through Gpr41/Gpr43. A study showed that fucose treatment increased the abundance of Akkermansia muciniphila in the gut and promoted its propionate metabolism. Propionate activates the Wnt signaling pathway in a GPR41/GPR43-dependent manner, thereby stimulating the proliferation of ISCs and supporting intestinal epithelial development . Furthermore, Clostridia clusters IV/XIVa also produce propionate, which induces the expression of Reg3B in Paneth cells via GPR43 . Reg3B promotes the proliferation of ISCs and accelerates injury repair, though its specific mechanism remains unclear. It is hypothesized that Reg3B may maintain the self-renewal capacity of stem cells by stabilizing the stem cell niche or activating relevant signaling pathways, such as Wnt/β-catenin and MAPK.
Butyrate demonstrates a context-dependent influence on ISCs. At low levels, it enhances stemness via Lgr5 upregulation through the SPIB–H3K4me3 axis and promotes enterocyte differentiation via HDAC inhibition and Notch activation , . However, elevated butyrate concentrations—such as those resulting from carbon nanomaterial fermentation—suppress Wnt signaling and deplete Lgr5+ ISCs, impairing epithelial renewal . Butyrate also fine-tunes immune–epithelial crosstalk; by inhibiting HDAC3, it derepresses Spry2, thereby attenuating EGFR/PI3K signaling and limiting tuft cell differentiation .
Isobutyrate, a branched-chain SCFA, contributes to ISC-mediated repair in conditions such as radiation injury. The herbal formulation Liangxue Guyuan Yishen Decoction or α-linolenic acid treatment both significantly increase intestinal villus length and the expression of proliferation markers , . These effects are mediated by an increased abundance of isobutyrate-producing bacteria and elevated isobutyrate production in the gut. Isobutyrate activates the Wnt pathway by phosphorylating and inhibiting GSK-3β—a key negative regulator of Wnt signaling that phosphorylates and degrades β-catenin, reducing its nuclear translocation. This leads to the upregulation of proliferation-related genes such as Cyclin D1 and c-Myc, directly promoting the proliferation of ISCs , .
Valerate operates through a neuro–immune–epithelial circuit. Megasphaera massiliensis-derived valerate enhances serotonin synthesis in enteric neurons by interfering with NuRD complex binding to the Tph2 promoter. Serotonin, in turn, stimulates macrophages to produce PGE2, which activates Wnt/β-catenin signaling in ISCs via EP1/EP4 receptors, supporting self-renewal and tissue regeneration .
Taken together, SCFAs orchestrate ISC fate and epithelial homeostasis through a multifaceted network of mechanisms. Acetate and propionate synergistically enhance Wnt/β-catenin signaling via GPR41/43 on Paneth cells, promoting ISC proliferation and differentiation. Butyrate exerts concentration-dependent effects, supporting stemness at low levels but inhibiting renewal at high concentrations, while fine-tuning differentiation via epigenetic and signaling modulation. Isobutyrate and valerate further expand this regulatory spectrum by activating Wnt signaling through GSK-3β inhibition and engaging a neuro-immune-epithelial circuit, respectively. Collectively, these SCFAs integrate microbial metabolism with host signaling pathways to dynamically regulate ISC behavior and support intestinal repair in a context-dependent manner.

## Regulation of ISCs by tryptophan metabolites
Tryptophan serves as a key precursor for gut microbiota metabolism, and its metabolites—such as indole-3-lactic acid (ILA), indole-3-carboxaldehyde (I3A), indole-3-acetaldehyde (IAld), and indole-3-acetic acid (IAA)—play vital roles in ISC regulation. These compounds activate the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) and modulate signaling pathways such as Notch and Wnt/β-catenin, thereby influencing ISC proliferation and differentiation, maintaining gut homeostasis, and orchestrating responses to stress and disease.
Among these metabolites, ILA is produced by certain Lactobacillus species, including Ligilactobacillus animalis, Lactobacillus mucosae, and Ligilactobacillus salivarius . In pig models, early time-restricted feeding enriches these bacteria and increases colonic ILA levels. ILA promotes the differentiation of ISCs into EECs by downregulating the Notch signaling pathway, while upregulating the expression of EEC differentiation transcription factors Math1 and Ngn3 . Consistent with this, in vitro experiments using intestinal organoids show that ILA increases organoid surface area without altering budding efficiency, underscoring its primary role in directing differentiation rather than enhancing proliferation .
The tryptophan metabolite I3A from Bifidobacterium acts as an AhR ligand, upregulating downstream target genes (e.g., CYP1A1) . This subsequently induces the expression of the anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10 and activates the Wnt3/β-catenin pathway, thereby promoting ISC proliferation and epithelial repair. Neutralization of IL-10 by anti-IL-10 antibody significantly reduces the activity of Wnt3 and β-catenin, demonstrating that IL-10 acts as an upstream activator of the Wnt3/β-catenin pathway. Similarly, IAld produced by Lactobacillus amylovorus and Lactobacillus reuteri enhances intestinal organoid budding efficiency and surface area by activating the AhR–Wnt/β-catenin signaling axis and upregulates both mRNA and protein expression of ISC markers (Lgr5, Olfm4) in piglets .
The effects of IAA are species- and context-dependent. Under psychological stress, Lactobacillus murinus-derived IAA inhibits the conversion of glutamine metabolism to the TCA cycle. This reduces mitochondrial OXPHOS and ATP production, specifically blocking ISC differentiation into secretory lineages (goblet cells, EECs). Consequently, it leads to crypt shortening and barrier dysfunction , . On the other hand, IAA produced by crypt-specific Acinetobacter radioresistens activates AhR, upregulating the Wnt receptor E3 ligase RNF43. This suppresses Wnt/β-catenin signaling and downstream EphB2 expression, thereby restraining excessive ISC proliferation .
The herbal formula Qingchang Wenzhong Decoction (QCWZD) reshapes the gut microbiota, increasing Lactobacillus and decreasing Bacteroides. This shift promotes the generation of AhR ligands like 5-methoxyindoleacetic acid and IAld from tryptophan. These metabolites activate AhR, upregulate Wnt pathway genes (Wnt3, β-catenin, Axin2), and expand the Lgr5+ ISC pool . They promote differentiation into goblet cells and enhance tight junction protein (ZO-1, Occludin) expression, accelerating mucosal healing in DSS-induced colitis. Fecal microbiota transplantation from QCWZD-treated mice reproduces these effects on ISCs and the Wnt pathway. Furthermore, 5-methoxyindoleacetic acid directly stimulates intestinal organoid proliferation in an AhR-dependent manner .
Additionally, serotonin is synthesized from tryptophan by enterochromaffin cells via tryptophan hydroxylase 1 (TPH1). Its intestinal levels are modulated by the microbiota. Aloe gel polysaccharides increase Bifidobacterium abundance and upregulate TPH1, boosting serotonin production . Serotonin then binds to the Htr4 receptor on ISCs, stimulating Kit+ goblet cells to secrete Wnt3. This activates the Wnt/β-catenin pathway, increasing ISC proliferation and upregulating barrier repair proteins. In organoids, serotonin counteracts TNF-α-induced injury, improving budding and reducing FITC-dextran permeability .

## Regulation of ISCs by bile acids
The gut microbiota plays a crucial role in modulating ISC fate through the production of various bile acid metabolites, which influence key signaling pathways such as FXR and Wnt/β-catenin. Understanding these microbiota–bile acid–ISC axes offers promising therapeutic avenues for ameliorating gut injury, such as that induced by weaning stress.
7-Oxo-deoxycholic acid (7-oxo-DCA), a secondary bile acid generated by gut bacteria such as Ruminococcus_UBA946 via 7α/β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase (7α/β-HSDH)-mediated oxidation of cholic acid, acts as an FXR inhibitor . By relieving FXR-mediated suppression of the Wnt pathway, it activates Wnt/β-catenin signaling, upregulates ISC stemness markers (e.g., Lgr5, Olfm4) and proliferation-related genes (e.g., CCND1, CTNNB1), and suppresses differentiation markers (e.g., Mucin 2, Krt20), thereby promoting ISC proliferation and maintaining stemness . In contrast, isodeoxycholic acid (isoDCA), produced by microbes such as Lachnospiraceae through epimerization of primary bile acids, also involving 7α/β-HSDH, functions as a potent FXR agonist with high binding affinity (binding energy: −11.3 kcal/mol) . Its activation of FXR target genes (e.g., SHP, FGF19) suppresses Wnt signaling via the TLE3–β-catenin axis. This decreases ISC proliferation and promotes differentiation into mature epithelial cells, as indicated by elevated expression of markers such as Mucin 2 and Krt20 .
Non-classical amino acid-conjugated bile acids (AA-CAs), synthesized by bacteria including Ileibacterium valens and Ruminococcus gnavus under high-fat diet conditions, exhibit divergent effects on ISCs . For example, Phe-CA and Tyr-CA activate FXR, downregulate stemness markers, and suppress ISC proliferation. Conversely, Ser-CA activates Wnt/β-catenin signaling, enhances stemness, and promotes proliferation . Another relevant metabolite, 7-keto-lithocholic acid (7-keto-LCA), is produced by Parabacteroides goldsteinii from chenodeoxycholic acid via the 7α-HSDH encoded by hdhA . As an endogenous FXR antagonist, it competitively inhibits CDCA-induced FXR activation, thereby relieving repression of Wnt signaling, promoting β-catenin nuclear translocation, and enhancing ISC self-renewal and injury repair, as seen in increased crypt proliferation and reduced apoptosis . Furthermore, gut microbes with high bile salt hydrolase activity (e.g., Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium) deconjugate bile acids into free forms such as lithocholic acid and deoxycholic acid . These free bile acids activate intestinal FXR signaling, leading to SHP upregulation, inhibition of the Wnt/β-catenin pathway, downregulation of ISC proliferation and stemness genes, and ultimately to ISC dysfunction with impaired epithelial renewal .

## Regulation of ISCs by other metabolites
Beyond SCFAs, tryptophan metabolites, and bile acids, the gut microbiota also modulates ISC function by producing metabolites such as fructose, N-carbamylglutamate (NCG), and lactate.
Fructose is a key metabolite generated by gut bacteria from soluble fiber. Studies show that raffinose, a soluble fiber in a standard chow diet, can be metabolized by Lactobacillus reuteri DSM 20016 into fructose . ISCs can directly take up fructose, which then activates the glycolytic pathway by upregulating key enzymes Pfk and Ldh2 . This enhances the production of glucose-6-phosphate, fructose-6-phosphate, and lactate, providing energy for ISC proliferation. Consequently, fructose increases the density of ISCs (Olfm4⁺ and Lgr5⁺ cells) and boosts organoid budding capacity under stress conditions .
N-carbamylglutamate (NCG) is a core metabolite secreted by Limosilactobacillus reuteri DS0384. It regulates ISC function through a dual mechanism. First, NCG acts as an N-acetylglutamate analog to activate carbamoyl phosphate synthetase-1, a key enzyme in the urea cycle . This promotes the conversion of glutamine to arginine, supplying nitrogen and biosynthetic precursors for ISC proliferation. Concurrently, NCG elevates intracellular spermidine levels, activating the mTOR signaling pathway and accelerating cell cycle progression. Second, NCG suppresses the expression of inflammatory factors (e.g., TNFα), reducing oxidative stress damage to the ISC niche and maintaining stem cell pool homeostasis. In vivo and in vitro studies confirm that NCG significantly increases ISC markers (Lgr5, Sox9), organoid size, and CD in infant mice .
Follow-up studies revealed that live Lactobacillus amylovorus significantly promotes intestinal organoid growth (increased area and budding), upregulates PCNA, Lgr5, and Wnt pathway gene expression, and enhances differentiation of ISCs into Paneth cells and tuft cells . These effects were not observed with heat-killed bacteria, whereas 5 mM lactate treatment mimicked the effects of Lactobacillus amylovorus. GPR81 knockout or its antagonist blocked lactate- or Lactobacillus amylovorus-induced organoid proliferation and Wnt pathway activation, while a GPR81 agonist replicated lactate’s effect in promoting ISC proliferation . Compared to Yorkshire pigs, Meishan pigs exhibit greater jejunal CD, significant activation of the Wnt pathway, and higher expression of ISC markers Lgr5 and Olfm4. They also show a higher abundance of Lactobacillus, increased expression of lactate production-related enzyme genes, and elevated lactate concentration in the jejunum . These findings indicate that lactate likely activates the Wnt pathway via GPR81 to stimulate ISC proliferation and may bias ISC differentiation toward secretory lineages, though the underlying mechanisms for the latter remain to be elucidated.

## Indirect regulation of the microbiota-immune-ISC axis
As shown in the left part of Fig. , the gut microbiota indirectly regulates the proliferation, differentiation, and functional homeostasis of ISCs by modulating the activation and cytokine production of various immune cells, forming a sophisticated microbiota–immune–ISC signaling network. 
For instance, the soluble dietary fiber inulin remodels the gut microbiota, enriching bacterial species such as Bacteroides uniformis and Akkermansia muciniphila . Metabolites from these bacteria activate colonic intraepithelial γδ T cells to secrete IL-22. Similarly, indole derivatives in extracellular vesicles from Lactobacillus reuteri GG act as AhR ligands, activating ILC3s to produce IL-22 . Moreover, Lactobacillus reuteri D8 metabolite I3A can also stimulate lamina propria lymphocytes to secrete IL-22 through AhR activation . The binding of IL-22 to its receptor IL-22R1 directly enhances the proliferative activity and migration of Lgr5⁺ ISCs via the STAT3 signaling pathway, ultimately promoting colonic epithelial remodeling and crypt deepening , . IL-22 not only promotes ISC proliferation and differentiation via STAT3 but also synergizes with Paneth cells to secrete antimicrobial peptides like Reg3b, thereby reducing inflammation-induced damage to stem cells .
In the ILC2s mediated-pathway, the microbial metabolite pravastatin promotes intestinal epithelial cells to release IL-33. IL-33 then activates ILC2s via its receptor ST2, inducing IL-13 secretion. IL-13 further activates the Notch pathway (Jagged1/Dll1-Notch1-Hes1 axis) and the Wnt pathway (Wnt3-Lrp5/6-β-catenin-c-Myc axis), cooperatively enhancing the self-renewal capacity of ISCs and mitigating intestinal damage .
Macrophages play a dual role in microbiota-mediated ISC regulation. Early colonizing bacteria can induce the polarization of CD206⁺ resident macrophages, prompting Wnt6 secretion. This sustains the proliferation of mesenchymal niche cells, which in turn support ISC differentiation into Paneth cells via Wnt signaling, thereby suppressing necrotizing enterocolitis . On the other hand, Akkermansia muciniphila may degrade mucin, compromise the barrier, and promote pathogenic bacterial translocation. This can induce M1-type macrophages to release pro-inflammatory factors like IL-6 and TNF, inhibiting ISC self-renewal and differentiation into goblet cells, forming a detrimental cycle of mucin depletion-inflammation amplification-stem cell inhibition .
Through metabolites, structural components, and ecological shifts, microbes modulate key signaling pathways—such as STAT3, Notch, Wnt, and AhR—to influence ISC proliferation, differentiation, and damage response. This crosstalk not only maintains epithelial homeostasis and supports repair but can also drive pathology when disrupted, underscoring the microbiota–immune–ISC axis as a critical determinant of intestinal health and disease.

## Impact of early-life microbial colonization on ISCs
As shown in the middle part of Fig. , early gut microbiota colonization plays an important role in intestinal development by directly or indirectly regulating ISC function, thereby influencing epithelial homeostasis and injury repair capacity.
Beaumont et al. demonstrated that oral colistin selectively depletes transiently dominant Gram-negative bacteria (such as Proteobacteria and Fusobacteria) in the gut of neonatal piglets, leading to altered metabolic functions (reduced LPS synthesis and elevated succinate). This resulted in significantly decreased expression of innate immune genes in the colonic epithelium, including TLR4 (LPS receptor), LYZ (lysozyme), and LBP (LPS-binding protein), along with downregulation of the stem cell marker Olfm4 and the proliferation marker PCNA, and upregulation of the differentiation marker AQP8 . Colonic crypt-derived organoids from colistin-treated piglets still exhibited significantly lower TLR4 and LYZ expression after multiple passages, indicating a long-term imprint of early microbiota disruption on stem cells. In vitro experiments showed that succinate, LPS, and colistin itself did not directly affect epithelial cells, supporting the notion that early structural changes in the microbiota are the core driving factor . However, this study did not clarify the mechanism by which early microbial colonization and structural changes influence ISCs.
Abo et al. addressed this issue more comprehensively using specific pathogen-free (SPF) and germ-free (GF) mice . They found that Erdr1 is expressed in Lgr5⁺ ISCs in the crypts, and can activate Wnt signaling to promote ISC proliferation and intestinal injury repair. The histone H3 acetylation level in the Erdr1 promoter region and Erdr1 mRNA expression were significantly higher in SPF mice than in GF mice . Importantly, when GF mice were co-housed with SPF mice at weaning to introduce microbial colonization for 3 weeks—simulating post-weaning microbiota restoration—the delayed colonization failed to restore Erdr1 expression . This suggests that the early microbiota regulates Erdr1 expression through epigenetic modifications, thereby promoting Lgr5⁺ ISC proliferation and regeneration, maintaining intestinal homeostasis, and accelerating injury repair. This mechanism operates within an early critical window, and once missed, cannot be compensated for by later microbial colonization. However, the receptor for Erdr1 remains unidentified, and the specific microbial metabolites responsible for early induction of Erdr1 require further investigation.
Additionally, during the first few weeks after birth, LTβR⁺ stromal progenitors give rise to the PDGFRα+ lineage, which highly expresses factors promoting epithelial differentiation (Bmp4/5, etc.) and Wnt antagonists (Frzb) . This lineage restricts excessive ISC proliferation and promotes epithelial differentiation (e.g., goblet cells and enterocytes). It also supports immune barrier maturation, serving as a key regulator in the postnatal transition of the intestine from an immature proliferative state to a functional barrier . Antibiotic treatment significantly reduces the number of PDGFRα+ stromal cells, indicating that microbes promote the development of this lineage . This further underscores the importance of early microbes in the functional maturation of ISCs. However, the specific microbes or metabolites involved and their mechanisms of action remain unclear and require further investigation.
Future studies could focus on elucidating the molecular dialog between early colonizing microbes and host signaling networks to uncover potential targets for supporting intestinal health through microbiota-directed interventions.

## Influence of maternal microbiota on offspring ISCs
As shown in the right part of Fig. , maternal gut microbiota can vertically regulate offspring ISC function. The research on the influence of maternal microbiota on offspring ISCs remains limited, with only a few studies providing direct mechanistic insights. A key recent investigation demonstrated that maternal supplementation with Akkermansia muciniphila during pregnancy profoundly affects offspring ISC function . This intervention enhanced ISC proliferation and altered differentiation patterns, mediated via cytoplasm-to-nucleus signaling pathways, including activation of the mTOR pathway by microbiota-derived metabolites . These changes persisted into adulthood, influencing intestinal physiology and long-term health outcomes.
Complementary studies highlight additional mechanisms. For instance, maternal high-fat diet exposure disrupts offspring ISC programming, impairing differentiation capacity and epithelial cell fate determination, potentially through epigenetic modifications and microbiota dysbiosis . Prenatal supplementation with microbial metabolites, such as indole-3-propionic acid (IPA), activates Wnt/β-catenin signaling to promote ISC proliferation and intestinal epithelial differentiation in offspring . Furthermore, maternal immune activation or infection can induce lasting epigenetic alterations in fetal ISCs via cytokines like IL-6, enhancing resistance to certain gut infections while increasing susceptibility to mucosal inflammation .
Overall, these findings underscore the maternal microbiota as a critical modulator of offspring ISC homeostasis, primarily through metabolite-mediated signaling and epigenetic programming during prenatal development. However, the field is still emergent, and more comprehensive studies are needed to elucidate the full spectrum of mechanisms and their implications for preventive interventions.

## Weaning-induced dysbiosis, ISC dysfunction, and intestinal Injury
Weaning is a critical developmental and stress event in swine production, characterized by sudden dietary shifts, maternal separation, and environmental change . These stressors typically lead to acute intestinal morphological disruption, manifested as villus atrophy and crypt hyperplasia, which compromise nutrient absorption and barrier integrity . Accumulating evidence indicates that the regenerative failure of the intestinal epithelium during this critical window is intrinsically linked to the dysfunction of ISCs, identifying them as the primary cellular target of weaning stress . Concurrently, the cessation of lactation and the introduction of solid feed trigger profound dysbiosis in the gut microbiota . Elucidating the triangular relationship among weaning signals, microbial dynamics, and ISC biology is essential for understanding the pathogenesis of post-weaning intestinal injury. For clarity, this review categorizes the post-weaning period of piglets into the early stage (0–7 days), the mid stage (7–14 days), and the late stage (>14 days). In the subsequent discussion, we summarize and compare the experimental results from the cited literature based on these categories.

## Dynamic changes in microbial diversity
Weaning significantly affects gut microbial alpha-diversity, typically marked by an initial decline followed by recovery. The extent and timing of these changes vary with time post-weaning, gut region, and host health status. An early transient reduction in diversity may occur, as illustrated by a marked drop in species richness on day 3 post-weaning . Most studies report a substantial increase in bacterial alpha-diversity as piglets adapt, leading to greater community complexity and stability at the late stage than the early stage post-weaning – . For instance, alpha-diversity was significantly higher on day 27 than on day 7 post-weaning , and the Shannon index had stabilized by day 14 post-weaning . In terms of beta-diversity, significant separation was consistently observed between pre- and post-weaning microbiota. The main factors driving these differences were age, intestinal segment, and sampling location (mucosa vs. lumen) , , . Following weaning, microbial dispersion decreases with increasing age , and the community composition becomes more similar among individuals , suggesting that microbial colonization progressively stabilizes into a more predictable pattern.

## Core successional patterns in community composition
At the phylum level, Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes remained the dominant phyla, but their relative abundances shifted dynamically after weaning. Firmicutes abundance generally increased or remained dominant after weaning, particularly in luminal samples (70–90%), including genera such as Lactobacillus, Clostridium, and Roseburia , , , . In contrast, Bacteroidetes often showed an initial decrease followed by a later increase. The early decline may reflect delayed adaptation to solid feed, while later recovery coincided with increased demand for fiber digestion, supported by genera like Prevotella and Bacteroides , , , . Some studies reported a persistent reduction in Bacteroidetes, possibly due to differences in sampling timing or diet composition , . Proteobacteria, which include potential pathogens such as Campylobacter and Helicobacter, were significantly enriched immediately after weaning and were directly linked to post-weaning diarrhea , , , . However, their abundance declined sharply to low levels at the late-stage after weaning . Fusobacteria consistently decreased or disappeared post-weaning; for instance, Fusobacterium dropped from 4.97% to almost absent, likely due to the loss of milk-dependent metabolic niches , , , .
At the family and genus levels, changes were more pronounced and reflected a functional transition in the microbiota. Taxa involved in fiber degradation and metabolic functions became enriched after weaning. Families such as Prevotellaceae, Ruminococcaceae, Lachnospiraceae, and Erysipelotrichaceae increased significantly. Corresponding genera—including Prevotella, Roseburia, Ruminococcus 1, and Blautia—became dominant in polysaccharide degradation , , , , , . For example, Prevotella and Clostridium sensu stricto were consistently enriched post-weaning and became core genera for plant polysaccharide degradation, positively correlated with fiber digestion capacity and SCFA production , – , , though different OTUs within the same genus may occupy distinct metabolic functions . In contrast, milk-adapted taxa declined. Families that were dominant before weaning, such as Enterobacteriaceae, Bacteroidaceae, and Fusobacteriaceae, decreased significantly, along with genera like Bacteroides, Fusobacterium, and Megasphaera , , , . Enterobacteriaceae, dominant pre-weaning, decreased overall post-weaning period, but potentially pathogenic members like Escherichia-Shigella could still colonize the small intestine , . Lactobacillus was highly dominant (>80%) in the distal jejunum and colonic lumen in the early-stage after weaning , but its abundance later decreased or stabilized. A marked reduction of Lactobacillus was associated with higher diarrhea risk , , . Fusobacterium showed a strong positive correlation with Lactobacillus pre-weaning but nearly disappeared post-weaning; its potential pro-inflammatory role requires further validation , , .
The community of SCFA-producing bacteria was also remodeled. After weaning, SCFA producers such as Roseburia, Ruminococcus, and Faecalibacterium prausnitzii increased, correlating with elevated propionate and butyrate levels , , , . Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, an anti-inflammatory genus, increased with weaning age, and delayed weaning further raised its abundance, correlating with reduced diarrhea incidence and more stable body weight . In contrast, succinate/acetate producers like Alloprevotella and Oscillospira showed transient decreases in the early-stage after weaning , . Concurrently, potential pathogens Campylobacter was significantly enriched in the early stage after weaning, especially in the colonic mucosa, and served as a key marker of dysbiosis in diarrheic piglets , , . Akkermansia. muciniphila is not only a well-known mucin-utilizing microorganism , but also a producer of acetate and propionate , . The abundance of Akkermansia. muciniphila peaked at 14-day-old but decreased abruptly in the early-stage after weaning .
Generally, the core microbiota stabilized post-weaning, resembling that of healthy adult pigs. In the early stage after weaning, the core genera included Lactobacillus, Prevotella, and Campylobacter (12 genera total). By the late stage, the core genera evolved to include Prevotella, Lactobacillus, and Clostridium (11 genera total) . A 16-genus phylogenetic core accounted for 42.93% of total sequences, including key functional genera like Prevotella and Roseburia . Therefore, weaning triggers a profound ecological succession within the gut microbiota, characterized by a functional shift from milk-oriented digestion to plant polysaccharide fermentation. This transition is marked by an increase in Firmicutes and a dynamic response in Bacteroidetes, alongside the enrichment of fiber-degrading genera and SCFA producers. The early-stage after weaning is also a window of vulnerability, featuring transient blooms of Proteobacteria and potential pathogens linked to diarrhea, before the microbial community stabilizes into a more fermentative and functionally resilient state.

## Spatial heterogeneity
The composition of the gut microbiota differed across anatomical sites and compartments. In the foregut, Prevotella was the dominant genus in the proximal jejunum (48.23%) shortly after weaning, whereas Lactobacillus accounted for over 80% in the distal jejunum . Microbes inhabiting the duodenum and jejunum have been associated with increasing DNA replication and repair . In contrast, the hindgut (cecum and colon) exhibited the highest microbial diversity, with enriched Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes involved in carbohydrate and energy metabolism, as well as SCFA production , . Archaea such as Methanobrevibacter were also highly abundant in the hindgut, where they contribute to hydrogen metabolism through methanogenesis .
Comparisons between mucosal and luminal compartments revealed that mucosal samples were enriched in Bacteroidetes and potentially pathogenic genera such as Campylobacter, while luminal samples were dominated by Firmicutes and Lactobacillus . Mucosal microbiota displayed higher alpha-diversity than luminal microbiota and included specialized functional bacteria like Mucispirillum , .

## Reduced the ISC number and proliferation inhibition
A consistent finding across studies is the significant downregulation of active ISC markers following weaning. In weaned piglets, there is a significant reduction in the volume of SOX9⁺ cells in the early stage, with the reduction being more pronounced in the jejunum compared to the colon, indicating segment-specific vulnerabilities . Similarly, in early-weaned mice, the numbers of Olfm4⁺ and Lgr5⁺ ISCs reduced by 36 and 38%, respectively, with a decrease in Ki67⁺ proliferating cells within the crypts . This decline is not limited to active ISCs; the population of quiescent reserve ISCs (marked by HOPX) also diminishes significantly . Functional evidence from ex vivo organoid cultures corroborates these in vivo observations: jejunal crypts from weaned mice exhibit a reduced crypt formation efficiency and organoid surface area, confirming an intrinsic impairment in ISC self-renewal capacity .

## Imbalanced ISC differentiation and functional disorder
Weaning stress disrupts the normal differentiation course of ISCs, leading to an imbalance in the production of functional epithelial cells. The generation of absorptive enterocytes (Villin⁺ cells) is compromised, resulting in reduced activities of key disaccharidases in the brush border. For instance, maltase activity is notably decreased in weaned piglets from the early to mid stage . The secretory cell lineage also exhibits considerable disorder: goblet cell numbers show dynamic fluctuations ; the expression of the EEC marker chromogranin A increases initially and then decreases , ; and Paneth cell numbers, critical for the Wnt-secreting niche, transiently increase in the early stage before normalizing later , . This collective differentiation disorder undermines both digestive and barrier functions of the intestine.

## Abnormal signaling pathways regulating ISC function
Weaning stress exerts multifaceted inhibitory effects on ISCs through the dysregulation of key signaling pathways. Firstly, the canonical Wnt/β-catenin pathway is significantly suppressed, as evidenced by a reduction in both total and nuclear β-catenin expression within crypts, along with a downregulation of mRNA levels of Wnt target genes such as Axin2 and Lgr5 . The functional relevance of this suppression is demonstrated by the partial rescue of organoid growth defects upon administration of the Wnt agonist R-spondin1 . Concurrently, oxidative stress emerges as another critical factor. In weaned piglets at the early stage, increased Keap1 expression and decreased Nrf2 phosphorylation led to diminished activity of antioxidant defense genes, including HO-1 and SOD1. This disruption results in a marked oxidative imbalance, characterized by increased malondialdehyde levels and decreased glutathione peroxidase activity in jejunum and serum . Genetic variations appear to influence this response, as stress-resistant pig breeds (Taoyuan black) are able to maintain Nrf2 pathway activity and mitigate ISC damage . In addition, weaning induces an inflammatory response and metabolic stress. In the early stage after weaning, pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-17 and IL-22 are elevated and, beyond a certain threshold, can paradoxically suppress ISC proliferation . Metabolically, weaning-induced decrease in plasma glutamine levels inhibit the mTOR pathway, thereby impairing energy-sensing mechanisms essential for ISC maintenance and repair , . Together, these alterations—Wnt pathway suppression, oxidative imbalance, inflammatory activation, and metabolic stress—collectively contribute to ISC dysfunction during the weaning transition.

## Intestinal villous morphological injury
The cumulative disruption of the above critical signaling pathways compromises the regenerative capacity of the crypt. When ISC proliferation fails to keep pace with the rate of epithelial shedding, this cellular imbalance structurally manifests as villus atrophy and crypt hyperplasia. For instance, in rats weaned at 18 days of age, jejunal villus height (VH) significantly decreased within 3 days post-weaning. Notably, such morphological damage persists into early adulthood (60 days), with recovery only observed in late adulthood (120 days), indicating long-term programming effects of early-life stress . Similarly, in weaned piglets, jejunal VH drops sharply from about 898 μm during lactation to approximately 202 μm in the early-stage after weaning, with villi shifting from long and finger-like to short and tongue-shaped . Breed differences also influence stress susceptibility: Duroc piglets exhibit significant jejunal shortening and a lower VH/CD ratio in the early stage after weaning, whereas Chinese indigenous Taoyuan black pigs show no significant changes, highlighting genetic variability in stress resilience . In response to the reduced absorptive capacity of the small intestine, the colon undergoes compensatory crypt hyperplasia, indicated by an increase in crypt depth (CD) from around 193 μm in suckling piglets to about 250 μm in weaned piglets at the early-stage. However, this adaptation is insufficient, as epithelial volume density decreases, branched crypts are reduced, and overall mucosal integrity remains impaired . Additionally, feed restriction—a common nutritional stress during weaning—aggravates intestinal morphological injury. Piglets limited to 20% of ad libitum intake show a 21.3% reduction in jejunal VH compared to fully-fed animals (255 vs. 324 μm) . Generally, VH is markedly reduced, whereas CD changes vary by species and intestinal segment. This leads to a significant decline in the VH/CD ratio, reflecting a substantial loss of functional absorptive surface area.

## Disruption of intestinal barrier function
Weaning induces a synergistic impairment of the intestinal barrier by disrupting its physical, chemical, and immune components. A key feature of this disruption is the consistent downregulation of tight junction proteins—such as ZO-1, Occludin, and Claudins—which compromises epithelial integrity. This molecular alteration may lead to functional deficits and increased intestinal permeability, evidenced by decreased jejunal transepithelial electrical resistance, elevated serum levels of D-lactic acid, and LPS in the early stage after weaning , . Similarly, in weaned mice, decreased expression of Claudin 2 and Claudin 4 has been observed, with the extent of Claudin 4 downregulation positively correlating with intestinal injury severity . The chemical barrier, represented by the mucus layer, is also significantly altered during weaning. Goblet cell numbers often follow a dynamic pattern of initial reduction followed by recovery. For example, in early-weaned rats, goblet cell counts are lower at 18 and 60 days but rebound to control levels by 120 days . Under pathogenic challenge, such as PEDV infection, acidic mucins—especially sialomucins—are upregulated during recovery, suggesting an adaptive mechanism to reinforce barrier defense . Additionally, weaning triggers localized intestinal inflammation, characterized by marked upregulation of pro-inflammatory cytokines. In weaned mice, jejunal proinflammatory cytokines TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, and IL-17 mRNA levels were significantly upregulated . This pro-inflammatory state further aggravates barrier damage and hinders mucosal repair. Altogether, weaning disrupts intestinal homeostasis through coordinated impairments in tight junction integrity, mucus composition, and immune regulation, collectively increasing susceptibility to gut dysfunction.

## Potential mechanisms of weaning-induced microbial changes impair ISC function
The previous sections have demonstrated that weaning stress leads to intestinal dysbiosis and epithelial atrophy. While the correlation between microbial alterations and epithelial impairment is evident, the precise molecular crosstalk defining the weaning-microbe-ISC axis remains complex and unclear. We hypothesize that weaning-induced dysbiosis impairs ISC function by triggering a negative regulatory signaling cascade driven by two key mechanisms: the depletion of beneficial microbes that maintain the crypt niche, and the accumulation of harmful microbes and metabolites that disrupt cell cycle progression. In this section, we consolidate these potential mechanisms to provide a comprehensive overview of how the altered microbial profile drives ISC dysfunction.

## Reduced probiotic abundance weakens the protective barrier for ISCs
Post-weaning, the abundance of various probiotics significantly decreases, directly diminishing their supportive role for ISCs. For instance, the genus Blautia shows a marked decline in the jejunal and colonic mucosa after weaning . As a key probiotic, its depletion may suppress the Wnt signaling pathway, thereby reducing ISC proliferation . Similarly, the abundance of Bifidobacterium decreases in the colonic content post-weaning . This genus promotes serotonin production, thereby maintaining the balance of ISC proliferation and differentiation . Its reduction may impair ISC stemness maintenance.

## Pathogen enrichment suppresses ISC activity
Post-weaning, the abnormal increase of some pathogens may contribute to suppressing ISCs by inhibiting stem cell niche factors or releasing toxic metabolites. For example, the genus Helicobacter increases in the jejunum after weaning . Its overgrowth may inhibit the expression of stem cell niche factors and ISC proliferation, hence compromising intestinal barrier integrity , leading to disorganized crypt structure. Additionally, transient expansion of Escherichia coli might indirectly suppress the expression of stemness markers in ISCs by activating inflammatory signaling pathways via LPS .

## Complexity of the effects of microbes and metabolites
In addition to the generally accepted view that weaning leads to a decrease in beneficial bacteria and an increase in harmful bacteria, the roles of certain bacteria and metabolites require more specific discussion. Taking butyrate-producing bacteria as an example, the abundance of certain butyrate-producing bacteria increases after weaning. For instance, the genus Roseburia significantly increases in colonic content and feces , . Clostridium sensu stricto and Ruminococcaceae_UCG-014 also show increased abundance in feces . As discussed in the regulation of ISCs by SCFAs, butyrate exerts a dual effect on ISCs: it promotes ISC function at low concentrations but can be inhibitory at high concentrations or under dysbiotic conditions. However, the inhibitory high concentration usually refers to in vitro models or extreme dysbiosis. In the piglet gut, the primary challenge is a deficiency in fermentation products due to reduced feed intake and microbiota disruption immediately post-weaning . The increase in butyrate producers usually happens in the late stage after weaning, which likely helps to restore physiological butyrate levels, rather than exceeding them. Therefore, in the context of weaning stress, the restoration of butyrate-producing bacteria should be beneficial for ISC-mediated repair.
A notable characteristic of the changes in gut microbiota after weaning is the reduction in the abundance of Lactobacillus , , . The effects of Lactobacillus on ISC function are not absolute but depend on the specific species and the metabolite produced. Lactobacillus amylovorus and Lactobacillus reuteri can produce IAld to promote ISC proliferation , while Lactobacillus murinus produces IAA to block ISC differentiation into secretory lineages and leads to crypt shortening and barrier dysfunction . Therefore, the reduction in Lactobacillus after weaning can be either detrimental or beneficial, depending on which specific strain is fluctuating. Whether this species-specific effect exists in other genera is an interesting question that needs further exploration in future research.
Akkermansia muciniphila is considered a next-generation probiotic due to its beneficial properties. Its abundance in the gut of piglets usually peaks during mid-lactation and declines markedly after weaning . Akkermansia muciniphila interacts with the ISC niche in a context-dependent manner, demonstrating a functional duality. The excessive mucin degradation by Akkermansia muciniphila may inhibit the self-renewal and differentiation of ISCs into goblet cells by inducing macrophages to release pro-inflammatory factors . In contrast to the inflammation caused by excessive mucin degradation, this bacterium can also drive epithelial regeneration through metabolic signaling. The acetate and propionate produced by Akkermansia muciniphila can synergistically support the proliferation of ISCs and intestinal epithelial development , . Under conditions of weaning stress, the abundance of Akkermansia muciniphila is unlikely to trigger excessive mucin degradation; rather, it is more likely that its deficiency leads to a reduction in supportive effects on ISC proliferation.

## Potential approaches to modulating ISCs via microbes for improving gut health in weaned piglets
Based on the close interactions between gut microbes and ISCs, a promising strategy to alleviate intestinal damage caused by weaning stress involves targeting the gut microbial community and its metabolites. The goal is to optimize ISC proliferation, differentiation, and microenvironment homeostasis, thereby mitigating intestinal injury in weaned piglets. Theoretically, microbial metabolites previously summarized, such as SCFAs, tryptophan metabolites, secondary bile acids, NCG, lactate, and fructose, along with their associated microbes, hold potential as interventions or targets for improving piglet gut health via ISCs. Zhang et al. demonstrated that dietary supplementation with IAld in weaned piglets promoted ISC proliferation and differentiation by activating the AhR and Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathways, leading to improved gut morphology and enhanced barrier function . Beyond this example, there is a lack of direct evidence showing that other mentioned microbes or metabolites improve weaned piglet gut health specifically through ISC regulation. Nevertheless, numerous studies have shown that certain interventions can significantly alleviate weaning stress-induced intestinal injury, and their mechanisms may involve microbial regulation of ISCs. The following discussion relies largely on inferential evidence from microbial and morphological results and parallel studies in model organisms.

## Post-weaning interventions
Post-weaning dietary supplementation with nutrients or functional compounds is the most common nutritional intervention for weaned piglets. SCFAs are key metabolites mediating the regulatory effects of microorganisms on ISCs. Consequently, there are many intervention strategies that aim to improve gut health, possibly by increasing intestinal SCFA levels to modulate ISC function. For example, supplementing the diet of weaned piglets with mulberry leaf powder, ellagic acid, or grape seed proanthocyanidins significantly reduces the diarrhea rate, increases VH and the villus height to crypt depth ratio (V/C) in the jejunum and ileum, and promotes the expression of tight junction proteins – . These supplements can enrich SCFA-producing bacteria in the gut and elevate the concentrations of SCFAs (primarily acetate, propionate, and butyrate). Furthermore, the beneficial effects of ellagic acid can be replicated through fecal microbiota transplantation , . However, these studies have not fully elucidated the specific mechanisms by which increased SCFAs improve intestinal morphology and barrier function. It is hypothesized that they likely involve the synergistic regulation of ISCs by different SCFAs.
Providing weaned piglets with metasilicate-based alkaline mineral water (AMW) significantly alleviates weaning stress-induced colonic crypt hyperplasia and increases the V/C in the duodenum , . It also promotes secondary bile acid metabolism, increasing deoxycholic acid levels in the colon. Subsequent research indicates that AMW activates the Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway, promoting the differentiation of ISCs into goblet cells and Paneth cells, with the gut microbiota playing a crucial mediating role in this ISC differentiation . Based on previous reviews (4.1.3 part), it is speculated that AMW may influence ISC differentiation via the FXR-Wnt pathway regulated by secondary bile acids. However, the specific role of the microbiota in this process requires further investigation, and the practical feasibility of changing drinking water in commercial pig farms needs evaluation.
Oral administration of Garlic-Derived Exosome-Like Nanoparticles (GELNs) to weaned Bama mini-pigs significantly increases mucin content in the jejunum, ileum, and colon, while markedly reducing levels of IL-8 and TNF-α . It also enriches Lactobacillus reuteri and increases IPA levels in the colonic content, with a positive correlation observed between them . Therefore, GELNs may regulate ISC differentiation, particularly promoting differentiation into secretory cells (e.g., goblet cells), through the anti-inflammatory effects and AhR ligands activity of IPA . However, the potential high cost of GELNs may limit their application in animal production. The development of more suitable intervention methods is needed.

## Early-life interventions
We have concluded that the gut microbiota undergoes a shift from a milk-oriented to a fiber-fermenting type post-weaning in response to dietary changes. Therefore, an important intervention strategy during the lactation period is to supplement piglets with appropriate fiber, allowing their gut microbiota to adapt in advance. Studies have shown that providing fibrous creep feed (containing galactooligosaccharides, GOS) in addition to sow’s milk during lactation enables the gut microbiota composition of piglets to resemble the post-weaning mature state even before weaning , . This is characterized by an increased abundance of fiber-degrading bacteria, laying a foundation for adapting to plant-based diets after weaning.
Compared to piglets without fiber supplementation during lactation, those with early adaptation exhibited increased jejunal villus length and surface area post-weaning, without the sharp decline in villus length or weight loss, indicating a smoother transition , . Transcriptomic analysis revealed that pathways related to oxidative phosphorylation and barrier repair were activated at weaning in the early intervention group, resembling the state of 21 days post-weaning . This suggests that microbial maturation also drives the maturation of intestinal status, consistent with studies showing that early microbiota promotes the maturation of PDGFRa+ stromal cells, regulating ISC differentiation toward secretory lineages, and thereby enhances intestinal barrier formation .
Notably, the galactooligosaccharides used in these studies are important prebiotics. However, research indicates that supplementing with GOS alone during lactation only increases the abundance of Ruminococcaceae (butyrate producers) and Faecalibacterium (anti-inflammatory bacteria) in the colonic content, without promoting the maturation of either the gut microbiota or the intestinal epithelium . Therefore, dietary fiber supplementation appears necessary to effectively drive the maturation of both gut microbiota and intestinal epithelium to mitigate weaning stress. Nevertheless, the specific mechanisms by which dietary fiber promotes the maturation of gut microbiota and intestinal epithelium remain unclear. Elucidating this process could facilitate the development of more targeted and convenient intervention strategies.

## Maternal interventions
Studies on Akkermansia muciniphila have demonstrated that maternal interventions can significantly influence offspring ISC function . Continuous supplementation of sows with a compound probiotic (Bacillus mesentericus, Clostridium butyricum, and Enterococcus faecalis) during gestation and lactation significantly reduced diarrhea in weaned piglets and improved small intestinal VH and the V/C . However, this study provided insufficient exploration of the underlying mechanisms of the action of the compound probiotic. In another study, supplementing pregnant sows with a compound probiotic (Lactobacillus johnsonii, Bacillus subtilis, and Bacillus licheniformis) from 20 days before farrowing significantly increased the abundance of Lactobacillus in the maternal intestine and notably altered tryptophan metabolism (indicated by a reduction in IAA) and primary bile acid synthesis in colostrum . Since Lactobacillus murinus-produced IAA may impair energy supply and disrupt ISC differentiation , , the reduction of IAA in colostrum could potentially promote the functional maturation of ISCs in the newborn piglets. Furthermore, changes in bile acid-related pathways might regulate ISCs via the FXR receptor.
Additionally, supplementing sows with inulin from day 85 of gestation until the end of lactation alleviated intestinal oxidative stress in piglets, enhanced intestinal tight junction integrity, and led to significant enrichment of Ruminococcus and Christensenellaceae_R-7_group—key bacteria involved in secondary bile acid synthesis—in the weaned piglet intestine . This was accompanied by increased concentrations of secondary and total bile acids in feces . Therefore, the maternal inulin supplementation may act through the various improved secondary bile acids as FXR ligands to activate or inhibit the FXR-Wnt pathway, thereby regulating ISC proliferation and differentiation. Generally, studies on maternal nutritional interventions have identified effective strategies for improving intestinal health in weaned piglets. However, most lack in-depth mechanistic exploration and seldom focus specifically on the regulation of ISCs. Therefore, investigating the regulatory impact on ISCs represents a promising new direction for future research.
Maternal and early-life interventions generally aim to seed the pioneer microbiome and program ISC development in utero or during suckling, potentially conferring resilience before the stress occurs. In contrast, post-weaning interventions act as rescue therapies to accelerate the transition from the acute atrophic phase to the recovery phase.

## Future directions and challenges

## Future research directions for regulatory mechanisms
Currently, most studies on the regulatory mechanisms of gut microbiota on ISCs have been conducted in mice. While murine models offer a potent genetic tool for elucidating fundamental signaling pathways, a significant bottleneck remains in the direct translation of these findings to porcine physiology. Future research should address the challenge of cross-species heterogeneity to establish precise regulatory mechanisms relevant to the weaned piglet.
Firstly, researchers should account for distinct anatomical and physiological disparities. Unlike the murine gastrointestinal tract, which relies heavily on cecal fermentation, the porcine gut possesses a distinct spiral colon and overall gastrointestinal architecture closer to humans than rodents . These anatomical differences influence transit times, pH gradients, and distinct fermentation niches that modulate metabolite availability for ISCs. Secondly, regarding the microbiome, although functional gene families related to metabolism are relatively conserved across different hosts, the specific taxonomic composition may differ substantially . Commensal taxa that drive homeostasis in mice may not be present or functionally equivalent in the swine gut ecosystem. Furthermore, broadly conserved intracellular signaling (e.g., Wnt/β-catenin, Notch) does not guarantee identical intercellular regulation. The specific architecture of the stem cell niche and the source of niche signals are less well-defined in swine compared to the extensively mapped murine crypt. Consequently, mechanistic hypotheses generated from mouse data should not be assumed to be universal. Therefore, pig-specific validation is required. Future investigations should prioritize the use of weaned piglets or porcine intestinal organoids (enteroids) to confirm species-specific receptor affinity, signaling kinetics, and the precise therapeutic windows for microbial interventions .

## Challenges in implementation
While the modulation of ISCs via gut microbiota presents a promising strategy for enhancing weaned piglet health, translating these findings from the lab to the farm faces substantial practical barriers.
First, manufacturing constraints limit the large-scale production of potential probiotics. Unlike traditional feed additives such as Lactobacillus or Bacillus, many candidate species identified for ISC support, including Faecalibacterium prausnitzii and Akkermansia muciniphila, are strict anaerobes. These organisms are difficult to culture at industrial scales and inactivate rapidly by oxidative stress during processing and storage in feed bags .
Second, effective delivery methods suited for livestock production remain a bottleneck. In murine studies, precise dosing is achieved via oral gavage, a method that is suitable in the lab and impractical for processing thousands of piglets in commercial herds. Practical administration routes include water lines or pelleted feed. However, the pelleting process involves high temperatures and pressure, which can kill living bacteria and denature bioactive compounds unless advanced encapsulation technologies are employed.
Third, the cost-efficacy ratio decides the feasibility of nutritional interventions. Supplementation with pure microbial metabolites, such as butyrate or I3A, offers direct and consistent ISC stimulation but is often too expensive for livestock production. Conversely, precursor strategies (e.g., dietary fibers) are economically viable but biologically less predictable, as their efficacy depends on the functional capacity of the resident microbiota to ferment these substrates into beneficial metabolites .
Finally, regulatory barriers for novel probiotics are significantly higher than for traditional probiotics. Such novel probiotics lacking a long history of safe use face strict scrutiny regarding their classification. Whether these agents function as feed additives or veterinary drugs must be determined, particularly if they modulate host signaling pathways directly. Furthermore, genomic safety assessments are mandatory to ensure these strains do not harbor transmissible antibiotic resistance genes or virulence factors, a critical concern in the context of global antimicrobial resistance .
To address these challenges, several technological and strategic advancements are emerging to bridge the gap between laboratory efficacy and farm application. First, to overcome bacterial instability and manufacturing constraints, a shift toward postbiotics (inactivated microbial cells or their cellular components) offers a practical solution. Postbiotics retain the biological function via surface antigens while eliminating viability concerns and reducing storage costs . Regarding economic feasibility, synbiotic strategies that pair specific probiotic strains with their preferred dietary fiber substrates can synergistically enhance colonization and endogenous metabolite production, offering a more cost-effective approach than supplementing with expensive pure metabolites . Finally, the integration of whole-genome sequencing into safety assessments allows for the precise detection and exclusion of transmissible antibiotic resistance genes, thereby streamlining the approval process for novel feed additives, ensuring they meet safety criteria for livestock use .

## Summary
The transition from lactation to post-weaning feed represents a critical bottleneck in swine production, where intestinal health is frequently compromised. As synthesized in this review, the structural and functional integrity of the intestinal epithelium relies fundamentally on the homeostasis of ISCs. A central theme emerging from recent literature is that the gut microbiota is not merely a passive resident but an indispensable regulator of ISC dynamics. The crosstalk between the microbiome and the ISC niche creates a complex regulatory network involving microbial metabolites, immune modulation, and direct epithelial-stromal interactions. Weaning-induced dysbiosis breaks this symbiotic link, depriving stem cells of essential proliferative signals while exposing the crypt to localized inflammation and oxidative stress that further suppresses regenerative capacity. Consequently, therapeutic strategies must evolve beyond simple pathogen elimination to focus on actively supporting epithelial regeneration. Nutritional interventions targeting the microbiota–ISC axis show significant promise. Evidence suggests that while post-weaning supplementation with probiotics, prebiotics, and specific functional nutrients can mitigate damage, a preventive strategy may be more effective. Interventions applied during the maternal gestation-lactation cycle or early neonatal life can seed a resilient microbial ecosystem and establish a robust ISC niche capable of withstanding weaning stress.
In conclusion, maintaining ISC function via precise microbial regulation offers a novel and effective pathway to alleviate weaning stress. Future research should employ multi-omics approaches to identify specific bacterial strains and metabolites that directly stimulate ISC self-renewal and differentiation. Translating these mechanistic insights into targeted nutritional technologies will be key to enhancing gut health, improving survival rates, and maximizing the growth potential of weaned piglets in modern animal husbandry.
