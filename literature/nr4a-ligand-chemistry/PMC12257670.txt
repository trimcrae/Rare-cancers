## Introduction
Diabetes mellitus (DM) is a metabolic disorder characterized by persistent hyperglycemia, resulting from either impaired insulin secretion or insulin resistance. Chronic DM is associated with a range of complications that can lead to damage, dysfunction, and even failure of various organs and tissues. Among these complications, endothelial dysfunction is particularly prevalent [ ]. Endothelial cells (ECs), which constitute a critical component of the vascular wall, play a vital role in maintaining both the structural integrity and functional capacity of the vasculature [ ]. These cells are responsible for the secretion of numerous vasodilatory substances, such as nitric oxide (NO), prostacyclin, hydrogen sulfide (H2S), and endothelium-derived hyperpolarization factors, as well as vasoconstrictive agents like thromboxane A2 and endothelin-1, which collectively regulate vascular tone [ ]. Prolonged exposure to hyperglycemia in diabetes leads to increased endothelial cell death, impaired NO signaling, heightened vascular inflammation, and augmented oxidative stress, all of which contribute to the onset and exacerbation of endothelial dysfunction [ ]. However, to date, the exact molecular mechanisms and the proteins involved in diabetic endothelial cell injury remain unclear.
Post-translational modifications (PTMs) refer to the chemical alterations made to amino acid residues in proteins after translation, achieved through the addition or removal of specific functional groups. These modifications significantly influence a protein's structure and dynamics, ultimately regulating its localization, folding, interactions with other biomolecules, and overall activity [ ]. Abnormalities in PTMs can lead to various diseases due to compromised protein functionality and biological processes. Research has established that PTMs are crucial in the physiological and pathological mechanisms underlying numerous non-infectious metabolic disorders. Therefore, a comprehensive understanding of the various types of protein modifications associated with diabetic endothelial dysfunction is essential, as it may elucidate the mechanistic pathways of diabetes and potentially pave the way for targeted therapeutic strategies for diabetic cardiovascular complications [ ]. Currently, over 600 distinct types of protein modifications have been identified, facilitated by advanced high-precision protein structure detection methodologies [ ]. PTMs can be classified into several categories: acylation modifications (including acetylation, lactylation, palmitoylation, myristoylation, succinylation, and crotonylation); ubiquitin-like protein conjugation (including ubiquitination, SUMOylation, and neddylation), lipid modifications (including prenylation and cholesterylation); redox modifications (including glutathionylation and S-nitrosylation); and various other special chemical modifications (including glycosylation, phosphorylation, methylation and carbonylation). Furthermore, ongoing advancements in high-throughput histology and sensitive mass spectrometry techniques continue to reveal novel post-translational modifications.
PTMs of proteins can significantly influence mitochondrial function, regulate insulin secretion, and impact cellular development and viability, as well as modulate insulin gene transcription and the transport of insulin vesicles [ , ]. These alterations may contribute to endothelial dysfunction in diabetes mellitus [ ]. This narrative review offers a comprehensive overview of both common and novel protein modifications associated with diabetic endothelial dysfunction. Furthermore, the review summarizes pharmacological interventions targeting PTMs to attenuate diabetic endothelial dysfunction, with the objective of identifying innovative therapeutic strategies for the prevention and treatment of diabetic endothelial dysfunction.

## Distinct types of protein modifications in diabetic endothelial dysfunction

## Glycosylation
Protein glycosylation refers to the enzymatic process of attaching carbohydrate moieties to proteins or lipids, facilitated by glycosyltransferase enzymes. This modification is recognized as one of the most prevalent and complex forms of post-translational modification, significantly enhancing the diversity of an organism's proteome beyond what is encoded in its genome, thereby imparting a broader range of biological functions to the modified proteins [ ]. Research indicates that protein glycosylation is associated with various of chronic diseases, including cancer, autoimmune disorders, rheumatoid arthritis, systemic lupus erythematosus, inflammatory bowel disease, and diabetes mellitus [ ]. The investigation of protein glycosylation encompasses several key steps: identifying glycosylated target proteins, pinpointing specific glycosylation sites, analyzing the resultant alterations in protein function, and ultimately exploring the biological roles of glycosylation in the target proteins.
Currently, various types of glycosylation modifications have been characterized, including N-glycosylation, O-glycosylation, C-glycosylation, and glycosylphosphatidylinositol anchoring, with the first two being the most prevalent and extensively studied. N-glycosylation involves the covalent attachment of a sugar chain to the side-chain amino group of a specific asparagine residue within a protein, typically initiated in the endoplasmic reticulum and completed in the Golgi apparatus. O-glycosylation entails the covalent linkage of a sugar chain to the hydroxyl group of serine or threonine residues in proteins, occurring predominantly within the Golgi compartment [ ]. O-glycosylation can be further categorized into mucin-type and O-GlcNAc glycosylation, with the latter receiving considerable attention in recent research.
Under diabetic conditions, modifications in N-glycosylation can significantly impact the functionality of various proteins, including cell surface receptors, signal transduction molecules, and cell adhesion molecules. N-glycosylation is primarily mediated by N-acetylglucosaminyltransferase and involves three key processes: the synthesis, transfer, and modification of N-glycans. Following N-glycosylation, specific chemical bonds are established. The inactivation of N-acetylglucosaminyltransferase can result in diminished insulin secretion and increased blood glucose levels [ ]. Research indicates that the altered N-glycosylation profile may provide novel insights and potential therapeutic targets for the treatment and drug development aimed at addressing diabetic endothelial dysfunction.
O-GlcNAc glycosylation refers to the enzymatic addition of a single N-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc) moiety to the serine (Ser) and threonine (Thr) residues of proteins, a process catalyzed by the enzyme O-GlcNAc transferase (OGT). This form of glycosylation is characterized by its dynamic and reversible nature, with the removal of GlcNAc facilitated by O-GlcNAcase (OGA). Specifically, OGT utilizes uridine diphosphate-GlcNAc as a substrate, which is synthesized through the hexosamine biosynthetic pathway. In diabetes, a persistent hyperglycemic state enhances hexosamine biosynthetic pathway activity, resulting in increased intracellular levels of OGT and subsequently promoting O-GlcNAc glycosylation [ , ]. OGT is ubiquitously expressed across various cell types, with particularly high expression levels observed in pancreatic β-cells. Notably, the overexpression of OGA in murine β-cells has been shown to significantly reduce insulin secretion and compromise glucose tolerance [ ]. Regarding cardiovascular health, the regulation of O-GlcNAc glycosylation is critical; depending on the nature of cardiovascular injury, either an increase in OGT or a decrease in OGA may confer benefits [ ]. It is well established that a hyperglycemic environment is associated with heightened apoptosis and an accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) in endothelial cells, which not only exacerbates oxidative stress but also adversely affects mitochondrial function and the activity and stability of both N-glycosylation and O-glycosylation enzymes.

Advanced glycation end products (AGEs) are defined as byproducts formed when the free amino groups of macromolecules, such as proteins, amino acids, and lipids, undergo condensation, rearrangement, cleavage, and oxidative modification in the presence of aldehyde groups from reducing sugars, all occurring under non-enzymatic conditions [ ]. The binding of AGEs to the receptor for advanced glycation end products (RAGE) exacerbates inflammatory responses and oxidative stress, thereby facilitating the development of diabetic complications. Prior studies have demonstrated that AGEs inhibit endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) activity, diminish NO production, promote endothelial dysfunction, and contribute to vascular injury. The pathophysiology and potential therapeutic targets associated with AGEs-induced vascular injury in diabetes mellitus are intricately linked to various molecules and signaling pathways, including nuclear factor-κB (NF-κB), ROS, interleukin (IL)-6 and IL-8, tumor necrosis factor-α, NO, mitogen-activated protein kinases (MAPK), platelet-derived growth factors, and transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β), among others [ ].
Research has demonstrated that Moesin contributes to the disruption of endothelial barrier integrity induced by AGEs through the activation of the glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) receptor/cAMP/PKA signaling pathway. This activation subsequently leads to the down-regulation of the Rho/ROCK, p38, and PKCβ signaling pathways [ ]. Additionally, peroxidase exacerbates AGEs-induced diabetic vascular endothelial dysfunction by inhibiting the phosphorylation of eNOS at Ser1177. Furthermore, AGEs have been shown to directly induce apoptosis in pancreatic β-cells, ultimately leading to a reduction in insulin synthesis and secretion, which contributes to endothelial dysfunction [ ]. In addition to the RAGE, AGEs interact with various other receptor proteins, indicating that multiple receptor-ligand interactions may be implicated in AGEs-mediated vascular injury [ ]. Collectively, these findings suggest that glycosylation primarily plays a role in maintaining and regulating normal physiological functions, whereas AGEs are associated with the progression of chronic damage.
While various forms of glycosylation modifications have been recognized for their impact on endothelial function in the context of diabetes, further research is necessary to elucidate the mechanistic roles and molecular pathways associated with alterations in glycosylation in this disease. Consequently, an in-depth investigation into the specific mechanisms of glycosylation is essential for enhancing our understanding of the interplay between glycosylation and endothelial dysfunction in diabetes, which may ultimately lead to the development of innovative therapeutic approaches.

## Ubiquitination
Ubiquitination is prevalent among eukaryotic organisms. It plays a crucial role in the formation of the ubiquitin–proteasome system, which is essential for protein degradation. Ubiquitin itself is a protein composed of 76 amino acids and contains seven lysine residues (K6, K11, K27, K29, K33, K48, and K63). Each of these lysine residues can undergo ubiquitination, thereby facilitating the formation of multimeric ubiquitin chains and contributing to a dynamic form of post-translational modification [ ]. Distinct signaling pathways, mediated by various polyubiquitin chains, can influence the fate of substrate proteins involved in processes such as DNA damage repair, autophagy, metabolism, apoptosis, immune responses, and tumorigenesis [ , ]. The ubiquitination system comprises several components, including ubiquitin, the proteasome, the ubiquitin-activating enzyme E1, the ubiquitin-conjugating enzyme E2, and the ubiquitin ligase E3, among others. The ubiquitination process is multifaceted, involving the activation of ubiquitin by the E1 enzyme, the binding of ubiquitin to the E2 enzyme, and the ligation of ubiquitin to substrate proteins via the E3 enzyme. E3 ubiquitin ligases are categorized into distinct families based on their structural characteristics and mechanisms of action, specifically RING E3s, HECT E3s, and RBR E3s. Furthermore, E3 ubiquitin ligases serve as crucial regulators of endothelial function, with significant implications for endothelial barrier integrity, endothelial activation, and the apoptosis of endothelial cells [ ]. Ubiquitination plays a significant role in various cellular processes, including endothelial cell apoptosis, vascular permeability, inflammatory responses, and endothelial cell metabolism, thereby contributing to the regulation of molecular mechanisms linked to diabetic endothelial dysfunction. Research has shown that the ubiquitin–proteasome system can influence the degradation of the insulin receptor and its associated substrates, potentially leading to insulin resistance or deficiency [ ]. Inhibiting the ubiquitin-activating enzyme E1 can mitigate insulin resistance [ ]. Therefore, targeting ubiquitination can significantly influence various pathophysiological processes.
Chronic hyperglycemia is known to cause irreversible damage to both microvessels and macrovessels, thereby increasing the risk of mortality associated with cardiovascular disease. The Cullin 3 ring E3 ubiquitin ligase (CRL3) complex is classified within the E3 ubiquitin ligase family. In the context of diabetes, dysfunction of CRL3 may be associated with heightened vasoconstriction and sodium reabsorption, potentially influencing the progression of diabetes [ ]. Two ubiquitin ligases, suppressor of cytokine signaling 1 and 3, can inhibit the expression of insulin receptor substrate (IRS), a critical intermediary in the insulin resistance [ ]. Mitsugumin 53 (MG53), a muscle-specific ubiquitin ligase, has been shown to exacerbate metabolic disorders under conditions of elevated glucose. Given the relationship between MG53 levels and cardiomyocyte damage, MG53 may serve as a biomarker for predicting adverse cardiovascular events [ ].
HECT, C2, and WW domain-containing E3 ubiquitin protein ligase 2 (HECW2) a newly identified E3 ubiquitin ligase, plays a crucial role in stabilizing and promoting endothelial cell junctions by regulating the stability of angiomotin-like 1 (AMOTL1). The absence of HECW2 results in the disruption of junctions in human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVECs) [ ]. Recent investigations have indicated that toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) is implicated in metabolism-related cardiovascular diseases, functioning through the E3 ubiquitin-protein ligase Triad3A, which facilitates the proteasomal degradation of TLR9 via K48-linked ubiquitination. Inhibition of Triad3A expression has been shown to enhance TLR9 activity, thereby promoting its proteasomal degradation and improving cardiac function and pathological conditions in diabetic cardiomyopathy [ ]. Additionally, 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl reductase degradation, an E3 ubiquitin ligase, interacts with lectin-like oxidized low-density lipoprotein receptor-1 (LOX-1) and promotes ubiquitination and degradation of LOX-1 by the proteasome. This process inhibits endothelial apoptosis induced by oxidized low-density lipoprotein (ox-LDL) [ ]. Furthermore, vascular calcification, a notable characteristic of diabetic patients, is recognized as a serious macrovascular complication associated with diabetes. Ubiquitin-specific protease 10 has been shown to mitigate vascular calcification by activating the AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) signaling pathway [ ]. In sum, E3 ubiquitin ligases serve as crucial regulators of ubiquitination, influencing endothelial function in the context of diabetes mellitus. However, the precise molecular mechanisms involved, as well as effective and targeted therapeutic strategies, require further comprehensive investigation.

## Phosphorylation
Protein phosphorylation, identified as the first form of post-translational modification in 1906, is prevalent across a diverse array of cell types. This process involves the transfer of high-energy phosphate groups, derived from compounds such as ATP or GTP, to specific amino acid residues, including Ser, Thr, and tyrosinase (Tyr), within target proteins. This transfer is facilitated by the enzymatic activity of protein kinases. Conversely, protein dephosphorylation, which serves as the reverse of phosphorylation, is mediated by protein phosphatases [ , ]. Phosphorylation is currently the most widely studied and deeply researched form of post-translational modification.
Elevated glucose levels influence protein phosphorylation, thereby impacting endothelial cell functionality. Research has identified that the phosphorylation of various proteins plays a critical role in regulating endothelial function under diabetic conditions. This phosphorylation process is prevalent in both insulin biosynthesis and the cytosolic signaling pathways associated with insulin [ ]. GLP-1 has been shown to mitigate endothelial barrier impairment induced by diabetes through the activation of the cAMP/PKA signaling pathway, which subsequently inhibits the Rho/ROCK signaling pathway. This inhibition results in a decrease in the phosphorylation of myosin light chain (MLC) [ ]. Furthermore, moesin, which is consistently expressed in endothelial cells, is targeted by GLP-1, exerting a protective effect on endothelial cell permeability by reducing moesin phosphorylation [ ]. This action enhances the alignment of the endothelial cell cytoskeleton and promotes intercellular adhesion in response to AGE stimulation.
Src homology-2 (SH2)-containing phosphatase 2 (SHP2) is a non-receptor tyrosine phosphatase that is widely expressed across various tissues. The knockdown of SHP2 has been shown to activate the phosphorylation of IRS-2 through the AKT and ERK1/2 signaling pathways, thereby promoting glucose consumption and improving insulin resistance [ ]. Prior research has demonstrated that the receptor-type vascular endothelial protein tyrosine phosphatase (VE-PTP) can form a direct complex with eNOS and subsequently dephosphorylate eNOS at the Tyr81 site. This interaction suggests that VE-PTP diminishes eNOS activity through dephosphorylation. However, the inhibition of VE-PTP prevents this dephosphorylation, which may result in increased phosphorylation of eNOS at alternative sites, such as Ser1177, thereby enhancing eNOS activity. Furthermore, the inhibition of VE-PTP also indirectly promotes eNOS activity. Specifically, the formation and activation of complexes involving Tie-2 and CD31/VE-cadherin/ vascular endothelial growth factor receptor (VEGFR) 2 facilitate the phosphorylation of eNOS, leading to increased NO production, improved endothelial function, and a reduction in diabetes-induced endothelial dysfunction [ ]. These findings suggest that VE-PTP may represent a viable therapeutic target for the treatment of diabetic endothelial dysfunction.
Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) and its principal receptor, VEGFR2, play a critical role in maintaining vascular integrity and facilitating angiogenesis. Methylglyoxal (MGO), a highly reactive dicarbonyl compound, serves as a significant precursor for the non-enzymatic glycosylation of proteins and DNA, ultimately contributing to the formation of AGEs. The phosphorylation of VEGFR2, which is vital for normal physiological angiogenesis, is inhibited under conditions such as diabetes or elevated MGO levels, thereby compromising its normal physiological functions. Pyruvate kinase isozyme type M2 (PKM2) is a crucial enzyme involved in glycolysis. Under diabetic conditions, the phosphorylation levels of PKM2 are elevated to counteract the impairment of endothelium-dependent vasodilation. TEPP-46, a specific inhibitor of PKM2 phosphorylation, enhances the stability of the tetrameric form of PKM2, consequently improving endothelial function [ ]. Research has shown that in animal models exhibiting metabolic disorders, the presence of abnormally high levels of insulin results in the over-activation of glycogen synthase kinase-3β. This over-activation subsequently leads to the phosphorylation of MG53 at the S255 site, which enhances its E3 ligase activity and diminishes insulin sensitivity. Conversely, inhibiting the phosphorylation of MG53 at the S255 site has been shown to mitigate cardiovascular damage associated with diabetes [ ]. Furthermore, the phosphorylation of Fas-associated death domain protein facilitates the nuclear translocation of forkhead box O1 (FOXO1), which in turn decreases the expression of the insulin-degrading enzyme. This reduction in IDE expression results in decreased insulin degradation, promotes glycogen synthesis, and ultimately lowers blood glucose levels [ ]. Actually, this review provides a concise overview of the role of phosphorylation in diabetic endothelial dysfunction. It is important to note that numerous protein phosphorylations associated with diabetes have been identified, which cannot be comprehensively covered in this review.
The mechanisms underlying phosphorylation are complex, and involve various signaling pathways and molecular processes. Therefore, further investigation and elucidation of the phosphorylation of additional diabetes-related proteins will enhance the identification of novel targets for the prevention and treatment of diabetic endothelial dysfunction.

## Acetylation
Protein acetylation is a significant post-translational modification characterized by its reversible nature, which allows it to either compete with or synergize with other modifications, such as methylation and phosphorylation. This modification can be categorized based on the substrate protein affected, leading to distinctions between histone acetylation and non-histone acetylation. Furthermore, protein acetylation can be classified according to the specific acetylated amino acid, resulting in two primary types: N-terminal acetylation and lysine acetylation [ ]. Lysine acetylation involves the transfer of an acetyl group (CH3CO-) from acetyl-coenzyme A (acetyl-CoA) to the ε-amino side chain of lysine, a process facilitated by lysine acetyltransferases. This modification is reversible through the action of lysine deacetylases, which can be categorized into two principal groups based on their structural and functional characteristics: Zn2+-dependent histone deacetylases (HDAC), which primarily target histones, and NAD+-dependent sirtuins, which predominantly act on non-histone proteins. Non-histone acetylation refers to the acetylation of proteins other than histones, including transcription factors, cell signaling molecules, and metabolic enzymes [ ]. Recent research has established a strong correlation between acetylation modifications and various health conditions, including diabetes, obesity, and cardiovascular dysfunction [ ].
It is widely recognized that hyperglycemia in individuals with diabetes can lead to mitochondrial dysfunction, subsequently impairing endothelial cell function. Research has demonstrated that angiotensin II (Ang II) elevates mitochondrial GCN5L1 levels while simultaneously reducing sirtuin (SIRT) 3 levels, which promotes the acetylation of cyclophilin D (CypD) at the K166 site. This cascade of events contributes to mitochondrial dysfunction, metabolic disturbances, and increased oxidative stress, ultimately resulting in diabetic endothelial dysfunction. The administration of Mito2HOBA, a mitochondria-targeted cytotoxic isoleucine scavenger, has been shown to reverse Ang II-induced cardiovascular damage by normalizing the GCN5L1/SIRT3 ratio, thereby reducing CypD acetylation and alleviating diabetic endothelial dysfunction [ ].
A recent study demonstrated that CycloZ, a compound composed of cyclic His-Pro and zinc with antidiabetic properties, effectively ameliorated hyperglycemia and lipid metabolism by modulating lysine acetylation in a type 2 DM model utilizing KK-Ay mice. Specifically, CycloZ was found to enhance the deacetylase activity of SIRT1 by elevating NAD+ levels, which subsequently led to a reduction in lysine acetylation of various proteins in the liver and visceral adipose tissues. These proteins included peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma coactivator 1α (PGC-1α), liver kinase B1 (LKB1), and the p65 subunit of NF-κB. Consequently, CycloZ, acting as a novel NAD+ enhancer and SIRT1 deacetylase activator, improved mitochondrial function and lipid oxidation, thereby mitigating the effects of hyperglycemia [ ]. This suggests that CycloZ may be considered a novel therapeutic option for improving diabetic endothelial dysfunction.
Peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor γ (PPARγ) is associated with various biological functions, including the promotion of adipocyte differentiation, enhancement of insulin sensitivity, and reduction of blood glucose levels. Thiazolidinediones, a class of PPARγ agonists, exhibit anti-atherosclerotic properties and insulin-sensitizing effects; however, their clinical application is often limited due to adverse side effects. Recent studies have demonstrated that mice harboring the deacetylated mimic PPARγ mutation K268R/K293R in an low-density lipoprotein (LDL) receptor knockout (Ldlr−/−) model displayed improved endothelial-dependent vasodilation and a reduction in the expression of pro-atherosclerotic factors, including inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), IL-6, and NADPH oxidase 2 [ ]. These findings indicate that by modulating the deacetylated state of PPARγ, it may be possible to preserve the insulin-sensitizing effects of thiazolidinediones while reducing their associated risks, thereby offering a promising therapeutic strategy for managing cardiovascular disease risk in the context of diabetes treatment.
LDL particles present in plasma undergo transcytosis across endothelial cells, leading to the formation of deposits beneath the endothelium. This phenomenon is recognized as an early stage of atherosclerosis (AS). SIRT6, an NAD+-dependent deacetylase, plays a significant role in this process, while Caveolin-1 is crucial for regulating endothelial cell function and vascular permeability in the context of cardiovascular diseases. Recent research has demonstrated that SIRT6 enhances the stability of Caveolin-1 through its deacetylation, which initiates the autophagic degradation pathway, thereby diminishing the transcytosis of LDL across endothelial cells, and ultimately retarding the progression of AS [ ]. Notably, α-tubulin, a principal component of the cytoskeleton, is subject to acetylation at the lysine 40 residue (Lys40 or K40). Increased acetylation at this site has been shown to inhibit the translocation of the glucose transporter protein 4 to the plasma membrane, resulting in reduced glucose uptake and glycolytic activity, which exacerbates cellular damage. The specific HDAC6 inhibitor Tubacin has been found to decrease the acetylation of α-tubulin at K40, thereby providing cardioprotective effects. Furthermore, findings indicate an upregulation of HDAC6 and SIRT2 expression in diabetic mice, with no corresponding change in SIRT1 expression [ ]. These studies offer potential therapeutic insights into the vascular complications associated with diabetes mellitus.
Another investigation revealed that, in the context of diabetic retinopathy (DR), elevated glucose levels resulted in increased acetylation of Mfn2, which subsequently inhibited GTPase activity in human retinal endothelial cells. This biochemical alteration contributed to mitochondrial depolarization, dysfunction, and fragmentation, thereby exacerbating damage to endothelial cells. Notably, the overexpression of deacetylase 1 was found to reverse the acetylation of Mfn2 and enhance the clearance of damaged mitochondria [ ]. These finding provide novel insights and potential therapeutic targets for the management of diabetic endothelial dysfunction.
The roles and mechanisms of acetylation in the process of diabetic endothelial dysfunction have been illustrated (Fig.  ). However, recent research has primarily focused on the histone acetylation modifications of various enzymes, revealing a lack of a consistent regulatory framework. The mechanisms by which the positioning of acylation modification sites and enzymatic reactions influence diabetes and its associated vascular complications remain largely unexplored. Therefore, further investigation is necessary to elucidate the role of acetylation in the pathogenesis of diabetic endothelial dysfunction. 
Acetylation of proteins, including CypD-K166, PGC-1α, LKB1, NF-κB p65, PPARγ, Caveolin-1, α-tubulin K40, and Mfn2, regulates metabolic disturbances, mitochondrial impairment, oxidative stress, and lipid peroxidation. These processes lead to pathological changes such as atherosclerosis, glycolysis, ultimately leading to diabetic endothelial dysfunction.

## Lactylation
Lactate has traditionally been regarded as a metabolic byproduct of glycolysis. However, recent research has demonstrated that lactate plays a significant role in regulating various cellular activities. Lactate is involved in post-translational modifications, specifically the lactylation of histones and non-histone proteins, which subsequently influence gene expression and protein functionality [ , ]. Furthermore, lactylation modifications are modulated by enzymes such as lactate transferase and delactonase, which are crucial in several cardiovascular diseases [ ].
Research has shown that lipid peroxidation facilitates the coordinated regulation of histone H3 lysine 18 (H3K18) lactylation by the histone chaperones ASF1A and P300. This process subsequently enhances the expression of the endothelial-mesenchymal transition (EndoMT) transcription factor SNAI1, thereby contributing to endothelial dysfunction [ ]. Furthermore, hypoxia induces lactylation of Snail1 through the lactate transporter protein monocarboxylate transporter (MCT). Following its nuclear translocation, Snail1 activates the TGF-β/Smad2 signaling pathway, which regulates EndoMT and exacerbates vascular endothelial injury. Inhibition of Snail1 or the application of MCT inhibitors, such as CHC, has been shown to mitigate EndoMT and alleviate vascular injury [ ]. VEGF has been shown to enhance the lactylation of histone H3 at lysine 9 (H3K9) in endothelial cells, which subsequently facilitates the upregulation of various genes, including EGFR, TGFBR2, and PRCP, thereby promoting angiogenesis. Furthermore, feedback mechanisms involving H3K9 lactylation and HDAC2 in endothelial cells modulate VEGF-induced angiogenesis. Specifically, elevated levels of H3K9 lactylation lead to a reduction in HDAC2 expression, while increased expression of HDAC2 results in decreased H3K9 lactylation, ultimately serving to inhibit angiogenic processes [ ]. Lactate serves as a crucial energy substrate for cardiac tissue, and research has identified that α-myosin heavy chain (α-MHC) undergoes lactylation at lysine 1897 (K1897). This post-translational modification plays a significant role in modulating the interaction between α-MHC and titin, both of which are vital for maintaining cardiac structure and function. Ang II reduces lactate production and decreases lactylation of α-MHC, subsequently impairing the interactions between α-MHC and titin, which exacerbates heart failure. Conversely, the administration of sodium lactate or the inhibition of specific lactate transporter proteins within cardiomyocytes can elevate lactate levels, thereby promoting the lactylation of α-MHC at K1897. This enhancement facilitates the interaction between α-MHC and titin, ultimately contributing to the alleviation of heart failure and the improvement of endothelial function [ ]. Diabetic retinopathy is a significant microvascular complication associated with diabetes, with microvascular endothelial cells being primary targets of damage induced by hyperglycemia. The N6-methyladenosine (m6A) demethylase known as fat mass and obesity-associated protein (FTO) has emerged as a potential regulatory factor in the context of DR. Research indicates that H3K18 lactylation-driven FTO targets cyclin-dependent kinase 2 (CDK2) to aggravate microvascular anomalies in diabetic retinopathy via an m6A-YTHDF2-dependent manner. This process disrupts the interactions between endothelial cells and pericytes, ultimately exacerbating microvascular leakage associated with diabetes [ ]. Microglia, the resident immune cells of the central nervous system and retina, are integral to the processes of angiogenesis and vasculopathy. In hypoxic conditions, the transcription factor YY1 within microglia undergoes lacylation at the lysine 183 (K183) position, a modification regulated by the histone acetyltransferase p300. The lactylation of YY1 directly facilitates the transcription of fibroblast growth factor (FGF) 2, thereby promoting angiogenesis [ ]. Consequently, targeting the lactate/p300/YY1 lactylation/FGF2 pathway may represent a novel therapeutic strategy for the treatment of proliferative retinopathy.
Orphan nuclear receptor NR4A3 plays a crucial role in inhibiting the phenotypic transition of vascular smooth muscle cells and the deposition of calcium salts in the aorta by facilitating histone lactylation modifications, particularly at the K18 site of histone H3 (H3K18). This modification enhances the binding of key glycolytic genes, specifically fructose bisphosphate aldolase A and hepatic phosphofructokinase, within their promoter regions, resulting in increased lactate production through glycolysis. Consequently, this process elevates the level of lactate modification at the H3K18 site in the promoter region of orphanin phosphatase 1 (Phospho1), leading to the upregulation of Phospho1 expression and subsequently promoting the progression of vascular calcification [ ]. Diabetic kidney disease (DKD) is a prevalent microvascular complication among individuals with diabetes. Recent findings indicate that lactate facilitates EndoMT in diabetic nephropathy via the H3K14 lactylation/ krüppel-like factor (KLF) 5 signaling pathway, thereby accelerating the progression of DKD [ ]. In sum, the novel modification of lactylation in diabetes and endothelial dysfunction is intricately linked (Fig.  ) and is expected to have significant implications for future research endeavors. 
Several factors including lipid peroxidation, hypoxia, VEGF signaling, Ang II and high glucose collectively influence the lactylation of proteins such as H3K18, Snail1, H3K9, α-MHC K1897, YY-1K183, and H3K14. The intricate network of lactylation pathways associated with diabetic endothelial dysfunction highlights the interactions among various molecular factors and their implications for cardiac function, angiogenesis, and metabolic processes.

## Palmitoylation
Palmitoylation, also known as S-palmitoylation or S-acylation, is a significant post-translational modification of proteins. This process involves the covalent attachment of palmitic acid molecules to cysteine residues through thioester linkages, resulting in modifications to the protein's structure and function. Palmitoylation plays a crucial role in regulating various aspects of protein behavior, including stability, subcellular localization, interactions, and signal transduction pathways [ ]. Notably, this reaction is reversible due to the inherent instability of the thioester bond formed between palmitate and cysteine.
Protein palmitoylation has been implicated in the vascular complications associated with diabetes. A deficiency of the depalmitoylating enzyme acyl protein thioesterase 1 (APT1) in endothelial cells has been shown to impair fibronectin processing, disrupt adhesion junctions, and inhibit lumen formation in vitro. R-Ras has been identified as a substrate of APT1, and hyperglycemic conditions have been found to reduce APT1 activity in HUVECs, thereby obstructing the transport of R-Ras. This reduction in APT1 activity leads to an increase in R-Ras palmitoylation [ ]. Previous research indicates that abnormalities in the circulation of palmitoylated proteins contribute to vascular remodeling issues induced by ischemia and hyperglycemia [ ]. Therefore, enhancing the circulation of palmitoylated proteins may facilitate neovascularization in ischemic tissues affected by peripheral artery disease. These findings suggest that targeting the palmitoylated circulation may represent a promising therapeutic approach for the management of vascular diseases.
Sphingomyelin phosphodiesterase-like 3B (SMPDL3B) is an enzyme that functions within lipid rafts and exhibits anti-inflammatory properties in various diseases. Silencing of SMPDL3B has been shown to exacerbate the DR in db/db mice, with the underlying mechanism associated with the activation of the NLRP3/NF-κB pro-inflammatory pathway. Furthermore, the palmitoylation of SMPDL3B, catalyzed by zinc finger DHHC-type palmitoyltransferase 5 (ZDHHC5), is elevated under high glucose conditions [ ]. It indicated that the palmitoylation of SMPDL3B may act as a compensatory mechanism that mitigates inflammation and vascular damage, thereby providing protective effects in diabetic retinopathy.
TGR5, also known as G protein-coupled bile acid receptor 1, is classified as a G protein-coupled receptor. The enzyme palmitoyl acyltransferase DHHC4 facilitates the attachment of fatty acids, such as palmitic acid, to cysteine residues within proteins. Research indicates that TGR5 deficiency leads to an increase in cardiac fatty acid uptake and lipid accumulation, which is mediated by the upregulation of DHHC4-driven palmitoylation of CD36. This process enhances the localization of CD36 at the plasma membrane, ultimately resulting in cardiac dysfunction [ ]. These findings suggest that the TGR5-DHHC4 signaling pathway plays a significant role in regulating cardiac function and structure, highlighting the potential for targeting TGR5 in therapeutic strategies for diabetic cardiomyopathy.
Furthermore, oxidative stress may aggravate diabetic endothelial dysfunction by impairing the palmitoylation and transport of caveolin-1, thereby disrupting endothelial cell signaling and vasodilatory capacity [ ]. Additionally, angiotensin II AT1 receptor (AGTR1) is recognized as a contributor to heart failure, while membrane palmitoylated protein 1, a palmitoylated protein associated with cardiovascular health, enhances the activity of AGTR1, thereby impairing vascular function [ ]. Therefore, investigating the role of palmitoylation in diabetes and its related mechanisms may provide new avenues for the treatment of diabetic endothelial dysfunction.

## SUMOylation
Small ubiquitin-related modifier (SUMO), a member of the ubiquitin-like protein family, is a polypeptide with a molecular weight of 10 kDa that covalently attaches to target proteins at specific lysine residues via the formation of isopeptide bonds. Although SUMOylation shares structural and molecular characteristics with ubiquitination, its functional roles are distinct. SUMOylation modifications are implicated in a range of cellular processes, including transcriptional regulation, nuclear translocation, maintenance of genomic integrity, and signal transduction [ , ]. In human cells, there are four primary SUMO proteins: SUMO1, SUMO2, SUMO3, and SUMO4. Dysregulation of these SUMO proteins is closely linked to various diseases, including tumors, central nervous system disorders, cardiovascular diseases, and metabolic disorders.
Hyperglycemia in individuals with diabetes disrupts the equilibrium between SUMOylation and de-SUMOylation of VEGFR2, resulting in abnormal angiogenesis. The enzyme SUMO-specific protease (SENP) 1 acts as a de-SUMOylating agent that removes SUMO modifications from VEGFR2, thereby modulating its activity and intracellular localization. Consequently, SENP1 represents a promising therapeutic target that may alleviate diabetes-related angiogenic complications by influencing the SUMOylation status of VEGFR2, thereby facilitating wound healing and tissue repair [ ]. Research has indicated that SENP1 levels are diminished in diabetic retinal tissues, which subsequently leads to an increase in SUMO2/3 modifications of the RUNX family transcription factor 1 (RUNX1). This modification promotes the proliferation of retinal microvascular endothelial cells, thereby exacerbating the progression of diabetic retinopathy [ ]. Additionally, the de-SUMOylation of dynamin-related protein 1 (Drp1), a critical factor in mitochondrial fission, may result in mitochondrial dysfunction, adversely affecting the functionality of retinal microvascular endothelial cells and worsening diabetic retinopathy [ ]. Conversely, inhibiting Drp1 de-SUMOylation may mitigate hyperglycemia-induced mitochondrial damage and apoptosis, thereby preventing the onset of diabetes-associated retinal microvascular damage.
PPARγ is a nuclear receptor that has been identified as a critical regulator of metabolic processes, inflammatory responses, and vascular endothelial function. Excessive SUMOylation of PPARγ can lead to insulin resistance in endothelial cells, negatively regulate the eNOS-NO signaling pathway, and contribute to endothelial cells dysfunction. This process may also initiate an endogenous SUMOylation cascade, further exacerbating dysfunction in endothelial cells [ ]. Furthermore, FOXO1, a significant member of the O subfamily of forkhead transcription factors, readily interacts with PPARγ1-K77 SUMOylation. This interaction diminishes the transcriptional activity of PPARγ1 and attenuates PI3K/Akt signaling, which promotes the accumulation of FOXO1 in the nucleus and aggravates insulin resistance [ ]. These insights enhance our understanding of the mechanisms underlying insulin resistance and endothelial dysfunction, while also identifying potential pharmacological targets for the treatment of diabetic vascular diseases.
In addition, there was an increased expression of both glia maturation factor beta (GMFB), a growth and differentiation factor, and SUMO1 in the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) during the early stages of diabetic retinopathy. The SUMOylation of GMFB regulates and enhances the protein stability and function of retinal pigment epithelial cells, thereby improving outcomes in diabetic retinopathy [ ]. SUMO1 has been also identified as a direct binding partner of the sarco/endoplasmic reticulum Ca2+-ATPase 2a (SERCA2a) in human aortic vascular smooth muscle cells (HAVSMCs). Treatment with high glucose and palmitic acid resulted in an upregulation of SERCA2a SUMOylation in HAVSMCs. Overexpression of SUMO1 restored the functional and phenotypic contractile capacity of HAVSMCs, thereby mitigating vascular injury [ ]. This restoration was associated with enhanced phenotypic transformation, proliferation, and migration, as well as a reduction in Ca2+ transporter activity and an increase in resting intracellular Ca2+ concentration.
SENP6 is a crucial desumoylation enzyme that modulates the SUMOylated state of proteins by cleaving SUMO moieties. SENP6 regulates Notch1 intracellular domain (N1ICD) transcriptional activity and ubiquitination by deSUMOylation, thereby influencing the crosstalk between podocytes and glomerular endothelial cells (GEC) in diabetic kidney disease [ ]. Research has highlighted the significant role of SUMO and SUMO acylation in the formation of atherosclerotic plaques, suggesting their potential as therapeutic targets for the prevention and management of atherosclerosis [ ]. Astragaloside IV, an active compound derived from Astragalus membranaceus, exhibits a range of biological activities, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and angiogenic properties. Studies indicate that Astragaloside IV can enhance angiogenesis and facilitate wound healing in diabetic rats by activating the SUMO signaling pathway [ ]. However, given the interactions between SUMOylation and diabetic endothelial function (Fig.  ), further investigations into the regulatory mechanisms of SUMOylation modifications in diabetic endothelial cells are warranted to elucidate their role in vascular disease and to develop novel therapeutic strategies. 
SUMOylation of proteins, including VEGFR2, RUNX1, PPARγ, GMFB, SERCA2a, and N1ICD, is involved in regulating angiogenesis, mitochondrial function, insulin sensitivity, and vascular injury. Astragaloside IV exhibits anti-inflammatory, angiogenic, and antioxidant effects through the process of SUMOylation. Collectively, these factors eventually regulate the progression of diabetic endothelial dysfunction.

## Methylation
Methylation refers to the biochemical process involving the addition of methyl (-CH3) groups to DNA, RNA, or protein molecules [ ]. DNA methylation is crucial for normal developmental processes, various significant biological functions, and metabolic disorders. The role of methylation on high glucose-induced microvascular endothelial damage has attracted considerable interest among researchers. Studies on the microvasculature associated with diabetic nephropathy have identified FTO as a demethylase responsible for the removal of m6A methylation. An increase in FTO activity correlates with a reduction in overall m6A levels under hyperglycemic conditions, while the silencing of FTO in endothelial cells leads to diminished inflammatory responses. Furthermore, TNFAIP3-interacting protein 1 (TNIP1) has been identified as a downstream target of FTO, with FTO inhibiting the expression of TNIP1 mRNA by removing its m6A methylation [ ]. These observations indicate that FTO may function as a protein associated with adiposity and obesity in diabetes-induced vascular endothelial dysfunction.
Alterations in m6A methylation can influence the expression of inflammatory mediators to exacerbate renal vascular inflammation. These modifications may contribute to renal cell injury by modulating the expression of genes associated with oxidative stress [ ]. Additionally, during states of hyperinsulinemia and insulin resistance, disruptions in insulin signaling pathways can lead to abnormalities in DNA methylation, resulting in damage to podocytes and renal tubular cells, ultimately culminating in DKD. Conversely, the application of DNA methylation inhibitors has been shown to mitigate renal inflammation and improve endothelial dysfunction by inhibiting the activity of DNA methyltransferase (DNMT) [ ]. Thus, m6A methylation, as a critical epigenetic modification in the pathogenesis of diabetic complications, has garnered considerable scholarly interest.
Methyltransferase-like 14, a methyltransferase, facilitates α-klotho methylation, thereby promoting glomerular endothelial cell apoptosis and injury [ ]. In diabetic retinopathy, tet methylcytosine dioxygenase 2 (TET2) acts as a DNA demethylase, with its aberrant expression linked to retinal capillary leakage and neovascularization in a hyperglycemic environment. This effect is detrimental and contributes to the progression of diabetic retinopathy. Mechanistically, TET2 promotes neovascularization in diabetic retinopathy by decreasing promoter methylation of ECM1 and subsequently activating their transcription [ ]. Thrombospondin-1 (THBS1), an endogenous inhibitor of angiogenesis, is genetically controlled by its methylation status, which influences the development of diabetic retinopathy [ ]. Diabetic neuropathy represents one of the most prevalent chronic complications of diabetes mellitus. Its underlying mechanisms may be linked to hyperglycemia-induced alterations in DNA methylation, which affect the functionality of relevant genes. This disruption can lead to disturbances in glucose, lipid, and energy metabolism, as well as inflammatory responses, ultimately resulting in vascular and nerve damage [ ]. Interestingly, DNA methylation may also serve as a potential biomarker for early atherosclerosis in diabetic patients [ , ]. Taken together, methylation appears to play a critical role in the pathophysiology of diabetes and its associated vascular complications, including atherosclerosis, nephropathy, and retinopathy. Future research is needed to further elucidate the specific mechanisms by which these epigenetic modifications contribute to diabetic vascular complications.

## Carbonylation
Carbonylation refers to the process in which biological macromolecules, including proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids, undergo oxidation of their amino acid residues in the presence of oxidizing agents such as ROS. This process results in the introduction of carbonyl functional groups (e.g., aldehydes and ketones) and the subsequent formation of carbonylated products [ ]. Protein carbonylation serves as a biomarker for oxidative stress and is frequently linked to the pathophysiological mechanisms underlying various conditions, including cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, Alzheimer's disease, and neuroinflammation [ ]. Elevated levels of protein carbonylation are associated with the initiation and progression of these diseases.
Proliferative diabetic retinopathy (PDR) represents a critical manifestation of diabetic retinopathy, characterized by the accumulation of carbonylated proteins that can disrupt intracellular signaling pathways and impair cellular function, thereby exacerbating retinal damage. Furthermore, elevated levels of carbonyl proteins and hypoxia-inducible factor-1 (HIF-1) α in the retinas of individuals with PDR are significantly correlated with microvascular injury and neovascularization [ ]. This process may activate downstream VEGF signaling pathways to promote pathological neovascularization and visual impairment, potentially serving as a mechanism for the further progression of PDR.
In diabetic nephropathy, increased levels of protein carbonylation are linked to endothelial dysfunction, which may facilitate the advancement of diabetic nephropathy and further impair endothelial function. This phenomenon may be attributed to heightened oxidative stress under hyperglycemic conditions, negatively impacting endothelial cell functionality and vascular integrity [ ]. Future research is necessary to elucidate the relationship between protein carbonylation and diabetic endothelial dysfunction, as well as to investigate potential therapeutic strategies aimed at improving endothelial function and preventing diabetic vascular complications.

## Other types of protein modifications in diabetic endothelial dysfunction

## Myristoylation
Myristoylation is the covalent attachment of the 14-carbon saturated fatty acid myristic acid to the N-terminal glycine residue of proteins, a process catalyzed by N-myristoyltransferase (NMT) [ , ]. Myristoylated PKCζ pseudosubstrate (mPS), a myristoyl peptide that inhibits PKC activity, maintains endothelial cell function by activating eNOS and increasing NO production in endothelial cells, which is dependent on PI3K/Akt signaling and elevated cytosolic calcium level. Given that myristoylation is implicated in atherosclerosis, it is reasonable to speculate that myristoylation may be associated with endothelial dysfunction in diabetes [ ]. Furthermore, research has demonstrated that cardiac hypertrophy and heart failure could be prevented by targeting NMT2. However, knockdown of cardiac NMT2 using adeno-associated virus 9 exacerbated cardiac dysfunction, remodeling, and failure in a mouse model of pressure overload, suggesting that post-translational N-myristoylation mediated by NMT2 may represent a potential therapeutic strategy to prevent cardiac remodeling [ ]. It has been found that blocking the myristoylation of the β1 subunit of AMPK enhanced the activity of AMPK, which led to increased fatty acid oxidation, decreased fat synthesis, improved insulin sensitivity, and maintained glucose homeostasis, which collectively reduced the risk of diabetic vascular complications [ ]. Nevertheless, the specific molecular mechanism of myristoylation in diabetic endothelial dysfunction remains to be further explored.

## Glutathionylation
Glutathionylation, also known as S-glutathionylation, is the covalent attachment of glutathione molecules to cysteine residues in proteins. During this process, the sulfhydryl group of glutathione (-SH) forms a disulfide bond with the sulfhydryl group of a protein's cysteine residue, resulting in the glutathionylation of the protein. This modification can be reversible, catalyzed by glutathione transferase, or it can be reversed by the action of enzymes such as glutaredoxin. Studies have confirmed its important role in cardiovascular diseases such as myocardial ischemia and reperfusion, cardiac hypertrophy, peripheral arterial disease, and atherosclerosis [ , ]. In endothelial cells, the glutathionylation of Rac1, a small G protein, may be critical for maintaining vascular barrier integrity and function. Under conditions of oxidative stress, endothelial cells may experience glutathione depletion, resulting in reduced glutathionylation of Rac1. This reduction can affect endothelial cell barrier function and increase vascular permeability, thereby promoting the onset and progression of diseases related to metabolic disorders [ ]. It can therefore be inferred that the glutathionylation of Rac1 plays an important role in redox homeostasis and vascular barrier function of diabetic endothelial cells.
Increased levels of S-glutathionylation of hemoglobin in the diabetic state may be associated with oxidative stress and diabetic microangiopathy [ ]. Glutaredoxin-1(Glrx) is a cytoplasmic enzyme that catalyzes the reduction of S-glutathione. The upregulation of Glrx stimulates the Wnt5a/sFlt-1 pathway by enhancing NF-κB signaling and inhibits angiogenesis [ ]. Nuclear factor E2-associated factor 2 (Nrf2) is a transcription factor that plays a key role in maintaining cellular antioxidant balance. Glutathione induces the negative regulation of Keap1 S-glutathione by activating Nrf2, which alleviates glucose-induced oxidative stress and pancreatic β-cell dysfunction [ ]. In addition, glutathione biosynthesis is beneficial to maintaining endothelial function and anti-fibrotic response. Further studies are needed to investigate how glutathione affects endothelial cell proliferation, migration, and vasodilation, as well as how these changes are associated with diabetes-related vascular complications.

## Citrullination
Citrullination consists of the conversion of arginine residues in protein peptide chains to citrulline residues catalyzed by peptidylarginine deiminase (PAD). In humans, five calcium-dependent isozymes, PAD1, PAD2, PAD3, PAD4 and PAD6, have been identified. PAD plays an important role in a variety of biological processes by modifying the structure and function of cellular proteins [ ]. Anti-citrullinated protein antibodies (ACPA) are implicated in several autoimmune diseases, including rheumatoid arthritis, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, Alzheimer's disease, and ulcerative colitis of the digestive system [ ]. Research has identified various citrullinated proteins and the enzyme PAD4 in human coronary plaques. Elevated levels of ACPA are proportional to the quantity or volume of aortic plaque, which reflects the severity of atherosclerosis. These high levels may indicate an increase in the process of citrullination in vivo, which is associated with the development of atherosclerosis and cardiovascular disease [ , ]. Additionally, the guanylation of the arginine residue R510 of glucose-regulated protein 78 (GRP78) may alter its structure, promoting the immune system to attack and destroy pancreatic β-cells, thereby reducing insulin secretion and exacerbating the progression of diabetes and vascular complications [ ]. Citrullination may also affect the composition and function of the extracellular matrix, as well as regulate inflammatory responses and angiogenesis. Although research on the mechanisms of citrullination in diabetic endothelial dysfunction is still in its early stages, existing studies suggest that it may play an important role in diabetes-related vascular complications.

## Prenylation
Prenylation is the covalent attachment of a prenyl group (e.g., farnesyl or geranyl) to a cysteine residue in a protein. Defects in prenylation are associated with a variety of diseases, including cancer, cardiovascular disease, and neurological disorders [ ]. Low-dose statins may not completely inhibit 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-CoA reductase, but they are sufficient to reduce Rho GTPase prenylation, thereby affecting Akt activation and eNOS phosphorylation, as well as regulating angiogenesis [ ]. Prenylation may be involved in the regulation of cellular stress responses and protective mechanisms, contributing to the improvement of pancreatic β-cell function in diabetic organisms by modulating the prenylation status of specific proteins [ ]. Small GTPases are a class of proteins involved in a variety of cellular processes, including cell signaling, cytoskeletal reorganization and cell proliferation. They also play a role in regulating vascular endothelial cell function, including influencing vasodilation and contraction, cell proliferation and migration. The prenylation of small GTPases may affect these processes, which could, in turn, influence the development of vascular complications in diabetes [ ]. GTPase proteins regulate endothelial cell function by activating or inhibiting specific signaling pathways (e.g., MAPK, PI3K/Akt, etc.). Inhibition of prenylation may alter the activity of these signaling pathways, subsequently affecting endothelial barrier stability, intercellular tight junctions and adhesion, and endothelial cell permeability [ ]. However, the exact effector molecules of prenylation and their pathological significance in diabetes remain unclear, and current studies have not yet provided direct evidence that prenylation is directly involved in diabetic endothelial dysfunction.

## ADP-ribosylation
ADP-ribosylation is an enzymatic modification reaction involving the transfer of ADP-ribose units from a donor molecule (e.g., NAD+, nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) to an acceptor molecule. This process is primarily facilitated by ADP-ribosyltransferase [ , ]. ADP-ribosylation plays a significant role in myocardial ischemia–reperfusion, cardiac energy metabolism, and myocardial remodeling. ADP-ribosylation may affect the expression of cell adhesion molecules, which may regulate leukocyte-endothelial cell interactions during inflammatory processes [ ]. ADP-ribosylation factor 6 (Arf6), a small GTPase protein found in endothelial cells, is essential for maintaining normal vascular function, including vasodilation, blood flow, and insulin signaling. One study showed that the development and severity of atherosclerotic plaques were reduced in Arf6 heterozygous mice fed an atherogenic diet [ ]. A reduction in the endothelial-specific expression of Arf6 may hinder vasodilation and compromise blood flow to skeletal muscle, thereby exacerbating insulin resistance [ , ]. In addition, pathological retinal angiogenesis was attenuated by inhibiting vascular endothelial growth factor-induced Arf6 activation [ ]. However, the current study may only represent the tip of the iceberg, and an in-depth study of the molecular mechanisms of ADP-ribosylation is essential for identifying new therapeutic targets for the treatment of diabetic endothelial dysfunction.

## S-nitrosylation
S-nitrosylation refers to the covalent attachment of a NO derived moiety to the thiol group of a cysteine residue, resulting in the formation of S-nitrosothiol [ ]. S-nitrosylation has been implicated in various pathologies, including cardiovascular diseases, cancer, and disorders of the central nervous system [ – ]. S-nitrosylation of guanine nucleotide-binding protein G(i) subunit alpha-2 (GNAI2) dephosphorylate the Hippo pathway kinase, thereby leading to nuclear translocation of YAP and promoting inflammation in endothelial cells [ ]. RhoGAP myosin 9A (Myo9A) is a protein that contributes to cellular motility and signaling. In the context of advanced DKD, alterations in the S-nitrosylation of Myo9A may affect its functional capacity [ ]. Furthermore, S-nitrosylation can influence the function of vascular endothelial cells in diabetes by modulating inflammation, permeability, oxidative stress, mitochondrial function, and insulin sensitivity, thereby directly affecting endothelial cell functionality, barrier integrity, and nitric oxide bioavailability [ , ]. Mitogen-activated protein kinase 4 has been shown to exacerbate cardiovascular injury by facilitating the S-nitrosylation modification of mitochondrial Drp1 [ ]. The S-nitrosylation of eNOS is particularly relevant in ox-LDL-induced endothelial dysfunction, as it modulates the interaction between eNOS and β-catenin, as well as decreases NO bioavailability [ ]. Basic fibroblast growth factor (bFGF), a multifunctional cytokine, may exert anti-inflammatory effects in diabetes-related endothelial injury through the S-nitrosylation pathway. bFGF activates the AMPK/Akt/eNOS signaling cascade, enhances the S-nitrosylation of IKB Kinase β (IKKβ) and p65, and significantly ameliorates vascular endothelial cell dysfunction induced by high glucose and high-fat conditions. This action promotes neovascularization and wound healing, thereby mitigating the inflammatory response and damage to endothelial cells [ ]. Further investigation into the specific types of proteins and modification sites associated with S-nitrosylation-modified proteins is essential for a comprehensive understanding of their role in diabetic endothelial dysfunction.

## Neddylation
Neddylation, a post-translational modification similar to ubiquitination, has been implicated in various pathological conditions, including tumors, immune disorders, neurodegenerative diseases, and metabolic disorders [ – ]. The neuronal precursor cell-expressed developmentally down-regulated protein 8 (NEDD8), an 81-amino acid polypeptide that closely resembles ubiquitin. It interacts with substrate proteins through a series of enzymatic reactions involving the NEDD8-activating enzyme, NEDD8-binding enzyme, and NEDD8 ligase. In the context of age-related metabolic diseases, NEDD8-mediated neddylation may contribute to the onset of insulin resistance and metabolic dysfunction [ ]. The Cullin-associated and endodecylated dissociated 1 protein interacts with unmodified Cullin proteins, thereby regulating neddylation and the subsequent proteasomal degradation of various proteins, influencing the degradation of calmodulin phosphatases and modulating the progression of cardiac hypertrophy and heart failure [ ]. One small molecule inhibitor, MLN4924, obstructs the neddylation process by inhibiting the NEDD8-activating enzyme. This inhibition may enhance vascular intimal hyperplasia and promote apoptosis in vascular smooth muscle cells by modulating the activity of critical proteins such as p53 and p62. Extracellular vesicles derived from mesenchymal stem cells have been shown to diminish neddylation and degradation of Nrf2 through miR-143-3p-mediated inhibition of neddylation, thereby enhancing Nrf2 stability and contributing to the reduction of oxidative stress and inflammatory responses in the diabetic retina, ultimately protecting retinal cells from damage [ ]. CRL3 is a significant protein complex whose activity may be modulated by neddylation depending on NEDD8. In diabetic retinopathy, alterations in neddylation may disrupt the function of CRL3, subsequently impacting oxidative stress and sodium reabsorption in vascular endothelial cells [ ]. Ongoing research aims to elucidate the specific mechanisms by which neddylation contributes to diabetic endothelial dysfunction, highlighting the need for further studies to clarify its detailed pathways and potential therapeutic implications.

## Succinylation
Succinylation, characterized by the addition of succinyl groups to specific amino acid residues, predominantly lysine, plays a critical role in various cardiovascular conditions, including myocardial infarction, myocardial ischemia, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, atrial fibrillation, myocardial fibrosis, and aortic diseases [ ]. N-succinylated chitosan can restore the physiological functions of dysfunctional HUVECs induced by high glucose [ ]. Succinyl coenzyme A, serving as the primary donor for succinylation, was elevated in high-glucose environments associated with diabetes or metabolic dysregulation due to glucose aerobic oxidation [ ]. Given that succinylation modifications are primarily involved in metabolic regulation and significantly contribute to energy production, it is plausible that obesity and type II diabetes are also linked to dysregulated succinylation [ ]. Desuccinylation is a biochemical process that counteracts succinylation and is facilitated by specific desuccinylases, which are enzymes that remove succinyl groups from proteins, thereby modulating their functional properties. The enzyme SIRT5, a known desuccinylase, has been demonstrated to enhance insulin secretion and maintain glucose homeostasis [ ]. Additionally, SIRT5 may participate in the desuccinylation of optineurin (OPTN), a protein implicated in cellular autophagy and inflammatory responses. This modification appears to contribute to the stabilization of Nrf2, which in turn mitigates oxidative stress and alleviates damage to diabetic retinal endothelial cells [ ]. Furthermore, recent findings suggest that astragaloside can alleviate diabetic nephropathy by enhancing the succinylation of 17beta-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 10 through the upregulation of carnitine palmitoyl-transferase1alpha [ ]. However, the exploration of succinylation modifications in diabetic endothelial dysfunction is still in its nascent stages, and a deeper understanding of the specific mechanisms underlying succinylation in diabetes and its associated complications is expected to be a significant area of future research.

## Crotonylation
Crotonylation refers to the addition of a crotonyl group to lysine residues within proteins. The primary donor of crotonylation modifications is crotonyl-coenzyme A (Crotonyl-CoA), which serves as an intermediate metabolite in fatty acid oxidation as well as in the metabolism of lysine and tryptophan [ , ]. Investigations have linked crotonylation to various metabolic disorders, including ischemic heart disease, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, type 2 diabetes, and obesity [ ]. In diabetic nephropathy, crotonylation has been shown to protect renal tubular epithelial cells from damage by modulating the expression of genes related to oxidative stress responses, such as IL-1β [ ]. It suggests that crotonylation may represent a potential molecular mechanism and therapeutic target in diabetic nephropathy.
However, research on the specific mechanisms of crotonylation in diabetic endothelial dysfunction is limited. Previous studies have proposed that crotonylation may influence the signaling and metabolic pathways within endothelial cells [ ]. Future investigations are expected to further clarify the mechanisms by which crotonylation operates in vascular complications associated with diabetes, potentially revealing new therapeutic targets.

## S-sulfhydration
S-sulfhydration involves the covalent attachment of sulfhydryl groups (-SH) to specific cysteine residues in proteins, resulting in the formation of persulfide groups, which are critical for regulating protein activity, signaling, and redox homeostasis. S-sulfhydration is implicated in various pathophysiological processes affecting the vascular and nervous systems [ – ]. Integrins, which are significantly modified by S-sulfhydration, play a crucial role in vascular function. Sulfiredoxin has been shown to modulate integrin activity by reducing excessive S-sulfhydration, thereby enhancing endothelial cell adhesion to the basement membrane and preserving vascular integrity [ ]. Additionally, H2S has been demonstrated to inhibit the ubiquitin-mediated degradation of KLF4 through S-sulfhydration at the Cys327 site of tumor necrosis factor receptor-associated factor 7 (TRAF7), which subsequently attenuates endothelial barrier damage in diabetes mellitus [ ]. H2S also reduces superoxide production in endothelial cells and enhances diabetic vascular endothelial function by promoting the translocation of Nrf2 via Keap1-Cys151 S-sulfhydration [ ]. Thus, H2S may play a protective role in diabetic endothelial dysfunction through the mechanism of S-sulfhydration.

## Cholesterylation
Cholesterylation is a relatively uncommon yet significant form of post-translational lipid modification, characterized by the enzymatic alteration of cholesterol molecules, which results in the production of various metabolites during the metabolic processes of living organisms [ ]. Stromal cell-derived factor 1 (SDF-1) is a crucial cytokine involved in angiogenesis. Small interfering RNA (siRNA) modified to target SDF-1 cholesterylation has the potential to inhibit the Akt signaling pathway, thereby suppressing angiogenesis. This approach may hold therapeutic promise for conditions associated with aberrant angiogenesis, such as diabetic retinopathy [ ]. Overall, the relationship between cholesterylation and diabetic endothelial dysfunction is a complex area of investigation that requires further elucidation of the impact of abnormal cholesterol metabolism on vascular complications in diabetes.

## Drugs or compounds targeting protein modifications to improve endothelial cell function in diabetes mellitus
In terms of pharmacological interventions aimed at enhancing endothelial cell function in diabetes mellitus, a variety of agents are currently available, including lipid-lowering medications, antihypertensive drugs, glucose-lowering agents, antioxidants, anti-inflammatory agents, antiplatelet drugs, anticoagulants, and nitrates. Recent research has identified that DPP-4 inhibitors, GLP-1 receptor agonists, and SGLT-2 inhibitors have the capacity to mitigate inflammation, oxidative stress, cellular apoptosis, and EndoMT. Although these drugs offer potential benefits for improving diabetic vascular diseases, their effects are often limited [ ]. Therefore, there is still a continued need to explore novel drugs to protect vascular endothelial cells and reduce diabetic vascular complications. Given the close association between multiple PTMs and endothelial function, targeting these modifications may represent a promising therapeutic strategy for addressing diabetic endothelial dysfunction.

## Targeting glycosylation
The excessive accumulation of the glucose metabolite MGO is detrimental to health. Glyoxalase 1 (Glo1), a vital detoxification enzyme, facilitates the conversion of toxic MGO into non-toxic D-lactic acid, thereby offering a novel approach for treating diabetic vascular endothelial dysfunction. For example, the combination of trans-resveratrol and hesperidin as Glo1 inducers has demonstrated efficacy in clinical trials by improving insulin resistance, regulating blood glucose levels, and reducing low-grade inflammation [ ]. Therefore, Glo1 inducers that target glycation may emerge as promising therapeutic agents for diabetic endothelial dysfunction.

## Targeting ubiquitination
Ginkgolide B, a natural compound derived from the leaves of Ginkgo biloba, exhibits beneficial effects on diabetes and its complications. By inhibiting the activity of E3 ubiquitin ligase, ginkgolide B decreases the ubiquitination of glutathione peroxidase 4, thereby enhancing antioxidant capacity, improving vascular endothelial function, and mitigating vascular injury [ ].

## Targeting phosphorylation
3-hydroxybutyrate (also known as beta-hydroxybutyrate), a ketone body, may phosphorylate PPARγ to enhance its activity, thereby improving insulin sensitivity [ ]. Although 3-hydroxybutyrate has shown promise in enhancing insulin resistance in animal models, its efficacy and safety in humans with type diabetes still require validation through clinical trials.

## Targeting acetylation
Sodium phenylbutyrate is recognized as a HDAC inhibitor that enhances peripheral insulin sensitivity while simultaneously decreasing plasma levels of branched-chain amino acids and glucose [ ]. Beta-hydroxybutyrate acts as an endogenous and selective inhibitor of HDAC. The inhibition of HDAC3 has been shown to facilitate the synthesis of Claudin-5, thereby alleviating the hyperpermeability of cardiac microvasculature in individuals with diabetes [ ]. This presents a novel therapeutic target for addressing diabetic cardiac microvascular complications.

## Targeting lactylation
Evodiamine, an alkaloid derived from the fruit of Cornus officinalis, exhibits a range of pharmacological properties, including anti-tumor, anti-inflammatory, anti-bacterial, and cardioprotective effects. Moreover, evodiamine has been shown to inhibit lactate-induced angiogenesis by restricting the lactylation of histones on HIF-1, thereby reducing the inflammatory response associated with diabetic cardiomyopathy and protecting cardiac and endothelial function [ ].

## Targeting palmitoylation
SMPDL3B is an enzyme associated with lipid rafts that demonstrates anti-inflammatory properties in various diseases. It can be palmitoylated and stabilized by ZDHHC5, a palmitoyltransferase. Under high glucose conditions, the palmitoylation of SMPDL3B is notably elevated to mitigate inflammation and oxidative stress in DR [ ]. This offers a potential therapeutic strategy for addressing diabetic endothelial dysfunction.

## Targeting SUMOylation
SENP1 acts as an endogenous activator of VEGFR2 and is involved in the translocation of VEGFR2 from the Golgi apparatus to the surface of endothelial cells. In the context of diabetes, hyperglycemia leads to the downregulation of SENP1, resulting in the accumulation of VEGFR2 within the Golgi and a subsequent reduction in its surface expression. This reduction negatively affects angiogenesis [ ]. It indicates that modulators of SENPs may help alleviate endothelial dysfunction.

## Targeting methylation
Curcumin, a polyphenolic compound extracted from the turmeric plant, possesses anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and anti-tumor properties. Recent studies have revealed that curcumin and its analogs can ameliorate diabetic retinopathy by enhancing resistance to oxidative stress and restoring the activity of DNMT-1 through mechanisms of DNA methylation [ ].

## Targeting carbonylation
Carbonylation is a protein modification typically linked to oxidative stress. Carglitazone not only lowers blood glucose levels and provides cardiovascular protection but also reduces the production of ROS and ROS-induced carbonylation in response to high glucose stimulation, thereby protecting endothelial cells [ ].

## Targeting other protein modifications
Additionally, carbamylation is implicated in the progression of cardiovascular disease. The administration of free amino acids through agents such as carglutamic acid can competitively inhibit protein carbamylation to protect against cardiovascular damage. Azeliragon (also referred to as TTP 488 or PF-04494700), as an orally bioavailable inhibitor of RAGE, exhibits potential effects in alleviating complications associated with diabetes [ ]. TTP399 functions as a selective hepatic glucokinase activator, enhancing glucokinase activity through the promotion of S-nitrosylation, which facilitates hepatic glucose uptake and utilization while simultaneously reducing blood glucose levels [ ]. Currently, the array of compounds targeting various protein modifications in the context of diabetic endothelial dysfunction remains limited. The majority of pharmacological agents or compounds aimed at addressing diabetic endothelial dysfunction are still in the preclinical research phase.
Overall, the range of pharmacological interventions aimed at addressing endothelial dysfunction associated with diabetes is steadily increasing, with the potential for the development of additional agents that target protein modification in the future.

## Limitations and knowledge gaps

## Limited methods for identifying and quantifying modifications
The identification and quantification of PTMs at specific sites present significant technical challenges. Although multiple PTMs have been identified, their specific biological functions and mechanisms of action in the context of diabetic endothelial dysfunction remain poorly understood, which may impose certain limitations.

## Insufficient research on the interactions among various modifications
It is important to note that various types of PTMs of proteins may collectively contribute to the onset and progression of diabetic endothelial dysfunction. Furthermore, interactions and cross-reactions among these PTMs may yield diverse biological outcomes, complicating the precise regulation of the mechanisms underlying diabetic endothelial dysfunction through PTMs. However, existing studies primarily focus on the roles of individual modification types, and there is currently a lack of in-depth research on the interactive regulatory network of different modification types in diabetic endothelial dysfunction. This gap in research makes it challenging to comprehensively explain the complex pathological processes that occur when multiple modifications act in concert.

## Absence of a dynamic modification map
During the progression of diabetes, protein modifications undergo dynamic change. Currently, there is a lack of systematic research on the comprehensive mapping of protein modifications across different disease stages, tissues, and cell types. Consequently, the temporal variation patterns of these modifications and their impact on the progression of endothelial dysfunction remain unclear.

## Lack of clinical evidence for targeting PTMs
Although previous research has elucidated the roles of various protein modifications in diabetic endothelial dysfunction, most mechanistic studies remain confined to the cellular or animal models, with relatively limited clinical evidence. The outcomes of preclinical experiments may not accurately reflect the actual conditions in humans, creating a translational barrier between basic research and clinical application. Moreover, Diabetic patients display considerable individual heterogeneity, characterized by variations in genetic background, lifestyle, and comorbidities. Current research often overlooks the influence of these individual factors on protein modification patterns, which complicates the development of personalized and precise diagnosis and treatment strategies for diabetic endothelial dysfunction.

## Conclusions and prospects
In summary, various forms of PTMs of proteins play a significant role in diabetic endothelial dysfunction. The primary factors contributing to endothelial dysfunction in diabetes mellitus include hyperglycemia, insulin resistance, oxidative stress, vascular inflammation, dyslipidemia, the formation of advanced glycation end products, mitochondrial dysfunction, and non-pharmacological RNA regulation. Currently, modifications such as glycosylation, ubiquitination, phosphorylation, acetylation, lactylation, palmitoylation, SUMOylation, methylation, and carbonylation are extensively implicated in diabetic endothelial dysfunction. Additionally, myristoylation, glutathionylation, citrullination, prenylation, ADP-ribosylation, S-nitrosylation, neddylation, succinylation, crotonylation, S-sulfhydration, and cholesterylation have also been increasingly reported to be associated with endothelial dysfunction in diabetes. Moreover, different types of PTMs can influence the functions of endothelial cells by targeting specific pathways, thereby regulating diabetic endothelial dysfunction (Tab. ). 
These modifications influence the pathological processes associated with diabetic vascular complications by affecting protein activity, localization, folding, and interactions. Additionally, targeting PTMs represents a significant avenue for developing therapeutic agents for diabetes. This approach has the potential to enhance endothelial function and mitigate the progression of diabetes-related complications through specific PTM interventions. However, since multiple types of PTMs collectively create a complex regulatory network in diabetic endothelial dysfunction, an integrated approach combining metabolomics and proteomics is essential for further elucidating the molecular mechanisms and therapeutic potential of these modifications in diabetes, ultimately aiming to improve endothelial dysfunction associated with the disease.
