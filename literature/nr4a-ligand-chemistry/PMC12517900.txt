## Abstract
Autoimmune hepatitis (AIH) is a chronic inflammatory disorder caused by an aberrant immune response directed against liver tissues. However, current treatments for AIH are insufficient in preventing disease progression, managing relapses, and minimizing adverse effects. Therefore, new treatments are urgently needed to improve the management of AIH. Protein quantitative trait loci data for 2940 plasma proteins were obtained from the UK Biobank Pharma Proteomics Project. Genetic association data for AIH were obtained from the FinnGen Consortium (including 264 AIH patients and 3,72,273 healthy controls) and from the study by Sakaue et al (including 821 AIH cases and 4,84,422 controls) and were considered as the discovery and validation sets, respectively. Summary-based Mendelian randomization and colocalization analyses were undertaken to pinpoint target proteins and to reveal the shared drivers between plasma proteins and AIH, respectively. In addition, we performed enrichment analysis, protein–protein interaction analysis, and drug availability analysis of AIH-related proteins to further explore the genetic mechanisms of AIH susceptibility and the potential of proteins as drug targets. Four plasma proteins, namely advanced glycosylation end product-specific receptor, butyrophilin subfamily 2 member A1, butyrophilin subfamily 3 member A2, and human leukocyte antigen E, were found to be significant in both the discovery set and validation sets, and their association with AIH was supported by both co-localization and sensitivity analyses. Phenome-wide association study analysis assessed the side effect profile of the 4 identified proteins; enrichment analysis and protein–protein interaction network showed that these proteins affected the occurrence of AIH by regulating immune response. Drug formation analysis suggested that advanced glycosylation end product-specific receptor and human leukocyte antigen E had the potential to be drug targets. This study highlights the important role of 4 plasma proteins in AIH risk. Further study of these proteins may reveal their potential in the prevention and treatment of AIH.
Keywords: autoimmune hepatitis, drug targets, linkage disequilibrium score regression, Mendelian randomization, plasma proteins

## 1. Introduction
Autoimmune hepatitis (AIH) is a chronic inflammatory disorder characterized by aberrant activation of the immune system, leading to liver inflammation and damage.[ ] The International Association for the Study of the Liver classifies AIH into 2 main types: type 1 AIH, which primarily affects both children and adults, is marked by the existence of anti-smooth muscle antibodies and antinuclear antibodies,[ , ] and often occurs alongside other autoimmune diseases; type 2 AIH is more common among children and adolescents and is identified by the presence of anti-liver and kidney microsomal antibodies.[ , ] In European and American populations, the incidence of AIH is roughly 1 to 2 cases per 1,00,000 individuals, and its prevalence is about 10 to 20 per 1,00,000.[ , ] While data on Asian populations are limited, some research indicates a slightly elevated incidence and poorer outcomes for Asians compared to their European and American counterparts.[ ] Currently, steroids and immunosuppressants are the primary treatments for AIH.[ , ] Although these therapies are effective in managing disease progression, they have several significant drawbacks: side effects: steroids, the primary treatment for inducing remission in AIH, can cause long-term complications such as diabetes, hypertension, and osteoporosis, significantly impacting patients’ quality of life[ , ]; variable effectiveness: immunosuppressants are used for ongoing treatment; however, research shows that up to 90% of patients may relapse once these medications are discontinued[ , ]; and adherence challenges: The complexity of treatment plans and associated side effects can hinder patient adherence, resulting in suboptimal therapeutic outcomes. Consequently, there is an urgent need to investigate novel treatment options for AIH to better manage disease progression, enhance prognosis, and minimize treatment-related complications. Current studies are exploring innovative therapeutic targets associated with the gut microbiota,[ ] mitochondrial metabolic pathways,[ ] and immune cell signaling pathways.[ ]
Mendelian randomization (MR) is a statistical method in genetics that simulates the design of randomized controlled trials at the genetic level, and it has emerged as a crucial tool in optimizing drug use and identifying novel therapeutic targets.[ , ] Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified specific single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) associated with the regulation of protein expression and protein quantitative trait loci (pQTL).[ ] These pQTLs are used as IVs in MR analysis to explore potential causal relationships between proteins and outcomes, which can subsequently be utilized for screening drug targets and biomarkers.[ , ] Compared with traditional observational studies, MR efficiently reduces the influence of confounding factors, thus improving the accuracy of causality evaluations.[ ]
Plasma proteins are of crucial significance in numerous biological processes, including signal transduction, substance transport, growth and development, as well as immune regulation. Plasma protein abnormalities are commonly observed in a wide range of diseases and have become key targets in drug development.[ ] In this study, we conducted a summary-based Mendelian randomization (SMR) analysis using pQTL data for plasma proteins as the exposure and GWAS data for AIH as the outcome, to uncover genetic associations between specific plasma proteins and AIH. We also conducted colocalization analyses to identify shared genetic drivers between potential therapeutic targets and AIH risk, and performed a phenome-wide association study (PheWAS) to examine the association of these targets with other traits. Enrichment analysis and the construction of protein–protein interaction (PPI) networks provided further insights into the biological functions of these proteins. Finally, we assessed the druggability of the identified target proteins.

## 2. Materials and Methods

## 2.1. Ethical clearance and informed consent
This research utilized pooled data from a GWAS and ensured that all participants had gone through the informed consent procedure. Since the analysis relied solely on secondary data methods to handle the summary statistics, it did not necessitate an independent ethical review.

## 2.2. Plasma pQTLs
Plasma protein data were obtained from the UK Biobank Pharma Proteomics Project (UKB-PPP), which reported pQTL data for 2940 plasma proteins measured in 54,219 UKB participants.[ ] The pQTLs are usually located near genes that regulate specific proteins. A pQTL located near its corresponding gene and potentially acts through that gene is known as a “cis-PQTL. Conversely, a pQTL that is distant from its corresponding gene, and may act through an intermediate gene is termed a “trans-PQTL.”[ , ] Various studies have used distance thresholds of 500 kb or 1000 kb to distinguish cis-PQTL from intrachromosomal trans-PQTL.[ , ] In this study, we included pQTL data that met the following criteria: SNPs (cis-pQTLs) within ±1 Mb of a gene region. To pinpoint SNPs with robust links to plasma proteins, we adopted a genome-wide significance threshold of P < 1.7 × 10−11. To maintain SNP independence and mitigate the effects of linkage disequilibrium (LD), we implemented stringent criteria: an LD parameter (r²) threshold of 0.001 and a genetic distance of 10,000 kb. The SNP with the smallest P-value in the cis-regions associated with each protein was chosen as the instrumental variable (IV) for subsequent SMR analysis.

## 2.3. GWAS data on AIH
The aggregated GWAS data concerning AIH were sourced from the FinnGen database, which included 264 cases alongside 3,72,273 controls (https://r12.finngen.fi/), as well as from the research conducted by Sakaue et al, comprising 821 cases and 4,84,413 controls (GWAS ID: ebi-a-GCST90018785).[ ] The FinnGen data were used as the discovery set, while the data from Sakaue et al were used as the validation set. Since disease assessment was already performed in the original studies, no additional confirmations were required.

## 2.4. SMR analysis
SMR is a statistical approach devised to determine if the impacts of genetic variation on phenotypes are mediated through gene expression. SMR uses genetic variation as an IV to test for associations between exposures (e.g., pQTLs) and outcomes (e.g., traits or diseases).[ ] In our SMR analyses, a threshold of PFDR < .05 was applied in the discovery set for screening target proteins, while PSMR < .05 was used in the validation set for confirmation. Proteins that showed significance and consistent directionally in both datasets were considered potential target proteins for further investigation.[ ] The significant associations identified through SMR analysis may result from either vertical pleiotropy, where exposure and outcome are correlated due to a shared genetic variant, or horizontal pleiotropy, where multiple genetic variants in LD independently influence both exposure and outcome. We conducted HEIDI tests to eliminate vertical and horizontal pleiotropy.[ ] Associations with a HEIDI P-value <.05 were considered to be driven by pleiotropy and were excluded from further analyses.

## 2.5. Co-localization analysis
The selected proteins and AIH were co-localized to identify shared drivers between target proteins and increased AIH risk and to minimize confounding factors. Co-localization analysis was conducted using the coloc R package, with default prior probabilities set as P1 = 1E−4, P2 = 1E−4, and P12 = 1E−5. Five hypotheses were tested: H0: the selected SNPs have no correlation with any trait; H1: the SNPs are linked to protein expression, yet have no connection to AIH risk; H2: the SNPs are associated with AIH risk, but not with protein expression; H3: the SNPs are associated with both protein expression and AIH risk, but this association is attributed to independent SNPs; and H4: the SNPs are linked to both protein expression and AIH risk, with this connection attributed to shared SNPs. Due to the limitations of co-localization analysis in this study, we restricted our analysis to proteins with a PPH3 + PPH4 value of ≥0.8.[ , ]

## 2.6. Sensitivity analysis
Utilizing AIH as the variable of exposure and notable proteins as the outcome, we conducted an inverse MR analysis to investigate the relationship’s directionality between plasma proteins and AIH. To evaluate the consistency and potential horizontal pleiotropy of the inverse MR results, we employed the Cochran Q test along with the MR-Egger intercept test, ensuring the robustness of our findings. To confirm that the observed plasma protein-AIH causality is not confounded by co-inheritance, linkage disequilibrium score regression (LDSC) was performed to assess the genetic correlation between the identified target proteins and AIH.[ ] LDSC assesses co-inheritance by analyzing the chi-square statistics of SNPs associated with 2 different traits. In this study, we used the “ldscr” R package for LDSC, drawing on reference European pedigree data from the 1000 Genomes Project Phase 3 to compute the LD scores.[ ] In the LDSC results, the genetic correlation between 2 traits is represented by rg, with values ranging from −1 to 1. Values closer to either extreme indicate a stronger correlation. The statistical significance of rg is represented by rg_P, with values below .05 considered statistically significant.

## 2.7. Phenome-wide association study
PheWAS, also called reverse GWAS, was performed to explore the relationships between specific SNPs or phenotypes and a wide range of other traits.[ ] This approach is especially effective for identifying possible side effects associated with drug targets.[ ] In this research, PheWAS analysis was conducted as previously described on the AstraZeneca PheWAS portal,[ ] utilizing exome sequencing data from the UKB-PPP, which includes information from approximately 4,50,000 participants (n = 3,94,695). This comprehensive database enables evaluation of the links between genetic variants and roughly 15,500 binary traits and 1500 continuous traits.[ ]

## 2.8. Enrichment analysis
In this study, org.hs.egg.db and the clusterProfiler R package were employed for gene ontology (GO) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) enrichment analyses[ , ] and the Enrichr platform (https://maayanlab.cloud/Enrichr/)[ , ] to study the functional and biological significance of the identified therapeutic target proteins. Due to the limited number of target proteins identified in this study, enrichment analysis may face challenges such as few pathways and potential for false positives. To address these limitations and better explore their biological characteristics, we used the STRING database to identify interacting proteins (confidence score ≥ 0.7) and included them in our subsequent enrichment analyses. This dual approach provided a more thorough evaluation of the functional and biological implications of our data.

## 2.9. PPI and druggability evaluation
PPI networks offer critical insights into how proteins interact. In this research, a PPI network was constructed using STRING with a minimum interaction score of 0.4[ ] and analyzed using GeneMANIA (https://genemania.org/).[ ] To evaluate the therapeutic potentials of the identified proteins, we examined their interactions with various drugs through the DGIdb and DrugBank databases,[ , ] which are useful for prioritizing potential drug targets by synthesizing information on drug-gene interactions, gene functions, and other pertinent data acquired through text mining. ## 3. Results

## 3.1. SMR analysis
After a stringent screening process, we selected 1855 cis-pQTLs from the UKB-PPP as IVs for our MR analysis. The details of these IVs are outlined in Table S1, Supplemental Digital Content, https://links.lww.com/MD/Q277. To boost the reliability of our findings, we combined the results from 2 sets of SMR analyses. During the discovery phase, 96 proteins were identified to be significantly related to AIH (all PSMR < .05). However, only 4 plasma proteins – advanced glycosylation end product-specific receptor (AGER), butyrophilin subfamily 2 member A1 (BTN2A1), human leukocyte antigen E (HLA-E), and butyrophilin subfamily 3 member A2 (BTN3A2) – remained statistically significant following FDR adjustment (Fig. and Table S2, Supplemental Digital Content, https://links.lww.com/MD/Q277). Notably, elevated plasma concentrations of BTN2A1 and HLA-E were correlated with an elevated risk of AIH (BTN2A1: odds ratio [OR] = 6.129, 95% confidence interval [CI]: [3.263, 11.511], PFDR = 1.43E−5; HLA-E: OR = 3.219, 95% CI: [1.905, 5.437], PFDR = .007). Conversely, increased levels of AGER and BTN3A2 were correlated with reduced risk of AIH (AGER: OR = 0.015, 95% CI: [0.005, 0.046], PFDR = 4.5E−10; BTN3A2: OR = 0.571, 95% CI: [0.440, 0.741], PFDR = .011). In the validation phase, all 4 plasma proteins met the validation criteria (Fig. and Table S3, Supplemental Digital Content, https://links.lww.com/MD/Q277) and passed the HEIDI test, suggesting that the SMR results were less susceptible to horizontal pleiotropy (Fig. and Table S4, Supplemental Digital Content, https://links.lww.com/MD/Q277).
 

## 3.2. Co-localization analysis
To investigate the shared drivers of these 4 plasma proteins and AIH, we conducted co-localization analyses within a 1 Mb window (±500 kb) around the corresponding genes. The findings indicated that AGER, BTN2A1, BTN3A2, and HLA-E may harbor shared pathogenic variants associated with AIH, as evidenced by a combined posterior probability (PPH3 + PPH4) exceeding 0.8. A comprehensive breakdown of these results is shown in Figures , S1 to S4, Supplemental Digital Content, https://links.lww.com/MD/Q279, and Table S4, Supplemental Digital Content, https://links.lww.com/MD/Q277. To confirm the reliability and orientation of our findings, sensitivity analyses were performed on the 4 plasma proteins.

## 3.3. Estimation of genetic correlations and reverse causality detection
We evaluated the genetic links between 4 plasma proteins and AIH via LDSC. The results revealed no significant genetic overlap between the target plasma proteins and AIH (AGER to AIH: rg = 0.134, rg_P = .491; BTN2A1 to AIH: rg = −0.168, rg_P = 0.417; BTN3A2 to AIH: rg = −0.012, rg_P = .966; HLA-E to AIH: rg = −0.20, rg_P = .941), indicating no shared genetic confounding in the causal link between plasma proteins and AIH. Reverse causality testing using AIH as the exposure (IV screening criteria: P < 5E−06, clump_kb = 5000, clump_r2 = 0.01) and the 4 plasma proteins as the outcomes revealed no reverse causal association between AIH and any of the 4 plasma proteins. Sensitivity analyses of the results using the Cochran Q test and MR-Egger intercept test indicated absence of horizontal pleiotropy or heterogeneity (Tables ; S5, Supplemental Digital Content, https://links.lww.com/MD/Q277).
 

## 3.4. PheWAS analysis
We carried out a PheWAS analysis via the AstraZeneca PheWAS Portal with the aim of evaluating the potential pros and cons of the 4 plasma proteins on 17,361 binary traits and 1419 continuous traits, and also to pinpoint any hitherto undetected pleiotropic effects. As indicated in Table and Figures S5 to S8, Supplemental Digital Content, https://links.lww.com/MD/Q279 only AGER was significantly associated with other phenotypes (P < 5E−8), suggesting that drugs targeting the other 3 proteins are unlikely to cause considerable side effects or undetected pleiotropic effects, thereby strengthening the reliability of our results. Conversely, AGER was negatively correlated with the FEV1/FVC ratio Z-score across 5 collapsing models, implying that AGER-targeting drugs may affect this trait in AIH. This finding also suggests that the link between AGER and AIH may involve some pleiotropic effects.
 

## 3.5. Enrichment analysis
Using the STRING platform, we identified 104 proteins that interact with the 4 target proteins with a confidence threshold of 0.7 and included them into subsequent functional enrichment analyses (Tables S6 and S7, Supplemental Digital Content, https://links.lww.com/MD/Q277). As shown in Figure , BPs associated with these proteins were primarily related to immune responses, including leukocyte-mediated immunity, immune effector regulation, and cell killing. Additionally, these proteins were linked to CCs such as the MHC protein complex, MHC class I protein complex, endoplasmic reticulum membrane, and plasma membrane, as well as MFs such as antigen binding, peptide binding, MHC complex binding, and receptor activity. Similarly, the top 5 pathways identified in the KEGG enrichment analysis (Fig. ) were antigen processing and presentation, phagosomal pathway, Epstein-Barr virus infection, NK cell-mediated cytotoxicity, and graft-versus-host disease. This highlights the potential role of the target proteins in immune responses, inflammation, and autoimmune diseases. Further GO and KEGG enrichment analyses using the enrichr platform yielded similar results, corroborating the immune-related functions of these proteins (Fig. S9, Supplemental Digital Content, https://links.lww.com/MD/Q279).
 

## 3.6. PPI network and druggability evaluation
PPI analysis using the STRING platform revealed limited interactions among the target proteins. Specifically, only BTN2A1, BTN3A2, and HLA-E interacted with each other, with no interaction between AGER and the other targets (Fig. S10, Supplemental Digital Content, https://links.lww.com/MD/Q279). A more extensive PPI analysis using the GeneMANIA platform identified 20 additional interacting proteins, yielding a total of 683 interaction links (Fig. ). These interactions were predominantly physical interactions (77.64%), followed by co-expression (8.01%), prediction (5.37%), and co-localization (3.63%). Functional analysis of this network confirmed the strong association of these targets with immune-related processes.
 Next, we searched the DGIdb and DrugBank databases to identify potential therapeutic interventions. Among the 4 target proteins, AGER and HLA-E have already been explored as drug targets (Table S8, Supplemental Digital Content, https://links.lww.com/MD/Q277). For example, azeliragon, a drug targeting AGER, is currently being studied for Alzheimer’s disease, while pyridoxamine, another AGER-targeting drug, is being investigated for its therapeutic potentials in kidney stones. Additionally, several drugs targeting HLA-E, such as certolizumab pegol, etanercept, infliximab, methotrexate, and adalimumab, are used to treat autoimmune diseases, whereas decitabine is used in hematologic cancers. However, no effective drug interventions were identified for BTN2A1 and BTN3A2.

## 4. Discussion
This SMR study identified 4 plasma proteins that may be linked to AIH onset, providing novel candidate biomarkers for AIH management. Co-localization and PheWAS analyses confirmed that all 4 proteins, namely AGER, BTN2A1, BTN3A2, and HLA-E, were causally linked to AIH with minimal associated adverse effects. Additionally, sensitivity and LDSC analyses confirmed the directionality and reliability of these findings. Enrichment analyses indicated that these proteins may play a key role in immune-related pathways, and this conclusion was further corroborated by the PPI network. Druggability evaluation revealed that AGER and HLA-E, both of which are already being explored as drug targets for autoimmune diseases and cancer, may be repurposed for AIH treatment.
AGER, also known as RAGE, is a member of the immunoglobulin superfamily and serves as a multiligand receptor that plays a crucial role in physical development, inflammation, and diseases, including diabetes and neurological disorders.[ ] Animal studies found that the absence of RAGE improved the outcomes of AIH in mice,[ ] suggesting that RAGE is a key driver of AIH onset and progression. In contrast, it has been shown that patients with AIH have reduced serum levels of soluble RAGE (sRAGE),[ ] hinting that sRAGE may be protective against liver inflammation and injury. Similarly, other studies have confirmed that sRAGE plays a protective role in hepatocyte damage[ , ] and in other autoimmune disorders, such as systemic lupus erythematosus,[ ] Hashimoto’s thyroiditis,[ ] and Guillain-Barré syndrome.[ ] Collectively, these findings suggest that AGER may be a promising protective agent in AIH. However, these data also highlight the complex role of AGER in the onset and advancement of AIH, necessitating further study. In our druggability analysis, we identified several drugs that target AGER, including azeliragon, pyridoxamine, and oxytocin. However, our PheWAS analysis indicated that drugs targeting AGER may lead to adverse outcomes in AIH, such as compromised respiratory function.
Butyrophilins (BTN) are also part of the immunoglobulin superfamily, playing a crucial role in immune system regulation, including activating T cells and natural killer (NK) cells.[ ] Among its members, BTN3A2 and BTN2A1 have been shown to modulate immune responses and control cell growth. BTN3A2 is an immune checkpoint modulator and contributes to self-tolerance.[ ] Research has shown that BTN3A2 expression in neonates is causally associated with various autoimmune and allergic conditions, and plays a protective role in primary sclerosing cholangitis and systemic lupus erythematosus.[ ] Further, cancer studies have revealed a strong link between BTN3A2 expression and number of infiltrating CD4+ T cells in tumors.[ ] Conversely, BTN3A2 has also been shown to inhibit immune responses, promoting gastric tumor growth and immune evasion.[ ] This suppressive role was further corroborated in another study, which found that antibodies targeting BTN3A2 induced BTN3 phosphorylation, thereby inhibiting T cell proliferation and cytokine production to dampen excessive immune responses.[ ] Since the disruption of immune tolerance is a key mechanism that drives AIH development,[ , ]
BTN3A2 may maintain immune homeostasis by inhibiting T cell proliferation. This reduces the damage caused by excessive immune responses to the liver and lowers the risk of AIH. This provides a reasonable explanation for why BTN3A2 is a protective factor against AIH.
Recent studies have demonstrated that BTN2A1 interacts with the T-cell receptor (TCR) of Vγ9Vδ2T cells, leading to their activation and heightened immune response.[ – ]
Vγ9Vδ2T cells are the most abundant subset of γδT cells found in human peripheral blood, comprising 50% to 95% of the total γδT cell population.[ ] While the relationship between BTN2A1 and AIH remains elusive, our study posits that BTN2A1 may drive AIH progression through 2 mechanisms: BTN2A1 activated γδT cells have the capacity to secrete a diverse array of cytokines and chemokines, among which are proinflammatory Th1-like cytokines like IFN-γ and TNF-α.[ ] The activation of numerous immune mediators, such as dendritic cells and Th1 cells, plays a pivotal role in the immune response.[ ] AIH is frequently marked by an active Th1 immune response.[ ] Over-activation of γδT cells by BTN2A1 might result in a Th1/Th2 immune imbalance and trigger an excessive immune assault on the liver.[ , ] Regulatory T cell dysfunction: Tregs are often reduced or dysfunctional in patients with AIH, impairing the liver’s ability to dampen excessive immune responses.[ , ]
BTN2A1 activated γδT cells can stimulate the proliferation and differentiation of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, enhance the cytotoxicity of CD8+ T cells, and inhibit the immunosuppressive effects of Tregs on effector T cells.[ ] Overall, these findings could assist in explaining the positive link between BTN2A1 and AIH risk in our study. In the PheWAS, neither BTN2A1 nor BTN3A2 showed significant associations with other phenotypes, suggesting that these proteins could be promising therapeutic targets for AIH.
HLA-E is a nonclassical major histocompatibility complex antigen that plays a crucial part in regulating NK cell activity through the presentation of signal sequence peptides derived from HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C, and HLA-G molecules.[ , ] As one of the least polymorphic members of the HLA class I family, HLA-E primarily consists of 2 functional alleles: HLA-E01:01 and HLA-E01:03. Research indicates that the HLA-E01:03 allele contributes to the onset and progression of autoimmune disorders. For instance, this allele has been linked to increased risks of Behcet’s disease (BD),[ ] pemphigus vulgaris,[ ] multiple sclerosis,[ ] and rheumatoid arthritis.[ ] Additionally, HLA-E01:01/01:03 heterozygosity is linked to more severe, early-onset type 1 diabetes mellitus cases.[ ] Studies have shown that HLA-E expression can inhibit the clearance of pathogenic T cells by NK cells, thereby affecting the regulation of autoimmune responses.[ ] Specifically, high expression of HLA-E*01:03 can increase inhibitory signals, decrease NK cell activity, and hinder the elimination of pathogenic T cells, which can lead to the development of autoimmune diseases. Conversely, low expression of HLA-E*01:01 may enhance T cell clearance and have a protective effect.[ , ] These findings are consistent with the results of this study, which indicate a positive correlation between HLA-E expression and the risk of AIH onset. Subsequent druggability analysis revealed several HLA-E targeted therapies, including certolizumab pegol, etanercept, infliximab, methotrexate, and adalimumab, that have been approved for treating autoimmune diseases. Notably, PheWAS analysis did not uncover any significant side effects associated with HLA-E, suggesting it may be an optimal therapeutic target for AIH treatment.
GO enrichment analysis revealed that 4 target proteins (AGER, BTN2A1, BTN3A2, and HLA-E) and their interacting proteins were significantly enriched in immune-related biological processes (Fig. ). The most significant enrichment at the BP level was observed in leukocyte-mediated immunity, regulation of immune effector processes, and regulation of cell killing. These results are highly consistent with the immunopathological mechanisms of AIH. At the cellular component level, the target proteins were significantly enriched in the major histocompatibility complex protein complex and the lumenal side of the endoplasmic reticulum membrane, which supports the functional localization of HLA-E as an antigen-presenting molecule.[ , ] MF analysis revealed the core role of target proteins in antigen recognition and inflammatory signal transduction through the enrichment of antigen binding and RAGE receptor binding. KEGG analysis revealed that the most significantly enriched pathway for the target proteins was antigen processing and presentation, with notable enrichment in pathways such as graft-versus-host disease and EBV infection (Fig. ). This suggests that virus-triggered cross-immune reactions may be involved in AIH pathogenesis,[ ] providing molecular evidence for the interaction between environmental factors and genetic susceptibility. The PPI network constructed using GeneMANIA (Fig. ) shows that the target protein interacts closely with multiple key immune-related molecules. HLA-E forms physical interaction modules with classical HLA-I molecules and antigen processing-related proteins (e.g., TAP2), accounting for 77.6% of interaction types. Meanwhile, BTN family members (e.g., BTN2A1, BTN3A2, and BTN3A1) form functional clusters through coexpression and colocalization. This network topology confirms that the target protein does not act in isolation, but rather collaborates within the immune regulatory network to drive AIH pathogenesis.
By combining pQTL data with GWAS data on AIH, this extensive proteome-wide MR study uncovered significant causal associations between 4 plasma proteins (AGER, BTN2A1, BTN3A2, HLA-E) and the risk of AIH. Our co-localization analysis further strengthened these associations. The study’s strengths lie in its MR design, which minimizes confounding factors and reduces the risk of reverse causality. We prioritized cis-pQTLs over trans-pQTLs and eQTLs and conducted gene co-localization analysis to increase the statistical power and reliability of our findings. In sensitivity analyses, LDSC was used to evaluate the genetic correlation between the identified plasma proteins and AIH, addressing any potential confounding arising from shared genetic inheritance, thereby reinforcing the findings. Subsequently, 2 types of enrichment analyses were conducted to evaluate the function and biological significance of the target protein, resulting in more reliable enrichment analysis results.
Several limitations should be considered in this study. First, the research focused exclusively on European populations, potentially restricting the generalizability of the findings to wider demographics. Second, horizontal pleiotropy poses a key challenge in MR design. While we utilized only cis-pQTLs as IVs to minimize the risk of horizontal pleiotropy, the possibility of residual pleiotropy cannot be completely ruled out. Third, due to limitations in the power of the co-localization analysis, we did not apply the more rigorous criterion of a posterior probability (PPH4) of 0.8 or higher. Fourth, the GWASs primarily focused on identifying genetic risk factors that contribute to AIH onset rather than its progression or outcomes. Therefore, the biomarkers we discovered may help prevent AIH onset, but their impact on disease outcomes and progression requires further validation in cohort studies. Fifth, Analyses stratified by age/sex/medication use in this study were excluded due to data availability. This is a common limitation of aggregated data MR; future MR studies should be prioritized in GWAS data with detailed stratification. Last, due to practical limitations, we were unable to validate our findings through cell and animal experiments. Therefore, we will incorporate these experiments into our future studies or collaborate with other laboratories to advance this area of research.

## 5. Conclusion
In conclusion, our SMR analysis revealed 4 plasma proteins that are causally linked to AIH. These plasma proteins are enriched in several immune-related pathways, highlighting their potential as therapeutic targets. Druggability assessments further suggest that these targets may be suitable for medicinal application. This study provides valuable insights into potential strategies for AIH treatment and underscores the importance of these plasma proteins in its management. However, additional fundamental and practical studies are needed to fully assess the feasibility and effectiveness of these potential targets.
