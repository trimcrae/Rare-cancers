## Abstract

## Background
Neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NAC) response in terms of tumor necrosis for osteosarcoma is of clinical significance for subsequent treatment, CT-acquired parameters indicating sarcopenia were reported to be the risk factors of low NAC efficacy in a variety of malignant tumors, yet the association between chest CT-derived pectoralis major muscle measurements and tumor necrosis of osteosarcoma have not been revealed.

## Methods
The pectoralis major muscle area, normalized pectoralis major muscle index (PMI) and CT attenuation acquired from preoperative chest CT as well as clinical data of 131 osteosarcoma patients were retrospectively analyzed and compared between favorable NAC response (tumor necrosis ≥ 90%) and poor NAC response group (tumor necrosis < 90%). For the evaluation of interobserver reproducibility, interclass correlation coefficient (ICC) with 95% confidence interval (CI) was used. The receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analysis was performed to determine the efficiency of each measurement and to calculate the optimal cut-off values of significant parameters. Multivariable binary logistic regression analysis was performed to determine the risk factors of low NAC efficacy.

## Results
A total of 79 lesions from 78 patients were eligible for this study (mean age, 17.6 ± 0.9 years; 45 male), 34 lesions constituted the favorable response (FR) group and 45 lesions constituted the poor response (PR) group. Pectoralis major muscle measurements were significantly higher in FR group than in PR group (muscle area, 11.56 ± 4.48 VS 9.42 ± 3.57, P = 0.021; PMI, 7.32 ± 2.23 VS 5.64 ± 1.54, P < 0.001; CT attenuation, 38.78 ± 8.44 VS 31.96 ± 11.37, P = 0.004). Excellent interobserver agreement was found in these parameters with ICC values ranging 0.925–0.944. In ROC analysis, PMI showed an area under curve (AUC) of 0.727, 95% CI: 0.612, 0.842, the threshold value for differentiating FR from PR was 7.147. PMI were risk factors of low NAC efficacy with the P value of 0.007.

## Conclusions
Chest CT-accessed pectoralis major muscle area, PMI and CT attenuation were significantly correlated with tumor necrosis in osteosarcoma, these can be potentially helpful imaging auxiliary biomarkers for predicting NAC response of osteosarcoma.

## Background
Osteosarcoma is a rare kind of malignant tumor of mesenchymal origin with an annual incidence of 3–4 patients per million. As the most common malignant bone neoplasm, osteosarcoma is leading to high mortality [ , ]. In the past few decades, with the development of adjuvant chemotherapy and neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NAC), the 5-year survival rate of osteosarcoma patients reached 65% ~ 75%, which also made it possible for the implementation of limb salvage surgery [ ]. NAC efficacy in terms of tumor necrosis based on surgical specimen is an important prognostic factor [ , ]. The most widely used tumor necrosis evaluation is Huvos grading, but this could only be accomplished post-operatively [ ]. Current pre-operative approaches for chemotherapy response evaluation includes signs and symptoms, laboratory examinations such as serum alkaline phosphatase (ALP) and lactic dehydrogenase (LDH), as well as imaging examinations. However, mismatches could show up among the above methods in clinical practice. Additional pre-operative approaches that help predicting NAC response could be potentially beneficial for hierarchical management of osteosarcoma patients. Sarcopenia refers to a kind of syndrome characterized by progressive, generalized decline of skeletal muscle mass and strength [ , ]. Body composition measurements that represent skeletal muscle mass and quality condition are of highly clinical importance for patients with malignant tumors. Sarcopenia at diagnosis affects 35.3% of solid tumor patients and its prevalence varies among different kinds of tumors based on CT-accessed data [ ]. Low skeletal muscle mass could result in physical function failure, lowered quality of life and further lead to poor prognosis of many acute and chronic diseases, especially, it is known to be associated with adverse events like treatment related toxicity, local foci recurrence, or distant metastasis in multiple cancer species [ – ]. Chest CT is a routine examination for both staging and surveillance of osteosarcoma since lungs are the most common site of metastasis. Area and CT attenuation measured from cross-sectional CT image are simple and feasible approaches that reflect muscle size and density and could help to determine whether the situation of muscle loss exists. Studies have shown that skeletal muscle measurements acquired from staging CT were associated with overall survival (OS) in sarcoma patients [ , ]. However, the association between chest-CT acquired pectoralis major muscle measurements and chemotherapy response in osteosarcoma has not been reported yet.
This study aimed to explore the association between low pectoralis major muscle area/CT attenuation and low tumor necrosis in osteosarcoma patients, seek for the imaging biomarkers that could be possibly helpful for predicting low NAC efficacy in osteosarcoma.

## Materials and methods

## Study population
This retrospective study was approved by the institutional review board (IRB) of our hospital and the requirement to obtain informed consent was waived (clinical trial: bc2021011). The data of 130 eligible patients with 131 biopsy confirmed osteosarcoma treated at our hospital between June 2011 and June 2023 were retrospectively collected. Inclusion criteria were as follows: (1) osteosarcoma was pathologically confirmed by biopsy; (2) patients accepted at least 2-cycle MAP regimen (methotrexate, cisplatin and doxorubicin) neoadjuvant chemotherapy followed by radical surgery; (3) preoperative chest CT data with diagnosable quality was accessible; (4) patients did not accept medication that affect skeletal muscle condition. Exclusion criteria were as follows: (1) patients did not receive radical surgery or no NAC was performed before radical surgery; (2) NAC was not accomplished due to severe abnormal bone marrow function indicated by laboratory exams before each cycle of chemotherapy; (3) definite histopathological results with tumor necrosis information of radically surgical specimen were incomplete; (4) Artifacts that would affect the measurements were found on CT image (Fig.  ).
 

## Clinical data collection
The clinical data of the patients were extracted from institutional inpatient registry, including sex, age, height, weight and Enneking staging at diagnosis. Body surface area (BSA) was calculated as: BSA = 0.0061 X Height (cm) + 0.0128 X Weight (kg) – 0.1529 [ ]. Tumoral characteristics including lesions’ localization and histological subtype were also obtained. The largest dimension, presence of pathological fracture or transphyseal lesion were evaluated from imaging examinations at diagnosis and recorded. Laboratory examinations including serum ALP, LDH, inorganic phosphorus and albumin were recorded.

## NAC efficacy evaluation
NAC efficacy was determined according to histopathologic Huvos grading on surgical specimen [ ]. The favorable response (FR) group (including Huvos III and IV grade) was defined as tumor necrosis ≥ 90%, and the poor response (PR) group was defined as tumor necrosis < 90% (including Huvos I and II grade). Subgroup analysis were based on tumor necrosis degree.

## Chest CT scanning protocol and pectoralis major muscle measurements
Availability of a chest CT of diagnostic quality prior to surgery was required. Imaging data was acquired with following parameters: tube voltage = 120 kVp, slice thickness = 1.25 mm, tube current varied automatically according to the patients’ habitus so as to limit the radiation dose. Patients were examined in supine position with the arms placed above the head. The scanning range was from thoracic entrance to costophrenic angle.
Images were evaluated on reconstructed cross sectional image with slice thickness = 5 mm and space interval = 5 mm at soft tissue window (window width = 320 Hu, window level = 45 Hu). The regions of interest (ROIs) were manually placed on both sides of pectoralis major muscle at the slice with best visualization of the muscle, care was taken to avoid obvious fat tissue (Fig.  ). The average area and CT attenuation of bilateral ROIs were used for data analysis. Those ROIs were accomplished independently by two radiologists (reader 1 and reader 2 with 8 and 5 years’ experience in musculoskeletal imaging) that were blinded to the subjects’ clinical and histopathological information. The measurements made by the two radiologists were used for evaluating interobserver reproducibility and were averaged for data analysis. ## Statistical analysis
All statistical analysis were performed with SPSS v26.0 (IBM, Chicago, IL, USA). Chi-square test was used for categorical variables. For continuous variables, distribution for data normality was evaluated with Shapiro-Wilk test. Data conforming to normal distribution were presented as mean ± standard deviation and the comparation between groups were performed with independent sample t-test. Data not conforming to normal distribution were presented as median (Q1 ~ Q3) and the comparation between groups were performed with Mann Whitney U test. The results with P < 0.05 was considered significant. For the evaluation of interobserver reproducibility, interclass correlation coefficient (ICC) with 95% confidence interval (CI) was used. An ICC of 0.7–0.9 was considered indicative of good agreement, an ICC > 0.9 was considered indicative of excellent agreement. The receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analysis was performed to determine the efficiency of each measurement and to calculate the optimal cut-off values of significant parameters, the area under curve (AUC), sensitivity, specificity and Youden index were calculated. Multivariable binary logistic regression analysis was performed to determine possible risk factors of low NAC efficacy.

## Results

## Patient, tumor and clinical characteristics
According to predefined in- and exclusion criteria, a total of 78 subjects with 79 lesions were included for this retrospective study, among which 34 lesions belonged to FR group and 45 lesions belonged to PR group. The cohort consisted of slightly more male (n = 45, 57.0%) than female patients with a median age of 15 (range 6–52) years at diagnosis of osteosarcoma. At initial diagnosis, 4 (5.1%), 65 (82.3%) and 10 (12.7%) lesions were classified as stage IIa, Iib and III according to the Musculoskeletal Tumor Society (MSTS) system [ ]. Six (7.6%) lesions presented with pathological fractures, 40 (50.6%) were transphyseal lesions. Conventional (classic) osteosarcoma was the most common histologic entities (93.7%), 5 lesions (6.3%) belonged to other subtypes (low-grade central, telangiectatic, parosteal, periosteal, high-grade surface, small cell subtypes, etc.). Femur and tibia were the most common sites (51.9% and 24.1%, respectively). No significant differences were found in age, sex, height, weight, KPS score, Enneking staging, lesions’ site, histopathological subtype, maximal dimension, the prevalence of pathological fractures, the prevalence of transphyseal lesions, numbers of NAC cyclyes, laboratory examinations between FR and PR group. ## Discussion
Though first defined as progressive muscle mass decline due to aging [ , ], sarcopenia has been widely found in cancer patients and considered as a predictor of post-treatment complications as well as poor prognosis for both short- and long-term outcomes in patients with a variety of malignant tumors [ – ]. The diagnosis of sarcopenia has not been standardized yet, skeletal muscle index (SMI) which is normally based on psoas major muscle area at the level of the third lumbar vertebra indexed for height could serve as indicators of sarcopenia. But pelvic CT that includes psoas major muscle is not always acquirable in staging radiological examinations of osteosarcoma patients. Rossi et al. reported good correlation between psoas muscle area on CT and pectoralis muscle area on MRI in breast cancer patients [ ]. Jo et al. also reported significant correlation between psoas and pectoralis muscle area on CT [ ]. This study focused on chest CT-derived pectoralis major muscle area and CT attenuation as they are more easily acquirable parameters than psoas muscle in osteosarcoma patients. Further, based on the epidemiological characteristics of osteosarcoma, the study cohort were mainly adolescents, the pectoralis major muscle area was normalized for BSA rather than simply height to avoid the impact of genders as well as ages. In this study, we retrospectively analyzed muscle area, PMI and CT attenuation between favorable and poor NAC response subjects, low CT measurements were found significantly associated with low NAC efficacy. Our results showed a higher prevalence of low PMI and low CT attenuation than previous studies, this was because that the cut-offs were calculated according to NAC efficacy rather than other signs and symptoms. Standardized reference values need to be explored in future studies.
Our findings consist with previous studies that analyzed the impact of sarcopenia to NAC response of different types of cancer. Prior studies reported that sarcopenia was associated with worse response to therapy in esophageal cancer [ ]. Bedrikovetski et al. found that sarcopenia was negatively associated with total neoadjuvant chemotherapy response in advanced rectal cancer patients [ ]. Ke et al. has found that high SMI could predict biochemical response to abiraterone acetate treatment in metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer patients with prior chemohormonal therapy at hormone-sensitive stage [ ]. In the study of Lin et al., low SMI was reported to be independent risk factor for non-remission for locally advanced gastric cancer [ ]. Possible explanations could be relating to the impact of body composition to chemotherapeutic drugs pharmacokinetics [ , ]. Skeletal muscle could work as an endocrine organ, producing and secreting different kinds of myokines that mediates communication between muscle and other organs, and affects lipid and glucose metabolism, bone formation, endothelial cell function, hypertrophy, and tumor growth [ ]. The interaction between skeletal muscle condition and other systems might further exert the effect on tumor treatment. Surov et al. reported that low skeletal muscle mass is a predictor of treatment related toxicity [ ], future studies focusing on sufficient data that reflect chemotherapy related toxicity could help explain the possible mechanisms of associations between NAC response and pectoralis major muscle measurements from the perspective of treatment related toxicity. According to another recent meta-analysis carried out by Surov et al., low skeletal muscle mass is a risk factor for poor treatment response in curative chemotherapy in the adjuvant and/or neoadjuvant setting [ ]. Exact mechanisms of the associations between sarcopenia and NAC efficacy still need to be investigated in further studies. But interestingly, baseline body composition parameters such as SMI was shown to have limited power in predicting NAC response in breast or bladder cancer [ – ]. Different results between cancer species might be due to the variety of study population, disease severity and progression.
Besides, our study showed that CT attenuation was significantly lower in PR group, which consist with the study carried by Lin et al. on gastric cancer cohort [ ]. In ovarian cancer, skeletal muscle radiodensity may be a better prognostic biomarker than SMI [ ]. Phan et al. reported that the increased skeletal muscle CT attenuation at the level of T12 vertebra was associated with higher OS and event-free survival in soft tissue sarcoma (STS) patients [ ]. Jo et al. also showed that higher baseline pectoralis muscle index and CT attenuation were associated with increased OS in STS patients [ ]. Besides, CT-derived skeletal muscle attenuation at the level of L4 pedicle was reported to be associated with OS in STS patients [ ].
There are some main limitations in this study. As a retrospective single-center study, our cohort included patients with heterogeneous age group, pathological subtypes, clinical staging and NAC durations, which could all influence the stability of the results. Besides, the very limited sample size could not support hierarchical researches nor further validations on the current study cohort. This study was more an attempt to explore the correlation between muscle size and density-related indicators and NAC efficacy, further studies with hierarchical researches based on age, staging, NAC duration, etc. are needed for optimizing those measurements acquired from the current study. In future, prospective, multi-institutional studies that focus on larger sample size with both internal and external validations need to be carried out to testify the feasibility of the measurements acquired form this study.
In conclusion, chest CT-accessed pectoralis major muscle parameters can be a significant indicator for NAC efficacy in osteosarcoma. Such indicator could potentially become imaging biomarkers for helping making clinical decisions on the comprehensive therapy in osteosarcoma.
