## Introduction
Musculoskeletal (MSK) disorders, a leading cause of physical disability and disease burden worldwide, are chronic and progressive conditions that gradually impair physical activity and body function, thereby exerting detrimental effects on both physical and mental health , . Numerous studies have reported substantial overlap in the symptoms, pathology and biological mechanisms among multiple MSK disorders – . However, the shared underlying etiology across MSK disorders remains incompletely understood.
Previous studies have revealed the existence of shared mechanisms among certain MSK disorders. For instance, rheumatoid arthritis (RA) and osteoarthritis (OA) share similar cartilage damage, which is associated with matrix-degrading enzymes and inflammatory mediators . Gout may contribute to the development of OA, potentially through mechanical damage and inflammatory responses resulting from monosodium urate accumulation . Impaired insulin signaling in skeletal muscle could accelerate sarcopenia and osteopenia, which in turn leads to functional decline and reduced lifespan, highlighting the importance of muscle-bone interactions in aging . Recently, genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified thousands of genomic risk loci for MSK disorders and suggested genetic pleiotropy as the underlying common etiology. For instance, a recent study revealed shared genetic associations between juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA) and adult RA, implicating shared risk loci involving immune pathways . Degenerative skeletal disorders, including OA and degenerative disc diseases, have been shown to share common genetic susceptibility , so as the shared genetic associations between RA and OA, with the role of immune cells highlighted . Muscles could provide structural support, contribute to joint stability, and modulate mechanical loading on bones and cartilage. The pleiotropic effects observed in muscle and bone were considered to provide valuable insights into their shared biological mechanisms, thereby informing improved strategies for diagnosis, prevention, and early targeted treatment . In addition, neuromuscular and peripheral nerve functions constitute integral components of MSK health – . Autoimmune neuromuscular disorders, such as myasthenia gravis (MG), may exhibit a potential causal relationship with RA and share immune-related genetic factors . Peripheral nerve entrapment disorders, such as carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS), can induce muscle atrophy and subsequent structural changes in surrounding bones and joints . Genetic component of CTS may act through muscle and bone tissues to influence tissue structure and integrity, thereby predisposing the median nerve to compression . Furthermore, the DIRC3 locus was identified to be shared between trigger finger (TF) and CTS . All these studies indicate the extensive genetic pleiotropy across MSK pathologies, highlighting the necessity of an integrated approach to elucidate their shared genetic architecture.
Deciphering pleiotropic genetic associations could uncover the shared genetic mechanisms across MSK disorders and inform integrated therapeutic and co-management strategies. However, previous pleiotropic association analyses were restricted to pairwise traits across a small subset of disorders, leaving the full extent of shared genetic liability unclear. Recently, multivariate genetic modeling methods have enabled systematic exploration of the shared genetic liability underlying multiple traits. In particular, genomic structural equation modeling (genomic SEM) enables investigation of joint genetic architecture and shared genetic liability of multiple traits in the form of latent factors. This approach has been successfully applied to psychiatric disorders , immune diseases and metabolic syndrome . More importantly, multivariate genetic approaches could increase the power to identify novel genomic risk loci by leveraging shared information across multiple traits .
In this study, we applied genomic SEM to characterize the shared genetic structure of MSK disorders through latent genetic factors. We then performed GWAS of these latent factors by incorporating individual genetic variants into the genomic SEM framework to identify factor-associated genomic risk loci, followed by extensive functional annotation, enrichment and gene prioritization analyses to elucidate the underlying biology. Finally, we integrated the factor GWAS with both spatial transcriptomics and single-cell RNA sequencing data to identify key cell types involved in MSK disorders.

## Results

## Three distinct genetic factors were identified in MSK phenotypes
An overview of the study design was displayed in Fig.  . Through a multi-stage, agnostic phenotype selection procedure (Supplementary Fig.  , Supplementary Data  , see “Methods” section), a total of 37 MSK phenotypes were included, with details provided in Table  . Univariate linkage disequilibrium score regression (LDSC) analysis showed nominally significant heritability for all 37 phenotypes (P < 0.05, Table  ). Bivariate LDSC analysis further revealed widespread global genetic correlations among them (Fig.  and Supplementary Data  ), with 346 (51.95%) out of 666 phenotype pairs passed the Bonferroni-corrected P-value threshold of 7.51 × 10−5 (0.05/666). The mean absolute value of genetic correlations among these significant phenotype pairs was 0.52, suggesting substantial shared genetic architecture. To explore the regional patterns of shared genetic architecture among phenotype pairs, we performed local genetic correlation analyses using Local Analysis of [co]Variant Association (LAVA) and observed a higher proportion of concordant effect directions between global genetic correlation and the corresponding local genetic correlations across genomic regions for phenotype pairs with both significant global and local genetic correlations (Supplementary Data  and Supplementary Fig.  ). 
Hierarchical clustering based on the global genetic correlation matrix explicitly identified three well-separated phenotype clusters encompassing 33 among 37 phenotypes (Fig.  ). Notably, ankylosing spondylitis (AS), diffuse idiopathic skeletal hyperostosis (DISH), palmar fascial fibromatosis (PmFF, also known as Dupuytren’s disease), and hand grip strength (HGS), showed relatively low correlations with the three phenotype clusters, indicating their distinct genetic architecture. We then adopted genomic SEM for multivariate genetic modeling to capture the shared genetic liability underlying 33 phenotypes in the three clusters. For phenotype pairs with extremely high genetic correlations (>0.9), we retained those with larger effective sample size as modeling both phenotypes, in such case, could introduce multicollinearity and model instability, whereas using the phenotype with larger sample size could provide more precise estimates of genetic covariance . In addition, for each phenotype pair, we alternatively replaced the phenotype with the larger sample size with the other to perform a sensitive analysis. Finally, 26 of the 33 phenotypes were included in the main analysis, and the remaining seven phenotypes for sensitivity analyses (Fig.  ). The three-factor model based on the genetic covariance structure of the 26 phenotypes showed good performance (Fig.  and Supplementary Data  ), with model fit indices of Akaike information criterion (AIC) = 1330, comparative fix index (CFI) = 0.9151 and standardized root mean square residual (SRMR) = 0.0732. These three factors collectively accounted for 48.27% of the genetic variance of 26 MSK phenotypes and represented three distinct dimensions of genetic risk: a degenerative musculoskeletal disorder factor (MSKdg) with high loadings on degenerative MSK disorders and related symptoms; a bone mass factor (MSKbm) comprising bone fractures (BF), osteoporosis (OP) and bone mineral density (BMD); and an autoimmune disorder factor (MSKai) primarily associated with autoimmune diseases including RA, JIA, and MG. The cross-loading of BF onto MSKdg was attributed to the widespread genetic correlations between BF and 14 out of 20 phenotypes (P < 0.05) loaded on the MSKdg. The interfactor correlations can only be significantly detected between MSKdg and MSKai (rg = 0.27, P = 1.95 × 10−16, Fig.  ), suggesting the distinct genetic mechanisms underlying these three factors. The model fit indices showed no substantial changes in sensitivity analyses (Supplementary Data  ), indicating that the three factors were robust to capture the shared genetic liability underlying MSK phenotypes.

## Multivariate GWAS identified 795 risk loci associated with MSK factors
By incorporating each single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) into the genomic SEM model, we performed multivariate GWAS analyses of these three factors, with the effective sample sizes estimated to be 1,131,013 for MSKdg, 815,643 for MSKbm, and 87,689 for MSKai, respectively. The genomic control (λGC) of GWAS was estimated at 2.07 for MSKdg, 1.61 for MSKbm and 1.11 for MSKai. The univariate LDSC intercepts for all three factors were close to 1, indicating that the observed inflation of test statistics is attributable to polygenicity rather than population stratification (Supplementary Fig.  ). All three factors exhibited significant SNP-heritability, with estimates of 0.0670 (se = 0.002) for MSKdg, 0.1132 (se = 0.0082) for MSKbm, and 0.1254 (se = 0.0107) for MSKai, supporting the biological plausibility and suitability for downstream genetic analyses. Heritability enrichment analysis in 28 annotated regions revealed that both MSKdg and MSKbm were most enriched in the conserved region (fold enrichment = 13.55, P = 3.95 × 10−23 for MSKdg, and fold enrichment = 14.31, P = 4.23 × 10−10 for MSKbm), whereas MSKai was most enriched in the transcription start site (fold enrichment = 18.98, P = 3.07 × 10−8), suggesting divergent distributions of heritability across functional regions among the three factors (Supplementary Fig.  ).
Using a clumping procedure, we identified a total of 795 genomic risk loci for three factors, including 306 loci for MSKdg, 440 loci for MSKbm, and 49 loci for MSKai (Fig.  and Supplementary Data  ). The most significant associations for MSKdg, MSKbm, and MSKai were detected within intronic regions of FOXP2 (rs12536335, P = 7.87 × 10−31), CCDC170 (rs4870044, P = 3.90 × 10−252), and AP4B1-AS1 (rs2358816, P = 7.44 × 10−85), respectively. FOXP2 has been previously reported to regulate osteogenesis and chondrocyte hypertrophy during long‑bone development . CCDC170 is essential for Golgi-associated microtubule organization and positively regulates bone formation . AP4B1-AS1 is located within the 1p13.2 locus, a region strongly associated with type 1 diabetes, a T cell-mediated autoimmune disorder characterized by the destruction of pancreatic beta cells . We observed a relatively low overlap of risk loci between factors, with only two overlapped loci across all three factors, suggesting their distinct genetic architectures (Supplementary Fig.  ). 
Among the 795 factor-associated loci, 154 (19.37%) did not overlap with any loci identified in GWAS of individual MSK phenotypes, highlighting the enhanced power of genomic SEM and its ability to reveal shared genetic mechanisms (Supplementary Data  ). Of these 154 loci, 136 were identified as novel, including 131 for MSKdg, 2 for MSKbm and 3 for MSKai, as they had not been previously reported in association with any MSK phenotypes in the GWAS Catalog (Supplementary Data  and Supplementary Fig.  ). Most of these novel loci were associated with MSKdg, likely reflecting the larger number of phenotypes contributing to this factor. For MSKdg, the top SNPs at four novel loci were located within exonic regions of genes EMC1 (rs12084825, P = 1.54 × 10−9), PYCRL (rs2242090, P = 5.80 × 10−9), SLC25A37 (rs10992, P = 7.21 × 10−9), and SMCO4 (rs2658797, P = 3.15 × 10−11), respectively. In addition, the top SNP near gene GPR27 (rs2654439, P = 3.97 × 10−15) exhibited the highest level of regulatory evidence, with RegulomeDB score of 1. For MSKbm, the top SNPs at two novel loci were within intronic regions of the genes MBD5 (rs13402768, P = 1.67 × 10−8) and SRGAP1 (rs116965801, P = 1.98 × 10−8). For MSKai, the top SNPs at three novel loci were near genes IL12A-AS1 (rs4680536, P = 4.25 × 10−9), IRF4 (rs7773690, P = 3.86 × 10−9), and CD226 (rs1790974, P = 4.82 × 10−9).

## Characterization of independent variants in MSK factor-associated risk loci
Conditional and joint association analysis (COJO) , identified 1073 independent variants across 795 loci (Supplementary Data  ), including 323 for MSKdg, 691 for MSKbm, and 59 for MSKai. For all three factors, we observed an inverse relationship between the effect sizes of the independent associated variants and their minor allele frequencies (Supplementary Fig.  ). MSKai-associated genetic variants exhibited larger effect sizes, consistent with its higher heritability and lower polygenicity. A total of 125 (15.7%) out of 795 loci included two or more independent variants. Notably, at the CPED1-WNT16 locus on 7q31.31, a region well-documented to be related with BF and BMD , we identified the largest number of 22 independent variants associated with MSKbm (Supplementary Fig.  ). Among the 1073 independent variants, 30 had a RegulomeDB score of 1 (Supplementary Data  ) and 27 were missense or stop-gained variants (Supplementary Data  ), highlighting their potential regulatory and functional significance. Three stop-gained variants were located in genes RAPSN (rs7111873 for MSKdg, P = 3.90 × 10−9), IDUA (rs114806891 for MSKbm, P = 1.26 × 10−56), and FAM111A (rs116918730 for MSKbm, P = 3.60 × 10−11). RAPSN encodes the rapsyn protein, which is essential for the formation and maintenance of the neuromuscular function and is associated with congenital myasthenic syndromes , . IDUA encodes the lysosomal enzyme α-L-iduronidase, and its deficiency could lead to skeletal deformities due to glycosaminoglycans accumulation . FAM111A has been reported to be associated with skeletal development and growth .

## Multi-trait colocalization identified shared causal variants at MSK factor-associated risk loci
Multi-trait colocalization analysis using HyPrColoc identified in total 208 colocalized phenotype sets (posterior probability [PP] > 0.7) across 204 out of 795 (25.66%) factor-associated risk loci (Supplementary Data  ), including 83 sets in 79 MSKdg loci, 104 sets in 104 MSKbm loci, and 21 sets in 21 MSKai loci. These results suggest that MSK factor-associated loci may be driven by shared causal variants across multiple phenotypes loading on each other. Notably, four MSKdg-associated loci in regions 1p21.1, 1p13.2, 8q12.3, and 12q24.31 contained two distinct colocalized phenotypes sets, indicating that these signals may be driven by more than one shared causal variant across multiple phenotypes.
For MSKdg, OA, one of the most common degenerative MSK disorders, appeared in 63 of 83 colocalized phenotype sets, suggesting extensive sharing of causal variants with other MSKdg-loaded phenotypes. This was followed by back pain (BP) in 36 sets and joint pain (JP) in 33 sets, consistent with pain being a common symptom of degenerative MKS disorders. The most significant colocalized phenotype set was observed between OA and CTS (PP = 0.9804), with the shared causal variant rs7225843 located in an intronic region of the SMG6 gene. In addition, we identified seven colocalized phenotypes (the largest number) at risk loci in regions 7q31.1 (PP = 0.9253) and 10p12.31 (PP = 0.7089), with shared variants near genes FOXP2 (rs2894699) and MLLT10 (rs12251016).
Among the l04 colocalized loci for MSKbm, 27 showed significant colocalization evidence for all three loaded phenotypes, 37 between BF and BMD, 39 between OP and BMD, and 1 locus between BF and OP. The two strongest colocalization signals were identified between OP and BMD, with shared causal variants near genes WLS (rs2566752, PP = 0.9999) and TNFRSF11A (rs2957137, PP = 0.9996), respectively. This was followed by three colocalized loci between BF and BMD, with shared causal variants near genes RP11-556E13.1 (rs1159798, PP = 0.9994), SLC8A1 (rs10490046, PP = 0.9992), and AHNAK (rs117111740, PP = 0.9992). Both WLS and TNFRSF11A play critical roles in bone development, homeostasis, and remodeling , , while SLC8A1 and AHNAK are key regulators of calcium signaling, participating in the regulation of osteocyte and muscle function , . The most significant colocalization signal across all three phenotypes was near the RSPO3 gene (rs10457487, PP = 0.9991). RSPO3 could promote osteoblast differentiation, bone formation, and the maintenance of bone mineral density by enhancing Wnt signaling .
Among the 21 colocalized loci for MSKai, 6 showed significant colocalization across RA, JIA, and MG, with shared causal variants located near the genes STAT4 (rs11889341, PP = 0.9892), ATXN2 (rs4766578, PP = 0.9864), IL2RA (rs706778, PP = 0.9830), IKZF3 (rs4795395, PP = 0.9788), AP4B1-AS1 (rs2358816, PP = 0.9482), and IL12RB2 (rs17129794, PP = 0.7150). In addition, 10 loci were shared between RA and JIA, and 5 between RA and MG.

## Gene prioritization and druggability assessment for MSK factors
We employed multiple methods to prioritize candidate genes for MSK factors. First, gene-based analysis implemented in Multi-marker Analysis of GenoMic Annotation (MAGMA) identified 648 genes for MSKdg, 1,197 genes for MSKbm, and 103 genes for MSKai (Supplementary Data  ). Next, summary-data-based Mendelian randomization (SMR) identified 116 factor-gene pairs in whole blood and 124 factor-gene pairs in skeletal muscle through integrating gene expression quantitative trait locus (eQTL) data (Supplementary Data  ). Integration with splicing QTL (sQTL) data revealed 88 isoforms of 59 unique genes in whole blood and 85 isoforms of 41 unique genes in skeletal muscle (Supplementary Data  ). Integration with protein QTL (pQTL) data further identified 14, 22, and 4 proteins associated with MSKdg, MSKbm, and MSKai, respectively (Supplementary Data  ). Finally, integration of single-cell eQTL (sc-eQTL) data with colocalization analysis revealed 540 significantly colocalized factor-gene pairs across 15 cell subsets (Supplementary Data  ), involving 65 unique genes for MSKdg, 64 for MSKbm, and 35 for MSKai.
Notably, the candidate genes obtained from different analytical methods, tissues, and regulatory levels showed very low overlap (Supplementary Fig.  ), indicating that each analysis could capture distinct biological mechanisms, which further motivated the integration of multiple evidence for robust gene prioritization. We combined results from all seven analyses and assigned each gene a score from one to seven, corresponding to the number of significant associations. We considered genes with a score of two or higher as candidates, yielding a total of 273 factor-gene pairs (Supplementary Data  ), involving 91 genes for MSKdg, 154 genes for MSKbm, and 28 genes for MSKai, with little overlap among the three factors (Supplementary Fig.  ). Among these genes, 65 exhibited a score larger than three (Fig.  ). In particular, gene PILRB showed the strongest association for MSKbm with a score of six, followed by 16 genes with a score of four, including eight genes (ACYP2, C18orf8, C6orf106, C7orf73, ITIH4, MAPK3, NPC1, and TMEM110) for MSKdg, seven genes (EIF2B2, FAM213A, FHL3, FLCN, IGHMBP2, LTBP3, and ZBTB38) for MSKbm, and one gene (TRAF1) for MSKai. 
We further identified 36 gene-drug interactions with high gene-drug interaction scores (larger than 5), involving 20 unique genes (Supplementary Data  ). These drugs directly or indirectly affect muscle, bone, and cartilage biology, providing valuable insights into drug development and repurposing for MSK disorders. Several agents, including α-Conotoxins, nerve growth factor, KAI-1678 are involved in neuromuscular signaling and pain transmission . Metabolic and small-molecule compounds such as acetylglucosamine, uric acid, vanadate, fructose, and lactose may regulate bone and cartilage metabolism , . In addition, immune- and inflammation-modulating agents such as recombinant lymphotoxin beta, recombinant chemokines, mepolizumab, oleclumab, and dalutrafusp alfa may influence MSK homeostasis through inflammatory pathways .

## Distinct biological pathways underlying MSK factors
Gene set analysis using MAGMA revealed distinct biological mechanisms among different MSK factors (Supplementary Data  ). MSKdg showed the highest enrichment in the biological processes of positive regulation of chondrocyte differentiation (P = 9.75 × 10−7). MSKbm exhibits high enrichment in biological processes relevant to skeletal development, such as skeletal system development (P = 2.11 × 10−19), skeletal system morphogenesis (P = 3.73 × 10−13), positive regulation of cartilage development (P = 1.96 × 10−10), embryo development (P = 3.22 × 10−10), and ossification (P = 6.33 × 10−10), as well as the Wnt signaling pathways (P = 1.24 × 10−7) and SMAD protein complex (P = 5.17 × 10−8). MSKai was significantly enriched in immune-related processes, such as response to interleukin 2 (P = 1.46 × 10−13), lymphocyte activation (P = 2.25 × 10−10), and leukocyte differentiation (P = 3.97 × 10−10), as well as the cytokine JAK–STAT signaling pathway (P = 9.22 × 10−13) and the IL12/23 to JAK–STAT signaling pathway (P = 2.73 × 10−7).

## Tissue and cell types relevant to MSK factors
We identified relevant tissues for each MSK factor by integrating spatial transcriptomics (ST) data of mouse embryos using genetically informed spatial mapping of cells for complex traits (gsMAP) (Fig.  ). After Bonferroni correction (P < 0.05/25), we observed extensive enrichment for MSKdg, with cartilage primordium showing the highest significance (P = 2.68 × 10−13), followed by brain (P = 1.12 × 10−12) and cartilage (P = 8.98 × 10−11). For MSKbm, cartilage (P = 3.22 × 10−8) and cartilage primordium (P = 2.93 × 10−7) emerged as the most significantly enriched tissues. For MSKai, only the gastrointestinal tract (P = 1.75 × 10−3) and liver (P = 1.84 × 10−3) showed significant enrichment, both of which could play important roles in the development of the embryonic immune system . 
By integrating multiple human single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data using single-cell disease relevance score (scDRS) , we identified key cell types and revealed distinct cellular signatures for the three MSK factors (Supplementary Data  ). MSKdg showed extensive enrichment. Specifically, in embryonic skeleton, MSKdg showed the strongest enrichment in neuronal cells (P = 1.88 × 10−20), followed by two early cranial mesenchymal progenitors (Fig.  ). In skeletal muscle (Supplementary Data  ), MSKdg illustrated the strongest enrichment in tendon cells (P = 1.55 × 10−14), followed by muscle fibers (P = 1.90 × 10−11), non-myelinating Schwann cells (P = 3.17 × 10−7), adipocyte (P = 6.60 × 10−5), and perineurial cells (P = 7.41 × 10−5). In quadriceps tendons (Supplementary Data  ), MSKdg exhibited significant enrichment in neural cells (P = 1.86×10−9) and stromal cells (P = 1.96 × 10−3). These results indicate that the genetic liability for MSKdg influences a spectrum of nerve-, muscle-, tendon-, and joint-cell types involved in maintaining joint stability, load transmission, neuromuscular signaling. Notably, the strong enrichment in brain tissue and neuronal cells implies a potential brain-joint axis . For MSKbm, in embryonic skeleton (Fig.  ), the most significant enrichment was observed in osteoblasts (P = 3.59 × 10−38), followed by mature osteocytes (P = 1.42 × 10−32), osteoprogenitors (HHIP+PreOB, P = 1.70 × 10−24), osteocyte (P = 1.59 × 10−21), hypertrophic chondrocytes (P = 5.25 × 10−9), and all of the cell types play a central role in regulating bone development and skeletal homeostasis. MSKai, as expected, showed significant enrichment in immune cells (P = 4.93 × 10−35) of embryonic skeleton (Fig.  ). We further leveraged a multi-tissue immune atlas to identify the immune cell types associated with MSKai and found strong enrichment in T cells (Supplementary Data  ), such as CD4 + CD25+ α-β regulatory T cell (P = 1.66 × 10−15), effector memory CD4+ α-β T cell (P = 2.63 × 10−9), CD4+ α-β memory T cell (P = 1.62 × 10−7). MSKai also showed significant enrichment in multiple immune tissues, particularly in gut-associated tissues (Fig.  ), including the mesenteric lymph node (P = 1.99 × 10−8), colonic epithelium (P = 4.36 × 10−7), and lamina propria of mucosa of colon (P = 1.58 × 10−6). These results, consistent with gsMAP findings, highlight the gut immune microenvironment as a central hub of shared genetic susceptibility for autoimmune MSK disorders and support the concept of a gut-joint axis .

## Discussion
Through multivariate genetic modeling, we identified three distinct genetic factors with significant heritability and polygenicity to represent the general shared genetic liability of MSK disorders. Notably, these three factors collectively explained 48.27% of the genetic variance across 26 MSK phenotypes, exhibited low genetic correlations, displayed distinct heritability distributions, showed minimal overlap in risk loci, and presented unique cellular signatures. Our findings highlighted the importance of genetics-guided stratification to optimize the co-prevention and co-management of MSK disorders. For example, a comprehensive intervention targeting joint stability, neuromuscular signaling, and cartilage homeostasis may be effective for individuals with predominant genetic risk of MSKdg. Strategies focusing on bone formation and remodeling pathways may benefit those with high genetic risk of MSKbm. Immunomodulatory strategies, particularly those modulating gut-associated immune responses, could be most relevant for individuals with elevated genetic risk of MSKdg.
The three genetically distinct MSK factors identified in this study reflect divergent underlying biological mechanisms. In particular, consistent with the complex and heterogeneous clinical and pathological manifestations of degenerative MSK disorders – , MSKdg captures a broad spectrum of MSK phenotypes and represents a coherent degenerative MSK disorder dimension, encompassing cartilage and bone pathology, local inflammation, abnormal joint mechanics, and neuromuscular dysfunction. MSKdg underscores the shared genetic architecture underlying conditions that seems to be quite different, suggesting a convergent biological process despite the existence of clinical heterogeneity , . Notably, MSKdg exhibits high loadings on pain-related phenotypes, underscoring that pain is not merely a secondary symptom, but a core and genetically influenced dimension of MSK disorders. Indeed, pain in degenerative MSK disorders has been increasingly recognized as a complex process involving mechanical wear, inflammatory signaling, and central nervous system sensitization – . Consistent with the well-documented relationship between bone density and fracture risk , MSKbm directly reflects a bone-centric architecture, encompassing BF, OP, and BMD. MSKai captures autoimmune-mediated MSK disorders, including RA, JIA, and MG. Both RA and JIA, all of which are chronic, immune-mediated inflammatory joint disorders, with shared clinical and pathological features . The low genetic correlations among the three MSK factors contrast with epidemiological studies reporting substantial clinical comorbidity , , suggesting that shared environmental exposures may drive phenotypic overlaps in the absence of shared genetic liability and highlighting the importance of considering both genetic and non-genetic factors in MSK disorder comorbidity , . Notably, some MSK phenotypes, such as DISH, PmFF, and HGS, were excluded from the multivariate genetic model due to their low genetic correlation with other MSK phenotypes. These MSK phenotypes appear to represent distinct genetic dimensions that are not well captured by the shared genetic architecture underlying the majority of MSK disorders. DISH is a non-inflammatory condition characterized by exuberant ligamentous calcification and ossification, and its pathophysiological mechanisms remain largely unclear , . Previous evidence have linked DISH to obesity, insulin resistance, and metabolic syndrome and suggested its shared biochemical pathways with metabolic disorders – , which may distinguish it from the degenerative, immune-mediated, or bone mass-regulating pathways commonly shared across other MSK disorders. PmFF is a highly localized fibroproliferative disorder of the palmar fascia, driven by abnormal extracellular matrix deposition, myofibroblast activation, and tissue-specific fibrosis , . Unlike most MSK disorders included in the model, it does not primarily involve joints, cartilage, or bone remodeling, which likely accounts for its limited genetic overlap with other MSK phenotypes. HGS is a quantitative functional trait that is widely used to reflect frailty, cardiometabolic health, and all-cause mortality risk – . Accordingly, the genetic basis of HGS may involve pathways related to muscle biology, central nervous system regulation, and overall physiological reserve , . Therefore, its genetic architecture is more likely to reflect systemic functional capacity and overall health status rather than susceptibility to specific MSK diseases. The distinct genetic architecture of these phenotypes highlights the coexistence of diverse, partially independent biological mechanisms in the MSK system.
GWAS of the three MSK factors using the genomic SEM model identified 795 genomic risk loci, including 136 novel loci. Many of these novel loci exhibited pronounced biological relevance. For example, we identified a novel MSKdg-associated variant, rs12084825, in an exon of gene EMC1. EMC1 encodes a subunit of the endoplasmic reticulum membrane protein complex, exhibits broad pleiotropic functions, and has been linked to neurological, muscular, and skeletal abnormalities, suggesting a potential role in MSK disorder , . Another example is MSKbm-associated variant rs13402768, an intronic variant in gene MBD5. Haploinsufficiency of MBD5 has been associated with toes absence of halluces, microcephaly and skeletal malformations , . For MSKai, a notable locus is rs7773690 near gene IRF4, a member of the interferon regulatory factor family of transcription factors and is involved in immune cell differentiation and mutation in IRF4 associated with human immunodeficiency , .
Investigating how factor-associated variants regulate gene or protein expression at the tissue or cellular level could gain mechanistic insights into the molecular pathways and benefit the discovery of drug targets or drug repurposing. We prioritized a total of 273 candidate genes for three MSK factors. For example, the MSKdg-associated gene MAPK3 is involved in osteogenic differentiation pathways and may mediate obesity-induced arthritis , . The gene ITIH4 for MSKdg plays a beneficial role in inflammation, wound healing, collagen synthesis, and repair of injured Achilles tendon . The gene FHL3 associating with MSKbm, is an essential regulator for acting cytoskeletal dynamics in skeletal myoblasts . The gene FLCN for MSKbm prevents excessive bone loss by controlling the metabolism and activity of bone-resorbing cells . For MSKai, the gene TRAF1 exhibited the highest level of evidence, which is implicated in chronic autoimmune diseases characterized by persistent inflammation and has been reported to be associated with RA , . We also identified several drugs interacting with factor-associated genes, in which some have been used in the treatment of MSK disorders, and some possess great repurposing opportunities. For example, the MSKbm-associated gene ATF1 interacts with α-conotoxins, which act as modulators of neuronal nicotinic acetylcholine receptors. Synthetic α-conotoxins modulate bone remodeling by enhancing osteoclast activity and inhibiting osteoblast function, highlighting both their therapeutic potential and risk for bone loss . The MSKai-associated gene ORMDL3 interacts with fluticasone, a kind of corticosteroid commonly used for rhinitis and asthma . A recent clinical trial also confirmed the benefits of fluticasone propionate intra-articular injection in knee osteoarthritis . In addition, nerve growth factor, which interacts with the gene PNMT, represents a promising target for alleviating chronic pain in MSK disorders , .
Cell type enrichment analyses highlight different tissue and cell types for three MSK factors. MSKdg was significantly enriched in neuronal, tendon, muscle, and stromal cell types, consistent with the evidence that MSK degeneration reflects impaired coordination among mechanical loading, neuromuscular control, and tissue repair processes rather than isolated structural failures , . The high enrichment of MSKdg in brain tissue and neuronal cells implies a potential brain-joint communication axis, in which central nervous system can modulate bone metabolism, peripheral tissue remodeling and inflammatory cascades , , and peripheral nerves also play a critical role in MSK homeostasis by regulating muscle tone, proprioception, tissue repair, and pain signaling , . Disruption of these neural components can exacerbate mechanical stress, impair tendon-muscle coordination, and promote chronic inflammation around joints , . Therefore, targeting neural cells may offer important opportunities for integrated management and intervention strategies across degenerative MSK disorders. MSKbm showed a highly bone-centric cellular architecture, indicating that genetic risk for bone mass-related phenotypes operates primarily through skeletal lineage cells that regulate bone formation, mineralization, and homeostasis. In addition, MSKai was enriched in immune tissues at multiple sites, underscoring the role of immune-mediated systemic regulation in autoimmune MSK disorders. Genetic liability in MSKai appears to act through mucosal immune homeostasis, modulating the systemic inflammatory milieu that secondarily affecting joints and bones. The strong enrichment of MSKai in gut-associated immune tissues highlights the concept of gut-joint axis, whereby intestinal barrier integrity, microbial sensing, and local immune regulation modulate bone remodeling and joint health .
Our study has several limitations. First, analyses were restricted to European ancestry due to the unavailability of well-powered GWAS summary statistics for non-European ancestries. Further studies integrating GWAS from diverse populations are warranted to validate and extend these findings. Second, we restricted our genetic analyses to autosomes, as high-quality and harmonized variants for sex chromosomes were not consistently available across all included MSK phenotypes. However, sex chromosomes play important roles in skeletal development, sexual dimorphism, and hormonal regulation, and may also contribute to MSK disorder risk , . Future studies incorporating sex-chromosome variants are also highly desirable. Third, our functional analyses for the three factors, including QTL mapping, spatial transcriptomics, and single-cell transcriptomics, relied on publicly available datasets rather than MSK disorder-specific data, which could introduce important limitations. Most existing sc-eQTL datasets were derived from blood or immune cells , whose enhancers differ substantially from those of cartilage, bone, or tendon, and may be less relevant to degenerative MSK conditions and bone mass, potentially leading to suboptimal gene prioritization. Although there was more than 80% correlation in expression across genes between mice and humans , , species differences cannot be ignored. The use of high-resolution spatial transcriptomic data from mouse embryonic tissue may limit our ability to capture cellular states or regulatory mechanisms relevant to human MSK disorders. Similarly, single-cell transcriptomics datasets also originated from tissues that do not exactly correspond to the MSK disorder-related biological contexts. Consequently, caution should be made to the biological interpretations of our findings and further validation should certainly be warranted using high-quality multi-omics data from MSK disorder-related tissues. Fourth, our results have not yet been replicated because no independent studies currently provide GWAS data covering all 37 phenotypes. Nevertheless, we leveraged the largest available data for each included phenotype to maximize statistical power, applied rigorous quality control procedures to ensure the reliability of the GWAS summary statistics, performed various sensitivity analyses to assess the robustness of our findings, and integrated multiple layers of functional evidence to support biological plausibility. As additional datasets become available, further validation will certainly be warranted. In addition, cross-ancestry analyses could serve as a potential validation approach in future studies.
Through multivariate modeling of large-scale genetic data across 33 MSK phenotypes, we identified three genetically distinct factors that capture the independent dimensions of MSK risk, each reflecting unique biological mechanisms and cellular signatures. Our study not only enhances the power to detect novel risk loci but also links genetic variants to relevant molecular pathways and potential therapeutic targets. Our findings underscore the benefits of multi-phenotypes and multi-omics analyses in unraveling the complex etiology of MSK disorders, offering an easily extended framework for risk stratification and genetic-informed intervention strategies in the co-management of MSK disorders.

## Methods

## Phenotype selection and GWAS summary statistics
We implemented a multi-stage phenotype selection procedure in an agnostic manner to provide a broad coverage of both pathological and physiological dimensions of the MSK system, involving bones, cartilage, joints, tendons, ligaments, fascias, and muscles, while taking data availability and statistical power into account. The detailed phenotype list and the selection procedure are illustrated in Supplementary Fig.  and Supplementary Data  . Specifically, the selection procedure was as follows:
First, we identified 190 MSK disorders with both clear Phecodes (https://phewascatalog.org) and ICD-10 codes (https://icd.who.int/browse10/2019/en). We then excluded 48 non-primary MSK conditions based on the following criteria: (1) recurrent or relapsing conditions; (2) other or unspecified diagnoses; (3) acquired conditions; (4) traumatic conditions; and (5) derivative symptoms (e.g., symptoms related to joints). Given that overall phenotypes exhibit substantial pathological and genetic overlap with their corresponding sub-phenotypes, and typically have much larger sample sizes, we further excluded 56 anatomical or pathological sub-phenotypes to mitigate overrepresenting any single disease. Statistically, simultaneously including multiple highly correlated sub-phenotypes in the model could assign disproportionate weight to their shared genetic liabilities in comparison with including distinct diseases. For example, we included only osteomyelitis (ICD-10 code: M86, Phecode: MS_701.1) while removed acute/subacute osteomyelitis (ICD-10 code: M86.0-M86.2, Phecode: MS_701.11) or chronic osteomyelitis (ICD-10 code: M86.3-M86.6, Phecode: MS_701.12). Then, 86 primary MSK disorders were retained. Additionally, we included 18 diseases from the ICD-10 category “Diseases of the myoneural junction and muscle, G70-G73”, as they could capture critical aspects of muscle and neuromuscular pathology relevant to MSK health . Based on the abovementioned exclusion criteria, we ultimately retained five diseases, including myasthenia gravis, muscular dystrophy, myotonic disorders, myopathies, and Lambert-Eaton syndrome. Carpal tunnel syndrome was further included as a peripheral nerve entrapment disorder with prominent MSK manifestations . We also included two common quantitative traits, bone mineral density and hand grip strength, that directly reflect MSK structure or function. In total, 94 MSK phenotypes were retained.
Second, we systematically searched publicly available European-ancestry GWAS summary statistics from different repositories for the 94 phenotypes selected in the first stage, including GWAS Catalog (https://www.ebi.ac.uk/gwas/), OpenGWAS (https://opengwas.io/), FinnGen Release 12 (https://www.finngen.fi/en/access_results), Pan-UKBB (https://pan.ukbb.broadinstitute.org/), UK Biobank Neale lab (https://www.nealelab.is/uk-biobank), Musculoskeletal Knowledge Portal (https://msk.hugeamp.org/), and published GWAS studies – . A total of 23 phenotypes were excluded due to the lack of publicly available data (Supplementary Data  ). For the remaining 71 phenotypes, we further applied a stringent exclusion criterion on GWAS data to ensure statistical power, where we sequentially removed 21 phenotypes due to insufficient case numbers (less than 1000) , 11 phenotypes due to extreme case-control imbalance (a case-to-control ratio less than 1:100) , and 2 phenotypes with non-significant SNP-based heritability (P > 0.05). Finally, 37 MSK phenotypes with well-powered and high-quality GWAS summary statistics were retained for subsequent multivariate analyses (Table  ).
GWAS summary statistics of the selected 37 MSK phenotypes were subjected to further quality control. Specifically, we excluded SNPs without rsIDs or with duplicate rsIDs, matched all SNPs to the reference genome of the European ancestry from 1000 Genomes Project phase 3, retained biallelic SNPs with minor allele frequency (MAF) > 0.01, and aligned all SNPs to the genome build GRCh37.

## Heritability and genetic correlation
Heritability and global genetic correlations for MSK phenotypes were estimated using LDSC , . We used pre-computed LD scores from the 1000 Genomes Project phase 3 European ancestry reference panel and restricted the analysis to approximately 1.2 million well-imputed SNPs in the HapMap3 reference. To account for potential population stratification and sample overlap, we placed no restrictions on the intercept term in the LDSC analysis. Heritability is reported on the liability scale for binary phenotypes, which accounts for both sample and population disease prevalence and provides a standardized measure across binary phenotypes. We further constructed a global genetic correlation matrix for all included MSK phenotypes and performed a hierarchical cluster analysis to identify the phenotype groups with similar genetic backgrounds, where the number of phenotype groups and the corresponding phenotypes in each group were served as a reference for the subsequent multivariate genetic modeling.
Global estimates of genetic correlation could reflect the average of shared genetic effects between different traits across the whole genome , while may fail to capture local genetic correlations, which can vary across different genomic regions. In addition, opposing correlations in different regions may lead to a non-significant global genetic correlation . To detect regions of shared genetic association between phenotypes, we conducted local genetic correlation analyses using LAVA . Focusing on semi-independent LD blocks, LAVA provides a more detailed view of genetic architecture compared to global estimates, while accounting for LD structure among variants. For each phenotype pair, we conducted LAVA analyses across 1703 genomic regions defined based on LD structure in the European ancestry reference panel from the 1000 Genomes Project . To ensure robust estimates, local genetic correlation analyses were performed only in regions exhibiting significant local SNP-based heritability (P < 0.05) for both phenotypes. Potential sample overlap between GWASs was accounted for by incorporating the intercepts estimated from bivariate LDSC into the LAVA models. P values were adjusted for multiple comparisons across the 1,703 genomic regions in each phenotype pair using the Bonferroni method, and all statistical tests were two-sided.

## Multivariate genetic modeling
Genomic SEM , a recently developed multivariate statistical genetic framework, enabled the investigation of the multivariate genetic structure and the genetic components shared by multiple MSK phenotypes in the form of latent genetic factors. Importantly, genomic SEM can control sample overlap bias by considering a sampling covariance matrix in the model construction. We used genomic SEM to model the genetic (co)variance matrix of MSK phenotypes based on the genetic clustering pattern derived from hierarchical cluster analysis mentioned above. The parameters of the genomic SEM model were estimated using the weighted least squares method. The model fit was evaluated using chi-square statistics, AIC value, CFI, and SRMR, with a good model defined as CFI >  0.9, SRMR  < 0.08 and a smaller AIC. Notably, we used unit variance identification so that the variances of the latent factors were fixed to one. We reported standardized factor loadings whose squared values represented the proportion of genetic variance explained by the corresponding factor. Statistical significance of model parameters was evaluated using two-sided tests against the null hypothesis of zero effect, and multiple testing was accounted for using Bonferroni correction based on the total number of parameters in the genomic SEM model, with significance declared at P < 0.05/56.

## Multivariate GWAS
Multivariate GWAS implemented in genomic SEM was performed to identify the SNPs associated with the latent factors. To do so, GWAS summary statistics of the analyzed phenotypes were harmonized with the 1000 Genomes reference panel of European ancestry using the sumstats function implemented in genomic SEM, resulting in 4,369,729 SNPs remaining in the multivariate GWAS analysis. In addition, the univariate GWAS effect sizes were standardized across the analyzed phenotypes. Heterogeneity tests were performed to evaluate whether the SNP effects on MSK phenotypes were mainly through the latent factors, and SNPs with significant heterogeneity were excluded from subsequent analysis. The genome-wide significance for association tests and heterogeneity tests was set to P < 5 × 10−8. For each factor, the overall effective sample size of the multivariate GWAS was calculated as the mean of the effective sample sizes for those SNPs with MAF between 0.1 and 0.4, where the effective sample size for a specific SNP was calculated as 1/(2 × MAF × (1−MAF) × se2).

## Genomic risk loci definition and annotation
Functional Mapping and Annotation of Genome-Wide Association Studies (FUMA) was used to define genomic risk loci. First, independent significant SNPs were defined with LD r2 < 0.6 and P < 5 × 10−8, from which the lead SNPs were further identified with LD r2 < 0.1. Then, lead SNPs in close proximity (<250 kb) were merged to determine the genomic risk loci. A single locus may contain multiple lead and independent SNPs, and the top SNP of a locus is defined as the most statistically significant SNP within the genomic risk locus. We declared the loci of latent genetic factors to be novel if they neither overlap with the loci of single phenotype GWAS nor have been reported in NHGRI-EBI GWAS Catalog for MSK phenotypes. FUMA also provided RegulomeDB score for significant variants. RegulomeDB score is a categorical score ranging from 1 to 7, indicating the likelihood of SNPs being involved in regulatory elements. We also annotated the variant function using the Variant Effect Predictor (VEP) .

## Conditional and joint association analysis
To further identify conditionally independent SNPs in each locus, we conducted a stepwise model selection procedure in each locus using the conditional and joint association analysis approach implemented in the genome-wide complex trait analysis software (GCTA-COJO) , . GCTA-COJO was able to select independent SNPs based on conditional P-values and convert the marginal effects of SNPs to joint effects using GWAS summary statistics and a matched LD reference panel. To avoid collinearity issues, if the squared multiple correlation between a tested SNP and the selected SNP(s) exceeds a default cutoff of LD > 0.9, the conditional P-value for that SNP is set to 1. Statistical significance for SNP effect estimates was determined using two-sided tests against the null hypothesis of zero, with the genome-wide significance threshold at 5 × 10−8. The 1000 Genomes Project phase 3 European population was used as reference panel.

## Multi-trait colocalization
Colocalization analysis was mainly developed to assess the shared causal variants across different phenotypes, with HyPrColoc enabling efficient evaluation of multiple phenotypes using GWAS summary statistics. More importantly, HyPrColoc employs a novel branch-and-bound divisive clustering algorithm to identify colocalized phenotype subsets driven by distinct causal variants at a given locus. Here, for each significant factor-associated loci from GWAS, we applied HyPrColoc to assess whether factor-associated loci were driven by shared causal variants of the factor-loaded phenotypes. Using the summary statistics harmonized by genomic SEM, HyPrColoc was conducted in each of factor-associated loci. We used the default settings of HyPrColoc and reported those loci with a posterior probability larger than 0.7, which was illustrated to offer an optimal balance between sensitivity and specificity .

## Heritability of latent factors
We estimated SNP-heritability of the latent factors using LDSC based on the GWAS summary statistics of factors, with the intercept to assess whether the observed inflation in the association statistics was due to polygenicity or population stratification bias. We also used stratified-LDSC to evaluate the partitioned heritability of the latent factors in 28 functional annotations without flanking region, including conserved, coding and regulatory regions of the genome and different histone modifications. For a specific annotation category, the enrichment of partitioned heritability was calculated as the proportion of heritability explained by these annotated variants divided by the proportion of these annotated variants in the total number of variants.

## Gene-based and gene-set analyses
We used MAGMA to perform gene-based and gene-set analyses. MAGMA evaluates gene and gene-set associations by aggregating SNP-level association statistics while accounting for LD. We first assigned SNPs to 5 kb upstream and downstream windows of 17,692 genes based on physical positions in the National Center for Biotechnology Information 37 build genome. Gene-based analysis was then performed using the default SNP-wise Mean model with the 1000 Genomes Project Phase 3 European population as the LD reference. Significantly genes were declared using a Bonferroni correction (P < 0.05/17,692). Finally, based on the gene-based results, we conducted gene-set analysis from 11,045 gene sets from the Molecular Signatures Database, including Gene Ontology (Biological Process, Cellular Component, Molecular Function) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes pathways. Gene-set analysis was performed using the competitive gene-set model, with significance evaluated with Bonferroni correction for multiple tests (P < 0.05/11,045).

## SMR analysis
SMR was adopted to identify factor-associated genes by integrating the GWAS of factors with the summary statistics of QTL, including eQTL, sQTL, and pQTL, respectively. Heterogeneity in dependent instruments (HEIDI) test was used to distinguish the pleiotropic associations from LD confounders . Both eQTL and sQTL data were derived from whole blood and MSK tissue of GTEx v8 , while the pQTL data included 4719 plasma proteins obtained from 35,559 Icelanders . For pQTL data, we retained common biallelic variants (MAF > 0.01) in the cis-region (1 Mb window around transcription start site) of each protein-coding gene and restricted the analysis only to proteins with at least one significant cis-pQTL (P < 5 × 10−8). We used the Bonferroni method for multiple testing correction in SMR analysis and considered non-heterogeneous associations when P-values from HEIDI test was larger than 0.01.

## Colocalization
To explore the cell type-context genes associated with the latent factors, we used coloc to perform colocalization analysis between the latent factors and the cell type-specific gene expression. In this study, we obtained sc-eQTL data of 15 annotated cell subsets from the Database of Immune Cell Expression project, which was obtained from CD4 + T cells of 89 healthy donors . We performed coloc on those genes with significant eQTL and factor GWAS signals at P < 1 × 10−5 in the cis-region of genes. In each genomic region, coloc assumed that at most one shared causal variant exists between the latent factor and the gene and calculated posterior probabilities for five mutually exclusive hypotheses: H0, no association with either trait; H1, association with the latent factor only; H2, association with gene expression only; H3, association with both gene expression and factor but with distinct causal variants; and H4, association with both gene expression and factor sharing the same causal variant. The default prior probabilities were used in analysis. We considered genes and factors to be colocalized when the posterior probability of the hypothesis H4 (PP.H4) was larger than 0.8 as in previous studies , .

## Gene prioritization and druggability assessment
To prioritize candidate genes, we integrated results from the seven analyses described above, including MAGMA, SMR-eQTL (whole blood), SMR-eQTL (skeletal muscle), SMR-sQTL (whole blood), SMR-sQTL (skeletal muscle), SMR-pQTL (plasma), and scc-eQTL colocalization, which capture complementary evidence of gene-factor associations across multiple molecular layers. Each significant factor-gene association was scored from 1 to 7 according to the total number of significant evidence. We defined genes with a score of two or higher as the candidate genes for the factors. We further explored the potential druggability of the candidate genes using the DGIdb to facilitate the identification of the potentially therapeutic targets for multiple MSK disorders, where only drugs with interaction scores greater than 5 were included.

## Spatial mapping of cells using gsMAP
We used gsMAP to identify key cell types and their spatial distribution associated with the factors by integrating GWAS summary statistics of latent factors with ST data. Specifically, gsMap first adopted graphic neural network to identify homogeneous spots in ST data, calculated gene specificity scores by aggregating information from homogeneous spots and linked each gene specificity score to SNPs within a 50 kb window of gene regions, followed by stratified-LDSC to assess stratified heritability for each factor. Finally, the Cauchy combination test was applied to aggregate P-values of spots within a spatial region to quantify the significance of the association with each factor. In this study, we were unable to analyze human tissues due to the limited high-quality ST data, while we chose to analyze ST data from a coronal slice of a mouse embryo at the developmental stage of embryonic day 16.5, which included 81,125 spots and 25 organs annotations . Previous studies have illustrated that the gene expression profiles of mice are quite similar to humans, with a correlation of over 80% at the tissue level and over 75% at the cell type level , .

## Polygenic enrichment analysis using scDRS
We integrated GWAS summary statistics of latent factors with scRNA-seq data to evaluate the polygenic enrichment of latent factors in specific cell types using scDRS . First, scDRS identified candidate genes for factors using MAGMA (top 1000 genes) and calculated the factor-relevance score (weighted by MAGMA Z-score) for each cell in the scRNA-seq data. Then, 1000 sets of cell-specific raw control scores were generated to assess the statistical significance of cell-factor associations at the single-cell level. Cells were further divided into different cell types and the top 5% quantile of the normalized factor scores of cells in a given cell type were used as the statistic to test the association between the cell type and the factors. Here, we used five scRNA-seq datasets to systematically investigate cell types associated with latent genetic factors: (1) Embryonic skeleton: scRNA-seq data of the embryonic skeleton were obtained from a multi-omics atlas of human embryonic joint and cranium development established from 12 donors of 5–11 weeks post-conception, including a total of 372,937 cells annotated into 47 cell types . (2) Skeletal muscle: scRNA-seq data for adult skeletal muscle were obtained from 17 donors, including 183,161 cells annotated to 43 cell types . (3) Quadriceps tendon: Single-nucleus RNA-seq data for human quadriceps tendons were obtained from patients undergoing fracture repair or tendon repair surgery, including a total of 12,808 nuclei annotated into 22 cell types . (4) Immune cell atlas: scRNA-seq data for immune cells were obtained from a large-scale immune profiling of over 1.25 million cells isolated from blood, lymphoid organs, and mucosal tissues of 24 human organ donors aged 20–75 years . Cells were annotated into 31 cell types across 14 tissues using a multimodal classifier. Using scDRS, we tested cell-type enrichment for all three MSK factors in embryonic skeleton. For MSKdg, we additionally tested polygenic enrichment in skeletal muscle and quadriceps tendon. For MSKai, polygenic enrichment was examined across immune tissues and cell types. Multiple tests were corrected using the Bonferroni method.
