Protective intravenous BCG vaccination induces enhanced immune signaling in the airways

18720232023.07.16.549208bioRxiv10.1101/2023.07.16.549208PMC1037004637502895Intradermal Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) is the most widely administered vaccine, but it does not sufficiently protect adults against pulmonary tuberculosis. Recent studies in nonhuman primates show that intravenous BCG administration offers superior protection against Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb). We used single-cell analysis of bronchoalveolar lavage cells from rhesus macaques vaccinated via different routes and doses of BCG to identify alterations in the immune ecosystem in the airway following vaccination. Our findings reveal that high-dose intravenous BCG induces an influx of polyfunctional T cells and macrophages in the airways, with alveolar macrophages from high-dose intravenous BCG displaying a basal activation state in the absence of purified protein derivative stimulation, defined in part by interferon signaling. Enhanced intercellular immune signaling and stronger T helper 1–T helper 17 transcriptional responses were observed following purified protein derivative stimulation. These results suggest that high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination creates a specialized immune environment that primes airway cells for effective Mtb clearance.

High-dose IV BCG vaccination alters the phenotype of airway innate and adaptive immune cells.

## INTRODUCTION
Bacillus Calmette-Guerin (BCG) is administered to approximately 130 million children every year to protect against Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb), the causative agent of tuberculosis (TB) ( – ). While intradermal BCG is the only licensed TB vaccine, it inadequately protects against pulmonary infection in adults ( ). Consequently, TB remains a leading driver of infectious disease–related death worldwide ( ). The development of an effective TB vaccine has been hampered by a poor understanding of protective immunity against Mtb ( – ). Intravenous administration of BCG was recently shown to be highly protective against Mtb infection and disease in nonhuman primates (NHPs), making high-dose intravenous BCG immunization a powerful system to identify immune correlates and mechanisms of protection against Mtb infection ( , ).
Several immune features are associated with intravenous BCG vaccination ( , , , ). Intravenous BCG–immunized rhesus macaques exhibit higher antibody titers and a broad increase in CD4+ and CD8+ T cell immunity in the airways ( , ). Further, lung-localized T cells in intravenous BCG–immunized macaques are enriched for a transcriptional signature of memory T cell functionality compared to macaques that received intradermal BCG or aerosol BCG vaccination ( ). However, the phenotypic effect of intravenous BCG vaccination on the larger cellular ecosystem in the lungs and airways has not been evaluated. For instance, myeloid cells such as alveolar and monocyte-derived macrophages represent a primary niche for Mtb upon infection ( , ). These cells also represent a central node in the cellular network against Mtb, as they can be modulated by other immune effectors such as T cells and antibodies to promote intracellular Mtb control ( , ). Despite evidence that the ability of macrophages to respond to and control Mtb infection is a function of their phenotypic state ( ), the impact of intravenous BCG on airway myeloid cell phenotype and function in NHPs remains largely unknown.
In the present study, we leveraged single-cell data from several cohorts of BCG-vaccinated NHPs to develop an integrated view of how high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination influences the airway immune cell environment compared to other vaccination regimens. We sought to profile this unique resource of airway cells across multiple vaccine administration routes and doses to reveal key transcriptional programs associated with intravenous BCG–mediated protection. High-dose intravenous BCG induces a robust increase in T cells and recruited macrophages into the airways. Notably, high-dose intravenous BCG also drives a prolonged state of transcriptional activation in alveolar macrophages (AMs), characterized by robust type I and type II interferon (IFN) signaling. Moreover, intercellular communication between select myeloid and T cell populations is notably increased in the bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) of high-dose intravenous BCG–immunized macaques upon stimulation with purified protein derivative (PPD), highlighting a broad increase in responsiveness to mycobacterial antigen stimulation. Last, transcriptional programs associated with key antimicrobial processes including reactive oxygen species production, hypoxia, and the IFN-γ response are up-regulated in AMs following PPD stimulation in the high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination group. Together, this work suggests that intravenous BCG immunization establishes a milieu of highly coordinated immune cells in the lung that robustly activates local myeloid cells to counter Mtb infection.

## RESULTS

## An integrated census of BAL cells from BCG-vaccinated macaques
BAL cells from 29 rhesus macaques across multiple BCG vaccination cohorts were transcriptionally profiled using single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) ( , and table S1). The first cohort consists of 15 total macaques immunized with either low-dose intradermal (ID) BCG [BCG dose, 5 × 105 colony-forming units (CFU); n = 3], high-dose ID BCG (BCG dose, 5 × 107 CFU; n = 3), high-dose aerosol BCG (BCG dose, 5 × 107 CFU; n = 3), or high-dose intravenous BCG (BCG dose, 5 × 107 CFU; n = 3), as well as naive, unvaccinated controls (n = 3) ( ). BAL cells from these macaques were collected 13 and 25 weeks following vaccination. The second cohort contains 14 macaques: 13 were vaccinated with intravenous BCG across four different doses [BCG doses, 1.37 × 105 CFU (n = 3), 3.11 × 105 CFU (n = 5), 4.23 × 105 CFU (n = 1), and 5 × 107 CFU (n = 4)], and 1 served as a naive, unvaccinated control. BAL cells were collected from these macaques 14 to 15 weeks following vaccination. To simulate antigen stimulation in these samples, we split BAL samples in half and stimulated one-half of each sample overnight with PPD (20 mg/ml) ex vivo as a surrogate for Mtb antigen stimulation. Including a PPD stimulation sample enabled the study of how different vaccination paradigms affect immune activation in addition to baseline responses. Analytically, scRNA-seq samples from different time points were grouped with the 13- to 15-week samples being grouped as an “early” time point and the 25-week samples being grouped as a “late” time point.
 BAL samples (with or without PPD stimulation) from each macaque at each time point were analyzed using Seq-Well ( , ), yielding 157,114 cells from 29 macaques after quality control and single-cell data integration using Harmony (see Materials and Methods; ; table S2; and fig. S1, A and B) ( ). We identified cell states in the unstimulated (n = 74,641 cells) and PPD-stimulated (n = 82,473 cells) BAL cells together after harmonization using cluster markers and established lung cell type signatures (see Materials and Methods, , fig. S2A, and table S3) ( ). Among these cells, we identified 53,979 T cells, 1837 natural killer (NK) cells, 70,130 AMs, and 8815 non-AM myeloid cells (MCs), including monocytes, dendritic cells (DCs), and macrophages. We performed subclustering in the T/NK cells and AMs/MCs, given the large abundance of these cell types and known diversity within these cell lineages (table S3). Cells were equally represented across batches, mitigating concern of batch-to-batch variation (Local Inverse Simpson’s Index = 8.70; see Materials and Methods). Further, all cell types were captured in both unstimulated and PPD-stimulated samples (fig. S2B). To determine whether high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination induces a unique cellular ecosystem in the lung, we grouped animals based on their vaccination regimen and PPD stimulation status and performed comparisons between these groups.

## Increased recruited macrophages in the BAL of high-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated macaques
Flow cytometry analysis previously showed that high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination drives a marked influx of T cells into the airways ( ). This influx reduces the proportional abundance of AMs, substantially increasing the T cell:AM ratio in the BAL ( ). We initially pooled all samples from all time points to identify cellular trends across vaccination regimens in the hope of identifying broad patterns independent of time point. Comparing cellular composition of the BAL across vaccination regimens by scRNA-seq revealed that the ratio of T cells to AMs was significantly higher in animals vaccinated with high-dose intravenous BCG compared to those vaccinated low-dose intravenous BCG, low-dose ID BCG, aerosol BCG, and naïve animals ( and table S4). Unexpectedly, the ratio of non-AMs to AMs was also significantly higher in high-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated animals compared to low-dose intravenous BCG, aerosol BCG, and naïve animals, pointing to a compositional difference in the myeloid compartment driven by high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination ( and table S4).
Given that myeloid cells play a well-established role in Mtb containment ( , ), we next analyzed the myeloid compartment in unstimulated BAL cells across each vaccination group with increased granularity. Subclustering the AM/MC populations revealed six primary cell types, namely, conventional type I DCs (cDC1s), plasmacytoid DCs (pDCs), mature DCs enriched in immunoregulatory molecules (mregDCs) (LAMP3+ DCs), monocytes, AMs, and recruited macrophages ( , fig. S3A, and table S3). To distinguish between recruited and resident macrophages, we used lineage-resolved signatures of recruited monocytes-macrophages in mice and humans ( ) to annotate each group (see Materials and Methods and fig. S3B). Recruited macrophages lacked tissue-resident AM markers such as MRC1, PPARG, and BHLHE41 and expressed chemotactic markers such as CXCL9/10/11, as well as genes implicated in driving adaptive immune trafficking, activation, and inflammation (table S3) ( – ). Our compositional analysis revealed that unstimulated BAL samples from high-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated animals had a significant positive enrichment of recruited macrophages, while AMs were significantly negatively enriched ( and table S4). Other myeloid populations showed more modest differences. For example, monocytes were significantly enriched in the BAL of high-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated animals compared to all vaccination groups other than low-dose ID BCG ( ).
 We next sought to orthogonally validate our observation about BAL macrophages following high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination. We leveraged a cohort of macaques that underwent high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination whose BALs were prepared specifically for cytometry by time of flight (CyTOF) analysis and sampled at baseline and 4 and 8 weeks following high-dose intravenous BCG ( ). Using FlowSOM, five clusters of macrophages were identified of which four showed statistically significant differences in abundance following vaccination (fig. S4A) ( ). We observed a significant decrease in the frequency AMs (CD68− and CD206+) comparing baseline to the 4- or 8-week time point (~90 to <30% at 4 weeks; ). We observed a significant increase in the frequency of recruited macrophages (CD68+ and CD206−) at the 4- and 8-week time point compared to baseline ( and fig. S4B). Together, these data suggest that beyond driving increased T cell numbers in the BAL, high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination also promotes an increase in inflammatory, recruited macrophages in the airways, altering the local myeloid cell ecosystem.

## High-dose intravenous BCG drives the robust activation of local macrophages
We next aimed to determine whether high-dose intravenous BCG resulted in a difference in transcriptional state in lung-localized macrophages following vaccination. We performed three transcriptional analyses. In one analysis, all time points were pooled to identify transcriptional signatures reflective of vaccination at any time point. In a second analysis, early time points (13 to 15 weeks) were analyzed separately. In a third analysis, late time points (25 weeks) were analyzed.
We initially sought to identify differentially expressed genes in unstimulated recruited macrophages between animals vaccinated with high-dose intravenous BCG and naïve animals. At early time points, several differentially expressed genes were identified including LTA4H, which has been previously implicated in susceptibility to Mtb, and LYZ, which encodes the protease lysozyme that contributes to degradation of bacterial cell walls (table S5) ( , ). At later time points, relatively fewer differentially expressed genes were detected, suggesting that the transcriptional effect of intravenous BCG on recruited macrophages in the lung may wane with time. Given the superior protection elicited by high-dose intravenous BCG in previous studies, we next sought to determine whether there were genes differentially expressed in high-dose intravenous BCG animals compared to all other vaccination regimens ( , , ). At both early and late time points, few differentially expressed genes between intravenous BCG and all other vaccine regimens were detected in recruited macrophages.
Previous reports suggest that AMs are highly permissive for Mtb since their activation is tightly regulated to limit undesired inflammatory responses ( ). We next sought to determine whether high-dose intravenous BCG resulted in any transcriptional changes in unstimulated AMs. Despite the well-documented tight regulation of AM function, we found many more differentially expressed genes in the AMs independent of time point (early, late, and pooled)—substantially more compared to the recruited macrophages. At both early and late time points, we observed strong up-regulation of IFI27, AXL, and FAM26F in AMs from high-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated animals compared to naïve animals ( ). Pathway analysis of the differentially expressed genes between high-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated animals and naïve animals at either time point revealed enrichment of inflammatory pathways including the type I and type II IFN response ( ). Again, we next sought to determine whether there were genes differentially expressed in high-dose intravenous BCG animals compared to all other vaccination regimes. At both early and late time points, we found that AMs from high-dose intravenous BCG animals had elevated expression of multiple genes including APOL2 and GIMAP4 (table S6). Neither of these proteins have been explicitly implicated in antimicrobial function; however, a related protein, APOL3, has been implicated in control of cytosolic Salmonella ( ). At early time points, pathway analysis revealed a significant enrichment of genes associated with IFN signaling. These pathway level differences were absent at the 25-week time point. Together, these data point to a marked compositional shift in the local macrophage compartment up to 6 months following high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination via two mechanisms: (i) the influx of inflammatory recruited macrophages into the airways and (ii) the phenotypic shift of resident AMs to a protracted state of activation, even without ex vivo PPD stimulation.

## Multiple receptor-ligand interactions are predicted to be active in the BAL of high-dose intravenous BCG animals
Our transcriptional analysis of myeloid cells in the airway pointed to an elevated transcriptional signature of IFN signaling in the setting of high-dose intravenous BCG at baseline. Myeloid production of IFN-γ was not observed at appreciable levels in myeloid cells, thus suggesting that interactions between myeloid and nonmyeloid cells might drive the transcriptional state of airway myeloid cells (table S7). Previous studies observed elevated IFN-γ production by T cells in the setting of high-dose intravenous BCG ( , ). Furthermore, depletion studies have further demonstrated that depletion of CD4+ T cells disrupts intravenous-BCG-mediated Mtb control ( ). These observations motivated us to examine additional receptor-ligand interactions that may be occurring in the airway especially between T cells and myeloid cells ( , ).
To investigate potential cell-cell interactions, we used CellChat (see Materials and Methods). CellChat predicts interaction strengths between cell subsets using ligand-receptor expression, cellular composition information, and gene expression ( ). We constructed two CellChat networks derived from unstimulated or PPD-stimulated samples. We used cells from all vaccination regimens to identify all candidate ligand-receptor interactions and then examined the strength of these receptor-ligand interactions across vaccination regimens. In unstimulated cells, CellChat predicted receptor-ligand interactions through CD6-activated leukocyte cell adhesion molecule (ALCAM), colony-stimulating factor 1 (CSF1)–CSF1 receptor (CSF1R), tumor necrosis factor (TNF)–TNF receptor superfamily member 1B (TNFRSF1B), as well as FMS-like tyrosine kinase 3 ligand-fms related receptor tyrosine kinase 3 (FLT3L-FLT3) and IFNG-IFNGR1/2 ( , left). Notably, CD6-ALCAM and FLT3L-FLT3 interactions are implicated in driving T cell effector function and DC activity ( – ). Upon PPD stimulation, we see an increase in other ligand-receptor interactions involved in T cell–induced macrophage activation including CD40LG-CD40 and lymphotoxin α (LTA)–TNFRSF1B ( , right), as well as increased TNF and IFNG interactions. Consistent with these observations, our published flow cytometry analysis confirms the production of TNF and IFN-γ by T cells across the vaccination groups ( ).
 We used CyTOF to validate the production of TNF, interleukin-6 (IL-6), IL-1β and C-C motif chemokine ligand 4 (CCL4) by BAL myeloid cells after overnight stimulation with Mtb lysate (table S8). The frequency of recruited macrophages (CD68+/CD206−) producing TNF was higher in intravenous BCG–vaccinated macaques compared to baseline. In contrast, there was no difference in the production of TNF by AMs at baseline versus after vaccination. A trend toward increased IL-6 and CCL6 production was noted in both CD68+/CD206− and CD68−/CD206+ cells, while similar frequencies of IL-1β production were seen between baseline and after intravenous BCG vaccination in both macrophage populations.
Last, we evaluated how the quality of the intercellular communication networks might vary across vaccination groups. We examined the expression of key ligands and receptors such as IFNG, IFNGR1, and CTLA4 across cells in the communication network. Scoring samples based on the expression of these key ligand-receptor pairs revealed a significantly higher expression score in high-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated animals compared to naïve animals in PPD-stimulated cells at 13 weeks, although this difference was not significant at 25 weeks ( and fig. S5). Together, these findings suggest that high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination primes an airway environment that promotes increased communication of myeloid cells with polyfunctional and helper CD4+ T cells upon antigenic stimulation.

## Myeloid-interacting T cells display increased T1-T17 polarization following high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination
Given the role of T cells in protection in the setting of high-dose intravenous BCG, we next examined the compositional and transcriptional differences in the airway T/NK cell compartment. We subclustered all the T and NK cell populations and identified 13 distinct cell subsets ( , and Materials and Methods), including IFNG- and TNF-expressing polyfunctional T cells (n = 1460) and IRF4- and CD28-expressing helper CD4+ T cells (n = 6540). Several transcription factors implicated in T cell activation and polarization were defining markers of cell states, including IRF4, RBPJ, BATF, NR4A3, and EGR2 ( – ). We next compared the proportional abundance of each subset across vaccination groups (table S4). In aggregate, the proportional abundance of several T cell subsets was significantly higher in high-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated animals compared to naïve animals. Regulatory T cells (Tregs), stem-like CD8+, and IL-2RA+ CD8+ T cells showed no differential abundance across the groups. Notably, in PPD-stimulated BAL cells, polyfunctional T cells, helper CD4+ T cells, and IL-7R+ CD4+ T cells—each identified previously as primary myeloid interaction partners ( )—were statistically significantly more abundant in samples from high-dose intravenous BCG animals than all other groups ( and fig. S6).
 To more precisely characterize the functionality and responsiveness of the different BAL T cell subpopulations, we asked which T cells were most transcriptionally responsive to PPD by analyzing the number of differentially expressed genes up-regulated after PPD stimulation per subset for all cells in our dataset. Polyfunctional and helper CD4+ T cells were most responsive to PPD stimulation based on the number of differentially expressed genes, followed by IL-2RA+ CD8+ T cells, activated T cells, and effector CD8+ T cells ( ). We next contextualized these T cell populations using recent and biologically relevant scRNA-seq–derived signatures. Specifically, following Mtb infection and the onset of adaptive immunity, a “T1-T17” population was found to be highly enriched in Mtb granulomas that promote bacterial clearance ( ). To determine whether a similar transcriptional signature was engendered in the setting of high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination, we scored T and NK cells on the top 50 genes defining this T1-T17 population. At early time points, the T1-T17 transcriptional signature was significantly expressed in high-dose intravenous BCG macaques compared to naïve animals when analyzing all T/NK cell populations ( and fig. S7). This broad shift across diverse T/NK cell subtypes toward a T1-T17 phenotype suggests that, in this context, the T1-T17 phenotype may represent a polarization state driven by high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination, rather than a specific subset of T cells. Here, the key myeloid-interacting T cell subsets—polyfunctional T cells, helper CD4+ T cells, and IL-7R+ CD4+ T cells—exhibited the strongest T1-T17 score ( ). To further define T cell phenotypic state, we scored T and NK cells using a signature of lung residency containing 10 genes derived from the human lung atlas (see Materials and Methods) ( ). We observed significantly higher expression of lung residency–defining genes within the polyfunctional and helper CD4+ T cell subsets and lower expression in naïve and stem-like subsets, potentially suggesting a temporal ordering of the various T cell subsets in the airway (fig. S8).
We next sought to uncover T cell genes that distinguished high-dose intravenous BCG macaques from naïve animals, as well as those that distinguished high-dose intravenous BCG from all vaccination regimens. In unstimulated T cells, at both early and late time points, TNFAIP3 was more highly expressed in T cells from high-dose animals compared to naïve animals (table S9). In T cells, TNFAIP3 has been previously shown to regulate T cell receptor–mediated signaling and cell death ( , ). Within PPD-stimulated T cells, a range of functionally relevant genes was significantly higher in high-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated animals ( and table S9). This included genes such as TNFSF8 (CD30LG and CD153), which is indicative of IL-17 secretion and has been previously implicated in resistant to Mtb infection ( , ). Secreted factors such as LTA and IFNG were more highly expressed in stimulated T cells from high-dose intravenous BCG animals, while TGFB1, which is associated with immunosuppression during Mtb, was more highly expressed in stimulated T cells from naïve animals ( ). When comparing animals that received high-dose intravenous BCG to all other animals, a small number of differentially expressed genes were identified at both early and late time points. At both early and late time points, T cells from high-dose intravenous BCG macaques expressed elevated levels of DAPK2 compared to all other vaccination regimens (table S9). Relatively little is known about DAPK2 in the context of TB or T cell function; however, a small number of studies suggest death associated protein kinase 2 (DAPK2) may regulate germinal center formation ( ). Collectively, these data indicate that beyond broad increases in T cell abundance, specific transcriptional programs in T/NK cells distinguish vaccine regimes, which may regulate protective to Mtb infection respectively.

## Select superinduced genes upon PPD stimulation track with vaccination group
Distinct expression patterns between unstimulated and PPD-stimulated T cells inspired the hypothesis that quantitative and/or qualitative differences in cellular responsiveness to PPD stimulation, a proxy for Mtb challenge, may vary across vaccination regimen. To investigate this possibility, we leveraged the paired unstimulated and PPD-stimulated samples from the BAL of each macaque to query the relationship between cellular responsiveness to PPD stimulation and group ( ).
 We constructed a PPD-response score using the top 50 genes up-regulated following PPD stimulation in polyfunctional, helper CD4+, and IL-7R+ CD4+ T cells (see Materials and Methods and table S10). We then scored T cells across all subsets using this response signature and compared the fold change induction of this signature between unstimulated and PPD-stimulated samples. Compared to naïve animals, all vaccine groups showed an elevated PPD-response score (fig. S9).
As an alternative approach, we next aimed to identify genes that showed differential responses to PPD stimulation across different vaccine groups. We calculated the log(fold change) (logFC) in gene expression between unstimulated and PPD-stimulated T cells within each group and identified genes which showed stronger fold changes in high-dose intravenous BCG animals. At both time points, transcriptional regulators such as IRF4 and RBPJ were more highly up-regulated following PPD stimulation in high-dose intravenous BCG animals compared to all other vaccine groups ( ) ( , , ). By contrast, IL7R was more highly up-regulated in naïve animals, showing that PPD stimulation induces disparate transcriptional responses in genes across vaccination groups that mark or drive distinct T cell phenotypes.
Given the notable increase in T cell–myeloid communication upon PPD stimulation identified in the ligand-receptor interaction analysis ( ), we sought to determine whether PPD stimulation resulted in differential responses in AMs across vaccination groups. At both early and late time points, SLAMF7, a marker of macrophage superactivation, IL1RN, an antagonist of IL-1 signaling ( ), and LAMP3, a lysosomal transmembrane protein, represented examples of genes selectively up-regulated upon PPD stimulation in high-dose intravenous BCG animals ( ). Pathway analysis of the genes enriched at both time points indicated that AMs from high-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated macaques included pathways such as TNF signaling and hypoxia signaling ( ) ( ). Together, these results suggest that high-dose intravenous BCG immunization primes a unique cellular environment in the lungs, defined by a sustained state of transcriptional activation and a heightened functional responsiveness to antigenic stimulation that may explain how high-dose intravenous BCG mediates robust Mtb protection in vivo.

## DISCUSSION
In this study, we exploited single-cell transcriptional data from several cohorts of BCG-vaccinated NHPs to find how different TB vaccination approaches, which vary in their protective efficacy, alter the immune ecosystem in the lung. We found that high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination remodeled the airway myeloid compartment, triggering a sustained compositional shift toward recruited macrophages and activated AMs that serve as master communicators of intercellular signaling in the lung. Upon PPD stimulation, intercellular communication between BAL macrophages and T cells increases in high-dose intravenous BCG–immunized macaques. This group also shows quantitatively and qualitatively enhanced transcriptional responses to PPD stimulation, with a distinct T helper 1 (TH1)–TH17 transcriptional phenotype in T cells and augmented transcriptional signatures of reactive oxygen species production, hypoxia, and IFN-γ responses within the AM compartment, collectively suggesting that intravenous BCG primes a unique cellular ecosystem in the lung, characterized by a protracted state of activation and a heightened responsiveness to antigenic stimulation.
In mice and humans, BCG vaccination results in the innate training of myeloid cells, characterized by a heightened responsiveness to stimuli and epigenetic remodeling ( – ). In light of this, our observation that AMs display an enhanced transcriptional response upon stimulation with PPD ex vivo is intriguing. Our data suggest that that myeloid cell priming may be an apt description of the impact of high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination on local macrophage cell state ( ). While immune activation returns to a baseline level following initial stimulation in innate training, during innate immune priming, the initial stimuli trigger a sustained transcriptional activation state such that secondary challenge engenders an even more potent response ( ). Consistent with this model, our data indicate that 6 months following intravenous BCG immunization, unstimulated airway macrophages of high-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated macaques display a protracted activation phenotype through IFN- and TNF-associated transcriptional programs. This heightened activation state was further amplified by PPD stimulation ex vivo. Measuring innate training or innate immune priming in this system of stimulating BAL with PPD is challenging, given that cell type–specific transcriptional responses to PPD stimulation likely represent an integration of cell-intrinsic, autocrine, and paracrine effects. Given that we observed an increase in activated T cells in the airway, it is essential that we recognize and consider the contribution of T cells to the altered myeloid cell states in addition to any classical innate immune training/priming that may be occurring. Recent work on the nonspecific, antiviral protection induced by intravenous BCG in mice support a requirement of T cell and/or IFN-γ priming of myeloid cells for cross-protection ( – ). Future work in which macrophages in the BAL are isolated via negative selection or plastic adhesion so as not to introduce experimental artifacts, followed by independent ex vivo stimulation, would help determine the extent to which the macrophage phenotypes observed are cell intrinsic.
AMs of high-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated macaques display increased induction of reactive oxygen species, hypoxia, and IFN-γ response transcriptional programs following PPD stimulation, highlighting the diverse intracellular and intercellular immune pressure triggered by antigenic stimulation in the setting of high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination. Similarly, intercellular communication between myeloid cells and T cells is enriched in high-dose intravenous BCG–immunized macaques following PPD stimulation. These observations reveal a heightened phenotypic responsiveness to PPD stimulation in the airway immune cells of macaques receiving high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination and further suggest that intravenous BCG primes a local biological network of immune cells, poised to efficiently respond to and mobilize anti-Mtb immune function upon challenge. We note that while macrophage markers were included in the previous flow cytometry characterization of BAL cells in intravenous BCG, scRNA-seq analysis in this study enabled the discovery of previously unknown signatures and markers that define differences in macrophage state that should be considered for inclusion in future flow cytometry panels ( ). Previously, a model of contained Mtb infection wherein mice are intradermally infected with Mtb showed enhanced AM activation ( ). While BAL BCG CFU was not enumerated in this study, a previous study showed detectable BAL BCG CFU 1 month after intravenous BCG vaccination ( ). This raises the possibility that residual mycobacterial burden may contribute to the tonic reprogramming of AMs and/or the persistent activation of lung-localized T cells. However, while chronic immune activation could enhance protective anti-Mtb immune responses, it may also pose a risk by driving unwanted inflammation that exacerbates or accelerates disease in other contexts. This potentially dual-edged nature of persistent immune activation highlights the need for caution when considering vaccination strategies that prime similar responses in humans.
Polyfunctional and helper CD4+ T cells, both enriched for tissue-resident gene programs, demonstrated higher frequencies in the BAL of high-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated animals. These T cell subsets also display the strongest ligand-receptor interactions with the recruited myeloid cell populations. We identified a range of transcription factors coexpressed within these T cell subsets, including EGR2, NR4A3, IRF4, and RPBJ, in addition to T-bet (TBX21), BATF, and RORC. These polyfunctional T cells include both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, indicating a consistent transcriptional signal of T cell polyfunctionality across CD4 and CD8+ T cells that are associated with vaccination group. Similar to recent work that identified the frequency of CD4 memory T cells producing TNF with IFN-γ, and TNF with IL-17 as key predictors of protection ( ), our work finds a TH1-TH17 polarization of T cells induced by protective vaccination. Furthermore, vaccination group–associated T cells in the BAL from our study, such as the polyfunctional and helper CD4+ T cell subsets, dominantly express a T1-T17 transcriptional signature previously found to be stronger in T cells present in Mtb granulomas with low bacterial burdens ( ), highlighting a convergence of T1-T17 transcriptional programs that associate with beneficial immune responses before and after Mtb infection. Consistent with this observation, T cell expression of IL23R, IL21R, PIK3CG, and TJP2, among others, before and after stimulation is highly associated with high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination in these analyses. These genes have been consistently implicated in regulating optimal T cell differentiation and activation toward long-lived, effector subsets ( – ). Overall, intravenous BCG biases the T cell compartment toward durable, primed polyfunctional states that mount stronger responses and intercellular communication after antigenic challenge. These data provide improved resolution into the molecular features underpinning these states and point toward a combination of transcription factors and receptors that can be exploited for future studies.
Rhesus macaques are highly susceptible to Mtb infection, making them a stringent and challenging model for evaluating vaccination efficacy ( ). This enhanced susceptibility also enables the identification of suboptimal immune responses, as in contrast to humans, most unvaccinated rhesus macaques fail to control initial exposure. Our observations underscore the complex logic of intravenous BCG vaccination, which may require both the presence of some key immunoregulatory molecules and the absence of others. For instance, T cells from naïve animals have higher expression of transforming growth factor–β1 (TGFB1), and naïve macaques are the least protected in these vaccination studies ( ). This pattern aligns with recent work demonstrating that TGF-β impedes T cell expansion and function in TB granulomas ( ). Thus, our work highlights two key objectives in sophisticated immune correlates analyses: understanding the immune determinants of failure and understanding multicellular profiles to capture immune system dynamics more holistically ( – ).
Limitations of the study include that we were not able to challenge all animals in this study with Mtb and we are, therefore, unable to define transcriptional signatures of protection. In our study, low-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated animals received between 135,000 and 423,000 BCG CFU. A recent dose-ranging study of intravenous BCG found that four of six low-dose intravenous BCG animals with the same range of BCG had detectable Mtb burden after challenge. High-dose intravenous BCG–vaccinated animals in this study received 50,000,000 BCG CFU. The previous dose-ranging study of intravenous BCG found that three of four animals vaccinated with 24,900,000 BCG CFU had no detectable Mtb burden after challenge. We instead focused on associations between cell types/states and vaccination group. A limitation of our study is that we did not have paired peripheral blood mononuclear cell samples to examine compartment specificity with single-cell resolution. Previous studies have identified an IFN gene expression program in peripheral blood mononuclear cells following high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination; however, the specificity of this signature to particular cell types was not determined ( ). It is interesting to speculate that the IFN signaling response seen in intravenous BCG at baseline is systemic. Another limitation is that we only performed a limited amount of follow-up studies in BAL samples. A portion was taken to test directed T cell hypotheses using flow cytometry and the rest for hypothesis-generating exercises using scRNA-seq. At the time of study design, we did not anticipate AM reprogramming that would have motivated additional functional analyses such as Mtb growth experiments in AMs. The strengths of the study lie in our unbiased and comprehensive exploration of immune signatures of high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination—the current gold standard for protective immunity against Mtb. More generally, these data provide a large reference of immune cell profiles in the lung after administration of a systemic immunogenic agent. Our analysis of genome-wide transcriptional dynamics in a pairwise manner between unstimulated and antigen-stimulated samples further offers a previously unknown approach to identifying gene programs modulated by antigenic challenge that correlate with a protective vaccination regimen.
Ultimately, this study contributes to increasing evidence that high-dose intravenous BCG vaccination fundamentally remodels the lung ecosystem of immunized macaques ( , , ), priming the multicellular coordination likely required for Mtb control ( , – ). Future work should investigate the temporal and expression dynamics of the key biologic networks identified herein immediately after BCG vaccination, before Mtb challenge, and after Mtb challenge to elucidate their collective role in creating a primed lung milieu capable of driving sterilizing protection against Mtb to catalyze the development of novel vaccination strategies to combat TB.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

## Study design
Indian-origin rhesus macaques (Macaca mulatta) used in these studies are outlined in table S1. All experimentation complied with ethical regulations at the respective institutions [Animal Care and Use Committees of the Vaccine Research Center, National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases (NIAID), National Institutes of Health (NIH)]. Macaques were housed and cared for in accordance with local, state, federal, and institute policies in facilities accredited by the American Association for Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care under standards established in the Animal Welfare Act and the Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals. Macaques were monitored for physical health, food consumption, body weight, temperature, complete blood counts, and serum chemistries. The approval numbers for the animal studies are 16-547 and 17-720.

## BCG vaccination
BCG Danish Strain 1331 (Statens Serum Institut, Copenhagen, Denmark) was expanded, frozen at approximately 3 × 108 CFUs/ml in single-use aliquots and stored at −80°C. Immediately before injection, BCG (for all vaccine routes) was thawed and diluted in cold phosphate-buffered saline containing 0.05% tyloxapol (Sigma-Aldrich) and 0.002% antifoam Y-30 (Sigma-Aldrich) to prevent clumping of BCG and foaming during aerosolization. Intravenous BCG was injected into the left saphenous vein in a volume of 2 ml; no loss of viability was observed for BCG after aerosolization. Text refers to nominal BCG doses.

## Rhesus BAL profiling and sequencing
BAL wash fluid (3 × 20 ml of washes of phosphate-buffered saline) was centrifuged, and cells were combined before counting, as previously described ( ). High-throughput single-cell mRNA sequencing by Seq-Well was performed on single-cell suspensions obtained from NHP BAL, as previously described ( , ). Approximately 15,000 viable cells per sample were applied directly to the surface of a Seq-Well device. At each time point after BCG, two arrays were run for each sample—one unstimulated and one stimulated ex vivo overnight with PPD (20 μg/ml) in R10. Sequencing for all samples was performed on an Illumina Nova-Seq.

## Data preparation
Libraries were aligned using Drop-seq tools against the M. mulatta genome using STAR50 with forced detection of 5000 or 10,000 barcodes. Cells were then identified per array using emptyDrops (DropletUtils, R) with a false discovery rate cutoff of 0.01. Scrublet was also used on each array, and cells labeled as doublets or in the top 99th percentile were removed. Data from Darrah et al. ( ) was accessed on Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO; GSE139598). Digital gene expression matrices and metadata were merged and used to generate a Seurat object for all downstream analyses. We removed cells with less than 400 genes detected, less than 700 unique molecular identifiers, and more than 10 or 25% reads from mitochondrial or ribosomal genes, respectively. We estimated ambient, contaminating genes using the estimateAmbience function (DropletUtils, R) and excluded genes detected in the top 99.7% percentile in at least 16 of the 32 batches within the Dose cohort samples. Additional filtering was performed after preliminary clustering of unstimulated and PPD-stimulated samples separately, following the procedure outlined below. Clusters defined by multiple cell lineage markers, low library metrics, and/or high expression of ribosomal or mitochondrial genes were excluded.

## Normalization, dimensionality reduction, and clustering
Single-cell analyses were performed using R and the Seurat package based on previous recommendations and benchmarking ( ). Counts were log normalized using NormalizeData, and the top 3000 variable features were selected using FindVariableFeatures (selection.method = “vst”). Principal components analysis was run on the scaled matrix on variable features only. Selection of downstream principal components was inspected using multiple methods including the “elbow” heuristic and an intrinsic dimension estimation (maxLikGlobalDimEst, intrinsicDimension, R). Batch effects were then corrected using Harmony across both stimulation and batch variables (theta = 2, sigma = 0.1, lambda = 1). Visualization of the Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) embedding was generated using RunUMAP across 30 dimensions. Features were visualized within the UMAP embedding using hexbin density plots (bins = 64, schex, R). Clustering was performed on the shared nearest neighbor (SNN) graph (knn = 20) using either the Walktrap algorithm (steps = 4, cluster_walktrap, igraph, R) or the Leiden algorithm (leiden_find_partition, leidenbase, R). Any clusters with less than 10 cells were automatically grouped into other clusters based on their SNN connectivity (modified GroupSingletons, Seurat, R). Leiden clustering was performed automatically by scanning resolutions between a lower bound and upper bound cluster limit then optimizing the modularity across resolutions. Resulting clusters were hierarchically clustered and reordered (BuildClusterTree, Seurat, R) based on expression of all variable features. For all cells, 30 principal components were used for Harmony and 30 Harmony-corrected components were used for clustering.

## Cell annotation
Clusters were then compared by calculating the Wilcoxon rank sum test statistic and area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC) for all genes (wilcoxauc, presto, R). Cells were then annotated on the basis of expert inspection of cluster features. Select cell populations were extracted and reprocessed using the procedure and parameters previously described to further annotate cell subsets. The following groups of cell populations were further detailed using this subclustering procedure: cycling cells, B and T/NK cells, AMs and myeloid cells, myeloid cells, and T/NK cells.
Among the T and NK cells, we identified 13 distinct cell states, including PRF1- and GZMB-expressing effector CD8+ T cells (n = 8088 cells), GZMK- and EOMES-expressing CD8+ T cells (n = 4354), IFNG- and TNF-expressing polyfunctional T cells (n = 1460), TIGIT-expressing regulatory T cells (n = 497), and IRF4- and CD28-expressing helper CD4+ T cells (n = 6540) ( , and table S5). NK cells were marked by the expression of GNLY, KLRC1, and KLRB1. Several transcription factors implicated in T cell activation and polarization were defining markers of cell states, including IRF4, RBPJ, BATF, NR4A3, and EGR2 ( – ). Activated T cells were primarily defined by genes associated with the expression of JUN and FOSB along with ATF3 and several heat shock proteins, potentially representing acutely activated T cell subsets from the BAL ( ). CD69, a widely used marker of T cell state, was most expressed in activated and polyfunctional T cells ( , ).

## Cellular abundance
Cell abundance analysis was performed using two different methods. First, we compared fractional abundances between groups using the Mann-Whitney U test. Second, we used a generalized linear model to model the observed counts. Counts for each cell type were extracted across specimens and the comparison variable of interest, such as quaternary group. Paired observations of a cell type and other cell types within each sample were modeled using two interaction terms, cell type × sample type and cell type × specimen. Cell type probabilities, odds ratios, and P values were then calculated using the emmeans package in R to compare the cell type × specimen model. To visualize abundance changes, we plot the odds ratio, representing the chance of observing the cell type under one condition relative to the contrasting condition and the Benjamini-Hochberg–adjusted P value.

## T cell signatures
Gene signature scores were calculated using AddModuleScore_UCell in R ( ). These scores represent the collective relative ranking of genes within individual cells. Lung residency genes were derived from a blood/tissue-resolved atlas ( ), highlighted in that publication’s figure 2C. These genes are CD69, CREM2, RGS2, SLA, NFE2L2, RGS1, LMNA, RGCC, DUSP6, and SOCS1. Module 2 genes were derived from a previously published study ( ), consisting of 173 genes.

## Identifying gene associations with vaccine group
Within a specific unstimulated or stimulated cell type, we performed a differential expression test between a specific vaccine group and other groups. This was applied to unstimulated and PPD-stimulated T cells and unstimulated and PPD-stimulated AMs/MCs.

## PPD-response signatures
We performed differential expression analysis (logistic regression with study and route as latent variables) between unstimulated and PPD-stimulated cells within selected cell types (AMs and T cells) to define the general response to PPD. We defined a PPD-response signature by taking the top 50 genes based on log2 fold change after filtering for high-confidence cell type–specific genes. Gene signature scores were calculated using AddModuleScore_UCell in R. We then summarized the score within each batch and calculated the difference in average score across the vaccine groups using nonparametric Mann-Whitney U tests. To define the number of differentially expressed genes between unstimulated and PPD-stimulated samples within each T cell subset, the same procedure described above was used but within each T cell subset.

## Gene fold change differences across groups after PPD stimulation
To identify genes that were differentially up-regulated after PPD stimulation across groups, genes were first filtered on their expression in the cell subset of interest. The gene expression differences and fold changes were then calculated per sample on a per group basis.

## Cell-cell interactions analysis
CellChat (v1.1.3) was used on a subset of the global dataset. Cycling cells were removed and 1000 random cells were used per cell type. Macaque gene symbols were converted to human symbols based on 1:1 orthologs from the Ensembl BioMart database. Cell communication probabilities were computed as described ( ) using the human database. Interaction strengths and ligand-receptor probabilities were visualized. Only the top three target cell-sender cell interactions were considered for each ligand-receptor calculation. Expression score represents the average expression across all cells of genes within ligand-receptor pairs minus the average expression of randomly selected control genes within representative expression bins. Ligand-receptor pairs were selected by identifying the most associated signaling genes and extracting all genes within the respective ligand-receptor pairs. The “T cell–myeloid cell interaction” signature composed of TNFSF8, TNFRSF8, CCL20, CCR6, LTA, TNFRSF14, CD80, CD28, cytotoxic T lymphocyte-associated protein 4 (CTLA4), CD40LG, CD40, TNFRSF1B, TNF, myelin protein zero like 1 (MPZL1), TNFSF14, intercellular adhesion molecule–2 (ICAM-2), integrin subunit alpha L (ITGAL), integrin subunit beta 2 (ITGB2), CD226, CD274, neural cell adhesion molecule 1 (NCAM1), IFNG, IFNGR1, and IFNGR2. Polyfunctional T cells activated T cells, helper CD4+ T cells, mregDCs, cDC1s, neutrophils, monocytes, AMs, and recruited macrophages were used.

## Gene set enrichment
Gene sets were input to Enrichr ( ). The MSigDB Hallmark database was used. Results were visualized in R as described in figure legends.

## Mass cytometry (CyTOF)
We used a recently optimized 42-parameter mass cytometry (CyTOF) designed to assess cellular and functional phenotypes of leukocytes in BAL of rhesus macaques ( ). Specifically, we analyzed cryopreserved BAL from a cohort of unvaccinated rhesus macaques (n = 5) and intravenous BCG–vaccinated macaques (n = 6; four animals with matched samples at 4 and 8 weeks after vaccination and two animals with BAL only for the week 4 time point). Total macrophages and macrophage subsets were identified using the following markers: CD68, CD206, CD163, CD14, CD11c, CD11b, HLA-DR and CD69. We also quantified the production of cytokines (CCL4, IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF) after stimulation with Mtb cell lysate (strain H37Rv, 100 mg/ml; BEI Resources, catalog NR-14822) at 37°C for a total of 18 hours. After 12 hours, brefeldin A (Sigma-Aldrich) was added at a final concentration of 10 μg/ml along with 1× GolgiStop Protein Transport Inhibitor (Thermo Fisher Scientific) for the remaining 6 hours of the incubation. Stained cells were acquired on a Helios instrument (Fluidigm) and normalized using EQ Four Element Calibration Beads (Fluidigm), as previously described ( ). Data were analyzed using FlowJo (v10, Tree Star) and in R version 4.4.1.

## Statistical analysis
All reported P values are from two-sided comparisons. Pairwise comparisons were performed in all instances using the Mann-Whitney U test. The Benjamini-Hochberg procedure was used to correct for multiple hypothesis testing. The Kruskal-Wallis test was used to compare sample distributions. Where applicable, all comparisons were performed by averaging cellular metrics within each batch and comparing batches, known as pseudobulking. This method was performed for the expression of specific genes and signature scores. P < 0.05 was considered significant. Adjusted P values are denoted as follows: *P < 0.05; **P < 0.01; ***P < 0.001; ****P < 0.0001. Statistical analyses were performed in R and RStudio.
