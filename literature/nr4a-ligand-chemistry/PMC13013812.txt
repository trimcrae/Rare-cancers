## Abstract
Bilirubin, a byproduct of heme metabolism, is traditionally recognized for its antioxidant properties. However, its accumulation under pathological conditions can result in severe clinical manifestations, including neurotoxicity. Here, we report that bilirubin acts as an activator of receptor-interacting protein kinase 3 (RIPK3), inducing necroptosis and contributing to neurotoxic effects. Unlike the canonical pathway, in which RIPK3-mediated necroptosis is dependent on RIPK1, bilirubin directly binds to the D161 and S102 residues within the kinase domain of RIPK3. This binding facilitates RIPK3 oligomerization and autophosphorylation, leading to the activation of MLKL, the primary effector of necroptosis. Notably, bilirubin-induced necroptosis does not require the RHIM domain of RIPK3 or other RHIM-containing proteins, such as ZBP1 and TRIF. Functionally, the depletion of Ripk3 reduces bilirubin-induced neurotoxicity in murine models. These findings reveal a previously unrecognized mechanism of RIPK3-mediated non-canonical necroptosis, establishing it as a key mediator of bilirubin-induced neurotoxicity.

## Introduction
Bilirubin is a metabolite primarily produced by the breakdown of red blood cells. Under normal physiological conditions, bilirubin is processed by the liver and excreted from the body to maintain intracellular concentrations at low levels, which is beneficial for overall health. At these low concentrations, bilirubin exhibits antioxidant properties that may confer neuroprotective effects, particularly in hippocampal neurons, by mitigating oxidative stress , . However, when present at elevated levels, unconjugated bilirubin can become neurotoxic. Bilirubin is highly lipophilic in unconjugated (free) form, allowing it to passively diffuse across lipid-rich biological barriers, including the blood–brain barrier (BBB) . Several pathological conditions facilitate its penetration into the central nervous system (CNS). For example, in neonatal kernicterus, elevated levels of unbound bilirubin can exceed the binding capacity of albumin, leading to an increase in free bilirubin capable of crossing the BBB. Additionally, systemic inflammation (e.g., in sepsis) and vascular dysfunction (e.g., in neonatal jaundice) can compromise BBB integrity, further promoting bilirubin entry into the CNS . Once in the CNS or peripheral tissues, bilirubin can enter cells via passive diffusion or through membrane transporters . This is especially true in newborns, where excessive free bilirubin can lead to kernicterus, a form of bilirubin-induced brain damage , . Additionally, high levels of unconjugated bilirubin can contribute to hepatic encephalopathy through enterohepatic recirculation . Despite the established link between elevated bilirubin levels and neurological damage , the precise molecular mechanisms underlying bilirubin-induced neurotoxicity remain poorly understood.
Necroptosis, a regulated form of necrotic cell death characterized by the disruption of plasma membranes, is involved in various human inflammatory diseases . Traditionally, necroptosis is initiated through the activation of death receptors, which requires the kinase activities of receptor-interacting serine/threonine kinase 1 (RIPK1) and receptor-interacting serine/threonine kinase 3 (RIPK3). This process culminates in cell death through the activity of the membrane-disrupting protein mixed lineage kinase domain-like (MLKL) – . A critical step in this pathway is the phosphorylation of RIPK3, which leads to its activation. Activated RIPK3 then phosphorylates MLKL, the primary effector of necroptosis. Phosphorylated MLKL oligomerizes and translocates to the plasma membrane, where it forms pores that compromise cellular integrity, resulting in cell death. In addition to death receptors, RIPK3 can also be activated by other stimuli, such as the Z-nucleic acid sensor Z-DNA binding protein 1 (ZBP1), which responds to viral infections, or by TIR domain-containing adapter molecule 1 (TICAM1/TRIF) following Toll-like receptor activation – . RIPK1, ZBP1, and TRIF contain a RIP homotypic interaction motif (RHIM) domain that is crucial for the canonical activation of RIPK3 in necroptosis . However, the mechanisms underlying the activation of necroptosis independent of RHIM-containing proteins remain poorly understood.
In this study, we unexpectedly found that bilirubin-induced neuronal necroptosis occurred independently of classical RHIM domain-containing proteins, including RIPK1, TRIF, and ZBP1. Instead, bilirubin directly interacted with specific amino acid residues (D161 and S102) within the kinase domain of RIPK3, leading to non-canonical activation of RIPK3. This alternative activation pathway subsequently triggered necroptosis-mediated neurotoxicity in both in vitro and in vivo models.

## Results

## Bilirubin induces MLKL-dependent necroptosis
To investigate the type of cell death induced by bilirubin, we performed a screen using inhibitors targeting major regulated cell death pathways. These inhibitors included zVAD-fmk (apoptosis), necrosulfonamide (NSA; necroptosis), ferrostatin-1 (Fer-1; ferroptosis), tetrathiomolybdate (TM; cuproptosis), and N-acetylcysteine (NAC; oxidative stress-induced cell death). Among these, only the MLKL inhibitor NSA significantly suppressed bilirubin-induced cell death in SH-SY5Y human neuroblastoma cells (Supplementary Fig. ). In contrast, inhibitors of apoptosis, ferroptosis, cuproptosis, and oxidative stress had no protective effect (Supplementary Fig. ). These findings suggest that bilirubin induces a form of cell death that is dependent on the necroptosis pathway. To investigate whether bilirubin induces necroptosis, we treated primary rat hippocampal neurons with bilirubin and analyzed its effects using immunofluorescence assays. The results revealed that bilirubin treatment led to the phosphorylation of RIPK3 and MLKL, key initiation events in necrosome formation, in primary neurons (Fig. ). These observations were further validated through western blot analysis, which revealed that bilirubin significantly increased the phosphorylation levels of RIPK3 and MLKL in both primary rat hippocampal neurons (Fig. ) and the mouse dopaminergic neuronal MN9D cell line (Fig. ) in a time- and dose-dependent manner. Consistent with observations in neurons, bilirubin also increased the phosphorylation levels of RIPK3 and MLKL in other well-established necroptosis-competent cell lines, including murine embryonic fibroblasts (MEFs) and L929 cells (Supplementary Fig. ). In contrast, the phosphorylation of RIPK1, the classical upstream activator of RIPK3, was not altered by bilirubin (Fig. ). As a positive control , the classical necroptosis activator TZ (TNF-α/zVAD-fmk) triggered the phosphorylation of RIPK1, RIPK3, and MLKL (Fig. ).
 We further tested the effects of bilirubin on glial cells using astrocytes (C8-D1A) and microglia (BV2). The apoptosis inhibitor zVAD-fmk effectively prevented bilirubin-induced cell death in both cell lines, whereas the RIPK3 inhibitor GSK'872 had no significant effect (Supplementary Fig. ). Moreover, bilirubin treatment did not induce the phosphorylation of RIPK3 or MLKL in either astrocytes or microglia (Supplementary Fig. ). These results indicate that bilirubin-induced necroptosis is likely specific to neurons in vitro.
To determine whether necroptosis contributes to bilirubin-induced neurotoxicity, we utilized MN9D cells in which the MLKL gene was knocked out. Cell death was assessed by propidium iodide (PI) staining and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) release assays. Consistent with the notion that MLKL acts downstream of RIPK3 activation, MLKL knockout abrogated bilirubin-induced phosphorylation of MLKL without affecting RIPK3 phosphorylation (Fig. ). Additionally, the absence of MLKL significantly reduced bilirubin-induced cell death, as evidenced by decreased PI staining and LDH release (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ). Reintroduction of MLKL expression in MLKL-deficient MN9D cells restored their susceptibility to bilirubin-induced cell death, confirming the critical role of MLKL in this process (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ). Furthermore, we investigated the effect of NSA, an inhibitor of human MLKL , on bilirubin-induced cell death in human SH-SY5Y neuroblastoma cells. Treatment with NSA reduced cell death, supporting the involvement of MLKL-mediated necroptosis in bilirubin toxicity (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ).
Caspase-8 can function as a molecular switch between apoptosis and necroptosis. Under normal conditions, it promotes apoptosis by cleaving downstream effector caspases and suppresses necroptosis by cleaving RIPK1 and RIPK3 . However, when caspase activity is inhibited — such as by the pan-caspase inhibitor zVAD-fmk — cells may undergo necroptosis instead. To assess the role of caspase-8 in bilirubin-induced cell death, we generated cells with combined knockout of caspase-8 and MLKL. The results of functional assays involving PI staining and assessment of LDH release revealed that compared with the deletion of MLKL alone, the co-deletion of both caspase-8 and MLKL more effectively suppressed bilirubin-induced cell death (Supplementary Fig. ).
Collectively, these findings suggest that bilirubin induces MLKL-dependent necroptosis, which leads to neurotoxicity.

## Bilirubin induces RIPK1-independent necroptosis
Our phosphorylation assays of RIPK1 and RIPK3 suggest that RIPK1 may not be required for bilirubin-induced necroptosis (Fig. ). To further validate this hypothesis, we used CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing to knock out the genes encoding RIPK3 and RIPK1 in MN9D cells. As anticipated, the deletion of RIPK3 abolished bilirubin-induced phosphorylation of both RIPK3 and MLKL (Fig. ). In contrast, the knockout of RIPK1 did not prevent bilirubin-induced phosphorylation of RIPK3 or MLKL (Fig. ). Moreover, MN9D cells lacking RIPK3, but not RIPK1, exhibited resistance to bilirubin-induced cell death (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ). However, RIPK1 deletion rescued cell death triggered by TNFα combined with zVAD-fmk, a well-established inducer of necroptosis (Supplementary Fig. ). Unexpectedly, the deletion of RIPK1 appeared to increase bilirubin-induced cell death, potentially because of the intrinsic role of RIPK1 in the suppression of spontaneous RIPK3 activation . Consistent with RIPK1 knockout, treatment with the RIPK1 inhibitor necrostatin-1 (Nec-1) failed to block bilirubin-induced cell death but effectively inhibited TNFα/zVAD-fmk-induced necroptosis (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ). Additionally, the RIPK3 inhibitor GSK'872, which induces apoptosis , partially mitigated bilirubin-induced cell death. Combined treatment with GSK'872 and the pan-caspase inhibitor zVAD-fmk significantly reduced bilirubin-induced death in MN9D cells and primary rat hippocampal neurons (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ). These results collectively demonstrate that bilirubin-induced necroptosis is dependent on RIPK3 activation but occurs independently of RIPK1.
 We also investigated the necroptosis-inducing potential of bilirubin in non-neuronal cell types. In bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs), the deletion of RIPK3 completely abolished bilirubin-induced cell death and RIPK3 phosphorylation (Supplementary Fig. ). Similarly, in murine embryonic fibroblasts (MEFs) and L929 cells, co-treatment with the RIPK3 inhibitor GSK'872 and the pan-caspase inhibitor zVAD-fmk significantly reduced bilirubin-induced cytotoxicity (Supplementary Fig. ). In HT29 cells, bilirubin-induced cell death was attenuated by the MLKL inhibitor NSA (Supplementary Fig. ). Together, these findings indicate that bilirubin induces necroptosis across multiple RIPK3/MLKL-expressing cell types in vitro.

## Bilirubin directly binds to RIPK3
To identify the key molecular machinery governing bilirubin-induced necroptosis, we synthesized biotin-conjugated bilirubin (biotin-bilirubin, as detailed in the Methods section). We found that similar to non-biotin-labeled bilirubin, biotin-bilirubin induced necroptosis in MN9D and mouse HT22 hippocampal neuronal cells, although at higher concentrations. Both bilirubin and biotin-bilirubin induced cell death in HT22 cells (Supplementary Fig. ), and the knockout of RIPK3 or MLKL prevented biotin-bilirubin-induced necroptosis (Supplementary Fig. ). To further investigate the interaction between bilirubin and its potential binding partners, we performed immunoprecipitation experiments using HT22 cell lysates labeled with biotin-bilirubin and pulled down with streptavidin-conjugated beads (Fig. ). Consistent with our previous finding that bilirubin can inhibit the proteasome , Gene Ontology (GO) analysis indicated that the proteasome complex, proteasome accessory complex, and proteasome regulatory particle were significantly enriched in the potential binding proteins of bilirubin (Supplementary Fig. ). Importantly, mass spectrometry and immunoprecipitation assays also revealed RIPK3 as a potential binding protein of bilirubin (Fig. ).
 To validate this interaction, we incubated biotin-bilirubin with cell extracts from both RIPK3-expressing and RIPK3-knockout MN9D cells, followed by pull-down with streptavidin-conjugated beads. Non-biotin-labeled bilirubin competed with biotin-bilirubin for binding to RIPK3, confirming the specificity of this interaction, which was further validated in RIPK3 knockout cells (Fig. ). Moreover, the RIPK3 inhibitor GSK'872 suppressed the binding of biotin-bilirubin to RIPK3, which further confirmed the binding between bilirubin and RIPK3 (Fig. ). To explore this interaction in more detail, we used UnaG, a fluorescent protein that fluoresces specifically upon binding to bilirubin . Using wild-type (WT) UnaG and an N57A point mutant (which disrupts hydrogen bonding with bilirubin) (Supplementary Fig. ) , we observed increased colocalization of UnaG with RIPK3 in response to bilirubin treatment in wild-type but not N57A mutant primary neurons (Fig. ). These findings were further corroborated in MN9D and HT22 cells (Supplementary Fig. ).
We also conducted fluorescence spectroscopy to assess the conformational changes in the recombinant RIPK3 protein upon bilirubin binding. A noticeable spectral shift (from 328 nm to 326 nm) was observed upon bilirubin treatment, indicating a direct interaction between bilirubin and RIPK3 (Supplementary Fig. ). To further quantify the stability of RIPK3 upon bilirubin binding, we performed a cellular thermal shift assay (CETSA). This assay tests protein stability in response to ligand binding by exposing protein lysates to increasing temperatures . Cell lysates treated with bilirubin or the DMSO control were subjected to repeated freeze‒thaw cycles across a temperature gradient (43 °C–64 °C). The results revealed that bilirubin increased the thermal stability of RIPK3 but not RIPK1, supporting the direct binding of bilirubin to RIPK3 (Fig. ). Additionally, when we incubated the biotin-bilirubin compound with recombinant RIPK3 and analyzed the biotin signal via SDS‒PAGE, we observed that bilirubin directly binds to RIPK3, whereas no binding was detected with RIPK1, ZBP1, or TRIF (Fig. ).

## Bilirubin promotes RIPK3 polymerization and activation
Although the phosphorylation of the RIPK3 activation loop is linked to RIPK1 activation in classical necroptosis, RIPK1 does not directly phosphorylate RIPK3 . However, RIPK3 can undergo intermolecular autophosphorylation, which is facilitated by the proximity of RIPK3 molecules within oligomers, subsequently recruiting MLKL to propagate necroptotic signaling . These findings suggest that bilirubin binding to RIPK3 might promote RIPK3 oligomerization and lead to its autophosphorylation. Our findings support this hypothesis; upon bilirubin stimulation, RIPK3 and MLKL were detected within a high-molecular-weight complex, indicating that bilirubin induces the oligomerization of both RIPK3 and MLKL (Fig. ). In addition, bilirubin slightly increased the oligomerization of the recombinant RIPK3 protein (Supplementary Fig. ).
 The phosphorylation of RIPK3 at Ser-227 is critical for its interaction with MLKL, which leads to MLKL phosphorylation and the subsequent propagation of necroptotic signaling , . To further investigate whether bilirubin increases the interaction between RIPK3 and MLKL, we conducted immunoprecipitation experiments. These experiments revealed that bilirubin selectively increased the binding of RIPK3 to MLKL but did not affect its binding to other necroptosis regulators, such as RIPK1, ZBP1, or TRIF (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ). Similarly, colocalization studies revealed an increase in the interaction between RIPK3 and MLKL in response to bilirubin treatment in primary neurons and MN9D cells (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ), further indicating that bilirubin promotes RIPK3 oligomerization and its subsequent binding to MLKL. In addition, we treated cell lysates with increasing concentrations of bilirubin in the presence of ATP and MgCl₂ to ensure optimal kinase activity. After 30 min of incubation, we observed that ATP and MgCl₂ alone promoted the phosphorylation of RIPK3 and its downstream target MLKL, and this effect was further increased by bilirubin treatment (Fig. ). In contrast, depletion of ATP using the mitochondrial ATP synthase inhibitor oligomycin, but not the glycolysis inhibitor 2-deoxyglucose (2DG), reduced bilirubin-induced phosphorylation of RIPK3 and MLKL (Supplementary Fig. ). These findings support the conclusion that bilirubin directly increases RIPK3 kinase activity in an ATP-dependent manner, leading to MLKL phosphorylation and necroptotic signaling.
Next, we transfected cells with plasmids encoding either full-length RIPK3 or a truncated kinase domain (amino acids 22–292) to examine bilirubin binding. Interestingly, we observed that compared with the full-length protein, the isolated kinase domain bound bilirubin more readily and exhibited increased oligomerization. We speculate that this difference arises because full-length RIPK3 adopts a relatively closed conformation, whereas the isolated kinase domain is fully exposed, making it more accessible for bilirubin binding (Fig. ).

## Molecular modeling of the binding of bilirubin with RIPK3
To estimate the binding affinity of bilirubin for RIPK3, induced-fit docking calculations were performed. The XP docking score for the human RIPK3 (hRIPK3)–bilirubin complex was –11.9 kcal/mol, whereas the XP docking score for the mouse RIPK3 (mRIPK3) –bilirubin complex was –11.4 kcal/mol (Fig. ). In the hRIPK3–bilirubin complex, bilirubin formed hydrogen bonds with the residues Arg37, Lys50, Ser101, and Asp160. Similarly, in the mRIPK3-bilirubin complex, bilirubin interacted via hydrogen bonds with Gly32, Lys51, Ser102, and Asp161 (Fig. ). These findings suggest that bilirubin interacts with specific amino acid residues in the kinase domain, including Lys50/51, Ser101/102, and Asp160/161, in both mouse and human RIPK3, indicating a strong binding affinity for RIPK3.
 To further explore the significance of these interactions, we generated RIPK3 mutants by substituting lysine at position 51 with alanine (K51A), serine at position 102 with alanine (S102A), and aspartic acid at position 161 with asparagine (D161N). These mutant plasmids were transfected into RIPK3-knockout MN9D cells. The results demonstrated that the S102A and D161N mutations disrupted the binding of bilirubin to RIPK3, suggesting that Ser102 and Asp161 are critical for this interaction (Fig. ). In contrast, the K51A mutation and alterations within the RHIM domain did not affect bilirubin binding (Fig. ). Additionally, UnaG immunofluorescence assays confirmed that Ser102 and Asp161 are key residues required for the binding of bilirubin to RIPK3 (Fig. ).
To further confirm the binding site of bilirubin to RIPK3, we performed biotin-bilirubin pull-down assays using purified recombinant human RIPK3, including WT protein and site-directed mutants (K50A, S101A, and D160N). The results revealed that recombinant WT RIPK3 robustly bound to bilirubin, whereas all three mutants failed to exhibit detectable bilirubin binding (Supplementary Fig. ). Notably, the recombinant WT RIPK3 protein appeared to readily form dimers, whereas the kinase domain mutants predominantly existed as monomers. However, the precise molecular mechanism underlying this difference remains to be fully elucidated. The minor differences in binding sites observed between the recombinant protein-based assays and cellular experiments are likely due to variations in protein context or conformation. Nonetheless, these results confirm that bilirubin specifically interacts with the Ser101 (human)/Ser102 (mouse) and Asp160 (human)/Asp161 (mouse) residues of RIPK3.

## The RHIM domain of RIPK3 is dispensable for bilirubin-induced necroptosis
Both RIPK3 and RIPK1 contain RHIM domains, which facilitate their interaction with each other . To further elucidate the molecular mechanisms underlying bilirubin-induced necroptosis, we investigated whether the RHIM domain of RIPK3 is essential for bilirubin-induced neurotoxicity. We generated MN9D cells with endogenous RIPK3 knockout and expression of mutant RIPK3 proteins: one with a D161N mutation in the kinase domain and another with mutations in the RHIM domain. Our results revealed that both the D161N and RHIM domain mutations disrupted necroptosis activation in response to treatment with TNFα/zVAD-fmk, a classical necroptosis inducer (Fig. ). However, only the D161N mutation, and not the RHIM domain mutation, inhibited bilirubin-induced phosphorylation of MLKL (Fig. ). Moreover, bilirubin-induced cell death was observed in cells expressing the RHIM domain mutant of RIPK3 but not in cells expressing the D161N mutant, whereas TNFα/zVAD-fmk failed to induce cell death in both mutant cell lines (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ).
 Additionally, adapter proteins such as TRIF and ZBP1, which also contain RHIM domains and interact with the RHIM domain of RIPK3, can mediate RIPK1-independent activation of RIPK3 , , . To explore their potential involvement in bilirubin-induced necroptosis, we knocked down ZBP1 (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ) and TRIF (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ) in MN9D cells. The knockdown of neither protein affected bilirubin-induced cell death or the phosphorylation of RIPK3 and MLKL. In contrast, ZBP1 knockdown blocked cell death induced by CBL0137, a necroptosis inducer that forms left-handed Z-DNA (Supplementary Fig. ) . Moreover, we observed that bilirubin strongly bound to RIPK3, whereas it did not bind to RIPK1, ZBP1, or TRIF (Fig. ). Moreover, the loss of RIPK1, ZBP1, or TRIF did not reduce the binding of bilirubin to RIPK3 (Fig. ). Furthermore, unlike IFNβ, which upregulates ZBP1 expression , bilirubin treatment did not alter ZBP1 expression in either MN9D or HT22 cells (Supplementary Fig. ).
These findings provide further evidence that the kinase domain of RIPK3, rather than the RHIM domain, is critical for bilirubin-induced necroptosis.

## The catalytic activity and dimerization of RIPK3 are indispensable for bilirubin-induced necroptosis
Next, we investigated whether the kinase activity of RIPK3 is essential for bilirubin-induced necroptosis. To this end, we used the RIPK3 V36F mutant, which lacks the intrinsic kinase activity of RIPK3 , and expressed it in RIPK3 knockout MN9D cells (Fig. ). We found that both bilirubin and T/Z failed to induce cell death in cells expressing the V36F mutant, in contrast to cells expressing wild-type RIPK3 (Fig. ). These results demonstrate that bilirubin-induced necroptosis is dependent on the kinase activity of RIPK3.
 Previous findings have shown that dimerization of the RIPK3 kinase domain promotes its activation via cis-autophosphorylation, independent of RHIM-mediated interactions . This dimerization is mediated by the surface residues R69, R96, and H156 . To test whether the dimerization of RIPK3 is required for the bilirubin-induced phosphorylation of RIPK3 and necroptosis, we generated R69H, R96G, and H156G RIPK3 mutants and expressed them in RIPK3-deficient MN9D cells. These mutations inhibited both bilirubin- and T/Z-induced cell death (Fig. ). In addition, bilirubin and T/Z failed to induce MLKL phosphorylation in RIPK3 knockout cells expressing the V36F, R69H, R96G, or H156G mutants (Fig. ). Together, these results indicate that both the catalytic activity and kinase domain dimerization of RIPK3 are essential for bilirubin-induced RIPK3 activation and necroptosis.

## Bilirubin induces RIPK3-dependent necroptosis in mouse nervous tissue
To further investigate the role of RIPK3 in bilirubin-induced neuronal necroptosis in vivo, we performed intraventricular injections of saline or bilirubin in age- and sex-matched 6-week-old WT and Ripk3–/– mice (Fig. ). Immunofluorescence analysis revealed that bilirubin treatment induced the phosphorylation of RIPK3 and MLKL in the hippocampal and cerebellar tissues of WT mice. In contrast, Ripk3 knockout mice exhibited markedly reduced phosphorylation of both RIPK3 and MLKL, confirming the suppression of necroptosis in the absence of RIPK3 (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ). Moreover, we performed immunofluorescence staining on brain tissue sections to analyze the expression of RIPK3 upstream signaling components, including RIPK1, phosphorylated RIPK1, ZBP1, and TRIF. The results revealed that bilirubin treatment did not affect the expression levels of these key upstream proteins of RIPK3 (Supplementary Fig. ).
 Furthermore, an increased number of terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase dUTP nick-end labeling-positive (TUNEL+) cells, which are indicative of DNA fragmentation and cell death, was observed in the hippocampal tissue of WT mice following bilirubin injection. However, this increase in TUNEL+ cells was attenuated in the hippocampal and cerebellar tissues of Ripk3–/– mice, suggesting a protective effect against bilirubin-induced cell death (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ). Additionally, the number and staining intensity of Nissl bodies, which are markers of neuronal health, were decreased in the hippocampal and cerebellar tissues of WT mice treated with bilirubin, whereas Ripk3–/– mice exhibited a preservation of Nissl bodies, indicating reduced neurotoxicity (Fig. ; Supplementary Fig. ). Additionally, the mRNA expression level of the pro-inflammatory cytokine IL-1α was elevated in the brain tissues of WT mice following bilirubin injection but markedly reduced in those of Ripk3–/– mice, suggesting that RIPK3 deficiency mitigates bilirubin-induced inflammatory responses (Supplementary Fig. ).
Next, we sought to determine whether bilirubin-induced neurotoxicity occurs primarily in neurons, immune cells, or both. To address this, we performed co-immunostaining of phosphorylated RIPK3 and MLKL with the neuronal marker NeuN and the microglial marker IBA1. The results revealed that p-RIPK3 and p-MLKL colocalized with both NeuN-positive neurons and IBA1-positive microglia in brain sections from wild-type mice treated with bilirubin. In contrast, this co-localization was absent in RIPK3-knockout mice (Supplementary Fig. ). These findings indicate that bilirubin induces RIPK3-dependent necroptosis in both neurons and microglia in vivo.
Bilirubin exists in two main forms under physiological conditions: unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin and conjugated (direct) bilirubin, the latter being formed through glucuronic acid conjugation in the liver . In this study, we focused on unconjugated bilirubin, which is lipid soluble and biologically active. To distinguish its effects from those of its non-toxic counterpart, we also examined conjugated bilirubin, a structurally related but physiologically inert analog .
Our experiments revealed that conjugated bilirubin did not induce cytotoxicity in MN9D neuronal cells, as shown by PI staining analysis (Supplementary Fig. ). Moreover, conjugated bilirubin did not compete with biotin-bilirubin for binding to RIPK3 (Supplementary Fig. ), suggesting a distinct binding mechanism.
Molecular docking analysis further supported this conclusion: the predicted binding residues for conjugated bilirubin (V27, K29, S146, R309, N312, and R313 in human RIPK3; R38, K51, M98, K145, S147, and H313 in mouse RIPK3) are distinct from those of unconjugated bilirubin (S101 and D160 in human RIPK3; S102 and D161 in mouse RIPK3) (Supplementary Fig. ). These differences suggest that although conjugated bilirubin may associate with RIPK3, it does not engage the critical residues required for RIPK3 activation.
Consistent with these findings, bilirubin conjugation failed to induce RIPK3 or MLKL phosphorylation, as determined by immunofluorescence assays (Supplementary Fig. ). Furthermore, Nissl staining revealed no evidence of neurotoxicity in the hippocampal or cerebellar tissues of mice treated with conjugated bilirubin. There was no reduction in the number or intensity of Nissl bodies, in contrast to the damage observed with unconjugated bilirubin (Supplementary Fig. ).
Together, these findings demonstrate that unconjugated bilirubin, but not conjugated bilirubin, induces RIPK3-dependent necroptosis and neural injury, highlighting the functional specificity of bilirubin’s unconjugated form in mediating neurotoxicity.

## Bilirubin induces RIPK3-dependent neuronal necroptosis in vivo
To further validate the pathophysiological relevance of bilirubin-induced necroptosis in vivo, we used a mouse model of liver dysfunction, a condition that commonly results in hyperbilirubinemia. Under systemic inflammation, lipopolysaccharide (LPS) can compromise BBB integrity, thereby facilitating bilirubin entry into the brain. We disrupted the BBB by intraperitoneal injection of LPS (54 mg/kg) and assessed barrier permeability using Evans blue dye . As shown in Fig. , LPS effectively disrupted the BBB, and Ripk3 knockout did not affect LPS-induced BBB permeability.
 Next, we established a liver injury model by treating mice with varying doses of CCl₄. We identified 50% CCl₄ as the optimal dose for inducing substantial liver injury and elevated serum bilirubin levels without excessive mortality (Supplementary Fig. ). Ripk3 knockout did not alter serum bilirubin levels in mice co-treated with CCl₄ and LPS (Supplementary Fig. ), indicating that RIPK3 does not influence bilirubin production or systemic accumulation.
Using LC-MS/MS, we detected elevated levels of unconjugated bilirubin in the brains of both WT and Ripk3 knockout mice following CCl₄ + LPS treatment (Supplementary Fig. ). However, phosphorylation of RIPK3 and MLKL was observed in the hippocampal neurons of WT mice but not in those of Ripk3-deficient mice (Fig. ), supporting the RIPK3-dependent activation of necroptosis in the CNS.
Functionally, Ripk3 knockout mice exhibited improved motor activity compared with that of WT controls after CCl₄ + LPS administration (Supplementary Videos , ). Furthermore, TUNEL staining revealed reduced neuronal death in the hippocampus of Ripk3 knockout mice (Supplementary Fig. ), and Nissl staining demonstrated a lower degree of neuronal injury in RIPK3-deficient hippocampi than in WT hippocampi (Fig. ).
Taken together, these results demonstrate that RIPK3-mediated necroptosis contributes to bilirubin-induced neurotoxicity in the context of liver dysfunction and compromised BBB integrity.

## Discussion
Necroptosis is a form of regulated cell death associated with pathological damage in various diseases. In this study, we identified a non-canonical mechanism of necroptosis mediated by bilirubin. Specifically, we demonstrated that bilirubin directly binds to key residues (D161 and S102) within the kinase domain of RIPK3 independent of the RHIM domain, thereby activating RIPK3 and triggering necroptosis (Fig. ). This direct interaction with RIPK3 may induce conformational changes that increase its kinase activity, leading to the subsequent activation of MLKL and necroptosis. However, we cannot entirely exclude the possibility that bilirubin may interact with other cellular components that indirectly contribute to RIPK3 activation.
 Our findings align with those of recent studies showing that osmotic stress can also induce necroptosis independently of the RHIM domain. In this context, RIPK3 is directly activated by an increase in the intracellular pH mediated by the activation of cell membrane sodium-hydrogen exchanger 1 (NHE1), leading to MLKL phosphorylation and necroptosis . These findings suggest that various stress signals can activate RIPK3 through non-canonical pathways that do not require RHIM domain interactions.
Bilirubin has been reported to inhibit the deubiquitinases USP14 and UCHL5 and reduce the activity of 20S proteasomes, contributing to its neurotoxic effects . Additionally, unconjugated bilirubin can induce apoptosis through increased oxidative stress . However, in our study, neither the pan-caspase inhibitor zVAD-fmk nor the antioxidant NAC blocked bilirubin-induced neurotoxicity (Supplementary Fig. ). These findings suggest that bilirubin might modulate apoptotic pathways downstream of or in parallel with necroptosis. Apoptosis is typically considered a non-inflammatory, self-limiting form of cell death , whereas necroptosis is often associated with inflammation, which is consistent with the inflammatory characteristics observed in bilirubin-induced pathology , , .
Furthermore, the effects of bilirubin on cell death may vary depending on the cell type. Our current research focuses primarily on the effects of bilirubin on neuronal cells, including those in the hippocampus, midbrain, and cerebellum, which are highly relevant to the nervous system. However, the specific role of bilirubin in other tissues or cell types remains to be elucidated. It is well known that albumin plays a critical protective role against bilirubin toxicity through high-affinity binding to unconjugated bilirubin . The differential protein content between the systemic circulation and the CNS is a key factor in determining tissue vulnerability. Specifically, albumin is abundant in the bloodstream (3.5–5.0 g/dL), allowing for efficient sequestration of unconjugated bilirubin and preventing its diffusion into most peripheral tissues, including necroptosis-prone organs such as the skin and gastrointestinal tract. When bound to albumin, bilirubin is unable to cross cell membranes and is rendered biologically inactive. In contrast, CSF has a very low protein concentration and limited bilirubin-binding capacity. Under normal conditions, the BBB further restricts bilirubin entry into the CNS. However, in pathological states such as neonatal hyperbilirubinemia or inflammation, the integrity of the BBB can be compromised, enabling unbound (free) bilirubin to cross into the brain, particularly into regions such as the hippocampus, which are especially vulnerable to bilirubin-induced toxicity.
An important consideration for future research is the ability of compounds to cross the BBB. Currently, no clinical studies have evaluated whether existing RIPK3 inhibitors, such as GSK'872, GSK'842, GSK'840, or UH15-38, can effectively penetrate the BBB , . Therefore, the development of BBB-permeable RIPK3 inhibitors for targeting brain inflammation represents a promising direction for future investigations.
Notably, our current animal model involved the direct intracerebral injection of bilirubin (Fig. ), which does not fully recapitulate the physiological conditions of disease-associated hyperbilirubinemia. However, our previous clinical case‒control study revealed that the indirect bilirubin/albumin (IBIL/ALB) ratio was the strongest risk factor for hepatic encephalopathy in patients with liver failure, suggesting that free bilirubin may be a pathogenic driver of neurological impairment in this setting . Consistent with these findings, the results of the current study demonstrated that bilirubin induces RIPK3-dependent necroptosis in the nervous tissue of a mouse liver dysfunction model (Fig. ). However, animal models cannot fully recapitulate human disease, and whether RIPK3 is similarly activated in the brains of patients with hepatic encephalopathy remains uncertain. Genetically modified models such as Gunn rats, which lack the UGT1A1 enzyme, could be used to study bilirubin-induced neurotoxicity. However, these models may exhibit systemic effects through unknown pathways, and homozygous individuals often present with chronic health issues, which could complicate the interpretation of nervous system changes. Future studies using human brain tissues or other disease-relevant models will be important to validate these findings and further assess the therapeutic potential of targeting RIPK3 in bilirubin-induced neurotoxicity.
In summary, our study reveals a novel, RIPK3-dependent pathway of bilirubin-induced necroptosis that does not rely on the RHIM domain, highlighting a potential therapeutic target for mitigating neurotoxic damage in conditions associated with elevated bilirubin levels.

## Materials and methods

## Reagents
The reagents used are listed in Supplementary Table .

## Preparation and formulation of bilirubin for in vitro and in vivo experiments
For the in vitro experiments, bilirubin (Sigma, catalog B4126; 99% purity) was protected from light throughout its handling. The required amount was weighed in a biosafety cabinet and dissolved in DMSO to prepare a 20 mM stock solution. The stock solutions were stored in the dark and added to the cell cultures under light-protected conditions.
For the in vivo experiments, bilirubin (Sigma, catalog B4126; 99% purity) was weighed and dissolved in sodium hydroxide to 40 mg/mL, after which the pH was adjusted to 7.0 with concentrated hydrochloric acid, and the solution was subsequently diluted with normal saline to a final concentration of 10 mg/mL. For intracerebroventricular injection, 5 µL or 10 µL of bilirubin was delivered using a microsyringe at 40 nL/min, and the incision was subsequently sutured. All the experiments included vehicle-only controls.

## Extracting primary neurons from the rat hippocampus
The tissue culture plates were coated with 0.01% PDL solution (Thermo Fisher Scientific, A3890401) and incubated overnight at room temperature. After incubation, the PDL solution was removed, and the plates were rinsed with sterile double-distilled water, dried, and set aside. The rats were rapidly decapitated, and the brains were isolated in calcium-free Hanks’ balanced salt solution (HBSS; Macgene, cc025). The tissue was cut into approximately 1 mm3 blocks using tissue scissors in HBSS. The hippocampus was incubated with 0.25% trypsin at 37 °C for 5 min. The cells were subsequently washed with 1 mL of FBS to terminate digestion, followed by centrifugation at 800 rpm for 5 min. The supernatant was discarded, and the cell pellet was resuspended in medium, followed by centrifugation at 1000 rpm for 5 min. After the supernatant was discarded, the cell pellet was resuspended in complete medium supplemented with 50× B27 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 17504044), 100× GlutaMAX™ (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 35050061), and 20% FBS in DMEM/F12 medium (Gibco, C11330500BT). Cells were plated on PDL pre-coated 12-well tissue culture plates or 6 cm tissue culture dishes for subsequent experimental analyses.

## Cell culture and treatment
Cells lines MN9D, SK-SY5Y, HT22, L929, HT29, and C8-D1A (American Type Culture Collection (ATCC): SCC281, CRL-2266, CL-0595, CL-0137, CL-0118, and CRL-2541, respectively) and the BV2 cell line (CRC, SCSP-5208) were cultured in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle medium (DMEM; Gibco, C11995500BT) or Roswell Park Memorial Institute (RPMI)-1640 medium (Gibco, C11875500BT) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and penicillin‒streptomycin (100 U/mL) at 37 °C in an incubator containing 5% CO2. All cells were mycoplasma free and authenticated using short tandem repeat DNA profiling analysis. Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) was used to prepare stock solutions of drugs. The final concentration of DMSO in the working solutions applied to the cells was < 0.01%. DMSO (0.01%) was used as a vehicle control in all the cell culture assays. Unless otherwise specified, bilirubin-treated cells were maintained in RPMI-1640 medium supplemented with 1% FBS.

## Transfection of sgRNAs, shRNAs, and plasmids
MN9D cells were infected with lentiviruses encoding RIPK3, MLKL, RIPK1, caspase 8, ZBP1, TRIF sgRNA or shRNA (Supplementary Table ), and positive cells were selected with puromycin (5 µg/mL). MN9D RIPK3ko cells were transfected with plasmids encoding HA-RIPK3 (WT, K51A, S102A, D161N, AAAA, and RIPK3 22–292 aa). MN9D MLKLko cells were transfected with plasmids encoding HA-MLKL. MN9D, HT22, and primary rat hippocampal neurons were transfected with Flag-UnaG protein (WT or N57A).

## Cell death assay
Cell death was analyzed using dual fluorescence staining with Hoechst 33342 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 62246) and propidium iodide (PI). Briefly, cells were seeded in 24-well plates at a density of 1 × 105 cells/well and allowed to adhere overnight. Following treatment, the cells were co-stained with 1.5 µL of Hoechst 33342 and 2.5 µL of PI for 30 min at 37 °C in a 5% CO₂ incubator. Fluorescence images were captured using a fluorescence microscope (ZEISS, Axio Observer 5).

## LDH assay
Cells were plated into 96-well cell culture plates and maintained at 80%–90% confluency prior to treatment. Bilirubin was added at the indicated concentrations, with vehicle controls included. After treatment, the cells were centrifuged at 400 × g for 5 min. The supernatants were collected into centrifuge tubes, and 150 μL of LDH release reagent (Beyotime, Cat# C0016; 1:10 diluted in PBS) was added to each sample. Afterward, the samples were vortexed gently to ensure homogeneous mixing and incubated under standard culture conditions (37 °C, 5% CO₂) for 1 h. After incubation, the samples were centrifuged again (400 × g, 5 min), and 120 μL aliquots were transferred to a new 96-well plate for sample analysis.

## Western blot analysis
Cells were lysed on ice with cell lysis buffer (Beyotime, P0013) containing protease inhibitor (Sigma‒Aldrich, P8340) for 10 min. Lysates were centrifuged at 12,000 × g for 15 min at 4 °C, and protein concentrations in the supernatant were quantified using a bicinchoninic acid (BCA) assay (BCA; Thermo Fisher Scientific, 23225). A total of 20–40 μg of protein per sample was separated by 12% or 8% sodium dodecyl sulfate‒polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS‒PAGE) and transferred to polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) membranes. Afterward, the PVDF membranes were blocked with 5% nonfat dry milk in PBS (Thermo Fisher Scientific, AM9625) supplemented with 0.1% Tween 20 (Sigma‒Aldrich, SLCG3047; PBST) for 1 h and incubated with primary antibodies at 4 °C overnight. After three washes with PBST, the PVDF membranes were incubated with horseradish peroxidase-conjugated goat anti-rabbit or anti-mouse immunoglobulin G (IgG) secondary antibodies (Cell Signaling Technology, 7076S or 7074S) at room temperature for 1 h. Chemiluminescence substrate was detected using western blotting luminol reagent (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, sc-2048).

## Immunoprecipitation analysis
Cells were lysed in ice-cold cell lysis buffer (Beyotime, P0013), and the lysates were subsequently centrifuged at 13,000 × g for 15 min at 4 °C. The protein concentrations were subsequently determined using the BCA assay. Before immunoprecipitation, samples containing equal amounts of proteins were precleared with Dynabeads (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 14311D) at 4 °C for 3 h and subsequently incubated with non-specific IgG or specific antibodies (5 μg/mL) in the presence of Dynabeads for 2 h or overnight at 4 °C with gentle shaking. Following incubation, the Dynabeads were washed with PBS, and the bound proteins were eluted by boiling in loading buffer (Cell Signaling Technology, 56036S) before SDS‒PAGE.

## Mass spectrometry
Biotinylated bilirubin (biotin-bilirubin) and biotin were each incubated with Dynabeads™ M-280 Streptavidin (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 11205D) for 2 h, followed by three washes with PBS for 5 min each. The biotin-bilirubin–streptavidin and biotin–streptavidin magnetic beads were then incubated with the cell lysates overnight at 4 °C. Following overnight incubation, the bound complexes were washed three times with ice-cold PBS and subjected to label-free quantitative mass spectrometry. The protein solution was enzymatically digested into peptides and separated by liquid chromatography. After separation, the peptides were ionized and analyzed by tandem mass spectrometry. The obtained mass spectrometry data were searched and matched against the corresponding database.

## Kinase assay
MN9D cells were cultured in 10-cm culture dishes for 24 h, washed with PBS, and harvested by scraping. The cell suspension was centrifuged at 12,000 rpm for 15 min, and the protein concentration was determined using a BCA assay kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 23225). Afterward, the cell lysates were incubated with 2 mM MgCl₂, 2 mM ATP, and the indicated concentrations of bilirubin at 37 °C for 30 min. The reaction was terminated by the addition of 1× SDS loading buffer, followed by boiling at 100 °C for 10 min prior to SDS‒PAGE analysis.

## Immunofluorescence staining of cultured cells
Cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde (Servicebio, G1101), permeabilized with 0.3% Triton X-100 (Solarbio, T8200), and incubated with primary antibodies in PBS supplemented with 1% bovine serum albumin (BSA; Sigma‒Aldrich, 9048-46-8) overnight at 4 °C. Following three washes with PBS, secondary antibodies were applied. The cells were counterstained with 4’,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI; Abcam, ab104139) for 10 min. After being washed with PBS three times, the sections were mounted with anti-fading medium and covered with a coverslip sealed with nail polish. Immunofluorescence images were acquired using a confocal laser scanning microscope (ZEISS LSM 980).

## Immunofluorescence staining of frozen tissue sections
The frozen slices were equilibrated at 37 °C for 20 min, fixed with fixation solution (Servicebio G1101) for 30 min, and then washed three times with PBS. The sections were subsequently blocked with a sealing solution containing 0.3% Triton (Solarbio, T8200) and 5% BSA (Sigma‒Aldrich, 9048-46-8) for 70 min, followed by three washes with PBS. Primary antibodies (anti-p-MLKL, anti-p-RIPK3, anti-MLKL, or anti-RIPK3) were incubated overnight at 4 °C. After the cells were washed with PBS, the secondary antibody was applied for 1 h, followed by additional washes with PBS. Slices were stained with DAPI for 5 min, washed, and then sealed with an anti-fluorescence quencher before being covered with a glass slide. Immunofluorescence images were acquired using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Leica TRIF + THUNDER with a 10× objective or ZEISS LSM 800 with a 40× objective).

## Cellular thermal shift assay (CETSA)
MN9D cells cultured in 10-cm culture dishes were treated with DMSO or bilirubin for 6 h. Following treatment, the cell suspensions were equally divided into eight aliquots corresponding to the experimental groups. Each aliquot was exposed to precise thermal stress at 43, 46, 49, 52, 55, 58, 61, and 64 °C for 3 min. Postheating samples were subjected to two freeze‒thaw cycles (–80 °C for 12 h to room temperature for 5 min) to induce protein denaturation. Afterward, the cells were centrifuged at 2000 × g for 20 min, and the supernatant was collected for protein quantification. Protein thermostability profiles were analyzed by SDS‒PAGE.

## Biotin-bilirubin precipitation
MN9D cells were seeded in 10 cm dishes, and the cells were collected for lysis the following day. Biotin-bilirubin and streptavidin Dynabeads (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 11205D) were incubated at 4 °C for 2 h to form complexes, which were then incubated with cell lysates at 4 °C overnight. To assess specificity, nonbiotinylated bilirubin was added to the lysate as a competitive control. After overnight incubation, the mixtures were washed four times with lysis buffer and resuspended in 1% SDS loading buffer for sample testing.

## The synthesis method of biotin-bilirubin

 

The mixture of bilirubin 1 (0.1 mmol, 1.0 equiv), biotinylamidoethylacetamide 2 (0.1 mmol, 1.0 equiv), HATU (0.12 mmol, 1.2 equiv), and DIPEA (0.12 mmol, 2.0 equiv) in DCM was stirred at room temperature for 4 h under air. Afterward, chloroform was added to the above mixture, which was subsequently washed with 0.1 M HCl and brine. The resulting organic layer was collected and concentrated in vacuo. Afterward, the concentrate was further washed with methanol to remove residual bilirubin and then centrifuged for 10 min, after which the precipitate was discarded, and the supernatant was evaporated. The obtained residue was purified by flash column chromatography (eluent: CHCl3/MeOH = 85:15) to obtain 3 and 4 as brownish red solids in 45% and 21% yields, respectively.

## Molecular docking
The crystallographic structures of human RIPK3 (hRIPK3) downloaded from the Protein Data Bank (PDB ID: 7MX3) have some missing loops. To repair these missing loops, the advanced homology modeling tool in Maestro 10.7 , was used to construct homologous structures of hRIPK3 by selecting the crystallographic structure as a structural template. The FASTA sequence of hRIPK3 (Q9Y572) was taken from UniProt . The resulting homologous structure of hRIPK3 was subsequently prepared, optimized, and minimized with an OPLS3 force field by using the protein preparation wizard , . Similarly, with the minimized homologous structure of hRIPK3 as a structural template, the homologous structures of mouse RIPK3 (mRIPK3) were constructed using the FASTA sequence Q9QZL0. Finally, the homology structure of mRIPK3 obtained was also prepared, optimized, and minimized with an OPLS3 force field by using the protein preparation wizard.
The 2D structure of bilirubin was manually drawn using the 2D sketcher and was converted to a 3D structure by saving it through the 2D sketcher to the workspace in Maestro. Afterward, the ionization/tautomeric state of bilirubin was generated at a pH = 7 using an Epik (an empirical pKa prediction program) calculation . Finally, the bilirubin with an ionization state was optimized by minimizing the potential energy using default parameters. Induced-fit docking is an ideal method for predicting ligand poses and associated structural changes in a receptor. The position where the original crystal ligand was located was selected as the active site of each protein, and a grid box was generated around it. Automatic Trim side chains based on the B-factor and the Glide extra precision (XP) function were selected , . The bilirubin with an ionization state was respectively docked to the prepared h/mRIPK3 homology structures. The ligand pose with the lowest XP score was selected as the preferred induced-fit docking conformation.

## Animals
All animal care and experiments were conducted in accordance with the Association for Assessment and Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care guidelines (http://www.aaalac.org) and were approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of Guangzhou Medical University (approval number: GY2023-581). The mice were kept under standard pathogen-free conditions with a 12-h light/dark cycle and constant 50%–60% humidity. Mice were allowed access to tap water and free (ad libitum) access to standard laboratory chow during the experimental period. Ripk3–/– mice (cat. no. NM-KO-200619) were purchased from Shanghai Model Organisms Center, Inc.

## Nissl staining
The frozen sections were thawed to room temperature, fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde (Servicebio, G1101) for at least 10 min, and then washed with water for 2 min. The sections were stained with Nissl solution for 2–5 min and washed until they were colorless. Differentiation was performed with 0.1% glacial acetic acid, adjusted according to tissue coloration. Brain tissue should appear as dark blue Nissl bodies against a light blue or colorless background. Excess water was removed, and the sections were heated in a 65 °C oven for at least 4 h to dry. Nissl staining images were acquired using a digital pathological slide scanner (Leica Aperio CS2).

## TUNEL staining
Slides were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde (dissolved in PBS) for 10–15 min, air-dried, and rinsed in water or PBS. A hydrophobic barrier pen was used to outline a 2–3 mm circle boundary around the tissue. Each sample was treated with 100 μL of proteinase K for 10 min, followed by rinsing with PBS. Afterward, 50 μL of equilibration buffer was added and incubated for 10 min. A labeling mixture containing 1 µL of TdT enzyme, 5 µL of FITC-12-dUTP, and 50 µL of equilibration buffer was added to each sample at 37 °C for 1 h, followed by four PBS washes (5 min each). Nuclear counterstaining was performed using DAPI solution for 8 min, followed by three washes with PBS. To preserve fluorescence, an anti-fade agent was applied prior to imaging. TUNEL-stained images were acquired using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Leica TRIF + THUNDER).

## Intraventricular injection
In brief, 6-week-old mice were anesthetized intraperitoneally with sodium pentobarbital and positioned in a stereotactic instrument (KOPF) (Rayward Life Technologies, 68801) with the head secured at a 180-degree angle facing forward. The fur on the head was shaved, and a longitudinal skin incision of approximately 1 cm was made above the midpoint between the eyes to expose the skull. With the fontanel used as a reference point, a small hole was drilled 1.5 mm posterior and 1.5 mm lateral (left or right) to the fontanel. Saline or bilirubin was then injected into the lateral ventricle using a microsyringe.

## Liver dysfunction model and BBB permeability assay
Six-week-old mice were intraperitoneally injected with 10%, 20%, 30%, 40%, or 50% carbon tetrachloride (CCl4). Three days after CCl₄ administration, the mice were intraperitoneally injected with LPS (54 mg/kg), while the control mice received an equal volume of PBS. After 6 h, 2% Evans blue was retro-orbitally injected into the systemic circulation. After an additional 2-h interval, blood and brain tissues were collected.
For the quantification of Evans blue staining, the brain was bisected along the midline. One hemisphere was weighed and homogenized with 600 μL of trichloroacetic acid, followed by centrifugation at 15,000 × g for 30 min. The fluorescence intensity was measured using an excitation wavelength of 620 nm and an emission wavelength of 680 nm. The OD values were normalized to the weight of the cerebral hemisphere and are expressed as micrograms per gram of tissue (μg/g) .

## LC-MS/MS liquid chromatography assay
Whole mouse brain tissue was collected and transferred into a centrifuge tube containing two metal grinding beads for homogenization. Tissue homogenization was performed using a grinder operating at 55 Hz with the following parameters: 20 s per cycle, 10 s intervals between cycles, and 3 total cycles. Subsequently, 20 mg of homogenized tissue was aliquoted into a new tube, followed by sequential addition of 140 μL of pre-cooled (–20 °C) methanol, 20 μL of ascorbic acid, and 35 μL of DMSO. The mixture was vortexed thoroughly for 1 min and incubated on ice for 5 min. The samples were then centrifuged at 14,000 rpm (4 °C) for 20 min, after which the supernatant was carefully collected. The supernatant was evaporated to dryness using a nitrogen evaporator, and the residue was reconstituted with 50 μL of mass spectrometry-grade methanol. A final centrifugation step (14,000 rpm, 4 °C, 20 min) was performed, and the resulting supernatant was collected on a chromatographic column (ACQUITY UPLC® BEH Phenyl 2.1× 100 mm, 1.7 μm) for subsequent LC-MS analysis (AB Sciex Triple Quad 7500).

## Statistical analysis
GraphPad Prism 10.1.2 software was used for data analysis. Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism 10 (GraphPad Software). The data shown in the graphs represent mean ± SD. P > 0.05 was considered not significant (ns). P values were determined using one-way ANOVA or two-way ANOVA.
