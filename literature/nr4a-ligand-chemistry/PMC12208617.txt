Further, overexpression of Esrrg in dopaminergic neurons reduces synuclein load in the pre-formed fibril model of synucleinopathy. For these reasons, we sought to understand ERRγ’s role in transcriptional regulation in spiny projection neurons (SPNs), one of the neuronal populations vulnerable to transcriptional dysregulation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and protein aggregation in HD. Here, we demonstrate that developmental deletion of Esrrg selectively in SPNs causes a transcriptional pattern consistent with a reduction of Drd1 and Drd2–positive neurons in the mouse dorsolateral striatum. To avoid effects of developmental deletion and explore Esrrg’s role within adult SPN populations, we deleted or overexpressed Esrrg in adult SPNs. While overexpression was sufficient to increase the expression of mitochondrial and lysosome-related transcripts, Esrrg deletion surprisingly caused increased expression of immediate-early genes and genes with enrichment of binding sites for transcriptional repressors. In contrast, these genes were downregulated by Esrrg overexpression. Concordantly, Esrrg-deficient mice exhibited lack of amphetamine-induced hyperactivity and further upregulation of immediate-early genes. To determine whether the alterations observed with ERRγ modulation have any relevance for understanding transcriptional changes in SPNs in neurodegeneration, we measured Esrrg and its responsive genes in two mouse models of HD. We found an increase in Esrrg expression in HD models, accompanied by a transcriptional profile with similarities to that observed with Esrrg overexpression, suggesting the existence of an ERRγ-dependent, stress-related response. Altogether, these studies suggest that ERRγ is a key activator of mitochondrial and lysosomal transcripts in SPNs with a potential bi-functional role as a mediator of immediate-early gene repression. Ongoing studies are investigating mechanisms underlying ERRγ’s roles in transcriptional activation and repression in SPNs to inform strategies to promote neuroprotective actions of ERRγ in SPNs in HD.

## Introduction
Mitochondrial function has been extensively studied as a contributor to neuronal dysfunction and cell death in neurodegenerative disorders. Mitochondrial processes implicated in neurodegeneration are related to oxidative stress, mutations in genes related to mitochondrial dynamics, and disruption of mitochondrial transcriptional programs of nuclear-encoded mitochondrial genes ( ; , ). Huntington’s Disease (HD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder caused by a CAG expansion in exon 1 of the Huntington gene (HTT) which causes cortical atrophy and loss of striatal spiny projection neurons and cortical glutamatergic neurons ( ; ; ; ). Mitochondrial dysfunction and mechanisms of transcriptional dysregulation have been extensively linked to HD ( ; ; ; ; ; ; ; ); however, recent studies are only just beginning to shed light on potential mechanisms for mitochondrial gene dysregulation in HD, with reductions in the expression and activity of transcriptional regulators enriched in SPNs such as retinoic acid receptor beta (RARB) and other members of the RAR and RXR family ( ).
One proposed strategy for rescuing transcriptional dysfunction in HD has been to increase the expression and/or activity of transcriptional regulators for nuclear-encoded mitochondrial genes. Peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma coactivator 1-alpha (PPARGC1A/PGC-1α) has been extensively studied by our lab and others in the regulation of nuclear-encoded mitochondrial genes in the brain ( ). A large body of data suggests that PGC-1α is a prime candidate for increasing mitochondrial biogenesis and promoting neuroprotection ( , , , , , , ); however, PGC-1α is incapable of being targeted by small molecules. Elucidation of the mechanisms by which PGC-1α drives gene expression programs in SPNs could be leveraged to identify novel targets for neuroprotection (reviewed in ( ).
Recent studies from our lab demonstrated the requirement of a family of transcription factors called Estrogen-related Receptors (ERRs) in the control of PGC-1α-responsive genes ( ; ). The three members of the ERR family (ERRα, ERRβ, and ERRγ) are expressed in metabolically active tissues, bind to the same consensus binding sequence (AAGGTCA) ( ), and regulate the transcription of PGC-1α-responsive genes outside of the central nervous system ( ; ; ; ). Despite expression of the ERRs in the nervous system, studies of ERR function in the brain are limited ( ; ; ; ; ; ; ). ERRβ’s known function is early in development ( ) with almost no expression in adult mouse neurons ( ). ERRγ’s role is the least understood in the brain; it is expressed in striatal SPNs and has been shown to control the expression of mitochondrial genes in other neuron types ( ; ). Recently, we demonstrated that overexpression of ERRγ in dopaminergic neurons reduces synuclein aggregation in the pre-formed fibril model of synucleinopathy, potentially by upregulation of genes involved in autophagy ( ). However, when these ERRγ-responsive transcripts were measured in mice lacking ERRγ in hippocampal neurons, there were only modest reductions in some of the transcripts, suggesting cell type-specific role of ERRγ ( ). Based on these observations, we wanted to explore whether SPNs also rely on ERRγ for the proper maintenance of gene programs for mitochondrial and lysosomal function.
To determine whether ERRγ is required for transcriptional homeostasis in SPNs, we used Cre-dependent models to test the transcriptional and behavioral effects of ERRγ deletion in development and adulthood. Here, we show that deletion of ERRγ selectively in SPNs in mice using a developmentally expressed Cre recombinase (Rgs9l-Cre) causes a reduction in genes enriched in Drd1 and Drd2-expressing SPNs and neuron density. In contrast, deletion of Esrrg in adult SPNs causes reductions in ERRγ-responsive genes as well as a subset of lysosome-related genes without an effect on cell marker expression, accompanied by an upregulation of immediate-early genes (IEGs). Conversely, overexpression of Esrrg in adult SPNs causes an increase in ERRγ-responsive mitochondrial and lysosomal genes and a reduction in the expression of IEGs. Lastly, in mouse models of HD, we find an upregulation of Esrrg expression and a transcriptional signature similar to that of mice overexpressing Esrrg, suggesting a possible role for Esrrg upregulation as a stress response in HD. These studies indicate that ERRγ can act as an activator and/or repressor of gene expression in vivo and suggest that ERRγ-driven strategies for promoting mitochondrial biogenesis should consider approaches for counteracting transcriptional repression.

## Materials and methods
2.1.

## Animal models
All experimental procedures were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of the University of Alabama at Birmingham and of Southern Research and performed in accordance with the Association for Assessment and Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care (AAALAC). Experimenters were blind to genotype for all experiments. All mice were maintained on a C57Bl/6 J genetic background and housed 2–5 in a cage at 26 ± 2 °C room temperature with food and water ad libitum. Breeding schemes were established using mice heterozygous for the Esrrg floxed allele to generate littermate controls for all mouse lines.
For deletion of Esrrg in developing SPNs, we crossed Esrrgfl/fl mouse line with mice expressing Cre-recombinase under control of the Rgs9l promoter (RgsCre; Jackson Laboratory Strain #020550). For deletion of Esrrg from forebrain excitatory neurons, mice expressing Cre-recombinase under control of the empty spiracles homeobox1 (Emx1) promoter ( ) (Emx1Cre Jackson Laboratory Strain #005628) were crossed with Esrrgfl/fl mouse line. Cre-recombinase-expressing females were used for breeding, and all control mice were Cre-positive with wildtype Esrrg alleles. For adulthood deletion studies, Esrrg+/+ and Esrrgfl/fl mice were injected with an AAV for Camk2aCre (AAV5-CamKIIa-GFP-Cre from UNC vector core) into 3-month-old mice dorsolateral striatum. For adulthood overexpression studies, mice were injected with overexpression virus for Esrrg (AAV5-CAG-Esrrg-NM_011935.3-IRES-EGFP-WPRE) or Gfp control (AAV5-CAG-Gfp) previously described ( ) into the dorsolateral striatum. All experiments were conducted using male and female mice at 3 or 4 months of age with the following time-points per experiment: 1) RgsCre; Esrrg mice were used at 3 months of age; 2) Esrrg+/+ and Esrrgfl/fl mice were injected with AAV5-CamKIIa-GFP-Cre at 3 months of age and allowed to age to 4 months before experiments were conducted; 3) non-transgenic mice maintained on a C57Bl/6 J line were injected with overexpression virus for Esrrg (AAV5-CAG-Esrrg-NM_011935.3-IRES-EGFP-WPRE) or Gfp control (AAV5-CAG-Gfp) at 3 months of age and allowed to age to 4 months before experiments were conducted.

2.2.

## Stereotaxic injections
Before surgery, a subcutaneous injection of buprenorphine (0.1 mg/kg; analgesic) and carprofen (3 mg/kg; anti-inflammatory) was administered. The mice were anesthetized with isoflurane (4–5 % induction, 1–2 % maintenance), immobilized in a stereotaxic apparatus (Stoelting), and kept on a heating pad through the surgery and recovery. Bregma was identified, and a small burr hole was made in the skull using a drill. Viruses included AAV5-CamKIIa-GFP-Cre, AAV5-Gfp, or AAV5-Esrrg (described above). 2 μl virus was bilaterally injected at a constant rate of 0.5 μl/min into the dorsolateral striatum of Esrrg+/+ or Esrrgfl/fl mice using the coordinates A/P: +0.2 M/L: +/− 2.0 D/V: − 2.6. The syringe was left in place for 4 min after injection and was then slowly withdrawn. All mice were monitored for activity and recovery post-surgical injection and then returned to their home cage. Mice were monitored for 3 days post-surgery to insure healthy recovery.

2.3.

## Small molecule fluorescence in situ hybridization (sm-FISH)
sm-FISH was performed using the Basescope fluorescent assay followed by the RNAscope Multiplex Fluorescent Assay (Advanced Cell Diagnostics/ACD, Newark, CA, USA) as described previously ( ; ; ). Briefly, mice were anesthetized with isoflurane prior to decapitation and tissue removal where tissue was frozen on dry ice. Tissue was sectioned (20 μm) using a cryostat and mounted on SuperFrost Plus slides (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and slides were immediately frozen in dry ice. For the Basescope assay for Esrrg expression, tissue was fixed with 4 % paraformaldehyde followed by dehydration with ethanol and treated with hydrogen peroxide and protease III (ACD, Newark, CA, USA). Next, slides were incubated with Basescope probes for 2 h at 40 °C followed by amplification and incubation with BaseScope Fast RED A and B (ACD, Newark, CA, USA). Due to the small length of transcript used to detect recombination, BaseScope probes were custom designed to recognize exons 1–3 (217–264 bp) of Esrrg (NM_011935.3; ACD, Newark, CA, USA). RNAscope was then performed by incubating tissue in a mixture of probes for 2 h at 40 °C followed by fluorescent amplification. All probes are listed in (ACD, Newark, CA, USA). Slides were coverslipped with Prolong gold antifade mounting media containing DAPI (Thermo Fisher Scientific), and images were captured with a Nikon A1+ confocal microscope. All settings, including laser intensity, gain, offset, and zoom, were held constant across all groups for a given experiment. For RNAscope/Basescope experiments 4 animals/genotype and n = 2 sections per animal were imaged.
To quantify mRNA expression as detected by sm-FISH, image thresholding for individual channels and quantification of mean pixel density for a given area was performed using FIJI ImageJ software ( ) as previously described ( ; ; ). Each experiment sets a threshold for each individual channel based on a wild-type control signal for a gene which makes 1 punctum correspond to 1 transcript of a gene. Each neuron positive for a cell marker is circled to generate mean pixel density values for each gene using FIJI. For generation of representative images, tiff files were imported into Adobe Photoshop CS3 (Adobe, San Jose, CA), and adjustments to contrast, sharpness and brightness were held constant across experimental groups.
To roughly estimate neuron counts from in RgsCre;Esrrg+/+
and
fl/fl mice, images were stained using FISH for SPN markers Drd1, Drd2, and Ppp1r1b. A total of 2 images were taken from 4 dorsolateral striatum sections for a total of 8 images per mouse with n = 4 mice per group. These images were used to determine mRNA puncta counts for Drd1 and Drd2 (as described above) as well as cell counts for a given animal (expressed as Total Neuron Counts/Region of Interest/Mouse).
For Egr1 analysis in spiny projection neurons, we used a Cell Profiler pipeline to quantify mean pixel density. Once a pipeline was established, Cell Profiler automatically circled every neuron that is positive for Drd1 or Drd2 cell marker and quantified mean pixel density of Egr1. To do this, the diameter of the pixel units for a gene was set to 1–6-pixel units, while the diameter for a neuron was set to 20–70-pixel units. While Cell Profiler accurately identified and circled most neurons, some manual work was required to account for any errors for neurons in close proximity or any missed neurons.

2.4.

## Quantitative reverse transcriptase PCR
All protocols were conducted as described previously ( ). Mice were anesthetized with isoflurane prior to decapitation where brains were removed, and regions of interest were dissected and frozen on dry ice. Tissues were homogenized in TRIzol using the Bead Ruptor 12 (Omni International) followed by RNA isolation using the TRIzol/choloform-isopropanol method following manufacturer’s instructions (Invitrogen). NanoDrop 2000 (Thermo Fisher Scientific) was used to determine RNA concentration and 1 μg of RNA was treated with DNAse I (Promega) at 37 °C for 30 min and DNase Stop solution at 65 °C for 15 min. The High-Capacity cDNA Archive Kit (Applied Biosystems) was used for RNA reverse transcriptase step following manufacturer’s instructions. A list of all transcripts and corresponding Taqman probes is provided in . Transcripts were measured using mouse specific primer probe sets from Thermo Fisher and JumpStart Taq Readymix (Sigma-Aldrich) with an initial ramp step (2 min, 50 °C; 10 min, 95 °C) and 40 subsequent cycles (15 s, 95 °C; 1 min, 60 °C). Relative concentration of transcript was calculated compared with a standard curve generated from pooled cDNA samples (1, 1:5, 1:10, 1:20, 1:40; calibrator method). These values were normalized to β-actin and expressed as ratio to control samples ± SEM.

2.5.

## HdhQ quantitative reverse transcriptase PCR
Samples previously isolated for RNA and converted to cDNA ( ) were used to measure genes of interest in for this study from HdhQ200/200 mice and Wild-type controls.

2.6.

## RNA-Seq analysis
Raw sequence reads were first trimmed with Trim Galore (version 0.6.6) to remove primer adapter contamination. STAR (version 2.7.7a) was used to align the trimmed RNA-Seq fastq reads to the mouse reference genome (GRCm38 p6, Release M24) from Gencode ( ). Following alignment, HTSeq-count (version 0.11.2) was used to count the number of reads mapping to each gene ( ). Normalization and differential expression were then applied to the count files using DESeq2 ( ) (version 1.30.1), using ±1.5 fold change and adjusted p value of 0.05 as a cutoff, with comparison of Esrrgfl/fl to Esrrg+/+ samples (GSE198214).

2.7.

## Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA)
GSEA was performed using R Studio BiocManager package. Organism was set to mouse, ontology type was set to molecular function, p-value cutoff was set to 0.5, and Benjamini–Hochberg statistical analysis was used as p-adjusted method. Minimum gene size annotated by Ontology term for testing was set to 10, while maximum was set to 500. Gene ratio (x-axis) is the percentage of significant genes over the total genes in a given pathway.

2.8.

## Behavioral acclimatization
Prior to testing, mice were brought into the behavioral testing core with lights on and a noise machine for 30 min. All behavior was conducted during the lights-on period with and in the presence of a white noise machine to reduce any startling responses to outside factors.

2.9.

## Open field assessment of ambulatory behavior
Behavior was assessed as described previously ( ; ; ). For assessment of baseline activity, animals were placed in a square apparatus (27.31 cm Length x 27.31 cm width x 20.32 cm height) consisting of 48 infrared beams (Med Associates) for 30 min where data was collected with Open Field Activity Software (Med Associates). For behavioral assessment with stimulation of dopamine release, mice were injected intraperitoneally (IP) with 5 mg/kg of d-amphetamine (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO) and allowed to freely explore the open field box for a total of 60 min.

2.10.

## Statistics
All statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism v10 (GraphPad Software, San Diego, CA, USA). Outliers were identified using ROUT with Q set at 1 %. Data are presented as the mean ± standard error of the mean (SEM). Behavioral analysis with two independent variables or more were analyzed using parametric two-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s multiple comparisons if groups were equal, or mixed-effects analysis ANOVA with Sidak’s multiple comparisons if groups were unequal. Normality was tested using the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test and QQplots, with tests for variance across groups using the Bartlett’s test. If assumptions of normality were not met, data were then analyzed using a Kruskal-Wallis followed by Dunn’s multiple comparisons test. For analyses of two groups (i.e., transcript analysis, sm-FISH), data were analyzed using a two-tailed unpaired t-test. One-sided t-tests were used in cases in which previous reports and/or RNA sequencing demonstrated directionality of response (rt-PCR, FISH). Significant differences were described in the graph when the p-value was less than 0.05 and assumed at *p < 0.05, **p < 0.01, ***p < 0.001, and ****p < 0.0001.

## Results
3.1.

## Developmental deletion of Esrrg causes a deficiency in SPN markers and reduced SPN density in adulthood
Due to the early postnatal lethality observed with constitutive, whole-body Esrrg deletion in mice ( ), we used Cre-lox technology to delete Esrrg selectively from striatal spiny projection neurons (SPNs). We used several approaches including 1) knockout of Esrrg in development selectively in SPNs using a RgsCre mouse crossed with Esrrg flox mouse model, 2) adulthood deletion using an injection of an AAV for Cre recombinase driven by the Camk2a promoter into the Esrrg flox mouse model, and 3) overexpression of Esrrg in non-transgenic mice using an AAV for Esrrg that we have previously validated in vivo ( ).
To explore Esrrg’s role in development in SPNs, we crossed Esrrg+/+ or Esrrgfl/fl mice with RgsCre-expressing mice to cause recombination in SPNs. To assess the extent of recombination in these mice, we first used small molecule fluorescent in situ hybridization (sm-FISH, BASEscope) with a probe designed for exon 2 in the Esrrgfl/fl mouse line ( ). We found a statistically significant reduction in the Esrrg transcript in the RgsCre;Esrrgfl/fl mice compared to RgsCre;Esrrg+/+ mice when considering all SPNs (positive for either Drd1 or Drd2; , ). When assessing transcript abundance by population subtype, we found a statistically significant reduction in Esrrg in the Drd1-positive cell type ( ). In RgsCre;Esrrg+/+ mice, there was no statistical difference in Esrrg expression between Drd1 and Drd2–positive SPN populations ( ).
We then performed q-rt-PCR to confirm Esrrg knockdown and assess effects on Esrrg-responsive genes previously measured and identified in hippocampal or dopaminergic neurons of Esrrg-deficient mice ( ; ). We found a reduction in Esrrg and its hypothesized responsive genes Stac2, Cox4i1, and Slc25a4, without effects on Esrra or Idh3a ( ). In the same samples, we measured SPN-enriched genes ( ; ) to determine whether reduction in Esrrg could influence SPN marker genes and/or the abundance of SPNs in a homogenate and found significant reductions in Ppp1r1b (encoding DARPP32), Ido1, Klf16, and Rarb ( ). We then measured genes selective for the Drd1- or Drd2–positive populations ( ; ) and found significant reductions in several genes including Cnr1 in the direct pathway category and Drd2, Adora2a, Gpx6, and Foxp1 in the indirect pathway category ( , ). Altogether, these data suggest developmental effects on SPN gene expression in existing SPNs. To address this, we designed a sm-FISH experiment using probes for Ppp1r1b, Drd1, and Drd2 to be able to label each population in both RgsCre;Esrrg+/+ and RgsCre;Esrrgfl/fl mice. When considered on a cell-by-cell basis, Drd1, Drd2, or Ppp1r1b expression did not differ by genotype ( ); however, when the number of neurons per region of interest were quantified, we found a statistically significant reduction in counts of neurons expressing Ppp1r1b, Drd1, and Drd2 (4 sections/mouse)( ; representative images in ). These data suggest that RgsCre;Esrrgfl/fl mice have a reduction in both Drd1 and Drd2-expressing SPNs, meaning that changes in genes using q-rt-PCR ( - ) could reflect either reduction in gene expression in existing cells or abundance of the cell type itself.

3.2.

## Adulthood deletion of Esrrg from SPNs causes a reduction in subsets of mitochondrial and lysosomal genes
To determine the potential roles for Esrrg in SPN gene maintenance in adulthood without the confound of developmental effects, we injected 3 month-old Esrrg+/+ and Esrrgfl/fl mice with an AAV-Camk2aCre virus into the dorsolateral striatum to cause deletion of Esrrg selectively in spiny projection neurons. The mice were then aged 1 month before tissue collection to ensure recombination. To confirm knockdown, we used sm-FISH with a probe specific for the floxed exon (exon 2–3 border) in the Esrrgfl/fl mouse line and found a statistically significant reduction in Esrrg mRNA equally in Drd1 and Drd2–positive populations of SPNs ( , ), to the point of almost being undetectable.
Next, we explored whether Esrrg deletion in SPNs causes changes in Esrrg-dependent genes previously identified in hippocampal and dopaminergic neurons ( ; ) and also responsive to PGC-1α overexpression ( ; ). Genes were measured using q-rt-PCR in striatal homogenates of AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrg+/+ and AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrgfl/fl mice. We found a significant reduction in Esrrg and its dependent genes Esrra, mitochondrially encoded Cox1 (mt-Cox1), and Slc25a4 ( ), without statistically significant reductions in Cox4i1 or Idh3a. Residual Esrrg expression was likely due to expression of Esrrg in other striatal cell types not expressing Camk2a, such as interneurons.
To determine whether these differences were due to the loss of SPNs, similar to what was observed with developmental deletion of Esrrg, we measured markers for D1 and D2 cell types (as in ) using quantitative q-rt-PCR in striatal homogenates. In contrast to the widespread reductions in SPN markers observed with deletion of Esrrg in development ( - ), most transcripts showed no change in abundance except for Klf16 and Foxp1, which were reduced ( - ). The few changes in SPN type-specific markers led us to conclude that Esrrg deletion in adulthood is unlikely to cause the acute loss of SPNs. For this reason, we chose mice with adulthood deletion to identify ERRγ-dependent and responsive transcripts without confounds of neuronal loss. We next sought to test whether previously identified ERRγ-dependent genes associated with lysosome function were reduced in this model ( ). We found reductions in Atp6ap1, Atp6v0e2, and Ctsl which are genes involved in lysosome acidification and protein degradation ( ). Altogether, these data suggest that the maintenance of some SPN-expressed genes involved in mitochondrial and lysosomal function does not rely solely on ERRγ.

3.3.

## Adulthood deletion of Esrrg causes an upregulation in immediate early genes (IEGs)
To explore novel transcriptional consequences of Esrrg deletion in SPNs, we performed RNAseq on striatal samples from Esrrg+/+ and Esrrgfl/fl mice injected with AAV-Camk2aCre collected at 1 month post-injection. This sequencing revealed 121 significantly differentially expressed genes (p < 0.05; ±1.5-fold change) with an increase in 67 protein-coding genes and a decrease in 54 protein-coding genes ( ). To identify putative transcription factors which may regulate genes altered by Esrrg deletion, we used transcription factor binding site enrichment analysis. Genes downregulated by Esrrg deletion have consensus binding sequences for SUZ12, a component of the polycomb repressive complex ( ), while genes upregulated by Esrrg deletion have consensus binding sequences for SRF, REST, and SUZ12 (p-adj < 0.005; ). We next performed Gene Set Enrichment Analyses for molecular function on the differentially expressed genes. Here we found categories of genes involved in signaling receptor activity and receptor ligand activity, as well as several other categories, for upregulated genes ( ). Transporter activity and transmembrane transporter activity were the only significant categories identified from downregulated genes ( ). An upregulation of gene expression was not necessarily expected from deletion of a transcription factor; this, in combination with enrichment for binding sites for repressors in ERRγ-modulated genes, suggests a potential requirement of ERRγ for maintaining normal repressive states of chromatin in SPNs.
To determine whether any of these genes could be putative direct targets for ERRγ, we explored whether genes altered by ERRγ knockdown in vivo were changed in SH-SY5Ys overexpressing ERRγ (previously published in ( )). We found an overlap in 15 genes ( ). While examining this list, we found genes that fell into the category of immediate early genes (IEGs) such as Fosl2, Fos, Junb, and Ret ( ). Follow-up q-rt-PCR confirmation of alterations in IEG expression from AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrgfl/fl and AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrg+/+ striatal homogenates revealed an increase in a number of IEGs including Arc, Egr1, Egr4, Ret, Nr4a1, Nr4a3, Junb, Fos, Fosb, Fosl2, and Npas4 ( ). We also measured these genes in our SPN developmental deletion model (RgsCre) and found an increase in Nr4a1 and Nr4a3 ( ). To determine whether these effects of ERRγ deletion on IEGs were selective to SPNs, we measured IEGs in hippocampus from mice lacking ERRγ in forebrain excitatory neurons (EmxCre;Esrrg); the only change was observed in Nr4a3 (modest increase; ), suggesting cell type-specificity of IEG regulation. Lastly, to understand if these changes were ERRγ specific, or if they could be caused by deletion of other members of the estrogen-related receptor family, we measured IEGs in the striatum of ERRα knockout mice and found no changes in any genes ( ). Together, these data suggest that ERRγ could play a novel role in the repression of IEGs in SPNs.

3.4.

## Overexpression of Esrrg in striatum upregulates ERRγ-responsive mitochondrial genes and reduces IEG expression
Our recently published findings ( ) indicate that Esrrg overexpression in the midbrain is capable of increasing the expression of nuclear-encoded mitochondrial genes and genes involved in lysosomal function and autophagy ( ), so we assessed whether overexpression of Esrrg in striatum could increase similar genes. To do this, we overexpressed Esrrg in the dorsolateral striatum using a previously used AAV vector (AAV-Esrrg; ( )) in non-transgenic mice at the age of 3 months and compared them to non-transgenic mice injected with a control virus (AAV-Gfp). Esrrg overexpression was confirmed using sm-FISH probes for Esrrg, Drd1 and Drd2 at 1 month post-injection, with increases observed in both Drd1+ and Drd2+ SPNs ( ). Using qPCR, we found induction in Esrrg and its responsive genes Esrra, mt-Cox1, Cox4i1, Idh3a and Slc25a4 ( ). Like adulthood deletion ( ), we saw few changes in SPN marker genes with a modest reduction in the Drd2-positive SPN-enriched gene, Penk ( - ). Interestingly, we found induction in several lysosomal and autophagy genes including Atg4d, Atp6v0b, Ctsd, and Becn1 ( ). When examining the expression of IEGs transcripts increased with Esrrg deletion ( ), we found reductions in several of the genes including Egr1, Ret, Fos, Fosb, Fosl2, and Npas4 ( ).
To explore the cellular specificity of changes in IEGs we identified through RNA-seq, we used sm-FISH to estimate the abundance of transcript levels of Egr1 in our Camk2aCre;Esrrg-deleted and Esrrg-overexpressing mice. We chose to explore the cellular specificity of changes in Egr1, due to its robust upregulation with knockdown and downregulation with overexpression. In a set of mice separate from the set used for q-rt-PCR, we determined that adult Esrrgfl/fl mice injected with AAV-Camk2aCre had an upregulation of Egr1 as compared to Esrrg+/+ mice injected with AAV-Camk2aCre. This upregulation in Egr1 occurred equally in both Drd1 and Drd2-positive SPN populations ( ). Conversely, AAV-mediated Esrrg overexpression caused a downregulation in Egr1 in Drd1 and Drd2 SPNs ( ).

3.5.

## Esrrg manipulation modulates the acute behavioral and transcriptional responses to amphetamine
Based on our findings of altered IEG expression with ERRγ modulation, we sought to determine whether mice deficient in or overexpressing ERRγ were differentially sensitive to amphetamine, a stimulus which causes the acute upregulation of IEGs in SPNs (Kufahl, Moore, and Olive 2016; de Bartolomeis et al. 2017; ). To do this, we used open field analyses to assess ambulatory activity at baseline (without any injections) and then following amphetamine treatment. AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrgfl/fl mice at one month post-deletion did not exhibit any differences in normal exploratory ambulation during a 30-min period in the open field box ( ), nor did AAV-Gfp and AAV-Esrrg mice ( ). Interestingly, when we injected AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrg+/+ and AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrgfl/fl with 5 mg/kg of d-amphetamine, we found that AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrgfl/fl mice have a significantly reduced ambulatory response compared to AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrg+/+ mice ( ). Conversely, when given an injection of 5 mg/kg of d-amphetamine, mice injected with AAV-Esrrg were hyperactive in their response to amphetamine compared to AAV-Gfp mice ( ).
Considering the increase in IEG expression observed with ERRγ deletion and their reduced responsivity to d-amphetamine, we hypothesized that mice lacking ERRγ in SPNs failed to respond normally to amphetamine injection because IEG expression was already at its maximum level (ceiling effect). To explore this idea, we first did a time-course experiment to identify when IEG expression was most robust following injection of amphetamine, with Fos as the prototypical gene (behavioral confirmation of amphetamine-induced ambulatory activity in ; gene expression in ). Using q-rt-PCR, we found that the most robust increase in Fos expression was observed 3 h post-injection, without statistically significant changes in Esrrg ( ); we chose this 3 h time-point for subsequent experiments ( ). While AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrg+/+ mice exhibited a significant increase in IEGs compared to saline-injected AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrg+/+ mice similar to what has been observed in wildtype mice ( ), AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrgfl/fl mice did not exhibit any additional increase in IEGs with amphetamine injection (expressed as fold of AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrgfl/fl mice injected with saline; ). This suggests that reduced ambulation in response to amphetamine injection observed in AAV-Camk2aCre;Esrrgfl/fl mice could be due to the lack of dynamic regulation of IEGs in these mice.

3.6.

## Transcriptional response to Esrrg overexpression partially mimics the transcriptional profile in mouse models of Huntington’s disease
To determine whether the alterations observed with ERRγ modulation have any relevance for understanding changes in transcription in HD, we explored evidence for alterations in ERRγ-responsive genes in HD models from published studies. Recent studies rigorously characterized transcriptional changes in SPNs in the mouse and human striatum with and without disease modifying Huntington CAG repeats (H. ). When we cross-referenced these genes with our RNAseq data we found an overlap of 52 genes with single nucleus RNA sequencing (snRNA-seq) from Q175 6-month-old mice, 55 genes with snRNAseq from R6/2 9-week-old mice, and 51 genes with human HD striatal samples ( ). Some of these genes were shared between datasets and are listed in . When examining this overlap, we noticed that 9 IEGs including Npas4, Arc, Egr1, Nr4a3, Fosl2, Egr4, Junb, Nr4a1, and Fos were differentially regulated in both our dataset and HD models. Interestingly, Esrrg expression is also increased in SPNs of the R6/2 and Q175 models (H. ).
To explore this in two additional HD models, we isolated RNA and measured ERRγ-responsive genes in striatum of BACHD and HdhQ 200/200, mouse lines which both express full-length mHTT with either 97 glutamine repeats and exhibit progressive motor deficits with age ( ), or express 200 repeats with earlier onset of motor, behavioral, and neuroanatomical abnormalities associated with HD (HDQ 200/200 (Heng et al. 2010)). Interestingly, in the BACHDmodel, we found an increase in Esrrg and its mitochondrial responsive genes Idh3a and Slc25a4 ( , ) as well as decreases in the IEGs Arc, Egr1 and Egr4 ( ), like what is shown with our Esrrg overexpression model. To understand if the increase in Esrrg was linked to changes in lysosomal genes known to be dysregulated in HD and shown to be changed with Esrrg expression ( ), we measured and found increases in several lysosomal/autophagy genes Atg4d, Atg9a, Atp6v0d1, Ctsd, Ctsl and Jmjd8 ( ).
In the HDQ 200/200 mouse line we found increases in Esrrg and its responsive genes Cox4i1, Idh3a, Slc25a4, and Stac2 ( , ), decreases in IEGs Arc, Egr1, Junb, and Fos ( ), and increases in lysosomal genes Atp6ap1, Atp6v0b, Atp6v0d1, Atp6v0e2, Atp6v1f, Ctsl, and Becn1 ( j), which mirror what is seen in the Esrrg overexpression model. Together, these data suggest the over-activation of ERRγ-dependent gene programs in HD, with overexpression being sufficient to recapitulate subsets of transcriptional changes observed in multiple models and patients.

## Discussion
In these studies, we explored the transcriptional and behavioral consequences of ERRγ modulation in striatal SPNs. Based on our recent work demonstrating a requirement for ERRγ in mitochondrial gene expression and number in dopaminergic neurons, we predicted that ERRγ would have similar roles in SPNs, which are vulnerable to oxidative stress and loss with neurodegeneration ( ). We demonstrate that ERRγ regulates similar mitochondrial genes in SPNs as ones previously published with ERRγ deletion in both DAergic neurons of the substantia nigra and hippocampal excitatory neurons ( ; ). We also demonstrate that ERRγ and PGC-1α are required for the maintenance of similar genes when deleted from SPNs, like Idh3a and Stac2, suggesting that ERRγ and PGC-1α may coordinately regulate the same transcripts ( ). Importantly, ERRγ overexpression can increase the expression of nuclear-encoded mitochondrial and lysosomal transcripts, suggesting that it can drive both transcriptional programs in neurons. However, it is especially important to note we did not measure whether protein expression or mitochondrial/lysosomal function is altered with ERRγ modulation; future studies are required to determine whether ERRγ overexpression can influence mitochondrial or lysosomal activity, with relevance for promoting those processes in the context of neurodegeneration.
Bioinformatic analyses of the genes reduced in the absence of ERRγ suggest that it is necessary for maintaining basic transcriptional processes like transcriptional activation and nuclear receptor binding. Also, as previously reported for ERRv in other cells like islet cells ( ), cardiomyocytes ( ; ; Lasheras et al. 2021; Lasheras et al. 2016), and dopaminergic neurons ( ), ERRγ is required for the maintenance of genes encoding potassium channel subunits. However, interestingly, the specific potassium channel subunits vary by cell type (e.g. Kcng4 in dopaminergic neurons and Kcnab3 in SPNs), suggesting that while ERRγ may be binding promoters of these genes, the transcriptional activators which form a complex with ERRγ could be cell type-specific. It is also important to point out that while many of the altered genes contain consensus binding sequences for ERRγ, ERR-binding sites did not emerge as a common characteristic of downregulated or upregulated genes, indicating that some of the differences in gene expression could be due to the effects of ERRγ on factors involved in general transcriptional processes. Of note, genes influenced by ERRγ deletion emerged as common targets for repressors such as REST and SUZ12, suggesting that alterations in ERRγ could influence transcription by shifting the repressive state of neurons.
One of the most surprising results from these studies was the indirect relationship between ERRγ expression and the expression of IEGs. This suggests that, while ERRγ activation could promote mitochondrial respiration and lysosomal activity, this may come at the cost of reducing activation of SPN activity-dependent processes. Interestingly, several of the IEGs reduced with ERRγ overexpression are also reduced in HD models and patients, suggesting that this is happening already in HD. The mechanisms underlying the bidirectional effects of ERRγ DNA binding on transcriptional state are not known; recent publications suggest that ERRγ could interact with repressors such as NCoR1 (Yamamoto et al. 2011). Providing supraphysiological levels of ERRγ could promote its interactions with both coactivators and repressors, giving rise to this effect. Ideally, ERRγ-targeting molecules should be prioritized based on their ability to activate transcriptional programs without promoting ERRγ’s interactions with transcriptional repressors.
Some of our work suggests that ERRγ may share some transcriptional targets with ERRα, which is also expressed in SPNs and cells with high PGC-1α expression ( ; ). ERRγ and ERRα can form heterodimers (Dufour et al. 2007; ) to drive gene expression, especially in cardiac tissue. In a cell culture model, when neurons were treated with inverse agonist for ERRα, the ERRγ-dependent transcripts Stac2, Atp5a1, and Idh3a were reduced in a dose-dependent manner ( ), suggesting that ERRγ and ERRα coordinate expression for these genes. However, when ERRγ-responsive mitochondrial genes were measured in the striatum of ERRα null mice, there were no reported differences ( ), like what we found when we measured IEGs in ERRα null mice ( ). Together, these data suggest that while gene programs for ERRα and ERRγ overlap, some gene programs are more dependent on ERRγ. This finding is consistent with the induction of ERRγ at an earlier stage in development ( ) and with the activation of ERRα expression by ERRγ itself ( ).
Mitochondrial dysfunction is a consistent finding in neurodegeneration, and early postmortem HD studies show a reduction in expression of genes encoding components of electron transport complexes in the caudate ( ; Brennan, Bird, and Aprille 1985; ) linking mitochondrial dysfunction to HD pathogenesis. However, when we cross-referenced our gene changes with ones seen in different models of HD (H. ), we found an overlap in the IEGs. For these reasons we measured IEGs and Esrrg-responsive genes in the BACHD model of HD which has evidence of mHTT aggregates. Here we found an upregulation in Esrrg mRNA and found dysregulation of genes similar to what is observed with Esrrg overexpression. Based on these gene changes, it would be interesting to test whether Esrrg reduction could be rescue deficiencies in IEGs in HD; small molecules such as 4-hydroxytamoxifen ( ) exhibit antagonist-like actions on ERRγ.
As we have reviewed previously ( ), it is challenging to target PGC-1α and its interacting factors, especially in a cell type-specific way. Based on the data demonstrating that ERRγ overexpression can increase gene expression for lysosomal activity (Ctsd), it is attractive to speculate that promoting its activity would improve clearance of mutant huntingtin and/or other damaged proteins in HD. However, the increase in the expression of Esrrg (R6/2, Q175, BACHD, HdhQ200/200 models) and several of its targets (e.g. Atg4d, Ctsd, Jmjd8; BACHDmodel) in HD models and patients suggests that the existing ERRγ may already be upregulated and driving transcription, potentially in response to cellular stress. In fact, PGC-1α, ERRα, and ERRγ are upregulated in response to oxidative stress and fasting in many different cell types ( ; ; ), a process which could represent a type of feed-forward pathway to promote the activation of mitochondrial biogenesis and antioxidant-mediated protection. However, it is important to note that even considering the upregulation of endogenous PGC-1α, ERRα, and ERRγ in these cases, additional overexpression can still confer protection from cell death-inducing stimuli ( ). Thus, it will be important to determine whether additional overexpression of ERRγ in the presence of mutant huntingtin could improve lysosomal function and clearance of mutant huntingtin.
Regarding the potential activation for activating ERRγ-dependent transcriptional programs with existing agonists: while there is some evidence for ERRγ agonists reaching the brain and promoting the expression of neuron-enriched genes such as tyrosine hydroxylase and BDNF receptors ( ; ), very few ERR modulators have been rigorously tested for stability and blood brain permeability in vivo. Also, considering that many ERRγ agonists and inverse agonists were designed to interact with the PGC-1α-binding domain and influence PGC-1α-dependent gene programs( ; ; ; ; ), it is possible that exposing cells with high ERRγ and PGC-1α expression to these small compounds could interfere with PGC-1α’s normal function as a coactivator. However, SPNs express lower levels of PGC-1α and ERRγ than local parvalbumin-expressing interneurons ( ; ; ); it is possible that ERRγ binding could improve transcriptional activation in cell types like SPNs that normally have lower expression.
Altogether, these studies highlight the novel roles for ERRγ in gene regulation in SPNs. Future studies will determine whether engaging ERRγ’s transcriptional activity can promote neuroprotection in SPNs in diseases such as HD. However, these studies must also focus on teasing apart the biology of ERRγ in neurons to assure that on-target, undesired effects of ERRγ-binding small molecules do not interfere with normal neuronal processes.
