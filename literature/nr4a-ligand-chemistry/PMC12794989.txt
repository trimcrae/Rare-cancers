## 1. Introduction
With the growing population of obese individuals, nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) has gradually become a major cause of chronic liver disease. Epidemiological surveys demonstrated that the incidence of NAFLD reached 25% in 2018,[ ] and the condition can progress to cirrhosis and even liver cancer, posing a serious public health concern and contributing significantly to the overall disease burden. Atherosclerosis (AS) is the pathological basis of coronary atherosclerotic heart disease. Unstable atherosclerotic plaques can rupture and erode, leading to acute coronary syndrome, which is a major cause of cardiovascular disease mortality. Growing evidence indicates that NAFLD is closely associated with atherosclerotic conditions, including coronary and peripheral artery AS.[ – ] In patients with type 1 diabetes mellitus, NAFLD is associated with carotid intima-media thickness and is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease.[ ] The strong correlation of NAFLD with cardiovascular events is noteworthy. A retrospective study demonstrated that the risk of acute myocardial infarction in NAFLD patients increased by 54% versus non-NAFLD populations.[ ] Therefore, NAFLD is regarded as an early indicator of cardiovascular disease and an independent predictor of poor prognosis.
There may be common mechanisms underlying the development of both NAFLD and AS that contribute to the progression of AS. Currently, fundamental mechanisms by which NAFLD may promote the development of AS include insulin resistance, alterations in gut microbiota, lipid metabolism abnormalities, dysregulation of liver-derived factors, coagulation dysfunction, activation of systemic inflammatory responses, and oxidative stress.[ ] However, the common molecular mechanisms underlying the occurrence of AS remain elusive and require further exploration. Advancements in next-generation sequencing and computational technology have enabled the use of various algorithms to analyze molecular biology data, providing deeper insights into the pathogenesis of various diseases and promoting the evaluation of treatment methods,[ ] giving rise to an interdisciplinary field that combines life sciences and computer science, known as bioinformatics. The Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) is an open gene expression database that contains gene expression data for different diseases and species, providing important genetic information for exploring disease mechanisms, identifying diagnostic and prognostic markers, and assessing treatment effects.
This study employed bioinformatics methods to analyze the differentially expressed genes (DEGs) shared between NAFLD and AS, investigating the potential molecular mechanisms underlying their co-occurrence and identifying biomarkers for comorbidity diagnosis. The aim was to discover and develop effective drugs for the precise treatment of NAFLD-related AS, ultimately preventing the occurrence of AS in the NAFLD population.

## 2. Materials and methods

## 2.1. Data sources for NAFLD and AS
The study flowchart is displayed in Figure . The GEOquery package (version 2.54.1) in R software (version 3.6.3) was utilized to obtain gene expression matrices from the GEO database (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/).[ ] Disease-related datasets were searched using keywords related to NAFLD and AS. Inclusion criteria were summarized as follows: NAFLD dataset samples sourced from human liver tissue, and AS dataset samples obtained from human carotid arteries. GSE89632 and GSE100927 datasets were selected for differential expression gene analysis. GSE89632 dataset includes samples from 19 NAFLD patients and 24 healthy controls, utilizing the GPL14951 detection platform (Illumina HumanHT-12 WG-DASL V4.0 R2 expression beadchip). GSE100927 dataset comprises samples from 29 AS patients and 12 healthy controls, employing the GPL17077 detection platform [Agilent-039494 SurePrint G3 Human GE v2 8 × 60K Microarray 039381 (Probe Name version)]. GSE63067 and GSE57691 datasets were selected as test datasets to validate the diagnostic value of key genes in the disease. As the data utilized in this study were acquired freely and openly, no approval from the hospital’s ethics committee was essential. To ensure the reliability of the selected datasets, the quality and normalization of each dataset were thoroughly evaluated. Box plots were generated to confirm sample normalization, and principal component analysis (PCA) plots were used to assess sample clustering and detect any potential outliers.
 

## 2.2. Methods

## 2.2.1. DEG screening
In order to explore the relationship between carotid AS and NAFLD, 19 cases with NAFLD and 24 healthy controls were selected from the GSE89632 dataset, along with 29 cases with carotid AS lesions and 12 healthy controls from the GSE100927 dataset for further analysis. Gene expression analysis was performed using the GEO2R online tool (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/geo2r). Box plots and PCA plots were drawn using the ggplot2 package (version 3.3.3) in R software. The normalization of samples was verified through box plots and PCA using the ggplot2 package (version 3.3.3) to ensure data consistency and accuracy prior to differential expression analysis.
DEG screening between NAFLD and control groups, as well as between AS and control groups, was implemented using the limma package (version 3.40.6) in R software. All datasets for DEG screening were required to meet the following criteria: |log2 (FC)| > 1 and P.adj < .05. The resulting DEGs were visualized using volcano plots and heatmaps through the ggplot2 package (version 3.3.3) and the ComplexHeatmap package (version 2.2.0). The Venn online tool (https://bioinfogp.cnb.csic.es/tools/venny/index.html) was utilized to identify common DEGs between the 2 disease groups, and a Venn diagram was generated.

## 2.2.2. Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA)
Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) was conducted using the clusterProfiler package (version 3.3.3) in R software. It was attempted to download the c2.cp.all.v2022.1.Hs.symbols.gmt subset to facilitate an evaluation of pertinent pathways and molecular mechanisms, relying on the gene expression profiles and phenotypic classification. P.adjust < .05 and false discovery rate < 0.25 were considered statistically significant. Then, the results of the GSEA were visualized using the ggplot2 package in R software.

## 2.2.3. Functional enrichment analysis of common DEGs
To further explore the potential biological functions and signaling pathways associated with the common DEGs between NAFLD and AS groups, Gene Ontology (GO) analysis was conducted to assess the biological roles of these genes, focusing on 3 categories: cellular component (CC), molecular function (MF), and biological process (BP). Additionally, the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) analysis was employed to identify the relevant signaling pathways linked to the common DEGs. The clusterProfiler package (version 3.14.3) in R software was utilized for the GO and KEGG pathway enrichment analyses, with significant enrichment results defined as P.adj < .05. Multiple validations were performed by methods Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate. The findings were subsequently visualized using the ggplot2 package (version 3.3.3) in R software.

## 2.2.4. Construction of a protein–protein interaction (PPI) network
The protein–protein interaction (PPI) network analysis was performed using the STRING online tool (https://cn.string-db.org), with a confidence score of 0.4, and the results were visualized using Cytoscape 3.9.1 software. The MCODE (molecular complex detection) algorithm in Cytoscape was utilized to identify and filter highly interconnected gene clusters, facilitating identification of key genes in the network.

## 2.2.5. Validation of hub genes in new datasets
GSE63067 and GSE57691 datasets were selected as test datasets, and the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis was performed using the pROC package (version 1.8.0) in R software to validate the diagnostic value of hub genes in the disease, with an area under the curve of > 0.7 and P < .05 as the criteria for judgment. The data were visualized using the ggplot2 package (version 3.3.3) in R software.

## 2.2.6. microRibonucleic acids (miRNAs)
The miRWalk (http://mirwalk.umm.uni-heidelberg.de/) serves as a predictive platform for elucidating the regulatory interactions between miRNAs and their corresponding messenger ribonucleic acid (mRNA) targets. In this study, miRWalk was utilized to comprehensively analyze and determine the miRNAs that potentially modulate the expression levels of key hub genes.

## 2.3. Statistical analysis
In this study, statistical analysis was performed using R software, with |log2 (FC)| > 1 and P.adj < .05 serving as the thresholds for identifying DEGs. For the GO and KEGG pathway enrichment analyses, a P.adj value of <.05 was utilized as the criterion for significance. Verification of DEGs involved 2 statistical approaches: an independent sample t-test was applied to data that met the assumptions of normality and homogeneity of variances, which were assessed through Shapiro–Wilk and Levene’s tests, respectively. In cases where the assumption of equal variances was violated, as determined by Levene’s test, Welch’s corrected t-test was employed. For all statistical tests, P < .05 was indicative of statistical significance.

## 3. Results

## 3.1. General characteristics of the datasets
In the GEO database, the keywords “Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease” and “atherosclerosis” were utilized for data retrieval. All included datasets were derived from human samples, with a total of 4 datasets selected for analysis. The general characteristics of each dataset are presented in Table .
 

## 3.2. Common DEGs in patients with NAFLD and AS
The 2 datasets included in the study, GSE89632 and GSE10097, had well-normalized samples, and the PCA plot exhibited notable reproducibility between groups, ensuring the reliability of subsequent research (Fig. ).
 Between NAFLD patients and healthy controls, 487 DEGs were totally detected, consisting of 279 downregulated and 208 upregulated DEGs. Notably, the differential gene expression analysis between AS patients and healthy controls revealed 990 DEGs, including 371 downregulated and 619 upregulated DEGs. Venn analysis revealed 45 common DEGs shared between NAFLD and AS, as displayed in Figure and Table .
 

## 3.3. GSEA
The results of GSEA revealed shared biological functions between NAFLD and AS, highlighting several key pathways Figure . Notably, muscle contraction (NSE: −2.729 in GSE89632, −1.42 in GSE10097), cardiac conduction (NSE: −2.64 in GSE89632, −1.56 in GSE10097), FC-gamma receptor (FCgr)-dependent phagocytosis (NSE: 1.91 in GSE89632, 1.80 in GSE10097), cellular response to chemical stress (NSE: 1.54 in GSE89632, 1.31 in GSE10097), lysosome (NSE: 2.07 in GSE89632, 1.64 in GSE10097), and FC-epsilon Ri signaling pathway (NSE: 1.51 in GSE89632, 1.49 in GSE10097) were prominently enriched in both datasets.
 

## 3.4. Functional enrichment analysis of common DEGs
The biological functions of the common DEGs were mainly enriched in regulating cytokine production, increasing responsiveness to external stimuli, and macrophage activation. Their MFs included cytokine receptor binding and cytokine activation. The GO analysis revealed enrichment in 105 BP categories, 4 CC categories, and 10 MF categories. The KEGG pathways were mainly enriched in cytokine–cytokine interaction, toll-like receptor (TLR) signaling pathway, and interleukin-17 (IL-17) signaling pathway, as illustrated in Figure .

A network was constructed consisting of 10 hub genes and 86 miRNAs using the Cytoscape software (Fig. ). ## 4. Discussion
AS is a progressive disease that manifests in the arterial intima, characterized by the accumulation of lipids in the vascular intima. This process leads to the development of atherosclerotic plaques, which constrict the lumen of blood vessels and can potentially result in complete occlusion. Consequently, this can induce prolonged ischemia or necrosis of the organs supplied by the affected vessels. Acute cardiovascular events, including acute myocardial infarction and stroke, attributed to AS, account for over 30% of global mortality, exceeding the combined death toll from cancer, trauma, and infectious diseases.[ ] The formation of coronary artery plaques, along with plaque erosion, rupture, and thrombosis, leads to severe cardiovascular events, such as myocardial ischemia and infarction, which are currently recognized as major risk factors for cardiovascular mortality. Previous studies have demonstrated that carotid atherosclerosis shares key pathological features with coronary atherosclerosis, in contrast to peripheral atherosclerosis.[ ] These shared features include a thin fibrous cap, a large lipid core, and inflammatory cell infiltration, which are linked to ischemic events, such as acute coronary syndrome. The maximum plaque thickness in the carotid artery has been identified as an independent risk factor for coronary events.[ ] Meanwhile, among cardiac patients without clinical evidence of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, a high prevalence of combined aortic and carotid plaques was observed. The presence of carotid plaque reflects the presence of aortic plaque. The occurrence of AS is associated with several risk factors, including lipid metabolism disorders, diabetes, obesity, and hypertension.[ ] Even after controlling for these risk factors, the incidence of AS remains remarkable, suggesting that there may be unknown causes or triggers for AS. Given that NAFLD shares numerous common risk factors with AS, including diabetes, dyslipidemia, obesity, and hypertension, this suggests that there may be shared pathophysiological mechanisms underlying both conditions. The present study found that their common mechanisms could be associated with BPs, such as inflammatory responses and macrophage activation, and lysosomal activity, potentially mediated through TLR signaling pathway, IL-17 signaling pathway, FCgr-dependent phagocytosis, and the FC-epsilon Ri signaling pathway. This study demonstrated the existence of a common mechanism between AS and NAFLD through bioinformatics methods, further elucidating the process of disease onset and development. The findings are of great significance in the study of common therapeutic targets for the 2 diseases, and provide a theoretical basis for the in-depth exploration of clinical management of comorbidities.
Epidemiological data indicated that NAFLD increases the risk of cardiovascular disease by 43%,[ ] which is associated with an excessive risk of nonfatal cardiovascular events, mainly leading to patients’ poor prognosis.[ ] Insulin resistance can lead to excessive accumulation of fat in the liver, triggering hepatocyte injury and inflammatory responses, which is one of the core pathological mechanisms of NAFLD.[ ] In AS, insulin resistance can cause dyslipidemia and endothelial dysfunction, promoting the formation of atherosclerotic plaques.[ ] Zhao et al[ ] demonstrated that an elevated TyG index, as an indicator of insulin resistance, is associated with the occurrence of coronary heart disease in NAFLD patients, and as the TyG index increases, the severity of coronary lesions in NAFLD patients with coronary heart disease also worsens. Correspondingly, in patients diagnosed with chronic liver disease, those who experience cardiovascular events experienced a significant increase in liver fibrosis scores (FIB-4 and NFS), suggesting that acute ischemic events caused by AS may also exacerbate liver damage, including conditions associated with metabolic dysfunction, such as fatty liver disease. The potential mechanism involves increased migration of CCR2 + Ly6Chi monocytes into damaged liver tissue, which exacerbates the detrimental progression of NASH and stimulates the production of fibrotic factors in extrahepatic organs, thereby accelerating liver lipid accumulation and fibrosis.[ ] In summary, AS and NAFLD mutually influence and promote each other during their occurrence and development. Therefore, it is crucial to explore their shared mechanisms for integrated disease management.
Inflammatory responses play a notable role in both AS and NAFLD. The progression of NAFLD is mainly accompanied by inflammatory responses in the liver, which may extend beyond the liver and affect other organs.[ ] Liver inflammation in NAFLD patients can promote the occurrence and development of AS through various mechanisms, including lipid metabolism disorders, insulin resistance, and oxidative stress.[ ] One of the pivotal factors triggering liver inflammatory responses is the activation of TLR signaling pathways, which can induce the production of inflammatory mediators by activating downstream signaling molecules, such as nuclear factor-kappa B (NF-κB) and interferon regulatory factors.[ ] The TLR signaling pathway also directly affects lipid metabolism in hepatocytes by regulating the activity of lipid metabolism enzymes and transcription factors, involving SREBP-1c and PPAR-γ.[ ] Therefore, activation of TLR4 signaling pathway can accelerate the progression of NAFLD by promoting inflammatory responses and lipid accumulation in hepatocytes.[ ] In AS, inflammatory responses are regarded as pivotal to the pathological processes underlying AS, as inflammation in the arterial wall contributes to the formation and instability of atherosclerotic plaques.[ ] The activation of TLR4 signaling pathway is noticeable in AS, where it triggers inflammatory responses in arterial endothelial cells, subsequently promoting the formation and progression of atherosclerotic plaques.[ ] TLR signaling pathways can further exacerbate the pathological process of AS by regulating lipid metabolism and the infiltration of immune cells.[ ] IL-17 is a cytokine secreted by Th17 cells, which primarily acts on various immune and nonimmune cells. The IL-17 signaling pathway is an important immune regulatory factor that modulates inflammatory responses and tissue damage repair by activating downstream signaling pathways.[ ] In NAFLD, IL-17 can promote the expression levels of fatty acid synthase (FAS) and acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC) in adipocytes, increasing lipid synthesis[ ] while inhibiting the activity of lipolytic enzymes in adipocytes, leading to lipid accumulation in the liver.[ ] IL-17 can also stimulate endothelial cells and macrophages to secrete pro-inflammatory factors and chemokines, thereby enhancing inflammatory responses and recruiting monocytes, neutrophils, T cells, and smooth muscle cells to localized areas. This process contributes to the formation of atherosclerotic plaques, exacerbates plaque inflammation, and increases plaque instability.[ ]
The miRNAs could modulate gene expression by disrupting transcription or translation. To identify miRNAs potentially regulating hub genes, construction of a miRNA–mRNA network was undertaken.[ ] Certain miRNAs have been identified as regulators of multiple genes, and their function in hub gene regulation is noteworthy, potentially contributing to NAFLD’s and AS’s progression.
In clinical practice, AS and NAFLD often coexist. The present study utilized bioinformatics to identify potential common biomarkers for AS and NAFLD, and also explored the common pathophysiological mechanisms of both diseases. The diagnostic efficacy of the selected common biomarkers in both diseases was evaluated through ROC curve analysis. Andújar-Vera et al analyzed the genetic correlation between AS and NAFLD using bioinformatics methods. In contrast, the present study first employed GSEA to identify common expression patterns between AS- and NAFLD-associated gene sets. Subsequently, the diagnostic potential of DEGs derived from traditional differential expression analysis was evaluated. The results indicated that these DEGs possessed favorable diagnostic performance in both AS and NAFLD. However, due to financial constraints and the limited availability of clinical samples, we were unable to perform validation in a cohort of patients concurrently diagnosed with AS and NAFLD. Given the methodological limitations, it is particularly important to interpret ROC results for genes with an area under the curve < 0.7 with caution. Thus, the findings were derived exclusively from analyses of publicly available transcriptomic datasets. Employing bioinformatic approaches to elucidate the interaction mechanisms between these diseases and identify potential therapeutic targets may provide valuable insights for their integrated management. However, several limitations of this study should be pointed out. Firstly, this study primarily explored the mechanisms of these comorbidities through bioinformatics, relying on publicly available databases. However, these datasets lacked detailed clinical and demographic information, including patient comorbidities and medication histories. Consequently, the analyses might carry biases due to the dataset’s inherent limitations. Secondly, the identified co-expressed genes have not been validated in patients with comorbidities, and this limitation was mentioned earlier in this section. Thirdly, the analysis was based on mRNA expression data, and the posttranscriptional and posttranslational modifications of genes were not considered. Future research will incorporate proteomic and metabolomic data to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the molecular mechanisms underlying these diseases. Additionally, further validation of the identified hub genes in larger cohorts and in vivo experiments are necessary to confirm their role in the pathogenesis of AS and NAFLD. Despite these limitations, the research provided preliminary insights for investigating the potential interaction between AS and NAFLD, establishing a basis for future studies, outlining that multidisciplinary collaboration may improve disease management and patient outcomes in the future.

## 5. Conclusions
Common DEGs were identified between AS and NAFLD, demonstrating that AS and NAFLD could mutually influence and exacerbate each other during their progression. Dysregulations in the TLR signaling pathway, IL-17 signaling pathway, Fcγ receptor-dependent phagocytosis, and FcεRI signaling pathway may represent key therapeutic targets for managing comorbidities between AS and NAFLD. Investigating the shared pathogenic mechanisms underlying both conditions may aid in the identification of novel therapeutic targets and help prevent the development of AS in patients with NAFLD. Further studies are necessary to validate these findings and assess their clinical implications.
