## Significance
The search for new antibiotics and elaboration of their antibacterial mechanisms of action constitute recognized medical needs. Traditional techniques for studying mechanisms require the lysis of bacterial cells to analyze their genomic and proteomic profiles following treatment with a putative antibacterial agent. Here, we report a photochromic glycomicelle system capable of probing of the dynamic morphological changes of bacteria in response to mechanistically different antibiotics. Our system adheres to bacteria through sugar–lectin interactions and releases antibiotics upon exposure to light in an on-demand fashion. It also exerts a “fluorescence blinking” effect when subjected to alternating ultraviolet/visible photoirradiation, thus enabling the monitoring of how the released antibiotic interrupts the structural integrity of the targeted bacteria in real time.

Bacterial evolution, particularly in hospital settings, is leading to an increase in multidrug resistance. Understanding the basis for this resistance is critical as it can drive discovery of new antibiotics while allowing the clinical use of known antibiotics to be optimized. Here, we report a photoactive chemical probe for superresolution microscopy that allows for the in situ probing of antibiotic-induced structural disruption of bacteria. Conjugation between a spiropyran (SP) and galactose via click chemistry produces an amphiphilic photochromic glycoprobe, which self-assembles into glycomicelles in water. The hydrophobic inner core of the glycomicelles allows encapsulation of antibiotics. Photoirradiation then serves to convert the SP to the corresponding merocyanine (MR) form. This results in micellar disassembly allowing for release of the antibiotic in an on-demand fashion. The glycomicelles of this study adhere selectively to the surface of a Gram-negative bacterium through multivalent sugar–lectin interaction. Antibiotic release from the glycomicelles then induces membrane collapse. This dynamic process can be imaged in situ by superresolution spectroscopy owing to the “fluorescence blinking” of the SP/MR photochromic pair. This research provides a high-precision imaging tool that may be used to visualize how antibiotics disrupt the structural integrity of bacteria in real time.

Commonly, antibiotics are used to eradicate bacteria; however, their overuse has led to the emergence of multidrug-resistant bacterial strains (or “superbugs”) ( , ). It is estimated that 700,000 deaths worldwide are associated with bacterial antimicrobial resistance every year ( ). Several reports have led to the suggestion that a newly approved antibiotic could become less effective against its bacterial targets within ~5 y of being commercialized ( , ). It is also predicted that unless unchecked, infections caused by drug-resistant bacteria could result in ~10 million deaths per annum by 2050, a number larger than that associated with cancer at present ( ). Superbugs attenuate the therapeutic efficacy of antibiotics through minimizing the uptake of drug molecules, inactivating them by endogenous enzymes, or through enhanced efflux of internalized antibiotics ( , ). The identification of new antibacterial agents that overcome these resistance pathways is thus viewed as global medical priority. A key aspect of this finding process is understanding the relevant modes of action.
The antibacterial mechanisms of current antibiotics include 1) the inhibition of cell-wall synthesis, 2) depolarization of outer-membrane, 3) functional intervention of biomacromolecules including proteins and nucleic acids, and 4) disruption of bacterium-specific metabolic pathways ( ). Currently, it is possible to screen for structurally diverse, bioactive small molecules ( ) and nanomaterials ( ) in a high-throughput manner. Of particular interest would be species that operate via unique mechanisms of action and which, therefore, might be less susceptible to the onset of acquired resistance. Unfortunately, effective techniques for assessing the mechanisms of action of potential antibacterial agents remain limited. Genomic and proteomic studies lie at the forefront of methods that are currently being used to examine mechanisms antibacterial action ( , ). While these techniques can provide insights as to how the genome and proteome of bacteria change upon antibiotic treatment, they require the lysis of bacterial cells. This limits their utility in terms of understanding how drug molecules interact with bacterial species in situ. Here, we synthesized two photochromic glycoprobes, Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr and Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr ( ) that form glycomicelles, allowing for the superresolution “fluorescence blinking”-based imaging of bacterial structural collapse triggered by treatment with antibiotics.
 Fluorescent proteins and small-molecule fluorescent probes have played a critical role in the development of live-cell imaging ( ). Many of these probes have proved effective for in situ studies of biomolecular localization ( , ) and activity ( ) in living bacterial cells. By exploiting bioengineering ( ), molecular engineering ( ), and spectroscopic techniques ( ), it has proved possible to monitor a diverse range of dynamic biological events, including those associated with bacterial growth, survival, and death without disrupting their functional integrity ( , ). However, there remains a need for agents with specificity for a given bacterial strain and which permit the superresolution imaging of bacteria ( ). In principle, such agents could provide detailed insights into the dynamic changes in bacterial morphology that presumably occur at the nanoscale as a result of interacting with an antibiotic.
To address the need for superresolution imaging bacterial probes, we synthesized two galactose-modified spiropyrans (Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr and Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr) that self-assemble into micelles in water ( ). Traditional superresolution imaging reagents require the use of imaging buffers and reducing agents to achieve “fluorescence blinking.” In contrast, the fluorescence intensity of photochromic compounds can be directly tuned by light irradiation ( , ); their outstanding reversibility in terms of light-activated structural transformations also makes them more resistant to photobleaching ( ). Upon ultraviolet (UV)-light irradiation, the nonfluorescent SP species is converted to the corresponding merocyanine (MR) form, which emits fluorescence in the 550 to 750 nm spectral range. The fact that the MR form can be converted back to the SP form under visible (Vis) light irradiation makes these constructs, Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr and Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr, of interest as possible probes for superresolution microscopy based on the stochastic optical reconstruction principle ( ). Moreover, the hydrophobic inner core of the glycomicelles may be exploited for antibiotic loading. After adhesion of the micelles onto bacterial outer membrane through multivalent sugar–lectin interaction, the payloads can be released from the system through UV-light irradiation. Finally, the “fluorescence blinking” of the in situ immobilized SP/MR photochromic pair, tuned by alternating UV/Vis irradiation, facilitates the superresolution imaging of the structural collapse of bacterial cells observed in response to treatment with two representative antibacterial agents ( ) as discussed further below.

## Results and Discussion

## Preparation and Characterization of the Glycomicelles.
Previously, we have shown that the SP/MR photochromic pair, when modified with a glycosyl group, can be used for the superresolution imaging of intracellular glycosidase activities with stochastic optical reconstruction microscopy (STORM) ( ). To generate systems of possible use in monitoring antibiotic activity, the basic SP/MR photochromophore was modified with galactose to allow targeting of Lec A. The Lec A protein is expressed on the outer membrane of Pseudomonas aeruginosa (P. aeruginosa), a gram-negative bacterial strain commonly detected in hospital infections. Briefly, the synthesis involved the coupling of a per-acetylated azido galactoside to an N-alkynyl SP via a Cu(I)-catalyzed azide-alkyne 1,3-dipolar cycloaddition (CuAAC) click reaction. Deacetylation then afforded Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr and Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr comprising trimeric and hexameric poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) linkers, respectively ( and SI Appendix, Scheme S1). A control compound (PEG6-Sp/Mr) without the galactosyl unit was synthesized using the same basic synthetic protocol ( and SI Appendix, Scheme S2).
The optical properties of probes Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr, Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr, and PEG6-Sp/Mr were tested using UV-vis and fluorescence microscopy. An absorption band centered at ~525 nm, characteristic of SP ( ), was observed in the UV-vis spectrum of the probes recorded in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS, 0.01 M, pH 7.4). Under 365 nm photoirradiation, the absorption intensity of the band increased with time with the absorption intensity reaching equilibrium after 30 min of irradiation for all probes (SI Appendix, Fig. S1A). This conversion is consistent with photoisomerization of SP to MR. Subsequently, upon continuous Vis-light irradiation, the absorption band decreased gradually, and a new equilibrium was reached within 30 min (SI Appendix, Fig. S1B). This was taken as evidence of light-induced structural conversion of the MR form back to the corresponding SP species ( ). These changes were recapitulated in the corresponding fluorescence emission spectra. For instance, a weak emission band centered at ~625 nm was observed for all probes upon photoexcitation at 525 nm. Similarly, upon UV-light irradiation, the fluorescence emission of all probes increased with time with equilibrium being reached after 30 min (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A). When Vis-light irradiation is applied, the emission band gradually decreased with time (SI Appendix, Fig. S2B). Both the absorption (SI Appendix, Fig. S3) and fluorescence emission intensity ( ) corresponding to the photoisomerized MR state of all probes were virtually unchanged through 10 rounds of UV/Vis irradiation.
 Next, we investigated the micellar behavior of the three probes considered in this study. In PBS, the galactose-modified probes, Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr and Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr, were found to self-assemble into spherical micelles as characterized by transmission electron microscopy ( ). In contrast, PEG6-Sp/Mr, which lacks a saccharide moiety, exhibited a rod-like morphology ( ). After UV light irradiation for 30 min, the spherical structures of Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr and Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr were converted to fiber-like species, while an amorphous morphology was seen in the case of PEG6-Sp/Mr ( ). This leads us to suggest that the in situ photoconversion of the hydrophobic SP unit to the more hydrophilic MR form reduces the propensity to form micelles. Analyses by dynamic light scattering (DLS) gave hydrodynamic diameters of 75 ± 11.8, 143 ± 10.5 and 48 ± 8.7 nm for Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr, Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr, and PEG6-Sp/Mr, respectively (SI Appendix, Fig. S4). When subject to photoirradiation with UV light, the diameter of all probes increased and a broadened particle size distribution was seen. Subjecting to Vis photoirradiation then caused a switch in the particle diameters back to their initial state.

## Fluorescence Imaging of Bacteria and Photocontrolled Antibiotic Release by Glycomicelles.
With their optical properties determined, the photochromic micelles were tested as targeted fluorescence probes for live bacteria. A P. aeruginosa strain (ATCC 27853) known to express Lec A was used as the bacterial model ( , ). Confocal laser-scanning microscopy was used to image P. aeruginosa stained with Gal-PEG3-SP/Mr, Gal-PEG6-SP/Mr, and PEG6-SP/Mr. Bacteria were incubated with the probes for 60 min and then rinsed three times with PBS to remove any putative unbound probe molecules. Bacteria stained with the probes were then exposed to UV light for 5 min prior to imaging over the 570 to 650 nm spectral range corresponding to the fluorescence emission of the MR form. An intensive staining effect was detected in the case of Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr and Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr with the fluorescence of the latter being slightly stronger than that of the former ( ). This finding is interpreted in terms of the longer PEG linker of Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr facilitating lectin binding. Support for this conclusion came from the finding that a competition assay by preincubating ATCC 27853 with an excess of free galactose (10 mM) suppressed the fluorescence of both Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr and Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr ( ). A similar result was also obtained by pretreating ATCC 27853 with methyl anthranilate, a known Lec A binding agent ( ) (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 A and B). Consistent with the proposed adhesion of the glycomicelles to bacterial cells, the fluorescence of glycoprobes Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr and Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr was found to overlap with that of a commercial bacterial staining agent, Syto9. Moreover, a control group stained using PEG6-SP/Mr (i.e., a probe lacking the galactosyl targeting unit) exhibited minimal fluorescence ( ). Taken in concert, these results provide support for the notion that the glycomicelles can selectively bind P. aeruginosa through galactose-mediated sugar–lectin recognition. The imaging experiments were also conducted with two other bacterial strains, methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) and Escherichia coli (E. coli), that do not express Lec A. The results revealed that the glycomicelles did not stain MRSA and E. coli effectively in that no appreciable difference in fluorescence intensity was observed between the glycomicelles and the PEG analog (SI Appendix, Fig. S6 A and B).
 Consistent with our design expectations, the SP to MR structural conversion could be induced in situ after allowing the Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr-based glycomicelles to adhere to P. aeruginosa, as evidenced by the reversibly enhanced and quenched fluorescence seen upon successive UV- and Vis-light photoirradiation ( ). The fluorescence intensity of the MR form was seen to remain almost unchanged through three rounds of this alternating photoirradiation, a finding taken as evidence that the glycomicelle probes of this study are stable when bound to the surface of bacteria. This inferred stability led us to explore whether they could be used to effect the photocontrolled release of antibiotics and actuation of fluorescence-blinking in situ for STORM-based superresolution imaging.
To test the above possibility, we prepared two antibiotic-loaded photochromic glycomicelles containing ceftazidime (CAZ) and levofloxacin (LEV), respectively. CAZ and LEV are first-line antibiotics with different antibacterial mechanisms. They were mixed with Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr and Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr in PBS, and the resulting mixtures were stirred at room temperature for 30 min under UV light. Then, the mixtures were illuminated with Vis light for another 6 h and dialyzed to obtain the antibiotic-loaded glycomicelles. The loading efficiency of Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr and Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr for CAZ/LEV was calculated to be 49/74% and 52/57%, respectively, as inferred from UV-vis spectroscopy (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 A and B). The antibiotic-loaded glycomicelles were then subject to continuous UV light irradiation. This resulted in a 59/53% and 55/52% release of CAZ and LEV from the micelles after 30 min, respectively (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 C and D). By increasing the UV irradiation time to 1 h, almost all the antibiotic contents were released. These results lead us to suggest that the glycomicelles allow for the effective encapsulation as well as photocontrolled release of two structurally unrelated antibiotics. The antibiotic-loaded glycomicelles were also found to be shelf-stable for at least 5 d as inferred from daily DLS analyses (SI Appendix, Fig. S8).
Viability assays were then carried out to test the photocontrolled antibacterial activity of the glycomicelles. For these tests, P. aeruginosa were incubated with the CAZ and LEV-loaded glycomicelles prepared from Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr and Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr for 60 min to permit adequate galactose-mediated micellar adhesion. The bacteria were then treated with UV light. Bacteria not subject to photoirradiation were used as controls. While minimal bacterial death was observed in the absence of UV light, a statistically significant decrease in viability was seen for the bacteria treated with light. Both concentration-dependent and irradiation time-dependent bacterial death was observed in all glycomicelle groups ( ). A maximum death rate of 90/88% was determined for the CAZ/LEV-loaded Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr-based glycomicelles, respectively. Other control experiments, including incubating CAZ- and LEV-free Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr and Gal-PEG3-Sp/Mr-based micelles with P. aeruginosa in the absence and presence of UV photoirradiation, revealed that neither the glycomicelles nor light alone was appreciably toxic to P. aeruginosa bacteria (SI Appendix, Fig. S9). In contrast, a much lower death rate of 28/31% was found for the CAZ/LEV-loaded PEG6-Sp/Mr micelles (SI Appendix, Fig. S10). This finding further supports the postulate that the photoinduced antibiotic release is enhanced by selective galactose-Lec A recognition.
 Next, STORM was used for the in situ monitoring of the interaction between P. aeruginosa and antibiotics. The Gal-PEG6-SP/Mr-based glycomicelles with and without CAZ and LEV loading were used for these superresolution imaging studies. Images were recorded using an electron-multiplying charge-coupled device camera running at 52 ms per frame. To acquire data characterized by enough switching events to render high-quality STORM images ( ), we used a combination of 405 and 561 nm laser light to irradiate the bacterial sample incubated with the antibiotic-loaded glycomicelles, with the emitted time-dependent photons being harvested (SI Appendix, Fig. S11). Photoillumination with a 561 nm laser with 10% power was first used to irradiate the sample. Then, a 405 nm laser was used to produce a readily monitored fluorescence signal and effect release of the incorporated antibiotics through presumed photoswitching from the SP to the MR state. Meanwhile, photoirradiation with 561 nm laser light was used to switch the fluorescently activated MR species back to the corresponding nonfluorescent SP form, thus enabling the “fluorescence blinking” desired for STORM imaging ( ). Using this procedure, a total of 15,000 frames were acquired every 52 ms per frame. The collected frames were divided and reconstructed to monitor what proved to be a gradual increase in the activated fluorescent hot spot. Superresolution images with nanoscale resolution (56 nm, ) were then obtained using a STORM analysis module.
 The ability to obtain superresolution images allowed us to follow the morphological changes produced in P. aeruginosa when the two test antibiotics used in this study were released from their glycomicelle carriers. In the absence of antibiotic loading, the morphology of P. aeruginosa retained constant with time with canonical fluorescent spots surrounding the bacterial cells being readily detected. In contrast, an evident time-dependent disruption of the bacterial cells was seen for P. aeruginosa treated with CAZ-loaded glycomicelles. In this case, monitoring of the photons produced by the constituent probes adhered to bacterial cells allowed the collapse of the bacteria to be readily monitored over the course of 60 min ( ). This finding is consistent with the antibacterial mechanism proposed for CAZ, namely that it disrupts directly the cell-wall biosynthesis of bacteria. In contrast, LEV is thought to interfere with DNA synthesis by inhibiting DNA gyrase and bacterial topoisomerase IV. Thus, a slower cellular collapse was seen for P. aeruginosa when LEV-loaded Gal-PEG6-Sp/Mr was used. Nevertheless, shrinkage of the bacterial cells was seen at 60 min, a finding interpreted in terms of collapse originating from intracellular perturbation. More broadly, our superresolution imaging results support the suggestion that our glycomicelles allow for the in situ release of antibiotics as well as visualization of how the released antibiotic molecules disrupt the structural integrity of the targeted bacteria.

## Conclusion
The photochromic glycomicelles developed in this study display the targeted adhesion to P. aeruginosa bacteria through multivalent sugar–lectin interactions. They also permit photocontrolled release of two test antibiotics that induce structural collapse of P. aeruginosa. Effective fluorescence blinking could be produced by subjecting the immobilized SP/MR photochromic-based probes to controlled photoirradiation with a set of complementary lasers. This blinking, in turn, enabled us to differentiate the collapsing dynamics of bacterial membranes induced by two different antibiotics. We believe that work along the present lines will provide chemical tools that facilitate the in situ analysis of the effects at the nanoscale of various potential antibiotic treatments. The glycosyl targeting group could also be extended to various other sugars, for example, fucose and mannose, which targets Lec B expressed by P. aeruginosa and FimH, an adhesin factor expressed by E. coli, respectively.
