## Abstract

Staphylococcus aureus is a pathogenic bacterium that is increasingly resistant to antibiotics. To identify genes that facilitate this resistance, we analyzed 1062 genomes from the NCBI Genome database. From these genomes, we compile a list of 229 antimicrobial resistance (AMR) genes from the CARD, ResFinder, MEGARes, and AR-ANNOT databases. We then constructed and analyzed their interaction network using multiple network analysis tools to identify critical central genes, designated as hub genes. Our findings revealed that mecA, fosB, arlR, mepA, mgrA, and arlS held the highest significance in the interaction network, suggesting their roles as key genes in AMR. To further examine these genes, we performed functional enrichment and molecular function analyses to better understand the biological processes these genes impact. Additionally, our results indicate that these central genes are predominantly associated with antibiotic efflux and target replacement. These mechanisms confer resistance to disinfectants, fluoroquinolones, antiseptics, tetracyclines, and β-lactam penicillin. Identifying core genes associated with AMR and characterizing their biological functions provides crucial insight into the potential for developing new therapeutic strategies.

## Introduction
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) poses a grave threat to public health, resulting in more than 1.27 million deaths worldwide each year [ ]. The World Health Organization (WHO) warns that failure to address AMR could lead to millions of preventable deaths by 2050 [ ]. The relationship between antibiotic consumption and resistance is well documented, with studies showing that increased antibiotic use correlates with higher resistance rates [ ].
Among the high-priority pathogens contributing to AMR, Staphylococcus aureus is especially concerning due to its versatility and significant impact as both a colonizer and a pathogen of both humans and animals. Identified in a recent WHO classification of global resistance risks [ , ], S. aureus colonizes nearly a third of the healthy human population [ ] and causes a wide range of illnesses, from mild skin infections to life-threatening conditions such as sepsis and necrotizing pneumonia [ ]. Its ability to develop multidrug resistance further limits treatment options, particularly as the incidence of multidrug-resistant (MDR) bacterial pathogens continues to rise in both clinical and community-acquired infections [ ]. Consequently, understanding the key genes involved in AMR in S. aureus is essential for developing effective therapeutic strategies.
AMR genes are often acquired through horizontal gene transfer via mobile genetic elements, including plasmids, transposons, bacteriophages, and the staphylococcal chromosomal cassette mec (SCCmec) [ , ]. SCCmec elements, for example, encode mecA, a gene responsible for methicillin resistance [ , ]. Beyond the acquisition of AMR genes, S. aureus possesses mechanisms that improve bacterial survival, such as efflux pumps (e.g. norA) that reduce intracellular antibiotic concentrations [ ] and β-lactamase enzymes encoded by blaZ, which inactivate β-lactam antibiotics [ ].
The regulatory networks of S. aureus also play a critical role in modulating AMR. For example, the accessory gene regulator (agr) system controls biofilm formation, which increases antibiotic tolerance by physically limiting antibiotic penetration [ – ]. Stress response regulators, like sigB, enable S. aureus to tolerate environmental fluctuations and oxidative stress, which often coincide with antibiotic exposure [ , ]. Two-component systems such as VraSR detect and respond to cell wall stress induced by β-lactams, ultimately improving AMR-related pathways [ ]. Given the diversity and transferability of AMR mechanisms, along with their serious impact on human health, there is an urgent need to improve analysis methods to better combat this growing problem.
Genomics and systems biology approaches offer an integrated perspective on the escalating antibiotic resistance crisis, enabling the identification of critical genes and pathways within gene interaction networks. This approach goes beyond identifying individual molecular mechanisms by integrating genomic and network analyses to reveal broader patterns of AMR gene interactions. Gene interaction networks are increasingly used to study how genomic features influence biological functions [ , ]. Previous research has applied these approaches to the study of multidrug susceptibility in other pathogens, various diseases, and other complex disorders [ , ]. These methodologies have proven useful for identifying genes linked to drug resistance and for developing novel treatments [ – ].
Recent studies have also demonstrated the usefulness of network-based methods in understanding AMR in S. aureus. For example, a protein–protein interaction (PPI) network constructed from the MRSA-252 strain identified hub proteins that had previously been overlooked as potential therapeutic targets [ ]. Specifically, this analysis highlighted proteins including uridylate kinase, 30S ribosomal protein S3, and elongation factor P due to their central roles in maintaining network integrity and cellular stability. Similarly, a genome-scale cofunctional network (StaphNet) constructed from the MRSA strain USA300 identified proteins involved in virulence-associated phenotypes, including biofilm formation and toxin secretion, further demonstrating the utility of network-based methods in the identification of potential therapeutic targets [ ]. Weighted gene co-expression network analysis has also been utilized to identify hub genes involved in host–pathogen interactions, particularly those associated with ubiquitination and autophagy during infection [ ].
Other studies using network-based approaches have taken an expansion-focused perspective. In one such study, researchers began with 52 known AMR genes and used network analysis to identify >1000 interacting partners, ultimately nominating 70 additional genes potentially involved in AMR [ ]. This strategy was designed to uncover novel resistance-related candidates through associations, resulting in a broad, heterogeneous network. Other network-based studies have addressed different biological questions altogether. For example, a recent analysis used gene expression data to distinguish between planktonic and biofilm-forming phenotypes in S. aureus, applying network methods to help identify genes associated with each phenotype and to reveal how these genes may interact or cluster into functionally relevant groups [ ].
Complementary to insights from genomic and network analyses, recent studies have explored additional approaches to targeting AMR in MRSA strains. Network pharmacology analysis showed that berberine disrupts cell-wall integrity, hence improving antibiotic effectiveness [ ]. Furthermore, prediction of PPI identified histone deacetylase as a host–pathogen interaction hub protein, suggesting it as a potential antimicrobial drug target [ ]. Collectively, these findings illustrate the efficacy of combining genomic data with systems biology methods to examine mechanisms underlying AMR to aid in the development of targeted therapeutic interventions.
Here, our objective was to analyze the genes, molecular mechanisms, and pathways that drive drug resistance in S. aureus using network analysis. Based on data from 1062 genomes, we identified 229 genes associated with AMR and constructed a PPI network among these genes. Through multiple clustering methods, we analyzed this network to identify hub genes and examine their pathways and functions to gain a deeper understanding of key AMR mechanisms and the drug classes to which they confer resistance.
Unlike previous studies that expanded outward to identify novel or indirectly connected genes, our analysis focuses exclusively on known AMR genes and their interactions. This core resistome-focused strategy allows us to examine the organizational structure of resistance. Additionally, by integrating network topology, we can assess not only which genes are central but also how robust and cohesive the AMR network is as a system. From a therapeutic perspective, these genes are particularly important because their centrality in interaction networks makes them attractive drug targets [ ]. This study demonstrates how integrating genomic data with systems biology approaches can help identify genes central to resistance networks, even in well-characterized organisms like S. aureus, and offers a framework for investigating resistance in less-studied bacterial species.

## Materials and methods
To better understand AMR in S. aureus, we developed a computational framework that relies on multiple publicly available datasets (Fig.  ). We retrieved whole genome sequences (WGS) of 1062 S. aureus strains from the NCBI Genome database [ ], including both methicillin-resistant (MRSA) and methicillin-sensitive (MSSA) strains. During genome selection, we excluded incomplete assemblies (chromosomes, scaffolds, and contigs), atypical genomes, metagenome-assembled genomes (MAGs), and genomes from large multi-isolate projects. Only complete genome sequences were considered. These genomes were deposited in GenBank over the years 1980 to 2023 ( ). After retrieving the complete genomes of S. aureus, we extracted AMR genes using AMR repositories, including CARD 3.2.8 [ ], ResFinder 4.2.3 [ ], MEGARes 3.0.0 [ ], and ARG-ANNOT 4.0 [ ] (Table  ), and subsequently analyzed these candidate AMR genes.
 The Comprehensive Antibiotic Resistance Database (CARD) [ ] is a curated resource offering detailed information on antibiotic resistance mechanisms. It includes reference DNA and protein sequences that have been uniformly annotated, as well as identification models and computational tools. CARD also incorporates the Antibiotic Resistance Ontology (ARO), developed by its biocuration team, to provide a structured framework for program configuration [ , ]. ResFinder [ ], MEGARES [ ], and ARG-ANNOT [ ] are repositories that index antibacterial resistance genes discovered in whole bacterial genomes. The queries of these databases are accomplished through the usage of BLAST with the wrapper ABRicate 1.0.0 with the default settings [ ] employed to standardize database handling, identity and coverage thresholds, and output formatting [ – ]. Using each of these databases, we compiled the AMR genes and the predicted drug classes associated with these genes. We then employed Venny 2.1 [ ] to gather only the unique AMR genes in our list of genes compiled across those four databases. Venny 2.1 is a tool for mapping and comparing gene lists [ , ]. Once identified, the 229 unique and nonredundant AMR genes were used to build a gene interaction network.

## PPI network construction and visualization
To construct the PPI network, we used STRING [ ]. As a starting point, we provided STRING with a list of the 229 unique AMR genes. During this process, STRING mapped the gene norA to ABD29836.1. We extracted the PPI network using low confidence parameters ( 0.150) and found that only 10 of our 229 genes shared interactions. These included mecA, fosB, arlR, mepA, mgrA, arlS, msrA2, msrA1, norA, and isaB. See for a description of each of these genes. We use Cytoscape v3.9.1 [ ], a widely used platform for the visualization and analysis of interaction networks, to examine our network.

## Hub proteins extraction
Hub proteins, also known as key proteins, were characterized by their high connectivity or associations with other proteins in PPI networks [ ]. A hub protein is a network-theoretic designation from centrality rankings. To identify these highly interconnected protein nodes and analyze network topology, we employed the cytoHubba plugin for Cytoscape [ , ]. Using this tool, we ranked potential hub genes within our network by applying nine distinct topological analysis methods to evaluate node centrality and importance. These methods included Degree, Edge Percolated Component (EPC), Maximum Neighborhood Component (MNC), Density of Maximum Neighborhood Component (DMNC), Maximal Clique Centrality (MCC), and four centrality measures-Bottleneck, EcCentricity, Closeness, and Betweenness (Table  ). These centrality measures are deterministic calculations based on network topology, providing consistent rankings for a given network structure. The use of multiple complementary measures improves the robustness of the analysis by capturing different aspects of node importance within the AMR network. Among these methods, MCC is known for its precision in identifying key functional proteins within biological networks [ ]. See for a description of each of these methods. Closeness, Degree, and MNC all shared the same rankings. Notably, mecA, fosB, arlR, mepA, mgrA, arlS, and msrA2 have more similar average rankings, all under 4. Then there is a jump in the average ranking for msrA1, ABD29836.1/norA, and isaB, suggesting that these three genes are less central to the network.
 

## Network topology and robustness analysis
To characterize the structural properties of our AMR gene interaction network, we assessed several network properties, including degree distribution, clustering characteristics, path length metrics, modularity, and assortativity using igraph [ ], applying approaches similar to those described in previous biological network studies [ , ]. Scale-free properties were evaluated through power-law distribution analysis by fitting a linear regression to log-transformed degree frequencies and calculating the coefficient of determination (R ).
Network robustness was evaluated through multiple approaches. We conducted node removal analysis by systematically removing the top five highest-degree nodes and measuring the impact on network connectivity and largest component size. We also performed an edge perturbation analysis by randomly removing 10% of edges over 1000 iterations and calculating correlations between the original and perturbed centrality measures. Finally, we assessed the consistency of hub gene identification by computing Pearson correlations between different centrality measures (degree, betweenness, closeness, and eigenvector centrality). These metrics were also computed using igraph [ ].

## Assessment of gene co-occurrence
To examine patterns of hub gene co-occurrence and distribution across the phylogeny, we generated a phylogenetic tree with hub gene presence/absence annotations, where each hub gene was color-coded and displayed as a heatmap adjacent to the tree tips with ggtree [ ]. We used UpSet [ ] to quantify the frequency and patterns of combinations of hub genes in genomes, identifying the most common hub genes and their co-occurrence patterns.
To determine whether hub gene presence/absence patterns exhibited phylogenetic signal we employed two statistical approaches, Pagel’s lambda ( ) to assess fit to Brownian motion evolution (0 = no signal, 1 = strong signal) with significance tested using likelihood ratio tests, and a phylogenetic conservatism test to determine whether hub gene presence/absence were more closely related than expected by chance using randomization tests that compared mean pairwise phylogenetic distances to null distributions from random sampling (1000 randomizations), with significant results indicating phylogenetic clustering. Both of these analyses were performed with phytools [ ].
To examine broader gene co-occurrence patterns across the dataset, we constructed a co-occurrence count matrix using all 229 unique AMR genes. For each genome, we identified the unique AMR genes present and calculated pairwise co-occurrences. The resulting co-occurrence counts were compiled into a symmetric matrix where each cell represents the number of genomes in which genes appear together, with diagonal elements representing the total number of genomes in which each individual gene appears. Due to the large number of genes in the complete dataset, for visualization purposes, we filter so that we only include genes that appeared in at least 400 genomes, reducing the matrix to a manageable size while preserving the most represented gene relationships along with many of our hub genes. The filtered co-occurrence matrix was visualized as a clustered heatmap using hierarchical clustering to group genes with similar co-occurrence patterns.

## Assessment of gene enrichment
We performed functional enrichment analysis on AMR genes with the use of ShinyGO [ ] v0.81. Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment is a method used to evaluate gene sets within the gene ontology classification system, where genes are grouped based on their functional properties [ ]. Gene Ontology is categorized into three distinct activities: biological activities, cellular activities, and molecular activities. Here, the term biological activities refers to the major cellular or metabolic significance of genes in coordination with other genes, cellular activities refer to the role of gene products within the cell, whereas molecular activities refer to the specific molecular function of a gene [ ].

## Examining the relationship between antibiotic resistance mechanism and drug class
Further, using the genes in our PPI network, we identified specific AMR mechanisms and associated drug classes. To better visualize and interpret these relationships, we generated a Sankey plot that illustrates the connections between individual genes, their AMR mechanisms, and the drug classes they affect, providing a high-level view of AMR pathways in S. aureus. This integrated approach allowed us to not only identify key genes but also to map their functional roles in conferring resistance against multiple drug classes.

## Results

## Collection of AMR gene
We retrieved the WGS of 1062 genomes of S. aureus from the NCBI Genome database and identified 30 320 AMR genes using four databases. Among these AMR genes, 11 047 were from CARD [ ], 3325 were from Resfinder [ ], 9110 from ARG-ANNOT [ ], and 6838 from MEGARes [ ]. Out of 30 320 collected AMR genes, 229 entries were nonredundant as identified by Venny 2.1 [ ].

## PPI network analysis and hub proteins identification
We used STRING [ ] to generate the PPI network from 229 genes. In a PPI network, proteins or genes are denoted as nodes, while interconnections between these nodes are denoted as edges. Figure shows the PPI network identified from our 229 unique genes. Of the 229 genes, 10 were found to have connections in STRING. Using the cytoHubba application [ ], we applied nine topological analysis methods and identified the genes: mecA, fosB, arlR, mepA, mgrA, arlS, and msrA2 as central within the network. These genes consistently ranked highly across all techniques, emphasizing their critical roles (Table  ).
 

## Network topology analysis
The AMR PPI network consisted of 10 nodes and 32 edges, had a high network density of 0.71 (71% of all possible connections), indicating extensive interconnectivity. The network exhibited a connected topology with degree values ranging from 2 to 9 (mean degree = 6.4), indicating that each protein interacts with an average of 6.4 other proteins in the network. Power-law distribution analysis revealed that the network does not exhibit scale-free properties (R = 0.053, power-law exponent = −0.27), which is expected [ ] especially for small, functionally constrained biological networks, as networks with fewer than 10 nodes lack sufficient data to reliably demonstrate scale-free properties [ ]. The network demonstrated high clustering characteristics, with a global clustering coefficient of 0.785 and an average local clustering coefficient of 0.809, indicating dense local connectivity. Path length analysis revealed efficient connectivity with an average path length of 1.289 and a network diameter of 2, again reflecting the highly connected nature of this PPI. Modularity analysis using both Walktrap and Louvain indicated minimal community structure (modularity = 0.00 and 0.03, respectively), suggesting the network is a single cohesive unit rather than containing distinct functional modules. Degree assortativity analysis revealed disassortative mixing (r = −0.28), indicating that high-degree hub genes tend to connect to lower-degree nodes, which is again consistent with high interconnectivity.
Node removal analysis demonstrated network robustness, with removal of any single node resulting in 10% connectivity loss while maintaining network integrity, as expected from our highly connected 10-node network. Edge perturbation analysis revealed high stability of centrality rankings, with strong correlations maintained between original and perturbed networks (mean degree correlation = 0.938 0.024; mean betweenness correlation = 0.884 0.135). Centrality correlation analysis validated our multi-metric hub identification approach, showing strong positive correlations between different centrality measures: degree-eigenvector centrality (r = 0.984), degree-closeness centrality (r = 0.982), and closeness-eigenvector centrality (r = 0.940). These strong intercorrelations highlight the robustness of the network structure.

## Distribution of hub genes across genomes
Analysis of hub gene distribution across the dataset revealed substantial variation in prevalence. Three hub genes arlR, arlS, and mgrA were present in 851 genomes, representing the most widely distributed hub genes in our dataset, while mepA was nearly as prevalent, appearing in 850 genomes. The most common hub gene combination was the co-occurrence of arlR, arlS, mgrA, mepA, and fosB together, which was observed in 230 genomes ( ).
The phylogenetic distribution of our hub genes shows that hub gene presence is represented across many of our genomes ( ). Phylogenetic signal analysis revealed that all hub genes showed significant phylogenetic signal under Pagel’s lambda test ( ). Lambda values ranged from 0.835 to 0.992, with all P-values 0.001, indicating strong departure from random trait evolution across the phylogeny. However, phylogenetic conservatism tests revealed no significant clustering of gene-positive taxa for any hub gene (all P-values 0.1). This pattern reflects the widespread distribution of hub genes across our dataset, since the majority of genomes possess these genes, random sampling of taxa is likely to include gene-positive genomes regardless of phylogenetic relationships. The combination of high lambda values with nonsignificant conservatism tests indicates that while hub gene presence/absence patterns follow phylogenetic constraints and are not randomly distributed, their near-ubiquitous presence across lineages prevents detection of phylogenetic clustering. This suggests that hub genes represent core functional components that are maintained across diverse lineages while still being subject to evolutionary processes that respect phylogenetic structure. Additional AMR genes that frequently occur in the same genomes with our highly represented hub genes included aminoglycoside resistance genes aph3’, dihydrofolate reductase dha1, and tetracycline resistance gene tet38, indicating that hub genes tend to co-occur with one another and with other known AMR genes ( ).

## Functional enrichment analysis
Functional enrichment analysis showed that eight of the ten genes identified as sharing a connection had statistically significant associations with biological processes (Fig.  ). This analysis highlighted the versatility in the two-component regulatory system genes arlS and arlR, which participated in the highest number of distinct biological processes, including signal transduction, phosphorelay signaling, cell communication, and pathogenesis, suggesting their critical function as regulators of antibiotic resistance. The enrichment of mgrA, arlS, and arlR in pathogenesis-related processes further emphasizes the interconnection between virulence and AMR in S. aureus. The msrA1 and msrA2 genes showed significant enrichment in oxidative stress response pathways, suggesting they may have a role in protecting the bacterium against oxidative damage that often accompanies antibiotic stress [ ]. Among all of the processes identified, “response to stimulus” emerged as the predominant biological process, involving seven AMR-associated genes (mepA, mecA(ClpC), msrA1, arlS, arlR, msrA2, and fosB). This suggests that S. aureus’s ability to detect and respond to stimulus, such as antibiotic exposure, is fundamental to its resistance mechanisms. Within this broader category, five genes (mecA (ClpC), msrA1, arlS, arlR, and msrA2) were specifically engaged in cellular response to stimulus. It is important to note that in this analysis, the mecA gene identified here represents a regulatory ClpC adaptor protein rather than the resistance-associated PBP2a protein, due to a gene name ambiguity issue, which is discussed in detail in the following section. To clarify this difference, we denote the version of mecA that represents a regulatory ClpC adaptor protein as mecA (ClpC).
 The functional enrichment analysis also shows significant enrichment of signal transduction pathways, particularly phosphorelay and intracellular signaling systems associated with arlS and arlR, highlighting the importance of these regulatory mechanisms in coordinating AMR responses. More specialized processes, such as cellular response to oxidative stress, exhibited high fold enrichment values ( 150), despite being associated with only two genes (msrA1 and msrA2), indicating the critical importance of these highly specific protective mechanisms in AMR. provides the complete list of enriched biological processes, their associated genes, fold enrichment values, and statistical significance.
The molecular function analysis, illustrated in Fig.  , identified statistically significant associations for five of the ten analyzed AMR genes, highlighting their specialized molecular functions. Most notably, msrA1 and msrA2 demonstrate high fold enrichment ( 400) for peptide-methionine (S)-S-oxide reductase activity, again suggesting their role in protecting bacterial proteins from oxidative damage during antibiotic stress. This function, essential for maintaining cellular integrity under stress conditions, appears to be a significant mechanism through which these genes contribute to AMR.
 Transcription-related functions are another significant category, with mgrA and arlR exhibiting extensive involvement in DNA-binding activities. These genes are enriched in multiple related functions, including transcription regulatory region sequence-specific DNA binding, regulatory region nucleic acid binding, and DNA-binding transcription factor activity. The consistent enrichment of these genes across multiple DNA-interaction categories suggests their function as transcriptional regulators that likely control the expression of downstream AMR genes in response to antibiotic exposure.
The analysis also highlights specialized signaling functions, particularly for arlS, which shows significant enrichment for histidine phosphotransfer kinase activity and molecular transducer activity. This reinforces the role of the arlS-arlR two-component system in sensing and responding to environmental stimuli, including antibiotics. The signal transduction capabilities of arlS complement the transcriptional regulatory functions of arlR, equipping S. aureus with a coordinated system to detect and respond to antimicrobial compounds through alterations in gene expression patterns. ## Gene name ambiguity and functional annotation of mecA
Notably, the mecA gene identified in this functional enrichment analysis is likely the regulatory ClpC adaptor protein found in non-MRSA strains, rather than the resistance-associated mecA (PBP2a) typically carried on SCCmec elements, as evidenced by its association with GO terms related to regulation (Fig.  ). This type of annotation ambiguity is common in large-scale analyses and, in this case, results from the reuse of gene names within a species, two genes both named mecA in S. aureus that are functionally distinct. This is a known limitation of name-based mapping approaches, particularly in bacterial genomes where conserved gene names are often repurposed across strains and functional contexts [ – ]. This specific annotation issue has been noted in a previous study [ ].
In our GO term enrichment analysis, gene annotation is performed by matching gene names from the input list to the reference genome of S. aureus NCBI Taxonomy ID: 1280. This strain, commonly used in functional genomics tools such as ShinyGO, does not possess the resistance-associated mecA (PBP2a) gene. Currently, ShinyGO only offers two S. aureus reference genomes for selection (NCBI Taxonomy IDs 1280 and 93061), neither of which represent all MRSA strains nor contain the resistance-associated mecA gene (PBP2a). Instead, they contain a gene called mecA annotated as a “conserved hypothetical protein” involved in the recognition and targeting of unfolded and aggregated proteins to the ClpC protease or other components of the proteolytic machinery. This protein is homologous to a competence gene mecA, and thus receives functional annotations based on phylogenetic assignment. As a result, the functional enrichment terms associated with mecA reflect the regulatory and proteostasis roles of this regulatory gene, not β-lactam resistance.
This highlights the importance of interpreting functional annotation results with caution, especially in non-model organisms, cases where gene names may be ambiguous, or duplicated across strains. Rather than removing mecA from our functional enrichment analysis, we have retained it and specifically denote the ClpC version as mecA (ClpC) to ensure clarity and transparency.

## Genes associated with resistance mechanisms and drug class analysis
Using data from the CARD [ ] database, we identified and visualized the relationships between specific S. aureus genes, their AMR mechanisms, and corresponding drug classes in a Sankey plot (Fig.  ). Note that only eight genes were annotated in CARD with specific resistance mechanisms and drug class associations. This visualization maps AMR genes to their respective mechanisms and drug classes, showing the pathways through which S. aureus resists various antimicrobial treatments. The Sankey diagram consists of three columns representing genes (left), resistance mechanisms (right), and drug classes (middle), with connecting flows indicating the associations between these elements.
 We find that antibiotic efflux is the most prevalent AMR mechanism in S. aureus, with five genes (mgrA, arlR, arlS, mepA, and norA) contributing to this process. Each of these genes connects to the antibiotic efflux mechanism and also branches from multiple drug classes. mgrA demonstrates connections to six different drug classes: fluoroquinolones, disinfectants, tetracyclines, cephalosporins, penams, and peptide antibiotics [ ]. The genes arlR and arlS, which comprise a two-component regulatory system, both connect to disinfectants and fluoroquinolone antibiotics [ ]. The gene mepA shows connections specifically to tetracyclines and glycylcyclines [ ], while norA links exclusively to fluoroquinolone antibiotics.
The diagram also displays three additional AMR mechanisms beyond antibiotic efflux. The antibiotic target protection mechanism represents the second most prevalent resistance strategy, with msrA as its contributing gene. This mechanism connects to three drug classes: macrolide antibiotics, streptogramin antibiotics, and streptogramin B antibiotics [ ]. The antibiotic target replacement mechanism is represented solely by mecA, which is specific to penam antibiotics, mecA encodes an alternative penicillin-binding protein that remains functional in the presence of β-lactams [ ]. Similarly, the antibiotic inactivation mechanism is represented by a single gene, fosB, which confers resistance exclusively to phosphonic acid antibiotics.
Analysis of the flow patterns in the Sankey diagram provides quantitative information about the distribution of AMR mechanisms. The antibiotic efflux mechanism receives the highest number of contributing genes, particularly from mgrA, indicating the predominance of efflux-mediated resistance in S. aureus. Among drug classes, fluoroquinolones have the most resistance-associated genes feeding into them, followed by disinfectants and tetracyclines, suggesting these antimicrobials are subject to the greatest number of AMR determinants. In contrast, the antibiotic inactivation mechanism, represented solely by fosB, has only one contributing connection, indicating a more specialized resistance strategy against phosphonic acid antibiotics. Similarly, the antibiotic target replacement mechanism, represented solely by mecA, is exclusively linked to penam antibiotics, reinforcing its primary role in β-lactam resistance by producing an alternative penicillin-binding protein. Additionally, mgrA is associated with beta-lactam resistance through its regulation of efflux pumps, suggesting that S. aureus employs both target modification and efflux to counteract β-lactam antibiotics.
The distribution of genes across AMR mechanisms in Fig.  reveals distinct patterns of connectivity. Most genes in the network (mgrA, arlR, arlS, mepA, norA, mecA, and msrA) each contribute to a single AMR mechanism type but influence multiple drug classes through these mechanisms. mgrA exhibits the most extensive connectivity, linking to six different drug classes through the antibiotic efflux mechanism. arlR and arlS each connect to two drug classes (fluoroquinolones and disinfectants), mepA links to tetracyclines and glycylcyclines, and msrA connects to three related drug classes through the target protection mechanism. This pattern demonstrates that while genes typically employ a single AMR mechanism, they often confer resistance to multiple antimicrobial drugs through that mechanism, highlighting the efficiency of certain AMR determinants in protecting against diverse therapeutic interventions.
The network topology (Fig.  ) demonstrates the centrality of certain genes in the AMR network. mecA, mepA, mgrA, and arlR appear as central in the network, consistent with their identification through network analysis methods earlier in our study. Among these, mgrA shows the highest number of connections to different drug classes, making it the most versatile AMR determinant in the network. The genes arlR and arlS show identical connection patterns, confirming their functional association as components of a two-component regulatory system. In contrast, mecA, fosB, and msrA display more specialized resistance profiles, each connecting to a single drug class or a small group of related drug classes.
The Sankey visualization also reveals the relative distribution of AMR mechanisms across the antimicrobial spectrum. Of the eleven drug classes appearing in the diagram, six connect exclusively to the antibiotic efflux mechanism, three connect exclusively to antibiotic target protection, one connects exclusively to antibiotic inactivation, and one (penams) connects to multiple mechanisms. This distribution underscores the predominance of efflux-mediated resistance in the S. aureus resistome, while highlighting the specialized nature of other resistance mechanisms such as target replacement, target protection, and antibiotic inactivation.

## Discussion
The rapid emergence of AMR in S. aureus poses significant challenges to public health worldwide. Our study used WGS data and systems biology approaches to provide insights into hub genes driving AMR in S. aureus. These findings contribute to a more nuanced understanding of how S. aureus develops multidrug resistance, providing a foundation for targeting these central genes in therapeutic strategies. One of the most notable findings of our study was the identification of hub genes, including mecA, fosB, and arlR, which were consistently ranked as central to the AMR network. These genes are implicated in key AMR mechanisms such as antibiotic target modification and antibiotic efflux. The central role of mecA in the PPI network reaffirms its well-established function in methicillin resistance, where it encodes an altered penicillin-binding protein (PBP2a) that prevents beta-lactam antibiotics from effectively inhibiting cell wall synthesis [ ].
The identification of additional hub genes, including mgrA and arlR, offers insights into the interconnected nature of the AMR network. For instance, mgrA has multiple regulator functions modulating the expression of genes linked to multiple antibiotic classes. Beyond its regulatory role, mgrA interacts with a wide range of AMR genes, suggesting its broader impact on multidrug resistance and its potential as a high-value target for therapeutic strategies [ ]. Similarly, arlR, part of the two-component arlR/arlS regulatory system, plays an important role in regulating cell wall biosynthesis and responding to environmental stresses, including antibiotic pressure. The centrality of arlR highlights the critical role of regulatory systems in enabling survival under antimicrobial stress. Interestingly, mgrA, a global transcriptional regulator, is functionally linked to arlR/arlS as both systems modulate bacterial responses to stress and regulate genes involved in virulence and resistance. Specifically, mgrA influences the expression of efflux pumps and other AMR mechanisms that complement the stress-adaptive roles of arlR/arlS. This interplay suggests that these systems may work together to improve S. aureus’s ability to withstand antimicrobial drugs [ , ].
Network topology analysis revealed that our AMR PPI network exhibits high density and clustering, demonstrating extensive interconnectivity. Robustness analysis supported these findings that the network remains stable under perturbation and that multiple centrality measures yield consistent results. The genomic distribution analysis further reinforces the functional importance of these hub genes, with arlR, arlS, mgrA, mepA, and fosB, representing the most widely distributed genes that frequently co-occur across S. aureus genomes.
Our functional enrichment analysis provided information on the biological processes and pathways involved in AMR. Processes such as cellular response to stimuli, signal transduction, and protein modification were significantly enriched, reflecting the diverse strategies employed by S. aureus to counteract stress [ ]. The predominance of stimulus-response pathways demonstrates how S. aureus has evolved detection and adaptation mechanisms to survive exposure to antimicrobial agents [ ]. These enrichment patterns support the idea that AMR is an active, coordinated response involving multiple cellular systems that work together to ensure bacterial survival under antibiotic pressure.
Pathways associated with response to stimulus and regulation were also significantly enriched, with regulatory genes like mgrA and arlR and arlS participating in multiple overlapping pathways. Additionally, protein modification processes, particularly post-translational modifications, were highly represented in our analysis. These findings align with prior studies suggesting that protein modifications play an essential role in activating efflux pumps and other AMR mechanisms [ ]. The enrichment of oxidative stress response pathways, particularly associated with msrA1 and msrA2, further suggests that AMR mechanisms are integrated with general stress response systems, enabling S. aureus to withstand both the primary effects of antibiotics and the secondary stresses they induce.
The identification of genes involved in signal transduction, particularly two-component systems like arlR/arlS, highlights the importance of bacterial communication and regulatory systems in the development of AMR [ ]. These systems allow for rapid adaptation to environmental changes, including the presence of antibiotics, by modulating gene expression and protein activity in response to external signals. The prominence of these systems in our network analysis suggests they serve as an important part of the AMR response system. Disrupting these signaling pathways may represent a potential therapeutic strategy to reduce resistance by impairing their ability to mount effective responses to antibiotics [ ].
Our analysis of the association between AMR genes and AMR mechanisms provides a detailed perspective on how S. aureus evades antibiotic treatment. AMR mechanisms such as antibiotic efflux, target modification, and target protection were consistent with previous studies [ , , ] while providing information on the interactions between these processes. Genes such as arlR/arlS, mgrA, and mepA were strongly associated with efflux-mediated resistance, improving S. aureus survival under antibiotic stress [ ].
The predominance of efflux-related genes in our network highlights the central role of active transport in AMR, suggesting that efflux inhibition could be a particularly effective approach against S. aureus. Meanwhile, mecA remains the only hub gene identified that acts through target modification, conferring resistance to β-lactam antibiotics by encoding an alternative binding protein. Additionally, msrA1 demonstrates the importance of target protection by preventing antibiotic binding to cellular targets, complicating treatment efforts [ ].
Our analysis of the association between AMR genes and specific drug classes revealed several key interactions. For instance, mecA was linked to resistance against only penams, while mepA was associated with resistance to tetracyclines and glycylcyclines [ , ]. The identification of hub genes such as mecA, mgrA, and arlR as central players in the AMR network, shows that these genes not only contribute directly to AMR mechanisms but also potentially coordinating with other AMR genes, making them possible candidates for multi-drug therapies aimed at disrupting AMR networks. Targeting regulatory systems such as arlR/arlS could be particularly effective, as inhibiting the signaling pathways that regulate gene expression in response to antibiotic exposure may sensitize S. aureus to existing antibiotics, restoring their efficacy. Similarly, intervention strategies directed at mgrA could potentially disrupt resistance to multiple antibiotic classes simultaneously, given its broad regulatory influence. Additionally, inhibitors of efflux pumps could prevent antibiotic expulsion, increasing intracellular concentrations and improving treatment outcomes [ ].
Our network-based approach to studying AMR provides several advantages over traditional gene-by-gene analyses or those limited to a single S. aureus strain. By examining the interconnected nature of AMR determinants, we gain insight into the system-level properties that enable S. aureus to adapt to diverse antimicrobial pressures. This perspective highlights potential vulnerabilities in the AMR network that might not be apparent when considering individual genes alone. Furthermore, the identification of central hub genes provides a more focused set of targets for future research, potentially accelerating progress toward effective interventions against resistant S. aureus infections.

## Peripheral genes and indirect resistance mechanisms
The hub genes identified in this study function within extensive regulatory networks that integrate peripheral genes involved in metabolism, stress response, and cell wall homeostasis to enhance bacterial survival under antimicrobial pressure. Central to these networks is the arlR/arlS-mgrA regulatory cascade, which controls genes involved in virulence, antibiotic resistance, autolysis, and biofilm formation. This cascade demonstrates remarkable regulatory breadth, with 70% overlap between arlR/arlS and mgrA regulons [ ], and mgrA alone regulating 355 genes (175 positively and 180 negatively) [ ].
Individual hub genes exhibit sophisticated regulatory mechanisms that exemplify these network connections. fosB can be upregulated through IS1182 element transposition, which provides additional promoters for fosfomycin resistance [ ]. The mepRAB cluster demonstrates substrate-responsive regulation, where mepR differentially controls multiple operators-substrate binding relieves repression of the mepA efflux pump while maintaining regulatory control over peripheral genes [ ]. These regulatory mechanisms are further integrated through the agr quorum-sensing system, which connects to resistance networks via RNAIII-mediated stabilization of mgrA mRNA, demonstrating how non-AMR regulatory elements contribute to resistance phenotypes through post-transcriptional mechanisms [ ].
These interconnected regulatory webs illustrate that AMR genes operate not as isolated AMR determinants but as components of integrated systems that coordinate metabolic stress responses, cell wall homeostasis, and global regulatory cascades to collectively to aid in AMR beyond direct drug-target interactions.

## Future directions
While our analysis provides valuable insights, it also underscores the need for experimental validation of the predicted gene–drug interactions and AMR mechanisms. Integrating network analysis with functional genomics, transcriptomics, and proteomics would offer a more dynamic and comprehensive understanding of how AMR networks function under antibiotic exposure.
Pan-genome analysis could clarify whether hub genes are part of the core or accessory genome, shedding light on their evolutionary stability and potential for horizontal gene transfer. Expanding the network to include non-AMR genes interacting with identified hubs may uncover auxiliary regulators, metabolic components, or stress response pathways that indirectly sustain resistance. Such an expanded view would contextualize the role of central regulators like arlR and mgrA within broader cellular networks, potentially identifying new contributors to resistance phenotypes and revealing additional therapeutic targets beyond the canonical resistome.
Molecular docking and simulation approaches could further assess the druggability of key hub genes, especially mgrA, arlR, and mecA. Previous studies have demonstrated the utility of machine learning and docking strategies in identifying antimicrobial targets in S. aureus [ , ]. Similar methods could guide the development of specific inhibitors targeting these hubs or their protein products.
It is important to note that PPI networks offer a static snapshot of gene interactions, which may not reflect the dynamic changes occurring under varying environmental conditions. Integrating transcriptomic or proteomic data could enhance our understanding of AMR-related regulatory circuits and their responsiveness to antibiotic pressure. Moreover, examining the evolutionary context of hub genes, whether they represent ancient, co-evolved mechanisms or recently acquired mobile elements, could provide insight into their persistence and transmissibility within bacterial populations.
Finally, although our current analysis focuses on S. aureus, this pipeline is broadly applicable to other priority pathogens with available genome data. These include Carbapenem-resistant Enterobacterales (CRE), Carbapenem-resistant Acinetobacter, Vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus (VRE), Extended-spectrum β-lactamase (ESBL)-producing Enterobacterales, and MDR Pseudomonas aeruginosa [ ]. Future applications of this framework could support the identification of resistance-associated hubs across a wider range of clinically relevant bacteria.

## Limitations
Although this study provides information on the key genes that contribute to AMR in S. aureus, several limitations should be considered. The analysis relies on in silico data from publicly available genomes, which may not capture the full diversity of genomes, particularly those emerging in specific clinical settings. Additionally, the functional predictions made in this study require experimental validation to confirm the roles of the identified genes and pathways in AMR. Another limitation is the restricted gene coverage achieved through STRING database analysis, capturing only a small fraction of resistance genes for network analysis. Alternatives such as integrative platforms [ ] and species-focused resources [ ] can increase coverage, yet coverage remains uneven. Species-focused databases are scarce and reduce generalizability; broader integrators still overrepresent model organisms and under-sample non-model systems. Furthermore, the use of gene-based annotations introduces potential for nomenclature-related errors, as illustrated by the ambiguity surrounding mecA, where distinct genes with different functions share the same name.

## Conclusion
AMR in virulent bacteria remains a pressing global health threat and a persistent challenge in clinical practice. In this study, 1062 S. aureus genomes were analyzed to identify key hub genes within the resistance network. By mapping connections among resistance mechanisms, drug classes, and specific genes, the analysis underscores the complexity of S. aureus AMR. This work builds on previous studies by integrating data from multiple AMR repositories into a unified, curated reference, offering a comprehensive view of AMR determinants. These findings lay the groundwork for future efforts to target influential nodes within the network and to explore regulatory systems as potential therapeutic entry points.
