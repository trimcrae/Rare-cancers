## Abstract
Abnormal amyloid beta (Aβ) aggregation is a key factor in Alzheimer’s disease (AD) pathology. Despite efforts to develop Aβ aggregation inhibitors, efficient screening strategies remain limited. Here, we initially applied structure-based virtual screening to target Aβ fibril ends, aiming to identify compounds that could effectively combat AD neurodegeneration. Using this approach, we screened a traditional Chinese medicine library with thousands of compounds, and selected high-scoring hits likely to inhibit fibril elongation or promote fibril depolymerization. Forsythoside A emerged as the most potent inhibitor, effectively reducing Aβ40 aggregation and disassembling pre-formed fibrils, as confirmed through biophysical assays and surface plasmon resonance analysis. Chemical kinetics further elucidated its inhibitory mechanism. In Caenorhabditis elegans, forsythoside A alleviated Aβ-induced toxicity and extended the lifespan of the AD transgenic models. Further virtual screening and experimental validation using a CNS-penetrant compound library confirmed the robustness of our strategy. Our study underscores the effectiveness of virtual screening in the identification of Aβ aggregation inhibitors and emphasizes the critical significance of targeting fibril ends.

## Introduction
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) continues to be a major health threat to human beings. In 2019, it was reported that 55 million people worldwide were suffering from dementia, and this number is expected to increase to 139 million by 2050 . The amyloid cascade hypothesis believes that the aggregation of amyloid beta (Aβ) is the primary cause of AD – . Based on this hypothesis, eliminating Aβ aggregates from the brain and inhibiting Aβ aggregation are two promising strategies for the prevention and treatment of AD.
Aβ-based antibody therapies have shown positive results in recent years. Between 2021–2023, the FDA has approved two monoclonal antibodies, aducanumab and lecanemab , for AD treatment. These antibodies bind to Aβ aggregates and can not only reduce amyloid burden in the brain but also slow cognitive decline. Another antibody, donanemab, which targets pyroglutamate Aβ – , has also shown promising results in clinical trials , and has been approved by the FDA on 2024 . Although these antibodies are far from curative and debates remain regarding the FDA approvals , the positive results from clinical trials support the development of AD therapies based on Aβ.
Considering the potential side effects and relatively high cost of antibody therapies, developing inhibitors for Aβ aggregation seems to be a safer and more affordable alternative for AD treatment. Various types of molecules have been developed to inhibit Aβ aggregation, including small molecules – , peptides – , macrocycles , and nanoparticles , . Due to their excellent druggability, small-molecule inhibitors are particularly promising for the prevention and treatment of AD. However, despite the development of many small-molecule inhibitors, no successful clinical trials have been reported, indicating the need for novel and effective inhibitory molecules.
To enhance the efficacy and efficiency of discovering aggregation inhibitors for Aβ, a rational screening method rather than a trial-and-error approach is essential. Virtual screening has emerged as an effective tool for drug discovery , once detailed information about drug targets and binding sites (pharmacophores) is obtained. It is important to note that amyloid fibrils, not monomers or oligomers, generally possess stable and well-defined structures. Therefore, virtual screening can only be performed on amyloid fibrils. Nonetheless, this approach has already proven successful in developing aggregation inhibitors for tau and α-synuclein. Seidler et al. obtained the cryoEM structure of a tau fibril complexed with EGCG and identified the EGCG binding sites . These binding sites were treated as pharmacophores for virtual screening of a library containing 61700 compounds. Through subsequent experimental validation and structural derivatization, aggregation inhibitors for tau and α-synuclein were discovered.
However, the development of inhibitors for Aβ aggregation is more complicated due to the limited knowledge about the optimal binding sites on Aβ fibrils for aggregation inhibitors. Jiang et al. considered the KLVFFA motif on Aβ fibrils as potential binding sites . They performed virtual screening on 18000 compounds and ultimately identified three compounds that could reduce Aβ cytotoxicity but were unable to inhibit fibril formation.
Knowledge about the microscopic processes of Aβ aggregation provides valuable insights for the rational design of aggregation inhibitors. Generally, the aggregation of amyloid proteins involves several key microscopic steps: primary nucleation, secondary nucleation, and elongation. Through chemical kinetics analysis, the rates of these steps can be determined by globally fitting the kinetic data from aggregation experiments , . The obtained rate constants and rate laws can then describe the detailed aggregation processes and mechanisms of the amyloid protein. With this knowledge in hand, the microscopic inhibitory mechanisms of various inhibitors can be elucidated through chemical kinetics analysis of the experimental kinetic data in the presence of inhibitors , – . For instance, it has been reported that the molecular chaperone proSP-C Brichos can bind to the surface of Aβ fibrils and inhibit the surface-catalyzed secondary nucleation process, while the molecular chaperone Hsp70 can bind to the fibril ends of Ure2p fibrils and suppress the elongation process .
Given the effectiveness of chemical kinetics analysis, it is rational to consider the fibril surface or fibril ends of Aβ fibrils as potential “binding sites” for virtual screening of aggregation inhibitors, aiming to suppress either the secondary nucleation or the elongation process, respectively. Chia et al. identified a potential binding pocket on the surface of α-synuclein fibrils . The authors considered this pocket as a potential binding site for aggregation inhibitors, which may suppress the surface-catalyzed secondary nucleation step of α-synuclein aggregation. The authors performed virtual screening on approximately 2 million compounds from the ZINC library and eventually tested 67 compounds based on the docking results. Five compounds were ultimately demonstrated to inhibit the aggregation of α-synuclein, likely through the inhibition of surface-catalyzed secondary nucleation.
Besides the fibril surface, fibril ends have also proven to be promising targets for developing aggregation inhibitors. Shvadchak et al. designed a small protein, Inh-β, with the aim of blocking the fibril ends of α-synuclein . Due to the relatively low concentrations of fibril ends compared to other intermediate species, inhibitors targeting fibril ends are expected to function at sub-stoichiometric concentrations . Shvadchak et al. found that 2 μM Inh-β could effectively suppress the aggregation of 50 μM α-synuclein in the presence of 0.5 μM seeds. Murray et al. designed a library of miniproteins to cap the fibril ends of various amyloid proteins . The authors performed virtual screening on thousands of miniproteins and experimentally tested 46 miniproteins, leading to the discovery of three effective inhibitors. These screened inhibitors could bind to the fibril ends of amyloid proteins and inhibit seeded aggregation. In our previous work, we discovered several Aβ aggregation inhibitors. Molecular docking and chemical kinetics analysis suggested that these inhibitors may exert their effects by binding to the fibril ends of Aβ – .
Based on these results, we propose the hypothesis that the fibril ends of Aβ can be treated as binding sites for virtual screening of small-molecule aggregation inhibitors. The screened inhibitors are expected to suppress the elongation process and may even depolymerize pre-formed Aβ fibrils. Considering that 30.6% of the FDA-approved small-molecule drugs (from 1981 to 2019) are natural products or natural product derivatives , we first tested our hypothesis with a library of traditional Chinese medicine, aiming to identify novel and effective natural small-molecule Aβ aggregation inhibitors with therapeutic potential.
The exact binding site on the Aβ fibril ends remains to be determined since no typical pocket exists in the structure . In this study, we first employed PharmacoNet to identify several hot spot regions at the 2LMN Aβ fibril ends (Fig.  ). The hot spots were then defined as the binding sites and virtual screening of a library containing 1561 natural product compounds was conducted. Nine molecules were selected for further experimental validation. Forsythoside A (For A) was identified as the most effective inhibitor of Aβ aggregation. Results from Thioflavin T (ThT) kinetics, dynamic light scattering (DLS), circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy, atomic force microscopy (AFM), dot blot, and cell experiments suggested that For A could not only inhibit the aggregation and cytotoxicity of Aβ but also depolymerize pre-formed Aβ40 fibrils. Additionally, the binding between For A and Aβ40 fibrils was confirmed by surface plasmon resonance (SPR) analysis. The fibril-depolymerizing mechanism was quantitatively assessed using chemical kinetics analysis. Optimal reaction rate constants for fibril depolymerization were determined separately for Aβ40 and Aβ42, under both non-seeded and seeded conditions, based on normalized ThT intensity. Finally, experiments on AD transgenic Caenorhabditis elegans CL4176 indicated that 10 μM For A was sufficient to extend their lifespan. We have also performed virtual screening with a CNS-penetrant compound library, and 6 out of the 13 tested molecules exhibited strong inhibitory effects on Aβ aggregation. These results cast new light on the rational screening of Aβ aggregation inhibitors, suggesting that more inhibitors can be found by exploring different molecular libraries for virtual screening.
 

## Results and discussion

## Virtual screening
We initiated our study by retrieving the two-fold symmetric Aβ40 fibril structure 2LMN from the PDB database. The 2LMN structure was originally derived from in vitro incubation of Aβ40 under buffered conditions, followed by multiple rounds of seeded aggregation induction. Given its formation process, we consider this structure to closely resemble fibrils observed in other in vitro experiments, thereby ensuring consistency between our experimental findings and virtual screening predictions. In our previous research, we performed autodocking of the 2LMN Aβ40 fibril with Trp-glucose conjugates and berbamine . We found that the Trp-glucose conjugates and berbamine tended to bind to the groove structure at the fibril ends of the 2LMN Aβ40 fibril. Despite these observations, the precise binding sites at the fibril termini remain unclear. To address this gap, we employed PharmacoNet, a state-of-the-art deep-learning framework specifically developed for protein-based pharmacophore modeling . PharmacoNet not only identifies critical residues but also visualizes them in a pharmacological context, streamlining the identification of promising small-molecule drug candidates. This tool pinpointed two primary hotspot regions (Fig.  ): one at the turn of the Aβ fibril (Region A) and another at the folded end (Region B).
Region B was defined by four key pharmacophore features: two hydrophobic groups, a hydrogen bond donor, and a hydrogen bond acceptor. However, structural surface mapping showed that Region B is buried within the protein and features a narrow opening, presenting significant challenges for effective small molecule binding (Supplementary Fig.  ). Conversely, Region A displayed a more diverse array of pharmacophore features, including ten hydrophobic groups, a π-π interaction site, two hydrogen bond acceptors, and a potential halogen interaction feature. This region also offers a more accessible and spacious binding pocket, making it a more suitable target for small-molecule interactions. Therefore, we prioritized Region A for subsequent molecular docking studies.
The molecular structures of the 1561 natural molecules were retrieved from Selleck . Molecular docking was then performed using Gold Suite 5.3, and we found that the binding pose with the lowest binding energy for most of the top-scoring molecules was located around the identified hot spot (Supplementary Fig. 1 ), consistent with our previous work . We excluded molecules with long alkane chains due to their high hydrophobicity, which may complicate experimental validation. The remaining molecules were then ranked by their docking scores, with the top 30 molecules scoring over 80 (Supplementary Table  ). Upon reviewing the literature, we found that some of these 30 molecules, such as tannic acid , and salvianolic acid B , had already been demonstrated to inhibit Aβ aggregation (Supplementary Tables  and ). We excluded these known compounds and selected nine previously unreported molecules for further experimental screening.

## Experimental screening based on end-point ThT assay
We purchased the nine selected molecules from local suppliers and prepared stock solutions by dissolving each in dimethyl sulfoxide at a concentration of 10 mM to ensure consistency. ThT kinetics experiments were subsequently carried out in phosphate-buffered saline buffer (PBS) buffer system containing 5 μM Aβ40, 20 μM ThT, and 50 μM of each molecule. The end-point ThT fluorescence signals, presented in Fig.  , indicated that three molecules showed potential inhibitory effects on Aβ40 aggregation. Further scrutiny revealed that the molecule Itra exhibited poor water solubility (Supplementary Fig.  ), which resulted in inconsistent ThT assay outcomes . Therefore, no further experiments were conducted on Itra. To address potential fluorescence quenching by For A and For B at the plateau phase, we tested their direct effects on end-point ThT fluorescence. Aβ40 solutions were incubated with ThT for 24 h to reach the plateau, after which PBS, For A, or For B were added separately. Immediate measurements of ThT fluorescence revealed that neither For A nor For B quenched ThT signals (Supplementary Fig.  ), suggesting that these compounds likely inhibit Aβ40 aggregation rather than interfering with ThT fluorescence. As shown in Fig.  , For A exhibited a stronger inhibitory effect compared to For B , prompting us to focus on For A for further investigation into its inhibitory mechanisms.

## For A inhibits Aβ aggregation and depolymerizes pre-formed fibrils
The chemical structure of For A is shown in Fig.  . Its polyhydroxy framework provides excellent aqueous solubility, enabling the preparation of a 10 mM stock solution in PBS. However, For A gradually degrades or undergoes oxidation over long-term storage (Supplementary Fig.  ). Therefore, it should be aliquoted and stored at −40 °C to maintain its stability. We conducted ThT kinetics assays using 5 μM Aβ40, 20 μM ThT, and varying concentrations of For A. As illustrated in Fig.  , For A demonstrated a concentration-dependent inhibition of Aβ40 aggregation. Specifically, For A reduced the plateau ThT signal by approximately 45%, 76%, and 83% at concentrations of 10 μM, 25 μM, and 50 μM, respectively. The half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) of For A was determined to be 11 μM (Fig.  ). Interestingly, even at 2.5 μM, For A appeared to depolymerize Aβ40 fibrils, as evidenced by a significant decline in ThT fluorescence signals over time. To confirm this depolymerization effect, we introduced various concentrations of For A to pre-formed Aβ40 fibrils and continuously monitored ThT fluorescence (Fig.  ). The results confirmed that For A effectively depolymerized Aβ40 fibrils, resulting in a marked decrease in ThT fluorescence.
 Following this, we evaluated the impact of For A on seeded aggregation of Aβ40, depicted in Fig.  . Introducing 5% pre-formed Aβ40 fibrils as seeds notably reduced the lag phase for Aβ40 aggregation. However, For A still exhibited a strong inhibitory effect on aggregation, even in the presence of these seeds, highlighting its potency in preventing fibril formation.
Encouraged by these promising results, we extended our study to Aβ42, a more aggregation-prone and toxic variant of Aβ. As shown in Supplementary Fig.  , For A effectively inhibited the aggregation of Aβ42 and depolymerized pre-formed Aβ42 fibrils, displaying a concentration-dependent inhibitory effect. However, the inhibitory effect of ForA on Aβ42 aggregation was weaker than that observed for Aβ40. It is worth noting that initial ThT fluorescence signals for Aβ42 alone (t = 0 min) were over 40%, significantly higher than those of ThT alone. This phenomenon may be attributed to incomplete monomerization or the inherently high aggregation propensity of Aβ42.

## DLS and CD results confirmed the inhibitory effects of For A on Aβ aggregation
To validate the inhibitory effects of For A, DLS experiments were conducted to measure the size of Aβ40 aggregates in the presence and absence of For A. When 5 μM Aβ40 was incubated alone for two days at 37 °C, 1100 rpm, aggregates approximately 1000 nm in size were observed (Fig.  ). However, when 5 μM Aβ40 was incubated with 10 μM For A, particles larger than 100 nm were rarely detected, demonstrating that For A efficiently inhibited the formation of larger Aβ40 aggregates. Additionally, to examine the depolymerization effects of For A, pre-formed Aβ40 fibrils were incubated with 50 μM For A under the same conditions for two days, leading to a significant reduction in aggregate size to around 100 nm (Fig.  ).
CD spectroscopy was used to assess the secondary structure of Aβ40 aggregates with and without For A. A robust CD signal was obtained using 10 μM Aβ40 in a 5 mm quartz cuvette. As shown in Fig.  , freshly dissolved Aβ40 exhibited a negative peak around 198 nm, while Aβ40 fibrils displayed characteristic β-sheet structures with a negative peak at approximately 218 nm. Incubating Aβ40 with 10 μM or 50 μM For A for 2 days decreased the intensity of the 218 nm negative peak, indicating a reduction in β-sheet structures. Analysis using the K2D3 and Contin methods further quantified this change, with K2D3 showing a reduction in β-sheet content from 25.09% to 17.53%, comparable to freshly dissolved Aβ40 (Fig.  ). Results from the Contin method also showed similar trends, with a decrease in β-sheet percentage and an increase in unordered structure content (Fig.  ). Furthermore, CD spectroscopy of pre-formed 10 μM Aβ40 fibrils incubated with 10 μM or 50 μM For A for 2 days also revealed a decrease in β-sheet content, suggesting that For A can depolymerize pre-formed Aβ40 fibrils. We also performed similar CD experiments on Aβ42, and the results, shown in Supplementary Fig.  , indicate that the inhibitory effect of ForA on Aβ42 is weaker than that on Aβ40.

## AFM and dot blot results confirmed the decrease of Aβ fibrils
To investigate the morphology and quantity of fibrils in various Aβ40 samples, AFM experiments were performed. Aβ40 (5 μM) was incubated with or without For A (25 μM) for 2 days at 37 °C, 1100 rpm. Additionally, pre-formed Aβ40 fibrils (5 μM) were incubated with For A (25 μM) for 2 days. The samples were deposited onto individual mica plates and subsequently observed using AFM. Aβ40 alone formed long, mature fibrils after 2 days (Fig.  ). In contrast, incubation with For A at a 1:5 ratio resulted in fewer fibrils (Fig.  ). Moreover, incubation of pre-formed Aβ40 fibrils with For A at the same ratio reduced fibril abundance significantly (Fig.  ). These findings indicate that For A effectively suppresses Aβ fibril formation and depolymerizes pre-formed fibrils.
 To validate these inhibitory effects, dot blot analysis was used to evaluate fibril content. The PA5 antibody (Thermo Fisher, Catalog # PA5-77843), which selectively binds Aβ fibrils, was employed alongside the 4G8 antibody, which recognizes the Aβ mid-region as a control for Aβ presence. Aβ40 samples, incubated with or without For A for varying durations, were spotted onto nitrocellulose membranes and stained with PA5 and 4G8. Aβ alone rapidly formed fibrils, as detected by PA5, while For A suppressed fibril formation (Fig.  , PA5, upper lane, uncropped image in Supplementary Fig.  ). After 36 h of incubation with For A, no PA5-responsive fibrils were detectable by dot blot (see Supplementary Fig.  for quantification). The results confirm that For A effectively inhibits the formation of Aβ40 fibrils. Pre-formed Aβ40 fibrils were also incubated with For A for different durations, and fibril content was monitored by dot blot (Fig.  , uncropped image in Supplementary Fig.  ). A time-dependent reduction in PA5-responsive fibrils was observed (see Supplementary Fig.  for quantification), demonstrating that For A effectively depolymerizes pre-formed Aβ40 fibrils. Dot blot experiments were also performed on Aβ42 (Supplementary Fig.  , ), and the results indicate that ForA can inhibit the formation and promote the depolymerization of PA5-responsive Aβ42 fibrils.

## Exploring the binding between Aβ and For A
To elucidate the potential inhibitory mechanism of For A on Aβ aggregation, we first examined its binding affinities with Aβ monomers and fibrils using SPR (raw data in Supplementary Fig.  , ). Due to the transient and unstable nature of Aβ oligomers, binding assessments between For A and Aβ oligomers were not performed. Freshly dissolved Aβ monomers and pre-formed Aβ fibrils were immobilized on separate CM5 chips, and various concentrations of For A were then flowed through the chips to obtain binding-dissociation curves. As depicted in Fig.  , the binding curve of For A with Aβ monomers did not fit well using kinetic models. We instead analyzed the data using a steady-state model (Supplementary Fig.  ), yielding a dissociation constant (KD) of 6.1 μM for the binding affinity between For A and Aβ monomers. In contrast, the binding curve for For A interacting with Aβ fibrils (Fig.  ) followed a more conventional kinetic model, resulting in a KD value of 0.198 μM. These findings suggest that For A exhibits a stronger binding affinity for Aβ fibrils compared to monomers.
To gain deeper insights into the specific binding interactions of For A with Aβ fibrils, we revisited our molecular docking results using the 2LMN Aβ fibril structure. Ten possible binding poses were generated, with the highest-scoring pose shown in Fig.  and additional poses scoring above 75 presented in Supplementary Fig.  . Consistent with earlier observations, For A showed a strong preference for binding near the identified hotspot regions at the fibril ends (Fig.  ). The hydroxyl groups of For A appeared to form multiple hydrogen bonds with Aβ residues, while its aromatic rings engaged in pi-alkyl interactions, enhancing the stability of the complex (Fig.  ). These interactions, combined with van der Waals forces, contribute to the robust binding affinity observed between For A and Aβ fibrils (Fig.  ).
To further investigate the stability of the docking results and the binding interaction between For A and Aβ40 fibril, we performed 100 ns molecular dynamics (MD) simulations using the optimized docking conformations as initial structures. The MD trajectories demonstrated that For A maintained stable binding to Aβ40 fibril throughout the simulation period, with no evidence of dissociation (Supplementary Fig.  ). Subsequent MMGBSA calculations provided detailed insights into the binding interface (Supplementary Fig.  ). The results revealed that For A establishes strong and specific interactions with key residues in the peptide, particularly Phe19 and Ile32 on the E and F chain, as well as Leu17 on the F chain. The average Gibbs free energy of binding was calculated to be −24.56 kcal/mol, reflecting a thermodynamically favorable and stable interaction.

## For A reduces Aβ-induced cytotoxicity
Building on our aforementioned findings, we assessed whether For A could mitigate the cytotoxic effects of Aβ and whether the depolymerized Aβ species were harmful to cells. We conducted cytotoxicity assays using N2a cells exposed to Aβ. Specifically, 5 μM Aβ40 was incubated with or without various concentrations of For A for two days. The samples were then diluted 10-fold in serum-free medium and applied to N2a cells for 24 h. The cell viabilities of different groups were determined using the CCK-8 assay (Cell Counting Kit-8). As depicted in Fig.  , For A effectively reduced Aβ-induced cytotoxicity. Cell viability was approximately 56% when incubated with Aβ40 alone, whereas it increased to about 96% with 1:5 For A treatment.
 We also tested whether For A could reduce the cytotoxicity of pre-formed Aβ40 fibrils. Pre-formed Aβ40 fibrils were treated with different concentrations of For A for two days, diluted, and incubated with N2a cells. The CCK-8 assay confirmed that For A efficiently reduced the cytotoxicity of pre-formed Aβ40 fibrils (Fig.  ), suggesting that the depolymerized Aβ species were not harmful. Additionally, For A alone exhibited no cytotoxic effects on N2a cells at any tested concentration (Fig.  ).

## For A extends the lifespan of AD model transgenic Caenorhabditis elegans
To explore the in vivo efficacy of For A, we utilized the transgenic C. elegans strain CL4176, which expresses human Aβ1-42 and is a well-established model for Alzheimer’s disease research – . The progressive expression and aggregation of neurotoxic Aβ in these worms result in phenotypes such as paralysis and reduced lifespan. We treated three groups of CL4176 worms with 0, 10, and 50 μM For A, respectively, and recorded their survival rates every two days (Fig.  ). The untreated worms had a median lifespan of 18 days, with a maximum of 24 days. Treatment with 10 μM For A extended the median lifespan to 24 days and the maximum to 36 days. A higher dose of 50 μM For A further increased the median lifespan to 32 days, with the longest lifespan reaching 38 days. These results indicate that For A significantly prolongs the lifespan of AD model worms. We also tested the effect of For A on the lifespan of wild-type N2 worms and found no significant impact, suggesting that For A’s effects are specific to Aβ-related toxicity (Supplementary Fig.  ).
To further examine the effects of For A on Aβ plaque deposition in CL2006 worms, we conducted ThT fluorescence staining. As illustrated in Fig.  , CL2006 worms exhibited substantial Aβ plaque accumulation, whereas wild-type N2 worms showed no detectable plaques. However, after 48-h co-incubation with 50 μM For A, plaque formation in CL2006 worms was significantly reduced (Fig.  ). These results indicate that For A not only alleviates Aβ-induced toxicity but also effectively reduces plaque deposition in transgenic AD model worms.

## Preliminary results with a CNS-penetrant compound library
To extend our findings, we performed virtual screening of a CNS-penetrant compound library containing 733 molecules . Using the same criteria and scoring system, we identified 23 top-scoring compounds and selected 13 (T1 to T13) for experimental testing (details about the molecules are summarized in Supplementary Table  ). The ThT assays revealed that, at a 1:5 concentration ratio, six of these compounds effectively inhibited Aβ40 aggregation, three showed moderate inhibition, and four had no significant effect (Fig.  ). We further investigated one of the compounds, Azeliragon (T5), which was originally developed as an antagonist of the receptor for advanced glycation end products for AD therapy . The molecular structure and docking results of Azeliragon are shown in Supplementary Fig.  . The ThT and CCK-8 results demonstrate that Azeliragon can effectively inhibit the aggregation (Fig.  ) and cytotoxicity (Fig.  ) of Aβ40 in a concentration-dependent manner. While further studies are ongoing, these initial results suggest that our virtual screening strategy, focused on targeting Aβ fibril ends, could be a promising approach for identifying new Aβ aggregation inhibitors.
 

## Conclusions
The recent FDA approvals of aducanumab, lecanemab, and donanemab have reinvigorated efforts to develop Aβ-targeted therapies for AD. However, despite the promise of these antibody treatments, their high costs and potential side effects pose significant challenges . Consequently, small-molecule Aβ aggregation inhibitors emerge as promising alternatives. Nevertheless, the rational design and efficient screening of these inhibitors remain underdeveloped. Based on our understanding of the microscopic processes underlying amyloid aggregation , , Aβ fibril ends have been identified as ideal targets for inhibitor design , . Leveraging structure-based virtual screening methods , , , , , we propose that Aβ fibril ends can be effectively used as “binding sites” to identify Aβ aggregation inhibitors.
The power of this method lies in its ability to accelerate and refine the drug discovery process. In this study, we successfully applied the above hypothesis by discovering a new Aβ aggregation inhibitor from a traditional Chinese medicine library. Notably, many of the top 30 scoring compounds in our virtual screen have been previously reported to exhibit anti-AD effects, reflecting the extensive research focus on natural remedies for AD (Supplementary Tables  and   ). This convergence suggests that these compounds may exert their effects through interactions with Aβ fibril ends. For instance, verbascoside, a compound structurally similar to For A and ranked 24th with a score of 81.57, has been reported to inhibit Aβ aggregation and bind to Aβ fibrils , . Previous tests in our lab confirmed that verbascoside could also depolymerize Aβ fibrils (Supplementary Fig.  ). Furthermore, previously known inhibitors like tannic acid , , and salvianolic acid B have demonstrated similar depolymerization effects. Therefore, the positive rate for virtual screening focused on fibril ends could potentially exceed 2 out of 9 (For A and For B among the test compounds).
The most potent Aβ aggregation inhibitor identified in this study is For A, a compound previously shown to reduce cytotoxicity , of Aβ25-35 and ameliorate AD-like pathology in APP/PS1 mice . We demonstrated that For A effectively inhibits both Aβ40 and Aβ42 aggregation and is capable of depolymerizing mature Aβ fibrils. Surface plasmon resonance analysis revealed that For A has a higher binding affinity for Aβ fibrils compared to monomers. Our chemical kinetics analysis suggested that For A’s mechanism of action involves binding to Aβ fibrils and subsequently promoting their depolymerization. Additionally, For A was shown to mitigate Aβ-induced cytotoxicity in N2a cells and extend the lifespan of AD model C. elegans, highlighting its therapeutic potential. Despite its potency as an inhibitor of Aβ aggregation and neurotoxicity, we acknowledge that its instability remains a major challenge for drug development. However, as demonstrated by David S. Eisenberg et al., our results suggest that For A can serve as a promising starting point for further structural optimization, similar to EGCG .
It is important to note that Aβ fibrils exhibit polymorphism, with structural variations influenced by their preparation methods and sources . We acknowledge that Aβ structures derived from patient samples, such as Aβ40 (6SHS) and Aβ42 (7Q4B) , have greater pathological relevance. Consequently, virtual screening based on these structures may identify molecules with enhanced in vivo applicability. As an initial step, we conducted a preliminary virtual screening using 6SHS and 7Q4B, docking our traditional Chinese medicine library and CNS-penetrant compound library against these fibril structures. The results of this screening are provided in the Supplementary Information (Supplementary Tables  and ). Given the substantial structural differences among the three fibril models, the docking rankings varied accordingly. In future studies, we aim to further analyze and experimentally validate the virtual screening results obtained from these structures.
In conclusion, our study showcases a successful application of virtual screening to identify a potent Aβ aggregation inhibitor by targeting fibril ends. This method represents a significant advancement in the rational development of small-molecule inhibitors for AD. With continued exploration of diverse molecular libraries, we anticipate that more effective Aβ aggregation inhibitors can be discovered and potentially translated into viable therapies for Alzheimer’s disease.

## Methods

## Materials
Aβ40 and Aβ42 were purchased from ChinaPeptides CO., Ltd. (QYAOBIO, Shanghai) with a purity of >95%. The antibody PA5 (Catalog # PA5-77843) was purchased from Thermo Fisher. The antibody 4G8 was purchased from BioLegend (Beijing). Secondary antibodies, goat anti-mouse IgG and goat anti-rabbit IgG, were purchased from Jackson ImmunoResearch. Nitrocellulose membranes (P/N 66485) were purchased from Pall Corporation. Fetal bovine serum (FBS), PBS, Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM), and minimal Eagle’s medium α (MEMα) were purchased from HyClone. N2a cells were purchased from the Cell Bank of the Chinese Academy of Sciences. N2 worms, OP50, and nematode growth media (NGM) were obtained from SunyBiotech (Fujian). CL4176 and CL2006 strains were provided by the CGC, which is funded by the NIH Office of Research Infrastructure Programs (P40 OD010440). The naturally occurring small molecules and other chemical reagents were obtained from Aladdin (Shanghai).

## Hot spots identification
To identify key hot spots in the aggregation process of Aβ—residues that are crucial for aggregation and capable of interacting with small molecules, thereby providing a foundation for structure-based virtual screening—we utilized the recently developed tool, PharmacoNet . In this study, we used the Aβ fibril structure with two-fold symmetry (PDB code: 2LMN) as the receptor. Due to the limited size of the recognition areas within PharmacoNet, we implemented multiple distinct recognition zones to comprehensively cover the protein’s surface. A score threshold of 0.75 was set to ensure that the identified hot spots had strong potential for interaction with small molecules.

## Docking
Prior to conducting molecular docking, all compounds from the traditional Chinese medicine library or CNS-penetrant compound library underwent pre-processing using RDKit, which included hydrogenation, 3D structure generation, and energy minimization. The Aβ fibril structure 2LMN served as the receptor structure. The definition of the binding site was based on the hot spots identified by PharmacoNet. Molecular docking was performed using Gold Suite 5.3 and the ChemPLP scoring function. The generic algorithm settings were set to “automatic”, allowing retention of up to 10 poses per molecule, with other parameters kept at default values. Subsequently, results were sorted based on ChemPLP scores and visually inspected to identify compounds demonstrating enhanced binding affinity with the Aβ fibril.
A similar workflow was applied to other Aβ fibril structures, including Aβ40 (PDB ID: 6SHS) and Aβ42 (PDB ID: 7Q4B). First, hot spot identification was used to determine the binding sites for each structure. Virtual screening was then performed using the same methodology described above, enabling a comparative analysis of compound interactions across different Aβ fibril polymorphisms.

## ThT kinetics
Aβ40 and Aβ42 were monomerized with 1% ammonium hydroxide as previously described , . Prior to the ThT kinetics, 6 mM NaOH was used to solubilize the monomerized Aβ to 100 μM. The 100 μM Aβ was then diluted with PBS and incubated with 20 μM ThT and varying concentrations of small molecules. The samples were added into a black 96-well plate (Greiner 655096) with a transparent bottom and incubated in a Tecan Infinite M200 PRO microplate reader. Continuous shaking at 37 °C, 886.9 rpm was applied. The fluorescence intensity of the samples was recorded every 6 min with λex = 440 nm and λem = 480 nm. For the end-point ThT kinetics, each group was performed in duplicate or triplicate. For other ThT kinetics experiments, each group was performed in quadruplicate. For the depolymerization experiments, 5 μM Aβ was first incubated with 20 μM ThT in the microplate reader using the same conditions as above for 24 h. Note that the ThT fluorescence reached a plateau at around 3 h. Afterwards, varying concentrations of small molecules were added to the Aβ samples and the fluorescence intensity was recorded immediately. The samples of 5 μM Aβ and 20 μM ThT incubated for 24 h were also used as seeds for the seeding experiments.

## DLS
The 100 μM Aβ40 in 6 mM NaOH was diluted to 5 μM with PBS and then incubated with varying concentrations of For A for 2 days at 37 °C, 1100 rpm. For depolymerization experiments, 5 μM Aβ40 was first incubated alone for 2 days under the same conditions, followed by the addition of 50 μM For A and further incubation for another 2 days at 37 °C, 1100 rpm. The sizes of the samples were measured using a Zetasizer Nano ZEN3700.

## CD
To minimize absorbance below 200 nm, a 10 mM sodium phosphate buffer solution with 140 mM NaF instead of NaCl was used for CD experiments , , . A Chirascan circular dichroism spectrometer was used. The 100 μM Aβ40 in 6 mM NaOH was diluted 10-times with the NaF buffer and incubated with different concentrations of For A at 37 °C, 1100 rpm for 2 days. The samples were added to a 5 mm quartz cuvette, and their CD spectra were recorded. For the depolymerization experiments, 10 μM Aβ40 was incubated in the NaF buffer for 2 days, after which different concentrations of For A were added and incubated for another 2 days. The samples were then used for CD experiments. Secondary structure calculations using K2D3 and Contin methods were performed as instructed on their respective webpages .

## AFM
40 μL of the samples were deposited on a 10-mm mica plate and incubated for 30 min. Then the mica plate was washed twice with water and dried at 37 °C. A NanoWizard 4XP Bio-AFM (Bruker) was used to record the AFM images. The resolution of the images was set to 512 lines.

## Dot blot
To ensure consistency, freshly dissolved 100 μM Aβ40 in 6 mM NaOH was aliquoted, snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at a −80 °C refrigerator. Prior to use, the stored Aβ40 was thawed on ice and immediately diluted with PBS, followed by incubation with or without For A at 37 °C and 1100 rpm for varying periods. Subsequently, 3 μL of each sample was applied to two nitrocellulose (NC) membranes. The NC membranes were blocked with 5% non-fat milk in TBS-T for 1 h at room temperature. After washing with TBS-T three times, the NC membranes were incubated overnight at 4 °C with 1:2000 diluted primary antibodies (4G8 or PA5). After washing with TBS-T three times, the NC membranes were incubated with 1:2000 diluted secondary antibodies (goat anti-mouse for 4G8, goat anti-rabbit for PA5) for 1 h at room temperature. Finally, the NC membranes were treated with an enhanced chemiluminescent substrate detection kit and imaged with a ChemiDocTM MP Imaging system (Bio-Rad).

## SPR
SPR analysis was conducted using a GE Biacore T200 system. Aβ40 powder was dissolved in 10 mM sodium acetate buffer (pH 4.5) to 50 μg/mL and immobilized onto a CM5 chip via standard EDC/NHS coupling chemistry. Separately, preformed Aβ40 fibrils (10 μM) were diluted to 5 μM with sodium acetate buffer and similarly immobilized on another CM5 chip. Various concentrations of For A were then flowed through the CM5 chips, and the binding response was recorded. The final curves were obtained by subtracting the corresponding blank responses.

## Molecular dynamics simulations
To investigate the binding stability of For A with 2LMN and elucidate its interaction mechanism, molecular dynamics simulations were conducted using the optimal docking conformations as initial structures. The MD protocol was implemented in GROMACS 2023 with the Charmm36-jul2022 force field. The system was solvated in a cubic box utilizing TIP3P water and 0.15 M NaCl, ensuring margins exceeding 20 Å on all sides. Non-bonded interactions were truncated at 12 Å, employing a switch function between 10–12 Å. The simulation workflow consisted of an energy minimization step followed by dual-stage equilibration: NVT (100 ps) and NPT (100 ps) at 310 K. A subsequent production run of 100 ns was performed under constant pressure (1 atm) and temperature (310 K), utilizing the Berendsen barostat and V-Rescale thermostat for accurate pressure and temperature coupling. For the binding affinity analysis, a trajectory sampling approach was employed. Specifically, the final 20 ns of the For A-2LMN complex’s trajectories were extracted and analyzed using gmxMMPBSA software with the GBSA (Generalized Born/Surface Area) algorithm to compute the binding free energy.

## Cell experiments
N2a cells were cultured in a 5% CO2 incubator with a medium of 1:1 DMEM:MEMα, including 10% FBS and 1% penicillin-streptomycin. The cells were seeded into 96-well plates (Corning 3599) with a density of 6000 cells per well. After incubating for 24 h, the medium was discarded, and different samples were added to incubate with the cells for another 24 h. CCK-8 was then added to the cells, and the absorbance at 450 nm (OD450) was measured after 4 h. The background OD450 of CCK-8 itself was subtracted, and the cell viabilities were calculated.
To prepare the Aβ samples for cell experiments, monomerized Aβ was first exposed to UV light for 10 min to avoid bacterial contamination. 6 mM NaOH was then used to solubilize the Aβ. Afterwards, Aβ was diluted with PBS to 5 μM and incubated without or with different concentrations of For A for 2 days at 37 °C, 1100 rpm. The as-obtained Aβ samples were diluted 10 times with serum-free medium (1:1 DMEM:MEMα and 1% penicillin-streptomycin) and incubated with the N2a cells. For the depolymerization experiments, 5 μM Aβ was first incubated alone for 2 days and then incubated without or with different concentrations of For A for another 2 days. The samples were then diluted 10-times with serum-free medium and incubated with N2a cells. To test the cytotoxicity of For A itself, 5, 10, 25, 50, and 100 μM For A in PBS was diluted 10-times with serum-free medium and incubated with N2a cells.

## C. elegans experiments
A total of 22.6 g of NGM powder was dissolved in 1000 mL of water and autoclaved. 5 mL of the NGM solution was poured into a 35 mm petri dish and left to solidify overnight. Meanwhile, E. coli strain OP50 was cultured in LB medium at 37 °C overnight. CL4176, CL2006 and N2 worms were cultured on the NGM plates and fed with E. coli OP50. The temperature was maintained at 16 °C for CL4176 and 20 °C for CL2006 to avoid the violent expression of Aβ. Approximately 25 CL4176 worms at the L4 stage were transferred to NGM plates supplemented with different concentrations of For A. Each plate also contained 40 μM 5-fluoro-2’-deoxyuridine to inhibit the progeny of the worms . The number of surviving worms was recorded every two days until all the worms had died. In certain cases, such as when bacterial or fungal contamination was observed, the worms were transferred to fresh plates .
Synchronized L4-stage CL2006 worms were cultured on NGM plates with or without 50 μM For A for 48 h. After incubation, the worms were washed three times with M9 buffer, transferred to 1.5 mL centrifuge tubes, and fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde at 4 °C for 24 h. The samples were then centrifuged at 1500 rpm for 1 min, the supernatant was removed, and the worms were treated with a 125 mM Tris buffer (pH 7.4) containing 5% β-mercaptoethanol and 1% Triton X-100 at 37 °C for 24 h with continuous shaking. Subsequently, the worms were stained with 0.125% thioflavin T for 2 h, washed with 50% ethanol, and mounted on glass slides for imaging using a Leica STELLARIS 5 confocal microscope.

## Chemical kinetics analysis on various possible inhibitory mechanisms of For A
In the main text, we argued that For A acts its inhibitory mechanism mainly through depolymerizing mature fibrils. To make a further validation, here we examined the possibilities of For A adsorbing Aβ monomers, fibril ends or fibril surface in detail.
Kinetic model for For A adsorbing monomers and suppressing their aggregation
Suppose For A has a strong adsorption ability on Aβ monomers, which consequently could reduce both nucleation and elongation processes to a great extent. Kinetic models for For A adsorbing fibril ends and suppressing fibril elongation
If For A could bind with mature fibril ends, the effective growth sites for fibril elongation would be greatly reduced. In this way, For A exhibits a strong inhibitory effect on Aβ aggregation. The corresponding kinetic model reads,dPfdt=knmnc+ksmn2M−kbPf(btot−Pb/Ra),dMdt=kemPf,dPbdt=kbPf(btot−Pb/Ra),where and are the number concentration of free fibrils and fibrils adsorbed by For A separately.
Kinetic models for For A adsorbing fibril surface and suppressing secondary nucleation
Alternatively, if For A could bind with the mature fibril surface, the secondary nucleation would be suppressed. The kinetic model for fibril-surface adsorption reads,
dPdt=knmnc+k2mn2Mb,dMdt=kemP,dMbdt=kb(M−Mb)(btot−Mb/Ra),where is the mass concentration of adsorbed fibrils by For A.
