HuSuwan ].The challenge in early diagnosis, combined with its heterogeneous nature, contributes to poor prognosis. Even within epithelial ovarian cancer, the most prevalent subtype, there are five major histotypes that exhibit distinct clinical and genetic characteristics [ ]. This heterogeneity extends beyond tumor cells to the complex tumor microenvironment, which plays a crucial role in disease progression and treatment response.
Understanding this heterogeneity at the molecular level requires advanced analytical approaches such as single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq). This technology enables the detailed examination of individual cells within the tumor microenvironment, revealing cell-specific gene expression patterns and regulatory mechanisms that may be masked in bulk tissue analyses [ , ]. Through scRNA-seq, researchers can identify distinct cell populations and their unique molecular signatures, providing insights into the complex cellular interactions within the tumor ecosystem.
Protein post-translational modifications, particularly ubiquitination and deubiquitination, are emerging as critical regulators of cellular processes in cancer. Ubiquitination, a common post-translational modification, plays an essential role in protein degradation and cellular metabolism [ , ]. It regulates key biological processes such as cell cycle progression, DNA repair, and cell signaling pathways. Conversely, deubiquitination, the process of removing ubiquitin chains from proteins, is equally vital for maintaining normal cellular function and homeostasis [ ]. The dynamic balance between ubiquitination and deubiquitination has been implicated in various aspects of cancer biology, including tumor initiation, progression, and drug resistance. Recent evidence suggests that deubiquitinating enzymes (DUBs) may have cell type-specific functions within the tumor microenvironment, potentially influencing both cancer cells and stromal components. However, the precise role of deubiquitination in different cell populations within ovarian cancer remains poorly understood. By integrating scRNA-seq analysis with deubiquitination pathway studies, we can better understand how these molecular mechanisms contribute to ovarian cancer heterogeneity and progression, potentially identifying new therapeutic targets and strategies.
With the rapid accumulation of high-throughput sequencing technologies and multi-omics data, machine learning methods are playing an increasingly crucial role in the screening and validation of prognostic biomarkers [ – ]. Machine learning algorithms can identify potential prognostic-related features from complex biological data and establish accurate prediction models [ – ]. Advanced algorithms such as deep learning can integrate transcriptomics, proteomics, and clinical data, enabling a more comprehensive evaluation of potential prognostic biomarkers [ , ]. These computational methods not only improve the efficiency of biomarker screening but also reveal molecular interaction networks that are difficult to discover through traditional approaches. Particularly in studying the complex relationship between DUBs and the tumor microenvironment, the application of machine learning provides new perspectives and tools for identifying key regulatory factors and predicting therapeutic responses.
With advance in high-throughput sequencing, particularly single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) [ – ], it is easier to investigate the complex molecular mechanisms in ovarian cancer by an novel resolution. This study focuses on the role of deubiquitination-related genes in ovarian cancer, and aims to explore their contributions to tumor pathogenesis and progression. By bioinformatics analysis in public data, this study seeks to identify potential biomarkers for improving prognosis and guiding new treatment in patients with ovarian cancer.

## Methods

## Preparation and preprocessing of scRNA-seq data
We comprehensively analyzed scRNA-seq profiles from 8 ovarian cancer samples retrieved from the GSE217517 dataset [ ]. Employing the Seurat R package [ ], we performed an integrated computational workflow for single-cell transcriptomic profiling. Expression data were standardized using LogNormalize methodology, with normalization scaling set at 10,000. Following identification of 2000 highly variable genes, we preprocessed their expression intensities and conducted principal component analysis (PCA). Batch effect mitigation was systematically implemented through the Harmony R package [ , ]. Our analytical strategy integrated sophisticated computational approaches, leveraging advanced functionalities from Seurat and Harmony packages—including NormalizeData, FindVariableFeatures, ScaleData, RunPCA, FindNeighbors, FindClusters, and RunUMAP—to enable comprehensive molecular characterization.

## Analysis of the bulk RNA-seq data
Transcriptome data and clinical annotations for ovarian cancer were retrieved from the Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) database (https://portal.gdc.cancer.gov), encompassing comprehensive RNA sequencing profiles, mutational landscapes, and longitudinal survival information. For robust model validation, we supplemented the primary dataset with seven independent cohorts sourced from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) database (http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo).
To standardize data preprocessing, all datasets underwent logarithmic transformation. Subsequently, we mitigated potential batch effects using the combat function from the sva R package [ ], ensuring analytical consistency and minimizing technical variability.
Investigating Potential Mechanisms and Pathways.
To elucidate potential mechanistic insights, we employed Gene Set Variation Analysis (GSVA) to comprehensively interrogate the underlying biological processes [ , ]. Our analytical approach leveraged a curated gene set collection obtained from the Molecular Signatures Database (MSigDB), enabling a robust and comprehensive pathway investigation.

## Cell–cell communication
We comprehensively integrated gene expression profiles to systematically dissect cell–cell communication dynamics utilizing CellChat [ ]. Employing the canonical CellChatDB ligand-receptor repository as our reference framework, we rigorously adhered to established CellChat analytical protocols. Through meticulous computational analysis, we identified cell type-specific interactions by detecting differentially overexpressed ligands and receptors across distinct cellular populations. Our approach enabled precise inference of intercellular communication networks, with particular emphasis on ligand-receptor interactions characterized by elevated expression signatures.

## Construction of a prognosis signature
We conducted comprehensive univariate Cox regression analysis to systematically assess the prognostic implications of individual genes in ovarian cancer patients. Employing a rigorous tenfold cross-validation approach, we meticulously evaluated 101 distinct machine learning algorithm combinations, encompassing an extensive array of predictive methodologies: stepwise Cox, Lasso, Ridge, partial least squares regression for Cox (plsRcox), CoxBoost, random survival forest (RSF), generalized boosted regression modeling (GBM), elastic net (Enet), supervised principal components (SuperPC), and survival support vector machine (survival-SVM). Our primary objective was to derive the most robust metabolism-related signature (MBRS) with optimal predictive performance, quantitatively determined by the highest concordance index (C-index). To validate the signature’s diagnostic precision, we comprehensively assessed its predictive accuracy through receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis and multidimensional PCA.

## Statistical analysis
We performed comprehensive data processing, statistical analyses, and visualization using R software (version 4.2.0). Survival characteristics specific to molecular subtypes were rigorously evaluated through Kaplan–Meier survival analysis, complemented by log-rank statistical testing. Comparative analyses of continuous variables between groups were conducted using appropriate parametric (t test) or non-parametric (Wilcoxon) approaches, while categorical variable associations were systematically assessed via chi-squared or Fisher’s exact tests. To mitigate multiple testing concerns, we implemented the false discovery rate (FDR) method for p-value adjustment. Interrelationships between variables were explored through Pearson correlation analysis. All statistical tests were two-tailed, with statistical significance conservatively defined as P < 0.05.

## Results

## Sample integration, cell clustering and annotation
This study utilized 8 scRNA-seq samples of ovarian cancer from the GSE217517 dataset, all obtained from human. First, this study used harmony to process the 8 samples, and then achieved a well-integrated dataset (Fig.  A). Next, 24 clusters were identified through Seurat (Fig.  B). To better characterize these clusters, the expression of 16 commonly recognized marker genes in each cluster was observed, including EPCAM, PECAM1, VWF, COL1A1, ACTA2, LYZ, CD68, KIT, MS4A2, NKG7, NCR1, MS4A1, CD79A, CD3E, CD8A, and FOXP3. 
The epithelial cell marker gene, EPCAM, was mainly expressed in cluster 3, 4, and 18; the endothelial cell marker genes, PECAM1 and VWF, were mainly expressed in cluster 8, 15, and 21; the fibroblast marker genes, COL1A1 and ACTA2, were mainly expressed in cluster 0, 6, 13, 17, and 22; the monocyte/macrophage marker gene, LYZ, was mainly expressed in cluster 5, 7, and 12, while the macrophage marker gene, CD68, was primarily expressed in cluster 5 and 7. The markers for mast cells, KIT and MS4A2, were mainly expressed in cluster 23. For NK cells and cytotoxic T cells, the marker gene NKG7 was mainly expressed in cluster 2, 11, and 14. The marker for NK cells, NCR1, was specifically expressed in cluster 11. The mature B-cell marker gene MS4A1 was expressed in cluster 16, while the B-cell marker gene CD79A was expressed in clusters 10 and 16. The T-cell marker gene CD3E was expressed in clusters 1, 2, 9, 11, and 14; the CD8 + T-cell marker gene CD8A was expressed in clusters 1 and 2; and the regulatory T-cell (Treg) marker gene FOXP3 was expressed in cluster 9 (Fig.  C, SUP3A).
At last, with the consideration of machine learning-based annotation, these clusters were identified as B cells, epithelial cells, NK/T cells, endothelial cells, myeloid cells, fibroblasts, Treg cells, and mast cells (Fig.  D).

## The activity score of deubiquitinating genes
A total of 245 deubiquitinating genes were selected from the Genecard database, and then AUCell was utilized to score in the cells. The results revealed that myeloid cells and T cells had relatively higher scores, which indicates active expression of deubiquitinating genes, while epithelial cells had lower scores (Fig.  E, SUP3B).
Cut by the median score, cells were divided into high-score (DUB-high) and low-score (DUB-low) groups. DUB-high cells were predominantly observed in myeloid cells, whereas DUB-low cells were more prevalent in epithelial cells. Other cell clusters had a mix of DUB-high and DUB-low cells, which were consistent with the scoring results (Fig.  F).

## Transcriptome similarities and differences between DUB-high and DUB-low cells
To explore the differences between DUB-high and DUB-low cells, hallmark gene sets from the Broad Institute were deployed. These gene sets are well-curated and integrate critical cancer characteristics, making them especially suitable for cancer research. Compared to DUB-low cells, tumor-related pathways in DUB-high cells predominantly showed upregulation, with 42 pathways up-regulated, 4 down-regulated, and 4 showing no significant difference (Fig.  A). The top five most significantly up-regulated pathways were Allograft rejection, Interferon gamma response, IL6 JAK STAT3 signaling, Inflammatory response, and TNF-α signaling via NF-κB, while the four downregulated pathways were Coagulation, Myogenesis, Epithelial mesenchymal transition, and KRAS signaling DN. 
Correlation analyses and clustering were performed, and heatmap was drawn. The results showed that among all these cell subtypes, transcriptomic expression between B cells and endothelial cells exhibited high correlation (P < 0.01), while fibroblasts and mast cells, as well as NK/T cells and Treg cells, showed moderate correlation (P < 0.05). No statistically significant correlation was observed among other cell types (Fig.  .B). For DUB-high cells, B cells and endothelial cells showed high transcriptomic correlation (P < 0.01), and fibroblasts and mast cells showed moderate correlation (P < 0.05). In DUB-low cells, B cells and endothelial cells also showed high correlation (P < 0.01), while NK/T cells and Treg cells showed moderate correlation (P < 0.05). No statistically significant correlation was observed among other cell types in either group (Fig.  C, D).
Single-cell Tissue Preference Analysis revealed that Treg cells were significantly enriched in the DUB-high group, while epithelial cells were depleted. In contrast, epithelial cells were enriched in the DUB-low group, while Treg cells were depleted. Other cell types, including B cells, NK/T cells, endothelial cells, myeloid cells, fibroblasts, and mast cells, showed no significant differences between the two groups (Fig.  E).

## Differences of cell–cell communication between two groups
To investigate differences in cell–cell communication between DUB-high and DUB-low cells, cell communication analysis was conducted. The results showed that both the number and strength of cell–cell interactions were significantly higher in the DUB-high cells than in the DUB-low cells (Fig.  A). In the DUB-high cells, all 8 cell subtypes exhibited increased communication, which indicating more active interactions. Both the outgoing and incoming interactions were higher in DUB-high cells compared to DUB-low cells (Fig.  B). Among all cell subtypes, fibroblasts had the highest outgoing interactions, while myeloid cells had the highest incoming interactions in the DUB-high cells. Fibroblasts interacted with all seven other cell types, mainly with epithelial cells, endothelial cells, and myeloid cells, with stronger interactions in the DUB-high cells than in the DUB-low cells. Endothelial cells also exhibited active interactions with epithelial cells and myeloid cells (Fig.  C, D). 
The heatmap further revealed differences in communication patterns. Compared to the DUB-low cells, endothelial and epithelial cells in the DUB-high cells exhibited increased interactions with all cell types except B cells, while fibroblasts showed reduced interactions with B cells. Additionally, endothelial cells and fibroblasts demonstrated stronger interaction strengths with all cell types, except for a decrease in mast cell interactions with Treg cells (Fig.  E).
In order to further detect the critical signaling pathways that exhibit differences, this study compared the information flow between DUB-high cells and DUB-low cells. Several pathways, including MIF, MHC-I, COLLAGEN, MHC-II, FN1, and LAMININ, were active in both groups without significant differences. However, pathways such as MK, SPP1, APP, CD99, VISFATIN, and ADGRE5 were significantly up-regulated in the DUB-high cells (Fig.  F, SUP1).

## Bulk transcriptome analysis and construction of a prognosis signature
To identify disease-related deubiquitinating genes, scRNA-seq data were integrated with bulk transcriptome analysis. Eight datasets (GSE140082, GSE14764, GSE17260, GSE26193, GSE32062, GSE49997, GSE63885, and TCGA-OV) comprising 1,623 samples were included (Fig.  A). After integration, no significant batch effects were observed (Fig.  B). Univariate COX regression analysis using TCGA-OV identified 23 prognostic genes with significant differences, including 15 genes associated with poor prognosis (FBN1, COL5A2, XPO4, PHF20, DPYSL3, NR4A3, NBL1, ITGB5, ID3, ANTXR1, PARVA, HECA, BGN, PRDM2, ACTB) and 8 genes associated with favorable prognosis (CLEC11A, PFKFB3, MEIS1, SOX17, CTLA4, ICOS, PSMB9, CD3G) (Fig.  C). To develop a consensus deubiquitination-related signature (DRS), 101 machine-learning algorithms were employed to evaluate the 23 prognostic genes identified through univariate Cox regression analysis. TCGA was selected as the training set, while 7 other datasets, which were obtained from GEO, served as validation sets. In the training dataset, this study built 101 prediction models using a tenfold cross-validation method and calculated the C index for both the training and validation sets, as shown in Fig.  D. Among the 101 models, the RSF + Ridge model was selected based on the highest mean C-index. This model performed well in both the training and validation sets. To evaluate the prognostic value of the RSF + Ridge model, survival analysis was conducted across all 8 datasets using the median risk score as the cutoff. The results demonstrated that patients in the high-risk group consistently had significantly worse prognoses compared to those in the low-risk group, with statistical significance observed (Fig.  E). The model’s predictive performance was evaluated using ROC curves (Fig.  A). In the TCGA-OV cohort, the AUCs for 1-year, 3-year, and 5-year survival predictions were 0.57, 0.65, and 0.64, respectively. In the GEO validation datasets, the AUCs varied across different datasets. For instance, the AUCs of GSE140082, for 1-year and 3-year value were 0.73 and 0.67, respectively; whereas the AUC of GSE63885 for 1-year value reached 0.80, but the AUC for 5-year value was only 0.53. Overall, the model demonstrated strong predictive performance in certain validation datasets, such as GSE17260 and GSE26193, where the AUCs for 1-year, 3-year, and 5-year survival predictions all exceeded 0.7. Based on these results from all 8 datasets, it can be concluded that the model exhibits good prognostic predictive value. 

## Immune microenvironment and DRS
By using DRS, samples were divided into high-risk and low-risk groups, and PCA was performed to observe expression differences. High-risk and low-risk samples exhibited clear separation, especially in datasets such as GSE26193, GSE32062, GSE63885, GSR49997 and GSE17260(Fig.  B). And also, a moderate separation was observed in TCGA-OV, GSE140082 and GSE14764. In order to explore the difference in immune micro-environment, immune infiltration analysis was used with seven algorithms in TCGA. The result revealed that cancer-associated fibroblasts, macrophage M2, endothelial cells, and neutrophils were more enriched in the high-risk samples. In contrast, macrophage M1, activated myeloid dendritic cells, CD8 + T cells, Tregs, and CD4 + T cells were more enriched in the low-risk samples (Fig. SUP2). Subsequently, the expression of immune marker genes such as MHC I, MHC II, other MHC, co stimulators, and co inhibitors was analyzed using the TCGA dataset. It can be observed that these immune related genes are mainly expressed higher in the low-risk samples, and this trend is more significant in MHC genes (Fig.  C).

## Discussion
Ovarian cancer is one of the common gynecological tumors with extremely poor prognosis. This study identified significant heterogeneity in ovarian cancer driven by the differential activity of deubiquitinating genes. Notably, DUB-high cells exhibited enhanced tumor-promoting pathways, active immune interactions, and enrichment of myeloid and Treg cells, while DUB-low cells were associated with epithelial phenotypes and reduced immune activity. A prognostic risk signature based on DUB expression was also established, offering clinical predictive value.
After annotation, this study identified 8 cell types, including B cells, epithelial cells, NK/T cells, endothelial cells, myeloid cells, fibroblasts, Treg cells, and mast cells. This is consistent with previous studies by Xu, Junfun et al., Regner et al., and Olbrecht et al., indicating the robustness of the annotation [ – ]. This provides a solid foundation for subsequent analyses.
Moreover, this study found that the Interference gamma response, IL6-JAK-STAT3 signaling, inflammatory response, and TNF-α signaling via NF-κB pathways are upregulated in DUB-high cells. These pathways are associated with various immune cells such as macrophages, T cells, B cells, NK cells, etc. [ – ]. This suggests that there is more active immune cell infiltration in DUB-high cells. And also it suggests that the DUB genes may be related to the immune killing and evasion mechanisms of ovarian cancer cells, and may be a potential therapeutic target for the disease.
The results of the cell–cell communication analysis showed that both the number and strength of interactions were elevated in DUB-high cells. This suggests that DUB-high cells have more active interactions and that the DUB genes may be associated with enhanced cell–cell communication. This findings has rarely been mentioned in previous studies. In addition, the information flow analysis revealed that MK (Midkine), SPP1, APP, CD99, VISFATIN (NAMPT), and ADGRE5 were highly expressed in DUB-high cells. Midkine is a neurotrophic growth factor crucial during embryonic development, playing significant roles in migration, proliferation, cell growth, survival, reproduction, repair, and angiogenesis [ ]. SPP1, a secreted protein, is closely associated with macrophages [ – ]. Both APP and CD99 are cell membrane proteins; APP is expressed in various cell types, with particularly high levels in the cells of nervous system, while CD99 is mainly found in various immune cells. VISFATIN (NAMPT) is a key regulator of the intracellular nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD) pool and closely linked to metabolism and stress [ ]. ADGRE5, on the other hand, belongs to the G-protein-coupled receptor family. These findings suggest that DUB-high cells exhibit highly active cellular communication and higher immune cell activity, warranting further investigation.
By univariate Cox analysis, this study identified 23 significant DUB genes involved in various essential cellular processes, including immune regulation, cytoskeleton organization, metabolism, regulation of transcription, cell adhesion, cell signal transduction, and development of embryo or tissue. Among these genes, CTLA4, a member of the immunoglobulin family, encodes a protein that transmits inhibitory signals to T cells. It is mainly expressed on T cells and plays a critical role in the negative regulation of immune responses [ , ]. CTLA4 is associated with favorable prognosis in this study and has potential as a target for tumor immunotherapy; it worths more attention and further investigation [ ]. MEIS1, a member of the Homeobox gene family, is involved in the regulation of cellular development. Although it was initially discovered in leukemia, subsequent studies have demonstrated its role in suppressing cell proliferation and migration in most solid tumors [ ]. Furthermore, reduced MEIS1 expression has been linked to poorer survive in colorectal cancer patients; elevated MEIS1 expression has been shown to overcome resistance to oxaliplatin [ ]. In this study, MEIS1 was also linked with favorable prognosis, highlighting its potential significance. CD3G, a marker gene of T cells, is usually positively correlated with the abundance of T cells within tumor tissues [ ]. In this study, CD3G was associated with favorable prognosis, suggesting that higher T cell infiltration may enhance the immune-mediated killing of ovarian cancer cells. However, there is still significant controversy over the inhibitory or promoting effects of T cells on tumor oncogenesis, so it is worth further research [ ]. This finding highlights the need for further exploration of its function in ovarian cancer immunity.
The DRS model showed a good predictive performance; it is a valuable tool for assessing the prognosis of ovarian cancer patients. By stratifying patients accurately based on their predicted outcomes, this model can provide meaningful insights for guiding the development of personalized and reasonable treatment. Additionally, it may help identify high-risk patients who require more aggressive interventions and treatment plans to their specific needs. Its application has the potential to optimize clinical decision-making, improve management of patients, enhance therapeutic efficacy and improve overall survival outcomes.
Finally, this study compared the expression of genes associated with MHC, co-stimulators, and co-inhibitors between high-risk and low-risk samples. The results revealed significantly higher expression of these genes in low-risk patients. It is well-known that MHC plays a critical role in antigen presentation and T cell recognition, co-stimulators enhance immune responses by promoting T cell proliferation and activation, while co-inhibitors suppress T cell and maintain immune tolerance [ – ]. This finding suggests that low-risk samples may have a bigger abundance of T cells and exhibit more active activity of T cell overall. Maybe the enhanced activity of T cell could contribute to stronger tumor-killing, ultimately leading to better prognostic outcomes.
This study integrated scRNA-seq data and bulk transcriptome data to systematically investigate the expression characteristics of deubiquitinating genes and their roles in cell–cell communication and immunity mechanisms. Using bioinformatics analysis, this study deepened the understanding of the DUB genes, provided new perspectives for exploring the tumor immune microenvironment, and laid a foundation for potential applications in tumor diagnosis, treatment, and personalized therapy. This study has valuable scientific value and application prospects. However, this study still has some limitations. First, because of cost and sample availability, this study relies on public datasets rather than own samples, which may limit the accuracy of these findings among certain populations. Second, experimental validation of these results cannot be performed, and then some conclusions in this study are limited to the computational level. Lastly, this study focused only on deubiquitination-related genes, which may overlook other essential molecular mechanisms in HGSOC. Despite this, these limitations did not downgrade the contribution of this study and provided guidance for the future research.

## Conclusions
In summary, this study identified important roles of deubiquitinating genes in ovarian cancer by bioinformatics analysis. The expression of these genes is uneven among 8 cell clusters. DUB-high cells were enriched in myeloid and Treg cells, exhibited upregulated tumor-related pathways, and showed enhanced cell–cell communication, particularly involving fibroblasts and myeloid cells. Conversely, DUB-low cells were enriched in epithelial cells and displayed reduced immune activity. A DUB-related risk signature (DRS) was developed; it has strong prognostic prediction value across multiple datasets and identifies a subset of important genes. These findings reveal the critical involvement of deubiquitinating genes in tumor progression and immune regulation, providing potential biomarkers and therapeutic targets for ovarian cancer.
