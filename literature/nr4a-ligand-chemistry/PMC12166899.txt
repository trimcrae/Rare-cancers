## Introduction
Several studies identified genetic factors and key cellular signaling associated with developing immunoglobulin A nephropathy (IgAN). However, there is still a lack of understanding regarding the relationship between hub-transcription factors (TFs) encoding genes and changes in immunogenic activity. Our objective was to identify hub-TF encoding genes associated with immune cell infiltrations, immunogenic pathway activity, and potential drug candidates in IgAN through bioinformatics techniques.

## Methods
We utilized GSE104948, GSE93798, GSE115857, GSE37460, and GSE35487 to identify key significant DEGs and validation in IgAN relative to the control. Next, we employed various bioinformatics approaches to investigate the key genes and their relationship with immunity in IgAN. Finally, we identitified enriched drugs and elcudate their molecular interactions with TFs via molecular docking approaches.

## Results
We identified 1,123 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) between IgAN and control samples, comprising 342 upregulated genes and 780 downregulated genes. The upregulated genes are linked to immune-related biological processes and KEGG pathways, while the downregulated genes are associated with metabolic processes. Five significant clusters were identified, enriched in several KEGG pathways. We explored 26 hub-TF encoding genes, including GATA2, HDAC1, TSC22D3, SOX9, RARA, RORA, KLF5, KMT2A, FOSB, and FOSL1, which were consistently dysregulated in IgAN patients. Immunogenic analysis revealed increased levels of Th1 cells, pDCs, monocytes, M2 macrophages, fibroblasts, endothelial cells, and activated dendritic cells in IgAN. The activity of various immunological pathways was also elevated. The expression of hub-TFs like GATA2, HDAC1, TSC22D3, SOX9, RARA, RORA, KLF5, KMT2A, FOSB, and FOSL1 correlated with immune signatures and pathways in IgAN. Additionally, these hub-TFs were linked to diagnostic efficacy and drug interactions. Molecular docking identified key drug candidates for inhibiting HDAC1 and modulating RARA, suggesting their potential for IgAN treatment.

## Conclusions
We identified key hub-TFs and their association with immune infiltration and immune pathways linked to IgAN initiation and progression. These findings provide important insights into the immunological mechanisms driving IgAN and propose potential treatment approaches. Molecular docking further revealed key drug candidates for inhibiting and modulating these targets, highlighting their therapeutic potential for IgAN.

## Introduction
Immunoglobulin A nephropathy (IgAN) is the leading type of primary glomerulonephritis globally. This condition reduces life expectancy by approximately a decade and results in end-stage kidney failure for 25–50 % of those affected within two decades of being diagnosed. IgAN is a clinical and pathological disorder that presents with various symptoms, including the presence of blood in the urine (hematuria), the accumulation of IgA1 in the mesangium of the glomeruli, increased proliferation of mesangial cells, and the enlargement of the extracellular matrix. It was thought that the occurrence and development of IgAN depend on genetic factors. The genetic factors included copy number variations, epigenetic changes, and transcriptome alterations. Shou-Liang et al. identified crucial genes and pathways in IgAN n using bioinformatics analysis. Xiaoxue Chen et al. identified key genes, signaling pathways, and potential therapeutic agents for IgAN by using gene expression profiles. Xue Jiang and colleagues investigated novel hub genes that play a crucial role in the development of IgAN. The miRNA-mRNA networks are key biological markers for the pathogenesis and development of IgAN. Abnormalities in the production and/or catabolism of IgA are considered important in the etiology and pathogenesis of IgAN. The immunological molecules, including IgA receptors, Toll-like receptors, and complements, have been crucially associated with the pathogenesis of IgAN. Furthermore, immunological factors, including dendritic cells, NK cells, macrophages, T-lymphocyte subsets, and B-lymphocytes, are strongly linked to the development and progression of IgAN. Disrupted cellular signaling and abnormal deposition of immune complexes in the kidney trigger glomerular damage. The improper formation and deposition of immune complexes in the mesangium, along with the abnormal expression of effector molecules and dysregulated immune cell activation and differentiation, further exacerbate local inflammation and immune responses, ultimately resulting in tissue injury in IgAN. 
Transcription factors (TFs) regulate the biological signaling and immunogenic activities in human diseases. TFs in human diseases critically control gene expression programs, and deregulations of TFs potentially influence the pathogenesis of human diseases. In addition to several clinical disorders, TFs play a significant role in regulating IgAN consequences. For example, Yingchun Cui et al. revealed that TFs-related regulatory networks are linked to inflammation which is ultimately related to the effectiveness as potential therapeutic targets for IgAN. TFs are connected to the multilayer regulatory network during the development of IgAN. According to a single-cell transcriptomics study, unregulated TFs are linked to EMT and fibrosis, which promote the development and progression of IgAN. A transcriptional factor NF-κB was expressed in mesangial, glomerular endothelium and epithelial cells, tubular epithelial cells, and infiltrating cells and activated various cytokines and growth factors to support inflammatory and immune responses. A transcriptional network contains early growth response protein 1 (EGR1) as the central node relating to the other DEGs in the kidneys of IgAN patients. These investigations hint at the crucial roles that immunologic and transcriptional factors engage in the initiation and progression of IgAN. Herein, we identified the key genes and correlation of hub-transcription factors (hub-TFs) encoding genes with the alterations of immunogenic activity in IgAN patients.

## Methods
2.1

## Datasets
We set the keywords “immunoglobulin A nephropathy”, “nephropathy”, “Immunoglobulin A”, “glomerulonephritis”, “glomeruli”, and “IgAN” for identifying the gene expression profiles in GEO module of NCBI (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/). We selected expression profiling datasets of IgAN, including GSE104948, GSE93798, GSE115857, and GSE37460, , The GSE104948 dataset consisting of 27 IgAN patients and 3 controls. The GSE93798 dataset contains 20 IgAN patients and 22 controls. The GSE115857 dataset comprising 62 samples, with 55 from IgAN patients and 7 from controls. Additionally, the GSE37460 datasetincludes 36 samples, with 27 IgAN patients and 9 controls.

2.2

## Identifying significant DEGs in IgAN
We utilized Network Analyst software to analyze and determine the key differentially expressed genes (DEGs) between IgAN and healthy samples. This tool enabled us to process the data and identify the most significant genes involved in the comparison between the two groups. By choosing the ComBat method of the R program in the Network Analyst software, we eliminated the batch effects of four separate datasets. We processed the data by applying either base-2 log transformation or quantile normalization. To identify the significant DEGs between IgAN and healthy samples, we used the “limma” R package within the NetworkAnalyst platform. The four datasets were merged into a single consolidated dataset, and Cochran's combination test was conducted for the meta-analysis across all four datasets. To control for false discovery rate (FDR), we applied the Benjamini–Hochberg method for adjustment. Significant DEGs were selected using a threshold of absolute combined effect size (ES) greater than 0.585 and an adjusted p-value of less than 0.05.

2.3

## Functional enrichment analysis of selected DEGs in IgAN
We performed functional enrichment analysis on the selected significant DEGs using Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) software. The significantly upregulated and downregulated DEGs were uploaded into the GSEA tool to identify the pathways most strongly associated with these genes. Our analysis revealed that several KEGG pathways were notably linked to the DEGs. We used FDR < 0.05 to determine the significant pathways,. 

2.4

## Construction of the PPI network, analysis of gene clusters, and exploration of hub-transcription factors in IgAN
To examine the molecular interactions among the significant DEGs, we created a PPI network using the STRING online server . We obtained PPI data from STRING and imported it into Cytoscape for analysis. Using the MCODE plugin, we identified key gene modules based on interaction patterns and their biological relevance . We identified the important gene modules through MCODE analysis, setting the MCODE score threshold to greater than 4. To determine the most influential hub genes, we used the Cytoscape plugin cytoHubba, which ranks genes based on their interaction degree within the network. The protein–protein interaction (PPI) network was built by setting a minimum interaction threshold of 0.40. We focused on selecting hub genes that were connected to at least 25 DEGs within the PPI network. To visualize the resulting PPI network, we used Cytoscape 3.6.1 software, which allowed us to represent and analyze the complex interactions between the genes Additionally, we identified the hub-transcription factor (TF)-encoding genes in IgAN by comparing the hub genes with the hub-TF encoding genes obtained from the GSEA database using a Venn diagram.

2.5

## Validation of Hub-TF expression in an independent cohort of IgAN patients
To validate the expression of hub-transcription factors (hub-TFs) in an independent IgAN cohort, we retrieved and processed the expression data from the GSE35487 dataset. This dataset is based on the Affymetrix Human Genome U133A Array platform and includes 25 IgAN patients and 6 control subjects. We used a two-tailed t-test to assess the significance of the selected hub-TFs (P < 0.05). For genes with multiple probes, we selected the probe exhibiting the highest LogFC.

2.6

## Exploring the correlation between Hub-TF encoding genes, immune signatures, and immune pathways in IgAN via ssGSEA
We used ssGSEA to calculate the enrichment scores of immune signatures and immune-related pathways for each sample and corresponding gene set in the IgAN cohort. We utilized marker gene sets corresponding to various immune signatures to calculate the ssGSEA scores for these immune signatures in IgAN samples. , , , We included the marker genes for immune signatures in Supplementary
 . Additionally, we retrieved the gene set for 21 immune-related pathways from the KEGG database (https://www.genome.jp/kegg/). The gene set for immune-related pathways is provided in Supplementary . We conducted Spearman's correlation analysis to evaluate the relationship between ssGSEA scores and key hub-TF (R > 0.30 and p < 0.05).

2.7

## Assessing the diagnostic value of hub-TF encoding genes
We employed receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis to evaluate the diagnostic potential of key hub-TF encoding genes in IgAN. The area under the ROC curve (AUC) was calculated using the “pROC” R package to determine the accuracy of these genes in distinguishing IgAN samples. For individual genes, a higher AUC value indicates a stronger diagnostic capability in differentiating between IgAN and healthy samples. An AUC value greater than 0.5 reflects the effectiveness of these genes in diagnosing IgAN within the dataset. 

2.8

## Evaluation of candidate drugs for key transcription factor (TF)
Using the drug gene interaction database (DGIdb), which allows for the exploration of the druggable genome for personalized medicine, drug molecules are extracted from the input of hub transcription factor (TF) encoding genes. The DGIdb database (https://www.dgidb.org/) is available through the Enrichr platform (https://amp.pharm.mssm.edu/Enrichr/), which is used for enrichment analysis and offers graphical representations of gene activity. 

2.9

## Molecular docking
We downloaded the GATA encoding protein (https://alphafold.ebi.ac.uk/entry/A0A7L2HB90) and detected the binding site by using PrankWeb. Moreover, We downloaded the HDAC1 encoding protein HDAC1 (4BKX) and active site was detected by the previous study and PrankWeb. 
RARA encoding protein was downloaded (3A9E) and agonist binding site was identified by the previous study and PrankWeb. We also downloaded RORA encoding protein (4S15) and KLF5 encoding protein (2EBT (https://www.rcsb.org/structure/2EBT)). The protein was prepared using BIOVIA Discovery Studio software. For the compounds selected for analysis, the SMILES structure converted into SDF format using DataWarrior software. We performed the molecular docking analysis using PyRx software. First, the protein structure was converted into the PDBQT format. Then, the binding site were identified and used to generate a grid. The ligands were then energy-minimized and also converted into the PDBQT format in preparation for docking. In the final step, the docking simulations were carried out to evaluate their binding affinity.

2.10

## Statistical analysis and overall in silico design of the study
We used R software (version 4.0.1) for conducting statistical analysis. Pearson’s correlation test was used to explore relationships between hub-TF encoding genes (R > 0.30 A and p < 0.05). Spearman’s correlation assessed associations between hub-TF genes and ssGSEA scores for immune signatures (R > 0.30 A and p < 0.05). A two-tailed t-test was applied to compare immune signatures and pathway activities between IgAN and healthy samples, with P < 0.05 considered significant. Data visualizations were generated using the ggplot2 package. illustrates the overall in silico procedure, which involves the integration of multiple datasets, identification of differentially expressed genes and transcription factors, validation of hub-transcription factors, and evaluation of potential therapeutic drug candidates.

## Extraction of potential drugs for the key hub-TFs
Evaluating protein–drug interactions is crucial for understanding the structural features that affect proein sensitivity. These interactions reveal how drugs bind to target proteins, influencing their efficacy and specificity. Transcription factors (TFs) are essential biological components in the development and progression of diseases. This knowledge aids in developing more effective drugs to specific TFs profiles, ultimately enhancing patient outcomes. The Enrichr tool is employed to identify therapeutic compounds linked to hub transcription factors (TFs), using data sourced from the DGIdb repository. The analysis of therapeutic drugs was conducted based on P-values and adjusted P-values, leading to the identification of several key drug candidates. Notably, melatonin, dexamethasone, daunorubicin, 4SC 202, adapalene, tazarotene, alitretinoin, ouabain, isotretinoin, and naloxone emerged as the top compounds most associated with the genes of interest (Supplementary ). Fasudil and bortezomib are promising drug candidates targeting GATA2 (Supplementary ). For HDAC1, several potential drug candidates have been identified, including 4SC-202, mocetinostat, belinostat, cudc-101, entinostat, panobinostat, vorinostat, and valproic acid (Supplementary ).. Additionally, for the RARA pathway, drug candidates such as adapalene, tazarotene, alitretinoin, isotretinoin, naloxone, acitretin, tretinoin, fenretinide, colchicine, and melatonin have been proposed as potential therapeutic options (Supplementary ).. Ouabain is a potential drug candidate for KLF5, while Venetoclax and Cytarabine are promising candidates for KMT2A
(Supplementary ).. Dexamethasone and Hydrocortisone show potential for targeting TSC22D3, and Melatonin is identified as a potential drug candidate for RORA
(Supplementary ). Each of these compounds is linked to modulating specific biological pathways, reflecting their therapeutic potential in influencing cellular processes and immune responses. These drugs, associated with the identified hub-TFs, hold particular relevance for the treatment of IgAN. In this study, several promising drug candidates have been identified for key genes and pathways of interest, including melatonin, dexamethasone, daunorubicin, 4SC-202, adapalene, tazarotene, alitretinoin, ouabain, isotretinoin, and naloxone (Supplementary ). To gain a deeper understanding of their therapeutic potential, molecular docking simulations will be performed to analyze how these candidate drugs interact with their target molecules. This will help assess the strength of their binding and the affinity for their targets, offering valuable information about their effectiveness and suitability for subsequent stages of development. summarizes the binding affinities of various drug candidates in complex with key proteins associated with genes of interest such as HDAC1, GATA2, RARA, KLF5, and RORA.

## Discussion
Microarray technology is an advanced, rapid, accurate, and cost-effective molecular diagnostic tool, known for its high specificity and sensitivity in constructing gene expression data. In this study, several statistical analyses were employed to integrate multiple public datasets, identify DEGs, and explore their functional enrichment. Differential expression analysis was performed using standard method such as limma package to determine significant genes across the datasets. To assess the association between identified Hub-TFs and immune pathways, ssGSEA was utilized, which provided a measure of immune-related pathway activity in individual samples. Molecular docking were also conducted to predict the interaction between candidate drugs and key Hub-TFs, aiding in the identification of potential therapeutic agents for IgAN. The overall results include the integration of multiple datasets, identification of differentially expressed genes, validation of hub-transcription factors, and evaluation of potential therapeutic drug candidates ( ). A detailed list of these significant DEGs is provided in . offers a visual comparison of the expression levels of the most notable DEGs between IgAN samples and control groups. Previous research has highlighted that these dysregulated genes are consistently linked to nephropathy, reinforcing their potential relevance to the disease. For example, the matrix-associated genes COL4A1 (collagen alpha-1(IV) chain) are increased in IgAN mesangial cells. 
MECOM, another upregulated gene, is also deregulated in the IgAN. The dysregulated expression of the HCLS1 gene is a key factor in the pathogenesis of IgAN. 
FGL2 was screened as an upregulated overlapping DEGs for critical genes in IgAN. According to Dongmei Zhang et al., the expression of FOSB is significantly decreased in IgAN. 
NR4A2, a transcription factor, is a differentially expressed TF gene associated with the inflammation of IgAN. In glomerulonephritis, SLC19A2 is differentially expressed in an in-vitro model of proteinuria. Altogether, these deregulated transcriptomic signatures may be regulating the IgAN-associated pathogenesis. Then, we found the immunological top biological processes associated with upregulated genes in the IgAN ( A). Ting Gan et al. demonstrated that innate and adaptive immunity regulates the nephropathy of IgAN patients. Furthermore, we identified the key metabolic biological processes linked to the downregulated genes in IgAN ( B). Judy Baek et al. discovered that disrupted lipid metabolism is linked to the advanced stages of chronic kidney disease (CKD) and serves as a predictor of CKD progression. It has been reported that circulating lipid levels are linked to various factors in IgAN patients, including body mass index (BMI), the progression of the disease, and the effectiveness of treatment outcomes. These lipid profiles may serve as important indicators for monitoring disease status and therapeutic responses in patients with IgAN. Moreover, GSEA identified various KEGG pathways significantly associated with the DEGs in IgAN. We revealed that the different immune-associated and cellular signaling pathways are upregulated, and metabolic pathways are downregulated in IgAN ( ). Some previous studies reported the association of IgAN with pathways abnormalities. For example, IgAN commonly develops in renal allografts within the first five years following transplantation. Immune-related processes play a significant role in the development and progression of IgAN, contributing to the underlying pathogenesis of the disease. These processes involve complex immune responses that impact the kidneys' function and structure. The chemokine signaling pathway is associated with the upregulated genes in IgAN. The lectin pathway of complement is linked to the pathogenesis of glomerular complement activation in IgAN. Similarly, the downregulated metabolic pathways are associated with the progression and development of IgAN or other renal diseases. For example, essential fatty acids are complementary treatment for IgAN. Francois Leblond et al demonstrated that a decrease in drug metabolism is associated with chronic renal failure. In non-diabetic CKD, the activity of the citric acid cycle and fatty acid oxidation pathways are reduced which suggests decreased mitochondrial function. PPAR signaling pathway, a protective pathway of IgAN pathogenesis, is also downregulated in the IgAN. Since these pathways are consistently deregulated in the IgAN, it suggests that the deregulated pathways may provide novel insight into the target of IgAN pathogenesis. 
In the protein–protein interaction (PPI) network analysis of differentially expressed genes (DEGs), we identified five crucial clusters that are highly associated with various cancer-related and immune pathways. Gene clusters play a critical role in regulating various human diseases by coordinating key biological processes such as immune response, metabolism, and cellular maintenance. , Cluster 1 was highly enriched in both cancer‐related and immune pathways, underscoring its dual role in tumor biology and immune regulation. Key cancer pathways included pathways in cancer, mTOR, MAPK, TGF-β, and specific malignancies such as renal cell carcinoma, melanoma, glioma, and pancreatic cancer. Concurrently, immune and adhesion processes—antigen processing and presentation, cytokine–cytokine receptor interaction, natural killer cell–mediated cytotoxicity, CAMs, focal adhesion, and ECM–receptor interaction—were also prominent. These findings suggest that Cluster 1 genes coordinate tumor progression, metastatic potential, and immune responses, making them attractive targets for combined anti-tumor and immunomodulatory therapies. Cluster 2 is enriched for both immune response and cell–matrix interaction pathways, including natural killer cell–mediated cytotoxicity, chemokine, Toll-like and T-cell receptor signaling, FcγR- and FcεRI-mediated phagocytosis, complement cascades, and cytokine–receptor interactions. It also highlights adhesion and motility processes (focal adhesion, actin cytoskeleton regulation, ECM-receptor interaction, CAMs, tight and gap junctions) alongside cancer-related pathways (prostate cancer, glioma, melanoma, small cell lung cancer) and infection/inflammatory signaling (Helicobacter pylori, Leishmania, viral myocarditis). Additionally, VEGF signaling, apoptosis, and cardiomyopathy pathways are represented, underscoring Cluster 2′s role in coordinating immune surveillance with tumor microenvironment remodeling.
Cluster 3 is predominantly associated with metabolic and immune pathways, reflecting its dual role in cellular energy homeostasis and inflammation. It is enriched for lipid and glucose metabolism processes—including PPAR and insulin signaling, fatty acid metabolism, glycolysis/gluconeogenesis, the TCA cycle, and amino acid degradation (valine, leucine, and isoleucine)—as well as peroxisome function and proximal tubule bicarbonate reclamation. Concurrently, Cluster 3 features immune‐related pathways such as complement and coagulation cascades, chemokine signaling, and links to autoimmune conditions like systemic lupus erythematosus, alongside metabolic disorders like type II diabetes mellitus. These findings underscore the importance of metabolic‐immune crosstalk in renal pathology and suggest that targeting metabolic regulators could modulate both energy balance and inflammatory responses. Cluster 4 is characterized by extensive enrichment in metabolic pathways—ranging from short‐chain fatty acid metabolism (propanoate, butanoate, β-alanine), amino acid metabolism (histidine, lysine, arginine, proline, valine/leucine/isoleucine), and central energy production (glycolysis/gluconeogenesis, pyruvate metabolism, oxidative phosphorylation, peroxisome)—to specialized biosynthetic routes (glycerolipid, tryptophan, ascorbate/aldarate, purine). It also encompasses key signaling cascades (calcium, GnRH, vascular smooth muscle contraction), reproductive processes (progesterone-mediated oocyte maturation, oocyte meiosis), and neural functions (long-term potentiation), with disease associations including dilated cardiomyopathy and melanogenesis. These findings suggest that Cluster 4 genes integrate core metabolic control with tissue-specific signaling and developmental pathways, presenting multifaceted targets for therapeutic modulation. Cluster 5 is enriched for infection‐related signaling and fundamental cellular maintenance pathways. It explores epithelial responses to Vibrio cholerae and Helicobacter pylori, underscoring barrier and host‐microbe interactions, alongside innate immune activation via the cytosolic DNA‐sensing pathway. Metabolic processes such as fatty acid, tryptophan, lysine, butanoate, and pentose phosphate metabolism are prominent, complemented by organelle functions in the lysosome and peroxisome. Finally, enrichment of basal transcription factors and nucleotide excision repair points to roles in gene regulation and DNA damage response. Together, these pathways suggest that Cluster 5 genes coordinate microbial defense, metabolic homeostasis, and genomic integrity in IgAN.
Additionally, we examined the relationship between the consistently dysregulated hub-transcription factor (TF)-encoding genes and the regulation of immune responses in IgAN patients. Our analysis revealed that 11 hub-TF-encoding genes—namely GATA2, HDAC1, CBX6, TSC22D3, SOX9, RARA, RORA, KLF5, KMT2A, FOSB, and FOSL1—are persistently dysregulated in IgAN ( ). Prior studies have established a connection between these dysregulated hub-TF genes and the onset and progression of IgAN, along with other kidney-related disorders. For example, Lei Yu et al. reported that GATA2 expression is detected in the principal cells of the kidney, suggesting its potential role in renal function and cellular processes. 
HDAC1, another hub-TFs encoding genes, can stimulate pro-proliferative, extracellular matrix synthesis, and profibrotic actions in human renal mesangial cells. The machine learning approaches revealed that FOSB acted as the essential gene in the IgAN. The severity of IgAN is critically related to the single nucleotide polymorphisms of the FOSL1 gene. Altered expression levels of KLF5 have been shown to regulate podocyte apoptosis, renal cell proliferation, tubulointerstitial inflammation, and the progression of renal fibrosis in a variety of kidney diseases. These previous findings and our results suggest that the aberrantly expressed hub-TFs encoding genes crucially contributed to the development and progression of IgAN.
Since TFs are associated with the stimulation of chronic inflammation in the human kidney, we speculated that the number of immune cells and immune-associated pathways is aberrantly activated in IgAN. We compared the quantitative variations in immune signatures ( ) and pathway activity ( ) between IgAN and control samples. Interestingly, we observed a significant increase in both immune cell populations and the activity of immune-associated pathways in IgAN ( , ). Some previous studies indicated the association of deregulated immunity with IgAN development and pathogenesis. For example, H. Suzuki et al. showed that Th1 cells play a key role in the pathophysiological processes of IgA nephropathy (IgAN) following the deposition of IgA in the glomeruli. Their findings suggest that these immune cells contribute significantly to the disease progression once IgA accumulates in the kidney. Nuoyan Zheng et al. found that the content of pDCs was increased in PBMCs of IgAN patients relative to the healthy controls. The gene signatures of monocyte are expansively expressed in IgAN. M2 macrophages, another immunosuppressive cell, could be found to increase in IgAN patients. In progressive renal failure, fibroblasts are associated with matrix-producing in the kidney, and hence they are clinically relevant as principal mediators of renal fibrosis. We uncovered that the hub-TFs, including GATA2, HDAC1, CBX6, TSC22D3, SOX9, RARA, RORA, KLF5, KMT2A, FOSB, and FOSL1, is significantly correlated with numerous immune-associated pathways ( ), suggesting that these key genes are substantially associated with the immunopathogenesis of IgAN. The molecular docking results shed light on the potential of several drug candidates that target HDAC1, GATA2, RARA, KLF5, and RORA in the context of IgAN, providing valuable information for future therapeutic development. For HDAC1, the molecular docking results showed that compounds like 4SC-202, Belinostat, and Panobinostat exhibited strong binding affinities, indicating their potential as effective inhibitors of HDAC1. Inhibition of HDAC1 could be particularly effective in IgAN, as HDAC1 is involved in the regulation of immune responses and inflammation, both of which contribute to disease progression. Considering the inflammatory characteristics of IgAN and the role of immune dysregulation in its development, targeting HDAC1 to alleviate inflammation and regulate immune cell function may represent a promising therapeutic strategy. The study demonstrated that co-administration of belinostat and 5-fluorouracil produces synergistic anti-tumor effects in preclinical models, suggesting a promising new therapeutic strategy for renal cancer. The study found that panobinostat and nelfinavir act synergistically to suppress renal cancer growth by triggering pronounced endoplasmic reticulum stress. The study revealed that the class I histone deacetylase inhibitor entinostat effectively suppresses regulatory T cells, thereby enhancing the efficacy of immunotherapies in both renal and prostate cancer models. These docking results support the idea that HDAC1 inhibition may help alleviate the inflammatory processes and immune-mediated damage associated with IgAN. For RARA, the identified compounds, including Adapalene, Naloxone, and Fenretinide, exhibited significant binding affinities, interacting with the agonist binding site of RARA, and indicating their potential to activate RARA. RARA, a key receptor for retinoic acid, is essential for regulating immune responses, cellular differentiation, and inflammation. In the case of IgAN, studies have found that RARA expression is reduced, which could play a significant role in the disease's pathogenesis. Nuclear retinoic acid receptors (RARs), including RARA, are key transcription factors involved in cellular signalling, such as immune regulation, cell differentiation, and inflammation. In this study, I focused on the agonist binding site of RARA and performed molecular docking with various drug candidates, including Adapalene, Naloxone, and Fenretinide. The docking results revealed strong binding affinities of these compounds at the RARA agonist site, indicating their potential to activate the receptor. Given that RARA plays a critical role in regulating immune responses and inflammation, the activation of RARA by these drugs could help modulate immune dysregulation, a key feature of Immunoglobulin A Nephropathy (IgAN). These findings suggest that these compounds may have therapeutic potential in restoring RARA function and alleviating inflammatory processes associated with IgAN. Additionally, the moderate binding of Fasudil to GATA2, Ouabain to KLF5, and Melatonin to RORA suggests their potential in regulating immune pathways and cellular processes relevant to IgAN. Further research is required to fully assess the therapeutic potential of these compounds in treating IgAN by modulating these key proteins.
Our study has several strengths and limitations. A major strength is that we selected samples from four gene expression datasets (GSE104948, GSE93798, GSE115857, and GSE37460) and compared them with control groups. Furthermore, by combining these four datasets for a meta-analysis, we were able to identify genes with a significant combined effect size across IgAN patient cohorts. However, a key limitation of our study is the absence of experimental validation for the bioinformatics findings. Consequently, further experimental and clinical investigations are needed to validate these results and explore their potential for practical application in treating IgAN patients.

## Conclusions
Exploring hub-TFs in IgAN and their association with various immune cells and immune-associated pathways may provide novel insight into IgAN-associated immunopathogenesis. This study provides insights into the role of hub-TFs in modulating the pathogenesis of IgAN. Emerging therapeutics based on immunological criteria will materialize as a result of this study, offering hints for comprehending the TFs-associated immunopathogenesis of IgAN. It may be possible to create treatments for particular diseases if the relationship between essential hub-TFs and the immunogenomic signaling of IgAN is clarified. In conclusion, molecular docking-based approaches have successfully identified key drug candidates for inhibiting and modulating hub-TFs, highlighting their potential as therapeutic options for treating IgAN.
