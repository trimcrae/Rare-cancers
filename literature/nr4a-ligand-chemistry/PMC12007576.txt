## INTRODUCTION
Autoreactive B cells play a crucial role in autoimmunity. B cell depletion therapies broadly targeting B cells are currently in clinical use for treating autoimmune diseases. However, these approaches affect not only autoreactive B cells but also beneficial B cells, which are essential for protecting against infections. Understanding how pathogenic autoreactive B cells are generated will allow for the development of therapeutics that specifically target harmful B cells. The body is capable of silencing harmful B cells through a process called self-tolerance, which can cause peripheral B cells to become anergic ( ). Anergic B cells are induced by chronic exposure of autoreactive B cells to self-antigens via the B cell receptor (BCR), resulting in the BCR becoming nonresponsive with a shortened lifespan ( , ). Autoreactive B cells accumulate when B cell anergy is disrupted, thereby increasing the risk of developing autoimmune diseases ( – ). The specific pathogenic B cell subtypes derived from autoreactive B cells are poorly understood; however, age-associated B cells (ABCs) have recently been identified as contributing to autoimmunity ( ). ABCs, first detected as a unique B cell subset in aged mice ( , ), can accumulate prematurely (independently of age) in murine autoimmune disease models ( – ). They have also been observed to accumulate in various human autoimmune diseases, including systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), scleroderma, Sjögren’s syndrome, rheumatoid arthritis, and multiple sclerosis ( , – ). In addition, it was shown that ABCs also expand in a number of intracellular infections ( , , ). In contrast to conventional mature B cells, ABCs are characterized by an absence of surface CD21 and CD23. Moreover, they frequently express myeloid markers such as CD11c, CD11b, and the transcription factor T-bet ( ). ABCs are a diverse subset of B cells with polyreactive and autoreactive BCR and are considered a type of memory B cells on the basis of the data of antigen-dependent secondary responses that induce plasma cell differentiation ( , ). They exhibit a distinct gene expression profile compared to other B cell subsets, including variations in transcription factors, cytokines, signaling molecules, and proteins related to adhesion and metabolism ( ). In humans, ABC-defining markers are inconsistent, and features of atypical, double-negative 2, and CD11c+CD21lo B cells resemble ABCs. Although the exact markers used to characterize ABCs vary depending on the experimental model or disease, they share several phenotypic features such as CD11c+ and CD21low; however, it is necessary to also define ABCs on the basis of function.
There is accumulating evidence regarding the signals and stimuli that promote the differentiation of B cells into ABCs. For example, in vitro ABC differentiation from naive B cells occurs on stimulation with Toll-like receptor 7 (TLR7) or TLR9 agonists and exposure to interferon-γ (IFN-γ) or interleukin-21 (IL-21) ( ). ABC generation in vivo has been suggested to be dependent on TLR7, Myd88, and CD40L in aged mice ( ) and on TLR7, IL-21, interferon regulatory factor 5 (IRF5), and zinc finger E-box binding homeobox 2 (Zeb2) in autoimmune diseases ( , , , ). Given that ABCs are enriched in autoreactive BCRs ( , , , , ), it has been suggested that they may arise from B cells that have encountered self-antigen exposure. In particular, because chronic self-antigen exposure is assumed in autoimmune diseases, persistent antigen recognition by B cells may promote ABC expansion and activation in concert with TLR engagement and cytokine signaling. TLR7-dependent ABC differentiation suggests a mechanism in which TLR7 ligands, such as RNA-associated self-antigens, are delivered to endosomes through BCR-mediated antigen uptake. Studies have demonstrated that RNA-associated immune complexes can activate B cells by engaging both BCR and TLR7 pathways, thereby promoting their activation and differentiation ( , ). This dual activation mechanism is particularly relevant in autoimmune diseases like lupus. Despite these insights, the precise mechanisms by which ABCs are generated and maintained under specific conditions, such as aging and autoimmunity, remain unclear.
Here, we show that anergic B cells become pathogenic autoreactive ABCs in aging and autoimmunity. These findings suggest that ABCs may undergo constitutive BCR activation and internalization, a feature reminiscent of autoreactive B cells experiencing chronic self-antigen exposure. Chronic BCR signaling is crucial for generating and maintaining ABCs, ultimately contributing to the progression of autoimmune diseases. We demonstrated that nuclear receptor subfamily 4 group A member 1 (Nr4a1), known to be a hallmark gene of anergic B cells ( , ), was down-regulated during the differentiation of anergic B cells into ABCs. Conversely, overexpression of Nr4a1 suppressed ABC generation. We further demonstrated that pharmacological inhibition of Bruton’s tyrosine kinase (Btk), a key BCR signaling molecule, in the ABCs of mouse models of aging/autoimmunity and humans with lupus markedly reduces their number and pathogenicity. These results provide essential insights into the mechanisms underlying the formation and maintenance of ABCs.

## RESULTS

## Constitutive activation of BCR signaling in ABCs
A wide range of markers have been used to study ABCs in mice and humans in various contexts including aging, infection, and autoimmunity ( , , , , ). In this study, we defined the B cell surface maker subset AA4.1−CD21loCD23−CD11c+ as ABCs on the basis of their function. In aged mice, these populations showed high expression levels of TLR7, TLR9, CD80, and CD86, which correspond to high reactivity to TLR7/9 ligands and strong antigen-presenting capacity to T cells (fig. S1, A to D). Because most of these cells exclusively express T-bet (fig. S1A), CD11c+T-bet+ B cells were also considered ABCs in certain experiments. Given that ABCs are potentially autoreactive B cells, sharing antigen specificity with anergic B cells persistently stimulated by self-antigens in vivo ( ), we hypothesized that ABCs show chronic self-antigen recognition by the BCR. When we assessed the BCR response on ABCs obtained from aged mice compared with follicular (FO) B cells after anti-IgM stimulation, ABCs showed less increase in Ca2+ levels ( ), which supports a previous study ( ). Notably, in the unstimulated steady state, ABCs had higher cytosolic Ca2+ levels than FO B cells ( ). These characteristics closely resembled those typically observed in anergic B cells ( ). We also observed that unstimulated ABCs had increased total phosphorylated proteins compared with FO B cells (fig. S2A). We further investigated the activation status of various intracellular BCR signaling pathways and found that ABCs showed greater phosphorylation of spleen tyrosine kinase (Syk), Btk, phospholipase Cγ2 (PLCγ2), Akt, and Erk in the unstimulated state than FO B cells ( and fig. S2A). In addition, unstimulated ABCs, but not FO B cells, had a robust nuclear translocation of the nuclear factor κB (NF-κB) subunit p65 ( ). Notably, this translocation is not found in anergic B cells ( , , ). Furthermore, we examined nuclear factor of activated T cells (NFAT), a transcription factor known to be activated by sustained Ca2+ signaling through the BCR. We found that unstimulated ABCs exhibited enhanced NFAT dephosphorylation (fig. S2A) and increased nuclear localization ( and fig. S2B), consistent with their elevated levels of sustained intracellular Ca2+. These results show that ABCs have a unique ability to constitutively activate the BCR signaling pathway.
 

## ABCs show cellular internalization of BCR at a steady state
A key question is whether the constitutive activation of BCR signaling in ABCs is due to continuous antigen recognition in vivo. When autoreactive B cells are exposed to self-antigens, they can become anergic and internalize their BCR. When crossing MD4 transgenic mice that express BCR with high affinity for hen egg lysozyme (HEL) with ML5 mice expressing soluble HEL in which all B cells were self-specific to the neoself antigen HEL (MD4 × ML5), B cells that survived to maturity become anergic ( ), with reduced immunoglobulin M (IgM) on their surface because of internalization (fig. S3A) ( ). Although we observed that ABCs and FO B cells have comparable levels of surface IgM expression, ABCs exhibited substantial intracellular IgM internalization ( ). High expression of surface IgD is a hallmark of anergic B cells (fig. S3A) ( , ); however, compared with FO B cells, ABCs express less surface IgD ( ). While some ABCs appeared IgM-low and IgD-low, IgG class-switched cells were a minor population, and the majority retained IgM expression (fig. S4, A to C), supporting the notion that the reduced surface IgM levels result from down-regulation rather than from class switching. Notably, confocal microscopy analysis revealed that in FO B cells, internalized IgM was detectable in lysosomal associated membrane protein–1 (LAMP-1)–positive late endosomes only when the BCR was stimulated (fig. S3B) ( , ). In contrast, IgM was found in the endosomes of ABCs at a steady state ( ). Given that in anergic B cells, the normal endocytic transit of the ligated BCR into late endosomes is abrogated ( ), this result suggests that BCR internalization of ABCs is different from that of anergic B cells.
 

## Anergic B cells can preferentially differentiate into ABCs
To determine whether continuous antigen recognition and constitutive activation of BCR signaling are required for ABC generation, we used MD4 mice, in which almost all mature B cells express a transgenic HEL-specific BCR (IgMa allotype; IgMa+IgMb−) that limits their response to antigens other than HEL and its homologs ( , ). To exclude endogenous nontransgenic B cells (IgMb allotype), B cells with HEL-reactive BCR were evaluated as IgMa+IgMb− B cells. We found that ABCs were generated in aged wild-type (WT) mice but not in MD4 mice ( ). These findings indicate that a diverse BCR repertoire and the recognition of certain endogenous antigens that emerge with age are vital for the development of ABCs. To further explore the in vivo requirement of endogenous self-antigens for ABC formation, we analyzed the MD4 × ML5 model, in which B cells become anergic on chronic stimulation with the self-antigen ( ). We found that the MD4 × ML5 mice exhibited marked ABC generation with age ( ). Anergic B cells exhibit marked down-regulation of surface BCRs, making it difficult to accurately distinguish IgMa expression on the cell surface. Therefore, an alternative approach, intracellular staining, was used, which resulted in essentially the same results (fig. S5A). We also confirmed that ABCs are induced in MD4 × ML5 mice without loss of HEL reactivity ( ). These results indicate that ABCs expanded in aged MD4 × ML5 mice derived from HEL-binding B cells, not leaky HEL-nonbinder B cells.
 To gain insight into the mechanism of ABC differentiation from anergic B cells, we directly compared naive B cells (MD4 mice) and anergic B cells (MD4 × ML5 mice). A previous study has shown that when stimulated with TLR7 or TLR9 agonists, IL-21, and anti-IgM, B cells can differentiate into ABCs ( ). When MD4 B cells were stimulated with TLR7/9 agonists and IL-21 without BCR stimulation, they differentiated less readily into ABCs ( ). In contrast, MD4 × ML5 anergic B cells survived and differentiated into ABCs under the same conditions, which was further enhanced by HEL antigen stimulation ( and fig. S5B). TLR7/9 or IL-21 stimulation alone cannot induce ABCs from MD4 × ML5 anergic B cells ( ). As a previous study has shown that green fluorescent protein (GFP)hi FO B cells in Nur77 (Nr4a1)-GFP mice exhibit the characteristics of anergic B cells with the natural diverse BCR repertoire ( ), we next used Nr4a1-GFP mice to investigate whether naturally occurring anergic B cells differentiate into ABCs. We confirmed that GFPhi FO B cells in these mice expressed low levels of surface IgM and did not respond to BCR stimulation ( ). Notably, ABCs in aged Nr4a1-GFP mice had lower levels of GFP ), which separated them from anergic B cells. We then sorted GFPhi (anergy) and GFP− (non-anergy) FO B cells ( ) and stimulated them with TLR7 ligand, IL-21, and anti-IgM. Compared with GFP− cells, GFPhi FO B cells differentiated more efficiently into ABCs ( ). These results suggest that anergic B cells chronically preexposed to specific or polyclonal self-antigens can efficiently differentiate into ABCs.

## Nr4a1 inhibits ABC generation
The finding that Nr4a1 expression decreases with ABC differentiation led us to hypothesize that Nr4a1 may inhibit ABC generation. To test this hypothesis, we retrovirally transduced Nr4a1 into GFPhi anergic FO B cells from Nr4a1-GFP mice and stimulated them with R848, a B cell activating factor belonging to the tumor necrosis factor family (BAFF), IL-21, and anti-IgM (ABC inductive stimuli) to drive ABC differentiation ( ). The enforced expression of Nr4a1 notably reduced ABC generation compared with mock-transduced GFPhi anergic FO B cells ( ). Overexpression of Nr4a1 reduced survival but did not influence cell proliferation under this condition (fig. S6, A and B). To further investigate the role of Nr4a1 during ABC differentiation, we performed RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) with unstimulated naive B cells and B cells transduced with mock or Nr4a1 (fig. S6C). Principal components analysis of the RNA-seq data showed a marked separation of the transcription profiles (fig. S6D). Overall, there were 2514 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) between naive and mock-transduced B cells and 1846 DEGs between mock- and Nr4a1-transduced B cells [fold change (FC) >2 and false discovery rate (FDR) <0.05; ]. A comparison of the data between mock- and Nr4a1-expressing B cells revealed that 835 genes were repressed by Nr4a1 transduction ( ). Notably, a core cluster of genes that define ABCs or are critical for ABC generation, including Tbx21, Itgax, Apoe, Bhlhe40, Lgals1, Fcrl5, and Zeb2 ( , ), was inhibited in Nr4a1-overexpressing B cells ( ). Comparison with the RNA-seq datasets of ABCs in MRL/lpr mice (GSE260576) (fig. S6E) and our previous data of aged mice (GSE237540) ( ) showed that the genes differentially down-regulated by Nr4a1 overexpression overlapped with those that are up-regulated in ABCs ( ). Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) also revealed that Nr4a1-transduced B cells showed decreased expression of large numbers of established ABC up-regulated genes in aged and MRL/lpr mice ( ). CD4+ T cells ectopically transduced with Nr4a1 increased the expression of anergy-related genes ( ). Comparison of the genes up-regulated by Nr4a1-expressing B cells with those of anergic B cell–expressed genes defined by the Goodnow Laboratory (GSE82091) ( ) revealed that 36 anergy-related genes were also induced by Nr4a1 expression during ABC-induced stimulation (fig. S6F). These data may provide insight into the Nr4a1-targeted signaling pathways required for ABC generation. Gene ontology (GO) analysis revealed that compared with mock-transduced B cells, Nr4a1-transduced B cells expressed fewer gene products mediating BCR, TLR, JAK-STAT, and NF-κB signaling but more gene products mediating several metabolic pathways ( and fig. S6G). Moreover, GSEA showed a significant decrease in enrichments for BCR and TLR signaling pathways in Nr4a1-transduced B cells ( ). While these results do not clarify whether the changes in gene expression induced by Nr4a1 are due to direct transcriptional regulation or indirect effects through upstream signaling pathways, leaving the precise molecular mechanisms unclear, they suggest that Nr4a1 regulates multiple genes involved in ABC differentiation.
 

## Btk is essential for ABC formation and maintenance in aged mice
As Btk, a central component of the BCR downstream pathway, is highly phosphorylated in ABCs, assessed by flow cytometry and immunoblotting ( ), we assessed the role of Btk in ABC formation and maintenance. First, we examined mice lacking Btk and found that aged Btk-deficient mice did not have ABCs, indicating the essential role of Btk in ABC formation ( ). Next, we investigated whether Btk signaling is necessary for maintaining ABCs in aged mice by using the Btk inhibitor acalabrutinib. Acalabrutinib, a second-generation Btk inhibitor, is highly specific for Btk and has a minimal effect on other kinases ( , ). Treatment of aged mice with acalabrutinib via drinking water led to a marked reduction in the number of ABCs ( and fig. S7A). The number of FO B cells also decreased, but their proportion among B cells remained unchanged ( and fig. S7A), indicating that ABCs were more sensitive to Btk inhibition. When we examined the proliferation of ABCs shortly after administering acalabrutinib, before their complete disappearance, treatment with acalabrutinib markedly reduced the proportion of Ki67-positive ABCs, suggesting that Btk inhibition impairs ABC proliferation or affects the survival of actively proliferating ABCs ( ). These data suggest that chronic Btk signaling is a critical mechanism for the formation and maintenance of ABCs.
 

## ABCs in autoimmune mice exhibit similar characteristics to ABCs in aged mice
To examine whether the features of ABCs in aged mice could also be applied to autoimmune settings, we used a bm12-induced lupus mouse model ( , ). Adoptive transfer of lymphocytes from bm12 mice to WT C57BL/6 mice results in the expansion of T follicular helper cells, germinal center B cells, plasma cells, and ABCs, leading to autoantibody production and inflammation of organs, including the kidneys, liver, and lungs ( , – ). We observed that bm12-induced ABCs exhibited similar functional markers compared to those in naturally occurring ABCs during aging ( ). bm12-induced ABCs showed enhanced phosphorylation of Btk, PLCγ2, Syk, and Akt and BCR internalization in the unstimulated state ( ). bm12-induced ABCs also showed lower Nr4a1 expression than that of FO B cells ( ). Given this, we examined whether Nr4a1 suppresses ABC differentiation in this model. Enforced Nr4a1 expression limited ABC generation as well as plasma cell differentiation in vivo, as assessed by transducing Nr4a1 into B cells using a retroviral vector and transferring them into bm12-induced autoimmune recipient mice (fig. S8, A and B). As autoreactive anergic B cells differentiate into ABCs during aging, we investigated whether the same occurs in the bm12-induced lupus model. When bm12 lymphocytes were transferred to MD4 and MD4 × ML5 mice, we observed that ABCs were generated by MD4 × ML5 mice but not by MD4 mice ( , and fig. S7A). In addition, these ABCs are reactive to HEL ( ). Therefore, these results indicate that even in the setting of autoimmunity, autoreactive anergic B cells preferentially give rise to ABCs in a BCR-dependent manner, suggesting that similar factors may operate in aging and autoimmunity to determine the fate of ABC differentiation.
 

## Btk inhibition prevents ABC formation and ameliorates autoimmunity in lupus mice
In autoimmune diseases, ABCs are thought to be pathogenic B cells originating from autoreactive B cells ( , ). As constitutive activation of Btk was observed in the ABCs of lupus mice, we investigated whether a Btk inhibitor could remove ABCs and have a therapeutic effect on autoimmune diseases. On day 7 after the transfer of bm12 lymphocytes, when ABCs and autoantibodies began to be detected, the mice were given drinking water containing acalabrutinib or vehicle ( ). Treatment with acalabrutinib markedly reduced the number of ABCs in the blood and spleen of bm12-induced lupus mice ( , and fig. S9). Despite this reduction, the numbers of FO B cells and marginal zone B cells in the spleen remained unchanged ( and fig. S10A). Acalabrutinib administration was also associated with a reduction in T follicular helper, T helper 1, and IFN-γ+CD8+ T cells and plasma cells and a less pronounced expansion of dendritic cells following bm12 lymphocyte transfer (fig. S10, B and C), all of which may be attributed to the loss of ABCs. It should be noted that macrophage and neutrophil numbers were not significantly altered by Btk inhibition (fig. S10C). Therefore, blocking Btk in mice developing autoimmune disease could markedly reduce pathogenic B cells.
 Next, we assessed the effects of acalabrutinib on the autoimmune pathogenesis. Acalabrutinib improves splenomegaly in bm12-induced mice ( ). ABCs detected in the kidneys and liver, the target organs of SLE, decreased after acalabrutinib treatment ( and fig. S11, A and B). Autoantibodies were also reduced after the disappearance of ABCs from peripheral blood ( ). Consequently, mice with bm12-induced lupus treated with acalabrutinib showed a marked decrease in IgG deposits in the kidneys and aggregation of lymphocytes around small vessels in the liver ( ). These results indicate that the formation or maintenance of ABCs in autoimmune diseases is dependent on Btk signaling and that its inhibition ameliorates disease progression. This does not exclude the possibility that the observed effects may also result from the impact of Btk inhibition on other cell types in vivo.

## Btk is crucial for the generation and maintenance of human ABCs
To extend the findings in mouse ABCs to humans, we investigated whether Btk is activated in the ABCs of humans with lupus. As previously described ( ), the number of ABCs with CD21loCD11c+T-bet+ is increased in the peripheral blood of patients with SLE ( ). This ABC population was nearly absent in the naive B cell fraction but was found in the CD27−IgD− double-negative, switched (CD27+IgD−), and unswitched memory (CD27+IgD+) fractions, with the highest concentration in the double-negative fraction (fig. S12, A and B). Compared with CD21+CD11c− B cell populations in the steady state, ABCs showed higher phosphorylation of Btk ( and fig. S12C). In addition, we cultured B cells from the peripheral blood of healthy donors with IL-21, R848, anti-Ig, and BAFF. This resulted in the generation of ABCs ( ) ( , ). These ABCs also demonstrated strong phosphorylation of Btk ( ). Next, we examined the role of Btk in the generation and maintenance of human ABCs. We observed that treatment of acalabrutinib from the beginning of the culture inhibited ABC differentiation ( ), as indicated by the reduction of ABC markers, although functional properties were not assessed. Furthermore, acalabrutinib reduced the number of ABCs even when administered starting on day 3 of culture, a time point at which ABC differentiation was already complete. This suggests that acalabrutinib affects the maintenance of ABCs rather than their initial differentiation ( ). These findings suggest that Btk is highly activated under lupus conditions and necessary for human ABC differentiation and maintenance. Therefore, Btk inhibitors may have therapeutic potential for treating patients with SLE.
 

## DISCUSSION
Understanding the process of generation and maintenance of autoreactive ABCs is crucial for elucidating the mechanisms underlying autoimmune diseases, developing diagnostic tools, and identifying therapeutic targets. In this study, we demonstrated that chronic exposure to endogenous antigens arising from aging and autoimmunity, followed by constitutive activation of BCR signaling, shapes the formation and maintenance of ABCs. We further demonstrated that ABCs can originate from autoreactive anergic B cells in vivo and in vitro. Mechanistically, Nr4a1 expression, a hallmark of B cell anergy, may suppress the ABC generation mainly via inhibition of the BCR signaling pathways. Several lines of evidence suggest that Btk may be essential for the development of ABCs in mice and humans.
ABCs have been shown to have unique properties that distinguish them from conventional FO B cells, not only in surface markers but also in their biological phenotype; ABCs do not proliferate upon stimulation with CD40 and are hyporesponsive to BCR stimulation but respond efficiently to TLR7 and TLR9 stimulation and secrete antibodies in response to TLRs ( ). In addition, this study provided new insights, specifically that ABCs have high intracellular Ca2+ concentrations, are constitutively activated in BCR signaling, and internalize the BCR, suggesting that ABCs chronically recognize endogenous antigens. Given that autoreactive B cells can become anergic because of continuous exposure to self-antigens, the question arises as to whether ABCs are anergic. ABCs share similarities with anergic B cells in that they have high levels of cytosolic Ca2+ even when at rest, leading to sustained NFAT activation, and a poor response to BCR stimulation ( ). In patients with common variable immunodeficiency and rheumatoid arthritis, ABC-like cells show an anergic phenotype ( , ). However, ABCs have very low Nr4a1 expression, a hallmark of B cell anergy, and retain BCR reactivity, albeit weakly. This may be because anergic B cells down-regulate surface IgM, whereas ABCs retain it to the same degree as FO B cells despite internalizing surface IgM. Decreased surface IgD expression on ABCs might also be involved in retaining BCR reactivity: Anergic B cells enable tolerance through increased surface expression of IgD ( , , ). Furthermore, the enhanced BCR signaling observed in ABCs (involving Syk, Btk, and Akt, and NF-κB signaling) is distinct from that observed in anergic B cells ( ). In addition, ABCs are strongly induced to differentiate into plasma cells by TLR7 or TLR9 agonist stimulation and have high antigen-presenting capacities to T cells, which is not seen in anergic B cells ( ). In this study, we demonstrated that ABCs exhibit low responsiveness to soluble antigens. However, previous studies on malaria-induced ABCs have shown that these cells are unresponsive to soluble antigens but respond robustly to membrane-associated antigens ( ). This raises the possibility that ABCs in aged and autoimmune mice might similarly respond to membrane-associated antigens through a comparable mechanism. This finding highlights the need for further studies to clarify the mechanisms governing ABC responsiveness to different antigen types in aging and autoimmunity. The model in which ABCs recognize self-antigen and continuously activate BCR signaling is supported by previous findings that ABCs have mutated BCR ( ) or are antigen-experienced cells with characteristics similar to those of memory B cells ( , , ). Given the phenotypic heterogeneity of ABCs with respect to proliferative potential and markers such as memory B cells, plasma cells, and anergic B cells ( , ), a closer examination of the ABC status would provide a better understanding of this diversity.
The expression of Nr4a1 suppresses the differentiation of ABCs; however, this study does not explain why the GFP reporter of BCR signaling is low in Nr4a1-GFP mice. Notably, GC B cells express much lower levels of Nr4a1-GFP despite the presumed antigen signaling in the light zone ( ). The precise mechanism behind this remains unclear, but one possibility is that ABCs, like GC B cells, experience dilution of the reporter GFP because of rapid proliferation, given their high turnover rates. Supporting this, some ABCs are Ki67 positive ( ), indicating elevated proliferation compared to FO B cells. Another potential explanation for the reduced Nr4a1-GFP expression could involve variations in upstream signaling pathways. For instance, novel factors may be acting to suppress Nr4a1 expression in ABCs, or crucial factors required to promote its expression may be absent. Future studies are required to elucidate the mechanisms underlying the low Nr4a1-GFP levels in ABCs in relation to their BCR stimulation.
ABCs have been shown to be generated in vitro from naive FO B cells and possibly other naive B cell subsets ( ). Their differentiation requires a combination of TLR7/9 in the presence of a certain cytokine such as IFN-γ or IL-21 ( ). Although BCR stimulation is an additional signal that stimulates the generation of ABCs from naive B cells ( ), we found that anergic B cells preferentially differentiate into ABCs without in vitro BCR stimulation. These results highlight that ABCs arise from anergic B cells that are prestimulated with self-antigen in vivo. Moreover, our study indicates that in aged mice and the lupus model, in vivo ABC generation is abrogated by replacing the polyclonal BCR repertoire with a monoclonal, non–self-reactive BCR and is further induced through self-antigen–mediated BCR stimulation from anergic B cells. These findings suggest that ABCs emerge from anergic B cells by disengaging anergy. Although the precise mechanisms driving the transition from anergic B cells to ABCs remain unclear, our finding that Nr4a1 suppresses ABC differentiation suggests that down-regulating Nr4a1 expression could be a potential contributing factor. In addition, beyond BCR signaling, TLR and IL-21 pathways may play a role in breaking anergy and inducing the transcription factors necessary for ABC differentiation. The differences in gene expression profiles between anergic B cells and ABCs may provide valuable insights into uncovering the underlying mechanisms of anergy disengagement and ABC formation. The concept that anergic B cells can be reawakened and differentiated into pathogenic ABCs could explain the mechanisms underlying the breakdown of self-tolerance.
Aging, driven by enhanced cellular stress, damage, and senescence, can be associated with an increased release of damage-associated molecular patterns, including endogenous nucleic acids ( , ). This may explain why ABCs expand in distinct environments: aging and autoimmunity. However, it is not clear in this study what antigens activate the BCRs during these processes. Even in germ-free environments, ABCs can still develop normally, suggesting that they are not prompted by commensal bacteria ( ). In addition, it is likely that ABCs are not always self-reactive B cells, as they can be induced by infections ( , ). Some research suggests that the development of ABCs during infection could be due to prolonged exposure to certain substances. Given our results showing the importance of chronic BCR stimulation of ABCs in aging and autoimmune diseases, it is possible that repeated exposure to antigens, whether self or foreign, may lead to the development of ABCs.
Now, regarding the transcription machinery, T-bet, IRF5, and ZEB2 are highly expressed in ABCs and have been proposed as functional drivers of ABC differentiation ( , ). This study revealed that Nr4a1 plays a negative regulatory role in ABC generation. The transcriptional analysis unveiled a set of genes that are down-regulated by Nr4a1 expression during ABC differentiation, including Tbx21, Itgax, Zeb2, Apoe, and Fcrl5. Moreover, our data suggest that Nr4a1 limits several signaling pathways, such as BCR, TLR, JAK-STAT, and NF-κB, which are crucial for ABC generation ( ) mediated by antigen, T cell cytokines, and TLR agonists. Considering that Nr4a1 inhibits activator protein–1 function in T cells, tumors, and endothelial cells ( , , ) and activator protein–1 regulates the expression of several genes including Zeb2 ( ), it seems likely that Nr4a1 serves as a negative regulator for ABC-driving genes in anergic B cells. As the levels of Nr4a1 expression are linked to anergy status ( , ), attenuated Nr4a1 expression may lead to the reacquisition of B cell responsiveness and breaking of the tolerance checkpoint, thereby promoting ABC differentiation. Further study using NR4a1-deficient B cells will be important to clarify the exact role of Nr4a1 in the differentiation and function of ABCs.
Our findings demonstrate that bm12-induced ABCs share numerous similarities with ABCs observed in aged mice, suggesting commonalities in their induction mechanisms and functions. However, it is crucial to recognize that ABCs are not a uniform population; instead, they display considerable diversity, with varying population ratios depending on the specific conditions. For example, aged ABCs tend to express higher levels of markers such as CD80, CD73, and PDL2, which are also associated with memory B cells. This distinction may reflect differences in the inflammatory environment, with autoimmunity typically characterized by acute inflammation and aging associated with chronic or lower-grade inflammation. These variations could influence the state, diversity, or specific functions of ABCs under different contexts.
Although the present results indicate that ABCs are likely to differentiate from autoreactive B cells in autoimmune and aged mice, future studies should examine the differentiation process of human autoreactive ABCs. In patients with type 1 diabetes, the existence of CD11c-positive activated autoreactive B cells (ND2) in the BND cell population, which are assumed to be human anergy B cells, has been reported ( ). This may be linked to human anergy B cells and CD11c+ ABC-like cells. Mouse ABCs are predominantly B cells with IgM-type BCRs, while human ABCs include not only IgM but also class-switched B cells ( ). Because human BND cells are IgM positive, determining when and where they become class-switched type ABCs would be an important issue.
Developing therapies that selectively target pathogenic autoreactive B cells could minimize immune-related damage while preserving overall immune function. Although ABCs are an attractive therapeutic target, the lack of specific markers has hindered selective intervention. Our findings demonstrate that Btk is highly phosphorylated in ABCs in aged/autoimmune mice and patients with lupus and that Btk inhibition effectively reduces ABC accumulation and autoimmune pathology and obstructs human ABC generation. While Btk inhibition primarily targets ABCs, our results suggest broader immune effects. Btk is expressed not only in B cells but also in myeloid cells, including macrophages, neutrophils, and dendritic cells. Although macrophage and neutrophil numbers remained unchanged, dendritic cell expansion was less pronounced under Btk inhibition, likely due to reduced inflammation. The observed decrease in activated T cells, which do not express Btk, further supports this indirect effect. Moreover, it is important to note that our current study does not fully exclude the potential contribution of non-ABC B cells to the observed phenotypes. Because Btk inhibitors suppress activation across multiple B cell subsets, it remains possible that the improvement in autoimmune pathology is partially mediated by effects on non-ABC B cells. Future studies using ABC-specific targeting strategies will be required to fully clarify the relative contributions of ABCs and other B cell subsets. These findings highlight the complexity of immune modulation by Btk inhibition and the challenge of distinguishing cell-intrinsic effects from cell-extrinsic effects. In addition, long-term Btk inhibitor use has been associated with increased risks of infection, bleeding, and cardiovascular complications in patients with chronic lymphocytic leukemia, necessitating caution in prolonged administration ( , ). Further studies are required to fully elucidate the mechanisms of Btk inhibition in immune regulation and its therapeutic potential. Nonetheless, our findings provide a better understanding of the nature of ABCs and provide a basis for exploring previously unrecognized therapeutic strategies.

Aged mice were bred until the age of 46 to 65 weeks. Mice were bred and maintained under specific pathogen–free conditions. All studies and procedures were approved by the Animal Experiment Committee of Kyushu University. Our animal study exclusively examined female mice because the lupus phenomenon and ABC accumulation are more likely to occur in females. All studies and procedures were approved by the Animal Experiment Committee of Kyushu University (approval nos. A23-090, A23-091, A23-103, A23-122, A23-029, A23-035, A23-149, and A23-171).

## B cell purification
B cell purification was performed using magnetic-activated cell sorting separation according to the manufacturer’s instructions. To obtain naive B cells for in vitro culture, CD43− cells (>95% positive for CD19 staining) were enriched using anti-CD43 magnetic beads and an LS column (Miltenyi). For purification of ABCs, CD19+ B cells (>95% positive for CD19 staining) were first enriched by positive selection using anti-CD19 magnetic beads, because B cell purification using CD43 beads with negative selection results in the loss of a significant proportion of ABCs. The enriched B cells were then stained with surface antibody cocktails and further sorted using FACSMelody (BD Biosciences) by gating ABCs (CD19+B220hiAA4.1−CD21loCD23−CD11c+). For experiments analyzing intracellular signaling in unstimulated states, the cells were fixed immediately after isolation to preserve their basal signaling states and minimize any alterations caused by the sorting or isolation procedures.

## In vitro culture assay
For in vitro culture of B cells from aged mice, sorted B cells were washed and resuspended in RPMI complete medium (RPMI containing 10% FBS, Hepes, l-glutamine, penicillin/streptomycin, and sodium pyruvate). A total of 5 × 104 B cells was cultured per well in 96-well round plates containing media alone or media containing R848 (0.5 μg/ml; InvivoGen) or CpG (5.0 μg/ml; InvivoGen) at 37°C with 5% CO2 for 3 days. For coculture of B cells and T cells, splenic OT-II transgenic naive CD4+ T cells (TCRβ+CD4+CD44loCD62LhiCD25−) were sorted using FACSMelody. B cells were cocultured with 5 × 104 Cell Trace Violet–labeled CD4+ T cells at a 1:1 ratio in the presence of OVA323–339 peptide (0.4 μg/ml; MBL) for 3 days. For in vitro induction of ABCs from WT mice and Btk-deficient mice, splenic B cells isolated by negative selection with anti-CD43 magnetic beads (>95% positive for CD19 staining) were cultured in the presence of R848 (0.5 μg/ml), mIL-21 (50 ng/ml; R&D), and anti-IgM F(ab′)2 (1.0 μg/ml; Jackson ImmunoResearch Laboratories) for 3 days. For in vitro induction of ABCs from MD4 and MD4 × ML5 mice, splenic B cells isolated by positive selection with anti-CD19 magnetic beads (>95% positive for B220 staining) were cultured in the presence of the appropriate combination of the following: R848 (0.5 μg/ml), CpG (5 μg/ml), mIL-21 (30 ng/ml; R&D), and HEL protein (5 μg/ml) for 3 days. For in vitro induction of ABCs from Nra4a1-GFP mice, FACS-sorted GFPhi and GFP−FO B cells were cultured in the presence of R848 (0.5 μg/ml), mIL-21 (30 ng/ml), and anti-IgM F(ab′)2 (1 μg/ml; Jackson ImmunoResearch Laboratories) for 3 days. For in vitro induction of human ABCs, human peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were isolated by density gradient centrifugation using Lympholyte (Cedarlane) or Lymphoprep (Axis-Shield). B cells were purified by using positive selection with anti-CD19 magnetic beads (>95% positive for CD20 staining) and were cultured in the presence of hBAFF (20 ng/ml; R&D Systems), R848 (1 μg/ml; Invitrogen), hIL-21 (50 ng/ml; BioLegend), and goat anti-human IgA + IgG + IgM (H + L) (10 μg/ml; Jackson ImmunoResearch Laboratories) supplemented with 1 μM acalabrutinib (MedChemExpress) or dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) for 3 days.

## Nuclei isolation/nuclear staining
The nuclei isolation protocol was based on previously published reports ( , ). For nuclei isolation, purified splenic B cells were collected from mice and prewarmed in a 96-well plate for 10 min at 37°C with 2.0 × 105 to 1.0 × 106 cells per 100 μl per well in complete medium (RPMI with 10% FBS, penicillin, streptomycin, glutamine, Hepes, β-mercaptoethanol, sodium pyruvate, and nonessential amino acids). Cells were stimulated with 100 μl of 2× stimuli for 30 min at 37°C. In some experiments involving the Btk inhibitor acalabrutinib (HY-17600, MedChemExpress), 10 μM acalabrutinib dissolved in DMSO (Wako) was pretreated for 30 min at 37°C before stimulation. Stimulated cells were then spun at 300g and 4°C for 5 min, and the pellets were immediately resuspended in 250 μl of ice-cold Buffer A [320 mM sucrose, 10 mM Hepes (Life Technologies), 8 mM MgCl2, EDTA-free complete protease inhibitor (Nacalai Tesque), and 0.1% (v/v) Triton X-100]. After 15 min on ice, the plate was spun at 2000g and 4°C for 5 min. This was followed by two washes with 250 μl of Buffer B (Buffer A without Triton X-100) and centrifugation at 2000g and 4°C for 5 min. After the final wash, pellets were resuspended with 200 μl of Buffer B containing 4% paraformaldehyde (electron microscopy grade; Thermo Fisher Scientific), and nuclei were incubated on ice for 30 min for fixation. The nuclei were spun at 2000g and 4°C for 5 min, followed by two washes and resuspension in FACS staining buffer [PBS (Nacalai Tesque) containing 2% (v/v) FBS, 0.5 mM EDTA, penicillin, streptomycin, and glutamine] and centrifugation at 1000g and 4°C for 5 min to sufficiently pellet nuclei.
For nuclei staining, isolated nuclei were washed with 200 μl of Perm buffer (FACS buffer with 0.3% Triton X-100) and spun at 1000g and 4°C for 5 min. Isolated nuclei were stained with 50 μl of rabbit anti-mouse NF-κB p65 (Cell Signaling, 8242S), rabbit anti-mouse NFAT1 (Cell Signaling, 5861), or isotype control (Cell Signaling, 3900) diluted 1:200 in Perm buffer for 30 min on ice. The 96-well plate was spun at 1000g and 4°C for 5 min and washed with 200 μl of FACS buffer. The nuclei were then stained with 50 μl of anti-rabbit IgG-PE (Cell Signaling, 79408S) diluted 1:400 in Perm buffer for 30 min on ice in the dark. The nuclei were spun at 1000g and 4°C for 5 min and washed with 200 μl of FACS buffer. Nuclei were resuspended in 100 μl of FACS buffer and analyzed immediately on Cytoflex (Beckman Coulter).

## Assessment of IgM/IgD internalization by flow cytometry
For the assessment of IgM/IgD internalization by flow cytometry, splenocytes were stained with surface antibody cocktails (anti-CD11c, anti-B220, anti-AA4.1, anti-CD19, anti-CD23, and anti-CD21) and then fixed with Intracellular Fixation Buffer for 20 min at 4°C. For the detection of surface IgM/IgD, cells were washed with PBS and stained with anti-IgM and anti-IgD in FACS staining buffer at 4°C for 20 min. For the detection of surface and intracellular IgM/IgD simultaneously, cells were washed with permeabilization buffer and stained with anti-IgM and anti-IgD diluted in permeabilization buffer at the same concentration of that used in the surface staining at 4°C for 20 min.

## Calcium measurement
For cytosolic calcium measurement, splenocytes were loaded with Cal520 acetoxymethyl ester (AAT Bioqwest), FuraRed-AM (Invitrogen), and Pluronic F127 (Invitrogen) in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium containing 2% FBS at 37°C for 45 min. Cells were washed twice with ringer solution (155 mM NaCl, 4.5 mM KCl, 1 mM MgCl2, 2 mM CaCl2, 5 mM Hepes, 5.6 mM d-glucose, and 0.025% bovine serum albumin) and stained with antibodies (anti-B220, anti-CD21, anti-CD23, and anti-CD11c). Steady-state intracellular calcium was measured for 60 s, and then cells were stimulated with anti-IgM F(ab′)2 (10 μg/ml) for 5 min. Changes in fluorescence intensity were monitored on FACS Fortessa (Becton Dickinson), and Ca2+ mobilization was measured by calculating changes in the fluorescence ratio of Cal520 to FuraRed.

## Immunofluorescence analysis of nuclear translocation of p65 and IgM internalization
For the assessment of p65 nuclear translocation, FACS-sorted 1 × 105 FO B cells or ABCs were immediately adhered to cover glasses coated with Cell-Tak (Discovery Labware Inc.). Cells were fixed and permeabilized with ice-cold methanol, blocked with Blocking One Hist (Nacalai Tesque), and stained with rabbit anti-p65 (Cell Signaling) at 4°C overnight. Cells were then stained with Alexa 647–conjugated anti-rabbit IgG (H + L) and 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI; BioLegend) at room temperature for 1 hour, protected from light. For the detection of IgM internalization, cells were immediately adhered to cover glasses or prestained with biotin-conjugated anti-IgM F(ab′)2 on ice for 30 min and then incubated at 37°C for 30 min before stacking to cover glasses. Cells were fixed and permeabilized with ice-cold methanol, blocked with Blocking One Hist, and stained with biotin-conjugated goat anti-IgM F(ab′)2 (Southern Biotech) and Alexa 488–labeled anti-LAMP-1 (Santa Cruz) at 4°C overnight. Cells were stained with Alexa 594–labeled streptavidin and DAPI at room temperature for 1 hour. Stained cells were washed in PBS and mounted in ProLong Diamond Antifade (Invitrogen) at room temperature for 24 hours. Images were obtained with an LSM 700 or LSM 900 confocal microscope (Zeiss). The number of cells with p65 nuclear translocation and IgM internalization was evaluated by collecting at least 100 cells from randomly taken photographs in each experiment. To quantify the extent of colocalization of IgM and LAMP-1, we used the JACoP plug-in (ImageJ). Threshold values of 100 and 50 were applied to IgM and LAMP-1, respectively, to account for differences in signal intensity and background noise between the markers. M1 (the fraction of IgM signal overlapping with LAMP-1) was used to assess colocalization. Cells with M1 ≥ 25% were considered positive, as previously reported ( ). For each experiment, at least 50 randomly selected cells were analyzed.

## Immunoblotting and antibodies
Purified splenic B cells were collected from mice, prewarmed for 10 min at 37°C, and stimulated in the presence or absence of anti-IgM F(ab′)2 (10 μg/ml; Jackson ImmunoResearch Laboratories) in RPMI medium for 3 min at 37°C. Following stimulation, cells were lysed with 1% NP-40 lysis buffer with protease and phosphatase inhibitors for 20 min at 4°C and centrifuged for 15 min at 15,000 rpm at 4°C to remove cellular debris. The supernatants were denatured at 95°C for 5 min in SDS sample buffer with 0.5 M dithiothreitol. Lysates were run on gradient (5 to 20%) gels (Wako) and transferred to 0.45-μm polyvinylidene difluoride membranes (Merck Millipore) by electrophoresis. Membranes were blotted with a primary antibody and horseradish peroxidase– or alkaline phosphatase–conjugated second antibody. For protein visualization, Chemi-Lumi One (Nacalai Tesque), ECL Prime Western Blotting Detection Reagents (Cytiba), and PhosphaGLO Reserve AP Substrate (SeraCare) were used as substrates and protein levels were detected using a FUSION SOLO S (VILBER). The following antibodies were used: Anti-phospho-tyrosine (clone 4G10, 96215T), pErk T202/Y204 monoclonal antibody (clone 197G2, 4377S), ERK1/2 monoclonal antibody (clone 281-2, 9102S), pBTK Y223 monoclonal antibody (clone D9T6H, 87141S), BTK monoclonal antibody (clone D3H5, 8547T), pAKT S473 antibody (9271S), AKT polyclonal antibody (9272S), and NFAT1 monoclonal antibody (clone D43B1, 5861S) were from Cell Signaling Technology. Anti-GADH antibody (6C5, MAB374) was from Sigma-Aldrich. Anti-mouse and anti-rabbit horseradish peroxidase– or alkaline phosphatase–conjugated secondary antibodies were from Invitrogen.

## Detection of HEL-binding B cells
For the assessment of HEL-binding B cells ( ), splenocytes were washed with PBS and incubated with HEL (100 μg/ml) at 4°C for 1 hour. Cells were then incubated with biotin-anti HEL monoclonal antibody (rabbit monoclonal, clone D1.3) at 4°C for 1 hour. Cells were washed with PBS and stained with streptavidin-BV650 and surface markers (anti-IgMb-FITC, anti-CD11c-APC, anti-B220-APC-Cy7, anti-AA4,1-BV421, anti-CD19-BV605, anti-IgMa-PE, and anti-CD21-PE-Cy7) at 4°C for 20 min in FACS staining buffer. Cells were washed with FACS staining buffer and analyzed on Cytoflex. The percentage of HEL binding was calculated on the basis of the negative control of WT B cells.

## Retroviral transduction and in vitro stimulation
To generate an Nr4a1 retroviral expression vector, a cDNA corresponding to Nr4a1 obtained from mouse splenocytes by polymerase chain reaction amplification was cloned into the pMYs-IRES-mCherry retroviral vector. The resulting retroviral vector (pMYs-Nr4a1-IRES-mCherry) and a mCherry-alone control “mock” vector (pMYs-IRES-mCherry) were transfected into PLAT-E cells with FuGENE HD (Roche Diagnostics). Supernatants were collected 24 hours after the medium was changed. Sorted GFPhi B cells were prestimulated in the presence of R848 (0.5 μg/ml) for 24 hours. B cells underwent “spin infection” for 2 hours at 25°C (800g) after the virus supernatant and polybrene (6 μg/ml) were added, and plates were additionally incubated at 37°C for 2 hours. After washing the cells with RPMI complete medium, B cells were cultured in the presence of R848 (0.5 μg/ml) for 24 hours. B cells were subsequently cultured for an additional 48 hours in the presence of R848 (0.5 μg/ml), mIL-21 (50 ng/ml), anti-IgM F(ab′)2 (1 μg/ml), and BAFF (50 ng/ml).

## Btk inhibition
Acalabrutinib (HY-17600, MedChemExpress) was dissolved in 2% hydroxypropyl-β-cyclodextrin. Mice were administered acalabrutinib (0.15 mg/ml) dissolved in drinking water for 3 weeks, as previously reported ( ). For the in vitro assay, acalabrutinib was dissolved in DMSO (Wako). Cells were treated with 10 μM acalabrutinib.

## bm12-inducible model of SLE
The bm12 transfer model, as described initially by Morris et al. ( ) and modified by Klarquist and Janssen ( ), was adopted. CD4+ T cells from mice expressing the MHC-II mutant allele I-Abm12 (bm12 mice), which differs from I-Ab by three amino acids, recognize and activate any B cell expressing I-Ab, as evidenced by the production of anti-DNA autoantibodies and lupus phenotypes in host mice adoptively transferred with bm12 lymphocytes ( , ). Single-cell suspensions of spleens and lymph nodes (superficial cervical, axillary, mesenteric, and inguinal) obtained from bm12 donor mice were prepared and resuspended in RPMI 1640 medium containing 10% FBS at room temperature without red blood cell lysis treatment. Lymphocytes were resuspended in PBS and injected intravenously. Donor and recipient mice were age and sex matched. For analyzing MD4 and MD4 × ML5 mice, 3 × 107 lymphocytes from bm12 or WT mice were intravenously transferred to MD4 or MD4 × ML5 mice and the mice were euthanized for analysis at day 14. For acalabrutinib treatment, mice were allowed to either vehicle or acalabrutinib in the drinking water ad libitum 1 week after bm12 lymphocyte transfer (3 × 107 cells) and exposed to solutions until the end of the experiment for a total of 3 weeks. Serum and PBMCs were obtained before and every week after the transfer. Mice were euthanized for analysis 4 weeks after cell transfer, and spleens, liver, and kidney were harvested and weighed. A single-cell suspension of each organ was prepared for flow cytometry analysis. For preparing a single-cell suspension of kidneys, isolated kidneys were minced finely and treated with collagenase D (0.5 mg/ml; Roche) and deoxyribonuclease I (25 U/ml; Worthington) in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium containing 10% FBS for 40 min at 37°C, with gentle stirring. Digested kidneys were manually ground using an EASY strainer (Greiner Bio-One). Mononuclear cells were isolated using a Percoll (Cytiva) density gradient centrifuge at 850g for 20 min. For preparing a single-cell suspension of livers, harvested livers were manually ground using an EASY strainer, and mononuclear cells were isolated using Percoll. Single-cell suspensions of both kidneys and livers were obtained after red blood cell lysis using ammonium chloride/potassium chloride solution and used for flow cytometry analysis.

## In vivo transfer of Nr4a1-transduced B cells in lupus-prone mice
We generated CD19-iCre ( ) × Rosa26-loxP-stop-loxP-DTR (diphtheria toxin receptor) mice ( ), which enable temporary depletion of recipient B cells only upon administration of diphtheria toxin. To deplete endogenous B cells in recipient mice, CD19-iCre × Rosa26-loxP-stop-loxP-DTR mice were treated with diphtheria toxin intraperitoneally for two consecutive days. Naive B cells, isolated by negative selection with anti-CD43 magnetic beads, were prestimulated with R848 (0.5 μg/ml) at 37°C for 24 hours and subjected to spin infection for 2 hours at 25°C (800g). Following spin infection, plates were incubated at 37°C for an additional 2 hours. B cells transduced with either mock (GFP) or Nr4a1 (mCherry) were collected, mixed equally, and transferred into recipient mice (4.0 × 107 B cells per mouse). The next day, 7.5 × 106 CD4+ T cells, purified from bm12 mice by negative selection using the MojoSort Mouse CD4 T Cell Isolation Kit (BioLegend, 480005), were transferred into the recipient mice. On days 13 and 14 after B cell transfer, diphtheria toxin was administered again, and the mice were analyzed on day 15.

## Histological analysis
For immunofluorescence studies of mouse kidneys, harvested kidneys were cut along the short axis at the maximum area of the whole kidney, fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde at 4°C for 2 hours, and incubated in 20% sucrose in PBS at 4°C overnight. Optimal cutting temperature compound–embedded (Sakura) kidneys were cryosectioned into 10-μm sections and mounted on slide glasses. The slides were dried and washed with PBS, followed by blocking with 1% goat serum and 3% bovine serum albumin in PBS at room temperature for 1 hour. The sections were stained with Alexa 488–labeled anti-IgG (polyclonal, BioLegend) at 4°C overnight. For hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining of the liver, mouse livers were prepared, and the right lobe was fixed in 10% formaldehyde, embedded in paraffin, sectioned (4.0-μm thickness), and then stained with H&E. Images of kidneys and livers were obtained with a Keyence BZ-X700 microscope (Keyence Corporation).

## Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA)
Plates were coated with double-stranded DNA (dsDNA; 10 μg/ml; Invitrogen), histone (10 μg/ml; Roche), or Smith/ribonucleoprotein (Sm/RNP) (0.1 μg/ml; GenWay) in PBS at 4°C overnight. The serum was diluted and incubated on coated plates at 4°C overnight. Plates were then incubated for 3 hours with horseradish peroxidase–labeled goat anti-mouse IgG (Southern Biotech). Optical density at 450 nm (OD450) was measured on a microplate reader (iMArk Microplate Reader, Bio-Rad).

## Human participants
The study recruited 4 healthy donors aged 31 to 36 years with no medication and 11 female patients with SLE attending Kyushu University Hospital aged 22 to 31 years with mild-to-moderate disease activity. Informed consent was obtained from all patients in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki. The Institutional Review Board of Kyushu University Hospital approved all research on human subjects (no. 2023-51).

## RNA sequencing
For RNA-seq, CD43− B cells (naive), GFP+ Nr4a1-transduced B cells (Nr4a1), and GFP+ mock-transduced B cells (mock) were sorted (n = 3). For RNA-seq of ABCs and FO B cells of MRL/lpr mice, FO B cells (AA4.1−CD19+B220hiCD21intD23+) and ABCs (AA4.1−CD19+B220hiCD21loCD23−CD11c+) were sorted from female mice (14 weeks, n = 3). Total RNA was isolated from cells using the miRNeasy Micro Kit (Qiagen) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Full-length cDNAs were prepared using the SMART-Seq HT Kit (Takara Bio, Japan) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. For global gene expression analysis, an Illumina library was prepared using a NexteraXT DNA Library Preparation Kit (Illumina, San Diego, CA) according to SMARTer kit instructions. Sequencing was performed on a NovaSeq 6000 sequencer (Illumina) in the 101-base single-end mode. Sequenced reads were mapped to the mouse reference genome sequences (mm10) using TopHat version 2.1.1 in combination with Bowtie2 version 2.2.8 and SAMtools version 0.1.18. The fragments per kilobase of exon per million mapped fragments were calculated using Cuffdiff 2.2.1. Differential expressions of genes were conducted by the DESeq2 (version 1.24.0) package, and simple plots were produced in R (version 3.3.3) ( ). GO enrichment analysis was undertaken using ShinyGO 0.82 ( ). GSEA was performed using the online software GSEA 4.3.3 (Broad Institute) ( , ). The “ABC up-regulated gene set” was created by extracting ABCs’ differentially up-regulated genes compared with FO B cells in aged mice (GSE237540) ( ) and MRL/lpr mice (FDR < 0.05, log2FC > 3.0, and P < 0.05). P values of less than 0.05 were considered statistically significant in GSEA.

## Statistical analysis
We performed statistical evaluation using Prism software (GraphPad, La Jolla, CA). A two-tailed, unpaired, or paired Student’s t test was applied for the statistical comparison of two groups. Comparisons of two nonparametric datasets were made by the Mann-Whitney U test. Analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukey’s multiple-comparisons test was applied for statistical comparison between multiple groups. P values of less than 0.05 were considered statistically significant.
