## Abstract

## Background
Breast cancer (BC) is a biologically heterogeneous disease, and no single imaging modality captures the full spectrum of phenotypes across all stages of the disease. This review summarizes advances in receptor-targeted nuclear imaging approaches that support patient stratification, treatment selection and response monitoring.

## Main body
We provide a comprehensive review of preclinical and clinical studies of PET and SPECT radiopharmaceuticals targeting BC-relevant biomarkers on tumor cells and within the tumor microenvironment, with emphasis on clinical use cases, practical limitations and theranostic translational readiness. Conventional imaging modalities and [18F]FDG PET/CT remain central to staging but can be limited by poor specificity and reduced sensitivity for small lesions. Although anatomical (RECIST) and metabolic (PERCIST) response criteria remain central in routine response assessment, their application in BC can be challenging, particularly in bone-predominant disease and in the presence of marked inter-lesional heterogeneity. Receptor-mediated nuclear imaging enables non-invasive, whole-body phenotyping beyond biopsy and maps spatial heterogeneity. Clinical progress has been achieved for steroid receptors (ER/PR/AR), HER2, GRPR and SSTR2 imaging, and extends to stromal targets such as FAP (FAPI tracers). Emerging targets, including CXCR4, NTSR1, NPY1R and TROP-2, further broaden the theranostic landscape, particularly in settings where biomarker profiles are heterogeneous or evolve over time.

## Conclusion
Multi-target imaging strategies may better address intra- and inter-lesional heterogeneity. Larger prospective cohorts are needed to define diagnostic performance, clinical relevance and theranostic value in BC.

Keywords: Breast cancer, Molecular imaging, Radiopharmaceuticals, PET/ SPECT, Theranostics

## Background
Breast cancer (BC) has outranked lung cancer and become the most prevalent cancer in women (2.3 million new cases ~ 11.7% of all cancer cases), accounting for about 30% of female cancers worldwide in 2020. BC is responsible for more than 685,000 deaths (Sung et al. ) and its worldwide incidence is predicted to reach 3.2 million new cases per year by 2050, reflecting the urgent need for new diagnostic tools and treatments (Momenimovahed and Salehiniya ).
BC is highly heterogeneous and based on gene-expression profiling. It can be divided into four different subtypes: Luminal A, Luminal B, HER2-enriched and basal-like. In daily practice, the immunohistochemical (IHC) subtyping is based however on the Ki-67 index, receptor (over)expression (estrogen receptor (ER), and/or progesterone receptor (PR), human epidermal growth factor 2 receptor (HER2)), and triple negative BC (TNBC), which does not express any of the previous receptors (Perou et al. ; Liu et al. ; Cornejo et al. ; Sorlie et al. ). There is an overlap between gene-expression subtypes and IHC subtypes, as shown in Fig.  . This IHC-based classification strongly informs both therapeutic strategy and prognosis.
 

## Main text

## Molecular imaging for BC
Oncologists frequently face the challenge of selecting the optimal diagnostic method to achieve a comprehensive assessment of a patient's condition, while minimizing time and resources, thereby enabling timely treatment decisions. As no imaging modality can discriminate all BC subtypes, the most effective approach relies on a combination of complementary techniques. To determine the extent of local disease progression, mammography, ultrasound and breast MRI are routinely used. However, all present shortcomings. Mammography suffers from poor sensitivity and specificity, and is difficult to interpret in subjects with dense breast tissue. MRI and ultrasound also lack specificity (Elmore ). To assess systemic disease staging, whole-body PET/CT with 2-deoxy-2-[18F]fluoro-D-glucose ([18F]FDG PET/CT) and bone scintigraphy provide valuable information (Tayama et al. ; Piva et al. ; Suzuki et al. ). However, [18F]FDG PET/CT is generally not recommended in stage I disease, where distant metastases are uncommon. Furthermore, it cannot supplant conventional diagnostic approaches for primary tumor detection due to the significant incidence of false positives (e.g., inflammation, fibroadenomas) and false negatives, limited sensitivity for small lesions, and reduced specificity after treatment, when inflammatory uptake may mimic active disease (Tian et al. ; Groheux et al. ). In addition, osteoblastic bone metastases often show lower [18F]FDG uptake, supporting the continued use of bone scintigraphy.
RECIST v.1.1 (Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors) is widely used to determine treatment response (Grimaldi et al. ; Eisenhauer et al. ). However, because it relies on anatomical measurements, RECIST inherently requires measurable lesions and is therefore suboptimal in many BC patients, particularly in bone-predominant metastatic disease (Niikura et al. ). As a consequence, metabolic response assessment using [18F]FDG-PET has gained interest, and PET Response Criteria in Solid Tumors (PERCIST) was recommended to provide a standardized framework for quantifying therapy-induced changes in tumor metabolism (Bensch et al. ). Yet, several key practical aspects remain unresolved, including classification of new lesions, variability in SULpeak implementation across software, and interpretation of progression when non-target lesions worsen despite improvement in target lesions (O et al. ). Together, these limitations support the need for more personalized imaging strategies.
In this context, precision nuclear medicine using PET and SPECT radiopharmaceuticals has emerged as a promising approach for the non-invasive detection, staging, and management of BC lesions at trace concentrations. Although PET outperforms SPECT in spatial resolution, SPECT is often more accessible and cost-effective due to lower radiotracer costs, with a similar sensitivity (Wagner ; Mariani et al. ). Radiolabeled biomolecules, such as peptides (Ahmadpour et al. ; Ahmadpour et al. ), small molecules (Tang et al. ; Hicks et al. ), monoclonal antibodies (Okarvi ) and aptamers (Tang et al. ; Kim et al. ; Varmira et al. , ) have been successfully used for the non-invasive diagnosis of BC with specific receptor overexpression (Bisi ; Ballinger ; Gennari et al. ; Cardoso et al. ; Salaün et al. ; Signore et al. ). Widely used radionuclides include technetium-99m (99mTc) for SPECT (Papagiannopoulou ; Chattopadhyay et al. ; Be ; Lee ), and copper-64 (64Cu), gallium-68 (68Ga), fluorine-18 (18F) for PET (McAfee and Neumann ; Mango ; Filippi et al. ; Shrivastava et al. ; Langbein et al. ).
In recent years, overexpression of specific receptors has been identified on BC tumor cells. The estrogen receptor (ER), progesterone receptor (PR), human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2), epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) (Sörensen et al. ; Bam et al. ; Baum et al. ; De et al. ), gastrin-releasing peptide receptor (GRPR) (Scopinaro et al. ), or even the fibroblast activation protein (FAP) are being explored for BC tumor targeting. This review highlights the great promise of radiopharmaceuticals for BC imaging and offers an outlook on emerging advances in the field.

## Steroid receptors
Steroid receptors are a class of ligand-activated transcription factors that exert central developmental roles and contribute to the regulation of reproductive processes and various organ systems in both adult males and females. They also act as key drivers of the growth and metastatic progression of hormone-dependent cancers. About 70% of BC are hormone-dependent, relying on estrogen and/or progesterone/androgen for growth, when stimulating nuclear estrogen receptors (ERs), progesterone receptors (PRs) and androgen receptors (ARs). Currently, the status of these receptors is assessed through immunohistochemistry (IHC) on tumor tissue obtained during biopsy or surgery. Although IHC assays have improved considerably (fixed tissue specimens, more robustly reproducible pathological processing), they have a number of shortcomings. One major drawback is that, despite guideline-defined ER/PR positivity thresholds (e.g., ≥ 1% nuclear staining), inter-laboratory variability and pre-analytical/analytical factors can affect assay performance, and single-site sampling may not reflect heterogeneous receptor expression across lesions. Furthermore, IHC assays only offer a partial readout of receptor functionality and endocrine therapy responsiveness (Linden and Dehdashti ).
Understanding the receptor status of BC lesions is crucial for accurately characterizing the disease, but also for determining eligibility to endocrine therapy, which is among the most effective systemic treatments available. ER+ and PR+ BCs are typically less aggressive and associated with more favorable outcomes compared to HER2-enriched and triple-negative subtypes (Tsuchida et al. ). However, identifying receptor status is not always straightforward, given the spatiotemporal heterogeneity of steroid receptor expression. Indeed, phenotypic drift between primary tumors and metastases (most commonly ER/PR loss under therapeutic pressure) is well documented in BC and occurs in about 20% of cases (Katzenellenbogen ; Amir et al. ; Simmons et al. ). This could lead to mixed responses across lesions and complicate treatment selection when only a single lesion is biopsied. In this context, non-invasive whole-body ER imaging may capture lesion-level receptor status when biopsy is infeasible or when heterogeneous disease and treatment-driven receptor loss are suspected.

## Estrogen receptors (ERs)
ERs are overexpressed in approximately 70% of BC patients, which makes targeted endocrine theranostics a rising approach. There are three main groups of endocrine treatments available: selective competitive ER modulators (e.g., tamoxifen), aromatase inhibitors (e.g., anastrozole), and selective ER down-regulators (e.g., fulvestrant). However, response to hormonal therapy is observed in only 55–60% of ER+ disease, and in fewer than 10% of ER− tumors (Amir et al. ; Simmons et al. ; Kruchten et al. ; Hilton et al. ).
In routine practice, treatment outcome is monitored with serial CT, although morphologic changes may only emerge after several months, delaying reliable appraisal of therapeutic efficacy. Presently, 16α-[18F]-fluoro-17β-estradiol ([18F]FES) is the most attractive ER-targeted PET radiopharmaceutical and enables non-invasive evaluation of ER expression in primary and secondary sites throughout the patient's body (Fig.  ) (Linden et al. ). In cases of unclear ER status or when disease progresses despite multiple cycles of endocrine therapy, [18F]FES-PET can provide valuable information on the expression of ERs in primary tumors, as well as metastases (Boers et al. ), thus informing treatment decisions. This allows for the consideration of alternative anti-hormonal therapies if the metastases still express ERs. Conversely, low uptake of [18F]FES is correlated to a higher failure rate of anti-hormonal treatment, making it a potential prognostic indicator and may indicate the need for an alternative treatment strategy (Gong et al. ; Peterson et al. ; Linden et al. ; van Kruchten et al. ).
 Although not recommended as a routine diagnostic test for ER+ BC, [18F]FES-PET can provide added value in cases where current modalities and [18F]FDG PET/CT yield ambiguous results (Fig.  ) (Liu et al. ). However, in premenopausal women, endogenous estrogen levels fluctuate during the menstrual cycle and can compete with [18F]FES for ER binding, which may slightly influence uptake, most notably in physiologic tissues (endometrium/ovaries). Nevertheless, these cycle-related effects are generally relatively small and do not appear to affect BC lesion uptake (Mankoff et al. ; Mankoff et al. ). Hence, documenting cycle phase is optional rather than required for routine interpretation. Beside this practical consideration, [18F]FES also has several drawbacks that limit its effectiveness. These include high liver uptake, which can hinder the evaluation of liver metastases; rapid blood washout, leading to moderate uptake in tumor tissues. Moreover, its sensitivity and specificity towards ER for tumor visualization were evaluated in four clinical studies (from 69 to 100% and 80 to 100%, respectively) (Ahmadpour et al. ; De et al. ; Smith et al. ; Morgat et al. ). To address these shortcomings, new radiopharmaceuticals have been investigated, namely 4-fluoro-11β-methoxy-16α-[18F]fluoroestradiol (4-F-M-[18F]FES) (Seimbille et al. ) and 1-(2-(2-(2-[18F]fluoroethoxy) ethoxy)ethyl)-1H-1,2,3-triazole-estradiol ([18F]FETE) (Linden and Dehdashti ; Clarke et al. ), which are currently under clinical evaluation (Fig.  ).

## Progesterone receptors (PRs)
ER positivity is observed in about two-third of BCs, and PR co-expression occurs in nearly half of these cases. PR is an estrogen-regulated gene, therefore its presence is commonly used as an indicator of functional ER signaling (Lanari et al. ; Horwitz et al. ; Natrajan et al. ; Horwitz and McGuire ). Besides, PR status may correlate more strongly to endocrine response than ER status alone (Lanari et al. ; Horwitz et al. ; Natrajan et al. ; Horwitz and McGuire ; Hammond et al. ; Santen et al. ; Bardou et al. ; Osborne and Tripathy ). In reality, ER−/PR+ patients are scarce (< 1% of all BCs) (Viale et al. ). ER+ /PR+ disease is typically associated with greater endocrine sensitivity (∼75%), whereas ER+ /PR − tumors show lower response rates (Linden and Dehdashti ). As a result, PR expression levels are routinely evaluated to support treatment selection and prognostic assessment (Linden and Dehdashti ; Clarke et al. ). Based on these premises, PR-targeted PET imaging serves as a surrogate for ER expression, and several PR radioligands have been identified. The most promising compound is 21-[18F]fluoro-16α,17α-[(R)-(1-α-furylmethylidene)dioxy]-19-norpregn-4-ene-3,20-dione ([18F]FFNP) (Fig.  ) (Vijaykumar et al. ; Lee et al. ; Dehdashti et al. ; Buckman et al. ). Fowler et al. carried out preclinical studies with [18F]FFNP to forecast endocrine therapy efficacy in BC (Fowler et al. ). Changes in ER/PR expression were studied through imaging studies in SSM1/ SSM2/ SSM3-xenograph bearing mice (mammary cell lines). They found that the uptake of [18F]FFNP increased in tumors after hormonal estrogen therapy, indicating a synergistic interaction between estrogen and progesterone receptors. Additionally, Linden et al. established a decrease in [18F]FFNP uptake in responding lesions after the end of therapy (Linden and Dehdashti ). In a study directed by Dehdashti et al., they demonstrated that [18F]FFNP exhibited greater uptake in BC compared to healthy breast tissue (Dehdashti et al. ). Out of all BC lesions, PR+ lesions showed higher uptake than PR− lesions (P = 0.001). Four clinical studies on [18F]FFNP reported scores for tumor visualization, ranging from 69 to 100% for sensitivity and 80% to 100% for specificity (Kenny ; Kole ; Zhou et al. ; Hanahan and Weinberg ). Furthermore, [18F]FFNP showed minimal defluorination, leading to minor bone accumulation, and was less influenced by hydrogenase metabolism compared to other PR radiotracers (Dehdashti et al. ). In 2021, in a longitudinal phase II single-center study (ClinicalTrials.gov ID: NCT02455453), Dehdashti et al. directly investigated the influence of estrogen on tumor PRs (Dehdashti et al. ). They conducted [18F]FFNP PET/CT scans on 43 postmenopausal women with advanced ER+ BC. The second PET was acquired after an estradiol challenge, in order to probe a "flare reaction". Notably, increased [18F]FFNP uptake was exclusively observed in the 28 patients who responded to estrogen therapy, whereas no increase was measured in the 15 non-responders. This demonstrated the perfect discrimination of responders in this cohort of patients (100% sensitivity and specificity). Responders were also found to experience significantly longer survival rates. Together, these findings support [18F]FFNP-PET as a compelling predictor of estrogen-based therapy response in ER+ BC (Furr and Jordan ; Namer et al. ).
 To summarize, radiopharmaceuticals targeting steroid receptors have been explored for decades, yet only a few have transitioned to routine practice ([18F]FES being a notable exception). Adoption has been limited by target biology. Classical ERα/ERβ receptors are predominantly nuclear receptors, so tracers must be sufficiently lipophilic to cross cell membranes, which drives slow intracellular binding kinetics, nonspecific uptake (especially hepatobiliary), and complicates quantification. Signal can be further dampened by competition with endogenous hormones or concurrent endocrine therapy, while receptor levels fluctuate with treatment and hormonal status (menstrual cycle), creating timing and interpretation challenges. However, it is important to note that estrogen signaling can also be triggered via the membrane-associated G protein-coupled estrogen receptor GPER30 (non-genomic signaling pathway) (Filardo et al. ).

## Gastrin-releasing peptide receptor (GRPR)
The GRPR is part of the mammalian bombesin (BBN) receptor family. It is a seven-domain transmembrane G protein-coupled receptor (GPCRs) (Mansi et al. ), with the gastrin-releasing peptide (GRP), a 27 amino acids neuropeptide, being its natural ligand. Upon binding of GRP, the receptor changes conformation and activates the G-protein anchored at the inner face of the cell membrane. This leads to a large scope of physiological and pharmacological pathways (i.e., the release of peptide hormones, gastric acid secretions, smooth muscle contractions, circadian rhythm regulation, and immune functions) (Smith et al. ). Overexpression of GRPR has been described in several cancers, including breast, prostate, GI tract, brain and lung cancers, as well as head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (Baratto et al. ; Patel et al. ; Yang et al. ). GRPRs are overexpressed and aberrantly activated in approximately 62–74% of BC tissues, and as such implicated in tumor growth, proliferation, survival, invasion, migration, angiogenesis and metastasis (Reis-Filho et al. ; Baratto et al. ; Lehmann et al. ; Masuda et al. ; Rakha et al. ). Thus, GRPR is an attractive biomarker for receptor targeted nuclear theranostics (De et al. ; Smith et al. ; Morgat et al. ; Bakker et al. ). A plethora of GRPR agonists and antagonists have been synthesized and investigated over the past few years for visualizing GRPR expression in both primary and metastatic lesions (Pourghiasian et al. ; Carlucci et al. ). However, to date, just a limited number of clinical trials have been performed on GRPR-mediated nuclear BC imaging.
The use of radiolabeled BBN-agonists in humans presented biosafety challenges. Agonist binding to GRPR leads to receptor-radiopeptide complex internalization, triggering severe adverse effects through activation of endogenously expressed GRPR, particularly in the GI tract (Maina et al. ; Kaloudi et al. ; Kratochwil et al. ; Ballal et al. ). Moreover, BBN-like ligands have been associated with mitogenic processes (Perrin et al. ; Delpassand et al. ). As a result, the design of GRPR radioantagonists emerged to obtain clinically safe BBN analogs (Cescato et al. ). By definition, antagonists compete with receptor natural ligand, blocking agonist-induced receptor activation, and do not internalize in target cells (Castiglione and Gozzini ). A few GRPR-antagonists were introduced as anti-mitogenic anticancer drugs and to study GRPR pharmacology. Most stem from structural modifications on the native backbone [DPhe7]-BBN(7–14), especially on the C-ter Leu13-Met14-NH2dipeptide (Smith et al. ; Jensen et al. ; Moreno et al. ; Maina and Nock ). In-depth investigations into the structure–activity relationship (SAR) have been performed in GRPR+ cells and animal models (Castiglione and Gozzini ). Alterations such as substitution of key amino acids with other residues or by their D- or ß-counterparts (Azay et al. ), truncation of Met14, alkylamidation/esterification of the carboxy group of Leu13 (Heimbrook et al. ; Wang et al. ) and reduction of peptide bonds, led to a wide range of highly potent GRPR antagonists, exhibiting inhibitory effects on cell proliferation of GRPR-expressing cells and tumors in mice (Castiglione and Gozzini ; Wiele et al. ). These preclinical studies demonstrated that these compounds were cleared rapidly from the background versus the tumors, and that they also exhibited higher in vivo metabolic stability and more favorable pharmacokinetic profiles compared to agonists (Maina et al. ). Additionally, other strategies were applied to improve their pharmacokinetic properties, like selecting the suitable linker/chelator and radiometal, co-administration of protease inhibitors (e.g. phosphoramidon (Dalm et al. ; Baun et al. )), or scaling up the injected peptide dose (Mansi et al. ), played crucial roles in generating novel radioligands eligible for clinical translation in BC patients (Table  ) (Maina et al. ).
 

## Radioantagonists based on the [DPhe6, Leu13-NHEt]-BBN(6–13) motif
One of the potent GRPR antagonists, [DPhe6, Leu13-NHEt]BBN(6–13) was generated by ethylamidation of the Leu13 at the C-terminus (Wang et al. ). Coupling to an acyclic tetraamine chelator by means of a para-aminomethylaniline-diglycolic acid (p-ADA) linker led to the development of the first GRPR radioantagonist investigated in mice with PC-3 tumors, [99mTc]Tc-Demobesin1 ([99mTc]Tc-DB1). The tracer demonstrated excellent binding affinity and specificity towards GRPR, high accumulation in tumor tissue, rapid clearance from non-target organs, even from GRPR-rich organs like the pancreas, and renal excretion (Wang et al. ). Attempts to enhance tumor retention implicated substitutions with different linkers and amino acids, ultimately leading to the identification of the Sar11-analog [99mTc]Tc-DB15 ([99mTc]Tc-N4-p-ADA-[DPhe6, Sar11, Leu13-NHEt]BBN(6–13) as a promising candidate (Nock et al. , ; Nock et al. ), which was confirmed by preliminary results in patients with advanced BC. [99mTc]Tc-DB15 successfully enabled the visualization of soft tissue and bone metastases on SPECT/CT scans, while showing good tolerability and a favorable pharmacokinetic profile (Nock et al. ). The universal chelator DOTA was then conjugated, resulting in SB3 (DOTA-p-ADA-[DPhe6, Leu13-NHEt] BBN(6–13)) for theranostic purposes (Fig.  ). In xenograft murine models, [67Ga]Ga-SB3 showed comparable tumor uptake and retention than [99mTc]Tc-DB1. Consequently, clinical evaluation was performed for [68Ga]Ga-SB3 in a focus group of subjects with disseminated PC and BC (Fig.  ). Malignant lesions were successfully observed in 50% of the patients on PET/CT scans (Maina et al. ). However, both the SPECT imaging agent, [111In]In-SB3, and the therapeutic [177Lu]Lu-SB3 underwent rapid neutral endopeptidase (NEP)-mediated degradation in the bloodstream, posing challenges to their clinical translation. Nevertheless, this limitation could be overcome by using specific peptidases inhibitors in co-administration (Bakker et al. ; Lymperis et al. ).
 

## Radioantagonists based on the [Dphe6, Sta13, Leu14-NH2] BBN (6–14) motif
By substituting the C-terminal Leu13-Met14-NH2by the dipeptide Sta13-Leu14-NH2, the GRPR antagonist JMV594 ([Dphe6, Sta13, Leu14-NH2]BBN (6–14)) was obtained (Llinares et al. ). Various chelators and linkers were coupled at the N-terminus to modulate its pharmacokinetics, receptor binding affinity, metabolic stability, and hydrophilicity. [111In]In-RM1 (RM1: DOTA-Gly-4-aminobenzoyl-JMV594) was initially synthesized, allowing for a direct comparison with [111In]In-AMBA (DOTA-Gly-4-aminobenzoyl-BBN(7–14)) (Mansi et al. ). In preclinical studies, RM1 exhibited higher uptake in PC-3 xenografts and faster clearance from the background compared to AMBA. The introduction of a positive charge at the N-terminus, either through a positively charged linker or radiometal chelate, showed to enhance GRPR affinity and in vivo pharmacokinetics (Mansi et al. ; Abiraj et al. ; Gourni et al. ; Marsouvanidis et al. ). One approach involved the use of the 4-amino-1-carboxymethyl-piperidine (Pip) linker, resulting in RM2 (DOTA-Pip-[Dphe6, Sta13, Leu14-NH2]BBN(6–14)) (Fig.  ). [68Ga]Ga-RM2 has been extensively assessed in several PC cell lines and tumor models, demonstrating enhanced metabolic stability and binding affinity to GRPR (Roivainen et al. ). It is the most evaluated PET tracer in the “statine-based”-class of GRPR antagonists in the clinic (Figs.  and ). In a pilot study of BC patients (15 biopsy-diagnosed cases of primary breast carcinoma) (Stoykow et al. ), [68Ga]Ga-RM2 exhibited good tolerability, high sensitivity, specificity, and accuracy in detecting primary tumors, plus most metastatic lesions (13 out of 18 lesions on PET/CT images) (ClinicalTrials.gov ID: NCT03831711). More recently, investigation of [177Lu]Lu-RM2 in patients with metastatic castration-resistant PC demonstrated encouraging therapeutic characteristics for clinical use (Kurth et al. ). Positively charged radiometal chelates were also explored (Gourni et al. ; Abiraj et al. ). The antagonist AR (H-PEG4-JMV594), was studied with four different metal-chelator pairs at the N-terminus: [68Ga]Ga-NODAGA, [111In]In-DOTA, [64Cu]Cu-CB-TE2A, and [99mTc]Tc-N4. This further validated the superiority of positively charged conjugates with regard to GRPR affinity, tumor uptake, and retention.
 When JMV594 was coupled, via or without a PEG (polyethylene glycol) spacer, to a NOTA chelator (1,4,7-triazacyclononane-1,4,7-triacetic acid) at the N-terminus, the [68Ga]Ga-RM26-PET tracer (NOTA-PEG3-[Dphe6,Sta13,Leu14-NH2]BBN(6–14)) was obtained and enabled imaging of pathological BC lesions in 85% of the cases, with significantly higher uptake observed in ER+ patients (Fig.  ) (Varasteh et al. ). RM26 derivatives with amino-acid-based chelators at the N-terminus (maSSS, mercaptoacetyl-Ser-Ser-Ser; maSES, mercaptoacetyl-Ser-Glu-Ser) were labeled with technetium-99m, using a kit approach (Fig.  ) (Ahlgren et al. ). [99mTc]Tc-maSSS-PEG2-RM26 and [99mTc]Tc-maSES-PEG2-RM26 were evaluated in vitro and in vivo (Zang et al. ; Abiraj et al. ; Jamous et al. ). Both peptides displayed subnanomolar specific binding affinities to GRPR in PC-3 cells (maSSS > maSES), while preserving GRPR antagonism (Tolmachev and Orlova ; Breeman et al. ). The difference in affinity towards GRPR resulted in lower tumor activity levels and overall lower tumor-to-organ ratios, together with significantly lower uptake in the pancreas for [99mTc]Tc-maSES-PEG2-RM26. The glutamic acid in maSES-PEG2-RM26 enhanced hydrophilicity and consequently lower hepatic uptake in comparison to [99mTc]Tc-maSSS-PEG2-RM26 in vivo. The first-in-human study, a single-center diagnostic phase I study (ClinicalTrials.gov ID: NCT04746638), evaluated the biodistribution, safety profile, and dosimetry after injection of [99mTc]Tc-maSSS-PEG2-RM26 in 7 BC patients (planar and full-body SPECT/CT scans). It showed that the tracer was well tolerated and accumulated in BC tumors in all patients of the study (Chernov et al. ; Abouzayed et al. ).

## Radioantagonists based on the {His25-NHCH[CHCH(CH3)2]2}]GRP(20–25) motif
The linker p-ADA was introduced at the N-terminus of the [Dphe6,Gln7,His12-NHCH(CH2CH(CH3)2)2] BBN(6–12) sequence to afford NeoB (Fig.  ). Labeled with In-111, Ga-68, and Lu-177 for SPECT, PET and PRRT, respectively, NeoB demonstrated promising theranostic characteristics. NeoB possessed great receptor affinity and cell-uptake, as well as extended tumor accumulation in GRPR tumor-bearing mice (Kaloudi et al. ; Ruigrok et al. ). In 2019, 19 patients with advanced GRPR-positive malignancies (including BC) were enrolled in the phase II NeoFIND study (NCT03724253) to evaluate [68Ga]Ga-NeoB (Djaileb et al. ). The tracer enabled clear delineation of at least one primary or secondary lesion in 17/19 patients. Moreover, the study demonstrated its safety and established full-body dosimetry analysis, laying the groundwork for a potential therapeutic implementation of [177Lu]Lu-NeoB. Hence, more recently, the international multicenter NeoRay phase I/IIa trial (NCT03872778, PMID: 41,344,852) has advanced [177Lu]Lu-NeoB radioligand therapy into first-in-human dose escalation, with eligibility requiring confirmation of tumor GRPR expression by [68Ga]Ga-NeoB uptake (Mittra et al. ; GRPR ). In the phase I dose-escalation cohort, 17 patients with GRPR-expressing advanced solid tumors (including GIST, PC, glioblastoma, BC) underwent increasing administered activities. Dose-limiting toxicities occurred only at the highest tested activity (11.1 GBq); the maximum tolerated dose and recommended phase II dose were identified as 9.25 GBq. Overall, 23.5% of patients experienced treatment-related adverse effects of grade ≥ 3. Dosimetry analysis (n = 16) reported mean absorbed dose coefficients of 0.10 Gy/GBq in kidneys, 0.055 Gy/GBq in pancreas, and 0.018 Gy/GBq in red marrow, supporting a favorable organ dosimetry profile and continued clinical development. Collectively, these early results corroborate feasibility and manageable toxicity for [177Lu]Lu-NeoB, while underscoring the importance of patient selection by [68Ga]Ga-NeoB and careful monitoring of hematologic and organ dosimetry, as data from larger cohorts become available.
 

## Radioantagonists based on the [Dphe6,Leuψ13,AA14-NH2]BBN(6–14) motif
Modifying the bond between residues 13 and 14 of BBN was described to produce potent GRPR antagonists (Coy et al. ). Building on this finding, the Schally group designed a library of pseudononapeptide antagonists with a reduced bond between residues 13 and 14 ([Leuψ13-AA14]BBN family). These antagonists showed high affinity for GRPR and demonstrated the ability to inhibit the growth of various cancer types in preclinical models (Jungwirth et al. ; Cornelio et al. ; Ba et al. ; Koppán et al. ; Shirahige et al. ; Cai et al. ). ProBOMB1 (DOTA-p-ADA-[Dphe6,Leuψ13,Pro14-NH2]BBN(6–14) was designed based on the previously reported RC-3950-II sequence, by replacing the terminal amino acid (Tac14: 4-thiazolidinecarboxylic acid) with a proline and coupling the universal chelator DOTA at the N-terminus of the GRPR-targeting sequence. [68Ga]Ga-ProBOMB1 exhibited nanomolar affinity for GRPR and PET imaging showed excellent tumor uptake and high-contrast images in a PC model, with significantly reduced pancreas uptake compared to both 177Lu- and 68Ga-labeled NeoBOMB1 (Rousseau et al. ; Lau et al. ). To further improve tumor uptake and contrast while mitigating pancreas uptake, the ProBOMB scaffold was optimized and the neutral p-ADA spacer was replaced by the cationic Pip linker to provide ProBOMB2. ProBOMB2 showed high specific tumor accumulation and more favorable tumor-to-organ ratios in preclinical models when radiolabeled with either 68Ga or 177Lu (Rousseau et al. ; Lau et al. ). These data suggest that ProBOMB2 is a promising imaging agent for GRPR-positive cancers. The benefit of replacing the terminal proline with a thioproline residue in TacBOMB2 was investigated and showed promising imaging results in mice models. [68Ga]Ga-TacBOMB2 enabled clear visualization of GRPR+ PC-3 lesions, suggesting that C-ter thioproline over proline improves GRPR binding. These preclinical studies provided useful structure–activity information for future development of ProBOMB and TacBOMB analogs.

## Human Epiderman growth factor receptor 2 (HER2)
HER2 is part of the tyrosine kinase recepto family (EGFR family), encoded by the HER2 proto-oncogene (Hamilton et al. ). The dimerization of HER2 triggers a myriad of cellular responses, such as cell proliferation, survival, differentiation, angiogenesis, metastatic spread/ invasion, and anti-apoptotic functions (Harari and Yarden ). HER2 is overexpressed in roughly 20–25% of BC patients and correlated to aggressive growth and poor prognosis (worse prognosis for HER2+ BC patients versus ER+ BC patients) (Burris et al. ; Velikyan et al. ; Ross et al. ; Kreutzfeldt et al. ). Accordingly, routine evaluation of HER2 status by biopsy is standard of care, since it guides therapeutic selection. To do so, several assays are available, IHC and in situ hybridization being the most commonly applied. Given the potential toxicity of HER2-directed therapies, accurate and robust evaluation of HER2 status is critical to predict response to therapy (Niikura et al. ; Gonzalez et al. ). As for the steroid receptors, HER2 has been targeted not only for non-invasive therapeutic interventions, but also imaging (Sekar et al. ). Hence, over the years, both preclinical and clinical studies have investigated the nuclear imaging of HER2, by means of radiolabeled monoclonal antibodies, affibodies, nanobodies and peptides to obtain in vivo status of the HER2 expression of all BC lesions.
Trastuzumab (Herceptin), a recombinant G1 immunoglobulin monoclonal antibody binding to the extracellular domain of HER2, is currently the most attractive option for HER2 expression assessment (Carter et al. ). Both SPECT and PET radiotracers, [111In]In-trastuzumab and [89Zr]Zr-trastuzumab, were developed. Preclinical studies showed that [89Zr]Zr-trastuzumab displayed superior image quality compared with [111In]In-trastuzumab, leveraging the higher spatial resolution and sensitivity of PET (Dijkers et al. ). Importantly, the long half-life of 89Zr (78.4 h) aligns with monoclonal antibody kinetics, enabling imaging until 7 days post-injection. The PET tracer was investigated in 14 patients to assess biodistribution and optimal dosage (Dijkers et al. ; Chang et al. ). Prolonged imaging up to 5 days after injection permitted detection of almost all known lesions and uncovered additional occult foci. This clinical trial demonstrated that administration of the tracer resulted in high tumor activity levels and clear delineation of HER2+ primary BC and metastatic lesions (bone, liver, lung, and brain). However, it also resulted in high “radiation exposure.” Further clinical validation is required to evaluate the tracer’s utility in BC management and patient selection (Dijkers et al. ; Chang et al. ), which is important since trastuzumab therapy has a significant cost and may cause serious sides effects (Slamon et al. ) (multicenter study “Imaging Patients for Cancer drug selection in metastatic BC”; ClinicalTrials.gov ID: NCT01957332). In an interventional trial, [89Zr]Zr-trastuzumab PET/CT was evaluated in 56 BC patients and showed that HER2 PET, alongside [18F]FDG, could provide a powerful readout of the performance of the HER2-targeted antibody drug conjugate trastuzumab-emtansine (T-DM1) (Gebhart et al. ). In the recent years, [89Zr]Zr-trastuzumab showed great potential in assisting clinicians for decision-making when HER2 status is inaccessible by biopsy (Fig.  A) (Bensch et al. ). [64Cu]Cu-DOTA-trastuzumab is a very attractive PET tracer, due to the shorter half-life of 64Cu (12.7 h) (Carrasquillo et al. ; Sasada et al. ). A study in 6 HER2+ BC patients by Tamura et al. showed high blood residence, with low background signal, and a tumor dose identical to that of [18F]FDG PET/CT (Fig.  B) (Tamura et al. ). The tracer was able to successfully estimate HER2 status, discriminate between HER2+ and HER2− BC, and follow HER2 levels after therapy. Nevertheless, strong liver activity limits sensitivity for small lesions. This problem can be solved in some measure by blocking the unwanted hepatic uptake andd administering unlabeled trastuzumab before PET imaging (Carrasquillo et al. ; Mortimer et al. ).
 Beyond monoclonal antibodies, other specific HER2 binders, such as affibodies, nanobodies, and antibody fragments have been developed to better match the physical half-life of short-lived radionuclides and enable same-day imaging. Their reduced size typically results in faster blood clearance and lower background, which can be advantageous for assessing lesion-level heterogeneity and for serial imaging. However, these scaffolds often show prominent renal uptake, and optimization (e.g., chelator, charge, and dose) is frequently required to balance image contrast and radiation burden (Keyaerts et al. ; Sörensen et al. ). HER2 affibody imaging has advanced to clinical evaluation in metastatic BC through the use of ABY-025. First-in-human studies with [111In]In-ABY-025 SPECT/CT showed that the tracer is safe and can discriminate HER2+ from HER2− metastases, even in patients receiving HER2-targeted antibody therapy (Sörensen et al. ). Subsequent investigation with [68Ga]Ga-ABY-025 PET/CT demonstrated feasibility for whole-body mapping of HER2 expression in metastatic disease, with protocols compatible with clinical routine, confirming its role as a non-invasive alternative to repeated biopsies for capturing inter- and intra-patient heterogeneity (Sörensen et al. ). More recently, feasibility in HER2-low metastatic BC has also been reported, highlighting potential utility beyond the classical HER2+ setting (Altena et al. ). HER2 nanobodies provide another clinically translated scaffold with very rapid kinetics. In a phase I study, [68Ga]Ga-HER2-sdAb PET/CT delivered an effective dose comparable to routinely used PET tracers, and achieved high contrast in HER2+ metastases with low background in most organs. Kidney uptake was the dominant off-target signal, consistent with renal clearance of small proteins (Keyaerts et al. ). Repeatability and lesion uptake patterns for HER2-targeted sdAb PET have also been evaluated in more recent clinical work, supporting continued translation of this class toward robust, quantitative HER2 imaging (Gondry et al. ). Antibody fragments (e.g., Fab, F(ab’)₂, minibodies, scFv-based constructs) provide an intermediate approach between full antibodies and small molecules, offering faster clearance than intact IgG while retaining high specificity and potentially improved penetration. Preclinically, [64Cu]Cu-NOTA-pertuzumab F(ab’)₂ detected changes in tumor HER2 expression in response to trastuzumab and delivered a lower total-body radiation dose than [111In]In-labeled pertuzumab (Lam et al. ).
Peptides have also been investigated for HER2 imaging, but their use is still scarce, as translation from bench to bedside has been limited (Ge et al. ). Multiple radiopeptides have enabled the quantification of HER2 expression levels in mice models, but despite high affinity and tumor uptake observed with few candidates, only a handful have reached first-in-human evaluation (Table  ).
 A notable early example is [99mTc]Tc-HYNIC-H10F (Fig.  ). H10F is a short HER2-binding peptide (KLRLEWNR) that binds to the extracellular domain II of HER2, which is a different site from the trastuzumab epitope (domain IV). This epitope separation is clinically convenient, since peptide binding is non-competitive with ongoing anti-HER2 antibody therapy, enabling longitudinal imaging without interrupting treatment (Ge et al. b). In a first-in-human experience (2 BC patients), lesion visualization was feasible and blocking experiments supported on-target binding, providing proof-of-mechanism for peptide-based HER2 imaging (Wu et al. ).
 Following these results, optimization of H10F produced the Ark variants (e.g., HP-Ark) that incorporate sequence stabilization (including D-residue) and spacer, while retaining the domain-II binding pharmacophore. These changes increased affinity and in vivo stability without sacrificing the favorable fast-washout peptidic profile. [99mTc]Tc-HP-Ark2 was investigated in a pilot study of 34 women with BC (ClinicalTrials.gov Trial registration: NCT04267900) (Fig.  ) (Shi et al. ). Using SPECT/CT, this tracer non-invasively reflected HER2 status and appeared capable of identifying patients likely to benefit from trastuzumab while also providing a tool for early monitoring of treatment response. The tracer achieved 89.5% sensitivity, 88.2% specificity and 88.9% accuracy in detecting 32/36 lesions, outperforming [18F]FDG (78.9% sensitivity, 70.6% specificity, 75.0% accuracy) (Shi et al. ; https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT04267900). A direct performance comparison between [99mTc]Tc-HP-Ark2 and [89Zr]Zr-trastuzumab is not currently available since these tracers have not been evaluated side-by-side. Conceptually, peptide-based SPECT tracers, such as [99mTc]Tc-HP-Ark2, enable same-day imaging with rapid blood clearance, whereas [89Zr]Zr-trastuzumab benefits from high specificity. However, delayed imaging is typically required for [89Zr]Zr-trastuzumab due to long blood circulation, resulting in greater radiation exposure. Therefore, these approaches should be viewed as complementary rather than interchangeable, until comparative studies are published. These results illustrate the clinical promise of peptide-based HER2 imaging, even though further validation of diagnostic performance in larger cohorts is required, as well as clarification of its role alongside or in place of existing PET tracers.
 

## Fibroblast activation protein (FAP)
The tumor microenvironment (TME) has caught a lot of attention lately as it correlates strongly with patient’s prognosis and survival across various solid tumors, including BC (Ostermann et al. ; Calais ). Within this framework, cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs), among the most abundant cell populations of the tumor stroma, exert a crucial role in promoting tumor growth, metastasis and angiogenesis through secretion of growth factors, cytokines, and matrix metalloproteinases which remodel the extracellular matrix (Narra et al. ).
The overexpression of the fibroblast activation protein (FAP), a serine protease expressed predominantly on the surface of CAFs, has been identified in over 90% of epithelial tumors, supporting its relevance across a broad range of cancer types. Consequently, the development of FAP-targeted tracers, particularly quinoline-based FAP inhibitors (FAPIs), has attracted substantial attention as an alternative to the widely used [18F]FDG PET/CT (Tsai et al. ; Scott et al. ; Thomas et al. ; Eager et al. , ; Jansen et al. ; Poplawski et al. ). From the first-generation of radiolabeled FAPIs, [68Ga]Ga-FAPI-02, demonstrated promising results with high tumor uptake, but suboptimal tumor retention time due to rapid renal clearance (Loktev et al. ). Accordingly, efforts have focused on optimizing the pharmacokinetic properties of FAPI-based tracers. Subsequent iterations, including FAPI-04, FAPI-46 and FAPI-74 (Jansen et al. ), have further refined the balance between tumor retention and background clearance, improving the diagnostic precision (Giesel et al. ; Loktev et al. ; Meyer et al. ). [68Ga]Ga-FAPI-04 showed improved tumor retention by a factor of 3 at 3 h p.i. compared to [68Ga]Ga-FAPI-02 in patients with BC metastasis (Fig.  ) (Giesel et al. ; Kratochwil et al. ). FAP-targeted radiotherapy application was documented for the first time in 2018, using FAPI-04 labeled with the ß-emitter 90Y (Lindner et al. ). [90Y]Y-FAPI-04 demonstrated significant uptake in patients with advanced metastatic BC, but tracer retention was relatively short (< 3 h), compromising its applicability for therapy. Mainz et al. explored the introduction of a squaric acid (SA) spacer between the bifunctional chelator and the quinoline-based scaffold to yield the DOTA.SA.FAPI (Fig.  ) (Moon et al. , ). More recently, DOTA.SA.FAPI effectively detected BC lesions post-chemotherapy in a 31 years old female patient, demonstrating the ability to reveal smaller lesions (Jansen et al. ; Moon et al. ). Multimeric SA.FAPIs are being explored to improve their therapeutic potential by enhancing tumor uptake and retention (Moon et al. ).
 Yet, among the 55 recruiting or not yet recruiting prospective trials, using FAPIs PET/CT, only a minor fraction of them concern BC (3/55) despite the potential of these radiotracers for this malignancy (Meletta et al. ). Early clinical studies suggest that the added value of FAPI imaging may be greatest in specific scenarios where conventional imaging modalities underperform, particularly for local staging and lesion characterization in invasive lobular carcinoma (ILC). ILC remains a recurring imaging challenge due to its unique growth pattern, and even breast MRI, despite being highly sensitive, can yield equivocal results. This can increase the biopsy burden and complicate surgical planning (Backhaus et al. ; Jung et al. ). In a cohort of women with invasive BC, [68Ga]Ga-FAPI-46 PET/MRI showed strong tracer accumulation in untreated primary tumors (mean SUVmax ~ 13.9), with clear lesion delineation. Here, PET findings helped establish or support extra-axillary nodal disease, highlighting the added value of combining high-resolution breast MRI with stroma-targeted PET at once (Backhaus et al. ; Backhaus et al. ). More recently, a prospective side-by-side study comparing [18F]FAPI-42 PET/MR with breast MRI reported that PET/MR improved diagnostic performance, notably increasing specificity for primary lesions (96% vs 68%), with a marked reduction in false positives (from 32% to 4%), and enhancing sensitivity for axillary lymph node metastases detection (95% vs 62%) (Hou et al. ). Overall, the available evidence suggests that FAPI-based imaging, especially when integrated with MRI, can improve lesion visualization and staging in challenging BC phenotypes such as ILC. Although ideal for imaging, validation in larger patient cohorts is still required to establish the therapeutic value of FAPI-based therapy agents and explore their full potential in personalized cancer management.

## Nectin-4
Nectin-4 (or PVRL4) is a cell-adhesion protein overexpressed across BC cohorts, notably enriched in triple-negative disease, and linked to proliferation, invasion, and adverse clinicopathology. Its readily accessible extracellular domain, together with limited normal-tissue expression, supports its use for non-invasive receptor phenotyping and may aid therapy selection. Recent reviews consolidate its biology, prevalence, and druggability, positioning Nectin-4 as a credible imaging target in BC (Wang et al. , ). Antibody-based Nectin-4 probes have been preclinically evaluated. F(ab′)₂ fragments derived from enfortumab vedotin (e.g., [64Cu]Cu-NOTA-EV-F(ab′)₂) enabled immunoPET in TNBC models with high, blockable uptake. [89Zr]Zr-DFO-Padcev also imaged Nectin-4-positive TNBC, albeit with slower kinetics (Huang et al. , ).
By contrast, the first BC patient imaging was reported with [68Ga]Ga-FZ-NR-1, a Nectin-4-targeted bicyclic peptide-based PET tracer evaluated in a bench-to-first-in-human study. After preclinical selection among three hits, [68Ga]Ga-FZ-NR-1 PET/CT identified lesions in 9 TNBC patients; positivity correlated with Nectin-4 IHC, and lesions overlapped with [18F]FDG findings (Fig.  ). While early and size limited, this pilot demonstrates feasibility of in-patient Nectin-4 PET and biologic specificity via tissue confirmation, key prerequisites for multicenter validation (Sun et al. ).
 

## Trophoblast cell-surface antigen 2 (TROP-2)
TROP-2 is a cell-surface glycoprotein overexpressed across BC subtypes, particularly TNBC and a meaningful fraction of HR+/HER2− BC, which makes it an attractive imaging biomarker and set a clear rationale for non-invasive TROP-2 phenotyping (Yao et al. ; Hu et al. ; Sakach et al. ). TROP-2 imaging in BC is moving from preclinical to first-in-human studies. In TNBC preclinical models, a theranostic anti-TROP-2 antibody (NY003) labeled with the 89Zr/177Lu pair enabled high-contrast immunoPET/SPECT and expression-dependent uptake with therapeutic growth control, setting in place a robust preclinical package for translation (Wu et al. ). Building on these results, [64Cu]Cu-NOTA-Trodelvy-F(ab′)₂ achieved same-day immunoPET and clear delineation of TROP-2-positive TNBC xenografts, with uptake correlating to target expression (Huang et al. ).
These studies motivated faster-kinetic agents, like nanobodies (e.g., [99mTc]Tc-TROP-2 VHH), for clinical translation in BC/TNBC patients (Sun et al. ). Likewise, a prospective, single-center study of the labeled nanobody, [68Ga]Ga-MY6349 PET/CT (NCT06188468), reported first-in-human safety/dosimetry and imaged 90 patients across multiple cancers, explicitly HR-positive BC and TNBC. In BC examples, [68Ga]Ga-MY6349 showed higher uptake and better lesion contrast than [18F]FDG, with detection of lymph-node, liver and bone metastases that were FDG-occult. Tracer uptake correlated with TROP-2 IHC (Fig.  ) (Chen et al. ). These data prove clinical feasibility of TROP-2 PET in BC patients for per-lesion accuracy and therapy-selection read-outs. These tracers could provide a well-suited solution for stratifying patients and monitoring TROP-2-targeted therapy.
 

## Other targets

## The chemokine receptor type 4 (CXCR4)
CXCR4 has emerged as an attractive theranostic biomarker in BC, owing to its crucial role in tumor growth, metastasis and therapy resistance. CXCR4 is a G protein-coupled receptor that, upon binding CXCL12 (also referred to as stromal cell-derived factor-1, SDF-1), triggers downstream signaling cascades implicated in cell survival, proliferation and migration (Dalm et al. ; Yu et al. ). [68Ga]Ga-Pentixafor is the most widely studied CXCR4-targeted PET imaging agent. It has demonstrated high specificity and favorable pharmacokinetics in various cancers, including BC (Albano et al. ). Vag et al. reported accumulation of [68Ga]Ga-Pentixafor in BC lesions with modest-to-good tumor-to-background ratios, with no significant association with molecular subtype, receptor status, proliferation index (Ki-67), or tumor grade (Fig.  ) (Vag et al. ). However, several challenges remain in its clinical implementation for BC despite these promising results. The heterogeneity of CXCR4 expression within and between tumors poses a significant hurdle (Yu et al. ). Moreover, off-target uptake in CXCR4-expressing tissues (bone marrow, liver, spleen) will necessitate careful dosimetry and treatment planning to minimize adverse effects, while maximizing theranostic efficacy. Looking to the future, the development of novel CXCR4-targeted imaging radiopharmaceuticals with improved pharmacokinetics and reduced off-target effects, such as [64Cu]Cu-UbCG4 (Dalm et al. ; Xu et al. ), could pave the way for more personalized treatment strategies (Yu et al. ; Wang et al. ).

## The neurotensin receptor 1 (NTS1)
NTS1 is a class-A transmembrane GPCR for the tridecapeptide neurotensin peptide (NTS). Upon binding in the periphery, NTS is involved in numerous functions in the GI tract, as well as growth and proliferation of various GI tissues by activating the ERK signaling pathway. NTS1 is highly prevalent in invasive ductal breast carcinoma, with one cohort reporting positivity in 91% of cases (Souazé et al. ). Also, elevated NTS1 levels correlate with larger primary tumors, higher Scarff-Bloom-Richardson grade, increased nodal metastasis, greater recurrence risk, and reduced survival rates (Dupouy et al. ). In contrast to tumors, normal epithelial breast cells lack NTS1, which provides a favorable biological target for molecular imaging, and possibly offering broad coverage in populations where other targets (e.g., HER2) are variably expressed (Evers ). Several stabilized ligands derived from neurotensin (8–13) have been developed for PET (18F/ 68Ga), addressing the past challenges of rapid proteolysis and unfavorable pharmacokinetics, that limited early NTS1 ligands. In this context, [18F]F-DEG-VS-NT provides clear tumor delineation and target specificity (blockable uptake) in NTS1+ xenografts, establishing proof-of-concept for NTS1-targeted molecular imaging (Wu et al. ). NTS1-targeting has progressed past standalone proof-of-concept tracers. Among DOTA-neurotensin analogues, NT-20.3 has emerged as a clinically translated tracer: a first-in-human study demonstrated that [68Ga]Ga-NT-20.3 PET is safe and well tolerated in patients with pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), establishing feasibility for NTS1 PET in humans (Hodolic et al. ). Most importantly, NTS1 has already transitioned into RLT in other solid tumors with [177Lu]Lu-3BP-227 (IPN01087). First-in-human feasibility data for NTS1-targeted radioligand therapy in PDAC were recently published, establishing clinical precedent for NTS1 as a theranostic target (ClinicalTrials.gov ID: NCT03525392) (Baum et al. ). Collectively, these data position NTS1 as one of the emerging GPCR targets for BC clinical applications.

## The neuropeptide Y receptor 1 (NPY1R)
NPY1R is also part of the GPCR superfamily (endogenous ligands: neuropeptide Y (NPY), polypeptide-YY(PYY), pancreatic polypeptide (PP)). Functionally, NPY1R activation is involved in oncogenesis and angiogenesis processes. NPY1R has been found to be co- and over-expressed in ER+ BCs (~ 85% of ER+ BCs) (Reubi et al. ; Morgat et al. ). Together, these correlations position NPY1R as a valuable biomarker for this specific subtype of BC, which accounts for a significant proportion of BC cases (Reubi et al. ). Notably, the receptor was reported to be the most abundant within the TNBC subtype (Liu et al. ). Available data are still parse, and further clinical studies are warranted to define its utility in the clinical setting. For example, [18F]-Fluoroglycosylated NPY analogues have been explored in mice models, and [18F]F-3b came up to be the lead peptide for future PET imaging studies (Hofmann et al. ). Likewise, the Y1-receptor antagonist [99mTc]Tc-(N-α-His)Ac-[Phe7,Pro34]NPY has shown promise in preclinical studies, with specific uptake and clear tumor visualization of Y1-receptor-positive xenografts (Khan et al. ). First clinical data demonstrate clear uptake of the peptidic tracer into primary BC tumors, but also metastasis, with high tumor-to-background contrast, confirming the potential of this approach (Fig.  ) (Khan et al. ).
 

## The vasoactive intestinal peptide receptor 1 (VIPR-1)
VIPR-1 (or VPAC1) is a class-B GPCR (endogenous ligand: VIP) implicated in cell proliferation, cell differentiation, and survival of BC cells (Reubi et al. ). It has been reported across multiple BC subtypes, with several studies noting higher expression in premalignant and early invasive lesions. Hence, VIPR-1-targeted radioligands might be particularly useful for initial detection and staging (Reubi ). VIPR-1-targeted tracers, [18F]F-dVIP (Jagoda et al. ) and [64Cu]Cu-TP3805 (Thakur et al. ; Tripathi et al. ), for BC imaging were evaluated in preclinical and clinical studies. These radiotracers have shown promise in preclinical studies, with favorable tumor-to-background in animal models. In a first-in-human trial, Thakur and colleagues reported that [64Cu]Cu-TP3805 unequivocally detected lesions in all 19 of 19 BC patients (including 15 invasive ductal carcinoma, 3 lobular carcinoma, 1 high-grade mammary carcinoma) (Thakur et al. ).

## Conclusions
Receptor-mediated nuclear imaging heralds a new era for precision oncology. The present review underlines the significant potential of radiopharmaceuticals for BC diagnosis, treatment planning and response monitoring. However, the inherent molecular heterogeneity of BC necessitates a nuanced, target-specific imaging strategy. For instance, ER+ luminal A and B subtypes of BC are promising avenues for imaging. Nuclear receptor, such as ER- and PR-targeting radiotracers, hold great promise for noninvasive concomitant assessment of receptor status and treatment response. Complementary to ER/PR imaging, GRPR-targeted approaches seem viable alternatives, especially in settings where ER/PR imaging proves suboptimal. These imaging techniques could be particularly interesting in tumors that lose ER expression overtime, and therefore represent a very important tool to monitor tumor evolution. The emerging role of NPY1R or CXCR4 as possible targets, especially in ER+ BCs, further expands the arsenal of imaging options. Such a variety of targets allows for a broader and more flexible imaging strategy, capable of facing the dynamic nature of BC biology.
However, several critical challenges remain. The complex relationship between GRPR and ER requires further elucidation. This understanding is crucial for developing robust imaging algorithms that can guide clinicians in selecting the most appropriate imaging protocol for individual patients throughout the course of their disease. Receptor-targeted imaging is most useful when it answers subtype-specific decision points that biopsy may miss because of spatial heterogeneity and treatment-driven target modulation. In HR+, [18F]FES PET is the most established tracer for whole-body assessment of ER expression (SNMMI/EANM practice guideline) and for clarifying ambiguous metastatic disease or suspected receptor discordance. Beyond this radioligand, peptide-receptor targets (e.g., GRPR/RM2 PET) can be leveraged as subset biomarkers within predominantly ER+ metastatic disease. For the HER2-enriched subtype (and potentially HER2-heterogeneous/HER2-low) disease, HER2-directed immuno-PET (e.g., [68Ga]Ga-ABY-025) provides whole-body mapping of target availability and can support treatment selection when biopsy is impractical or when heterogeneity is suspected. In ILC and MRI-equivocal local staging, early evidence suggests a particular role for FAPI imaging integrated with MR (PET/MR) to improve lesion visualization and nodal staging performance. Finally, in TNBC, early feasibility data on Nectin-4 and TROP-2-targeted PET support the concept of imaging-based confirmation of target presence for patient selection, pending prospective validation in larger cohorts.
