## Highlights
Oral GLP-1RA OHP2 has the potential to treat Alzheimer’s disease (AD) by promoting the reprogramming of microglia via enhancing the secretion of IL-27 from astrocytes.
IL-27, acting as a metabolic regulator, can induce the reprogramming of microglia and may treat AD.
OHP2 and IL-27 regulate the reprogramming of microglia from the M1 type to the M2 type via the glycolysis/cGAS lactylation clock/mTOR pathway.

## Introduction
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is a neurodegenerative disorder characterized by progressive cognitive decline and behavioral disturbances. The clinical manifestations of AD are multifaceted, including cognitive impairments (such as memory loss or even complete amnesia, impaired communication skills, disorientation, and spatial discrimination deficits), psychiatric symptoms, and urinary and fecal incontinence [ ]. It is projected that the global prevalence of dementia will rise from a 57.4 million individuals in 2019 to 150 million individuals by 2050 [ ]. By the age of 85, it is projected that one in every three individuals will be afflicted with AD [ ]. A more aggressive treatment option for AD would hold significant implications for everyone.
The two most significant pathological alterations in the brains of patients with AD are the deposition of Amyloid β (Aβ) in the temporal cortex, frontal cortex, and hippocampus, leading to the formation of senile plaques, and the hyper-phosphorylation of microtubule-associated protein Tau, which results in the formation of neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs) that accumulate in the cytoplasm of neurons primarily located in the hippocampus, amygdala, and temporal cortex [ , ]. Furthermore, pathological changes such as increased glia and neuronal loss were also observed [ , ]. Although the Aβ cascade hypothesis is a mainstream theory, it has faced skepticism and rejection. It alone cannot fully explain neuronal damage in AD, as both autopsy and imaging studies have shown the presence of Aβ and NFT in healthy elderly subjects with normal cognitive function [ ]. A number of clinical trials aimed at eliminating various forms of Aβ in the brain of AD patients have ended in failure. Although some antibodies can reduce Aβ in the brains of patients, their cognitive function has not been significantly improved [ ]. It was not until the approval of the Aβ monoclonal antibody such as Aducanumab and Lecanemab that this dispute ended, and the Aβ hypothesis regained widespread acceptance [ ]. Through these failures, researchers gained a deeper understanding of the effects of different forms of Aβ. For example, indiscriminate clearance of Aβ monomers and aggregates is likely to be detrimental to cognition [ ]. Microglia can phagocytose diffuse Aβ, thereby contributing to the formation of dense plaques that restrict the spread of toxic plaque precursors, Aβ oligomers, throughout the brain [ ]. Thus, microglia play a decisive role in Aβ deposition.
Microglia, accounting for 10%-15% of the total glial cell population, are the pivotal cells responsible for mediating neuroinflammation [ ]. Functioning as monocyte-macrophages, they exhibit a bifurcated morphology under normal physiological conditions and serve as immune sentinels to uphold CNS homeostasis, constituting the brain’s primary defense mechanism against injury [ ]. Similarly to macrophages, metabolic reprogramming of microglia is closely associated with altered polarization. Microglia in the brains of AD mice exhibited enhanced glycolytic levels and diminished phagocytic capacity [ ]. During M1 polarization, there was an increase in lactate production accompanied by a decrease in ATP production, which reveal a metabolic shift from oxidative phosphorylation to aerobic glycolysis [ ]. In addition to demonstrating the anti-inflammatory properties of the M2 phenotype, microglia treated with IL-10 also exhibited a significantly diminished rate of glucose uptake [ ]. Moreover, knockdown of GLUT1 or using 2-DG to inhibit glucose uptake suppressed microglial activation and pro-inflammatory cytokine production [ ]. HK2, the rate-limiting enzyme in the glycolytic pathway, exhibits increased expression in microglia during AD pathogenesis. Moreover, targeted knockout or inhibition of HK2 specifically in microglia enhances microglial phagocytosis during AD treatment [ ]. Metabolic reprogramming of microglia through the utilization of metabolic regulators emerges as a promising therapeutic strategy for AD treatment [ ].
Previously, we found that OHP2, an orally administered GLP-1RA peptide capable of crossing the blood-brain barrier, demonstrated promising therapeutic efficacy against AD [ ]. OHP2 exhibited the ability to ameliorate abnormal neuroinflammation in the brains of AD transgenic mice. Interestingly, the expression of GLP-1R is predominantly localized on astrocytes while being scarcely detected on microglia. Astrocytes exhibit close communication with microglia, engaging in autocrine feedback and bidirectional signaling through the release of various molecules [ ]. The secretion of Interleukin-33 (IL-33) by astrocytes enhances microglial phagocytosis, facilitating synaptic pruning and neuronal maturation [ ]. Recent advancements in detection technology have revealed the presence of signals within the brain that were previously believed to be exclusive to the peripheral immune system. Interleukin 3 (IL-3) is conventionally believed to be secreted by peripheral T cells and NK cells; however, recent evidence has revealed its secretion by astrocytes in the brain, where it interacts with IL-3Rα (also known as CD123) on microglia. This regulatory interaction induces transcriptional, morphological, and functional reprogramming of microglia, leading to neuroprotective effects and amelioration of AD pathology [ ].
IL-27, a member of the IL-12 family referred to as “immune playmakers”, orchestrates the initial stages of Th1 cell differentiation and stimulates Treg cells to produce IL-10, which subsequently suppresses the Th17 response [ ]. Currently, clinical trials are underway for the IL-27 monoclonal antibody Casdozokitug against various cancers, including advanced solid tumors and non-small cell lung cancer [ ]. Moreover, IL-27 exerts direct effects on adipocytes by activating p38 MAPK-PGC-1α signaling, thereby enhancing thermogenesis and protecting against obesity [ ]. Notably, elevated levels of IL-27 have been observed in the brains of multiple sclerosis (MS) patients with astrocytes being identified as its primary source [ ]. In MS, intracerebral hemorrhage, and ischemia-reperfusion injury models, IL-27 has demonstrated neuroprotective properties [ ]. Additionally, through activation of microglial IL-27R, it plays an anti-inflammatory role in inhibiting retinal degeneration [ ]. While IL-27 is recognized as a pleiotropic cytokine with roles in immunometabolism [ , – ], its specific function, cellular source, and regulatory mechanisms within the AD brain remain largely unknown [ , , ].
In this study, we have demonstrated that OHP2 exerts anti-inflammatory effects in the treatment of AD by enhancing IL-27 secretion from astrocytes. The production of IL-27 by astrocytes triggers microglial reprogramming from M1 to M2 phenotype through the glycolysis/cGAS lactylation clock/mTOR pathway, thereby promoting migration and phagocytosis and ultimately ameliorating AD pathology.

## Materials and methods

## Preparation of OHP2
OHP2 was chemically synthesized by SynthBio (Hefei, China), following its amino acid sequence (HGEGTFTSDLSSQMEEEAVKEFIEWLVNGGPSSGAPPPSC). The chemical name of OHP2 is C156H168N38O43S2.

## Mice
6–8 weeks old male C57BL/6J mice were purchased from GemPharmatech (Nanjing, China). Mice were housed 3–5 per cage and maintained on a 12 h light/dark cycle at 22 °C with free access to standard mouse chow and autoclaved water. All experimental animal procedures were approved by the Animal Care and Use Committee of China Pharmaceutical University.

## Preparation of Aβ1−42 oligomers
Aβ1−42 was dissolved in hexafluoroisopropanol and sonicated for 10 min to ensure complete dissolution [ ]. The solution was incubated at 4 °C overnight. On the following day, the solvent was removed by nitrogen evaporation, followed by pre-freezing at -80 °C for 2 h and lyophilization to remove residual hexafluoroisopropanol. DMSO was added to reconstitute the peptide, which was then diluted with PBS. The solution was subsequently incubated at 4 °C for 24 h to promote oligomerization.

## Surgery
Prior to i.c.v. , mice were anesthetized with bromethol (Nanjing Aibei Biotech, China) and erythromycin eye ointment was applied to their eyes. The scalp was incised and a hole was drilled into the skull for slow injection of virus (at a rate of 0.5 µL/min) into lateral ventricles (AP: -0.5 mm; ML: -1 or + 1 mm from bregma; DV: -3.0 mm below the dura) with 5 nmol of Aβ1−42 oligomers. Following surgery, penicillin treatment was administered over the wound and mice were kept warm until they regained consciousness.

## LPS injection
Starting from the second day after IL-27 antibody administration, mice were intraperitoneally injected with LPS at a dose of 1 mg/kg daily for 4 consecutive days to establish an inflammatory model [ ]. The control group received the same volume of normal saline via the same route.

## Drug administration to animals
OHP2 was administered in a manner and dose as previously described [ ]. Mice were intragastrically administered OHP2 twice daily at doses of 1.06 mg/kg (0.25 µmol/kg) or Donepezil once daily at doses of 1.50 mg/kg (3.95 µmol/kg), or 2.12 mg/kg (0.50 µmol/kg). For the OHP2 group, all the administered doses were 2.12 mg/kg (0.50 µmol/kg), unless otherwise specified. Semaglutide (MW ~ 4114 g/mol) was subcutaneously injected once daily at doses of 0.04 mg/kg (9.72 nmol/kg) or 0.02 mg/kg (4.86 nmol/kg). OHP2 (MW ~ 4208 g/mol) was subcutaneously injected once daily at doses of 0.07 mg/kg (16.63 nmol/kg). The dose of Semaglutide in mice was calculated as equivalent to the maximum injectable formulation dose for clinical treatment of diabetic humans (1 mg/week), using the specific surface area method. IL-27 (SRP4187, Sigma-Aldrich, United States) was administered intraventricularly at a dose of 10 ng/µL (2 µL per side) every seven days. The IL-27 p28 antibody (BE0326, BioXCell, United States) was administered intraventricularly at a concentration of 1 µg/µL (2 µL per side). Groups that did not require drug administration received an equal volume of PBS. The Aβ model mice were administered the drug for 14 days, while the LPS model mice were administered the drug for 5 days.

## Extraction, culture, and isolation of primary astrocytes and microglia
The extraction and isolation of primary cells were carried out according to the method described by Gilulian et al. [ ].
The T25 bottles were coated with 0.01% poly-L-lysine. The cerebral cortex and hippocampus were dissected from neonatal mice within 24 h of birth, and the meninges and blood vessels were carefully removed. The tissues were finely minced and gently dissociated by pipetting 10 times. After standing for 2 min to allow debris to settle, the supernatant was collected and passed through a 200-mesh sieve. This procedure was repeated twice. The resulting single-cell suspension was resuspended in complete DMEM/F12 medium containing 10% FBS, and inoculated into the coated T25. The cells were incubated at 37 °C with 5% CO2. Three days after primary glial cell extraction, the medium was changed every two days. After 12–14 days of culture, the bottles were gently tapped to detach microglia, which were subsequently collected. The remaining adherent cells represented astrocytes.

## Establishment of the co-culture system
Primary microglial cells were resuspended and seeded at a density of 5 × 105 cells per well in 1000 µl of medium in the lower chamber of the Transwell insert. Primary astrocytes were seeded at a density of 2 × 104 cells per well in 200 µL of medium in the upper chamber. The co-culture system was then incubated overnight at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere containing 5% CO2.

## Drug administration to cells
Drug administration to cells was performed using DMEM (for BV2 cells) or DMEM/F12 (for primary cells) medium supplemented with 1% FBS. The drug concentrations were as follows: Aβ1−42 (5 µmol/L), OHP2 (5 µmol/L), IL-27 (5 ng/mL), LPS (100 ng/mL), and Anti-IL-27 p28 (1 µg/mL). Unless otherwise specified, the treatment duration was 24 h.

## Behavioral tests
To ensure unbiased data collection, all behavioral experiments were conducted by investigators blinded to treatment conditions. Testing sessions were performed during daylight hours (9:00–16:00) under controlled lighting conditions.

## Morris water maze (MWM) test
The MWM test assessed spatial learning and memory in mice using a modified protocol from Vorhees and Williams [ ]. One day before testing, mice were acclimated to a 110-cm circular water maze. The maze contained opacified water (19–22 °C), replaced regularly, and had a 10-cm platform submerged 1 cm below the surface in a random quadrant. During training, mice swam for 60 s to locate the hidden platform; those that failed were guided to the platform and allowed to remain on it for 30 s. Training occurred twice daily for 5 days. In the probe trial conducted 24 h after the last training session, the platform was removed to assess memory retention. Swimming activity of each mouse was recorded using an overhead video camera and automatically tracked via ANY-maze behavioral tracking software (Stoelting).

## Y-maze test
The Y-maze consists of three arms (30 cm × 8 cm × 16 cm) positioned at 120° angles, with geometric patterns as visual cues. Short-term spatial memory was evaluated via spontaneous alternation. The ANY-maze software recorded arm entry sequences and frequencies over 5 min. Spontaneous alternation involved consecutive entries into all three arms without repetition. The alternation rate was calculated as: (spontaneous alternations / possible alternations) × 100%.

## Novel object recognition (NOR) test
The NOR test spanned two days. On day one, mice interacted with two identical objects for 5 min each. Post-test, the box was sanitized with 75% alcohol to eliminate residual odors. On test day, mice explored one familiar and one novel object for 5 min each. Behavior was recorded via overhead camera and tracked using ANY-maze software. Preference was calculated as: Recognition Index (%) = (Time on novel object / Total time on both objects) × 100%.

## ELISA analysis
Frozen brain tissue was homogenized in PBS. ELISA analysis was conducted following the manufacturer’s instructions for each ELISA kit. Absorbance values were measured using a microplate reader within 30 min after the reaction termination. The Human Aβ1−40 ELISA Kit and Human Aβ1−42 ELISA Kit were purchased from Dakewei (Shenzhen, China). The Mouse IL-6, IL-1β, and TNF-α ELISA Kits were obtained from Abclonal (Wuhan, China).

## Quantitative real-time PCR
After treating cells for 24 h, total RNA was extracted from the cells. Cells were washed three times with RNase-free PBS. Subsequently, following the manufacturer’s instructions for the Allzyme kit, BB4, ethanol, CB4, and WB4 were sequentially added to extract RNA. The extracted RNA was reverse-transcribed using the Vazyme Biotech (Nanjing, China) reverse transcription kit. Finally, RT-qPCR was performed in a 10 µl reaction system. Primer sequences are listed in Table  .
 

## Scratch test
Microglial cells were inoculated in 6-well plates or 6-well Transwell chambers and cultured overnight. The following day, a consistent scratch was made using a 200 µL pipette tip to ensure uniform scratch areas across all wells. Subsequently, the designated treatment was administered, and bright-field microscopic images were captured at 0 h and 24 h post-treatment.

## Preparation of FITC-Aβ1−42
Weigh 2 mg of FITC powder and dissolve it in DMSO under light-protected conditions, ensuring complete dissolution through mixing. Gradually add the dissolved FITC solution to the Aβ1−42 oligomers solution while stirring gently, followed by thorough mixing. Incubate the mixture at 4 °C in the dark with gentle inversion for 8 h. On the following day, dialyze the reaction mixture against PBS using a 2000 Da molecular weight cutoff dialysis bag at 4 °C until no absorbance is detected at 495 nm in the dialysis buffer. Store the prepared FITC-Aβ1−42 at -20 °C in the dark for long-term use.

## Aβ phagocytosis test
Treat the cells with FITC-Aβ1−42 and the drugs. After 6 h, wash the cells three times with PBS, followed by incubation at room temperature with 10 mg/mL trypan blue solution for 5 min to quench surface-bound fluorescence. Discard the trypan blue solution and gently wash the cells three times with pre-chilled PBS. Subsequently, perform flow cytometry analysis or capture cellular fluorescence using a confocal microscope.

## Western blotting
Brain tissues or cells were lysed with RIPA buffer (Beyotime, China) containing protease and phosphatase inhibitor cocktails (Millipore, United States). Protein concentrations were measured by BCA assay (Beyotime) and normalized to the same concentration. Equal protein amounts were separated by SDS-PAGE, transferred to PVDF membranes (Millipore), and blocked with 5% non-fat milk in TBST for 2 h at room temperature. Membranes were incubated with primary antibodies overnight at 4 °C, followed by HRP-conjugated secondary antibodies. Protein bands were detected with an ECL kit (Vazyme) and imaged using a Tanon 5200 Multi system. Band intensities were quantified using ImageJ. The antibodies used are as follows: Rabbit monoclonal anti-Aβ (803015, 6E10, Biolegend), Rabbit monoclonal anti-TNF-α (A24214, Abclonal), Rabbit monoclonal anti-IL-1β (A22257, Abclonal), Rabbit monoclonal anti-ACTB (AC026, Abclonal), Rabbit monoclonal anti-IL-27 (sc-390482, Santa cruz, United States), Rabbit monoclonal anti-Pan-Kla (PTM-1410RM, PTM BIO, China), Rabbit monoclonal anti-cGAS (A8335, Abclonal), Rabbit monoclonal anti-p-TBK (AP1418, Abclonal), Rabbit monoclonal anti-TBK (A3458, Abclonal), Rabbit monoclonal anti-p-IRF3 (AP0623, Abclonal), Rabbit monoclonal anti-IRF3 (A2172, Abclonal), Rabbit monoclonal anti-p-mTORC1 (5536T, Cell Signaling Technology, United States), Rabbit monoclonal anti-mTORC1 (2983T, Cell Signaling Technology), Rabbit monoclonal anti-p-IκBα (82349-1-RR, Proteintech, China), Rabbit monoclonal anti-IκBα (80019-1-RR, Proteintech), Rabbit monoclonal anti-NFκB (80979-1-RR, Proteintech). All primary antibodies were used at a 1:1000 dilution.

## Transcriptome sequencing analysis
Whole-brain transcriptome: Brain tissues from AD mice treated with OHP2 at 6 months of age and aged to 8 months were collected for whole-brain transcriptome sequencing, which was performed by GENE DENOVO (Guangzhou, China). Astrocyte transcriptome: Primary astrocytes derived from AD mice were treated with OHP2 for 24 h, followed by the addition of Trizol reagent. Subsequent RNA sequencing was conducted by GENE DENOVO (Guangzhou, China). The RNA-seq data of Fig.  E was obtained from the National Center for Biotechnology Information (GSE5281). IL-27 FPKM values were derived from the count data and utilized to generate violin plots, illustrating the transcriptional disparities of IL-27 between healthy individuals and patients with AD across various brain regions.
 

## Immunofluorescence
After the animals were sacrificed, the brain tissues were fixed with a fixative for 5 days, followed by embedding, sectioning, staining, and scanning. The experiments were performed by Servicebio (Wuhan, China).

## L-lactate assay
The cultured cells were homogenized with the lysis buffer and centrifuged, after which the supernatant was collected. The lactate content was measured using the L-lactic acid assay kit (Abcam, United States), following the manufacturer’s instructions.

## Seahorse extracellular flux assay
The ECAR were measured using a Seahorse XFe 96 Analyzer (Agilent, United States). Cells were cultured in 6-well plates with Transwell inserts and treated as specified. The cells were incubated until immediately prior to Seahorse assays, after which they were collected. Following collection, 8,000–16,000 cells per well were seeded onto PLL-coated XFe96 cell culture microplates. All experiments were conducted in accordance with the reagent and instrument instructions. Data were analyzed using Seahorse XF96 Wave software.

## Quantification and statistical analysis
All data are expressed as mean ± standard error of the mean (SEM). Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism software (version 8.0) and the significance of differences was assessed by two-tailed unpaired Student’s t-test or one-way or two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukey’s multiple comparisons test. The statistical parameters can be found in the figures and figure legends. Significance was set as p < 0.05 and expressed as *p < 0.05, **p < 0.01, ***p < 0.001, #p < 0.05, ##p < 0.01, and ###p < 0.001.

## Results

## OHP2 is capable of mitigating the AD-like pathological alterations induced by Aβ accumulation
To investigate the effects of OHP2 on Aβ-induced AD-like lesions, we administered OHP2, Donepezil (the most widely used AD drug in clinical practice [ ]), and Semaglutide (a GLP-1 receptor agonist similar to OHP2, currently in phase III clinical trials for AD [ ], with mouse doses determined using the body surface area normalization method, based on the clinical treatment doses for diabetic patients) to Aβ- intracerebroventricularly injected mouse models (Fig. A). The results demonstrated that oral administration of OHP2 and donepezil or subcutaneous injection of OHP2 and semaglutide for 14 days did not significantly affect the body weight of i.c.v.-injected AD model mice (Figure S1A). This result is not surprising; while GLP-1 analogues typically reduce body weight in obese mice, this study was conducted using lean AD mice The Morris water maze experiment revealed that OHP2 significantly decreased the escape latency of model mice during the training phase (Fig. B&C, Figure S1B&C). Additionally, in the probe phase, OHP2 markedly increased the escape latency, the time spent in the target quadrant, and the number of crossings over the original platform location (Fig. D&E, Figure S1D). Further ELISA analysis of Aβ levels in the mouse brains demonstrated that OHP2 significantly reduced both Aβ1−42 and Aβ1−40 levels, particularly the more toxic Aβ1−42 (Fig. F-H). Additionally, OHP2 decreased tau phosphorylation in the model mice brains (Figure S1E&F). OHP2 also effectively attenuated the levels of inflammatory cytokines IL-6, IL-1β, and TNF-α in the brain (Fig. I-K). Moreover, the therapeutic efficacy of OHP2 for AD was superior to that of the control drugs donepezil and semaglutide, and comparable to the efficacy of a double dose of semaglutide.
 

## OHP2 promotes microglial phagocytosis and degradation of Aβ, which depends on co-culture with astrocytes
Through the analysis of the i.c.v. AD model, we found that OHP2 exhibits significant therapeutic effects on Aβ clearance and inflammation reduction. These effects are typically attributed to microglial activity. However, Evidence from both our other research and the literature converges on the conclusion that GLP-1RAs, including OHP2, mainly act through astrocytic GLP-1R [ , ]. We further explored whether and how OHP2 modulates microglial function via astrocyte-mediated mechanisms. Therefore, we established a primary microglia-primary astrocyte co-culture system and treated it with oligomeric Aβ1−42 (oAβ) and OHP2 (Fig. A). Aβ1−42 induced the polarization of microglia toward the pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype, while OHP2 promoted their transformation into the anti-inflammatory M2 phenotype. This effect was more pronounced in the co-culture model compared to microglia cultured alone (Fig. B, Primer sequences are listed in Table ). By assessing migration capacity, we observed that the wound confluence rate was significantly higher in the co-culture group than in the single-culture group, indicating that OHP2 enhances microglial migration more effectively under co-culture conditions (Fig. C&D). The phagocytic and degradative capacity for Aβ is also an essential criterion for evaluating microglial function [ ]. We determined that the peak time for Aβ phagocytosis and its near-complete degradation by microglia occurred at 6 h and 18 h, respectively (Figure S2A&B). Flow cytometry analysis after 6 h of FITC-oAβ treatment revealed that OHP2 significantly increased Aβ phagocytosis in the co-culture group compared to the single-culture microglia group (Fig. E&F). Furthermore, western blot analysis at 6 and 18 h post-treatment with oAβ demonstrated that OHP2 enhanced both Aβ phagocytosis and degradation by microglia, with this effect being more prominent in the co-culture system (Fig. G-I). In summary, these findings indicate that astrocytes play a crucial role in mediating the pharmacological effects of OHP2 on microglia.
 

## The effect of OHP2 on microglia is mediated by astrocyte secretions
Astrocytes and microglia in the brain exhibit extensive cross talk, primarily through secretory signaling and gap junction coupling [ ]. To further elucidate the mode of astrocyte-microglia communication mediated by OHP2, we treated primary astrocytes with oAβ and OHP2, then collected the conditioned medium from these astrocytes and applied it to microglia, defining this as the OHP2-ACM group. OHP2-ACM significantly reduced the mRNA levels of M1-specific transcript markers (Fig. A) while upregulating the mRNA levels of anti-inflammatory markers such as Il10, Arg1, Mrc1, and Chil3 (Fig. B). It is noteworthy that the more pronounced effect observed in the OHP2-ACM group relative to the OHP2 group further indicates that OHP2 plays a significant additive role in promoting microglial polarization through reprogramming the secretome of astrocytes. (Fig. A&B). Additionally, OHP2-ACM, but not OHP2, markedly enhanced the wound confluence rate of microglia, reflecting an improvement in their migratory capacity (Fig. C&D). Furthermore, OHP2-ACM increased the uptake of FITC-oAβ by microglia (Fig. E-G). Western blot analysis confirmed that OHP2-ACM significantly promoted both the uptake and degradation of oAβ by microglia (Fig. H-J). Collectively, these findings demonstrate that astrocyte-conditioned medium derived from OHP2-treated astrocytes recapitulates the effects of astrocytes on microglia observed in co-culture systems. Based on these observations, we speculate that OHP2-induced astrocyte-microglia communication is mediated via secreted factors.
 

## OHP2 significantly promotes IL-27 secretion in astrocytes
To identify the specific secretory factor through which OHP2 mediates astrocyte-microglia crosstalk, we performed transcriptome sequencing on brain tissues from AD mice treated with OHP2 and primary astrocytes derived from an AD model treated with OHP2. Notably, in the whole-brain RNA-seq analysis, differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in the OHP2-treated group compared to the AD model group were significantly enriched in KEGG pathways such as cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction and Th17 cell differentiation (Fig. A), suggesting that cytokines may serve as the key secretory factors associated with OHP2. Further analysis of DEGs in these pathways within the astrocyte RNA-seq data revealed that Il27 and Il27ra exhibited substantial changes (Fig. B), potentially identifying IL-27 as the cytokine of interest. IL-27 is a heterodimeric protein composed of two subunits, p28 and EBI3 [ ] (Fig. C). To validate this finding, we conducted qPCR experiments targeting highly variable or commonly implicated molecules in astrocyte-microglia communication. We observed that the transcriptional levels of both IL-27 subunits were significantly upregulated in the OHP2-treated group compared to the AD model group (Fig. D). To further elucidate the role of IL-27 in AD, we analyzed its differential expression in the hippocampus and entorhinal cortex of normal individuals versus AD patients using the GSE5281 dataset from the GEO database [ ]. Our results demonstrated that IL-27 levels were reduced in AD patients compared to healthy controls (Fig. E). Consistent with this observation, IL-27 levels were also significantly decreased in the brains of 5xFAD mice (Fig. F-I). Collectively, these findings suggest that OHP2 may exert its anti-AD effects by inducing astrocytes to secrete IL-27. Moreover, the significant reduction of IL-27 in AD highlights its potential as a key player in AD pathogenesis.

## IL-27 can augment microglial phagocytosis and degradation of Aβ, like OHP2
IL-27 has demonstrated protective effects in mouse models of ischemic stroke and intracerebral hemorrhage [ , ]; however, its role in AD remains unclear. To further investigate the role of IL-27 in AD, particularly its effects on microglia, we established a model involving lateral ventricle injection of Aβ1−42 and IL-27 administration to examine the impact of IL-27 on AD mice (Fig. A). In the novel object recognition test, we observed that the recognition index of mice in the IL-27 group was significantly elevated (Fig. B-C). Additionally, in the Y-maze test, the alternation rate of mice in the IL-27 group was markedly increased (Fig. D). These findings suggest that IL-27 enhances cognitive function in AD mice. Furthermore, IL-27 not only significantly reduces the levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines IL-6, IL-1β, and TNF-α in the brains of AD mice (Fig. E-G) but also decreases Iba1 expression (Fig. H&I), indicating an alleviation of Aβ-induced abnormal microglial activation and neuroinflammation. Further analysis of the BV2 cell line revealed that IL-27 downregulates M1-specific markers while upregulating M2-specific markers (Fig. J&K), suggesting that IL-27 promotes the polarization of Aβ-activated M1-type microglia into the M2 anti-inflammatory phenotype. Moreover, IL-27 reduces the production of inflammatory factors TNF-α and IL-1β in microglia (Fig. L-N) and enhances their migration capacity (Figure S3A&B) as well as phagocytic and degradation abilities for Aβ (Figure S3C&D, Fig. O-Q). Collectively, upon direct administration of IL-27 to microglia, we observed effects analogous to those elicited by OHP2 in the astrocyte-microglia system, implying that IL-27 may serve as a key mediator of the anti-AD effects induced by OHP2. In addition, IL-27 may possess considerable potential for the treatment of AD.
 

## Blocking IL-27 attenuates both metabolic changes and the therapeutic effect of OHP2 on AD
To further validate the critical role of IL-27 in the anti-AD effects of OHP2, we employed an IL-27 p28 antibody to antagonize IL-27 and examined its impact on the actions of OHP2 in AD mice and microglia. Aβ1−42 and Anti-IL-27 p28 were injected into the lateral ventricles of C57/BL6J mice, followed by daily oral administration of OHP2 at a dose of 2.12 mg/kg (Fig.  A). We observed that OHP2 significantly enhanced the recognition index in the novel object recognition test of the model mice, and this improvement could be abolished by the IL-27 p28 antibody (Fig.  B&C). In the Y-maze test, the alternation rate in the OHP2-treated group was significantly higher than that in the model group, whereas the Anti-IL-27 p28 group exhibited a significantly lower rate compared to the OHP2 group (Fig.  D). These findings indicate that OHP2 improves cognitive function in AD mice, and blocking IL-27 diminishes this effect. Additionally, Anti-IL-27 p28 could inhibit the OHP2-mediated reduction of inflammatory cytokines IL-6, TNF-α, and IL-1β in the brains of mice (Fig.  E-G), as well as the OHP2-induced improvement in Iba1 levels (Figure S4A&B). This suggests that the OHP2-mediated amelioration of microglial activation and neuroinflammation can be partially blocked by Anti-IL-27 p28. Furthermore, we investigated the effects of Anti-IL-27 p28 on OHP2 action in a co-cultured primary cell system. The results demonstrated that the OHP2-induced reduction in M1-specific markers and increase in M2-specific markers could be blocked by Anti-IL-27 p28 (Fig.  H&I). Moreover, Anti-IL-27 p28 could also inhibit the OHP2-mediated enhancement of microglial migration (Figure S5A&B), reduction of pro-inflammatory cytokines (Fig.  J-L), upregulation of phagocytic activity and degradation capacity for Aβ (Figure S5C&D, Fig.  M). To further confirm the importance of IL-27 in inflammation regulation, we employed the LPS model (Figure S6A). We found that OHP2 attenuated the inflammatory response in LPS-treated mice, and this effect was abolished upon blocking IL-27 (Figure S6B-F). Similarly, in LPS-induced inflammatory cell models, Anti-IL-27 p28 exacerbated the pro-inflammatory response (Figure S7A-E). Collectively, these results demonstrate that the OHP2-mediated improvement of microglial function through astrocytes can be blocked by Anti-IL-27 p28, thereby confirming that IL-27 is a key mediator in the OHP2-induced amelioration of AD via the astrocyte-microglia system.
 Microglia are one of the primary consumers of glucose, and their sugar metabolism, particularly glycolysis, is closely associated with their functional state and behavior [ ]. In our previous study, we demonstrated that OHP2, as a GLP-1RA, could regulate brain metabolism via astrocytes. This raises the question: can OHP2 or IL-27 modulate the metabolic activity of microglial cells? To address this, we analyzed the levels of metabolic enzymes in microglia within a co-culture system 24 h after treatment with Aβ and OHP2. Our results revealed that OHP2 (at 24 h) attenuated the Aβ-induced elevation in key glycolytic enzymes, including Hk2, Ldha, and Pkm. Furthermore, blocking IL-27 significantly diminished this effect. In contrast, neither OHP2 nor Anti-IL-27 p28 appeared to substantially influence Pfk, a critical enzyme in oxidative phosphorylation (Fig. N, Figure S8A&B). Using a cellular energy metabolism analyzer, we further investigated glycolytic metabolism in microglia and found that OHP2 effectively reduced both glycolytic activity and maximal glycolytic capacity (Fig. O-Q). Consistent findings were observed in the LPS model (Figure S9A-G). These results suggest that OHP2 may suppress excessive microglial activation by downregulating glycolysis, with IL-27 likely serving as an important metabolic regulator in this process.

## IL-27 and OHP2 exert their effects via the glycolysis/cGAS lactylation clock/mTOR pathway
Furthermore, we investigated how OHP2 and IL-27 modulate microglial behavior by regulating glycolytic metabolism. Glycolysis produces substantial amounts of lactate, and our findings indicate that the effect of IL-27 on L-lactate levels is time-dependent, aligning with the concept of the “lactate clock“ [ ]. In the first four hours, Aβ and IL-27 cause a continuous increase in lactate levels, likely due to microglial activation, enhanced phagocytic and migratory capabilities, and increased energy demands. Between 4 and 24 h, the Aβ group exhibited sustained increases in lactate levels, whereas the IL-27 group showed a decrease (Fig. A). This observation correlates with the lower energy consumption of M2-type microglia compared to M1-type microglia, consistent with our previous findings. Lactylation is positively correlated with lactate levels [ ], and we detected a dynamic change in lactylation (Pan-Kla) levels in the IL-27 group, showing an initial increase followed by a decrease (Fig. B-C). cGAS plays a critical role in regulating the transcriptional state of microglia associated with aging, contributing to inflammation, neurotoxicity, and memory impairment. Blocking the cGAS-STING signaling pathway represents a potential strategy for neurodegeneration [ ]. Notably, cGAS can be lactylation, leading to its inactivation [ ]. Based on these, we hypothesized that IL-27 promotes the polarization of microglia from M1 to M2 by regulating cGAS lactylation. Subsequent analysis revealed that IL-27 significantly increased cGAS lactylation levels at 4 h compared to total lactylation levels or cGAS levels in the Aβ group, followed by a marked decrease after 16 h (Fig. D-E). More intriguingly, while both lactate production and Pan-Kla increased in the Aβ1−42 group, cGAS lactylation remained unchanged. This further suggests that the regulation of cGAS lactylation is specifically induced by IL-27. Similar trends were observed in the LPS model (Figure S10A-D). Further investigation of downstream cGAS pathways demonstrated that IL-27 and OHP2 reduced the activity of P-TBK/TBK, P-IRF3/IRF3, P-mTORC1/mTORC1, P-IκBα/IκBα, and NF-κB (Fig. F, Figure S11A, S12A&B), facilitating the transition of microglia from a pro-inflammatory to an anti-inflammatory state. Moreover, the blockade of IL-27 or lactylation would result in an upregulation of these pathways (Fig. G, Figure S11B, S13A-D). We propose that IL-27 enhances the lactylation of cGAS in microglia during the early phase, inhibiting the cGAS-STING/mTOR/NF-κB pathway and promoting the conversion of pro-inflammatory microglia to anti-inflammatory microglia. Following this transformation, cGAS lactylation levels return to a lower baseline as overall lactate levels decline, preparing for subsequent activation. Collectively, these findings suggest that IL-27 acts as a time-dependent metabolic regulator, converting over-activated microglia into anti-inflammatory M2-type microglia via the glycolysis/cGAS lactylation clock/mTOR pathway (Fig. H).
 

## Discussion
Taken together, our study elucidates a novel mechanism by which the orally administered GLP-1RA peptide OHP2 alleviates neuroinflammation in AD through modulating astrocyte-microglia crosstalk. Despite the limited expression of GLP-1R on microglia, our findings demonstrate that OHP2 primarily exerts its anti-inflammatory effects via astrocyte-derived IL-27, which subsequently reprograms microglial polarization from the pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype to the neuroprotective M2 phenotype. This transition is mediated through the glycolysis/cGAS lactylation clock/mTOR pathway, thereby revealing an unrecognized metabolic-immune axis in AD pathogenesis.
Specifically, OHP2 exerts its anti-inflammatory effects by inducing astrocytes to secrete IL-27, which subsequently modulates microglial activity. Inhibiting IL-27 significantly impairs the anti-AD efficacy of OHP2. As a metabolic regulator, IL-27 can regulate glycolysis in microglia, leading to dynamic changes in cellular lactate levels. This process results in the lactylation of cGAS. Initially, increased glycolysis supports the energy demands associated with microglial activation. Over time, cGAS undergoes lactylation and becomes inactivated, thereby downregulating the cGAS-STING/mTOR/NF-κB pathway. This reprograms microglia into an M2 phenotype, promoting the release of anti-inflammatory factors and alleviating neuroinflammation in AD.
Our findings position IL-27 as a crucial anti-inflammatory mediator in the context of Aβ pathology, yet we acknowledge that IL-27 signaling is highly context-dependent and can exhibit both pro- and anti-inflammatory functions in different disease settings [ ]. The protective role observed in our study may be attributed to the unique immunometabolic microenvironment of the AD brain. Specifically, the OHP2-induced astrocytic IL-27 acts on microglia that are concurrently exposed to high levels of lactate, a byproduct of enhanced glycolysis. This metabolic stress, in concert with IL-27 signaling, may preferentially engage the novel cGAS lactylation/mTOR pathway we identified, thereby steering microglial responses toward a protective, anti-inflammatory phenotype and facilitating Aβ clearance. Moreover, this time-dependent transformation may promote a shift in microglial behavior, whereby microglia exhibit enhanced migration toward Aβ during the early stages and increased phagocytosis and degradation of Aβ in the later stages [ ]. This paradigm aligns with the concept that IL-27 can promote tissue homeostasis and resolve inflammation in non-infectious, chronic disease contexts [ ], as opposed to its role in potentiating T cell responses during acute infections [ ]. Furthermore, the predominant anti-inflammatory outcome likely depends on the target cell type—microglia in our case—which contrasts with its effects on neurons or T cells that might exacerbate damage under different conditions [ ]. Therefore, we propose that the metabolic reprogramming initiated by OHP2 creates a specific niche where astrocyte-derived IL-27 is funneled through a lactylation-dependent pathway in microglia, unveiling its protective potential in AD.
The involvement of the glycolysis/lactylation/mTOR pathway in microglial polarization further deepens our understanding of how metabolic reprogramming modulates neuroinflammation. Lactylation, a recently identified post-translational modification mediated by glycolytic flux [ ], plays a pivotal role in regulating cGAS activity and downstream inflammatory signaling pathways [ ]. By linking OHP2’s effects to this pathway, our study elucidates the mechanistic basis for the observed shift in microglial phenotypes and highlights the potential of targeting metabolic-inflammatory crosstalk as a therapeutic strategy for AD.
These findings have important translational implications. In addition, our findings indicate that IL-27 may hold potential as a therapeutic agent for AD. Future studies should investigate IL-27 as a potential therapeutic target or biomarker for AD. IL-27 has been shown to promote thermogenesis in adipocytes, suggesting its potential utility in obesity treatment [ ]. However, to date, neither IL-27 nor related compounds have advanced to clinical trials. Furthermore, the administration route and dosing frequency of IL-27 for AD therapy could pose challenges to its clinical development. Pharmaceutical research aimed at enhancing patient compliance and optimizing delivery methods may be necessary to overcome these limitations. Notably, emerging techniques such as intracranial stereotactic injection and mRNA-based therapies currently under clinical investigation might facilitate the development of IL-27-based treatments for AD.
It is noteworthy that OHP2 treatment also reduced tau hyperphosphorylation in our Aβ intracerebroventricularly injected mouse model. Given that Aβ pathology can trigger a downstream cascade leading to aberrant tau phosphorylation, the observed reduction in p-tau levels may stem from OHP2’s efficacy in attenuating the Aβ burden and associated neuroinflammation. Furthermore, we cannot exclude the possibility that OHP2 modulates tau phosphorylation more directly via astrocyte-neuron metabolic crosstalk, a potential mechanism that merits further investigation in future studies [ ].
In conclusion, our study not only reinforces the therapeutic potential of GLP-1RA peptide OHP2 in AD but also uncovers a novel astrocyte-microglia signaling axis involving IL-27 and metabolic reprogramming. These findings lay the foundation for further pharmacological investigations into OHP2 and underscore the broader implications of metabolic immunomodulation in neurodegenerative diseases. 

## Limitations of study
While this study establishes a functional link between the OHP2/IL-27 axis and cGAS lactylation, several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the pharmacological tools used—a neutralizing IL-27 antibody and the p300 inhibitor C646—have inherent constraints. The neutralization efficiency of the IL-27 antibody was partial, and C646, as a broad-spectrum lactylation inhibitor, may affect other protein targets and epigenetic modifications beyond cGAS. Future studies employing astrocyte-specific Il-27 knockout or microglia-specific Il-27ra knockout models would provide more definitive genetic evidence. Secondly, lactylation is not exclusively mediated by p300; for instance, AARS2 has also been reported to lactylate cGAS [ ]. Therefore, the precise regulatory mechanism by which IL-27 signaling promotes cGAS lactylation warrants further investigation. Ultimately, identifying the specific lactylation sites on cGAS modulated by this pathway and validating their functional necessity through site-directed mutagenesis will be crucial to fully elucidate the underlying molecular mechanism.
This study primarily evaluated the therapeutic potential of OHP2 and the associated IL-27/glycolysis/cGAS lactylation/mTOR signaling axis using acute models of AD, namely the Aβ-infusion and LPS-injection paradigms. While these models are valuable for investigating specific mechanisms related to Aβ pathology and late-stage inflammatory responses, they do not fully recapitulate the slow, progressive nature of the human disease. A key limitation is the lack of data on the intervention’s effects during the early phases of disease progression. Furthermore, the findings are derived from a focused set of neuropathological conditions, and their generalizability to other AD contexts remains to be established.
