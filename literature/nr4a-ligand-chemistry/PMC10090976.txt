## Abstract

## Introduction
Patients carrying interstitial deletions of the long arm of chromosome 9 show similar features. These phenotypes are often characterized by developmental delay, intellectual disability, short stature, and dysmorphism. Previously reported deletions differ in size and location spanning from 9q21 to 9q34 and were mostly detected by conventional cytogenetic techniques.

## Methods
Based on clinical features suggesting primarily chromosomal diseases, aCGH analysis was indicated. We report on de novo overlapping interstitial 9q deletions in 3 unrelated individuals presenting neurodevelopmental disorder and multiple congenital anomalies.

## Results
An 8.03-Mb (90 genes), a 15.71-Mb (193 genes), and a 15.81-Mb (203 genes) deletion were identified in 9q affecting 9q22.33q33.3. The overlapping region was 1.50 Mb, including 2 dosage-sensitive genes, namely EPB41L4B (OMIM #610340) and SVEP1 (OMIM #611691). These genes are thought to be involved in cellular adhesion, migration, and motility. The non-overlapping regions contain 24 dosage-sensitive genes.

## Conclusion
Besides the frequently described symptoms (developmental delay, intellectual disability, skeletal abnormalities, short stature, and dysmorphic facial features) shared by the patients with interstitial deletions of chromosome 9q reported thus far, two of our patients showed distinct forms of epilepsy, which were successfully treated, and one had a bilateral cleft lip and palate. Possible candidate genes for epilepsy and cleft lip and palate are discussed.

Keywords: Interstitial 9q deletion, Neurodevelopmental disorder, Multiple congenital anomalies, Array-CGH

## Introduction
Interstitial deletions of the long arm of chromosome 9 have been reported in the context of many types of cancer and congenital syndromes with variation in size and location [Midro et al., 2004; Gamerdinger et al., 2008; Kulharya et al., 2008; Siggberg et al., 2011].
While subtelomeric deletion of the long arm of chromosome 9 is associated with a specific clinical syndrome (Kleefstra syndrome-1; OMIM 610253), patients with interstitial deletions of 9q show a wide variety of symptoms [Kulharya et al., 2008]. Developmental delay, intellectual disability of varying severity, skeletal abnormalities, short stature, and dysmorphic facial features, including low hairline and short palpebral fissures, are presented in most of the patients with interstitial deletion of the long arm of chromosome 9 [Midro et al., 2004; Gamerdinger et al., 2008; Xu et al., 2013; Mucciolo et al., 2014; Ehret et al., 2015].
Initially, in the majority of reported cases chromosome banding was used for analysis of the observed deletion without additional molecular cytogenetic methods [Ying et al., 1982; Farrell et al., 1991; Park et al., 1991; Kroes et al., 1994]. Considering the similarity of banding patterns between 9q22q32 and 9q32q34.1, the distinction between published cases by the affected genomic region encounters difficulties just as the genotype-phenotype correlation [Gamerdinger et al., 2008]. Array comparative genomic hybridization (aCGH) analysis is the most rapid diagnostic tool and a powerful method for detection of pathogenic copy number alterations in the whole genome providing the most relevant information about the genomic segment involved and the gene content of the deleted region. Over the past decade, it has become the first-line standard test used in clinical cytogenetic evaluation, particularly in patients with intellectual disability and multiple congenital malformations [Miller et al., 2008]. One of the most important applications of high-density genome-wide arrays is the evaluation of patients with birth defects, dysmorphic features, and intellectual disability or developmental delay, especially in cases with negative family history for the given abnormality, which seems to indicate a de novo chromosomal aberration at any part of the genome.
In the present study, we report on 3 unrelated patients demonstrating several general and a few different clinical features with overlapping interstitial deletions of chromosome 9, involving 9q31.3.

## Case Presentations

## Patient 1
She is the first child of nonconsanguineous, healthy parents and was born at the 38th week of gestation. Because of advanced maternal age (37 years) prenatal karyotyping was performed, which showed a normal female karyotype. Her birth weight was 2,870 g (10th–25th percentile), her length was 48 cm (10th–25th percentile), and her head circumference was 34.5 cm (50th–75th percentile). A paternal half-sister died of metachromatic leukodystrophy at the age of 7 years. She has 2 healthy paternal half-brothers. Bilateral ptosis and dysmorphic features were observed from birth. She underwent surgery because of umbilical hernia at 2 months of age. Subsequently, hypothyroidism was recognized and thyroid hormone substitution was indicated until 8 month of age. Generalized epilepsy mainly with myoclonus seizures started at 4 months of age, electroencephalography (EEG) showed multifocal, generalized epileptic discharges. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the brain showed normal intracranial morphology. Her epilepsy was well controlled with levetiracetam monotherapy. Radiographic survey showed cubical vertebral bodies and C1-C2 vertebral cleft. Cardiology and audiology examinations were normal. Ophthalmology examination found astigmatism. Her developmental milestones were delayed. She was referred for genetic evaluation at the age of 6 years because of dysmorphic facial features, developmental delay, and generalized epilepsy. She had a low posterior hairline, downslanting palpebral fissures, hypertelorism, bilateral ptosis, broad and dense eyebrows, short philtrum, macrostomia, thick lips, and high-arched palate (Fig. ).
 During her follow-up, at the age of 17 years (Fig. ), she had mild intellectual disability and autistic behavior. She had short stature (height was 147 cm, <3rd percentile), her weight was 51.1 kg (10th–25th percentile), and her head circumference was 54 cm (25th percentile).

## Patient 2
She is the first child of a healthy, nonconsanguineous couple. She was born after an uneventful pregnancy at the 40th week of gestation. Family history was unremarkable. She was small for gestational age, birth weight was 2,280 g (<3rd percentile), length was 42 cm (<3rd percentile), and head circumference was 30 cm (<3rd percentile). As a neonate, she was referred for genetic evaluation because of dysmorphic features and bilateral cleft lip and palate. Her physical and neurological examination revealed severe generalized hypotonia, chest asymmetry, preaxial polysyndactyly on the right hand, low frontal hairline, hypertelorism, downslanting palpebral fissures, protruding ears with absent antihelix, broad nasal root, and prominent nose (Fig. –e). Chest X-ray showed developmental abnormalities of the ribs: she had 10 ribs on both sides and both of her fifth ribs were fork-shaped. Orthopedic examination revealed left-sided congenital hip dislocation and scoliosis. Her cleft lip and palate were closed in 2 steps; however, this did not solve her feeding difficulties. Cardiology, ophthalmology, and audiology examinations were normal. Subsequently, her severe global developmental delay became evident. Epilepsy has not been noted so far. By the age of 11 years, she had severe intellectual disability: she could not stand or walk alone, she could not say meaningful words, she had problems with chewing, and she needed full-day care, and stereotypic movements were also characteristic for her. At that time, her weight was 15 kg, her height was 116 cm, and her head circumference was 44 cm, all of them below the 3rd percentile.

## Patient 3
He is the first child of nonconsanguineous healthy parents. His family history was unremarkable. He was born at the 38th week of gestation with a birth weight of 3,070 g (25th–50th percentile), his length was 49 cm (3rd–10th percentile), and his head circumference 36 cm (90th–97th percentile). His postnatal adaptation was uneventful. After birth, generalized hypotonia and dysmorphic features were detected, and he was referred for genetic evaluation. He had relative macrocephaly, low frontal hairline, micrognathia, deep nasal bridge, short and downslanting palpebral fissures, deep-set ears, ankyloglossia, supernumerary nipple (on the left side), and glandular hypospadias (Fig. ). Cardiology examination revealed patent foramen ovale. He was hospitalized at the age of 4 months because of failure to thrive, increased sweating, and recurrent bronchitis. Abdominal ultrasound showed no abnormality, and chest X-ray raised the possibility of thymus asymmetry. Brain MRI showed no intracranial pathology. Subsequently his severe global developmental delay became evident. His first epileptic seizure occurred at the age of 9 months; short absence seizure with oral automatism, however EEG was normal at that time. At 15 months of age, his seizures became more frequent and EEG showed bilateral asymmetric periodic discharges; therefore, he was commenced on topiramate. He was admitted to hospital several times because of periodic vomiting, and epileptic dysfunction was confirmed by video-EEG examination in the background of his vomiting-related attacks. The best seizure control was achieved by valproate monotherapy. Audiology examination revealed auditory hearing loss.
At the age of 9 years (Fig. ), he had short stature (height 121 cm, <3rd percentile), his weight was 23.5 kg (3rd–10th percentile), and head circumference was 50 cm (3rd–10th percentile). At that time, he could not walk alone and could not say meaningful words. He was not able to drink or eat on his own and showed autistic behavior. His dysmorphic features were as follows: flat occipital region, prominent forehead with low frontal hairline, triangular face, downslanting palpebral fissures, long eyelashes, broad eyebrows, gingival hypertrophy and irregular teeth with enamel hypoplasia, pointed chin, and failure to thrive.

## Methods

## DNA Samples
Genomic DNA was extracted from peripheral leukocytes of the patients using E.Z.N.A. Blood DNA Maxi extraction kit (OMEGA®, Bio-tek, Inc., GA, USA). The concentration and purity of extracted DNAs were measured with the NanoDrop 2000 spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA).

## Karyotyping and Array Comparative Genomic Hybridization
Karyotyping of all patients and their parents was performed by Giemsa-Trypsin (GTG) banding from peripheral blood lymphocytes using standard procedures [Caspersson et al., 1970].
Array-CGH was performed on DNA samples obtained from Patient 1 using the Agilent Human Genome Unrestricted G3 ISCA v2 SurePrint 8×60K oligo-array (Amadid 031746, Agilent, Santa Clara, CA, USA). This microarray contains 18,851 60-mer oligo probes in ISCA regions + 40,208 backbone probes, which contain coding and noncoding genomic sequences with an average 60-kb overall median probe spacing, respectively. For Patients 2 and 3 an Agilent Human Genome G3 SurePrint 8×60K Microarray was used, a high resolution 60-mer oligonucleotide-based microarray containing 55,077 probes, spanning coding and noncoding genomic sequences with spacing of 41 kb and 33 kb, respectively.
Labeling and hybridization were accomplished with Agilent Oligonucleotide Array-Based CGH for Genomic DNA analysis - Enzymatic labeling protocol. Array images were acquired using an Agilent laser scanner G2565CA (Agilent) and analyzed with the Agilent Feature Extraction software (v10.10.1.1). Results were presented by Agilent Cytogenomics Software (v2.5.8.11). DNA sequence information refers to the public UCSC database (GRCh37/hg19). The detected deletions were aligned to known aberrations listed in public databases, such as DECIPHER (DatabasE of genomiC varIation and Phenotype in Humans using Ensembl Resources), DGV (Database of Genomic Variants), Ensembl and ECARUCA (European Cytogeneticists Association Register of Unbalanced Chromosome Aberrations).

## Results
The affected patients were evaluated and ascertained at the Department of Medical Genetics, University of Pecs. Phenotypic and dysmorphic features of the 3 examined patients and comparison with previously reported cases are summarized in Table .
 Karyotyping showed normal female and male karyotypes in the patients and their parents (only the mother of Patient 1, and both parents of Patient 2 were available for sampling). Parental array analyses proved to be negative. Array-CGH revealed an 8.03-Mb (chr9:105122085_113149157) deletion from 9q31.1 to 9q31.3 in Patient 1. The deleted region includes 90 genes. The deletion in Patient 2 was 15.71 Mb (chr9:111644635_127356988), spanning 9q31.3 to 9q33.3. The deleted chromosomal region contains 193 genes. In Patient 3, aCGH showed a deletion of 15.81 Mb (chr9:102589027_118400548) on the long arm of chromosome 9, involving cytogenetic bands 9q22.33 to 9q33.1 (Table ). This affected chromosomal region contains 203 genes. Deleted gene numbers were defined by ClinGen (https://clinicalgenome.org). No additional pathological copy number variations (CNVs) were identified. The different deletions, the overlapping region, and affected genes are illustrated in Figure .
 The shared overlapping region in these cases spans approximately 1.50 Mb (chr9:111644635_113149157) and contains 23 genes. Among these genes, 13 genes are protein-coding (Table ), only 2 of them were dosage-sensitive.
 The overlapping region of Patients 1 and 2 is in fact the shared overlapping region (Fig. ). The overlapping region of Patients 1 and 3 spans 6.52 Mb (chr9:105122085_111644635) and contains 68 genes, the overlapping deleted region of Patients 2 and 3 spans 5.25 Mb (chr9:113149157_118400548) and comprises 78 genes (Fig. ).
We found 24 dosage-sensitive genes in non-overlapping regions that are discussed in 4 sections (Fig. , A–D; Table ).
 

## Discussion and Conclusion
9q deletion syndrome can be considered as a neurodevelopmental disorder with non-neuronal symptoms affecting brain development and function and is characterized by wide genetic and clinical variability. The leading symptoms are intellectual disability, epilepsy, and autism spectrum disorder [Niemi et al., 2018]. Rare CNVs are known to be implicated across many neurodevelopmental disorders and contribute to their shared genetic etiology. Dosage alterations of CNV regions contribute predominantly to defects in central nervous system (CNS) development, but they also lead to early developmental features involving other organ systems [Zarrei et al., 2019].
In this study, 3 unrelated patients with overlapping interstitial deletions in the long arm of chromosome 9 were described, and previously published cases with interstitial deletions overlapping or at least partially overlapping the currently identified deletions were reviewed. The phenotypic features in our patients are similar to those of other patients with similar deletions reported in the literature (Table ). The general characteristic features compared to those in the previous literature are short stature, developmental delay, intellectual disability, hypotonia, low hairline, and dysmorphic facial features. However, it is important to note that the overall appearance of our cases shows different phenotypes compared to each other (Fig. ).
Array-CGH analyses highlighted that Patients 1, 2, and 3 have an 8.03-Mb, 15.71-Mb, and 15.81-Mb deletion, respectively on 9q with a 1.50-Mb overlapping chromosomal loss. The shared deleted region affects 23 genes (including 13 protein-coding and 2 dosage-sensitive genes) and comprises 9q31.3 in all 3 cases. Based on aCGH analyses of the parents, these deletions proved to be de novo. Since being relatively large in size and de novo, deletions were reported as pathogenic.
The overlapping region in our cases contains the following protein-coding genes: IKBKAP, FAM206A, CTNNAL1, TMEM245, FRRS1L, EPB41L4B, PTPN3, PALM2, AKAP2, C9orf152, TXN, TXNDC8, and SVEP1 (Table ). Genes, which are located in the overlapping deleted region, are characterized by various functions and are correlated with distinct diseases. Current literature and knowledge on the function of the affected genes in this region is either limited or is not in accordance with the evolved phenotypes (i.e., tumorigenesis, aberrant spermatogenesis, etc.). Several of these genes are correlated with autosomal recessive disorders, thus likely do not affect the phenotype in our cases with heterozygous deletions. For instance, the IKBKAP gene is located within the familial dysautonomia (DYS; OMIM 223900) candidate region in 9q31 [Chadwick et al., 1999; Slaugenhaupt et al., 2001], and FRRS1L is associated with autosomal recessive early infantile epileptic encephalopathy-37 (EIEE37; OMIM 616981) [Madeo et al., 2016; Shaheen et al., 2016]. However, the phenotypic trait could be explained in some cases by the “unmasking” phenomenon, when a deletion of one allele of a gene may also exert a phenotypic effect. This happens if, for the same locus as the deletion, a single nucleotide or CNV residing on the chromosome from the other healthy parent is transmitted. In a previous report, Poot and Haaf [2015] summarized published cases of inherited recessive mutations unmasked by hemizygosity due to a de novo deletion, regarding disorders such as Prader-Willi, Smith-Magenis, and Wolf-Hirschhorn syndrome.
There are only 2 dosage-sensitive genes in the overlapping region of our patients' 9q deletions. One of these is EPB41L4B (OMIM #610340; pLI score: 1.00) which has a role in adhesion, migration, and motility of the cells based on available in vitro experimental data [Bosanquet et al., 2013]. The other dosage-sensitive gene is SVEP1 (OMIM #611691; pLI score: 1.00); it is also involved in cell attachment processes. Most of the data on gene function were obtained in a mouse model [Shur et al., 2006] where SVEP1 was localized in the cell membrane and cytoplasm of stromal osteogenic cells. The gene plays a decisive role in embryonic development and differentiation (proved by homozygous knockout embryos showing multiple organ defects). It is involved in regulation of the bone homeostasis by influencing the interaction of the tissue microenvironment and osteoblasts, which is crucial in many tissue functions such as embryonic development, bone morphogenesis, and remodeling [Glait-Santar et al., 2012]. The regulation of SVEP1 expression is relatively well known, but little is known about the effect of heterozygous deletion on the human phenotype.
The skeletal system is affected in the vast majority of cases with 9q interstitial deletion in various ways [Kulharya et al., 2008]. In formerly reported cases, mainly the vertebrae, ribs, skull, and fingers exhibited deformities (Table ). The most frequently described symptoms are cervical or bifid ribs, scoliosis, gibbus of the cervico-thoracic region, spina bifida occulta, and small clavicle [Shimkets et al., 1996; Chen et al., 2006; Mucciolo et al., 2014]. As far as fingers and hand malformations are concerned, postaxial polydactyly, distal digital hypoplasia, syndactyly, clinodactyly, and camptodactyly were observed [Kroes et al., 1994; Chen et al., 2006; Kulharya et al., 2008; Dugan et al., 2018]. Moreover, Kroes et al. [1994] reported on a case with 9q22q32 deletion, where X-ray examination showed marked delay in skeletal age of the patient. Different kinds of abnormal skeletal structures were observed in all 3 patients reported here. The cervical vertebrae (C1-C2) are affected with cleft and fusion of vertebrae, in Patient 1 and Patient 3, respectively. Physical examination and X-ray of Patient 2 revealed chest asymmetry, developmental abnormalities of the ribs, and congenital hip dislocation. Additionally, Patient 2 had preaxial polysyndactyly (Fig. , e).
Although the FRRS1L gene in the shared region is not dosage sensitive, it would be important to discuss its role in the function of CNS. In AMPA-type glutamate receptor-mediated rapid excitatory neurotransmission, which is essential for normal brain function, this gene plays a significant role whereas several studies have confirmed the pathogenic role of homozygous alleles in autosomal recessive intellectual disability families [Rauch et al., 2012; Madeo et al., 2016]. One study [Brechet et al., 2017] described an observation of knockdown effects on AMPA receptors caused by decreased FRRS1L protein level via a lentivirus-induced deletion of the gene performed in rat hippocampal neurons. The manipulation decreased the number of AMPA receptors in synapses and extrasynaptic sites.
We could identify a further non-dosage-sensitive gene in this region that may play a role in the development of CNS. The FAM206A gene encodes an actin-binding protein, called Simiate, which acts as a direct link between transcription regulation and dendritogenesis. Simiate is involved in filopodia motility and arborization of developing hippocampal neurons [Derlig et al., 2014]. Interestingly, these informations have revealed that the deleted shared region contains genes that are not dosage sensitive, but nonetheless play a role in development and function of CNS and may be associated with symptoms such as intellectual disability, developmental delay, epilepsy. These phenotypic features may occur with or without any brain MRI findings.
A review of the genes deleted in the overlapping region in the present study demonstrates that it is a hard challenge to determine the effective gene(s) behind the clinical feature because of limited information and the possible multiple gene interactions even if the precise breakpoints of deletions are determined. Thus, no such critical locus has been established behind the phenotype, in contrast with the subtelomeric 9q deletions where one single gene (EHMT1) was associated with the disease (Kleefstra syndrome-1; OMIM 610253) [Kulharya et al., 2008].
Since the overall appearance of our patients exhibited to some extent different phenotypes compared to each other (Fig. ), we focused on the gene content in the non-overlapping regions. We could identify 24 dosage-sensitive genes in these regions (Table ). The gene products are related to some cellular processes like cell division (SMC2, RABGAP1, BRINP1, and PRPF4), cell differentiation (LHX2 and KLF4), or inhibitors of cell growth (DAB2IP and STRBP). Three genes encode a component playing an important role in protein degradation through the ubiquitination-proteasome system (ERP44, RAD23B, and RC3H2).
Furthermore, we would like to emphasize the importance of genes presumably affecting the manifestation and/or severity of symptoms like intellectual disability, behavioral features, and epilepsy. A good example of this is that Patients 1, 2, and 3 from this study are characterized by mild, profound, and severe intellectual disability, respectively. To evaluate intellectual functions and learning disabilities, patients were assessed by psychological tests appropriate for their age: Stanford Binet Intelligence Scale (SB), Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children (WISC).
Among the genes that are important in the development of the nervous system is LHX6. Its product is the LIM homeodomain factor Lhx6 which takes part in the regulation of many other genes' expression that influence the migration of GABAergic neurons to the cerebral cortex during its development. The role of Lhx6 is also known in the specification of interneuron subtypes in the cerebral cortex [Lavdas et al., 1999; Zhang et al., 2013]. A further gene, MEGF9, encodes a protein containing EGF-like domains, and it is expressed primarily in the cerebellum. Based on its domain content, it is most likely involved in cell adhesion and receptor-ligand interactions [Nakayama et al., 1998]. NR4A3 is highly expressed in the fetal brain, but the NOR1 protein is barely detectable in adults [Ohkura et al., 1998]. In rat forebrain cells, the inhibition of gene expression had a positive effect on migration and neurite extension of neuronal cells, clearly indicating thereby the relationship of the gene to brain development at fetal age [Ohkura et al., 1996]. The fourth dosage-sensitive gene related to neural development is LHX2 that encodes a transcriptional activator of the alpha-glycoprotein gene. Mangale et al. [2008] determined that it functions as a selector gene in the initial phase of the process of cortical neuroepithelium development from stem cells. The last gene is ASTN2, being dosage-sensitive based only on the pHI score, what is not supported by the pLI score (Table ). In several studies where CNVs involving the ASTN2 gene have been investigated, the authors observed some kind of neurodevelopmental disorders like autism spectrum disorder, attention deficit hyperactivity disorder, seizures, besides delayed speech development, intellectual disability, and dysmorphic features [Vrijenhoek et al., 2008; Lionel et al., 2011, 2014; Husson et al., 2020].
Only a few previous studies reported that interstitial deletion of 9q was associated with epileptic disorders in some patients [Chen et al., 2006; Kulharya et al., 2008; Yamamoto et al., 2009; Tos et al., 2014]. Chen et al. [2006] reported on a patient with 9q22.32q31.3 deletion who had epileptogenic activity on EEG. Tos et al. [2014] described a female patient with interstitial deletion of 9q32q34.1 who showed seizures. Those patients had no overlapping region of deletion on 9q. In our cases, 2 patients (Patient 1 and Patient 3) demonstrated epileptic seizures, but interestingly Patient 2 with the most severe intellectual disability had no epilepsy. In a study, Yamamoto et al. [2009] reported 2 epileptic patients with 9q deletions. Patient 2 from their study and Patient 1 and Patient 3 from our study show overlap in the 9q31.1 region.
Studying the 9q31.1 region, we found the SLC44A1 gene that is involved in the deletion of our Patients 1 and 3. The gene's product is the choline transporter CTL1. Because of the disruption of choline transport, the production of cell membranes, acetylcholine and myelin are affected. In addition, neurological damage can occur with epilepsy [Blusztajn et al., 2017].
Summarized, based on data from previous authors, patients with 9q interstitial deletion show similarities in their clinical features, however, we observed a bilateral cleft lip and palate in our Patient 2, which is a relatively rare symptom [Yamamoto et al., 2009; Chien et al., 2010; Dugan et al., 2018]. In a follow-up association study, Letra et al. [2010] genotyped 50 SNPs spanning 339 genes present in region 9q22.3q34.1 in different populations, which previously suggested harboring cleft susceptibility genes. Their observations demonstrated that STOM is a possible new candidate gene as contributor for cleft lip/palate. The STOM gene encodes a membrane protein called stomatin [Zhang et al., 1999, 2001]. The function of this protein remains unclear despite the association with different diseases (anemia, cancer, and kidney failure). In humans, the absence of stomatin is associated with hereditary stomatocytosis, the lower expression of the mentioned protein leading to an increase in the basal rate of glucose transport [Zhang et al., 2001; Yokoyama et al., 2006]. Furthermore, pregnant women with diabetes are at an elevated risk for having offspring with oral-facial clefts and neural tube defects, and extreme exposure to glucose has been presumed to play a role in the pathogenesis of nonsyndromic cleft lip/palate [Spilson et al., 2001; Krapels et al., 2004]. However, STOM is not a dosage-sensitive gene, the 15.71-Mb deletion in Patient 2 (current study) also contains the STOM gene.
Based on our findings we can conclude that the most frequently identified features of 9q31.3 deletion syndrome are developmental delay, intellectual disability, hypotonia, short stature, dysmorphic features, and skeletal abnormalities. In addition, rare symptoms may occur in individual patients.
In conclusion, this is the first report describing 3 patients with overlapping interstitial deletions in chromosome 9q with additional rarely published features, including epilepsy and bilateral cleft lip and palate. On the one hand, this study conduces to a more accurately established genotype-phenotype relationship with detailed clinical reports, comparison with previously reported cases, and discussion of the role of individual genes involved in development of “9q interstitial deletion syndrome.” On the other hand, it is important to note, that previous results and our study are in line with a multigene model for CNV disorders, as opposed to models where individual causative genes are responsible for specific phenotypes. Additional reports of further investigations with overlapping deletions and detailed clinical descriptions are needed to define a better genotype-phenotype correlation.
