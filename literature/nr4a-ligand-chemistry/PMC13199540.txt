## Abstract

## Background
Radiotherapy resistance in lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) complicates treatment and worsens outcomes. Growing evidence indicates that cancer stemness contributes to radioresistance and poor prognosis. This study aimed to develop a prognostic model based on radioresistant stemness genes and to identify candidate therapeutics.

## Methods
Machine-learning methods were used to identify key radioresistant stemness genes (RRSKGs) and construct a prognostic risk score, the Radioresistant Stemness Risk Prognostic Score (RRSRPS). Its prognostic value was evaluated by Cox regression. PLK1 was prioritized for mechanistic analysis. Single-cell RNA sequencing and immune deconvolution characterized PLK1’s cellular distribution and associations with immune infiltration. Molecular docking and flow cytometry examined the relationship between cisplatin and PLK1. Clonogenic assays and xenograft models assessed whether cisplatin enhances radiosensitivity.

## Results
RRSRPS was an independent prognostic factor. PLK1 was identified as the target through screening. Knockdown of PLK1 significantly suppressed cell viability and enhanced radiosensitivity, increasing apoptosis by 40.06 ± 1.524% versus control. Mechanistic studies showed PLK1 acts via the JAK-STAT3 pathway, confirmed by rescue experiments. Drug sensitivity analysis and molecular docking identified cisplatin as a specific PLK1 inhibitor. Clonogenic and in vivo assays demonstrated that cisplatin combined with radiotherapy synergistically inhibited tumor growth, reducing tumor volume by 697 ± 41.81 mm³ compared to control.

## Conclusions
RRSRPS is a robust, independent prognostic indicator in LUAD. Cisplatin increases radiosensitivity, potentially via PLK1-related mechanisms, offering a feasible precision-medicine strategy for patients with radioresistant disease.

## Introduction
 Lung cancer is one of the most prevalent malignant neoplasms, with a global incidence of 11.4% and a mortality rate of 18% [ ]. Non-small cell lung cancer is the predominant form of lung cancer, representing 80–85% of lung cancer cases, with lung adenocarcinoma accounting for approximately 40% of those cases [ , ]. Approximately 60% to 70% of LUAD of patients require radiotherapy; however, cancer cells often develop resistance to radiation during treatment, ultimately leading to treatment failure, recurrence, and distant metastasis [ , ]. Therefore, elucidating the molecular mechanisms underlying radioresistance and developing prognostic markers with high predictive accuracy are critical goals.
Recent evidence has demonstrated that cancer radioresistance is primarily attributable to cancer stem cells [ , ]. This resilience can be ascribed to their adeptness at repairing DNA damage, scavenging reactive oxygen species (ROS), and entering prolonged periods of quiescence under unfavorable conditions [ ]. Additionally, these cells activate cell-cycle checkpoints that delay cell-cycle transitions, thereby providing extended opportunities for DNA repair [ ]. Moreover, the cancer stem-cell microenvironment facilitates evasion of radiation-induced damage [ ]. These cells often exist in a hypoxic state, which reduces radiation-induced ROS production during radiotherapy. Given the pivotal role of cancer stem cells in conferring resistance to radiotherapy, we focused on cancer stem cells and associated genes in lung adenocarcinoma to investigate the mechanisms underlying radioresistance and to identify prognostic markers for this malignancy.
In this study, we performed an initial screening of cancer stemness genes and genes related to radiotherapy resistance. Subsequently, we intersected the two gene sets and applied machine-learning algorithms to the resulting 63 genes to prioritize key candidates. A prognostic model was then constructed and evaluated for independent prognostic value. Finally, we prioritized PLK1 for downstream analysis by intersecting radioresistance hub genes with RRSRGs. We characterized PLK1 distribution across diverse cell populations using a LUAD single-cell RNA-seq dataset. Given the crucial role of PLK1, we evaluated its druggability and screened candidate small-molecule compounds.

## Materials and methods

## Outline of the present study
The general flow of the study is depicted in Fig.  . Following the analysis of the RRSKGs, a prognostic model was constructed. Furthermore, the sensitivity of the RRSRPM was evaluated.
 

## Screening of radiotherapy resistance genes and selection of external validation gene sets
The radiotherapy-related genes obtained in this study were all derived from previously reported literature [ ]. In this research, we classified TCGA-LUAD patients into the radiation-sensitive (RS) group and the radiation-resistant (RR) group based on clinical data. A total of 1121 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified between the RS and RR groups. Subsequently, the GSE72094 dataset was utilized as the external validation cohort to assess the accuracy and reliability of the risk model and subsequent predictive model.

## Single-cell sequencing analysis
In this study, the GSE143423 lung adenocarcinoma single-cell sequencing dataset was utilized. The single-cell sequencing results presented in this study were obtained from the website TISCH (http://tisch.comp-genomics.org/).

## WGCNA screen for tumor stem genes

The settings for the WGCNA (Weighted correlation network analysis) R package and parameters for screening cancer stemness genes were established according to previously published articles [ ]. Briefly, we incorporated the differentially expressed genes into the WGCNA algorithm and utilized mRNAsi as the feature for conducting the scale-free network analysis.

## Identification of radiotherapy resistance hub genes
The 63 genes obtained were imported into the STRING database to construct the PPI (protein-protein interaction) network. Subsequently, Cytoscape software was used to screen the hub genes [ ].

## Application of machine learning algorithms, cross-validation, and permutation testing
Several machine learning algorithms were used in this study to screen and identify key genes. These algorithms consisted of SVM, KNN, DT, and Logistic Regression. The screening results were evaluated using the confusion matrix and ROC curve to achieve optimal accuracy. Relevant R packages, including caret, DALEX, ggplot2, kernlab, and pROC, were utilized for this purpose.A stratified 70/30 train–test split was used for model evaluation. Within the training set, we performed stratified five-fold cross-validation. For models requiring scaling (SVM/KNN/Logistic), standardization was fitted only on the training subset of each fold and applied to the validation subset to prevent information leakage. Fold-wise AUC values were summarized as mean ± SD and min–max. To further assess robustness, a label permutation test (n = 150) was performed to generate a null AUC distribution; the permutation p-value was computed as the fraction of permuted AUC values ≥ the observed AUC.

## Small molecule compound screening
Small molecule compounds were selected based on risk genes for radioresistance and stemness. The CellMiner database (https://discover.nci.nih.gov/cellminer/home.do) was used to download data on FDA (Food and Drug Administration)-approved drugs, and drugs related to radiotherapy resistance stem risk genes were calculated using the R package “impute”. A correlation map was then generated. NetworkAnalyst (https://www.networkanalyst.ca/NetworkAnalyst/home.xhtml) and the Comparative Toxicogenomics Database (https://ctdbase.org/) were used to screen for small molecule compounds related to PLK1.

## Ki67 staining
Please refer to previously published literature for detailed procedures and related antibodies [ ]. Twelve hours after cell plating, cells were fixed with paraformaldehyde, followed by a blocking treatment with 5% BSA in PBS for one hour. Subsequently, the Ki67 antibody (Abclonal, A20018) was applied and incubated overnight. Finally, secondary antibodies were added and allowed to incubate for one hour at room temperature.

## Cell activity assay
Cells from different treatment groups were trypsinized, resuspended in complete medium, and the cell concentration was adjusted to 20,000 cells/mL. The resulting cell suspension from each treatment group was seeded into 96-well cell culture plates at a volume of 100 µL per well, with five replicate wells for each group. After a 12-hour incubation period, 10 µL of CCK8 reagent (MCE, HY-K0301) was added. Following an additional two hours of incubation in a cell culture incubator, the absorbance at 450 nm was determined using a microplate reader.

## Flow cytometry
We applied flow cytometry to examine the influence of cisplatin and PLK1 knockdown on the apoptosis of lung adenocarcinoma cells. The procedure followed the manual of the Annexin V-FITC Apoptosis Detection Kit (Beyotime, C1062M). Finally, the detection was performed using a flow cytometer (BD, C6-plus).

## Xenograft tumor construction and radiotherapy
For xenograft tumor construction and radiation therapy, please refer to previously published literature [ , ]. In brief, three-week-old male nude mice were obtained from Huachuang Sino Company. Cells from different treatment groups were digested, collected, counted, and prepared at a concentration of 5 × 106 cells per mouse for subcutaneous injection into the dorsal region of the mice. The tumors were allowed to develop for 24 days before being excised, weighed, and measured in terms of volume (volume calculation formula: V = 0.5 × length × width × height). Once the tumor volume reached 200 mm³, the mice were subjected to four sessions of 4 Gy radiotherapy. Additionally, during the radiotherapy procedure, lead plates were utilized to protect other body parts, with the exception of the tumor. Xenografts that developed ulcers were excluded from the study. During this period, the experimental group of mice was intraperitoneally injected with cisplatin (Selleck; S1166) at a dose of 5 mg/kg every four days until the end of the experiment. In this animal study, a fractionated radiotherapy regimen was employed to treat the mice. Radiation therapy was delivered to each mouse for a duration of two minutes on the second day following cisplatin administration. Finally, all the animals involved in the experiment were euthanized with isoflurane under the supervision and guidance of the Animal Welfare and Ethics Committee of Nantong University.

## Cell culture
The human lung adenocarcinoma cell line A549 was obtained from the Cell Bank of Type Culture Collection (Chinese Academy of Sciences, Shanghai Institute of Cell Biology, Shanghai, P. R. China). The cell line was cultured in RPMI 1640 medium (GIBCO, Gaithersburg, MD, USA) supplemented with 12% fetal bovine serum (GIBCO).

## Statistics
Statistical analyses were performed using R(4.3) software and GraphPad Prism(8.0). Data are presented as mean ± standard deviation (SD). Student’s t-test was used for comparisons between two groups, while One-way or Two-way ANOVA was employed for multiple group comparisons. For bioinformatics, differential expression analysis and heatmaps were generated using the “limma” and “pheatmap” R packages, with filtering criteria of |LogFC|≥2. A p-value < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

## Results

## To screen the radioresistant tumors stemness genes
Differential gene analysis of expression data from the TCGA database (Fig.  A) was performed using the limma R package. A total of 6778 genes displaying differential expression were identified and subjected to scale-free network analysis, utilising the WGCNA R package. This analysis involved setting beta to 4 (Fig.  B), followed by merging of similar modules with height set to 0.3. The resulting modules were plotted as a cluster tree (Fig.  C and D). We obtained 13 modules that were associated with mRNAsi and EREG-mRNAsi(Fig.  E). The stemness genes pertaining to tumour were extracted from the blue modules and analyzed (GS: 0.25 and MM༚0.25), resulting in the identification of 880 tumour stemness genes.
 We analysed the distribution of cancer stemness in pathological parameters. Our findings showed that mRNAsi levels in tumour tissues were notably higher than in paracancerous tissues (p = 4.508e-30, Fig.  F). There were also noteworthy differences regarding age, gender, N, STAGE, and T parameters(Fig.  G-K). Finally, we conducted an intersection analysis of genes associated with cancer stemness and radiotherapy resistance (Figure S1A). A total of 63 genes were identified. The protein interaction network of these genes were under investigation. According to FigureS B and C, these genes were categorised into two main subgroups, and NEK2, TPX2, AURKA, BUB1B, PLK1 and PRM2 were identified as the hub genes by analysing them through cytoscape software.

## Machine learning to screen radioresistant tumor stemness key genes
To objectively identify critical radioresistance genes associated with tumor stemness, the expression matrix of the aforementioned 63 genes was extracted. Four machine learning algorithms (SVM, KNN, DT, and Logistic Regression) were utilized to screen for RRSKGs. The top 20 genes from each algorithm were then ranked (Fig.  A–D). The predictive performance of these models was evaluated using the pROC package, while the confusion matrix was used to assess classification errors (Fig.  E–I). Specifically, the SVM model exhibited the lowest error rate. The misclassification ratios for the other algorithms were 0:3 (KNN), 1:6 (DT), and 1:4 (Logistic Regression). The AUC values for these algorithms were 0.999 for SVM, 0.999 for KNN, 0.997 for Logistic Regression, and 0.952 for DT. Following a comprehensive evaluation of the confusion matrices and ROC curves, the SVM algorithm was identified as the optimal method for screening radioresistance-associated genes. Given the near-perfect AUC values, we additionally evaluated model robustness to address potential overfitting. Specifically, we performed stratified five-fold cross-validation within the training set and conducted a label permutation test (n = 150) as a sensitivity analysis. The SVM model remained stable across folds and markedly outperformed permuted-label baselines, supporting that the observed performance is unlikely due to chance (Figure S2A–C; Table  ).
 

## To construct a radioresistant tumor stemness genes risk prognostic model
The prognosis is often unfavourable for patients with radiotherapy resistance; thus, construction of an accurate prognostic model is imperative.First, we randomly split the TCGA samples into a validation set and a training set for subsequent calculation and validation. Then employing the previously mentioned RRSKGs, we have created such a model (Fig.  J and K).Five genes - PKP2, PLK1, DARS2, EZH2, and PPP1R14B - were identified. Additionally, OS curves based on RRSRPS indicated that the subgroups with higher RRSRPS exhibit a significantly worse overall prognosis in both the training and validation cohorts. (Fig.  L and M). Subsequently, the prognostic accuracy of RRSRPS was evaluated using receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis in both the validation and test sets, as illustrated in Fig.  N and O.Considering the efficacy of risk stratification by RRSRPS, we aimed to investigate its independent prognostic value. The results of univariate COX regression analysis demonstrated a significant association between RRSRPS and OS (p < 0.001), with a higher hazard ratio (9.718) compared to other parameters, suggesting its superiority as a prognostic factor (Fig.  P). Similar outcomes were obtained from multivariable COX regression analyses. Figure  Q illustrated that the p-value between RRSRPS and OS remains less than 0.001.Meanwhile, the validation dataset also yielded statistically significant analysis results (Fig.  R and S).
For a more comprehensive and objective analysis of RRSRPS, we investigated their distribution in pathological parameters. The results revealed significant differences in RRSRPS distribution in terms of gender, stage, and T parameter (Fig.  A). We proceeded to conduct a sequential analysis of RRSRPS distribution within these parameters (Fig.  B-D).
 We finally investigated the expression and prognosis of RRSRGs. All RRSRGs displayed higher expression levels in tumour tissue (Fig.  A-E), and only DARS2 and PLK1 exhibited a prognostic effect on patient OS. The OS curves of remaining RRSRGs were not statistically distinct (Fig.  F-J).
 

## Immunoinfiltration and Immunotherapy analysis of PLK1
To investigate the involvement of RRSRGs in lung adenocarcinoma in more detail, we intersected RRSRGs and radioresistant stemness hub genes for further analysis. PLK1 emerged as a key candidate following screening and subsequent evaluation of its expression in cellular subsets (Fig.  A). As depicted in Fig.  B and C, PLK1 expression was significantly higher in CD8 + T cells, indicating its potential role in tumor immunity.To rigorously validate the cell-type-specific expression of PLK1, we extended our analysis to four independent single-cell RNA sequencing datasets (GSE162498, GSE139555, GSE153935, and GSE99254). As shown in the UMAP clustering and violin plots across all four cohorts (Figure S3A–D), PLK1 expression exhibited a striking and consistent specificity: it was almost exclusively enriched in the proliferating T cell subpopulation (“Tprolif”). In sharp contrast, negligible expression was observed in other major immune and stromal lineages, including resting CD4+/CD8+ T cells, B cells, macrophages, dendritic cells, and endothelial cells. This robust consistency across multiple datasets strongly supports the notion that PLK1 is intrinsic to the high-proliferative state of T cells within the tumor microenvironment, rather than being a generalized immune marker.
 Consequently, the correlation between immune infiltration and immune checkpoint was investigated based on the expression level of PLK1 in different subgroups. The analysis of immune infiltration revealed that the tumour purity was higher in the subgroup with high expression of PLK1 (Fig.  A). Activated CD4 memory T cells, M0 and M1 macrophages, resting NK cells, and activated mast cells were positively correlated with PLK1 expression. CD8 T cells and follicular helper T cells were also positively correlated with PLK1 expression. On the other hand, activated dendritic cells, plasma cells, activated NK cells, monocytes, resting CD4 memory T cells, resting dendritic cells, and resting mast cells were negatively correlated with PLK1 expression. No correlation was found with other cell types (Fig.  B-D).
 Immune checkpoints are crucial to tumor immunity. Hence, we examined the association between PLK1 expression and diverse immune checkpoints. According to Fig.  E and F, LAG3,VTCN1,CD276 and TNFRSF9 exhibited a stronger correlation with PLK1 expression. Because of PLK1 high-level expression in immune cells, we probed if distinct PLK1 expression subgroups would gain advantage from immunotherapy. As illustrated in Fig.  G, the PLK1 low-expression subgroup displayed a greater score when not subjected to CTLA4 and PD1 treatment, and similarly, exhibited a higher score when only subjected to CTLA4 treatment(Fig.  H). No statistically significant differences were found in the immunisation scores for the remaining groups(Fig.  I and J).

## The role of PLK1 in radiotherapy response and the screening of small-molecule inhibitors targeting PLK1
To elucidate the molecular mechanisms underlying PLK1-mediated radioresistance and tumor stemness in lung adenocarcinoma, we performed differential expression analysis between PLK1-high and PLK1-low subgroups. As shown in the heatmap (Figure S4A) and volcano plot (Figure S4B), a distinct gene expression profile was observed, with 1121 genes significantly differentially expressed. Notably, several key genes associated with proliferation and radioresistance, including TPX2, FOXM1, TOP2A, and BIRC5, were significantly upregulated in the PLK1-high group (Figure S4B).
Subsequent functional enrichment analysis provided further mechanistic insights. KEGG pathway analysis revealed that PLK1-associated signaling is intricately linked to DNA damage response (DDR) mechanisms, including Mismatch repair, Homologous recombination, the Fanconi anemia pathway, and the p53 signaling pathway (Figure S4C). Given that radiotherapy primarily kills cancer cells by inducing DNA double-strand breaks, the enrichment of these DNA repair pathways suggests that PLK1-high tumors possess a superior capacity to repair radiation-induced damage, thereby conferring radioresistance. Furthermore, Gene Ontology (GO) analysis corroborated these findings, demonstrating that PLK1 is heavily implicated in positive regulation of the cell cycle, mitotic cell cycle checkpoints, and chromosome segregation (Figure S4D). These processes are pivotal for maintaining genomic stability and sustaining the rapid proliferation and self-renewal characteristics of cancer stem cells under therapeutic stress.
To examine whether PLK1 would make a potential therapeutic target, we performed in vitro experiments to investigate how PLK1 affects the functioning of lung adenocarcinoma. Following the knockdown treatment of PLK1, the data demonstrated a significant reduction in cellular activity (Fig.  A) and proliferative capacity of lung adenocarcinoma cells (Fig.  B). Furthermore, the impact of PLK1 knockdown in combination with IR on lung adenocarcinoma cell was investigated. As shown in Fig.  C and D, the clonogenic ability of lung adenocarcinoma cells was significantly impaired by either PLK1 knockdown or IR alone, with the most pronounced suppression observed in the combination group. Consistent with these findings, apoptosis analysis by flow cytometry revealed that both PLK1 knockdown and IR individually induced cell apoptosis, and their combination exerted a markedly enhanced effect (Fig.  E and F). Specifically, the combination treatment led to a striking increase in the apoptotic rate by 40.06% ± 1.524% compared to the control.
 Subsequently, we applied GSEA and western-blot to study and verify the biological pathways through which PLK1 exerted its function. As shown in Fig.  A and B, PLK1 can regulate tumor cell proliferation by modulating the activity of the JAK-STAT pathway.To mechanistically interrogate whether PLK1 regulates radiosensitivity through the JAK-STAT pathway, we performed a rescue assay. The results demonstrated that exogenous IL-6 not only significantly promoted clonogenic formation but also, more importantly, effectively reversed the inhibitory effects on clonogenic survival caused by combined PLK1 knockdown and IR (Fig.  C and D).
 Given the critical role of PLK1 in radioresistance and tumor stemness, this study aims to identify PLK1-sensitive drugs in lung adenocarcinoma and to explore their potential for enhancing the efficacy of chemoradiotherapy. In our initial investigation, the expression data for various drugs were obtained from the CellMiner database, and drug correlations were calculated based on RRSRG expression levels. A total of 42 drugs were selected for the correlation map(Fig.  A; Table  ). Then, we utilized Networkanalyst’s website and the Comparative Toxicogenomics Database (CTD) to identify small molecule compounds linked to PLK1 (Fig.  B; Table  ).Subsequently, intersection analysis was performed on the drugs from the previous steps, and the outcomes revealed cisplatin as the sensitive drug of PLK1(Fig.  C).The results of molecular docking demonstrated an interaction between cisplatin and the PLK1 protein, indicating that cisplatin may enhance the efficacy of therapeutic strategies targeting PLK1 expression(Fig.  D).
 In light of these findings, we postulated that cisplatin might enhance the sensitivity of radiotherapy. To test this hypothesis, we performed clonogenic assays in vitro and a subcutaneous xenograft model in vivo. In the xenograft experiment, once tumor volumes reached ~ 200 mm3, mice were allocated into four groups: Control, Cisplatin, RT (IR), and Cisplatin + RT. Cisplatin was administered intraperitoneally at 5 mg/kg every 4 days (q4d) until the end of the experiment, and RT was delivered as four fractions of 4 Gy, with irradiation performed on day 2 after cisplatin administration (treatment schedule summarized in Figure. 10G).
As shown in Figure. 10E and F, combined cisplatin and RT significantly reduced the number of colony-forming cells compared with either cisplatin or RT alone. Consistent with these in vitro results, the in vivo xenograft study demonstrated that the Cisplatin + RT group exhibited superior antitumor efficacy, reflected by delayed tumor growth and reduced tumor burden (Fig.  G–J).
Specifically, antitumor efficacy was quantitatively evaluated by tumor volume and weight. Compared with the Control group, RT (IR) alone and cisplatin alone produced significant tumor volume reductions of 519.3 ± 42.46 mm³ and 496.3 ± 44.57 mm³, respectively. Notably, the Cisplatin + RT combination resulted in a substantially greater tumor volume reduction of 697 ± 41.81 mm³, which was significantly superior to either monotherapy, yielding additional reductions of 200.7 ± 17.51 mm³ and 177.7 ± 11.11 mm³ beyond RT (IR) and cisplatin alone, respectively. Consistently, the combination therapy also achieved the most pronounced decrease in tumor weight (0.377 ± 0.03 g vs. Control), supporting a synergistic antitumor effect between cisplatin and radiotherapy.

## Discussion
Radiotherapy is a primary modality for cancer treatment. It functions by delivering radiation to damage tumor cell DNA, thereby inhibiting indefinite proliferation and ultimately inducing cell death. However, the therapeutic efficacy of radiotherapy remains suboptimal. Various factors contribute to radioresistance and tumor recurrence, prominently including CSCs, which possess distinct biological characteristics. CSCs can enter a quiescent state in unfavorable microenvironments and exhibit robust DNA replication and anti-apoptotic capabilities [ ]. Therefore, this study investigates tumor stemness genes to elucidate the mechanisms underlying radiotherapy resistance.
In this research, an intersection analysis of tumor stem cell genes and radiotherapy resistance genes was conducted, followed by the identification of radiotherapy resistance stem cell genes using machine learning. Specifically, the SVM algorithm was selected for this screening. SVM is a robust supervised learning method commonly used for classification and regression tasks [ ]. It is distinctive in that its optimization goal is to identify a hyperplane that maximizes the distance between the classification boundary, or regression function, and the closest training sample. The superior accuracy and reliability of the SVM algorithm have also been previously reported in the literature [ ]. Therefore, our screening of key genes for radiotherapy resistance related to stemness possesses a high degree of reliability.
We used stemness key genes related to radiotherapy resistance to develop a prognostic model, identifying five RRSRGs. Among these, PLK1 and DARS2 displayed significant prognostic value for the OS of patients. DARS2 has previously been reported to promote hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) development by enhancing the cell cycle and inhibiting apoptosis [ ]. Furthermore, promoting the cell cycle is also a characteristic of cancer stem cells. Recently, DARS2 has been found to play an essential function in the progression, adverse prognosis, and glucose metabolism of lung adenocarcinoma cells. The significance of the RRSRG DARS2 in distinguishing risk status and its prognostic value were consistent with previous reports. Liu et al. discovered that DARS2 exhibited high expression levels in lung adenocarcinoma and possessed independent prognostic value. They also found that inhibiting DARS2 expression in lung adenocarcinoma cell lines A549 and H1299 reduced cellular proliferation [ ]. As a mitochondrial enzyme, DARS2 is related to various metabolic diseases and may impact disease or cancer progression by altering metabolism. For instance, Liu et al. discovered that DARS2 has the capability to influence the glycolysis process in lung adenocarcinoma cells [ ]. This is a crucial mechanism for tumor cells to withstand and compete for scarce nutrients following hypoxia or anti-tumor vascular therapy. Suppression of glycolysis can enhance the efficacy of cancer treatment and inhibit tumor development. Moreover, DARS2 fosters lung adenocarcinoma via the ERK/c-Myc pathway [ ]. The c-Myc gene is also a significant regulator of tumor stemness during this process [ ].
To explore the RRSRGs thoroughly and pinpoint promising drugs or therapeutic targets, we conducted an intersection analysis between these five genes and the radiotherapy-resistant stemness hub gene, identifying PLK1 as a key candidate. Following analysis of the single-cell data, an increase in PLK1 expression was observed in CD8+ T cells, indicating the involvement of PLK1 in tumor immunomodulation in lung adenocarcinoma. Furthermore, our immune checkpoint analysis suggested that PLK1 may contribute to immune escape and hence tumor progression. For instance, the expression of PLK1 in lung adenocarcinoma showed a significant correlation with LAG3, CD276, and VTCN1. Previous studies have highlighted the role of CD276 and LAG3 in immune evasion. By employing single-cell sequencing technology, Wang, Cheng, and their team discovered that CD276 was involved in the remodeling of the heterogeneity of squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) stem cells, reducing mesenchymal stromalization, and thus contributing to immune evasion of SCC stem cells [ ]. PLK1 has also been reported to be involved in the regulation of cancer immune escape or checkpoints [ – ]. Additionally, PLK1 is highly linked to TNFRSF9. The expression status of TNFRSF9 and TNFSF9 is linked to the immune status of cancer cells. If the expression of TNFRSF9 is high in tumor cells, the body becomes more efficient at eradicating them. However, if TNFSF9 expression is low and the TNFRSF9/TNFSF9 signaling pathway is not adequately established, tumor immune surveillance becomes weakened, making immune escape more likely [ , ]. Therefore, additional specific and in-depth studies are necessary to determine the role of PLK1 in the immunoregulation of lung adenocarcinoma.
Given its involvement in tumor stem cells and resistance to radiotherapy, we screened sensitive drugs with the expectation that they would enhance the therapeutic efficacy of chemotherapy when administered to patients. Following analysis, it was determined that cisplatin may be a sensitive drug for PLK1. It is also noteworthy that the mechanism of action of cisplatin is closely related to that of radiotherapy, as both kill cancer cells by destroying their DNA structure. Cisplatin has also been reported to inhibit tumor stem cells and increase sensitivity to radiotherapy. A study by Johannes Routila and colleagues demonstrated that cisplatin was effective in overcoming resistance to radiotherapy in OCT4 overexpressing squamous cell carcinoma of the head and neck, resulting in improved patient outcomes [ ]. Islam et al. discovered that the combination treatment of cisplatin and eugenol had inhibitory influences on tumor stemness through the Notch signaling pathway [ ]. The combined therapy of cisplatin and eugenol successfully hindered tumor growth and effectively reduced the population of cancer stem cells. This treatment also exhibited hampered self-renewal capacity and significantly enhanced disease-free survival rates in tumor-bearing animal models compared to the treatment administered alone.
In addition, PLK1 has been demonstrated to play a pivotal role in the DNA damage repair process, providing further evidence that PLK1-high-expressing cells exhibit heightened resistance to radiotherapy. For instance, during the S/G2 phase and in response to DNA damage, PLK1 phosphorylates Ser14 of RAD51. A transient increase in p-Ser14 occurs 20–40 min after DNA damage, which permits the subsequent phosphorylation of RAD51 at Thr13 by casein kinase 2. This dual phosphorylation of RAD51 facilitates direct binding to the Nijmegen breakage syndrome (Nbs1) protein, which is part of the MRN complex involved in the early stages of the DNA damage response (DDR). The results of molecular docking experiments demonstrated the existence of an interaction between PLK1 and cisplatin. This suggests that cisplatin, in addition to promoting DNA damage, may hinder the repair function of genes such as PLK1, thereby further enhancing the therapeutic effect [ ].
Although a prognostic model was established based on the characteristics of radiotherapy and the impact of tumor stem cells on radiotherapy resistance, this study possesses some limitations. A key limitation of the present study is that we did not directly test JAK–STAT loss-of-function to establish pathway hierarchy. Although our GSEA/Western blot results and the IL-6 rescue assay are consistent with the involvement of the IL-6/JAK-STAT axis in PLK1-mediated radioresponse, we did not directly test whether genetic or pharmacological inhibition of JAK-STAT/STAT3 is sufficient to phenocopy the cellular phenotypes induced by PLK1 knockdown, nor did we perform epistasis experiments to establish pathway order. Therefore, our current data support a functional association but do not establish a definitive causal hierarchy (i.e., PLK1 → JAK-STAT/STAT3 → radiosensitivity) in lung adenocarcinoma, and this point should be interpreted cautiously. Nevertheless, the proposed pathway hierarchy is biologically plausible and is supported by prior mechanistic studies. For example, Stat3 and PLK1 have been reported to form a positive regulatory loop, where Stat3 transcriptionally activates PLK1 and PLK1 in turn enhances Stat3 signaling; blockade of either component suppresses cancer cell survival and tumorigenicity in vivo [ ]. In lung adenocarcinoma cells, PLK1 has also been shown to promote malignant phenotypes through STAT3 signaling, and PLK1 depletion reduces STAT3 activation [ ]. In parallel, irradiation can activate STAT3 and upregulate downstream effectors such as MMP2/VEGF in pulmonary adenocarcinoma models, supporting STAT3 as a radiation-responsive node [ ]. Consistently, inhibiting JAK2/STAT3 signaling has been shown to enhance radiosensitivity in NSCLC models [ ]. To rigorously define causality and pathway order, future studies will perform loss-of-function experiments using STAT3/JAK inhibitors and/or genetic approaches (siRNA/CRISPR), combined with rescue/epistasis designs (e.g., testing whether STAT3/JAK inhibition phenocopies PLK1 knockdown and whether constitutively active STAT3 reverses PLK1-knockdown-induced radiosensitization). In addition, we will validate this axis in independent radiotherapy-treated LUAD cohorts and primary patient-derived models to improve generalizability.
Several other limitations should also be acknowledged. Although we established a radiotherapy-resistance-related stemness prognostic model and experimentally supported the functional role of PLK1, several constraints limit the generalizability and clinical translatability of our findings. First, the discovery and model construction were primarily based on transcriptomic (RNA-level) data from public cohorts, and the number of radiotherapy-annotated LUAD samples with standardized treatment and response assessment remains limited. Consequently, the robustness of the RRSRPM across diverse clinical contexts (e.g., different radiotherapy regimens, stages, and comorbid therapies) requires further confirmation. Future studies should validate the model in independent, multi-center cohorts enriched for radiotherapy-treated primary patient samples with standardized response endpoints, and ideally in prospective collections. Second, while our analyses highlight PLK1-associated pathways and immune features, integrating multi-omics layers (e.g., proteomics/phosphoproteomics and genomic alterations) and performing tissue-level validation (IHC/IF on paired tumor–normal samples) would strengthen biological interpretation and improve external validity. Third, our drug-sensitivity inference for PLK1 was largely hypothesis-generating, relying on public pharmacogenomic correlations, network-based prediction, and molecular docking. These approaches do not fully capture pharmacokinetics, tumor microenvironment, or patient heterogeneity; therefore, the therapeutic implication should be interpreted cautiously. Future work should include biochemical binding/target engagement assays, systematic in vitro dose–response and synergy testing across multiple LUAD models (including primary cultures/organoids), and in vivo validation in PDX or immunocompetent models to evaluate the efficacy and safety of candidate combinations.
Although the SVM classifier achieved near-perfect AUC values, such high performance can raise concerns regarding overfitting or inadvertent data leakage. To mitigate these risks, we implemented resampling-based validation and explicitly confined preprocessing to the training portion of each fold. The classifier demonstrated stable performance across stratified cross-validation and retained high AUC in the independent holdout set, while permutation testing supported that the observed discrimination was unlikely due to chance. Nonetheless, we acknowledge that our current evaluation is primarily based on TCGA data and a tumor-versus-normal setting with class imbalance. Therefore, external validation in independent cohorts—ideally including radiotherapy-treated LUAD patient samples with well-annotated response phenotypes—is required to confirm generalizability, calibrate decision thresholds, and assess clinical utility in real-world scenarios.
In conclusion, this study established a validated RRSRPM with a strong prognostic role. This model can inform treatment decisions and monitor disease progression in clinical radiotherapy settings. Furthermore, the five RRSRGs identified in this model can serve as therapeutic targets for drug development and screening, potentially benefiting patients.
