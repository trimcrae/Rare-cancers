## Abstract

## Introduction
Sepsis, a systemic inflammatory syndrome, is frequently associated with intestinal dysfunction, which in turn exacerbates disease severity. Intestinal epithelial Paneth cells exhibit increased susceptibility to necroptosis upon inflammatory stimulation. Nuclear receptor Nur77 has been implicated in multiple programmed cell death pathways. However, the precise role of Nur77 in regulating Paneth cell necroptosis during sepsis remains unclear.

## Objectives
This study elucidates the role and underlying molecular mechanisms of nuclear receptor Nur77 in regulating Paneth cell necroptosis during sepsis.

## Methods
We employed both systemic and Paneth cell-specific Nur77 knockout mouse models. Paneth cell necroptosis was assessed using TUNEL staining, immunofluorescence, and transmission electron microscopy. Endoplasmic reticulum (ER) homeostasis was evaluated based on ultrastructural integrity, ER-phagy, and ER stress. Protein modification and protein–protein interaction were validated by structure prediction and immunoprecipitation.

## Results
Systemic or Paneth cell-specific Nur77 knockout exacerbated intestinal inflammation by enhancing Paneth cell necroptosis during sepsis. Nur77 deficiency in Paneth cells altered ileal microbiota rather than intestinal stem cell niche after LPS challenge. Nur77 deficiency-induced Paneth cell necroptosis was attributed to impaired ER homeostasis caused by defective ER-phagy. Mechanistically, LPS induced Nur77-PKCα interaction and their subsequent translocation to the ER, which promoted AMFR phosphorylation, FAM134B ubiquitination and subsequently ER-phagy. Treatment with Nur77 agonists (BTP and Csn-B) alleviated intestinal inflammation and restored Paneth cell homeostasis in sepsis mice.

## Conclusion
This study demonstrates that Paneth cell necroptosis plays a critical role in intestinal inflammation. Our work also identifies Nur77 as a potential therapeutic target to protect Paneth cells and maintain intestinal homeostasis during sepsis, pointing out the therapeutic potential of Nur77 agonist in sepsis.

## Introduction
Sepsis, a life-threatening global public health concern, is characterized by a dysregulated systemic inflammatory response to infection, often leading to multi-organ dysfunction and high mortality. Notably, sepsis is frequently accompanied with intestinal disorders [ ]. Impairment of the intestinal barrier increases permeability and facilitates bacterial translocation, thereby exacerbating sepsis severity in turn [ , , ]. Thus, maintaining intestinal homeostasis is critical for mitigating sepsis-associated inflammation.
Located at the bottom of crypts, Paneth cells serve as an essential part of intestinal innate immunity due to their diverse functional contributions [ ]. Paneth cells secrete antimicrobial peptides (AMPs), including α-defensins and lysozyme, to modulate intestinal microbiota and maintain crypt microenvironment stability [ ]. Additionally, Paneth cells sustain the intestinal stem cell (ISC) niche by providing growth factors (such as Wnt family member 3 (Wnt3), delta like canonical Notch ligand 4 (Dll4), and epidermal growth factor (EGF)) and metabolites (such as lactate) [ , ]. Paneth cell depletion in murine sepsis models suggests a significant disruption of intestinal homeostasis mediated by Paneth cells. [ ].
At the end of life, Paneth cells are capable of undergoing multiple forms of programmed cell death, including apoptosis, pyroptosis, and necroptosis [ ]. In humans and mice, Paneth cells express high levels of receptor-interacting protein kinase 3 (RIP3), a critical mediator of necroptosis, making them particularly susceptible to necroptosis during inflammation [ ]. Paneth cell necroptosis reduces their population, potentially downregulating AMPs and ISC-supporting factors and thereby disrupting ISC niche homeostasis and microbiota balance [ ]. Furthermore, necroptotic cells release abundant pro-inflammatory mediators, activating immune cells and exacerbating intestinal inflammation [ ]. Therefore, targeting Paneth cell necroptosis may represent a promising therapeutic strategy for mitigating intestinal inflammation.
Orphan nuclear receptor Nur77 (also called NR4A1, nuclear receptor subfamily 4 group A member 1) is a member of NR4A receptor subfamily, and plays a critical role in diverse biological processes [ ]. As an immediate-early gene, Nur77 expression is rapidly induced by various stimuli, including stress and inflammatory signals [ ]. In the intestine, Nur77 could regulate programmed cell death in multiple cell types. We have previously reported that Nur77-mediated mitophagy in intestinal epithelial cells alleviates dextran sulfate sodium (DSS)-induced intestinal inflammation and oxidative stress [ ]. Furthermore, in Citrobacter rodentium-induced colitis, Nur77 suppresses macrophage pyroptosis by inhibiting NLR family pyrin domain containing 3 (NLRP3) inflammasome activation and assembly, thereby attenuating intestinal inflammation [ ]. However, whether Nur77 could modulate Paneth cell necroptosis to ameliorate intestinal inflammation remains unknown.
In this study, we generated both systemic and Paneth cell-specific Nur77 knockout mice to elucidate the protective role of Nur77 in sepsis-associated intestinal inflammation. Nur77 deficiency exacerbated Paneth cell necroptosis, thereby intensifying intestinal inflammation. Mechanistically, Nur77 interacted with protein kinase C alpha (PKCα) and co-translocated to the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), where it facilitated autocrine motility factor receptor (AMFR) phosphorylation, thereby inducing family with sequence similarity 134 member B (FAM134B)-mediated ER-phagy. Collectively, our results demonstrate that Nur77 acts as a novel regulator of Paneth cell necroptosis to mitigate intestinal inflammation in sepsis.

## Material and methods

## Animals
6–8-week-old wild type (WT) C57BL/6N mice were acquired from the Animal Experiment Center at Huazhong Agricultural University (Wuhan, China). Heterozygous Nur77+/− mice, heterozygous Nur77 floxed mice (Nur77f/+ mice), and Lyz1CreERT2 mice were purchased from Cyagen US Inc (Guangzhou, China). Systemic Nur77 knockout mice (Nur77−/− mice) were obtained from the backcross of heterozygous Nur77+/− mice. Homozygous Nur77 floxed mice (Nur77f/f mice) used for Nur77 conditional knockout were obtained from the backcross of heterozygous Nur77f/+ mice. Lyz1CreERT2 mice were constructed by homologous recombination at start codon ATG of endogenous Lyz1 (Paneth cell marker gene) with a CreERT2 cassette which could be activated by tamoxifen. Then, Nur77f/f; Lyz1CreERT2 mice (Nur77ΔPC mice) in which Nur77 could be specifically deleted in Paneth cells by tamoxifen administration were generated by breeding Nur77f/f mice with Lyz1CreERT2 mice. Besides, we crossed Nur77f/f mice with Nur77ΔPC mice to generate Nur77f/f and Nur77ΔPC littermates. The numbers of mice used in our study were listed as follow: (18), (12 + 36), (10 + 10 + 21), (14), (20), (40 + 24), (20), (10), (20). Both male and female mice were utilized in this study, and each mouse experiment was performed with same-gender mice.
 All offspring were identified by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) of tail deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA). The following primers were used for PCR genotyping of Nur77 floxed allele: forward primer 5′-CCTGTCATGGATTTTACTGTGCG-3′ and reverse primer 5′-CAGCTCCTGGTCTTGATCTACTCA-3′. The floxed allele generates a 203 bp PCR product while wild-type allele gives 143 bp product. For Lyz1CreERT2 PCR genotyping, the following primers were used: forward primer 5′- TTCCATAGAAAAGCCTTGACTTGAG-3′ and reverse primer 5′- CAAAGACATGTCACCAAACTCGG-3′. The expected PCR product is 2300 bp for Lyz1CreERT2.
In this study, all mice were housed at constant temperature (22 ± 3℃) and humidity (55 ± 10 %) with a 12 h light/dark cycle. Mice were allowed to have food and water ad libitum. All animal experiments were conducted in accordance with the procedures approved by Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Huazhong Agricultural University (ID number: HZAUMO-2024–0204).
For Cre activity induction to delete Nur77 in Paneth cells, intraperitoneal injection of tamoxifen (1 mg/mouse, dissolved in corn oil, S1238, Selleck) was performed on mice for five consecutive days.

## Mouse model for lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-induced sepsis
For LPS experiment in , a total of 18 wt mice were randomly divided into 5 groups (n = 3–4 mice/group): healthy control (HC) group, LPS-1 h group, LPS-3 h group, LPS-6 h group, and LPS-9 h group. LPS (L2630, Sigma) was dissolved in sterile saline (G4702, Servicebio). Then, mice in LPS groups were intraperitoneally injected with LPS solution at a dose of 10 μg/g body weight. The dosage of LPS was based on our previous work [ ]. At the indicated timepoints after LPS treatment, mice were asphyxiated with carbon dioxide inhalation and sacrificed painlessly. Tissue samples were collected as required.
For LPS experiment in , a total of 36 mice (18 wt mice and 18 Nur77−/− mice) were divided into 4 groups (n = 9 mice/group): WT group, Nur77−/− group, WT + LPS group, and Nur77−/− + LPS group. Mice in WT group and Nur77−/− group were intraperitoneally injected with sterile saline, and mice in WT + LPS group, and Nur77−/− + LPS group were intraperitoneally injected with LPS. After 6 h from LPS treatment, mice were asphyxiated and sacrificed painlessly for sample collection.
For LPS experiment in , a total of 10 mice (5 Nur77f/f mice and 5 Nur77ΔPC mice) were divided into 2 groups (n = 5 mice/group): Nur77f/f + LPS group and Nur77ΔPC + LPS group. All mice were intraperitoneally injected with LPS. After 6 h from LPS treatment, mice were asphyxiated and sacrificed painlessly for sample collection. In this study, we also performed additional interventions on Nur77ΔPC + LPS group, such as Nec-1, anti-high mobility group box 1 (HMGB1) neutralizing antibody, and rapamycin. For Nec-1 administration, mice were intraperitoneally injected with Nec-1 (4 μg/g body weight, S8037, Selleck) 1 h before LPS challenge according to the previous study [ ]. For anti-high mobility group box 1 (HMGB1) neutralizing antibody administration, mice were intravenously injected with anti-HMGB1 neutralizing antibody (100 μg per mouse, ARG66714, Arigo Biolaboratories) via tail vein 1 h after LPS challenge according to the previous study [ ]. For rapamycin administration, mice were intraperitoneally injected with rapamycin (2 μg/g body weight, S1039, Selleck) 1 h before LPS challenge according to the previous study [ ].
For LPS experiment in , WT mice were randomly divided into 4 groups (n = 6 mice/group): control group (Ctrl), LPS group, LPS + bioactive triple peptide (BTP) group, and LPS + Cytosporone B (Csn-B) group. Mice in LPS + BTP group and LPS + Csn-B group were orally gavaged with BTP (100 mg/kg body weight) or Csn-B (10 μg/g body weight, S6674, Selleck) for ten consecutive days before LPS challenge. The dosages of BTP and Csn-B were based on previous studies [ , ]. Subsequently, all mice except for control group were intraperitoneally injected with LPS. For survival experiment, the deaths of mice were recorded within 24 h. For intestinal health assessment, after 6 h from LPS treatment, all mice were asphyxiated and sacrificed painlessly for sample collection.

## Mouse model for cecal ligation and puncture (CLP)-induced sepsis
For CLP experiment in , a total of 20 wt mice were randomly divided into 5 groups (n = 4 mice/group): HC group, Sham group, CLP-6 h group, CLP-12 h group, and CLP-24 h group. Mice in HC group did not receive any treatment. The procedures of CLP operation were based on a previous study [ ] and listed as follow. Firstly, mice were weighed and anesthetized. Abdominal area was shaved by a hair-clipper and disinfected using iodophor. To expose cecum, a 0.5–1 cm skin midline incision was made using a surgical scissor. Surgical sutures were used to ligate the middle of murine cecum. The ligated section of cecum was punctured with a 21 G needle to release a small quantity of cecal contents. Then, the punctured cecum was relocated to abdominal cavity, and skin midline incision was sutured and disinfected immediately. The mice were placed back in cages to wake up after injecting 500 μL saline subcutaneously. Except for the ligation and puncture of cecum, mice in sham group were treated as similar as CLP mice. The sham group was set to simulate most experimental procedures without the ligation and puncture of cecum, thus eliminating the influence of the surgical procedure itself. Body temperature was evaluated using a rectal thermometer. The mice were sacrificed painlessly for sample collection at the indicated timepoints.
For CLP experiment in , a total of 10 mice (6 wt mice and 4 Nur77−/− mice) were divided into 2 groups: WT + CLP group (n = 6) and Nur77−/− + CLP group (n = 4). CLP operation was conducted on all mice. After 12 h from CLP operation, mice were sacrificed painlessly for sample collection.

## Histology, immunohistochemistry, and immunofluorescence staining
Ileal tissues were collected from mice and were fixed in 4 % paraformaldehyde (G1101, Servicebio), embedded in paraffin and cut to 5 μm sections. Notably, we collected 2–3 cm ileum to produce enough paraffin sections, and the paraffin sections were prepared when we intended to conduct staining. Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining, immunohistochemistry and immunofluorescence were carried out in line with the procedures in previous studies [ ]. The evaluation of histology scores was conducted in a blinded manner based on the methods in previous study [ ]. The assessment of Paneth cell number was analyzed based on H&E staining results. The primary antibodies for immunohistochemistry and immunofluorescence: anti-Nur77 (NB100-56745, Novus, 1:200), anti-Lysozyme (ab108508, Abcam, 1:250), anti-F4/80 (28463–1-AP, Proteintech, 1:500), anti-cleaved-caspase-3 (341034, ZEN-BIOSCIENCE, 1:200), anti-phospho-RIP3 (91702, CST, 1:200), anti-HMGB1 (10829–1-AP, Proteintech, 1:250), anti-Lgr5 (D220920, BBI, 1:200), anti-Ki67 (27309–1-AP, Proteintech, 1:500), anti-BIP (11587–1-AP, Proteintech, 1:500), anti-CHOP (15204–1-AP, Proteintech, 1:500), and anti-LC3 (12741, CST, 1:200). Secondary antibodies: Alexa Fluor® 488 AffiniPure Goat Anti-Rabbit IgG (111–545-003, Jackson ImmunoResearch) and Cy™3 AffiniPure Goat Anti-Rabbit IgG (111–165-003, Jackson ImmunoResearch). The sections were examined with Olympus IX83 microscope with objectives (10×, NA = 0.30; 20×, NA = 0.65; 40×, NA = 0.95; 100×, NA = 1.40). We took one representative image per mouse in each experimental group. Lgr5/Ki67 positive cells or Paneth cells per crypt were calculated by counting the numbers of corresponding cells and crypts in one microscopic field (total 3–5 fields per mouse) and dividing corresponding cell number by crypt number in the same field.

## Isolation of small intestine crypt cells
Crypt cell isolation was conducted according to the procedures in previous study with little modification [ ]. In brief, about 10 cm small intestine was flushed using cold Dulbecco's phosphate-buffered saline (DPBS, G4200, Servicebio) and cut into pieces. The intestinal fragments were rocked in cold DPBS containing 2 mM ethylene diamine tetraacetic acid (B540625, Sangon Biotech) for 30 min at 4℃. Then, the intestinal fragments were placed in cold DPBS containing 54.9 mM D-sorbitol (A100691, Sangon Biotech) and 43.4 mM sucrose (GC205014, Servicebio) and violently shaken for 3 min to dissociate crypt cells. After centrifugation, the supernatant was carefully removed, and the pellet (crypt cells) was collected for subsequent analyses.

## In vitro Nur77 inhibitor (Nur77i) experiment
Murine intestinal epithelial cell line MODE-K cells were preserved in our lab [ ]. MODE-K cells were cultured in RIPA 1640 (61870036, Gibco) containing 10 % fetal bovine serum (A5256701, Gibco) and 1 % penicillin/streptomycin at 37℃ under a 5 % CO2 atmosphere. MODE-K cells were treated with 15 μ M Nur77i C-DIM8 (S6799, Selleck) for 2 h. Then, MODE-K cells were challenged with 1 μ g/mL LPS for 3 h. Cells were harvested for subsequent analyses. The dosages of C-DIM8 and LPS were based on the previous studies [ , ].

## Quantitative real-time PCR (qPCR)
Total ribonucleic acid (RNA) from ileum tissue or ileal crypt cells was acquired using RNA isolater Total RNA Extraction Reagent (R401-01, Vazyme) and reversely transcribed into cDNA with ABScript Neo RT Master Mix for qPCR with gDNA Remover (RK20433, Abclonal). Then, mRNA levels were examined by qPCR analysis with ChamQ SYBR qPCR Master Mix (Q321, Vazyme). Relative mRNA expression was normalized to glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH) mRNA with the “2−ΔΔCt” method. The primers in this study were listed in .
 

## Immunoblot analysis and immunoprecipitation (IP) assay
Cells or tissues were extracted with Radio Immunoprecipitation Assay lysis buffer (P0013B, Beyotime) supplemented with protease inhibitor cocktail. Proteins (20–40 μg) were separated by sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis followed by blotting on polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) membrane (ISEQ00010, Immobilon). After blocking by 5 % skim milk (GC310001, Servicebio) for 2 h, PVDF membrane was then incubated with the primary antibody and secondary antibodies. The primary antibodies: anti-Nur77 (12235-1-AP, Proteintech, 1:1000), anti-GAPDH (AC002, ABclonal, 1:10000), anti-Histone H3 (A2348, Abclonal, 1:2000), anti-Calnexin (66903-1-Ig, Proteintech, 1:1000), anti-BIP (11587–1-AP, Proteintech, 1:1000), anti-CHOP (15204–1-AP, Proteintech, 1:1000), anti-β-actin (AC026, ABclonal, 1:100000), anti-SQSTM/p62 (ab109012, Abcam, 1:20000), anti-LC3 (12741, CST, 1:1000), anti-FAM134B (83414, CST, 1:1000), anti-Ubiquitin (3936, CST, 1:1000), anti-AMFR (16675–1-AP, Proteintech, 1:1000), anti-PKCα (A11107, ABclonal, 1:1000), and anti-Phospho-Serine (ab9332, Abcam, 1:1000). Second antibodies: Goat Anti-Rabbit IgG HRP (S0001, Affinity Biosciences) and Goat Anti-Mouse IgG HRP (S0002, Affinity Biosciences).
For IP experiments, cells were lysed by Nonidet P 40 lysis buffer (P0013F, Beyotime) and incubated with IP-grade antibodies (1:50), followed by pull-down with protein A/G beads (161–4023, Bio-Rad) for subsequent immunoblot analysis. To avoid the disturbance of heavy chain, after incubation with primary antibody, PVDF membrane was subsequently treated with HRP-conjugated Mouse Anti-Heavy Chain of Rabbit IgG (SA00001-7H, Proteintech).

## Terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase dUTP Nick end Labeling (TUNEL) assay
TUNEL assay was conducted using In Situ Cell Death Detection Kit (11684817910, Roche) with the manufacturer’s instructions. As for the quantification, we counted the whole numbers of crypts and TUNEL-positive cells in crypts in ileal section of each mouse. Then, we divided TUNEL-positive cell number by crypt number to get TUNEL-positive cells/crypt ratio.

## Transmission electron microscopy
The harvested ileum tissues were fixed with 2.5 % glutaraldehyde (G1102, Servicebio) in 0.1 M PBS (G4202, Servicebio) at 4℃ overnight. Subsequent procedures were conducted according to previous study [ ]. Specimens were examined using a FEI TECNAI G2 20 TWIN transmission electron microscope. For the quantification of Paneth cell necroptosis, 10–15 Paneth cells per mouse were analyzed regarding mitochondrial swelling and extensive vacuole formation as described before [ ]. For the quantification of Paneth cell ER-phagy, approximately 10 Paneth cells per mouse were analyzed regarding degradative autophagic vacuoles filled with filamentous ER fragments as reported before [ ]. Observation of ER dilation in Paneth cells was conducted according to previous study [ ].

## Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA)
HMGB1 level in murine ileum was detected using mouse HMGB1 ELISA kit (2M-KMLJM219570m, Nanjing Camilo Biological) based on the manufacturer’s instructions.

## 16S rRNA sequencing
Mouse ileal contents were carefully collected in sterile tubes. Then, QIAamp DNA Stool Mini Kit (51604, QIAGEN) was utilized to acquire the genomic DNA. After PCR amplification of V3-V4 region of the bacterial 16S rRNA gene (338F, 5′-ACTCCTACGGGAGGCAGCAG-3′; 806R, 5′- GGACTACHVGGGTWTCTAAT-3′), MiSeq sequencing was conducted by the Majorbio Bio-Pharm Technology Co., Ltd (Shanghai, China). Bioinformatic analysis of the ileal microbiota was carried out by using the Majorbio Cloud platform (https://cloud.majorbio.com). The datasets were uploaded to SRA (PRJNA1245095).

## Isolation of nuclear, cytoplasmic and ER protein
Nuclear and cytoplasmic protein isolation of crypt cells was performed using Nuclear and Cytoplasmic Protein Extraction Kit (P0027, Beyotime) according to the manufacturer’s instructions.
ER protein isolation of crypt cells was performed using MinuteTM ER Enrichment Kit for Tissues and Cultured Cells (ER-036, Invent Biotechnologies) according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

## Structure prediction
The amino acid sequences of Nur77, AMFR, and PKCα were acquired from the National Center for Biotechnology Information. After importing these amino acid sequences into AlphaFold3 (https://www.alphafoldserver.com), the interactions between Nur77 and AMFR or PKCα were predicted, followed by visualization using PyMOL software. Subsequently, PDBePISA tool (https://www.ebi.ac.uk/pdbe/pisa/) was applied to analyze the binding free energy and the interface area of protein–protein interactions.

## Statistical analysis
In this study, data were analyzed with GraphPad Prism 8 software. For normally-distributed data, unpaired Student’s t-test (two-tailed) and one-way ANOVA with Tukey's multiple comparisons test were used to assess the differences between groups. Data were expressed as the mean ± standard error. Differences were considered as significant at P < 0.05.

## Results

## Nur77 expression is boosted in the ileal crypts of sepsis mice
To investigate Nur77 dynamics in the small intestine during sepsis, we first evaluated intestinal pathology and Nur77 expression in the ileum of LPS-challenged mice ( A). LPS induced time-dependent intestinal injury ( B), with mice in the 3, 6, and 9 h groups showing significantly higher histology scores than those in the HC group ( C). Notably, Paneth cell debris was observed in ileal crypts at 6 and 9 h post-LPS challenge ( B), suggesting that LPS induced disruption of Paneth cell homeostasis. Therefore, we next quantified Paneth cell abundance following LPS treatment. H&E staining revealed significant decreases in Paneth cell numbers at 6 and 9 h post-LPS challenge ( D). Immunohistochemistry of Paneth cell marker lysozyme also showed that the number of lysozyme positive cells in ileal crypts was decreased at 6 h post-LPS challenge ( E). We then isolated murine ileal crypts and assessed Nur77 expression at indicated timepoints post-LPS challenge ( F). Nur77 expression was markedly upregulated in ileal crypts at 6 and 9 h post-LPS ( G-I).
To confirm Nur77 expression dynamics in the ileum during sepsis, we further established CLP-induced mouse sepsis model ( A). Histopathological analysis via H&E staining revealed progressive ileal injury following CLP ( B). Compared with sham controls, mice subjected to CLP exhibited significantly elevated histology scores at 6, 12, and 24 h post-CLP ( C). Quantification of Paneth cells and lysozyme immunohistochemistry demonstrated a significant reduction in Paneth cell numbers at 12 and 24 h post-CLP ( D, E). Furthermore, qPCR and immunofluorescence analyses revealed a significant upregulation of Nur77 expression in ileal crypts at 6 and 12 h post-CLP ( F, G). Taken together, these findings indicate that Nur77 expression is markedly elevated in the ileal crypts during sepsis.

## Nur77 deficiency worsens intestinal inflammation in sepsis mice
We then utilized Nur77−/− mice to investigate the potential role of Nur77 in intestinal inflammation during sepsis. The deletion of Nur77 in small intestine was validated by qPCR and immunofluorescence ( A, B). Survival experiment showed that compared with WT mice, Nur77−/− mice exhibited decreased survival rate after LPS challenge ( C). The body temperature of Nur77−/− mice was significantly lowered at 4 h after LPS injection ( D).
Compared with WT mice, Nur77−/− mice exhibited more severe epithelial damage, increased inflammatory cell infiltration, Paneth cell shedding ( E), and elevated histology score ( F). Consistent with histology findings, qPCR analysis revealed significantly higher mRNA levels of proinflammatory cytokines, including inflammatory factors tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-α, interleukin (IL)-1β, and IL-6, in Nur77−/− mice compared with WT mice ( G). Immunostaining for the macrophage marker F4/80 demonstrated that Nur77 deficiency promoted multifocal macrophage aggregation in the ileum of sepsis mice ( H). Paneth cell counting and lysozyme immunohistochemistry revealed that indicated that Nur77 deficiency significantly reduced Paneth cell numbers in both control and sepsis mice ( I, J). Furthermore, TUNEL staining showed a marked increase in dying cells (TUNEL-positive cells) in the ileal crypts of Nur77−/− mice following LPS challenge compared with WT mice ( K), suggesting that Nur77 deficiency might exacerbates Paneth cell death during sepsis.
To further validate the protective role of Nur77 in intestinal inflammation during sepsis, we subjected Nur77−/− mice to CLP operation. Histopathological analysis via H&E staining revealed that Nur77 deficiency exacerbated intestinal injury and elevated histological scores in sepsis mice ( A, B). TNF-α expression was significantly boosted in the ileum of Nur77−/− mice following CLP ( C). Additionally, compared with WT controls, Nur77−/− mice exhibited a reduced Paneth cell population post-CLP ( D), which was further demonstrated by lysozyme staining ( E). Taken together, these findings indicate that Nur77 deficiency exacerbates intestinal inflammation in sepsis mice.

## Nur77 deletion-induced Paneth cell necroptosis aggravates LPS-induced intestinal inflammation
It has been reported that Paneth cell death drives spontaneous ileitis in mice [ ], suggesting that Nur77 deficiency-mediated Paneth cell death may play a critical role in intestinal inflammation during sepsis. To investigate the role of Nur77 in Paneth cells during sepsis, we generated Paneth cell-specific Nur77 knockout mice (Nur77f/f; Lyz1CreERT2 mice, Nur77ΔPC mice) and subjected them to LPS-induced sepsis. After tamoxifen-induced knockout of Nur77 in Paneth cells, we isolated crypt cells to examine Nur77 expression level and found that Nur77 expression was reduced by 50 % in crypt cells ( A), indicating the successful deletion of Nur77 in Paneth cells. The number of Paneth cells was comparable between WT mice, Nur77f/f mice, and Nur77ΔPC mice ( B). We then induced sepsis in Nur77ΔPC mice via LPS challenge. Histological analysis revealed exacerbated intestinal injury in Nur77ΔPC mice compared to Nur77f/f controls ( C), with significantly higher ileal histology scores ( D). qPCR analysis further demonstrated elevated ileal TNF-α and IL-1β mRNA levels in Nur77ΔPC mice ( A). Immunostaining for F4/80 revealed enhanced macrophage infiltration in the ileum of Nur77ΔPC mice following LPS challenge ( B). Notably, Nur77 deficiency in Paneth cells resulted in a marked reduction in Paneth cell abundance post-LPS challenge ( E, F). Meanwhile, after LPS injection, Nur77ΔPC mice displayed increased number of dying cells in ileal crypts ( G), implicating enhanced Paneth cell death as the underlying cause of their depletion in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice. However, apoptosis marker cleaved caspase-3 staining revealed that the number of cleaved-caspase-3 positive cells (apoptotic cells) had a slight but not significant increase in Nur77ΔPC mice compared with Nur77f/f mice, and apoptotic cells mainly resided in the middle of crypts ( H). Previous study has reported that both human and murine Paneth cells express high RIP3 levels [ ], rendering them susceptible to necroptosis during severe intestinal inflammation. We next assessed Paneth cell necroptosis in LPS-challenged mice of both genotypes. Necroptosis marker phospho-RIP3 staining showed that a higher number of necroptotic cells (p-RIP3+) in the crypt base, where Paneth cells reside, in Nur77ΔPC versus Nur77f/f mice ( I). The increased abundance of p-RIP3 was also demonstrated by western blot ( J). In addition, transmission electron microscopy was further used to observe Paneth cell necroptosis. We found that Nur77-deficient Paneth cells exhibited typical necroptosis features ( K), such as mitochondrial swelling (red arrow) and extensive vacuole formation (red arrowhead). In summary, these data suggest that Paneth cell Nur77 deletion exacerbates LPS-driven intestinal inflammation and Paneth cell necroptosis.
To further elucidate the contribution of Nur77 deficiency-mediated Paneth cell necroptosis to intestinal inflammation, we utilized Nec-1, a necroptosis inhibitor, to block Paneth cell necroptosis in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice ( L, M). Nec-1 treatment restored Paneth cell abundance in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice, as quantified by cell counting and lysozyme immunostaining ( N, O). Nec-1 treatment significantly ameliorated intestinal injury and reduced ileal histology scores in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice ( P, Q). Collectively, these results demonstrate that Nur77 deficiency exacerbates intestinal inflammation during sepsis by promoting Paneth cell necroptosis.
Necroptotic cells release substantial amounts of pro-inflammatory mediators, such as HMGB1 [ ]. Consistent with enhanced Paneth cell necroptosis in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice, qPCR, ELISA, and immunofluorescence analyses revealed that Nur77 deficiency upregulated HMGB1 expression in crypt cells during sepsis ( A-C). HMGB1 is a vital mediator of sepsis [ ]. We therefore investigated whether HMGB1 neutralization could mitigate intestinal inflammation in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice ( D). As expected, anti-HMGB1 antibody treatment ameliorated intestinal injury in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice ( E, F). These findings suggest that HMGB1 contributes to intestinal inflammation triggered by Paneth cell necroptosis.

## Paneth cell Nur77 deficiency leads to alterations in ileal microbiota but does not affect ISC niche in sepsis mice
The necroptosis of Nur77-deficient Paneth cells could reduce their abundance, which might further affect Paneth cell functions such as intestinal microbiota regulation and ISC support. To test this hypothesis, we first assessed AMP-encoding gene expression in Paneth cells. Nur77 deficiency significantly downregulated Lyz1 and Defa1 ( A), indicating compromised antibacterial ability in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice. We next performed 16S rRNA sequencing on ileal microbiota of two genotype littermates with sepsis. In ileal microbiota, substantial differences in amplicon sequence variant (ASV) composition were observed between Nur77f/f mice and Nur77ΔPC mice ( B). Sequencing analysis also exhibited a reduced Chao1 index in Nur77ΔPC mice compared to Nur77f/f controls ( C), whereas the Shannon index remained unchanged ( D). Principal coordinate analysis revealed clear segregation between Nur77ΔPC mice and Nur77f/f mice ( E). Furthermore, Nur77 deficiency in Paneth cells markedly altered ileal microbiota composition at the phylum and genus levels ( F, G). LEfSe analysis demonstrated LPS-induced enrichment of Ileibacterium, Candidatus Saccharimonas, and Marvinbryantia in Nur77f/f mice, whereas Streptococcus and Pseudomonas were enriched in Nur77ΔPC mice ( H). Collectively, these data indicate that Paneth cell-specific Nur77 deficiency remodels the ileal microbiota following LPS challenge.
In addition to microbiota regulation, Paneth cells contribute to ISC niche maintenance through the secretion of ISC-supporting factors, thereby preserving intestinal homeostasis [ ]. We next examined alterations in the ISC niche following Paneth cell-specific Nur77 deletion in sepsis mice. qPCR analysis of Paneth cell-derived ISC-supporting factors showed reduced mRNA levels of Wnt3 and Dll4 in the ileal crypts of Nur77ΔPC mice relative to Nur77f/f controls ( I). Unexpectedly, compared with Nur77f/f mice, Nur77ΔPC mice exhibited comparable mRNA expression levels of ISC markers including leucine rich repeat containing G protein-coupled receptor 5 (Lgr5), achaete-scute family bHLH transcription factor 2 (Ascl2), and olfactomedin 4 (Olfm4) ( J). Immunohistochemical staining for Lgr5 confirmed that ISC abundance remained unaltered upon Paneth cell-specific Nur77 deletion in sepsis mice ( K). Moreover, proliferative cell marker Ki67 immunohistochemistry demonstrated no significant difference in proliferative cell numbers between Nur77f/f mice and Nur77f/f mice ( L). Overall, these findings indicate that Paneth cell-specific Nur77 deletion does not perturb the ISC niche in sepsis mice.

## Paneth cell Nur77 deficiency disrupts ER homeostasis in sepsis mice
Given that Paneth cell necroptosis exacerbates intestinal inflammation in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice ( ), we next investigated the mechanism by which Nur77 deficiency promoted Paneth cell necroptosis. Nur77 plays an essential role in maintaining autophagy which is of great importance to Paneth cell homeostasis [ , ]. We therefore assessed autophagy activity in Paneth cells from both genotypes of septic mice. Immunoblotting revealed diminished microtubule-associated protein 1 light chain 3 (LC3)-I/II conversion and elevated p62 accumulation in crypts of Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice ( A). Immunofluorescence confirmed reduced LC3 staining in crypt bases of Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice ( B). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) detected degradative autophagic vacuoles containing filamentous ER fragments in Paneth cells from both Nur77f/f and Nur77ΔPC septic mice ( C), indicating that LPS challenge induces ER-phagy in Paneth cells. Notably, Paneth cells from Nur77ΔPC mice exhibited fewer ER fragment-containing vacuoles ( C), suggesting impaired ER-phagy upon LPS challenge. Given that autophagy defects disrupt ER homeostasis [ ], we next examined ER homeostasis in sepsis mice with Paneth cell-specific Nur77 deletion. Nur77ΔPC septic mice exhibited elevated level of ER marker calnexin compared with Nur77f/f sepsis mice ( D). Using transmission electron microscopy, we found that compared with Nur77f/f controls, Paneth cells from Nur77ΔPC mice displayed ER dilation ( E). ER disorders are usually accompanied with ER stress [ ]. qPCR and immunoblotting demonstrated marked upregulation of ER stress markers (BIP, CHOP, IRE1α, and XBP1) in crypt cells from Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice ( F-G). Together, these findings demonstrate that Paneth cell-specific Nur77 deletion disrupts ER homeostasis during sepsis.
To address the role of ER-phagy in Paneth cell necroptosis in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice, we administered rapamycin, an autophagy inducer, to Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice ( H). Rapamycin treatment significantly reduced BIP and CHOP expression, indicating improved ER homeostasis in Paneth cells of Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice ( I; A, B). Furthermore, Paneth cell number in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice was increased after rapamycin treatment ( J, K). The TUNEL assay revealed that rapamycin attenuated Nur77 deficiency-induced Paneth cell necroptosis, as evidenced by fewer TUNEL-positive cells in crypts ( L). Consistent with these findings, rapamycin reduced HMGB1 levels in crypt cells and mitigated intestinal injury in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice ( M), and rapamycin administration also alleviated intestinal injury in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice ( C, D). Collectively, these findings demonstrate that Nur77-mediated ER-phagy enhances ER homeostasis and suppresses necroptosis in Paneth cells during sepsis.

## Decrease in ER-located PKCα by Nur77 deletion impairs AMFR phosphorylation contributing to FAM134B ubiquitination
It has been reported that various stimuli can induce the nuclear export of Nur77 to unlock multiple functions of Nur77 [ , ], and the phosphorylation of Nur77 facilitates its nuclear export [ ]. Expectedly, we found that LPS triggered serine phosphorylation of Nur77 in crypt cells ( A). To determine Nur77 localization, we isolated nuclear and cytoplasmic proteins from crypt cells. Immunoblot analysis showed that LPS treatment reduced nuclear Nur77 while increasing its cytoplasmic level ( B), confirming LPS-induced nuclear export of Nur77. Nur77 immunofluorescence further demonstrated diminished nuclear Nur77 in crypt base cells following LPS exposure ( A). Moreover, we also isolated ER proteins of crypt cells and found that Nur77 abundance in ER was elevated after LPS challenge ( C), suggesting its translocation from the nucleus to the ER.
 FAM134B, the first identified ER-phagy receptor in mammals, plays an essential role in ER-phagy induction [ ]. We therefore hypothesized that Nur77 might regulate FAM134B to induce ER-phagy in Paneth cells. We assessed FAM134B expression in crypt cells from sepsis mice of both genotypes. However, Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice exhibited no significant change in FAM134B protein levels compared to Nur77f/f controls ( B). Interestingly, Paneth cell Nur77 deficiency dramatically weakened FAM134B ubiquitination, a critical process for ER-phagy induction and ER remodeling ( D) [ , ]. Recent study has shown that FAM134B ubiquitination is catalyzed by AMFR, an endogenous ER-anchored E3 ligase [ ]. However, AMFR protein levels in crypt cells remained unchanged in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice compared to Nur77f/f controls ( C). The E3 ligase activity of AMFR is dependent on its serine phosphorylation [ ]. We found that Paneth cell Nur77 deficiency reduced serine phosphorylation level of AMFR in crypt cells ( E). These findings suggest that Nur77 deficiency debilitates FAM134B ubiquitination through modulating serine phosphorylation of AMFR rather than AMFR expression. Previous work has demonstrated that PKCα interacts with AMFR and promotes its phosphorylation [ ]. Nur77 deficiency did not alter PKCα protein levels in crypt cells ( D), indicating that impaired AMFR phosphorylation was independent of PKCα expression. Strikingly, Nur77 deficiency attenuated the AMFR-PKCα interaction in crypt cells ( F), which could result in decreased AMFR phosphorylation. Besides, Nur77 deficiency reduced ER-localized PKCα levels in crypt cells ( G). Given that LPS induces Nur77 translocation from nucleus to ER ( B, C), we hypothesized that PKCα might co-translocate with Nur77 upon LPS stimulation. To verify this hypothesis, we conducted structure prediction with AlphaFold3 to evaluate assess potential Nur77-PKCα interactions. The Nur77-PKCα complex exhibited a surface interface area of 2684.1 Å2 and a binding free energy (ΔiG) of −12.6 kcal/mol ( H), and the negative ΔiG value represents stable combination. Then, IP assay demonstrated the interaction between Nur77 and PKCα, which was strengthened by LPS treatment ( I). Interestingly, AlphaFold3 analysis showed that Nur77-AMFR complex had a surface interface area of 3205.2 Å2 and a binding free energy (ΔiG) of −25.8 kcal/mol ( J). IP assay also confirmed Nur77-AMFR binding in crypt cells ( K), suggesting a possible Nur77-AMFR-PKCα complex in ER.
To further validate the underlying mechanism, we treated MODE-K cells with LPS and/or Nur77i (C-DIM8). We found that in LPS-challenged cells, Nur77 defect by Nur77i treatment impaired the ER translocation of PKCα and AMFR serine phosphorylation ( A, B). Collectively, our data demonstrate that Nur77 interacts with PKCα and co-translocates to the ER, which promotes AMFR phosphorylation and thereby enhances FAM134B ubiquitination and ER-phagy ( ).

## Nur77 agonists alleviate intestinal inflammation by improving Paneth cell homeostasis in sepsis mice
To evaluate the therapeutic potential of Nur77 activation in sepsis, we administered BTP or Csn-B, two Nur77 agonists [ , ], to septic mice ( A). Survival experiment found that BTP or Csn-B significantly reduced the mortality of sepsis mice ( B). Histological analysis showed that compared with control mice, LPS-treated mice developed severe ileal injury, characterized by epithelium damage, Paneth cell shedding, and inflammatory cell infiltration ( C, D). Treatment with either BTP or Csn-B significantly mitigated these pathological alterations and improved histological scores in sepsis mice ( C, D). qPCR analysis demonstrated that BTP or Csn-B treatment significantly suppressed the mRNA levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines TNF-α and IL-6 in sepsis mice ( E). Additionally, Paneth cell counting and lysozyme immunohistochemistry indicated that both BTP and Csn-B restored Paneth cell numbers in sepsis mice ( F, G). The number of dying cells (TUNEL positive cells) was lowered in Csn-B-treated sepsis mice ( H). In addition, Csn-B treatment efficiently down-regulated the expression of ER stress marker BIP ( I, J). Taken together, these findings demonstrate that pharmacological activation of Nur77 via BTP or Csn-B effectively attenuates intestinal inflammation and restores Paneth cell homeostasis in sepsis mice.

## Discussion
Although extensive studies have established that Paneth cells contribute to intestinal homeostasis through diverse functions, including microbiota modulation and ISC support [ ], the role of Paneth cell death in intestinal homeostasis remains poorly understood. Here, we investigated the role of Nur77 in regulating Paneth cell necroptosis and its impact on intestinal inflammation. We demonstrate that Nur77 suppresses Paneth cell necroptosis, thereby mitigating intestinal inflammation in sepsis.
Sepsis is frequently associated with intestinal dysfunction, which promotes bacterial translocation and exacerbates disease severity in turn [ ]. It has been reported that sepsis mice exhibit increased expression of Nur77 in lung and liver [ ], and mice with Nur77 deletion are more susceptible to LPS or CLP-induced sepsis as evidenced by elevated multi-organic inflammation [ , ]. These clues suggest the important participation of Nur77 in sepsis, but the role of Nur77 in intestinal inflammation during sepsis still remains unclear. In the present study, we found that LPS challenge also led to the increase in Nur77 level in ileal crypts wherein Paneth cells are located, implying the potential involvement of Nur77 in modulating Paneth cell homeostasis during sepsis. While we previously demonstrated that Nur77 promoted Paneth cell differentiation to sustain their population [ ], here we employed mice with Paneth cell-specific Nur77 deletion (Nur77ΔPC mice) to circumvent this effect. By utilizing Nur77ΔPC mice, we specifically investigated the role of Nur77 in Paneth cell biology post-differentiation and maturation. Our previous work has revealed that Nur77 regulates various forms of programmed cell death such as autophagy and pyroptosis to preserve intestinal homeostasis [ , ]. This study demonstrates that Nur77 deficiency aggravates Paneth cell necroptosis in sepsis mice, identifying Nur77 as a potential therapeutic target for Paneth cell necroptosis.
At the end of their lifespan, Paneth cells undergo multiple forms of programmed cell death, including apoptosis, pyroptosis, and necroptosis [ ]. Paneth cells exhibit increased susceptibility to necroptosis owing to their high RIP3 expression [ ]. Although Paneth cell death could trigger spontaneous ileitis in mice [ ], but it is still unclear about the specific pattern of Paneth cell death triggering ileitis. In addition, as a specific group of intestinal epithelial cells, Paneth cells were located above the lamina propria and surrounded by abundant self-maintaining macrophages [ ]. Thereby, it could be speculated that once pro-inflammatory Paneth cell death such as necroptosis occurs, the pro-inflammatory mediators released by Paneth cells might activate surrounding macrophages to aggravate intestinal inflammation. As expected, in this study, we demonstrated that Nur77 deficiency-triggered Paneth cell necroptosis could worsen intestinal inflammation during sepsis, which could be rescued by necroptosis inhibitor Nec-1 administration. Our findings underscore the therapeutic potential of inhibiting Paneth cell necroptosis in intestinal inflammation.
Within the small intestine, Paneth cells regulate intestinal microbiota and support ISC niche, thereby maintaining intestinal homeostasis [ ]. Paneth cell necroptosis led to a decrease in Paneth cell number, potentially compromising their functions in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice. In the present study, we demonstrated that Paneth cell Nur77 deficiency indeed altered ileal microbiota in sepsis mice, as indicated by increased abundance of harmful bacteria Streptococcus and Pseudomonas. This finding could also afford a credible explanation for intestinal microbiota disorders observed in sepsis patients [ ]. There is a close connection between microbiota and intestinal homeostasis, but it was not clear whether Nur77 deficiency-induced disorders in microbiota would aggravate intestinal inflammation under sepsis, which needed further exploration in our future work. Furthermore, despite the downregulation of Paneth cell-derived ISC-supporting factors, ISC numbers remained unchanged in in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice. We speculate that this unexpected result may due to the short LPS treatment duration (6 h), which may be insufficient to give rise to alterations in ISC niche in Nur77ΔPC sepsis mice.
Due to the vigorous secretory nature, Paneth cells possess abundant ER network to synthesize and process various proteins [ ]. Consequently, ER homeostasis is of great importance to Paneth cell biology. Mice with ER defects in Paneth cells display the decrease in Paneth cell number, or even Paneth cell death [ , ]. Consistent with these findings, our results demonstrated that Nur77 deficiency disrupts ER homeostasis in Paneth cells, potentially triggering necroptosis. Notably, the previous study has proved that Nur77 can inhibit TRAF6 auto-ubiquitination to reduce the production of pro-inflammatory factors such as TNF-α in liver, thus alleviating sepsis [ ]. In the current work, we also found that Nur77 deficiency could increase the expression of TNF-α in ileum in sepsis, and TNF-α is a robust inducer of Paneth cell necroptosis [ ]. However, whether Nur77 modulated Paneth cell necroptosis via TNF-α-mediated canonical necroptosis signaling is still unknown, which needs to be further explored in future. Severe ER stress can induce ER-phagy to restore ER homeostasis [ ]. Mice with ER stress in Paneth cells exhibit elevated level of autophagy [ ]. As a form of selective autophagy, ER-phagy contributes to redundant ER degradation and ER remodeling [ ]. Autophagy impairment results in spontaneous ER expansion and ER stress [ , ], while autophagy activation significantly inhibits ER stress [ ]. Multiple studies have revealed that Nur77 plays a crucial role in motivating autophagy, such as mitophagy and ER-phagy [ , ]. As expected, in this study, we observed fewer ER fragment-containing vacuoles in Nur77-deficient Paneth cells, suggesting impaired ER-phagy upon Nur77 loss.
Upon stimulation, nuclear Nur77 can migrate to cytoplasm or organelles, such as mitochondrion, ER, and trans-Golgi, to modulate cell homeostasis [ , ]. Our previous investigation has also revealed that Nur77 can interact with NLRP3 and jointly migrate to trans-Golgi, thereby inhibiting the assembly and activation of NLRP3 inflammasome [ ]. We observed that LPS challenge promoted Nur77 phosphorylation and the migration of Nur77 from nuclear to ER in murine Paneth cells, suggesting Nur77 could directly participate in manipulation of Paneth cell ER homeostasis in sepsis mice.
In this study, we observed that LPS increased Nur77 level in the ER and enhanced its interaction with PKCα. Notably, Nur77 deletion reduced ER-anchored PKCα levels following LPS treatment, suggesting that Nur77 interacted with PKCα and jointly migrated to the ER. Previous studies have shown that ER-located PKCα promotes ER-anchored AMFR phosphorylation which is vital for its E3 ligase activity [ , ]. Consistent with this mechanism, Nur77 deletion decreased AMFR phosphorylation due to impaired PKCα translocation to the ER. It has been proved that based on its phosphorylation-mediated E3 ligase activity, AMFR could catalyze the ubiquitination of FAM134B, the first identified ER-phagy receptor in mammals, to trigger ER-phagy and control ER remodeling [ ]. Notably, Nur77 deletion impaired FAM134B ubiquitination without affecting its protein levels, suggesting that Nur77 modulates ER-phagy via the PKCα-AMFR-FAM134B axis. Collectively, these findings reveal a novel mechanism by which Nur77 regulates ER-phagy. Notably, AlphaFold3 is a recently developed tool for high-accuracy structure prediction of protein, nucleic acid, and small-molecule complexes [ ]. Using AlphaFold3 and IP assay, we identified a potential Nur77-AMFR-PKCα complex during ER-phagy. A limitation of this study is the lack of a suitable Paneth cell model; future work will validate these findings in alternative cell lines, such as MODE-K and IEC-6 cells.
In summary, we identify Nur77 as a critical regulator of Paneth cell necroptosis in sepsis. Nur77 improved Paneth cell ER homeostasis to suppress Paneth cell necroptosis, thus attenuating intestinal inflammation in sepsis. Mechanism investigation revealed that the decrease in ER-located PKCα by Nur77 deletion disrupted AMFR phosphorylation, leading to FAM134B ubiquitination impairment and eventually ER-phagy defects. Our study illustrates that ER homeostasis in Paneth cells is of importance for intestinal inflammation alleviation through the inhibition of Paneth cell necroptosis, which could be a new approach for improving intestinal homeostasis in sepsis. These findings advance our understanding of Nur77 in sepsis and provide a scientific basis for Nur77-targeted therapeutic strategies for alleviating intestinal inflammation.
