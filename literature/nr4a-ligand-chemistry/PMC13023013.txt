## Abstract
A plethora of studies have demonstrated the pathophysiological roles played by paraxonase 2 (PON2) in oxidative stress control, inhibition of apoptosis, infections, and the progression of various types of malignancies. The continuous interest in PON2 has not gone hand in hand with the development of its inhibitors. Indeed, only one inhibitor for PON2, namely TQ416, is known, although neither its preparation nor a systematic structure-activity relationship analysis has been so far reported. Herein, we outline the first study aimed at the definition of structure-activity relationships of TQ416 by the preparation of a small library of its analogues. Successfully, we identified some [1,2,4]triazolo[4,3-a]quinoline derivatives more potent than TQ416 as PON2 inhibitors, and among them one endowed with an IC50 value in the nanomolar range. We tested the parent TQ416 and its most effective congener 2 in cells showing their effectiveness and complex behaviour.
Keywords: Enzymatic inhibitor; microscale thermophoresis; non-competitive inhibition; paraxonase-2; [1,2,4]triazolo[4,3-a]quinoline

## Introduction
Paraxonase 2 (PON2) is one of the three isoforms constituting the paraxonase protein family, historically so called for its ability to hydrolyse the organophosphate insecticide paraoxon to the corresponding metabolite 4-nitrophenol . Whereas PON1 and PON3 are mainly secreted and functional in the bloodstream, PON2 is anchored to cellular membranes, particularly to those of endoplasmic reticulum (ER), mitochondria and nucleus. PON2 is ubiquitously expressed in vital organs, such as heart, brain, and lungs. PON2 is a multifaceted protein endowed with enzymatic and non-enzymatic abilities; as an enzyme, PON2 acts mainly as a lactonase showing a high specificity towards the hydrolysis of N-acylhomoserine-lactones (HSLs), which are bacterial quorum sensing molecules . HSLs, such as N-(3-oxododecanoyl)-L-homoserine lactone (3OC12HSL) produced by P. aeruginosa, are useful for bacteria to ascertain cell density, to regulate their growth, biofilm formation and virulence factors expression. Depending on the cell lines, 3OC12HSL is also able to induce apoptosis , , chemotaxis, and expression of proinflammatory genes . By contrast it has been reported that 3OC12HSL inhibited the lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-induced inflammatory response in vitro and in vivo by repressing nuclear factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B-cell (NF-κB) signalling . Thus, by mediating HSLs hydrolysis, PON2 acts as an innate defense mechanism against bacterial pathogens. However, the hydrolysis products from HSLs, namely the ring-opened biologically inactive carboxylic acids, accumulate in cells promoting a pH lowering of the cytosol and mitochondria with a consequent calcium release and phosphorylation of stress signalling kinases . Hence PON2 appeared as a key regulator of host cell responses to HSLs, because able to decrease the availability of these mediators for receptor-mediated effects, and in the meantime capable of promoting effects, such as calcium release and stress signalling, consequent to the intracellular acidification.
Along with hydrolytic capability, PON2, as well as the other two homologs, possesses well-established antioxidant properties, being able to defend cells from the damage induced by Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS) , . Although different studies have reported about the mechanisms underlying the antioxidant protection of PON2 in mitochondria and ER, the lack of known PON2’s substrates or targets within any specific physiological environments left several questions unanswered .
PON2 plays a multifaceted role in cancer, too: by its regulatory expression, PON2 effectively circumvents both oxidative stress and pro-apoptotic pathways, thereby facilitating tumour cell survival . In this context, too, the understanding of the mechanisms underlying PON2 contribution to tumour heterogeneity, progression, and resistance to therapy remains incomplete.
The unavailability of small molecule probes able to target PON2 has undoubtedly slowed down the generation and validation of hypotheses on the function of PON2 in physiology and pathological contexts.
To date 5,9-dimethyl-1-[2-((p-tolyloxy)ethyl)thio][1,2,4]triazolo[4,3-a]quinoline (TQ416, 1) is the unique small molecule reported as able to inhibit the enzymatic activity of PON2 , .

 

Actually, TQ416 (1) was not born as PON2 inhibitor, but identified, by a high throughput screening of a large library, as a compound able to restore NF-κB transcriptional activity suppressed by 3OC12HSL . Only a year later Horke et al. demonstrated that some of the effects showed by TQ416 (1) on 3OC12HSL-mediated cellular pathways resulted from the inhibition of the hydrolytic activity of PON2: indeed, TQ416 (1) was able to inhibit 3OC12HSL hydrolysis and to prevent cytosolic acidification in HEK293T cells . The connection between TQ416 (1) and PON2 was then reinforced by Tao et al. , who investigated the effect of 3OC12HSL on cell viability and mucus secretion in LS174T cells, validating the ability of TQ416 (1) to rescue 3OC12HSL-induced cell death through PON2 inhibition.
The lack on the market of TQ416 (1) along with the awareness that the availability of chemical probes targeting PON2 would have a positive impact on research community concerned with the study of PON2 pushed us first to develop an efficient route for the synthesis of this probe and then to design a small library of TQ416 analogs to lay the foundation of the first rationale and systematic structure-activity relationship (SAR) study of this class of compounds. In particular, we designed to evaluate the influence on the activity of the following structural features of TQ416 (1): a) the length of the alkyl spacer by preparing the elongated analogs 2 and 3, b) the nature and the position of the substituent present at the terminal aryloxy moiety by synthesising the derivatives 4a–k, and c) the impact of the substituent at C-9 position of the core by the preparation of its 9-nor analog, namely 5 ( ). Finally, we evaluated all the library items by biophysical and in vitro biological assays.

## Chemistry

## General methods
All reagents and solvents were reagent grade or were purified by standard methods before use. Anhydrous toluene was obtained by distillation from sodium. All reactions requiring anhydrous conditions were performed under a positive argon atmosphere, and all glassware was oven dried and/or flame dried.
Melting points (m.p.) were determined in open capillaries on a StuartTM SMP3 apparatus and are uncorrected.
1H-,13C- and 19F-NMR spectra were obtained on a Bruker DRX 400 spectrometer as solutions in CDCl3 unless otherwise indicated. Chemical shifts were reported in parts per million (δ) and are referenced to residual protium in the deuterated solvent. The spin-spin coupling constants (J) were reported in Hertz (Hz) and refer to apparent multiplicities rather than true coupling constants. The spin multiplicities are indicated by the symbols s (singlet), d (doublet), t (triplet), q (quartet), m (multiplet), and bs (broad signal). Data are reported as: chemical shift, multiplicity, coupling constant, integration. Purification of the compounds were performed on Purezza® Daily cartridges (Sepachrom) or on Merck LiChroprep Si 60 Lobar columns. Analytical thin-layer chromatography (TLC) was performed on TLC plates (silica gel 60 F254, aluminium foil). Compounds were detected under UV light or by permanganate stain. Microanalyses were carried out on a Carlo Erba 1106 elemental analyser, and the results were within ± 0.4% of the theoretical value. Purity was determined by microanalysis to be >95% for all final compounds.

## General procedure for the preparation of 1-substituted [1,2,4]triazolo[4,3-a]quinoline derivatives
Sodium ethoxide (2 eq) was added to a stirred solution of 12 (or 20, 1 eq) and the opportune aryloxyalkyl halide (1.2 eq) in ethanol (12 ml/mmol) kept under argon atmosphere at room temperature. ## Protein expression and purification
Expression and purification of the recombinant rPON2 was carried starting from 7 L cultures of recombinant E. coli cells with well-consolidated procedures . Briefly, recombinant PON2 was expressed in E. coli BL21(DE3) cells (obtained from the commercial supplier Invitrogen, Ca, USA) and after cell lysis harvested as inclusion bodies by centrifugation. The inclusion bodies were washed with buffers containing low concentrations of detergents (Triton X-100) and urea to remove impurities. The solubilisation was performed in 8 M urea to fully denature the protein. Denatured rPON2 was purified by Ni2+-NTA affinity chromatography under denaturing conditions. Refolding was achieved on-column by gradually reducing the urea concentration in a stepwise manner, allowing recovery of the native conformation. Finally, size exclusion chromatography was used to further purify and polish the enzyme preparation .

## Microscale thermophoresis
MicroScale Thermophoresis (MST) is a biophysical technique used to quantify the dissociation constant (Kd), which is a key parameter for evaluating the strength of molecular interactions. Thermophoresis describes the movement of molecules in a temperature gradient (ΔT), which is induced by an IR laser. This movement is represented by the Soret coefficient (ST), and depends on the size, charge, conformation and solvation entropy of the biomolecules . During the binding event, at least one of these properties may change, resulting in a variation in the thermophoretic behaviour of the particles in solution, which the instrument detects and uses to derive the Kd. Generally, in experiments, one partner is fluorescently labelled, and 16 solutions are tested with increasing concentrations of the ligand partner. Analysis of normalised fluorescence as a function of ligand concentration produces the binding curve from which Kd is obtained .
Before the labelling reaction, a protein buffer exchange step was carried out using an ionic exchange column, according to the NanoTemper protocol. ## Enzymatic activity assessment
Lactonase activity was assessed by monitoring the hydrolysis of the substrate 3OC12HSL, which leads to acidification of the reaction medium. This pH change was detected using the indicator dye cresol red, following the method described by Chapman and Wong .
Reactions were carried out at 37 °C using a Varian Cary 100 spectrophotometer (Agilent, Santa Clara, CA, USA) equipped with temperature control. Absorbance changes were recorded at 572 nm, using a 1 cm path length cuvette. The reaction mixture consisted of 0.4 mM HEPES buffer (pH 8.5), 150 mM NaCl, 0.12 mM cresol red (pKa 8.5 at 25 °C), and 5% acetonitrile. The substrate concentration was set at 0.5 mM.
Prior to enzymatic assays, a calibration curve using acetic acid was performed to determine the rate factor (ΔOD per mole of H+), allowing calculation of specific enzyme activity. The maximum reaction rate (Vmax) was expressed in μeq·min−1·mg−1, representing the micromoles of acid produced per minute per milligram of purified rPON2.
To determine kinetic parameters, reactions were conducted in the same buffer system (0.4 mM HEPES, pH 8.5, 150 mM NaCl, 0.12 mM cresol red, 5% acetonitrile), with 1 μg of enzyme per well, and incubated at 37 °C. The reaction was initiated by adding 3OC12HSL, and the decrease in absorbance at 572 nm was measured in 96-well plates using a Victor Nivo Multimode Microplate Reader (PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA) in a final volume of 200 μL.
Substrate solutions (5 or 50 mM in acetonitrile) were used to prepare a concentration range from 10 to 500 μM, keeping the final acetonitrile content constant at 5% in all assays. The inhibitor TQ416 (1) was dissolved in 100% acetonitrile to prepare a 1 mM stock solution, from which serial dilutions were made to obtain the following final concentrations: 0.25 µM, 0.5 µM, 0.75 µM, and 1.25 µM.
Each measurement included subtraction of a blank (no enzyme control) to correct for baseline drift. Initial velocities (v0) were plotted against substrate concentrations, and kinetic constants were calculated by fitting the data to the Lineweaver–Burk linear transformation of the Michaelis–Menten equation, using GraphPad Prism v6.01.

## Cell cultures
HeLa and A549 cell lines (ATCC, Manassas, VA, USA) were cultured in high-glucose Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM; Gibco™, high glucose, pyruvate) supplemented with 10% foetal bovine serum (FBS; Gibco) and 1% penicillin/streptomycin (Euroclone). Cells were maintained at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere containing 5% CO2.
A total of 1.5 × 106 cells were seeded in 10-cm dishes. After 24 h, cells were treated with 1 µM TQ416 or 1 µM PPM3 (both solubilised in 100% DMSO) for 2 h or 4 h. Cells treated with an equivalent volume of DMSO served as controls.
Following treatment, cells were scraped in 700 µL of lysis buffer (20 mM HEPES, pH 8.5; 150 mM NaCl; 1% NP-40; 5% glycerol; and 1× protease inhibitor cocktail). Lysates were incubated on ice for 30 min and centrifuged at 13,000 rpm for 15 min at 4 °C. Supernatants were collected, and protein concentration was determined using the Bradford assay.

## SDS-PAGE and Western blot analysis
Thirty micrograms of total protein were separated on 12.5% SDS-PAGE gels and transferred onto polyvinylidene difluoride (PVDF) membranes (Bio-Rad). Membranes were blocked in 5% BSA (Sigma-Aldrich) prepared in PBS containing 0.1% Tween-20 (PBST).
Membranes were incubated overnight at 4 °C with a mouse monoclonal anti-PON2 antibody (C-5, sc-374158; Santa Cruz Biotechnology; 1:1000). After washing with PBST, membranes were incubated for 1 h at room temperature with goat anti-mouse IgG (H + L) HRP-conjugated secondary antibody (Bio-Rad, catalogue #1706516) diluted 1:5000. Protein bands were visualised using Clarity Western ECL substrate (Bio-Rad) and imaged with the ChemiDoc system (Bio-Rad).
Membranes were then stripped using stripping buffer (Abcam) and reprobed with mouse anti-tubulin antibody (Cell Signalling Technology; 1:1000) as a loading control, followed by HRP-conjugated secondary antibody (dilution 1:5000). Incubation and chemiluminescent detection as described above.
Densitometric analysis was performed using ImageLab software (Bio-Rad).

## Molecular modelling
Homology Modelling. The primary sequence of human PON2 was submitted to AlphaFold v.2.0 for the generation of a homology model of its folding . The statistical confidence of the homology model was calculated with the pLDDT (predicted Local Difference Distance Test) scoring function, as implemented in AlphaFold v.2.0, allowing the identification of well-folded regions and regions with uncertain folding. The N-terminal transmembrane region, encompassing residues 1–48, was then removed from the structure with the aim of fitting the model to the corresponding sequence of the recombinant protein used for the biophysical and biochemical studies. The atomic coordinates of the homology model were submitted to the Protein Preparation Wizard (PPW) of Maestro for structural refinement, adding hydrogen atoms and capping the N- and C-terminal groups. Calcium ions were docked into the model according to their atomic coordinates observed in the crystal structure of human PON1 (pdb code: 3SRG) . A fast energy optimisation was carried out on the resulting model with the OPLS4 force field , setting a restrain of 0.30 Å on the atomic coordinate displacement of heavy atoms. The analysis of the geometrical quality of the final homology model was performed with the Ramachandran plot (Figure 4 of supplementary data) .
Mapping of Protein Sites. Putative ligand binding sites in the homology model of human PON2 were identified using SiteMap with default parameters as implemented in the Maestro (Schrödinger Release 2023–1: Maestro, Schrödinger, LLC, New York, NY, 2023), calculating the druggability score (Dscore) for each site . Specifically, the Dscore ranked the identified sites according to their suitability to establish binding interactions with drug-like compounds, scoring an undruggable site with value below 0.6, difficult/fair site with value amid 0.6 and 0.9, and acceptable/good site with value higher than 0.9. As a result, two binding sites (site #1 and #2) were identified in the homology model of PON2 with good druggability scores (DScore > 0.9).
Molecular Docking. The chemical structure of ligands was built using Maestro, assigning protonation and tautomeric states at pH = 7.2 with LigPrep (Schrödinger Release 2023–1: LigPrep, Schrödinger, LLC, New York, NY, 2023) . The geometry of the resulting chemical structures was optimised using OPLS4 force field with an energy gradient convergence criterion of 0.05 kJ Å−1 mol−1. For each ligand, a conformational search was then carried out using the “mixed torsional/low-mode sampling” algorithm of MacroModel (Schrödinger Release 2023–1: MacroModel, Schrödinger, LLC, New York, NY, 2023), a grid box was defined in site #1 and #2, with the centre located at coordinates x = −7.432, y = −13.118, z = 27.269 (site #1), and x = 0.3666, y = −39.5785, z = 19.8426 (site #2). The inner grid box was sized 20 × 20 × 20 Å in cases. Docking studies of the library of conformations generated for each ligand structure were carried out with Glide (Schrödinger Release 2023–1: Glide, Schrödinger, LLC, New York, NY, 2023) , using the standard precision (SP) method and the GScore scoring function (kcal/mol). The selection of the initial pose was set at the default values, scaling Van der Waals radii to a value of 0.80 for non-polar atoms with partial charge < 0.15. A number of 5000 poses per ligand was kept for the initial phase of docking within a scoring window of 100 kcal/mol. The best 400 poses per ligand were kept for energy minimisation. For each ligand, the top scored binding poses in site #1 and site #2 were stored for visual inspection of the intermolecular interactions.

## Results and discussion

## Chemistry
From a structural point of view, TQ416 (1) consists of 5,9-dimethyl-[1,2,4]triazolo[4,3-a]quinoline core bearing at C-1 position a sidechain, in turn consisting of a terminal aryl moiety connected to the heterocyclic nucleus by a two heteroatoms-containing, four terms alkyl spacer. Despite the three papers mentioned the use of TQ416 (1) , , , no references on its synthesis are available in the literature. By a retrosynthetic point of view, TQ416 (1), as well as its analogs, could be accessible through the reaction between [1,2,4]triazolo[4,3-a]quinoline-1-thiol (A) and a suitable ω-(aryloxy)alkyl halide (B), in turn obtained by the reaction of a phenol (C) and a 1,ω-alkyl dihalide (D) ( ).
 The preparation of 5,9-dimethyl[1,2,4]triazolo[4,3-a]quinoline-1-thiol (12) was realised as depicted in the . The condensation between o-toluidine (6) and diketene (7) gave the ß-ketoanilide 8, which was then heated in concentrated sulphuric acid to promote the cyclisation to the desired 4,8-dimethylquinolin-2-ol (9) . The subsequent treatment of 9 with phosphorus oxychloride in toluene at high temperature gave the corresponding 2-chloro derivative 10, obtained in 85% yield after purification by chromatography. Through a nucleophilic aromatic substitution with hydrazine monohydrate, 10 was then converted into the corresponding 11 (55% yield). The formation of the fused triazole nucleus, substituted at C1-position with a thiol group, was realised by reacting 11 with carbon disulphide in pyridine at 75 °C . The desired 12 was obtained in 35% yield. The preparation of (p-tolyloxy)ethyl bromide (14), needed for the installation of the side-chain of TQ416 (1), was first carried out following Awolade et al.’s procedure by adding dibromoethane to a solution of p-cresol (13) and aqueous sodium hydroxide, in the presence of a catalytic amount of tetrabutylammonium bromide (TBAB). However, in our hands, the reaction led to the exclusive formation of the dimer 1,2-bis(p-tolyloxy)ethane, and then different reaction conditions were screened; by changing the base from sodium hydroxide to potassium carbonate and by reversing the order of addition of the reagents, the desired 14 was finally obtained in 40% yield ( ). With the core 12 and (p-tolyloxy)ethyl bromide (14) in hands, the last step of the synthetic route was realised by reacting the two components in ethanol and in the presence of sodium ethoxide. After purification by chromatography, the title compound TQ416 (1) was obtained in 35% yield ( ). This route was easily adapted for the preparation of the analogue 5 devoid of the methyl group at C9-position compared to TQ416 (1), as well as for the obtainment of all the other novel compounds reported herein. Thus, aniline (15) was condensed with 7, to give the ß-ketoanilide 16, then cyclized to 17 under acidic conditions. The reaction of 17 with phosphorus oxychloride gave 18, then converted into the corresponding 4-methy-2-hydrazinoquinoline (19). The formation of 5-methyl[1,2,4]triazolo[4,3-a]quinoline-1-thiol (20) was realised by reacting 19 with carbon disulphide in pyridine at 75 °C ( ). Finally, the alkylation of the thiol 20 with 14 gave the desired 5-methyl-1-[(2-(p-tolyloxy)ethyl)thio][1,2,4]triazolo[4,3-a]quinoline (5) ( ).
 The preparation of TQ416’s analogues 2 and 3 required the alkylation of p-cresol (13) with 1,3-dichloropropane and 1,4-dibromobutane, respectively ( ).
 Using the same conditions above described for the preparation of 14, [(p-tolyl)oxy]propyl bromide (21) and [(p-tolyl)oxy]butyl bromide (22) were obtained in 45% and 60% yield, respectively. The alkylation of the thiol 12 with the bromides 21 and 22, gave the corresponding 5,9-dimethyl-1-[(4-(p-tolyloxy)propyl)thio][1,2,4]triazolo[4,3-a]quinoline (2) (30% yield) and 5,9-dimethyl-1-[(4-(p-tolyloxy)butyl)thio][1,2,4]triazolo[4,3-a]quinoline (3) (40% yield) ( ).
For the preparation of the derivatives 4a–k, characterised by the same side-chain length than TQ416 (1), but a different substitution at the terminal aryloxy group, the preparation of the appropriate 2-(aryloxy)ethyl bromides (24a–k) was necessary by reacting the appropriate substituted phenol (23a–k) with 1,2-dibromoethane in the presence of potassium carbonate ( ). With the suitable bromides in hands, the nucleophilic substitution by the triazoloquinoline 12 was performed to achieve the corresponding title compounds 4a–k ( ).
 

## Biophysical assessment and SAR considerations
TQ416 (1) and the library of its analogs 2–5 were submitted to both MST analysis and enzymatic assay with the aim to evaluate their binding properties towards PON2 and their ability to inhibit the lactonase activity of the protein, respectively. PON2 used in both the assays was a recombinant form expressed in E. coli (rPON2) . Figure S1 shows the Coomassie stain and western blot of the purified protein.
MST experiments were carried out using the fluorescently labelled rPON2 (NT650-PON2). First, the protocol was developed using TQ416 (1) as a control compound and then the entire library was screened. The results are reported in as dissociation constants (Kd) and confidence intervals, with signal to noise (S/N) ratio of the relative binding efficiency index (BEI).
 As a first observation, all ligand binding curves yielded an S/N value higher than 12, suggesting the robustness of the results. Notably, two compound subsets could be identified based on the profile of their relative binding curves ( ). The compounds belonging to the first group (hereafter group A), exemplified by TQ416 (1), showed two inflection points along the binding curve ( ), whereas the remaining derivatives, as compound 5 ( ), generated only one inflection point in the binding curve.
 This observation suggested that TQ416 (1), and the congeners belonging to group A may occupy two binding clefts of the protein with different affinities. On the contrary, a single binding event would suggest a single interaction into a single binding pocket of NT650-PON2. This hypothesis appeared plausible considering two structurally quite different compounds, such as TQ4126 (1) and 12, the latter derivative devoid of the sidechain: hence, it could be postulated that the presence of this structural feature was responsible of the second binding event showed by 1. However, the high structural similarity between other compounds of group B and ligands of group A prompted an alternative hypothesis yet. It was indeed likely that the compounds of group B may be endowed with two binding events like the congeners of group A, but that the two events were not experimentally observable due to the proximity of the values of the corresponding dissociation constants. In other words, the binding curve of the compounds belonging to group B showed only one single inflection point as the result of two overlapping ones.
The inspection of the dissociation constants of TQ416 (1), 2 and 3 allowed to cast lights on the effect of the elongation of the alkyl spacer on the binding. All three compounds showed a two-inflection point binding curve; however, the longer analogues 2 and 3 showed one order of potency reduction with respect to TQ416 (1) considering Kd1, and a shift from low to high micromolar value with regards to Kd2. The removal of the sidechain (as in 12) determined the suppression of the second binding event while the affinity remained good respect to TQ416 (1) for the observed binding event. Notwithstanding, the binding efficiency index of 12 (BEI = 25.8) was significantly better than that of TQ416 (1) (BEI = 20.3), highlighting that the main contribution to the binding energy originated from molecular interactions involving [1,2,4]triazolo[4,3-a]quinoline core of the compounds. The 9-nor analog of TQ416 (1), namely 5, showed only one binding event in the low micromolar range of potency, thus resulting in albeit one order of magnitude less affine than TQ416 (1). These observations suggested the relevance of the presence of C-9 methyl group that may favour π-interactions of the core with binding site residues (Phe291, Tyr192) through its electron donating effect, as well as it could generate a putative conformational constraining effect on the sidechain of TQ416 (1), positive for the binding. With regard to the methyl group present on the terminal phenyloxy group of the sidechain, it is worth noting that its shift from para position, as in the parent 1, to ortho (4a) or meta one (4b) determined a single binding event endowed with a weaker potency than the first binding event of the parent compound 1. Accordingly, it is likely that this methyl group, if located at ortho or meta position, provided steric hindrance to the phenyloxy moiety preventing the corresponding derivatives 4a and 4b from occupying the second binding cleft, but not the other pocket.
Different substituents were also assessed at the para position of the terminal phenyloxy moiety: with the exception of derivative 4j, bearing a nitro group that induced aggregation of NT650-PON2, the other substituents kept the first binding activity of the corresponding derivatives in the nanomolar or low micromolar range of potency, with 4h, characterised by the presence of the trifluoromethyl group resulting in the major improvement of potency for both binding events.
Three different behaviours were observed depending on the values of the substituent constant of the 1-octanol/water partition coefficient (π) (Table 1 of supplementary data) . The items with negative π values (as 4k and 4i) showed low micromolar binding activity, while compounds with positive π values (1, 4d–i) gave nanomolar binding activity for the Kd1. In the latter group, an inverse relationship was observed between the π value and Kd1: the more high was π value, the less the corresponding item was potent; the derivative 4h was an exception that suggested a beneficial effect of the para-trifluoromethyl group in strengthening the molecular interaction of the phenyloxy ring into the binding site of the enzyme .
Once observed the ability of all the items of the library to bind NT650-PON2, although to different extent and behaviour, we moved on to evaluate their ability to inhibit the enzymatic activity of the target.

## Screening of the library and kinetic analysis of rPON2 in the presence of the inhibitor TQ416 (1)
The lactonase activity of rPON2 was assessed by monitoring the pH decrease consequent to the hydrolysis of the substrate 3OC12HSL . Each compound was used at concentrations ranging from 0.3 to 100 µM. First, it was confirmed the ability of the known inhibitor TQ416 (1) to be a PON2’s inhibitor and the corresponding IC50 was calculated as 0.775 ± 0.060 µM. It is worth pointing out that, although TQ416 (1) was a known PON2 inhibitor, no IC50 value had been reported so far.
All the members of the library, albeit at different levels, were able to inhibit lactonase activity ( ).
 We were delighted to find that some of the member of the library exhibited lower IC50 values than that of TQ416 (1), thus demonstrating a greater inhibitory capacity than the parent compound. In particular, 2 showed an IC50 of approximately 56 nM, thus resulting 14 times more efficient than TQ416 (1) in inhibiting the lactonase activity of PON2. This finding, while confirming the crucial role played by the alkyl spacer length in determining the potency profile of the corresponding derivative, was not aligned with the result derived from MST study: in that case, indeed, the elongation of the alkyl spacer led to a compound still able to bind PON2, but endowed with a higher Kd value than that of TQ416 (1). On the contrary, enzymatic and MST assays agreed in evidencing that the presence of a fluorine atom, as in 4d and 4h, was positive for the enhancement of both inhibitory ability and binding potency.
The availability of TQ416 (1) gave us the opportunity to study the kinetic behaviour of rPON2 in the presence of increasing concentration of the inhibitor with the aim to evidence whether TQ416 (1) altered the enzyme’s kinetic parameters respect to those observed under standard condition .
The kinetic analysis of rPON2 activity on 3OC12HSL (ranging from 5 to 700 μM) in the presence of increasing concentrations of 1 (0.25, 0.5, 0.75, and 1.25 μM), as shown in , confirmed an inhibitory effect of the compound. Specifically, a decrease in Vmax was observed, while the KM remained largely unchanged, suggesting a non-competitive inhibition mechanism for TQ416 (1). Data were further analysed using the Lineweaver–Burk double reciprocal plot, shown in . As the concentration of TQ416 (1) increased, Vmax progressively decreased without significant variation in KM, reinforcing the hypothesis of a non-competitive inhibition of rPON2 by 1.
 

## Evaluation of the cellular effect of TQ416 (1) and 2
Based on the inhibitory effects observed on rPON2, we next assessed the impact of TQ416 (1) and the most potent analog within the library, namely 2, on endogenous PON2 in immortalised HeLa and A549 cell lines. As previously reported in the literature , HeLa and A549 cells were treated for 2 and 4 h with 1 µM TQ416 (1). Western blot (WB) analysis using a monoclonal anti-PON2 antibody revealed a time-dependent increase in endogenous PON2 expression in both cell lines. In HeLa cells, PON2 levels increased by approximately 20- and 40–50% at 2- and 4 h, respectively, whereas A549 cells showed a 15- and 45–60% increase of PON2 level at 2- and 4 h respectively, in comparison to untreated controls (Nt) ( ). Despite the increase in protein abundance, lactonase assays on 3OC12HSL demonstrated a substantial inhibition of enzymatic activity in the cells treated with TQ416 (1) ( ). Residual activity in HeLa cells dropped to ∼40- and ∼35% at 2- and 4 h, respectively ( C), whereas A549 cells retained only 45–50% activity compared with controls ( ). Thus, TQ416 (1) increased endogenous PON2 protein levels while simultaneously impairing its catalytic activity. A different regulatory profile emerged from the treatment of the cells with the novel inhibitor 2 ( ). In both cell lines, 2 promoted a decrease of both PON2 expression ( ) and lactonase activity ( ), albeit to differing degrees. In HeLa cells, PON2 levels declined by 30–40% at 2 h and approximately 50% at 4 h. In contrast, A549 cells showed a more moderate reduction of PON expression of 15–20% at 2 h and 25–30% at 4 h relative to untreated cells. Consistent with this decrease, lactonase activity dropped to 60–65% in HeLa at 2 h and 55–60% at 4 h, whereas A549 cells maintained 70–80% activity ( ).
 The different behaviour showed by the two inhibitors in cells was not surprising considering that tumour cells, as HeLa and A549 are, can be endowed by increased expression of PON2; moreover, the mechanism by which PON2 sustains cancer may differ among different cell lines, as recently reported . Furthermore, stability of the protein often regulated by post-translational modifications (PTMs) can be different in the two cell lines used: it could be possible that the stronger effect observed in HeLa cells was due to its more active ER-associated protein degradation (ERAD)/proteasome system, which accelerated the removal of misfolded or perturbed proteins, whereas A549 cells exhibit slower ER-associated turnover .
Together, these findings suggested that TQ416 (1) and its novel congener 2 not only were able to inhibit rPON2 but also modulated endogenous PON2 through distinct mechanisms that can either favour or disfavour activity. Importantly, the observation that lactonase activity was reduced even in the presence of increased PON2 expression highlighted the necessity of evaluating both protein levels and functional readouts when assessing PON2 modulation in physiological and pathological settings.

## Molecular modelling studies
The folding structure of human PON2 was generated using AlphaFold v.2.0 and edited removing the N-terminal transmembrane region (residues 1–48) to match the sequence of the recombinant protein used in biophysical and biochemical experiments. Calcium ions were docked into the model according to their atomic coordinates observed in the crystal structure of human PON1 (pdb code: 3SRG). The geometrical quality of the resulting model was assessed using the Ramachandran plot (Figure 4 of supplementary data) . Results showed that the model of PON2 had high geometrical quality, with most residues adopting ϕ- and ψ-torsional angle values in the core and allowed regions of the plot (PON2 = 98.1%). This was also in agreement with the average per-residue confidence score of AlphaFold v.2.0 that was very high (pLDDT = 96.94) for the model of PON2 structure ( ).
 Two ligand binding sites were identified in PON2, with comparable values of druggability score (Dscore, and ). Site #1 shaped a channel that started from the solvent exposed surface and led to the catalytic site in the core of the enzyme. It was lined by residues His114, Lys191, Tyr192, Thr195, Tyr196, Asp268, Phe291 and the catalytic calcium ion at the bottom that was held by the interactions with Glu53, Asn167, Asn223, Asp268 and Asn269. Site #2 was symmetrically located on the other solvent exposed side of PON2, beyond the structural calcium ion bound to residues Asp54 and Asp168. Site #2 was larger than site#1 and composed of an inner ring of polar residues (Asp56, Ser117, Asn226 and Ser271) and an outer ring of hydrophobic residues (Leu58, Ile120, Ile227 and Ile272).
 Docking studies were carried out using five representative compounds, selected among the most active ligands of the library (TQ416 (1), 2, 4d, 4e and 4h), with the aim of casting lights on their binding modes in sites #1 and #2. The top scored binding poses were selected and submitted to visual inspection. As a first observation, it was found that the energetic scores are slightly better at site#1 than site#2 ( ), suggesting the presence of stronger interactions in the former binding site. Specifically, it was found that the derivatives 4d and 4e adopted an extended binding mode which was conserved with the parent compound TQ416 (1), engaging the side chain of Phe291 with π–π stacking interactions from the aromatic scaffold and the substituted aryl group ( ). This pattern of interactions was also observed for 4h, albeit the central aromatic scaffold of this compound assumes a flipped conformation in the binding site ( ). Notably, the longer linker of the side chain of 2 determined the inversion of the binding mode of the compound, with the aryloxy moiety entering deeper into the core of the enzyme and closer to the catalytic calcium ion (distance 3.3 Å, ), whereas the central aromatic scaffold keeps π–π stacking interactions with Phe291. The closest distance to the catalytic calcium ion observed for 2 might account for its nanomolar inhibition of potency, which is unique among tested compounds. Regarding site #2, all docked compounds adopted a folded conformation in the binding site, with the triazole ring of the aromatic core making hydrogen bond interactions with the side chains of Ser117 and Asn226 ( ). No aromatic interactions were observed, whereas the central scaffold and the aryl side chain of the ligands engaged with van der Waals contact the outer ring of hydrophobic residues.
 

## Conclusions
This study focused on the development of chemical probes for human PON2, a key member of the PON family, implicated in various pathologies, including cardiovascular, neurodegenerative, metabolic diseases, infections, and cancer. Despite growing interest in PON2, selective inhibitors of this enzyme have been scarcely explored. Prior to this work, TQ416 (1) was the only known compound with documented inhibitory activity but lacking a SAR analysis. The lack of a reported synthesis for TQ416 (1) along with its unavailability on the market has made its disappearance from the scene after its discovery. The first convergent route for the preparation of TQ416 (1), herein reported, has been applied to the synthesis of a library of fourteen TQ416 analogous, which allowed us to describe a first attempt of SAR study. Within the library, three derivatives − 2, 4d and 4h – demonstrated improved in vitro inhibitory potency with respect to the parent TQ416 (1). The compound 2, structurally characterised by a longer alkyl spacer than 1, was the most potent inhibitor of the lactonase activity of PON2 of the series, underscoring the importance of this region for activity.
Moreover, a key outcome of this study was the characterisation of inhibition kinetic of TQ416 (1), which first demonstrated its profile of a non-competitive inhibitor.
Ligand binding experiments, mapping of protein sites and molecular docking calculations supported the presence of two binding sites in the structure of human PON2, with a preferential interaction at site#1 wherein better interaction scores (GScores) were observed for the docked compounds (1, 2, 4d, 4e, 4h). This binding site was constituted by a channel linking the solvent exposed surface to the catalytic site of the enzyme. The dissociation constants (Kd1) assigned to site#1 ranged from nanomolar to low micromolar range of potency. With this regard, it should be mentioned that Kd1 values encode the strength of the interaction of a ligand to a binding site and do not necessarily correlate with the functional outcome of enzyme inhibition, such as in cases wherein the binding site is different from the catalytic site (i.e. non-competitive inhibition). Accordingly, the nanomolar inhibition potency (IC50) of compound 2 was explained by the presence of an optimal linker in its chemical structure, which allowed the aryloxy moiety entering deeper into the channel and closer to the catalytic calcium ion, while keeping π–π stacking interactions between the central aromatic scaffold and Phe291.
In summary, our work provided the first chemical probes for PON2 which will be valuable tools for the study of the biology of this protein; furthermore, our findings showed that both TQ416 (1) and the novel inhibitor 2 could inhibit endogenous PON2 activity in tumour-derived cells, despite exerting opposite effects on the expression level of the protein. These distinct responses likely reflected inherent differences between the two cancer cell lines and their PON2-related regulatory pathways. Collectively, these observations indicated that such molecules may hold potential for future applications aimed at modulating PON2 function, including exploration of their use alongside chemotherapeutic approaches.
