## Abstract

## Background
Although mitochondrial dysfunction is an established hallmark of ventricular remodeling, the molecular mechanisms governing this process remain incompletely characterized. This study systematically investigates the regulatory role of Src homology 2 domain-containing protein tyrosine phosphatase 2 (SHP2) in AMPK-mediated mitochondrial dysfunction and its pathological consequences in ventricular remodeling.

## Methods
Ventricular remodeling was induced by angiotensin II (Ang II) or isoproterenol (ISO) in vivo and in vitro, with assessment of hypertrophy and fibrosis using echocardiography, molecular analyses and histopathology. Bulk RNA-seq demonstrated that SHP099, an SHP2 inhibitor, modulated the AMPK pathway. LC-MS/MS and Co-IP confirmed the interaction between AMPK and SHP2. We constructed SHP2 mutant plasmids and employed AAV9-mediated cardiac SHP2 overexpression together with AMPK activator A769662 administration to validate the functional significance of this interaction.

## Results
SHP2 expression is upregulated in Ang II- or ISO-induced ventricular remodeling models. Both SHP099 (pharmacological inhibition) or siSHP2 (genetic knockdown) ameliorated cardiac hypertrophy and mitochondrial dysfunction, whereas SHP2 overexpression exacerbated these pathological changes. Mechanistically, SHP2 directly interacts with AMPK via its protein tyrosine phosphatase (PTP) domain at cysteine 459, dephosphorylating AMPK at Thr172. AAV9-mediated SHP2 overexpression aggravated ventricular remodeling, which was rescued by AMPK activator A769662.

## Conclusions
This study demonstrates that SHP2 acts as a key phosphatase directly dephosphorylates AMPK, thereby triggering mitochondrial dysfunction and exacerbating ventricular remodeling. Our findings provide novel mechanistic insights into heart failure progression and highlight SHP2 as a potential therapeutic target for heart failure treatment.

## Introduction
Ventricular remodeling is caused by multiple factors such as an increase in cell volume, impaired contractile function, post-translational modification of proteins, fibrosis, and inflammatory response [ ]. Chronic pressure-overload stimulation on the heart can trigger cardiac hypertrophy and fibrosis. This pathological change in structure and/or function is known as ventricular remodeling, and the ultimate clinical outcome for patients is a gradual progression to heart failure (HF) [ ]. HF is the leading cause of cardiovascular disease-related deaths globally. Although certain advancements have been made in the standardized pharmacological treatment and device-based intervention for HF, its global incidence and mortality rates still rank first [ ]. Consequently, identifying key molecular regulators of ventricular remodeling and elucidating their mechanisms holds critical clinical significance.
Mitochondria are the primary energy producers in the heart, generating approximately 95% of cardiac ATP through integrated processes including the electron transport chain (ETC), tricarboxylic acid cycle (TCA), and oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) [ ]. Adenosine monophosphate-activated protein kinase (AMPK), a cellular energy sensor, regulates multiple cardiovascular physiological processes. Our previous work revealed that PRL2 induces AMPK inactivation and mitochondrial dysfunction in Angiotensin II (Ang II)-stimulated myocardial hypertrophy [ ]. AMPK can suppress the expression of Nur77 and Drp1, thereby improving mitochondrial function and alleviating cardiac injury caused by diabetes [ ]. Overexpression of TLR9 positively regulates mitochondrial biofunction and activates the AMPK-PGC1α pathway, thereby protecting the heart against diabetic cardiomyopathy [ ]. Studies have also shown that FGF21 prevents mitochondrial dysfunction and oxidative stress in human induced pluripotent stem cell-derived cardiomyocytes by activating the AMPK/FOXO3/SIRT3 signaling pathway [ ]. Therefore, targeting mitochondrial homeostasis and AMPK activity represents a potential therapeutic mechanism for HF.
Protein tyrosine phosphatases (PTPs) are essential regulators of multiple signaling pathways involved in cell proliferation, differentiation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and inflammation [ ]. Among the 107 PTP-encoding genes in the human genome, 17 encode non-receptor protein tyrosine phosphatases (NRPTPs) [ ]. Src homology 2 domain-containing protein tyrosine phosphatase 2 (SHP2/PTPN11), a ubiquitously expressed NRPTP, contains a conserved structure comprising two SH2 domains, a PTP domain, and a C-terminal tail, and plays critical roles in cardiac development and function [ , ]. In vitro studies demonstrated that SHP2 inhibition in cultured human lupus T cells reduces IFN-γ and IL-17 A/F production, suggesting its regulatory role in systemic lupus erythematosus pathogenesis [ ]. SHP099, a pharmacological SHP2 inhibitor, suppresses signal transduction and STAT3 phosphorylation, thereby inhibiting tumor cell proliferation [ ]. Our previous work showed that SHP2 contributes to renal inflammation in diabetic mice, and macrophage-specific SHP2 knockout alleviates both renal inflammation and diabetic nephropathy [ ]. In a Noonan syndrome cohort, 50% of cases harbored PTPN11 missense mutations, which were associated with cardiac defects [ ]. Investigating SHP2’s role in cardiac morphogenesis remains challenging due to the early embryonic lethality of SHP2 homozygous knockout mice [ ]. Moreover, the mechanisms by which SHP2 contributes to cardiovascular diseases are incompletely understood, warranting further studies to define its precise functions and downstream signaling pathways.
In this study, integrative analysis of public datasets and experimental validation revealed significant SHP2 upregulation during ventricular remodeling, confirming its pivotal role in this pathology. Both in vivo and in vitro experiments demonstrated that inhibition of SHP2, either by pharmacological or genetic intervention, could effectively alleviate Ang II- or ISO-induced myocardial hypertrophy and fibrosis. Bulk RNA-seq combined with proteomic mass spectrometry identified SHP2-AMPK binding, which promotes AMPKT172 dephosphorylation and consequent mitochondrial dysfunction. Collectively, our findings establish SHP2 as a promising therapeutic target for ventricular remodeling.

## Methods

## Animals
Male C57BL/6J mice (weighing 20 ± 2 g) were obtained from the Laboratory Animal Center of Hangzhou Medical College (Hangzhou, China). The mice were housed in a controlled environment with a temperature of 23–26 °C, relative humidity of 40%–60%, and a 12-hour light-dark cycle, with access to food and water ad libitum. Animals used in these studies were acclimated to laboratory conditions for two weeks. All experimental procedures were conducted in accordance with the guidelines of the National Institutes of Health (USA). The study was approved by the Ethics Committee of Laboratory Animal Care and Welfare at Hangzhou Medical College with approval number ZJCLA-IACUC-20,040,076.

## Establishment of animal models
The ventricular remodeling model was established through the subcutaneous implantation of angiotensin II (Ang II; 1 µg/kg/min; HY-13948, MedChemExpress, New Jersey, USA) or isoproterenol (ISO; 15 µg/kg/min; cat. no. HY-B0468, MedChemExpress). These reagents were administered via osmotic minipumps (cat. no. Alzet model 1004, DURECT Corporation, Cupertino, USA) for four weeks. Mice implanted with minipumps containing normal saline served as the sham group. During the surgery, C57BL/6J mice were anesthetized with 2% isoflurane mixed with oxygen, delivered through an air anesthesia machine. An osmotic pump was then implanted subcutaneously on the back of mouse. Starting from the third week, the Ang II-challenged mice were randomly divided into three experimental groups (n = 6 per group): the vehicle group (0.5% Carboxymethylcellulose sodium (CMC-Na) solution), the low-dose SHP099 group (2.5 mg/kg), and the high-dose SHP099 group (5 mg/kg). SHP099 (99.80% purity; cat. no. S6388, Selleck, Houston, USA), an SHP2 inhibitor, was diluted in 0.5% CMC-Na solution, and administered to the mice via oral gavage for last two weeks. Meanwhile, the vehicle group received an equivalent volume of the CMC-Na solution over the same period. Blood pressure was monitored using a noninvasive tail-cuff pressure analysis system (BP-2010 A, Softron Biotechnology, Beijing, China), and body weight was recorded weekly. The ISO treatment mice were subjected to the same experimental procedure. Mice implanted with minipumps containing normal saline served as the sham group. After four weeks, the cardiac function of the mice was evaluated using an ultrasound imaging system. Following this assessment, the mice were sacrificed with an intraperitoneal injection of phenobarbital sodium (40 mg/kg), and all hearts were harvested. The heart weight and tibia lengths were measured. For further analysis, heart tissues were either snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen for subsequent gene and protein expression studies or fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde for histological examination. Blood samples were also collected, and serum was obtained by centrifuging the samples at 4℃ and 3000 rpm for 15 min, which was later used for ANP (cat. no. JL 20612, Jianglai, Shanghai, China) and Ang II (cat. no. JL31138, Jianglai) determination.

## Cardiac function evaluation
Systolic function in hearts was determined by a multi-mode small animal ultrasound imaging system (VINNO Technology, Suzhou, China). For echocardiography, anesthesia was induced with 2% isoflurane, and the mice were positioned on a plate without the use of mechanical ventilation. Two-dimensional M-mode echocardiography was performed from a long-axis view at the level of the mitral valve. Fractional shortening (FS) was determined by calculating the percentage difference between left ventricular end-systolic and end-diastolic diameters (LVESD and LVEDD, respectively) using the formula FS% = (LVEDD - LVESD) / LVEDD × 100%. Ejection fraction (EF) was derived as (LVEDV - LVESV) / LVEDV × 100%. For accuracy, each echocardiographic parameter was assessed using images captured from five consecutive cardiac cycles in each mouse.

## Cell culture
Human embryonic kidney line (Hek293T) cells were obtained from the Stem Cell Bank of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (Shanghai, China). The cells were cultured in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM) containing 4.5 g/L glucose and 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS), and incubated in the incubator at 37℃ with 5% CO2. To extract and isolate primary cardiomyocytes, specifically neonatal rat cardiomyocytes (NRCMs), a rigorous protocol was followed as previously reported [ , ]. Briefly, the heart tissues of neonatal rats were excised, washed in PBS, and digested using a mixture of pancreatin and collagenase. Fibroblasts were removed through differential adherence culture. Both fibroblasts and myocytes were cultured in DMEM containing 4.5 g/L glucose and 10% FBS incubated in incubator at 37℃ with 5% CO2. After 48 h of culture, NRCMs exhibited regular pulsations and were subsequently used for experiments. Cells were transfected with siRNA or expression plasmids for further experiments.

## Cell viability assay
Cell viability was assessed using the Cell Counting Kit-8 assay (CCK-8; Dojindo Laboratory, Kumamoto, Japan) in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, NRCMs were seeded in 96-well plates at a density of 5,000 cells per well and cultured overnight to allow for adherence. The cells were then exposed to various concentrations of SHP099 (0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100, and 1000 µM). After 24 h of incubation, the cells were washed with PBS and subsequently incubated in 100 µl of DMEM containing 10% CCK-8 solution for 1 h at 37℃. The optical density was measured at 450 nm using a Microplate Reader (Bio-Rad, CA, USA).

## Small-interfering RNA (siRNA) Silencing and plasmid overexpression
The SHP2 gene was silenced using SHP2 siRNA (forward: 5ʹ-GGGACUACUAUGACCUCUATT-3ʹ; reverse: 5ʹ-UAGAGGUCAUAGUA-GUCCCTT-3ʹ) and AMPK siRNA (forward: 5ʹ-GUGGCAGUUAAGAUCUUAATT-3ʹ; reverse: 5ʹ-UUAAGAUCUUAACUGCCACTT-3ʹ), which were obtained from GenePharma Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Additionally, plasmids such as Flag-AMPK-WT, HA-SHP2-WT, HA-SHP2-lacking SH2 domain (HA-SHP2-ΔSH2), HA-SHP2-lacking PTP domain (HA-SHP2-ΔPTP), and SHP2 carrying the C459A mutation (HA-SHP2-C459A) were acquired from Hunan Keai Medical Equipment Co., Ltd. (Changsha, China). These siRNAs or plasmids were transfected into cells using jetPRIME Reagent (cat. no. PT-114-15, Polyplus-transfection SA).

## Rhodamine phalloidin staining
The surface area of primary cardiomyocytes was determined using rhodamine phalloidin staining. After the NRCMs exhibited regular pulsations, the cells were washed with PBS and fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde. Following three rinses with PBS, the cells were permeabilized with 0.5% Triton X-100 for 20 min. Subsequently, the samples were stained with Actin-Tracker Red-Rhodamine (100 nM; cat. no. C2207S, Beyotime, Shanghai, China) for 1 h at room temperature in the dark. DAPI (cat. no. C1005, Beyotime) was then used to stain the nuclei. Images were captured using an inverted microscope (Leica Microsystems, Wetzlar, Germany). Fluorescence images were randomly captured by an investigator who was blinded to the treatment assignments.

## Real-time PCR analysis
Total RNA was extracted from heart tissues and cells using the Trizol reagent (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA). One microgram of RNA was reverse-transcribed into cDNA using the Hifair III 1st Strand cDNA Synthesis SuperMix (cat. no. 11141ES10, Yeasen Biotechnology, Shanghai, China). Quantitative PCR was performed with the Hieff UNICON qPCR SYBR Green Master Mix (cat. no. 11198ES08, Yeasen Biotechnology) on a Bio-Rad CFX96 Touch Real-Time PCR Detection System (Bio-Rad Laboratories). The primers for Ptpn11, atrial natriuretic peptide (Anp), myosin heavy chain 7 (Myh7), collagen type I alpha 1 chain (Col1a1), and transforming growth factor beta 1 (Tgfb1) were synthesized by Sangon Biotech (Shanghai, China), with sequences provided in Supplementary Table S1. The expression level of β-actin was used to normalize the data.

## MitoSox fluorescence staining
Mitochondrial reactive oxygen species (mtROS) were detected using MitoSox™ Red (cat. no. M36008, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA). The cardiac tissues were embedded in paraffin. Paraffin sections were cut to be 5 μm thick. For NRCMs, cells were collected. Both tissue and cell samples were washed three times with Hank’s balanced salt solution (HBSS) and then incubated with 5 mM MitoSox™ Red at 37 °C in the dark for 30 min. After incubation, the samples were washed three times with HBSS and placed in HBSS for imaging. The images were captured using an inverted microscope at 200x magnification (Leica Microsystems).

## Measurement of ATP content
The ATP levels in NRCMs were quantified using a firefly luciferase-based ATP assay kit (cat. no. S0026, Beyotime). To begin, the growth medium was discarded, and the NRCMs were lysed. Following lysis, the lysate was then centrifuged at 12,000 g for 5 min at 4 °C, and the supernatant was collected. The total protein concentration was quantified using a Bicinchoninic Acid (BCA) protein assay kit (cat. no. P0010, Beyotime). Subsequently, the ATP assay working solution and the collected supernatant were combined in a 96-well white plate. The ATP concentration was expressed in nmol/mg of protein.

## Measurements of oxygen consumption
The oxygen consumption rate in NRCMs was evaluated using a SeahorseTM XFe96 extracellular flux analyzer (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA), as described in previous studies [ ]. Approximately 0.8–1.2 million cells were placed into the chamber for analysis. After measuring the basal respiration, ATP synthase inhibitor oligomycin (0.1 mM), the uncoupling agent carbonyl cyanide 4-(trifluoromethoxy) phenylhydrazone (FCCP; 0.01 mM), and antimycin A (10 mM) were sequentially added to the chamber to assess mitochondrial proton leak and maximal respiration. The final data were adjusted based on the cell count obtained from the chamber.

## Histopathology analysis
The harvested cardiac tissues were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde at room temperature for 48 h and then embedded in paraffin. Paraffin Sect. (5 μm thick) were stained with hematoxylin-eosin (H&E), Masson trichrome, or Sirius red according to standard protocols. Cardiac pathology alterations were subsequently examined using a light microscope (200× magnification; Leica Microsystems). To assess the cross-sectional areas of cardiac myocytes, the tissue sections were stained with FITC-conjugated wheat germ agglutinin (WGA), and the nuclei were stained with DAPI. Images were acquired with an inverted microscope (200× magnification; Leica Microsystems). The cell sizes were analyzed using ImageJ software (NIH, Bethesda, MD, USA). For each staining method, three sections from each group were analyzed, with four randomly selected microscopic fields assessed by an investigator who was blinded to the treatment groups.

## Immunofluorescence analysis
For immunofluorescence staining, frozen heart sections were prepared. In brief, heart tissues were embedded in Optimal Cutting Temperature compound and sectioned into 5-µm thick slices. The slides were then incubated overnight at 4 °C with primary antibodies, including SHP2 (1:100; cat. no. 3751, Cell Signaling Technology, USA) and α-actinin (1:100; cat. no. ab9465, Abcam, Cambridge, UK). The following day, after washing with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), the sections were stained with secondary antibodies conjugated to Alexa Fluor 488 (1:500; cat. no. GB25303, Servicebio, China) or Cy3 (1:500; cat. no. GB21301, Servicebio) for 2 h at room temperature in the dark. The nuclei were stained with DAPI for 20 min. All stained sections were examined using a confocal microscope (200× magnification; A1R-SIM-STORM, Nikon, Tokyo, Japan). Fluorescence images were captured randomly by an investigator who was blinded to the treatment groups.

## RNA-seq analysis
The RNA quality of heart samples was primarily assessed with an Agilent 2100 bioanalyzer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The mRNA was isolated from 1 mg of total RNA by employing Oligo(dT)-attached magnetic beads. The purified mRNA was then fragmented into smaller pieces using fragment buffer at an optimal temperature. Subsequently, random hexamer-primed reverse transcription was used to transcribe the fragmented RNA into cDNA. The construction of the cDNA library and sequencing were carried out on the BGISEQ-500 platform (Beijing Genomics Institute, Shenzhen, China). High-quality reads were aligned to the mouse reference genome using Bowtie2 (version 2.2.5) and HISAT2 (version 2.0.4). Gene expression levels were normalized to fragments per kilobase of exon model per million mapped reads (FPKM) using RSEM (version 1.2.12). Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified using the R package DESeq2 and filtered using a threshold of an FDR-adjusted P-value (Q-value) < 0.05 and |log₂FC| > 1. Gene set enrichment analysis was performed, considering a P-value < 0.05, a false discovery rate < 0.25, and a normalized enrichment score > 1 as significant criterion.

## Transmission electron microscopy analysis
Freshly isolated heart tissues were cut into 1 mm3 pieces and fixed in glutaraldehyde. Following a 4-hour fixation at 4 °C, tissue samples were rinsed with PBS and subsequently fixed with 1% osmium tetroxide at 4 °C for 2 h. The samples were then stained with 2% uranyl acetate for 2 h after being washed in water three times. Dehydration was carried out in a series of buffers: 50% alcohol for 15 min, 70% alcohol for 15 min, 90% alcohol for 15 min, followed by 100% alcohol for 15 min, and two rounds of 100% acetone for 20 min each. Afterward, the samples were immersed in 100% acetone for 2 h at 25 °C and then embedded overnight in pure Epon 812 resin (14120, Electron Microscopy Sciences) at 30 °C. Ultrathin sections were collected on formvar-coated grids and stained with uranyl acetate and lead citrate after polymerization at 37 °C for 12 h, 45 °C for 12 h, and 60 °C for 48 h. Images were captured using a FEI Tecnai G2 transmission electron microscope (Thermo Fisher Scientific).

## Flow cytometry
Intracellular levels of superoxide anions were measured using flow cytometry. NRCMs were incubated with 5 mM DCFH-DA (cat. no. EEA019, Thermo Fisher Scientific) for 1 h at room temperature in the dark. After incubation, the cells were washed twice with PBS, centrifuged at 300 g for 5 min at 4 °C, and then resuspended in 400 mL of PBS. The samples were promptly analyzed using a FACSCelesta flow cytometer (BD, Franklin Lakes, NJ, USA), and the resulting data were processed with FlowJo V10 software (FlowJo, Ashland, OR, USA).

## Western blot analysis
Whole proteins were isolated using a cell and tissue total protein extraction kit (cat. no. KC415, Kang Cheng Bioengineering, Shanghai, China). Protein concentrations were measured with a BCA protein assay kit (cat. no. P0010, Beyotime). The proteins were separated on SDS-PAGE gels and transferred onto PVDF membranes. After blocking with 5% non-fat milk or 5% BSA at room temperature for 1 h, the membranes were incubated overnight at 4 °C with primary antibodies, including p-AMPK (1:1000; cat. no. 50081, Cell Signaling Technology), AMPK (1:1000; cat. no. 5832, Cell Signaling Technology), SHP2 (cat. no. 3397, Cell Signaling Technology), GAPDH (1:1000; cat. no. 60004-1-Ig, Proteintech, Wuhan, China), Grim19 (1:1000; cat. no. sc-365978, Santa Cruz Biotechnology, USA), SDHA (1:1000; cat. no. ab14715, Abcam), UQCRC2 (1:1000; cat. no. ab14745, Abcam), MTCOI (1:1000; cat. no. ab14705, Abcam), and ATP5A (1:1000; cat. no. ab14748, Abcam). HRP-conjugated secondary antibodies (1:3000, Servicebio) were applied for 2 h at room temperature. Specific protein bands were detected using an ECL chemiluminescence kit, and band density was quantified using ImageJ software (NIH, Bethesda). GAPDH was used as the internal control.

## Co-immunoprecipitation (Co-IP)
For Co-IP assay, protein A + G Agarose beads (cat. no. P2055, Beyotime) were first incubated with the appropriate primary antibody for 6 h at 4 °C. Proteins were isolated using Cell Lysis Buffer for Western and IP (cat. no. P0013, Beyotime) containing a protease inhibitor cocktail. A small portion of the lysate was set aside as input. The remaining protein lysate was then incubated with the primary antibody-coated beads overnight at 4 °C. After incubation, the mixture was centrifuged, and the supernatant was discarded. The Protein A + G Agarose beads were washed and then resuspended in loading buffer (cat. no. P0015L, Beyotime). The samples were heat-denatured at 100 °C for 5 min and prepared for subsequent immunoblotting.

## Statistical analysis
Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism software (version 8.0.1; GraphPad Software, Inc., La Jolla, CA). Data are presented as mean ± SEM. For normally distributed data with equal variance, Unpaired Student’s t-test was applied for comparing between two groups, and one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukey’s multiple comparisons test was applied for comparing multiple groups of data. For normally distributed data with unequal variance, Welch’s t-test was applied for comparison between two groups, and Welch’s ANOVA with Dunnett’s T3 multiple comparisons was performed to analyze multiple groups of data. For the data with non-normal distribution, Mann-Whitney U test was performed to analyze two-group comparisons, and Kruskal-Wallis test with Dunn’s multiple comparisons for multiple groups. For samples that required a single individual to be measured more than once, data sets were analyzed independently using two-way ANOVA analysis with a single pooled variance and a Tukey correction for pairwise comparisons within groups for parametric data. P value < 0.05 was considered as statistical significance.

## Results

## SHP2 is upregulated in ventricular remodeling
To investigate the alterations in the PTPNs subfamily associated with ventricular remodeling, we analyzed the gene expression profiles of PTPNs in 6 control subjects without heart failure (Ctrl) and 21 patients diagnosed with non-ischemic cardiomyopathy (NICM), utilizing data obtained from the public database (GSE1869). Notably, PTPN11 (the gene encoding SHP2) was significantly upregulated in the NICM group (Fig.  A). This finding was further validated by three additional datasets from human heart tissues (GSE180313, GSE89714, and GSE135055), all of which consistently demonstrated elevated mRNA levels of PTPN11 in heart tissues from patients with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) or heart failure (HF) (Fig.  B-D). To validate these findings, we established mouse models of ventricular remodeling induced by either angiotensin II (Ang II; 1 µg/kg/min) or isoproterenol (ISO; 15 µg/kg/min) administered via osmotic minipumps. After four weeks, cardiac tissues were harvested to assess Ptpn11 mRNA levels. Both models demonstrated a significant upregulation of Ptpn11 expression during ventricular remodeling (Fig.  E-F). Similarly, the protein levels of SHP2 were also elevated in the ventricular remodeling models (Fig.  G-H). Stimulation of primary neonatal rat cardiomyocytes (NRCMs) with Ang II or ISO demonstrated a time-dependent upregulation of SHP2 protein expression (Fig.  I-J). Furthermore, immunofluorescence staining confirmed enhanced SHP2 expression in Ang II-induced heart tissues (Fig.  K). Collectively, these findings underscore that SHP2 may play a critical role in the pathological progression of ventricular remodeling.
 

## Targeting SHP2 at Cys459 attenuates ventricular remodeling
To investigate the role of SHP2 in ventricular remodeling, NRCMs were stimulated with Ang II (1 µM) or ISO (1 µM) for 24 h and concurrently treated with SHP099, an allosteric inhibitor of SHP2 that suppresses its phosphatase activity by stabilizing the auto-inhibitory closed conformation [ ]. NRCMs were exposed to increasing concentrations of SHP099 (0.01-1,000 µM), and cell viability was assessed. The results showed that SHP099 at concentrations ranging from 0.01 to 10 µM did not affect the viability of NRCMs compared to the control group (Fig. S1A). Therefore, SHP099 at concentrations of 1 µM and 5 µM was used in subsequent experiments. Ang II stimulation increased the mRNA levels of cardiomyocyte hypertrophy markers (Anp and Myh7) and fibrosis-related genes (Col1a1 and Tgfb1) in NRCMs, whereas SHP099 treatment markedly suppressed the expression of these genes (Fig. A-D). SHP099 also attenuated Ang II-induced cardiomyocyte area enlargement (Fig. E). Furthermore, in ISO-stimulated NRCMs, SHP099 similarly reversed the upregulation of hypertrophy and fibrosis markers and reduced cardiomyocyte area (Fig. S1B-F).
 To further elucidate the functional role of SHP2, we constructed siRNA (siSHP2) and an SHP2 overexpression plasmid (SHP2OE) to knockdown or overexpress SHP2, respectively (Fig. S2A-B). We observed that SHP2 knockdown suppressed the mRNA levels of hypertrophic and fibrotic genes, whereas SHP2 overexpression exacerbated their expression in Ang II-stimulated NRCMs (Fig. F-I). Similarly, SHP2 knockdown effectively reduced Ang II-induced enlargement of the area of NRCMs, while SHP2 overexpression further increased the area of NRCMs (Fig. J). Moreover, similar results were observed in ISO-stimulated NRCMs following SHP2 knockdown or overexpression (Fig. S2D-H). SHP2 is reported to dephosphorylate downstream proteins through its catalytic motif at cysteine 459 (Cys459) [ ]. To investigate whether SHP2 regulates ventricular remodeling via Cys459, we designed and constructed a site-specific mutation plasmid, SHP2C459A (substituting cysteine with alanine at position 459), and a wild-type SHP2 (SHP2WT) plasmid, both of which were stably expressed in cardiomyocytes (Fig. S2C). Using siSHP2 to knockdown basal SHP2 expression, we found that NRCMs transfected with SHP2C459A exhibited reduced expression of hypertrophy- and fibrosis-related genes, as well as decreased cell area upon Ang II stimulation, compared to those transfected with SHP2WT (Fig. K-O). Collectively, these findings demonstrate that SHP2 regulates Ang II-induced ventricular remodeling through its C459 site in cardiomyocytes.

## SHP099 effectively attenuates Ang II-induced ventricular remodeling in mice
To elucidate the significance of SHP2 in Ang II-induced ventricular remodeling, we infused mice with Ang II (1 mg/kg/min) for 4 weeks. Starting from the third week of Ang II modeling, two doses of the SHP2 inhibitor SHP099 were administered (Fig.  A). SHP099 did not alter Ang II-induced systolic blood pressure elevation (Fig.  B). Additionally, neither body weight nor serum Ang II levels were influenced by SHP099 (Fig.  C-D). Echocardiographic showed that SHP099 improved cardiac function, increasing ejection fraction (EF%) and fractional shortening (FS%) (Fig.  E-G). SHP099 also reduced serum ANP levels and suppressed Anp and Myh7 expression in heart tissue (Fig. S3A, Fig.  H-I). Consistently, treatment with SHP099 reversed the increase in heart size induced by Ang II, and the ratio of heart weight/tibia length (HW/TL) was significantly reduced (Fig.  J-K). H & E and WGA staining showed that SHP099 markedly reduced Ang II-induced structural changes in heart tissue (Fig.  L). The results of Masson staining, Sirius red staining, and the changes in the mRNA levels of fibrosis-related genes (Col1a1 and Tgfb1) collectively indicated that SHP099 treatment alleviated Ang II-induced cardiac fibrosis (Fig.  M, Fig. S3B-D). Taken together, these results demonstrate that SHP099 effectively attenuates Ang II-induced ventricular remodeling in mice.
 

## SHP099 suppresses ISO-induced ventricular remodeling in mice
On this basis, we established an ISO-induced mouse model to further validate the role of SHP2 in ventricular remodeling. Mice received continuous subcutaneous ISO infusion (0.25 µL/h) for 4 weeks to induce ventricular remodeling. At the third week of ISO treatment, mice were treated with two different doses of SHP099 (Fig.  A). Compared with the Sham group, ISO induced cardiac dysfunction, while SHP099 restored cardiac function (Fig.  B-D). SHP099 treatment also mitigated the ISO-induced increase in serum ANP levels and the expression of Anp and Myh7 (Fig.  E-F, Fig. S4A). Moreover, SHP099 treatment significantly reduced ISO-induced myocardial hypertrophy and fibrosis (Fig.  G-J, Fig. S4B-D). These results indicate that pharmacological inhibition of SHP2 can also alleviate ISO-induced ventricular remodeling in mice.
 

## SHP2 suppression ameliorates ventricular remodeling by modulating AMPK phosphorylation
Subsequently, we conducted bulk RNA-seq analysis on the Sham, Ang II, and Ang II + SHP099 (5 µM) groups to explore the molecular mechanism of SHP2 in ventricular remodeling. Compared to Sham, the Ang II group showed 104 downregulated and 158 upregulated differentially expressed genes (DEGs) (Fig. A). Comparison between the Ang II + SHP099 group with the Ang II group, 1106 genes were downregulated and 3749 genes were upregulated (Fig. B). Both Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) and Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) analyses of the DEGs indicated that the AMPK signaling pathway was highly enriched (Fig. C-E, Fig. S5A). These findings suggest that AMPK may be a key factor through which SHP2 deficiency alleviates ventricular remodeling. Under conditions of energy stress, AMPK can directly phosphorylate multiple pathways to restore energy balance [ ]. Notably, phosphorylation of Thr172 is essential for AMPK allosteric activation [ ]. In vivo experiments demonstrated that both Ang II and ISO significantly suppressed pT172-AMPK expression in heart tissues, whereas SHP2 inhibition enhanced AMPK phosphorylation levels (Fig. F, Fig. S5B). The same conclusion was drawn from the Ang II and ISO induced cell hypertrophy models of NRCMs (Fig. G, Fig. S5C). Overexpression of SHP2 in NRCMs revealed that pT172-AMPK was significantly reduced in a dose-dependent manner with increasing SHP2 levels (Fig. H). Transfection of the SHP2C459A mutant into Ang II-stimulated NRCMs resulted in a significant increase in pT172-AMPK (Fig. I). Finally, we investigated whether SHP2 affects Ang II-induced cardiomyocyte hypertrophy and fibrosis through AMPK. As shown in Fig. J, in NRCMs with AMPK knocked down by siRNA, treatment with SHP099 had no effect on cardiomyocyte area. Knockdown of AMPK also did not affect the changes in the mRNA levels of genes related to myocardial hypertrophy (Anp and Myh7) and fibrosis (Col1a1 and Tgfb1) (Fig. K-N). These results indicate that SHP2 inhibition attenuates ventricular remodeling in an AMPK-dependent manner by preventing Ang II/ISO-induced dephosphorylation of AMPK at T172.
 

## SHP2 inhibition reverses mitochondrial dysfunction in Ang II- or ISO-induced ventricular remodeling
Impaired mitochondrial function and quality contribute to the development of ventricular remodeling, ultimately leading to HF [ ]. Therefore, we investigated whether SHP2 affects the process of mitochondrial dysfunction in ventricular remodeling. MitoSOX staining showed Ang II treatment increased mitochondrial reactive oxygen species (ROS) accumulation, while SHP099 treatment reduced it (Fig. A). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) showed that SHP099 attenuated Ang II-induced mitochondrial swelling and cristae disruption (Fig. B). Inhibition of SHP2 significantly enhanced the ATP production in the heart tissues induced by Ang II (Fig. C). Consistently, SHP099 alleviated ISO-induced mitochondrial ROS accumulation and enhanced the ATP content in heart tissues (Fig. S6A-B). Moreover, we found that pharmacological inhibition of SHP2 reversed the Ang II-induced downregulation of mitochondrial respiratory chain complexes II and IV, while the expression levels of complexes I, III, and V remained unaltered (Fig. D, Fig. S6C). Similarly, treatment with SHP099 could reverse the decrease in complexes II and IV induced by ISO (Fig. S6D). Furthermore, DCFH-DA and MitoSox staining assays demonstrated that SHP2 inhibition significantly improved mitochondrial function in NRCMs exposed to Ang II or ISO (Fig. E-F, Fig. S6E-F). To further evaluate the impact of SHP2 inhibition on mitochondrial dysfunction, we performed oxygen consumption rate (OCR) analysis. NRCMs treated with SHP099 following Ang II or ISO stimulation exhibited increased ATP production, enhanced maximal respiratory capacity, and reduced proton leak (Fig. G-K, Fig. S6G and S6I). These findings collectively indicate that SHP099 alleviates mitochondrial dysfunction in cardiomyocytes. Restoration of the protein expression of mitochondrial complexes II and IV by SHP2 inhibition was also observed in NRCMs (Fig. L, Fig. S6H and S6J). Conversely, overexpression of the SHP2C459A mutant did not affect the protein expression of mitochondrial complexes II and IV in Ang II-induced NRCMs (Fig. M), indicating that SHP2 regulates mitochondrial homeostasis through its catalytic activity at the C459 site. Together, these results show that SHP2 inhibition reverses Ang II- or ISO-induced mitochondrial dysfunction.
 

## SHP2 interacts with AMPK via its PTP domain to regulate dephosphorylation
To determine how SHP2 regulates AMPK dephosphorylation, we performed LC-MS/MS and co-IP in Hek293T cells (Fig. A). Based on the LC-MS/MS results, we identified AMPK as a binding protein of SHP2 (Fig. B). As AMPK inhibits protein synthesis and is considered a putative inhibitor of cardiac hypertrophy [ ], we further investigated the interaction between SHP2 and AMPK. The results of the Co-IP assay indicated that the interaction between endogenous SHP2 and AMPK was enhanced in heart tissues treated with Ang II or ISO (Fig. C-D). Similarly, co-localization of the two proteins under the stimulation of Ang II or ISO was also observed in NRCMs (Fig. E). Furthermore, we employed bio-layer interferometry to confirm the direct interaction between recombinant human SHP2 (rhSHP2) and recombinant human AMPK (rhAMPK) proteins, with an association constant (KD) of 3.93 × 10− 8 M (Fig. F). We co-transfected HA-SHP2 and Flag-AMPK plasmids into Hek293T cells to exogenously validate the interaction between the two proteins (Fig. G). It has been reported that SHP2 encompasses two Src homology 2 (SH2) domains along with a canonical protein-tyrosine phosphatase (PTP) catalytic domain [ ]. To clarify which domain of SHP2 interacts with AMPK, we constructed SHP2WT, SHP2ΔSH2, and SHP2ΔPTP plasmids (Fig. H). Upon deletion of the PTP domain of SHP2, the interaction between SHP2 and AMPK was abrogated, and SHP2 was no longer capable of dephosphorylating AMPK at Thr172 (Fig. I-J). Collectively, these findings demonstrate that SHP2 directly interacts with AMPK through its PTP catalytic domain and exerts a dephosphorylation effect.
 

## AMPK activation alleviates SHP2-mediated cardiac dysfunction and mitochondrial damage
 Finally, we used a recombinant adeno-associated virus 9 (AAV9) vector carrying SHP2 and injected it into mice via the tail vein to overexpress SHP2. To explore the involvement of AMPK in SHP2-mediated regulation of ventricular remodeling, the AMPK agonist A769662 was intraperitoneally injected into mice at the third week after the induction of a ventricular remodeling model using Ang II (Fig.  A). Neither SHP2 overexpression nor AMPK activation altered Ang II-induced changes in systolic blood pressure, body weight, or serum Ang II levels (Fig.  B-D). Echocardiography showed that SHP2 overexpression exacerbated cardiac function impairment, while treatment with A769662 could improve cardiac function damage induced by Ang II or SHP2 overexpression (Fig.  E-G). Changes in serum ANP levels and the mRNA levels of Anp and Myh7 suggested that activating AMPK could alleviate ventricular remodeling in mice (Fig.  H-I, Fig. S7A). Additionally, activating AMPK significantly reduced myocardial injury and myocardial fibrosis levels induced by Ang II or SHP2 overexpression (Fig.  J-O, Fig. S7B-D). Finally, we confirmed that SHP2 overexpression decreased the phosphorylation of AMPK at T172 and the protein levels of mitochondrial respiratory chain complex II and IV, but activating AMPK could reverse this phenomenon (Fig.  P-Q). Similarly, treatment with A769662 improved mitochondrial dysfunction caused by SHP2 overexpression (Fig.  R). These results indicate that the AMPK signaling pathway plays a crucial regulatory role in the process of SHP2-mediated ventricular remodeling.
 

## Discussion
In this study, we found that the expression of SHP2 was upregulated in the ventricular remodeling models of mice. In both in vivo and in vitro experiments with Ang II or ISO treatment, pharmacological intervention using the SHP2 inhibitor SHP099 or transfection with siSHP2 could reduce cardiac dysfunction, myocardial hypertrophy, and fibrosis. Bulk RNA-seq results indicated that inhibiting SHP2 improved Ang II- or ISO-induced ventricular remodeling by regulating AMPK phosphorylation and simultaneously alleviated mitochondrial dysfunction in cardiomyocytes. Mechanistically, we discovered that the PTP domain of SHP2 directly binds to AMPK and dephosphorylates AMPK at the Thr172 site through its catalytic site C459. Activation of AMPK with A769662 significantly mitigated the detrimental effects of SHP2 overexpression on Ang II-induced ventricular remodeling in mice. This study provides a novel direction for developing SHP2 inhibitors for the treatment of HF.
An increasing number of preclinical studies have shown that PTMs of proteins, particularly the regulation of protein phosphorylation and dephosphorylation, play a crucial role in the progression of ventricular remodeling and HF [ , , ]. The PTP family regulates protein dephosphorylation by removing phosphate groups from tyrosine residues. Both our work and previous studies have demonstrated that several PTPs, including PRL2, DUSP14, and PTP1B, are involved in the pathological progression of myocardial hypertrophy [ , , ]. As a member of the NRPTPs, SHP2 has been implicated in cardiac developmental defects in human genetic studies. Research indicates that SHP2 mutations can lead to two developmental disorder syndromes, namely Noonan and LEOPARD syndrome, which share the common feature of congenital heart defects [ ]. The roles of SHP2 in the RHOA, PI3K/AKT, NF-κB, and JAK/STAT pathways have been proposed [ – ]. In this study, both in vivo and in vitro experiments demonstrated that inhibiting SHP2 can ameliorate ventricular remodeling induced by Ang II or ISO. However, the underlying mechanism of its action in ventricular remodeling remains elusive.
Over the past few decades, researchers have focused on developing inhibitors targeting the SHP2-PTP domain. SHP099 was the first reported allosteric PTP inhibitor targeting SHP2 [ ]. Other known SHP2 inhibitors also include TNO155, JAB-3068, JAB-3312, and RMC-4630 [ ]. Notably, SHP2 inhibitors have emerged as a highly promising therapeutic strategy for drug-resistant cancers. Oral administration of SHP099 at a dose of 100 mg/kg per day to nude mice bearing KYSE520 xenografts tumors significantly inhibited tumor growth over 24 days [ ]. In the KrasG12D; Trp53−/− NSCLC allograft model, oral administration of SHP099 at 75 mg/kg/day also demonstrated significant antitumor activity [ ]. However, due to the wide range of substrates of SHP2 phosphatase, there may be potential toxicity in cancer treatment. Low-dose or local administration may reduce side effects. In addition, some cancer patients may develop mutations such as SHP2C459S, SHP2E76A, SHP2D61Y, and SHP2E69K, which render existing inhibitors ineffective [ ]. This implies that more novel inhibitors targeting multiple SHP2 mutations need to be developed in the future. This study demonstrated that SHP099, at different concentrations (1 µM and 5 µM in vitro) and doses (2.5 mg/kg and 5 mg/kg in vivo), attenuated ventricular remodeling in Ang II- or ISO- induced models, with even low doses showing efficacy. This finding is of great significance for clinical translation. Collectively, targeting SHP2 tyrosine phosphatase represents a promising strategy against heart failure.
Although several AMPK activators have been reported and tested in animal studies, none have yet received clinical approval [ , ]. Therefore, developing specific and safer approaches to modulate AMPK activity in ventricular remodeling represents an important research direction. Our bulk RNA-seq revealed that SHP2 inhibition suppresses AMPK dephosphorylation, thereby attenuating ISO- or Ang II-induced ventricular remodeling. These findings suggest that targeting SHP2 may offer a novel strategy for treating ventricular remodeling through AMPK activation, making the development of SHP2 small-molecule inhibitors potentially more feasible than direct AMPK activators. Furthermore, we demonstrated that SHP2 dephosphorylates AMPK at Thr172 via its catalytic residue C459, while the PTP domain serves as a scaffold maintaining this post-translational modification. Thus, designing small molecules that either disrupt the protein-protein interaction between the SHP2 PTP domain and AMPK or directly target the C459 residue could provide significant advantages in enhancing selectivity and reducing potential off-target effects. These discoveries provide critical guidance for developing specific SHP2 inhibitors to treat heart failure.
Mitochondria act as the central metabolic hub of the heart, serving as the predominant energy reservoir powering cardiac function [ ]. Mitochondrial dysfunction manifests as reduced activity of electron transport chain complexes and excessive generation of ROS, driving cardiomyocyte injury and cardiac remodeling [ , ]. Mitochondrial dysfunction impairs energy metabolism, leading to reduced ATP production. Consequently, the energy demands of cardiac contraction and relaxation cannot be fulfilled [ ]. AMPK senses intracellular AMP/ATP ratio changes and is activated during energy deficiency, subsequently regulating mitochondrial function via multiple pathways to maintain cardiac energy homeostasis [ , ]. Studies have shown that 5-aminoimidazole-4-carboxamide 1-β-D-ribofuranoside (AICAR), a well-recognized activator of AMPK, has proven to enhance fatty acid oxidation in cardiac mitochondria [ ]. In H9c2 cardiomyocytes, treatment with A769662, another AMPK activator, prevents the loss of mitochondrial membrane potential (ΔΨm) and inhibits the opening of mitochondrial permeability transition pore (mPTP), thereby reducing the production of ROS and increasing resistance of cardiomyocytes to oxidative stress [ ]. Evidence also indicates that metformin activates AMPK, mitigates oxidative stress and prevents the progression of heart failure [ ]. Together, these studies demonstrate that AMPK activation enhances mitochondrial function and attenuates heart failure progression, underscoring its therapeutic potential for cardiac diseases.
Our multifaceted data demonstrate that SHP2 inhibition stabilizes mitochondrial ETC integrity, as shown by the restored expression of complexes II and IV, resulting in enhanced energetic output (increased ATP production) and mitigated damage (reduced oxidative stress and preserved ultrastructure). Importantly, the increased maximal respiration suggests a marked improvement in metabolic flexibility [ ], indicating an enhanced capacity of cardiomyocytes to meet energy demands under stress. The recovery of complex II, a key enzyme in succinate oxidation, further suggests the normalization of specific metabolic pathways critical for cardiac energy homeostasis [ ]. In the present study, the use of standard cell culture medium (containing glucose, glutamine, and other substrates) imposed limitations on the precise investigation of substrate utilization. Although specific substrate oxidation stress tests—such as employing the glutaminase inhibitor BPTES or the fatty acid oxidation inhibitor Etomoxir to dissect the contribution of individual substrates—were not directly performed, this represents a highly valuable direction for future research. Nonetheless, our findings collectively establish SHP2 as a central regulator of both mitochondrial structure and metabolic function in the Ang II- and ISO-induced ventricular remodeling models.
In summary, our results demonstrate that Ang II- or ISO-induced ventricular remodeling upregulates SHP2 expression and promotes its interaction with AMPK, leading to dephosphorylation of AMPK at Thr172. Pharmacological inhibition of SHP2 with SHP099 effectively reverses cardiac hypertrophy and ameliorates mitochondrial dysfunction. Our study reveals a novel mechanism by which the interaction between SHP2 and AMPK regulates ventricular remodeling. These findings not only deepen our understanding of the pathophysiology of ventricular remodeling but also offer new therapeutic strategies.
