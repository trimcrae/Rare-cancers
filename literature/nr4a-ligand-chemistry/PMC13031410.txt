## Abstract
Skeletal muscle aging causes loss of both muscle mss and strength, often leading to sarcopenia. Clinical manifestation of sarcopenia has been found to improve with exercise intervention. The molecular mechanisms in response to exercise intervention in aged skeletal muscles are not fully understood. We performed transcriptomic profiling of aged animal model with exercise intervention for identifying the plausible mechanism leading to enhanced muscle function. Expression levels of 43,629 RNAs were analyzed for the process of aging and exercise intervention. Differentially expressed genes showed 22,196 protein-coding and 21,433 non-coding RNAs that were found to be significantly altered with intervention. Genes associated with extracellular matrix and inflammatory responses exhibited significant change with intervention. Slpi (Secretory Leukocyte Protease Inhibitor)- a vital gene in the intervention group with its role as an inflammatory regulator and tissue repair gene. The activation of quisqualate receptor, neurotransmitter receptors and postsynaptic signal transmission pathways were most relevant for upregulated genes in the intervention group. Downregulated genes in the intervention group were mostly associated with ATP-dependent protein disaggregase activity. Our study provides a comprehensive analysis of the global transcriptome that governs aerobic exercise induced changes in aged muscle leading to compensatory adaptation with exercise in aged model group.

## Introduction
Aging is the time-related deterioration of the physiological functions necessary for activities of daily living. Several changes occur in skeletal muscles with aging. Human beings are reported to achieve peak skeletal muscle mass and strength in mid-life and then gradually experience a progressive decline of up to 50% by the ninth decade . Age-related loss of skeletal muscle mass and strength has been defined as sarcopenia. The term was coined a few decades ago in the nutrition and body composition field , and it has been widely used to describe low muscle mass, until it became evident that muscle function was a better predictor of outcomes , .
The aging muscle, compared to the young, undergoes several morphological changes that are linked to age-related changes in the central and peripheral nervous systems, including a gradual loss of motor neurons and degeneration of neuromuscular junctions . The process of denervation is followed by immediate re-innervation by the remaining motor neurons, resulting in fiber type conversion and grouping . With advanced age, motor neurons progressively die off, leading to denervated muscle fibers that are not fully reclaimed by surviving neurons through a process called motor unit remodeling. Exercise, whether in mice or humans, triggers a cascade of molecular events in various tissues, including skeletal muscle, heart, and brain, leading to motor neuron growth, enhancement in neuromuscular junction integrity, and improved physical health. Many of these pathways are conserved across species; the changes in gene expression observed in mice can provide information regarding similar changes in humans , .
Physical exercise can increase muscle mass, strength, function, metabolic diseases in older adults with a reduction in morbidity and mortality even into the 9th decade of life – . An increase in muscle strength and hypertrophy is the main phenotypic outcome of resistance exercise programs in younger adults ; however, resistance training can also increase mitochondrial capacity , reduce markers of oxidative stress, and increase anti-oxidant enzyme activity in older adults . Several articles have reported the beneficial effects of exercise on skeletal muscle function. Geriatric research in clinical setting have even recently reported a meta-analysis regarding older adults with sarcopenia exhibiting a high or moderate effect of resistance exercise with or without nutrition and the combination of resistance exercise with aerobic and balance training, and it was found to be the most effective intervention for improving quality of life – .
Transcriptomics studies have provided deeper insights into gene expression patterns, revealing the molecular mechanisms behind muscle function, adaptation, and disease. This field helps us understand how genes respond to various stimuli like exercise, aging, or disease, and how these changes impact muscle health and performance – . It supports studying the intricate communication network between muscle cells and other tissues, such as bone, and how these interactions impact muscle function and overall health .
Life-long endurance exercise is associated with a “younger” transcriptome profile in cardiac muscle of inbred mice . Different studies have evaluated transcript abundance in skeletal muscle and implicated mitochondrial impairment in healthy adults – . It has been suggested that transcriptome signatures of aging could be used to assess therapies meant to counter sarcopenia in humans and explore the therapeutic options, including exercise . Despite the profound benefit of exercise for the treatment and prevention of age-related metabolic disease, knowledge of the transcriptomic change and molecular mechanisms by which it improves metabolic health is insufficient , , .
Our study first time reports the comprehensive details of transcriptome profiling data and identifies molecular alterations in response to treadmill intervention impacting fiber type switching in the aged animal model, particularly the cellular and molecular pathway which helps to restore skeletal muscle function.

## Results
The results were analyzed for the signature genes and associated pathways in the different model groups. The comparisons were made among young versus aged, aged versus age INT, and young versus age INT.

## Age-induced changes in the transcriptome profiling of skeletal muscle
To begin our analysis, we first determined the number of protein-coding and non-coding RNAs (ENSGs) that were reliably detected. A total of 43,629 RNAs were detected as differentially expressed genes, among which 22,196 were protein-coding RNAs, and 21,433 were non-coding (including Antisense RNA 1839, lincRNA 3011, miRNA 2011) and other types of RNAs.

## Transcriptomic changes in young versus aged
To identify key genes that may regulate the process of aging, comparisons were made for RNA-seq data on young (14 months) versus aged animal models (24 months old).

## Upregulated genes
The differential gene expression analysis of RNA (ENSGs) expression profiles identified 87 RNAs that were upregulated in the aged animal model compared to the young (log2FC ≥ 1). Of the total 87 RNAs, 62 genes were found to be protein-coding, whereas others included TEC (To be Experimentally Confirmed), pseudogenes, miRNAs, antisense, lincRNAs, immunoglobulin gene, Mt_rRNAs (listed in Supplementary Table - ). The top 10 most significant genes were characterized and listed in Table , for their plausible role and the fold changes in the expression of the young versus aged model. Interestingly, of the total 87 RNAs we found, two microRNA Mir7212 and Mir23b, were significantly upregulated. The change for Mir7212 was found to be 3.38 (p value < 3.5 × 10−5), and for Mir23b change was 2.89 (p value < 0.00054). Moreover, one lincRNA (Long intergenic non-coding RNA), Gm13833, was found to be upregulated, with a change of 1.76 (p value < 3.67 × 10−5). Gene ontology (GO) analysis using the upregulated genes, revealed few important biological processes that were primarily related to an organ system process carried out at the level of a muscle (GO: 0003012, FDR = 6.58 × 10−9), muscle contraction (GO: 0006936, FDR = 2.35 × 10−6), and conversion of fast-contracting muscle fibers to a slower character (GO: 0014883, FDR = 5.46 × 10−5) in age model group. The molecular function associated with upregulated genes that were significantly altered with aging included actin binding (GO: 0003779, FDR = 2.32 × 10−5), cytoskeleton protein binding (GO: 0008092, FDR = 0.0012), and microfilament motor activity (GO: 0000146, FDR = 0.0064). The cellular component analysis for upregulated genes in aged skeletal muscle showed significant change in myofibrils (GO: 0030016, FDR = 4.22 × 10−9), contractile fibers (GO: 0043292, FDR = 3.99 × 10−9), and myosin II complex (GO: 0016460, FDR = 6.83 × 10−6) (Fig. ).
 Reactome pathway analysis for upregulated genes identified three pathways, i.e., Striated Muscle Contraction (R-HSA id; R-MMU-390522, FDR = 1.58 × 10−6), Cardiac conduction (R-HSA id; R-MMU-5576891, FDR = 0.009), and Ion homeostasis conduction (R-HSA id; R-MMU-5578775, FDR = 0.009) that were altered with the process of aging (Fig. ).

Of the total 55 RNAs, 54 RNAs were protein-coding, and single RNA was found to be non-coding. The top 10 most significant genes were characterized and listed in Table , for their plausible role and the fold change in the expression of young versus aged. A single non-coding lincRNA, Gm26809 (ENSMUSG00000097815), was found to be significantly downregulated in the aged model group compared to the young (log2-fold change = −4.89, padj = 0.0093). GO analysis of downturned gene identified genes associated with biological processes such as brown fat cell differentiation (GO: 0050873, FDR = 0.046) and molecular function, such as potassium ion leak channel activity (GO: 0022841, FDR = 0.044), leak channel activity (GO: 0022840, FDR = 0.024) and narrow pore channel activity (GO: 0022842, FDR = 0.036) (Fig. ).
Reactome pathway analysis identified associated pathways for downregulated genes with the process of aging, i.e., Tandem pore domain potassium channels (R-HSA id; R-MMU-1296346, FDR = 0.0071) and Phase 4—resting membrane potential channels (R-HSA id; R-MMU-5576886, FDR = 0.013) (Fig. ).

## Discussion
Transcriptomic profiling of aerobic exercise-induced aged skeletal muscle reveals distinct molecular signatures based on exercise type, intensity, and duration. Treadmill-induced exercise intervention profoundly affects multiple tissues and is well recognized as beneficial to various aspects of human health – . The present study investigated the response of exercise intervention on muscle-specific transcriptome profiling in young compared to aged model group, aged model compared to age INT group, and young compared to age INT group. We have identified the pathways associated with exercise adaptations that include phenotypic responses to exercise, including cellular and molecular responses in the aged model group, as well as the intervention group. Few recent studies have reported alterations in aged skeletal muscle in response to exercise, but the most important element of difference in the present study is the fact that aging leads to noticeable loss of fast-twitch muscle fibers, which are crucial for power and speed in older adults. Our study incorporated the treadmill exercise protocol, which has shown a beneficial effect in improving skeletal muscle function. Our transcriptome profiling data is one of the first to report the comprehensive molecular alterations specific to treadmill impact on an aged animal model in response to exercise.
Our analysis revealed distinguished signature molecules, which included both protein-coding as well as non-coding genes. We found a few relevant non-coding RNAs, like Mir7217 and Mir23b, exclusively expressed in the aged model compared to the young model. Mir7212 has an important role in post-transcriptional gene regulation and is believed to target FGFR3 (fibroblast growth factor receptor 3), a protein having a crucial role in skeletal muscle development and function, primarily by regulating bone growth and development. Mir23b plays a multifaceted role in various biological processes, regulating skeletal muscle, particularly in the context of muscle atrophy and regeneration . Apart from non-coding RNAs, we have also identified a key lincRNA 13833, which may play an important role in deciphering the mechanism of the complex process of aging.
The GO analysis of upregulated genes showed a few relevant gene set which are quite fascinating to comparing it from previous reports. The GO 0003779, which was previously reported to be enriched in slow muscle fiber by ref. , has also been found to be upregulated in our aged model group . This finding is very consistent, as it has been well established that with aging, there is a greater proportional loss of fast-twitch muscle fibers compared with slow-twitch muscle fibers , . Another key gene set, GO 0000146 (associated with microfilament motor activity), was found to be upregulated in the aged model group. This gene set was previously reported by Qiu et al. as alterations of the methylation level in aged Rhesus Macaque . Interestingly, two more gene sets involved in muscle contraction (GO 0006936) and myofibril-associated (GO 0030016) were found to be upregulated in the aged model group. These particular GO IDs enriched in similar types of muscles of our study are consistent with our findings and have a role in muscle development .
Moreover, our investigation of protein-coding genes using Panther database analysis in the young versus the age model group identified some important genes involved in skeletal muscle function. We found a two-fold increase in the expression of the Myozenin2 gene, which serves as an intracellular binding protein involved in linking Z line proteins such as alpha-actinin, gamma-filamin, TCAP/telethonin, LDB3/ZASP, and localizing calcineurin signaling to the sarcomere. It plays an important role in the modulation of calcineurin signaling and plays an important role in myofibrillogenesis , . Another protein-coding gene, tropomyosin 3 (TPM3) was found to be upregulated in the present study, which plays a role in myopathy . Cytoskeletal protein, Pdlim1 gene, which acts as an adapter that brings other proteins (like kinases) to the cytoskeleton, was highly expressed in our age model group. This gene has been reported to be involved in the assembly and disassembly of stress fibers in fibroblasts , . MYH6, myosin heavy chain 6 gene, showed almost 3-fold increase in expression in the aged model group, which was previously reported in HLHS (Hypoplastic left heart syndrome) and other congenital heart disease as a genetic link to heart-related disorders .
Importantly, how exercise impacts the transcriptomics profile of skeletal muscle with aging at both the coding and non-coding gene expression level were examined in this study. We analyzed Long intergenic non-coding RNAs (lincRNAs), which have recently emerged as an important regulator of diverse biological processes. We identified four lincRNAs that were significantly expressed in the exercise intervention group compared to the aged model group, i.e., Gm26522, Gm26809, RP23-103B18.18, and 1700001L05RiK. Whereas the analysis of non-coding genes in the age INT group compared to the young showed two lincRNAs (Gm15713, Gm13833), two miRNAs (MirRNA 7212, MirRNA 486), and one SnRNA (Gm25328). The functional aspect of these novel non-coding genes would be interesting to decipher the mechanism of aerobic exercise-induced benefits in the aged model group.
The GO analysis of upregulated gene sets showed some consistent findings in the aged exercise model group compared to both the young and aged model groups. The aerobic exercise induced expression of plasma membrane-associated gene set (GO 0005886) was highly expressed in both the group young versus aged INT and aged versus age INT. Thus, this gene set becomes one of the most relevant to explore for the aerobic exercise-induced mechanism in aging. Previously, Jialiang Yang et al. in 2017 had reported this gene set (GO 0005886) as a functional enrichment of age co-expressed genes in adipose tissue and Panther database analysis revealed that it may also plays important role in memory or learning . This becomes an interesting way to identify the association of skeletal muscle aging with cognitive dysfunction in the brain through a muscle-brain axis, a process where the muscle communicates with the brain via circulating factors called myokines. Shao-Yong had reported a decline of GO 0007611 in the hippocampus, leading to cognitive impairment in young mice. We also found GO 0007611 to be upregulated in the exercise group, thus being an important indicator of cognitive protection upon exercise intervention in the aged model group . Our findings of the Slpi gene expressing more than two-fold in the aged intervention group are remarkably encouraging. This gene has been found to play a crucial role in modulating inflammation, particularly in response to exercise and in various disease contexts. Slpi is known to inhibit the activity of enzymes like neutrophil elastase and other proteases, which can contribute to tissue damage, thus benefiting aged skeletal muscle. Moreover, exercise intervention has also shown a remarkable impact on ECM (extracellular matrix) associated genes, GO 0003102 (FDR = 3.02 × 10−5) in the age versus age INT group, which implies exercise intervention may play a role in keeping ECM intact with the growing age. This gene set attracts special attention as not much has been reported on these genes before. An expected increase in the expression of the gene set, GO 0006954, associated with the inflammatory response in the age INT group compared to the aged model group was noticed in our analysis. Gita Shafiee et al. had reported enriched expression of GO 0006954 in DEGs in women as an important indicator in muscle-associated disease (sarcopenia). Therefore, it would be worth exploring the mechanism of the most significant non-coding genes associated with exercise intervention in aging.
The analysis of the expression profile of the age INT group compared to the age model group showed a significant 3-fold decline in Nr4a3 gene expression. Nr4a3 has been previously reported to play an important role in the regulation of proliferation, survival, and differentiation of many different cell types. It has also been found to play a role in inflammation .
DGE analysis showed increased expression of the Versican gene, which has been reported to play an important role in intercellular signaling and in connecting cells with the extracellular matrix , . Exercise intervention has long been known to induce myogenesis; GO analysis typically found an altered myogenesis GO pathway. On comparing young versus aged, we observed that the greatest number of differentially expressed genes were enriched in myogenesis. The previous study showed that the progenitor cell activity (myogenesis) in aged muscle can be restored when treated with young serum .
The analysis of young versus the age INT model showed increased expression of genes involved in immune response . Igkv4-59, Immunoglobulin Heavy Variable 4-59, showed a 3-fold increase in expression in the age INT group compared to the young. An important gene, Myh7b, has shown a 2-fold increase in the age INT group. This protein-coding gene has been reported to be involved in muscle contraction . On comparing both the young versus aged model and the young versus age INT group, a common gene, Nr4a3, showed 3-fold decline in the age INT group compared to the young, thereby being an important signature molecule for exercise intervention.
The comprehensive analysis of a distinctive gene set in response to exercise has been reported here as one of the first studies to compile the significantly altered transcripts. The gene set GO 0007200 (FDR = 0.0261) was significantly altered in the age INT compared to the aged model group. The annotated function for this gene set is in the biological process as a G-protein-coupled receptor signaling pathway. The gene set GO 0140776 (FDR = 0.0459) was significantly downregulated in our findings, which is unique and needs further characterization. The comparison of the young versus age INT group revealed two gene sets, GO 0014877 (FDR = 0.019) and GO 0014870 (FDR = 0.012), which were found to be significantly downregulated in our study and had no previous report on their function. Thus, it makes these genes set an interesting molecular event in the process of skeletal muscle aging for further analysis and exploration.
To summarize the findings, the present study provides comprehensive information on the mRNA, lincRNA, miRNA, snRNAs expression profiles in young, aged, and age INT model groups. The findings of the study could lead to further exploration of the mechanisms of gene regulation in humans with exercise intervention. The study also opens a direction which emphasizes that age influences the metabolic changes with the increasing years, and exercise intervention triggers the adaptive response via anti-inflammatory pathways, immune response-regulating signaling pathway, and regulation of the immune system. The pathophysiologic response evaluation combined with genomic profile examination will allow for a better picture of events and explain the variation in results greater than one single layer observation at the transcriptional level.
Further work on muscle pathophysiological changes could be conducted using both animal models and clinical research. More importantly, our transcriptomic data will help to develop interventions to combat age-related diseases, especially sarcopenia in the elderly, providing further insight into the molecular basis of sarcopenia and the impact of aerobic exercise at the muscle and fiber type-specific level.
However, while transcriptomics offers valuable insights into gene expression changes, it is crucial to acknowledge the limitations, such as our study included only male mice because of the higher skeletal muscle content in males compared to females. This study does not include female animals as the study subject, and also tissue-specific analysis was not performed due to the intended hypothesis of broad-spectrum RNA-seq analysis.

## Methods

## Animal grouping procedure
Male C57BL/6 mice were procured from the Central Animal Facility, All India Institute of Medical Sciences, New Delhi. All the animal experiments were approved by the Institute Animal Ethics Committee of All India Institute of Medical Sciences, New Delhi (73 IAEC-1/2018). Animals were divided into three different groups: young (14 months old), age (24 months old), and age INT 24 months (aged intervention; 22 + 2 months INT). Considering the study’s objective, statistical power, and desired significance level of p < 0.05, the sample size was six animals in each of the three groups as biological replicates. Animals were allowed to acclimatize for a period of 2 weeks. Mice were kept on a 12:12-h light/dark cycle. The mice were fed standard rodent chow ad libitum. Before euthanasia, mice were fasted overnight and euthanized at the start of the light cycle. At the time of tissue collection, mice were anesthetized (3–5% isoflurane) via inhalation; anesthetic depth was confirmed by toe-pinch assessment, and mice underwent cervical dislocation prior to tissue collection.

## Treadmill exercise intervention
Treadmill exercise was performed as previously described with some modifications. Briefly, the age INT group mice were given an exercise intervention beginning at 22nd months of age. Exercise was performed for a total period of 8 weeks; Exercise Intervention frequency was 6 days per week. Mice were acclimatized to the treadmill (an LE8700 treadmill; Panlab, Harvard, USA) 2 days before the first session. Acclimation consisted of running at 5% grade for 5-min at 5 m/min, 5 min at 10 m/min, and finally, 5-min at 15 m/min. The exercise intervention protocol was adjusted by altering the exercise duration. The exercise intervention was adjusted to prevent injury by starting with a warm-up at a comfortable pace and then gradually increasing the speed. Each session consisted of a 5-min warm-up at 10 m/min, 5% grade, after which speed was increased to 15 m/min. Mice were allowed to run at this speed for 15 min. Treadmill speed (15 m/min) and grade (5%) remained constant throughout each session. The mice were sacrificed at the age of 24 months after 8 weeks of treadmill exercise (n = 6 each group).

## Isolation of skeletal muscle
C57BL/6 Male mice at 14, 24, and 24 months of age were sacrificed for skeletal muscle harvesting of tibialis anterior, gastrocnemius, and soleus. The obtained skeletal muscle tissues were minced. The whole lot of minced tissues was quickly frozen in liquid nitrogen, ground to powder using a mortar and a pestle, and stored in small volumes in −80 °C for later use. Three biological replicates from each of the three groups were used for transcriptomic sequencing. The samples that passed the quality controls after data trimming were represented in the analysis and figures.

## RNA Extraction
Total RNA extraction was performed for all the tissue samples (n = 6 animals) from all three groups using the Trizol method (Invitrogen, Cat# 15596026). The extracted RNA samples were quantified using Qubit RNA Assay HS (Invitrogen, Cat# Q32852). The purity of RNA was checked using QIAxpert, and RNA integrity was checked using Tape station RNA HS ScreenTapes (Agilent, Cat# 5067-5579). After confirmation of a valid QC result, the samples were taken further for library preparation and sequencing.

## RNA-seq library preparation
NEB Ultra II directional RNA-Seq Library Prep kit (NEB, Cat #E7760L) protocol was used to prepare libraries for total RNA sequencing. An initial concentration of 250 ng of total RNA was used for the assay. First, the ribosomal RNA, which constitutes ~95% of the total RNA population, was removed using QIAseq FastSelect -rRNA HMR Kit (Qiagen, Cat# 334387). The cleaved RNA fragments were copied into first-strand cDNA using reverse transcriptase. Second-strand cDNA synthesis was performed using DNA Polymerase I and RNase H enzyme. The cDNA fragments were then subjected to a series of enzymatic steps that repair the ends, tail the 3′ end with a single “A” base, followed by ligation of the adapters. The adapter ligated products were then purified and enriched using the following thermal conditions: initial denaturation at 98 °C for 30 s; 13 cycles of 98 °C for 10 s, 65 °C for 75 s; final extension at 65 °C for 5 min. PCR products were then purified and checked for fragment size distribution on Fragment Analyzer using HS NGS Fragment Kit (1–6000 bp) (Agilent, Cat# DNF-474-1000) or Tape Station using D1000 DNA ScreenTapes (Agilent, Cat# 5067-5582).

## Sequencing protocol
The prepared libraries were quantified using Qubit High Sensitivity Assay (Invitrogen, Cat# Q32854). The obtained libraries were pooled and diluted to the final optimal loading concentration before cluster amplification on the Illumina flow cell. Once the cluster generation was completed, the clustered flow cell was loaded on the Illumina HiSeq X instrument to generate 150 bp paired-end reads. RNA was sequenced at a depth of >80 million single-end reads.

## RNA-seq data analysis
The raw reads were filtered using Trimmomatic for quality scores and adapters. Filtered reads were aligned to the Mus musculus genome using splice-aware aligners like HISAT2 to quantify reads mapped to each transcript. Alignment percentages of reads were 94-95% for all the samples. Total numbers of uniquely mapped reads were counted using feature counts. The uniquely mapped reads were then subjected to differential gene expression using DeSeq2.

## Workflow for RNA-seq analysis: read quality check
The parameters were checked from fastq files for base quality score distribution, sequence quality score distribution, average base content per read, GC distribution in the reads, PCR amplification quality, and over-represented sequences were checked, and adapter trimming was done. Based on the quality report of fastq files, trimming of sequence reads was done to ensure high-quality sequences for further analysis. In addition, the low-quality sequence reads were excluded from the analysis. The adapter trimming was performed using Trimmomatic (v-0.36)

## Contamination removal
For the RNA-Seq analysis, the unwanted sequences were removed, especially non-poly-A-tailed RNAs from the sample (assuming that poly-A-tailed RNAs are sequenced). The unwanted sequences include mitochondrial genome sequences, ribosomal RNAs, transfer RNAs, adapter sequences, and others. Contamination removal was performed using Bowtie2 (2.2.4).

## Read alignment
The paired-end reads were aligned to the reference Mus musculus genome. Alignment was performed using HISAT2 (2.1.0).

## Expression estimation
The aligned reads were used for the estimation of expressed genes. The raw read counts were estimated using Feature Count (1.5.2). Read count data were normalized using DESeq2.

## Differential expression analysis
The raw read counts were normalized using DESeq2.The ratio of normalized read counts for treated over control was taken as the fold change. Genes were first filtered based on the P value ≤ 0.05. A distribution of these log2 (fold change) values was found to be normally distributed. Those genes that were found to have −1 ≤ log2 (fold change) ≥ 1 were considered statistically significant.

## GO annotation and pathway analysis
GO annotation and Reactome pathway information for differentially expressed genes were obtained using the Panther database.
