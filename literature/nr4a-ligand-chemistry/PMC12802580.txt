## Background and Aims:
Although chemotherapy and anti-PD-L1 antibodies are the standard of care for cholangiocarcinoma (CCA), resistance is common and limits durable benefits for patients. This hurdle underscores the urgent need to innovate combination approaches that promote durable immunity in patients. MEK inhibitors (MEKi) have shown potential to enhance immunotherapy in CCA models, but early clinical trials combining MEKi with anti-PD-L1 therapy have yielded suboptimal results.

## Approach and Results:
We hypothesized that the addition of a CD27 agonist would salvage MEKi-induced T cell impairment and reduce CCA burden in vivo. We show that CD27 agonism potentiates T cell activation in the presence of MEKi in vitro. Further, triple therapy with CD27 agonist, anti-PD-L1, and MEKi elicits efficacy in a subcutaneous CCA model, accompanied by increases in tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells with memory phenotypes. Although triple therapy enhanced CD8+ T cell infiltration in mice with orthotopic CCA liver tumors, its impact on overall survival was less pronounced. Further investigation revealed that orthotopic CCA tumors harbored more CD11b+Gr-1+ cells when compared to either subcutaneous CCA tumors or to normal liver. Finally, in mice with orthotopic CCA tumors treated with triple therapy, depletion of Gr-1+ cells triggered severe toxicity, characterized by rapid weight loss and uncontrolled systemic and hepatic inflammation.

## Conclusions:
These findings identify myeloid cells as key mediators of both immunotherapy resistance and toxicity in CCA, highlighting the critical importance of tumor site and use of physiologically relevant preclinical models when evaluating immunotherapy strategies.

## INTRODUCTION
Cholangiocarcinoma (CCA) is a rare, aggressive malignancy and is refractory to chemotherapeutic and immunotherapy approaches. Recent results from the TOPAZ-1 and KEYNOTE-966 clinical trials demonstrate that immunotherapy with antibodies (Ab) targeting the Programmed Cell Death Protein-1 (PD-1)/Programmed Death Ligand-1 (PD-L1) pathway benefits CCA patients when combined with chemotherapy. Yet therapy for metastatic patients remains an unmet need, with a median overall survival (OS) of ~13 months in these trials for patients receiving combination therapy. , Advanced understanding of genomic features in CCA has improved management of patients with tumors containing fibroblast growth factor receptor (FGFR) fusions or altered isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH) through targeted inhibitors. However, most CCA patients do not have actionable genomic alterations, and resistance to FGFR and IDH pathway–targeted agents is common. These tumors, regardless of genotype, display activation of the MAPK pathway, which promotes mitogenic activity. Given these features, inhibitors of the upstream kinase, MEK (MEKi), have been employed, providing single-agent efficacy in metastatic disease. – 

The CCA tumor microenvironment features notable deficits in T cell functionality, alongside a surplus of myeloid cells. , , These suppressive elements are amplified via activation of MAPK signaling, offering a rationale for dual targeting with immunotherapy. Prior studies in subcutaneous CT26 colon cancer models showed MEKi protects T cells from activation-induced cell death (AICD) and bolsters T cell infiltration into tumors. Motivated by this data, we conducted a phase II trial of atezolizumab ± cobimetinib in patients with advanced biliary tract cancers (BTC). While this trial met its primary mPFS endpoint, antitumor responses in patients were rare (<5%), suggesting other mechanisms of immunotherapy resistance need to be circumvented.
Analysis of biospecimens from this trial was useful for generating hypotheses as to why dual MEK/PD-L1-targeted therapy was suboptimal. – Using patient samples, we validated theoretical concerns that MEKi impairs T cell activation, despite increasing their tumoral infiltration. Subsequent studies revealed that while genetic ablation of MEK1 in CT26 tumors increased their immunogenicity, systemic administration of MEKi in mice paradoxically reduced CD8+ T cell activation. Importantly, targeting CD137 overcame MEKi-induced T cell activation and elicited in vivo antitumor activity. Based on these findings, we shifted our focus to exploring other T cell agonists that might pair with MEKi for CCA. Published RNAseq data from tumors treated with MEKi or dual MEK/PD-L1 blockade revealed CD27 upregulation. These results suggested CD27 agonists may synergize with MEK/PD-L1 blockade as a data-driven approach to enhance immunotherapy.
CD27 is a costimulatory molecule of the TNF receptor superfamily on naïve and memory T cells. , Upon engagement of its ligand CD70, CD27 enhances activation, proliferation, survival, effector capacity, and memory features of T cells. Targeting CD27 acts synergistically with anti-PD-1/PD-L1 in mice. Moreover, early trials indicated CD27 agonist Abs were well-tolerated​​​​​, with clinical responses and functional impact on T cells in solid and hematologic malignancies. , , – Thus, we hypothesized that CD27 agonist Ab would overcome MEKi-mediated impairment of T cell activation in CCA.
While agonist Ab enhances T cell function, even in the presence of MEKi, the CCA TME has redundant features that limit antitumor immunity. For example, suppressive myeloid cells are abundant , , and plasma levels of myeloid-associated cytokines and chemokines (ie, IL-6, MCP-1, RANTES) are elevated in patients. Notably, these factors and myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSC) remained unchanged in patients treated with cobimetinib and atezolizumab in our prior trial. Thus, while MEKi/immunotherapy combinations improve T cell function, they appear insufficient to overcome the myeloid-dominant nature of CCA.
Herein, we show that combining CD27 agonists with MEKi and anti-PD-L1 elicits efficacy in a subcutaneous CCA model. This was accompanied by increased CCA-infiltrating CD8+ T cells with memory phenotypes. While triple therapy improved CD8+ T cell infiltration in orthotopic CCA liver tumors, survival in this physiologically relevant model was somewhat limited. Immune analysis uncovered that orthotopic CCA tumors harbored more CD11b+ Gr-1+ myeloid cells. Upon depletion of Gr-1+ cells in concert with triple therapy, mice with orthotopic tumors displayed rapid weight loss, systemic elevation of inflammatory factors, and severe hepatitis with T cell infiltration into the liver. These findings highlight the crucial impact of disease site on immunotherapy efficacy in preclinical models and underscore that integrating myeloid-targeted agents should be approached systematically and with caution to enhance outcomes.

## METHODS

## Cell lines, antibodies, and therapeutic agents
URCCA4.3 and fibroblast-activated cancer (FAC) murine cholangiocarcinoma cells were from Dr Linehan (University of Rochester) and Dr Ilyas (Mayo Clinic), respectively. The URCCA4.3 line is from spontaneously arising intrahepatic CCA in LSL-Kras

G12D
; Trp53

Flox/Flox
; Alb-Cre (KPPC) mice, , backcrossed onto the C57BL/6 strain. The FAC cell line is from tumors in mice following hydrodynamic tail vein injection of a dominant negative form of the E3 ubiquitin ligase F-box and WD repeat domain containing protein (Fbxw7ΔF) tumor suppressor gene, paired with constitutive Akt activation. 

URCCA4.3 cells were cultured on collagen-coated flasks in RPMI-1640 with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1% antibiotic:antimycotic solution (Gemini Bio). FAC cells were cultured in DMEM with 10% FBS and 1% antibiotic:antimycotic solution. Cells were mycoplasma negative (MycoAlert Detection Kit, Lonza). Rat IgG2a CD27 agonist Ab (RRID: AB_3665171) was from Celldex, Inc. under a material transfer agreement. In vivo antibodies were: rat anti-mouse PD-L1 Ab (BioXCell, clone B7-H1, RRID: AB_10949073), rat IgG2b isotype control, anti-keyhole limpet hemocyanin (BioXCell, clone LTF-2, RRID: AB_1107780), rat IgG2a isotype control, anti-trinitrophenol (BioXCell, clone 2A3, RRID: AB_1107769) and anti-mouse CD8α (BioXCell, RRID: AB_1125541), anti-mouse Ly6G/Ly6C (Gr-1) (BioXCell, clone RB6-8C5, RRID: AB_10312146). Clinical-grade cobimetinib (GDC-0973 RG7420, Selleckchem) was made into a solution according to the manufacturer’s protocol.

## Mice
Male and female Pmel-1 (Jackson Laboratory, RRID: IMSR_JAX:005023) and female C57BL/6 mice (Jackson Laboratory, RRID: IMSR_JAX:000664) were bred in Emory University facilities and fed a standard diet. Mice were housed on the same auto-water rack for the study duration. Pmel-1 mice were bred within facilities. Mice were 5–7 weeks old prior to experiments. This work was approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee, Emory University (IACUC protocols #: 201900225, 201700322), via ARRIVE guidelines.

## In vitro T cell assays
Pmel-1 transgenic mice , were euthanized (n=9), and spleens were harvested. Pmel-1 spleens were collected into complete media and then processed over a 70 μm filter. RBC lysis buffer (BioLegend) was used for 5 minutes at room temperature, then inactivated using complete media. Cells were plated at 1×106 cells/well in a 48-well plate in 1 mL of media. Splenocytes were activated using 1 μmol/L of human glycoprotein 100 (hgp100) peptide and 100 IU/mL of IL-2 (NIH repository). Drugs administered to Pmel-1: cobimetinib 1 μM (MEK inhibitor GDC-0973, Selleckchem) and CD27 agonist 1 μg/mL (rat anti-mouse CD27 AT124-1, CellDex). Cells were cultured for 4 days, then processed for flow to evaluate activation via CD69 and CD44 or TCR-driven apoptosis via Nur77.

## In vivo efficacy
All murine studies were conducted under IACUC-approved protocols at Emory University (IACUC protocol # 201700322), via ARRIVE guidelines. Sample sizes ranged from 30 to 55 mice. For subcutaneous studies, URCCA4.3 or FAC cells (1×106) were implanted into the flanks of female C57BL/6 mice. Study inclusion criteria required palpable tumors to form (~11–12 d). Upon confirmation of tumors, mice were randomized by surgical date and surgeon to a treatment group. Anti-murine CD27 (100 μg), anti-PD-L1 Ab (100 μg or 200 μg) and/or cobimetinib (7.5 mg/kg) were administered via intraperitoneal (i.p.) injection 3×/week (M, W, F). Treatments were administered in the same sequence for the duration of each study. Tumor growth was monitored via calipers. Mice were euthanized between days 24 and 33 post-tumor implantation. Tumors and tumor-draining lymph nodes were harvested, and lymphocytes were analyzed via flow cytometry. For depletion studies, CD8-depleting antibodies were given i.p. at day −3, −1, +1, and +3 relative to tumor implantation, then every 3–4 days until endpoint. For short-term in vivo studies, mice were excluded from analysis if euthanasia was required per Emory’s Tumor Burden Scoring policy or if animals died prior to the endpoint.
To establish orthotopic liver tumors, we performed surgery as described. , For the first orthotopic study, treatment began 12 days post-surgery, via i.p. injection of 50 μg/mouse CD27Ag, 200 μg/mouse anti-PD-L1 and/or 7.5 mg/kg/mouse MEKi 3×/week for 2 weeks. On day 27, mice were euthanized. For survival studies, mice were treated via i.p. injection of 100 μg/mouse CD27Ag, 200 μg/mouse anti-PD-L1, and/or 7.5 mg/kg/mouse MEKi 3x/week for 3 weeks. For survival studies involving Gr-1 depletion, Ab depletion began on day 14. Treatment with therapeutic Abs began the following day (day 15 post-surgery), via i.p. injections of 100 μg/mouse CD27Ag, 200 μg/mouse anti-PD-L1 and/or 7.5 mg/kg/mouse MEKi, and 400 μg/mouse anti-Gr-1 3x/week for 3 weeks. Mice were euthanized when they met criteria according to the Tumor Burden Scoring policy of Emory University IACUC.

## T cell suppression assay
CD11b+Gr-1+ cells were isolated from n=20 orthotopic URCCA4.3 tumors 26 days following tumor implantation using a negative selection kit (StemCell, Cat# 19867). Bulk CD3+ T cells were isolated from the spleen of non-tumor-bearing mice using a negative selection kit (StemCell, Cat#19851). T cells were stained with 2.5 μM CFSE stain (Invitrogen, Cat# C34554), activated using anti-CD3/CD28 beads, then co-cultured 1:1 with isolated tumor CD11b+Gr-1+ cells. After 72 hours in culture, T cells were de-beaded, and flow cytometry was used to analyze CFSE in CD8+ cells. 

## Flow cytometry
At the endpoint of murine studies, the tumor and draining lymph nodes were processed into single cells, stained and assessed by flow cytometry. Samples were stained with viability dye, followed by surface antibody in FACS buffer (PBS + 2% FBS). For intracellular antibodies, the FoxP3/Transcription Factor Kit (Invitrogen) was used. T cell antibodies were used at 1:500 for surface and 1:200 for intracellular; the myeloid antibodies were used at 1:200 for surface and 1:100 intracellular. A Cytek Aurora (Cytek Biosciences) was used, and data were analyzed using FlowJo (BD Biosciences, RRID: SCR_008520). Antibodies and clones are in Supplemental Tables S1–S3, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861.

## Statistical analysis
Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism (RRID: SCR_002798) or the SAS statistical package v9.4 (Statistical Analysis System, RRID: SCR_008567). Tumor growth was our primary endpoint in subcutaneous studies, while survival was used in orthotopic models. One-way ANOVA followed by multiple comparison testing with Tukey correction was used where appropriate. Linear mixed effects models were implemented to estimate and compare average tumor volume and growth rate among groups. Tumor volumes were log-transformed to meet normality and variance assumptions for statistical models. For survival, the log-rank test and proportional hazard model were used. The resampling method checked the proportional hazard assumption and confirmed no violation. Data were statistically significant at p<0.05. Power: a minimum of 5 mice/group with 8 measures/mouse over 4 weeks gave 80% power (2-sided significance=0.001, reduced for multiple tests) to detect a minimum 26% difference in growth rate. Calculations were in R and the longpower package. The test was based on mixed-model regression (significance=0.050). To account for variability, at least 8 mice per group were used for orthotopic studies.

## RESULTS

## CD27 agonist antibody salvages MEKi-induced impairment of T cell activation
We tested whether targeting CD27 could overcome limited CD8+ T cell activation mediated by MEK inhibitors. To address this, we used the TCR-transgenic Pmel-1 system, whereby all murine CD8+ T cells recognize the tumor antigen (gp100 melanoma antigen). Splenic Pmel-1 T cells were activated with gp100 peptide with MEKi (1 μM) and/or CD27 agonist Ab (1 μg/mL) (Figure A). MEKi reduced T cell activation as evidenced by CD69 and CD44 reduction on CD8+ T cells (Figures B–D). Decreased activation with MEKi was rescued by CD27 agonistic Ab, as seen by significant increases in CD69 on CD8+ T cells (Figures B–D and Supplemental Figures S1A–C, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). Consistent with prior studies, MEKi treatment reduced apoptosis in activated Pmel-1 CD8+ T cells, as Nur77 decreased, a transcription factor associated with AICD (Supplemental Figure S1D, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). Although CD27Ag and MEKi further upregulated CD69 and CD44, Nur77 remained suppressed. These findings suggest MEKi and CD27 agonism preserves T cell viability while maintaining an activated phenotype.
 

## CD27Ag/MEKi/αPD-L1 triple therapy enhances efficacy in subcutaneous CCA
We tested whether CD27 agonist antibody (CD27Ag) would enhance the efficacy of dual MEK/PD-L1 blockade. Mice with subcutaneous URCCA4.3 tumors were randomized to groups that underwent therapy for 2 weeks (Figure A). Monotherapy with cobimetinib (MEKi), individual anti-PD-L1 Ab or CD27Ag did not impact tumor growth (Figure B, cobimetinib: p=0.767, anti-PD-L1: p=0.636, CD27Ag: p=0.416) versus control. In contrast, some doublet therapies, including MEKi + anti-PD-L1 and MEKi + CD27Ag, reduced tumor growth (Figure B, p=0.044, p=0.012), while anti-PD-L1 + CD27Ag did not have significant impact (p=0.137). Tumor growth was further inhibited by the triplet of CD27Ag + MEKi + anti-PD-L1 (Figure B; p<0.001), which was well-tolerated, evidenced by stable body weight during each study (Supplemental Figure S2, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). Consistent with reduced tumor growth, end-of-study tumors were smallest in mice receiving triple therapy (Figure C; p=0.0173 vs. control). Efficacy of triple therapy was reproducible in multiple experiments, and across dose levels of anti-PD-L1 Ab (Supplemental Figure S3, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861).
 

## Triple therapy increases memory CD8+ T cells in subcutaneous CCA
To interrogate how triple therapy delays progression, analysis of cellular immune biomarkers in CCA tumors and draining lymph nodes was conducted 2 weeks post-treatment. We hypothesized that triple therapy would improve T cell infiltration, catalyzing changes in CD8+ T cells that favor immune responses. Several striking differences between groups were observed. A trend toward more CD8+ T cells was detected in tumors of mice treated with triple therapy, compared to other arms (Figure A). T cell composition in tumors also differed between groups. Namely, greater proportions of CD8+ T cells had effector-memory phenotypes (TEM, CD44+CD62L−) after triple therapy (Figure B; p=0.0004 vs. control). While all groups had more CD8+ T cells with stemness (Tcf-1+Tim-3−) in the draining nodes (Figure C), mice given MEKi + anti-PD-L1 or triple therapy had a trend toward greater stem-memory cells in tumors (Figures D, E and Supplemental Figures S4, S5, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). Finally, more than half of CD8+ T cells in tumors possessed resident memory (TRM) phenotypes, by CD69 and CD103 co-expression following triple therapy (Figures A, B, p<0.0001 vs. control; Supplemental Figures S4, S5, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). , 

 

## CD8+ T cells contribute to the efficacy of triple therapy
The dynamic phenotypic changes of CD8+ T cells in subcutaneous murine CCA tumors led us to posit that these cells mediated the efficacy of triple therapy. Thus, CD8+ T cells were antibody-depleted before treatment in mice bearing subcutaneous URCCA4.3 tumors to address their role (Figure A). CD8+ T cell depletion was verified by flow at endpoint (Supplemental Figure S6, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). Consistent with initial data (Figure B), tumors were controlled with triple therapy. However, tumor growth was faster and end-study volume trended higher when CD8+ T cells were eliminated, although mice still benefited versus control (Figures B, C; p<0.0001 triple therapy vs. control; p=0.005 triple therapy + αCD8 vs. control). These data indicate that CD8+ T cells foster maximal efficacy in this subcutaneous tumor model.
 

## Triple therapy increases stem and effector-memory CD8+ T cells in orthotopic CCA
To examine immunomodulatory effects of therapy in a physiologically relevant microenvironment, we directly injected URCCA4.3 cells into the liver. Mice with established tumors were randomized to anti-PD-L1 as monotherapy, dual therapy of anti-PD-L1 plus CD27Ag or MEKi, or triple therapy over 2 weeks (Figure A). Similar to the subcutaneous model, tumoral CD8+ T cell infiltration was increased by triple therapy (Figure B, p<0.0001 vs. control; Supplemental Figure S7, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). Interestingly, our data indicate that triple therapy promoted CD8+ T cells to adopt memory programs. For example, while dual anti-PD-L1/MEKi enhanced stem-memory (TCF-1+Tim-3−) CD8+ cells in the tumor (Figure C; p=0.02 vs. control), triple therapy prompted greater conversion to effector-memory phenotypes (Figure D; p=0.0479 vs. control). Similar to the subcutaneous model, tumors from triple therapy–treated mice had the greatest TRM frequency (Figure E; p=0.0003 vs. control mice). Consistent with the expected mechanism of action, mice receiving regimens containing CD27Ag had trends toward fewer T regulatory cells in tumors compared to other groups (Figure F; p=0.0391 for CD27Ag + αPD-L1 vs. control; p=0.2090 for triple therapy vs. control). Given the potent immunomodulatory activity, we next hypothesized that combination therapy would improve survival in this physiologically relevant, aggressive model. For this, mice with established liver tumors were treated over 3 weeks and monitored for survival (Figure A). While triple therapy improved survival versus controls, the overall effect was modest (Figure B, median survival 37.5 vs. 29.5 d, p<0.01). Notably, there was no survival benefit from triple therapy versus other groups. These data led us to hypothesize that inherent differences in immune populations between sites may contribute to resistance.
 

## Orthotopic CCA tumors harbor abundant myeloid cells
Suboptimal efficacy of triple therapy in orthotopic CCA prompted a thorough characterization of immune features in tumors. Hence, we established both orthotopic and subcutaneous URCCA4.3 tumors in separate groups of mice (Figure C), then harvested them after 2 weeks (Figure D). Normal livers from age-matched mice without tumors were controls. While no difference in CD3+ T cells was evident between orthotopic (p=0.0003) and subcutaneous (p<0.0001) models, they were lower in normal liver (Figure E). Moreover, myeloid cells were abundant in both models (p=0.0002) compared to the normal liver (Figure F). In this model, a greater frequency of CD19+ B cells was also in orthotopic compared to subcutaneous tumors and normal liver (Supplemental Figures S8A, C, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). Similar trends were evident for cells with B reg phenotypes (CD19+PD-L1+Tim-1+; Supplemental Figure S8D, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861); however, their frequency (as % live) calls into question their impact in this model. Contrasting these data were prominent CD11b+ myeloid cells, which were equivalent between orthotopic and subcutaneous tumors (Figure F; p=0.9573). However, significantly higher CD11b+Gr-1+ cells were detected in orthotopic versus subcutaneous URCCA4.3 tumors (Figure G; p=0.0010). Both cell populations were only a fraction of total live cells (<1%) in the normal liver (Figures F, G). In contrast, F4/80+ macrophages were more abundant in subcutaneous URCCA4.3 tumors (Supplemental Figure S8E, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). Notably, orthotopic tumors with FAC cells harbored high CD11b+ and CD11b+Gr-1+ myeloid cells compared to subcutaneous counterparts, indicating myeloid enrichment is conserved across genetically distinct CCA (Supplemental Figure S9, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861).

## Gr-1+ cells restrain triple therapy-induced systemic inflammation
Myeloid cells are a barrier for CCA immunotherapy, which may limit the efficacy of combining CD27Ag with MEK/PD-L1 blockade. We hypothesized that depleting myeloid cells would augment the efficacy of triplet therapy. To address this, mice with orthotopic tumors were treated with triple therapy, concurrent with an Ab targeting Gr-1+ cells (Supplemental Figure S10A, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). Unexpectedly, animals exhibited rapid and severe toxicity within 8 days of treatment, marked by profound weight loss that led to death or required euthanasia in 6/10 mice (Figures A, B and Supplemental Figure S10B, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). Blood and organs were harvested before mice became moribund, but given this was unexpected, collections were limited to mice sustaining weight during treatment and control groups. Plasma analysis revealed Gr-1+ cell depletion in combination with triple therapy, elevated erythropoietin (EPO) compared to mice treated with triple therapy and controls (Figure C). Similar trends were observed in other inflammatory cytokines and chemokines, including GM-CSF, MCP-1, TNF-α, IFN-γ, M-CSF, IL-5, IL-7, IL-10, IL-15, IL-17, KC, RANTES, and MIP-1β (Figure C and Supplemental Figure S10C, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). Many of these proteins are associated with liver damage, – prompting a closer histologic examination of the liver. Hepatocellular injury was most pronounced in mice receiving Gr-1+ depletion plus triple therapy, with frequent spotty and confluent necrosis in both tumor-involved and non-involved liver (Figure D). This was accompanied by marked hepatitis and splenomegaly (Supplemental Figure S10D, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861), the latter largely absent in other groups. These observations led us to consider whether Gr-1+ cell depletion might unmask T cell reactivity against normal liver. Immunohistochemistry confirmed CD3+ T cell infiltration in non-tumor liver regions (Figures E, F). Collectively, these data suggest Gr-1+ cells restrain potentially pathogenic inflammatory T cell responses, and their depletion in the context of triple therapy leads to an unbridled systemic and hepatic inflammation with detrimental consequences.
 

## DISCUSSION
This study demonstrates that CD27 agonists rescue T cell dysfunction due to MEK inhibition. Further, triple therapy with CD27Ag, anti-PD-L1 and MEKi increased memory T cells in CCA, which contributed to antitumor immunity in subcutaneous tumors but were insufficient in orthotopic models. Instead, these tumors harbored abundant CD11b+Gr-1+ myeloid cells capable of suppressing autologous T cells (Supplemental Figure S11, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). Depletion of myeloid cells in concert with triple therapy led to lethal inflammation, characterized by weight loss, systemic cytokines, and hepatocellular injury, accompanied by increased T cells in the liver. These data highlight the importance of orthotopic CCA models in uncovering the potential of novel therapies and toxicity. This work implies that inducing favorable phenotypic changes in CD8+ T cells alone is insufficient to overcome myeloid suppression. Our results also illustrate that strategies to deplete myeloid cells should be approached with caution, possibly favoring approaches to reprogram myeloid phenotypes toward those that foster productive immunity.
Identifying approaches to combat immunotherapy resistance is critical. This is especially relevant since CCA patients receive anti-PD-1/PD-L1 with chemotherapy, as front-line, standard of care. , Unfortunately, most CCA patients either have primary resistance or develop secondary resistance to immunotherapy. , , This finding has spurred efforts to explore combinatorial strategies against key suppressive mechanisms. Our group has tested dual MEK/PD-L1 blockade in preclinical models and BTC patients. – Unfortunately, efficacy was limited, mirroring outcomes of other immunotherapy efforts in CCA. , , However, patient specimens from these trials have uncovered why these approaches lack activity. Using these samples, we verified that MEKi reduced T cell activation, consistent with our mouse data. , , Subsequent studies indicated that agonist antibodies overcame this deficit and restored T cell function, providing a rationale for this study. 

This triple therapy enriched CD8+ T cells with stem and resident memory potential in tumors, regardless of site. To our knowledge, this is the first example of efficacy from this combination in any in vivo tumor model. From a mechanistic standpoint, the changes aligned with how each therapy component might act independently. For example, prior studies show MEKi enhances stem-like properties in T lymphocytes. However, studies in CD27-deficient mice implicate CD27 interactions as critical for expanding Tregs. It is well-established that CD27 agonism expands antigen-primed T cells and depletes T regulatory cells in mouse models and patients. Aligned with this data, we observed decreased T regulatory cells (CD4+CD25+FoxP3+) in orthotopic tumors of mice receiving CD27Ag regimens. Interestingly, triple therapy was most effective at facilitating CD8+Tcf-1+ cells to access subcutaneous tumors, versus other treatments. Greater effector and resident memory CD8+ T cells were also found in tumors of mice receiving triple therapy, regardless of site. These data suggest that CD8+ T cells with enhanced stemness potential migrate to tumors after therapy, giving rise to diverse CD8+ T cells that act as effectors or facilitate tissue establishment. 

Despite robust CD8+ T cell changes, we observed a trend toward decreased T regulatory cells in orthotopic tumors of mice receiving CD27Ag-containing regimens. However, tumors in 2 separate CCA models harbored more suppressive myeloid cells than when the same cells were implanted subcutaneously. These findings are significant for multiple reasons. First, they validate that orthotopic models are more relevant in approximating CCA. In fact, consistent with our data, preliminary results from the interim analysis of a randomized phase II clinical trial (NCT04941287) indicate limited responses in advanced BTC patients receiving either dual CD27Ag (varlilumab) and anti-PD-L1 (atezolizumab), or these agents combined with a MEKi (cobimetinib). Second, these results indicate suppressive myeloid cells or neutrophils may regulate both efficacy and adverse events resulting from immunotherapy that engages T cells. Therefore, it is crucial to evaluate biomarkers to identify similar T cell changes and determine if myeloid cell populations contribute to resistance in real-world settings.
This work implicates myeloid cells as a barrier to immunotherapy in CCA, but cautions that manipulating them must be approached carefully to guard against toxicity. For example, prior studies indicate that elevated tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), MDSCs, and tumor-associated neutrophils (TANs) are associated with poorer outcomes in CCA patients. , , , Similarly, cytokines and chemokines that support myeloid cells are elevated in patient CCA tumors. Likewise, targeting myeloid-like factors, GM-CSF in particular, improves outcomes in mice with CCA. , Yet, the inherent complexity and redundancy of myeloid cell networks in CCA are highlighted in this report, and further emphasize the potential risks of broadly targeting them. Redundancy in myeloid cell populations is also an issue for optimal targeting. In an elegant preclinical study by Loeuillard et al, TAM blockade resulted in a compensatory emergence of granulocytic MDSC that impaired antitumor immunity. Other work by Zhang et al uncovered how myeloid features of CCA are fueled by microbial changes that co-opt hepatocytes to recruit MDSCs to the liver. These data underscore the need for preclinical studies that define how depleting myeloid cells, or altering their differentiation state, impacts efficacy and toxicity that derive from T cell–stimulating regimens. This work will be key for de-risking future clinical efforts targeting myeloid cells for CCA immunotherapy.
While our work informs barriers to immunotherapy efficacy, we acknowledge some limitations. For instance, we are cognizant that populations beyond myeloid cells also contribute to resistance. While T regulatory cells were decreased, our analysis also revealed a greater B regulatory cells (PD-L1+Tim-1+) in the orthotopic versus subcutaneous URCCA4.3 models (Supplemental Figure 8D, http://links.lww.com/HEP/J861). Despite this, their frequency was low (<1% of total live cells), calling into question their contribution to this system. Further, data from URCCA4.3 and FAC models, although established models, do not capture the genetic heterogeneity of CCA in patients. It is now appreciated that, depending on genotype, CCA may have different immune features. For example, isocitrate dehydrogenase 1 (IDH1)-mutant tumors are characterized by T cell exclusion, while other studies have identified different immune features of CCA tumors in the context of distinct genomic, transcriptomic, or cellular signatures. – 

Taken together, this study emphasizes that triple therapy of CD27Ag, combined with anti-PD-L1 and MEKi, elicits potent changes in CD8+ T cells. However, these T cell changes alone were insufficient to promote lasting immunity in orthotopic CCA. Our data also implicate suppressive myeloid cells as contributors to differences in efficacy between the 2 models, while revealing unintended consequences upon their depletion. Further research is required to identify the optimal therapeutic means to overcome dominant myeloid features within CCA.
